J U D G M E N T

 

 

DR. S. TWUM, J.S.C.:  This is an appeal from the judgment of the Court of Appeal dated 12th November 2004. It substantially confirmed the judgment of the trial High Court delivered on 24th May 2001.

 

Even though the Court of Appeal claimed to have confirmed the judgment of the trial High Court, dated 24th May 2001, its final order dismissed the trial Court’s holding that “the 2nd Plaintiff is liable to the 1st Defendant in this matter and must indemnify the 1st Defendant who raised indemnity in its Statement of Defence.”

 

It is against this judgment that the 1st Defendant/Appellant (hereinafter called “the Appellant”) has appealed to this Court. It was aggrieved by the decision of the Court of Appeal holding it liable for the loss of the sum of US $82,000.00 belonging to the 1st Plaintiff/Respondent (hereinafter called “the Respondent”)

 

The Appellant filed three grounds of appeal, namely:

 

a.    The Court erred in the light of statutory and case law position to the contrary, in affirming the decision of the High Court that the 1st Defendant/Appellant is liable for the loss of the 1st Plaintiff/Respondent’s money. The Court of Appeal should have held and ordered the 1st Plaintiff to take out a Writ against the Co-Plaintiff.

 

b.    The Court erred in holding that the 1st Defendant/Appellant was liable in negligence for the loss of the 1st Plaintiff/Respondent’s money.

 

c.    The High Court having found out that the Co-Plaintiff must indemnify the 1st Defendant/Appellant, the Court of Appeal erred in law by refusing to make a consequential order that the Co-Plaintiff should pay the Plaintiff to the extent of the loss occasioned the Plaintiff. This grave error of law has occasioned the 1st Defendant/Appellant substantial miscarriage of justice.”

 

Pleadings

The Respondent in the High Court sued the Defendants for:

(a)   The sum of US $82,000 being monies fraudulently transferred by the 2nd Defendant to Account No. 010010279101 at the 1st Defendant’s Liberia Road Branch and negligently paid by the 1st Defendant for the 2nd Defendant.

 

(b)   Damages for the conversion of the said sum

 

(c)   Damages for the conspiracy to injure the Plaintiffs financially

 

(f)    General damages and special damages

 

It pleaded extensively and elaborately facts on which it relied to prove its case against the Appellant. For a better understanding of the issues thrown up for Your Lordships’ decision, permit me to reproduce important paragraphs of the Amended Statement of Claim as well as some paragraphs of the Statement of Defence! After all, it is said that justice is not an abstract concept. It has to have a context and a content. The context is provided by the facts underlying the dispute. The content is the law.

 

In paragraph 14 of the Amended Statement of Claim, the Plaintiff pleaded as follows:

 

“By two telefax instructions dated 2nd and 4th September, respectively, addressed to the Co-Plaintiff, the 2nd Defendant fraudulently instructed that two sums of US $12,000.00 and US $70,000.00 be transferred to the fictitious account.

In paragraph 17 (later renumbered as paragraph 15) the Plaintiffs pleaded that “the Co-Plaintiff duly transferred the sums of US $12,000 and US $70,000 into the Fictitious Account.”

Paragraph 17: The Plaintiff did not become aware of the fraud until sometime in January 1998 when the Plaintiff requested a statement of its accounts from the Co-Plaintiff.

In my opinion, this allegation of conspiracy is scandalous and vexatious. No iota of evidence was led in support of it. Indeed, it is an abuse of the process of the court and I will exercise my inherent jurisdiction to strike out paragraphs 20 and 21 of the Amended Statement of Claim.

 

The 1st Defendant pleaded in paragraphs 8 and 9 of their Statement of Defence thus:-

 

“8   The 1st defendant expressly denies any knowledge of the fraudulent nature of the 2nd defendant’s instructions to the 2nd Plaintiff and states that there was no way they could have known until they were informed by the 2nd Plaintiff”.

 

“9  The 1st defendant consequently denies sub-paragraphs (a) to (d) of paragraph 14 of the Statement of Claim.”

 

In paragraph 10, The 1st Defendant denied that they were negligent in complying with the 2nd Plaintiff’s instruction and asserted that they duly carried them out. They denied paragraph 18 of the Statement of Claim, including all its sub-paragraphs.

 

It is worthy of note that the Co-Plaintiff filed no Statement of Claim. Indeed, it made no claim at all so the contest was between the Plaintiff and 1st Defendant. A representative of the Co-Plaintiff was not even called as a witness and substantially, the 1st Plaintiff’s case was based on hearsay. However, this was not objected to and the evidence led remains in the record and will be considered. There is also no evidence that the 2nd Defendant’s account at the 1st Defendant’s Liberia Road Branch was a fictitious account.

 

In The Court of Appeal

The facts in this case are fairly represented by the pleadings. I do not intend to make a summary of them but in the course of this opinion, where necessary, I will point out some fact not clear from the pleadings.

 

The quintessence of the Respondent’s case against the Appellant is that as a result of its negligence in opening a bank account at its Liberia Road Branch, Accra, with the 2nd Defendant, as well as its further negligence in paying over the “colossal sum of $82,000” to the 2nd Defendant, the Respondent has sustained loss of similar magnitude and the Appellant should make good that loss. This submission of negligence was based on section 81 of our Bills of Exchange Act 1961 (Act 55) hereinafter “BEA”. The courts below accepted it and gave judgment for the Respondent. The Appellant says the Court of Appeal erred and invites Your Lordships to reverse that Court.

 

In the very first paragraph if its judgment the Court of Appeal noted: “It (the case) dramatizes the horrors of some of the sophisticated banking frauds often heard of in these days. They are mostly technically designed criminal offences capable of detection only through the same lenses of high technology typical of our current computer age. The emphasis here is that modern criminals have left the streets and are using high technology to infiltrate our banking and other financial institutions for their fraudulent operations. It is within this background that the facts of this case must be scrutinized and analysed.”

 

My Lords, this appeal deals with an important aspect of modern banking; ie. international funds transfer. The payment message in this case was communicated by electronic means. A few centuries ago, the world mercantile community invented negotiable instruments to facilitate international trade. The bill of exchange was also developed to obviate the necessity of carrying large sums of money across international frontiers. Laws were ultimately enacted to cover their use and new nomenclature was devised to distinguish various parties to a bill, such as drawer, drawee, payee, holder, holder for value and holder in due course. The English passed the Bills of Exchange Act, 1882. This was fallowed by the Sale of Goods Act, 1893 to regulate both domestic and foreign sale of goods. Again, new terms were coined, such as F.O.B., F.A.S, C & F and C.I.F.

 

As a special function of banking, foreign trade was financed by commercial documentary credits (popularly known as “letters of credit”). By this device, a banker lent its name and standing to overcome lack of knowledge of, or confidence in, one of the parties to the transaction, by the other. In due course, commercial documentary credits became subject to a body of rules formulated by the International Chamber of Commerce.

 

I am persuaded that in the not-too-distant future, internationally agreed laws will be hammered out by the international business community. For the moment, the evidence is that Europe, America and some Commonwealth countries are evolving their own rules. Obviously, these rules are disparate and in a state of flux. In the main, the laws of contract and agency are being pressed into service where possible. But so far, I have not come across any country which is relying on its bills of exchange laws to govern international funds transfer transactions where the payment message was communicated by electronic means.

 

I am afraid, learned counsel who appeared for the parties in this case allowed themselves to be mesmerized by our Bills of Exchange Act 1961, as well as foreign authorities which dealt with cheques or other negotiable instruments. In the result little or no assistance was offered to the courts.   

 

 

In view of the fact that, all the three grounds of appeal are collectively complaining about the same thing, ie. the holding that the Appellant was liable to the Respondent, for the loss of its US $82,000.00, I will deal with all of them together..

 

In their said judgment the Court of Appeal stated at page 5:

“He (Counsel for the Respondent) relied upon statute law which can be found in section 81 of the Bills of Exchange Act 1961 (Act 55). He submitted that this legal provision imposed a statutory duty of care upon all collecting bankers not to cause damage in the form of financial loss to innocent persons. It is provided under section 81 of the Bills of Exchange Act (supra) as follows:-

 

“Protection to Collecting Banker”

 

“81. Where a banker in good faith and without negligence –

(a)   receives payment for a customer of a cheque, whether crossed or uncrossed; or

 

(b)   having credited a customer’s account with the amount of such a cheque, receives payment thereof for himself; and the customer has no title or a defective title, to the cheque, the banker does not incur any liability to the true owner of the cheque by reason only of having received payment thereof”.

 

The Court of Appeal continued:

“The above provision when construed and interpreted by the elementary rules of statutory interpretation, seeks to impose upon all banks a statutory duty of care to all persons (my emphasis), irrespective of whether they directly or indirectly do business with them. The statute reveals clearly the legislature’s knowledge of and apprehension that criminals exist within or outside the society and care must be taken to check them so that they may not use the banks as vehicles for their criminal acts to the detriment of innocent citizens or foreign nationals. The statute imposes sanctions upon banks who do not exercise due care and vigilance”

 

Again at page 6, the court said, “The common law situation where a sort of relationship is required between the Plaintiff and the Defendant as stated by Lord Atkin in Donoghue’s case does not apply to the provisions of Section 81 of Act 55. The crucial issue, however, is whether the respondent proved satisfactorily that the appellant bank was negligent.”

 

I am afraid this quotation is rather lengthy but I hope its impact will soon be evident.

 

It was argued by learned Counsel for the respondent that section 81 should be read as one with section 82 of the BEA 1961 (Act 55). That is true enough, but in my view, that does not advance the position of the Respondent. The facts, as I have stated them above, show that none of these documents mentioned in S.82 was involved in the electronic transfer.

 

Paragraph 14 of the 1st Plaintiff’s statement of claim stated that the 2nd defendant, gave the Co-Plaintiff two telefax instructions to transfer the sums of US $12,000 and $70,000. respectively to the 1st defendant – the Appellant.

 

The so-called forged instruction allegedly issued by the 2nd defendant was said to have been by two telex messages. They were not tendered in evidence by the Plaintiff so it is difficult to assess how the chairman’s signature was forged in the telex messages. I find that difficult to fathom. But whatever form the messages took, it is quite clear to me that there was nothing wrong with the mechanism for the actual transfer of the money from Switzerland to Ghana via New York. See exhibit B. In terms of the mechanism for the transfer, what happened was no different from what would have happened if the Co-Plaintiff had not been allegedly deceived by the alleged forgery of the signature of the only signatory to the Plaintiff’s account with the Co-Plaintiff.

 

The Plaintiffs were themselves not explicit in their pleadings whether the moneys were transferred by electronic transactions. But exhibits B and B1 clearly state that the  communication to the Appellant for the payment of the money to the 2nd Defendant was by network operated by SWIFT from Geneva, via New York and then to Accra. In Exhibit B Stanchart, the defendant, used the SWIFT system to communicate with ANZ Grindlays Bank – the Co-Plaintiff. In B1, a similar method was used by Grindlays to communicate with Stanchart.

 

“SWIFT” stands for “The Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication.” It is an organisation maintained by banks and financial institutions throughout the world. It operates a network of communications which can be used by banks and other financial institutions for money transfers. My researches further show that SWIFT is merely a messaging system. It does not have a settlement component.

 

Swift operations are transmitted by the originator’s bank on the day instructions are received from the customer and are expected to reach their destination the same day.

 

(This account is taken from “Modern Banking Law.” 3rd Edition, by E.P. Ellinger, E. Lommicka and R.J.A. Hooley, page 489).

 

A little reflection will show that in the SWIFT system, nothing like cheques or other negotiable instruments pass between the originator’s bank and the beneficiary’s bank. As Millet J. put in Agip (Africa) Ltd v Jackson (1990) Ch. 265 at 286 only “a stream of electrons” passes!

 

Electronic Money Transfers

The one and only object of all the different types of money transfer operations is to instruct the originator’s bank to perform a service on behalf of the originator who is the bank’s customer and principal. The orginator’s bank therefore acts in a representative capacity; in most cases, as an agent of its customer and is obliged to carry out the instructions given to it with reasonable skill and care. See Royal Products Ltd v Midland Bank Ltd (1981) 2 Lloyds Rep. 194 at 198.

 

The originator’s bank’s duty of care and skill imposes on it two main specific obligations –

 

(i)            The amount has to be transferred on time

(ii)          The bank’s duty of care is owed only to the originator or transferor, who is a customer. This duty of care, does not extend to the beneficiary of the transfer because there is no contractual link between them. Further, no duty of care is owed by the originator’s bank to the beneficiary in tort, unless the beneficiary can bring himself within the “special relationship” of Hedley Byrne vrs. Heller & Partners 1964 AC 465.

 

Legal Nature of Electronic Transfer

What is the legal nature of electronic money transfer orders? The wording of the communications in exhibits B, B1, B3 and B4 made it clear that the SWIFT system used in money transfer transactions did not involve negotiable instruments; at any rate the recorded acknowledgements are not bills of exchange. We know that a cheque is a bill of exchange drawn on a banker and payable on demand. (s.72) BEA 1961 (Act 55). A bill of exchange is an unconditional order in writing, addressed by one person to another, signed by the person giving it, requiring the person to whom it is addressed, to pay on demand or at a fixed or determinable future time, a sum certain in money, to or to the order of a specified person, or to bearer. Electronically conveyed messages do not fit this definition of a bill of exchange in section (1) of the BEA. 1961. Such messages are not unconditional orders. They are really banker to banker requests among members of SWIFT and the sums are not payable on demand. Indeed, in Tenax Steamship Co Ltd vrs. Brimnes (Owners of) The Brimnes (1975) 1 QB 929 Lord Justice Edmund Davies (at 949), and Lord Justice Cairns (at 969) pointed out that the Bank Giro form was not an “order”. Their Lordships held further that a telex transfer order was not a negotiable instrument. 

 

The Co-Plaintiff – The Originator’s Bank

In this case before your Lordships, the Plaintiff was the supposed originator and its bank was the ANZ Grindlays Bank Ltd, the Co-Plaintiff. The Plaintiff pleaded that the 2nd defendant instructed the Co-Plaintiff by two telex messages. It is not clear whether the telex messages bore the forged signature of the Plaintiff’s chairman. They were not tendered in evidence! The Plaintiff does not state which of the Co-Plaintiff’s officers sent the SWIFT messages to the 1st defendant. But as I have pointed out above the Co-Plaintiff owed a duty of care and skill to the Plaintiff. The overwhelming evidence is that the Co-Plaintiff dismally failed in its duty of care and skill to the Plaintiff.

 

It is the Plaintiff’s case that there were two previous fake instructions allegedly by the 2nd Defendant to the Co-Plaintiff to transfer moneys from the Plaintiff’s account abroad. In the first place the fraudster’s scheme was frustrated because the sum instructed was such that the Co-Plaintiff could not comply because it had insufficient funds to transfer. The fraudster, subsequently reduced the amount to US $70,000.00. The Co-Plaintiff transferred it. The beneficiary’s bank, at that time – International Commercial Bank – received the money but as a result of its misgivings it returned the money to the Co-Plaintiff. Indeed the 1.C.B had taken the decision not to deal with Nigerians – (see Exhibit A) A few weeks later two instructions allegedly by the same 2nd Defendant to transfer US 12,000 and US $70,000 to E. O Effiong was received by the Co-Plaintiff. Without any enquiries whatsoever it proceeded to execute the instructions and transferred the total sum of $82,000 to the 1st Defendant bank – the beneficiary’s bank, using the SWIFT system of communication. It must be obvious that all the alleged fake previous instructions must have named E. O. Effiong as the beneficiary. The International Commercial Bank had returned the US $70,000 because it suspected that the beneficiary was a fraudster. How can the Co-Plaintiff turn round and blame the 1st defendant for the loss? It had ample warning that something was amiss. The net result at this stage, in my view, is that the Co-Plaintiff dismally failed to discharge its duty of care and skill which was owed to its customer – 1st Plaintiff, who had issued no instructions. That was a contractual duty. At the very least the Co-Plaintiff must have acted as the Plaintiff’s agent. For breach of that duty, the Plaintiff was entitled to sue the Co-Plaintiff for breach of contract and claim appropriate damages from it which, at least, would be the value of the $82,000.00 transferred. Why this action was not pursued only the 1st Plaintiff or his legal advisers would know.

 

Beneficiary’s Bank

Now to the 1st defendant – the beneficiary’s bank. In the ordinary course of business, the beneficiary’s bank (Stanchart) credits the beneficiary’s account in reliance on the instruction given to it by the originator’s bank. Once this is done, the beneficiary’s bank holds the funds to the customer’s order. In the American case of Shawmut Wocester County v First American Bank and Trust Co. 731 F Supp. 57 (1990), it was held that for this purpose alone, the beneficiary’s bank is the agent of the originator’s bank. It was further held in that case that as the beneficiary’s agent, it was not entitled to obey the subsequent instruction’s of the originator’s bank to reverse the credit entry in the beneficiary’s account and refund the money. See also the American case of Middle East Banking v State Street Bank International 821 F 2d 897 (2nd (ir). 1987.

 

It follows from what has been said above that when the beneficiary’s bank credits the beneficiary’s account with the amount involved, its position changes, in that its principal is no longer the originator’s bank but the customer who becomes entitled to draw on the funds. For example, in Dovey v Bank of New Zealand (2000) NZLR 641, the Court of Appeal of New Zealand held that the beneficiary, by nominating the beneficiary’s bank to which the funds are to be transferred, gave that bank authority to accept funds on its behalf despite the fact that no account had been opened for it. But in Mardorf Peach & Co Ltd v. Attica Sea Carriers Corporation of Liberia, The Laconia (1977) AC 850 it was held that the beneficiary’s bank acts as agent of the beneficiary.

 

These cases which obviously mirror the state of the practice of banking in different countries, show that the law in this area is disparate and developing. I will prefer to rest my decision on well-settled principles among international bankers.

 

Section 81 BEA (1961)

A lot of scholarship and industry was expended by Counsel for the Respondent and naturally resisted by Counsel for the Appellant with equal zeal and erudition that the Appellant was negligent in terms of section 81 of our Bills of Exchange Act 1961, (Act 55) and therefore liable for the Plaintiff’s loss.

 

I have pointed out above that electronic money transfer orders are not negotiable instruments. Consequently, in my view, the Bills of Exchange Act, 1961, is not applicable to such transfers. However, in deference to counsel on both sides, I now turn to discuss the Plaintiff’s claim in conversion mounted against the Appellant and how that claim, if established could be affected by S. 81 of BEA. For the sake of our discussion, let us suppose that the Appellant was a collecting bank. This is truly a contradiction in terms. In a traditional banking operation, the collecting bank would receive a cheque (or some other documentary intangible) from its customer. It would take that to the paying banker and literally collect the value of that cheque for its customer and credit it to its account on which its customer could draw. There was nothing in this case for the collecting bank to take to the paying bank to collect the proceeds for its customer.

 

The appropriate description of the Appellant in international money transfer is the “beneficiary’s bank.” Let us further assume, notwithstanding what we know, that the beneficiary’s bank handled something (in the traditional banking system, this will be a cheque) or some other documentary intangible on behalf of the fortfeasor, its customer, who has misappropriated the instrument. Still, using the traditional analysis, the “true owner” of such an instrument may wish to recover the amount of the instrument from the collecting banker. The ensuing action would be based on the cheque and not on the proceeds thereof, as the law does not recognize an action in conversion for the misappropriation of money or a mere chose in action. See Bavins Jnr and Sims v London and South Western Bank 1900 1 QB. 270.

 

The traditional view is that for conversion to be established, there must be an unjustified handling of the plaintiffs goods by the defendant such as to deny his title. Obviously then, where there are no goods to be handled by the Appellant, it cannot not be liable in conversion.

 

Learned Counsel for the Respondent realized the obstacle created by the absence of cheques in SWIFT transfers, but suggested that Section 82 of the BEA must be read together with S.81, and according to him, section 82 incorporated any instrument whatsoever and against any person. That is, of course, untenable. Section 81 concentrates on cheques and S.82 merely extends the collecting banker’s protection to cover the following:-

 

(a)   instruments intended to enable a person to receive payment from his banker.

 

(b)   Banker’s draft

 

(c)   Dividend warrants

 

It provides that section 81 should apply to these instruments as they apply to cheques. In sum, section 82 extends the statutory protection of bankers who handle any of the said instruments, provided they do so without negligence. If I may state the obvious. The protection the collecting banker gets is that it does not incur any liability in conversion which it would otherwise have incurred. In the case before Your Lordships, the Appellant did not handle any cheque or any of the instruments listed in section 82.

 

The Tort of Conversion

The authorities establish that except for an action for money had and received or its modern siblings which are known under the rubric of “restitutionary claims,” conversion is the only action available to the true owner of the cheque or bill or note which has been unjustifiably handled by a collecting bank. See United Australia Ltd v. Barclays Bank Ltd (1941) AC 1.

 

Conversion is a strict tort. It is the wrongful interference with goods of another; for example, by taking, using or destroying them, inconsistent with owner’s right of possession. In the case of cheques the law is that the cheque is discharged when the collecting banker collects payment on it in good faith and in the ordinary course of business, for its customer. The true owner loses its value which is the amount the cheque was drawn for. Intention is no element in conversion. But there must be goods and for this purpose, a bill, note, cheque or the paper on which it is written is “goods” within the definition of goods.

 

In the case before Your Lordships, there were no “goods” – no cheque, no bill, no note; mere movement of electrons. Consequently, the 1st defendant could not be liable to the 1st Plaintiff in conversion.

 

As I have pointed out above, the message which was sent to the 1st defendant, (Stanchart) was transmitted electronically. There was no cheque bill or any other documentary intangible. As it was explained in Agip (Africa) Ltd v Jackson 1990 Ch. 265 “nothing passed” between Switzerland and Ghana “but a stream of electrons.” In these circumstances, it is not possible to treat the money received by the 1st defendant, as representing the proceeds of the forged payment order or any other physical asset previously held by the Co-Plaintiff and delivered by it to the Appellant. It was simply a direction by the Plaintiff to its bank, the Co-Plaintiff. Consequently, neither S.81, nor S.82 was applicable to the payment by the Appellant to the 2nd Defendant.

 

Origin of S.81 BEA

The tort-feasor’s good faith is not a valid defence in an action in conversion. This exposed bankers to very serious risks unless they verified that each of their customers had a good title to every cheque paid to the credit of his account. A bank may be liable in conversion if it pays a cheque or similar instrument on a forged endorsement, (Smith v Union Bank of London (1875) L.R. 10 QB 291 at 295; or collects a bill, note or cheque with a forged endorsement, or to which the customer has no title. (Baker v Barclays Bank Ltd (1955) 2 AIIER 57. It is for these forms of dealings that section 81 was enacted to relieve the bank of liability in conversion. To save banks from having to collect cheques at their peril, it was felt necessary to introduce a statutory defence. This was achieved by the English S. 82 of the BEA, 1882. This was repealed and replaced by S.4 (1) of the English Cheques Act, 1957. This is the forerunner of our section 81 which establishes a well defined test for the collecting banker’s protection. To invoke it successfully, the bank must satisfy four requisites – the most important for our present purpose, is that the bank must act “without negligence.”

 

Of course, it goes without saying that where the banker has not handled any goods so as to expose it to a possible claim in conversion, it does not need the protection of section 81.

 

In the trial court, the learned trial judge accepted the submission of Counsel for the Plaintiff that the 1st defendant was negligent in the opening of the 2nd Defendant’s account and also in the payment of the money “transferred” to it by the Co-Plaintiff. It would seem that this was based on the negligence mentioned in section 81 of the Bills of Exchange Act 1961 (Act 55). Section 81 provides:- “Where a banker in good faith and without negligence –

 

(a)    receives payment for a customer of a cheque whether crossed or uncrossed; or

 

(b)    having credited a customer’s account with the amount of such cheque, receives payment thereof for himself; and the customer has no title or a defective title, to the cheque, the banker does not incur any liability to the true owner of the cheque by reason only of having received payment thereof.

 

It should be emphasized that the section relieves a banker only if he handles a cheque (or a section 82 instrument). There was no cheque in the dealings between the Co-Plaintiff and the Appellant.

 

Statutory Negligence

My Lords, what constitutes “negligence” for the purpose of S.81 which requires a banker to act “without negligence” The statute does not define negligence. Paget’s Law of Banking, 7th edition, page 375 explains that the importation of negligence at all into this section, in a sense, is an anomaly.” In my view, negligence in this sense is artificial. There can be no negligence without a duty. See Carlisle and Cumberland Banking Co v. Bragg (1911) 1 KB 489. There is really no contractual relation between the collecting banker and the true owner, giving rise to a duty on the part of the former to the latter. The banker’s only contractual obligation is to his customer.

 

In Bissell & Co v Fox Brothers & Co (1884), 51 L.T. 663 Denman J and the Court of Appeal confirmed that the duty inherent in S.81 is a purely statutory one imposed on a banker in favour of the true owner, and the negligence consists in the disregard of his interest, apart from those of the customer. Obviously, the assumption of this duty and liability to a stranger must be regarded as part of the price paid by bankers for protection under the statute. “Broadly speaking, the banker must exercise the same care and forethought in the interest of the true owner with regard to cheques paid in by the customer, as a reasonable business man would bring to bear on similar business of his own”. The authorities establish that the bank’s action must be in accordance with the “ordinary practice of bankers” see per Lord Dunedin in Taxation Commissioners vr. English, Scottish and Australian Bank (1920) AC, 683 at 689. 

 

There is a plethora of cases on what constitutes negligence in the bank. This appears to have informed the Plaintiff in the particulars listed in paragraph 18 of its statement of claim. The cases suggest that whether a banker has been negligent or not will be decided by reference to the practice of reasonable men carrying on the business of banking and endeavouring to do so in a manner calculated to protect themselves and others against fraud.

 

It was argued forcibly by the Respondent’s counsel that the failure of the Appellant to obtain or follow up references when the account was being opened for the 2nd Defendant was evidence of negligence. That may be so. However, it is equally correct to say that if the banker acts on the reply of only one referee, as happened in this case, the banker, (ie the Appellant) was not necessarily negligent, though where the prospective customer is a foreigner, (as the 2nd Defendant was) it was desirable for the bank to confirm the reply by an examination of the customer’s passport. This is what Marfani & Co v Midland Bank Ltd (1968) 1 W.L.R. 956 actually decided.

 

In this present case, there is evidence on record that this practice was followed. It is really a matter of degree how much more the Appellant ought to have done in the circumstances of this case when there appeared to be a question mark on the referee.

 

There is also the issue of officials at the Head Office of the Appellant identifying the 2nd Defendant by looking at his passport before paying the money to him across the counter. This, to my knowledge, is a legitimate practice for cashing travellers’ cheques abroad. Since the liberalization of our foreign exchange laws, there is some confusion as to whether moneys transmitted from abroad to non-residents in Ghana can be paid in foreign currency to them. I am no authority on modern banking practice but payment of foreign currency to the non-resident true owner across the counter, is not forbidden practice. In this particular case before Your Lordships, what I find negligent in terms of Section 81 of BEA was the fact that the passport which was examined by the Head Office bank officials did not bear the photograph of the 2nd defendant. The fact that it was issued a few days before the payment is not necessarily negligent. In other words, I do not wish it to be made a rule of law for the banks that a recently acquired passport cannot be relied upon to identify a person for purposes of paying money to him. I think the matter must be left to the bankers themselves.

 

One other matter on which I want to comment is the practice that when opening an account, the banker was under a duty to inquire as to the name of the new customer’s employer, or as counsel suggested in this case, the line of business of the new customer. The full statement of the law, in relation to that practice as I understand it, is that this inquiry should be made if the banker knows that the customer occupies a position which involves the handling and an opportunity of stealing his employer’s cheques. In my view, the practice would be too burdensome if followed to its logical conclusion. Would the banker be entitled to request that it be informed any time the customer changed his job? I am aware that the practice of making such enquiries has been extant since Lloyds Bank Ltd v Savory & Co (1933) AC 201. These days, there is good reason for doubting the entire usefulness of the request for a reference. Fundamentally, the prospective customer’s referee may be either a person who is well-known to the bank, such as an existing customer, or a stranger. In the former case, the bank is deemed to be entitled to rely on the referee’s judgment. In the latter case, the manuals of most banks prescribe that a referee who is unknown to the bank be asked to give the name of his own bankers when he replies to the enquiry. Marfani’s case supra, shows the ease with which even a respectable customer of a bank can be misled by an imaginative rogue who wishes to use him as a referee.

 

Let me hasten to add that these comments are meant to caution that too slavish a reliance on some of these practices of “absence of negligence” can rather play into the hands of rogues.

 

It must be pointed out that contrary to what counsel suggested, S. 81 does not establish a direct duty of care owed by the bank to the true owner. Rather, it prescribes a standard with which the bank has to comply in order to be able to invoke the defence conferred on it. The distinction between a Donoghue v Stevenson duty of care and a general observable standard by bankers is of practical importance. The bank loses the protection of section 81 whenever it fails to act “without negligence.” It does not create a cause of action whereas Donoghue v Stevenson negligence, does. That is the essential mistake which was made by both the High Court and the Court of Appeal. In appropriate circumstances, the only cause of action against the banker is the unauthorised handling of the true owner’s goods. That gives the action in conversion.  

 

Section 81, of Act 55, as I have explained above was enacted to relieve a bank of liability in conversion if it wrongfully dealt with a customer’s cheque. It is when a bank claims protection under section 81 of Act 55, that it must establish that it acted in good faith and without negligence in circumstances which would otherwise make it liable in conversion. All the special fact-situations confirmed by the courts as constituting statutory negligence are aimed at defeating the statutory protection. Singly or collectively, they do not create any cause of action. In the case on appeal, the Appellant was not liable to anybody in conversion and did not therefore need to be relieved of liability by section 81. Therefore a finding that the Appellant was negligent under S.81, even if valid would be of no avail to the Respondent.

 

The Court of Appeal made some pronouncement on the legal effect of S.81 of BEA which should not be glossed over. It is not correct as that Court declared that “the section seeks to impose upon all banks a statutory duty of care to all persons, irrespective of whether they directly or indirectly do business with them…………. The statute imposes sanctions upon banks who do not exercise due care and vigilance.”

 

A cursory look at the heading of Section 81 clearly shows that it is meant to provide protection to bankers, not to impose sanctions on them. Furthermore, the section deals with only two categories of persons – the true owner of cheques and the bank’s customer. Finally the section is limited to unjustified handling by the banker of cheques and other documentary intangibles stated in S.82. I have dealt adequately with this matter elsewhere in this judgment. This piece here is meant to specifically address the mistaken interpretation of S.81 by the Court of Appeal.

 

Claims in Restitution

My Lords, before I am done, I wish to call attention to a potent cause of action which could have been resorted to by the lawyers in the High Court. In these proceedings, nobody claimed any relief for money had and received or any other restitutionary claim on the basis of money paid under a mistake of fact or even of law. In the circumstances, I need not examine the principles under which such a claim, including the right to trace funds both at common law and in equity could succeed.

 

From what I have discussed above it is clear to me that both the High Court and the Court of Appeal as well as counsel failed to realize that the old law, both statutory and common law, governing banking transactions involving negotiable instruments, particularly cheques, was inadequate for today’s electronic banking operations. What is needed is a vigorous search and the development of new and relevant principles of law for the guidance of the Ghanaian banking community. This opinion is a modest effort in that direction. One of the most potent causes of action for recovery of money paid in circumstances similar to what happened in this case is tracing both at common law and in equity under the principle of payment under a mistake of fact or a mistake of law. It may be advisable for bankers and legal practitioners in this field to bear this in mind. The principles developed in the case of Agip (Africa) Ltd v Jackson (supra), both in the English High Court and the Court of Appeal would probably have adequately addressed the Plaintiff’s woes, subject, of course, to any appropriate defences available to the Defendants, including the defence under the Limitation Decree, 1972, (NRCD 54).

 

But for now for the reasons I have stated above, I have no alternative but to allow the appeal and to set aside the judgments of both the High Court and the Court of Appeal. The Appellant was not liable to the Respondent in conversion, and the S.81 negligence therefore went to no issue.

 

Appeal is allowed accordingly.
