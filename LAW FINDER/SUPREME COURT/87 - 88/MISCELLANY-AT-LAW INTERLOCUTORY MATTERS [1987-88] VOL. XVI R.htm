MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: INTERLOCUTORY MATTERS [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 41—53

 Osei-Hwere P V

 

INTRODUCTION

It is said that an effective way of teaching a beginner how to swim is to have him thrown into deep water. A similar approach prevails in the training of judges. The spectacle of recently qualified but untrained judges seeking to master court work by a process of trial and error is not uncommon. It is not fair to the public or the initiated judges themselves to encourage entry into the world of administering justice in this way. A more primitive method of instruction could hardly be found. Whilst the extrovert and brash personality may thrive upon such treatment, an intelligent though sensitive beginner may not be inclined to accept the psychological pressures involved. Logic, experience and the public interest all demand that judges should, before they take their seat for the first time on the Bench, receive a course of practical instruction in the work they are appointed to do. Although we do not have with us an institution like the New York University Institute of Judicial Administration which is well known for the important role it plays in educating American judges. I am convinced that this humble beginning by arranging this seminar or conference, if repeated periodically, may go a long way in achieving the desired end.

Scope of the Subject

Interlocutory matters, as the expression signifies, are concerned with those matters done during the pendency of a law-suit and which are not final. The spectrum of these matters is very wide and may cover activities as the removal of a writ, substituted service, striking out and adding parties, ordering further and better particulars, striking out pleadings, amendment of pleadings, interlocutory proceedings from the summons level to the directions stage, discovery by interrogatories, discovery of documents, production of documents, etc.

Content of the Subject

Every interlocutory matter involves the exercise of a power by the court or judge. The words in which the provisions governing each particular matter are couched leave the prima facie impression of giving the court or judge an absolute discretion as to whether to exercise the power or not. This is because it says that the power “may” be used. But absolute discretion is a dangerous star to stear by. Wherefore the law demands that the discretion must be exercised judiciary that is, according to justice and right reason. It must always be remembered that although the public’s expectations will allow those exercising discretion, at least in a judicial context, generous room to manoeuvre their response to what seems to them to be an indiscretion will understandably be correspondingly severe. It is for this reason that it has become part of the received wisdom of our times that judges should have due regard to the wider social and other implications of the cases they have to try.

Let me explain myself. Those of you who are keen followers of the results of our football league matches will remember how poorly Accra Hearts of Oak had fared by the end of the first half of the last league season. At the beginning of the second half they were billed to play their rival club, Accra Great Olympics, on a Sunday. On the immediately preceding Friday the managers of Hearts sealed a writ against the management of Olympics claiming damages for inducing the breach of contact by three of their regular players who had then registered with Olympics. In addition they sought an order of injunction to restrain those offending players from playing for Olympics. For this purpose they filed an ex parte motion for leave to serve their motion for an injunction alongside with the writ and also, as a matter of emergency, to stop those players from being fielded against Hearts of Oak in the impending match. It is common knowledge that, perhaps next to party-politics, the league matches whip up public feelings. Could the public resentment (at least from the host of supporters of Olympics) be imagined if they woke up that Sunday afternoon to find that without Olympics being heard three of their players had been forced out of the field by the court. This motion was rushed before me that Friday at about 12 noon because I happened to be sitting. But, emergency or no emergency, I firmly booted them out of court because its repercussions would have been unjust not only to Olympics but to their host of supporters. Besides the emergency was the creation of the plaintiffs themselves, ie management of Hearts, who knowing well in advance of the scheduled match, waited till the nick of time to mount their action.

The practice of hauling GFA to court by an aggrieved football club in the thick of the league season is a familiar-sight. An interim injunction granted by the court in these circumstances do not only disrupt the league matches which are, no doubt, planned months in advance, they also tend to embarrass GFA in their continental commitments. A case of this nature came before me once where an attempt was made to stop the league matches which were already behind schedule. A week delay in court over the dispute would have cost GFA dearly because they could not have concluded the matches in time to declare the winner for the continental assignment. Even before I could pronounce on the motion (which I would have thrown out any way) GFA yielded to the plaintiffs the point they were after. I will say that the plaintiffs obtained that point by “blackmail.”

In short the exercise of discretion must, where relevant, be considered against the social background of the particular application.

Appeals on Interlocutory Matters

The spate of appeals that come before the Court of Appeal on the exercise of their discretion by trial judges in interlocutory matters disclose the slips they may be prone to make. Yaw Buabeng v Kwaku Forkuo, Civil Appeal No 110/68 dated 2 February 1970, reviews the authorities as to how far an appellate court can entertain an appeal based on an order made by a trial judge on a matter of discretion. Such an order may be reversed or varied where it is shown that it was made under a mistake of the law: Evans v Barthlam [1937] AC 473; or it will result in injustice being done: Evans v Barthlam (supra); or that no weight, or sufficient weight, was given to relevant considerations: Charles Osenton & Company v Johnston [1942] AC 130 at p 138; or under a misapprehension as to the facts; Young v Thomas [1892] 2 Ch 134. These circumstances are, of course, not exhaustive and they have been recited only as warning posts to guide your steps in the exercise of your discretion. But your safest conduct, perhaps, is generally to avoid making a “speaking order”, that is an order that argues out the reasons supporting the exercise of your discretion in a particular way. For if you do so the appellate court will readily examine the soundness of those reasons. Afterall the application that calls for the exercise of your discretion is interlocutory and the display of the depth of erudition can properly await the final judgment.

Instances of Interlocutory Matters at Work: Renewal of Writ

By Order 8, r 1 of the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954 LN 140A) an original writ shall be in force for 12 months; but if the defendant in the writ is not served within that period the plaintiff may, before the expiration of the 12 months, apply to the court for leave to review the writ. If the judge is satisfied that reasonable efforts have been made to serve such defendant or good reasons have been shown he may order that the writ be renewed for six months from the date of such renewal inclusive, and so from time to time during the currency of the renewed writ.

It must be noted that the application to renew must be brought before the twelve months or before the renewed period of six months has expired. Again the power conferred on the judge to renew from time to time excludes the doctrine that a statutory power is exhausted by its first exercise. The discretion to extend the validity of the writ ought to be exercised with caution, and renewal ought not to be granted as of course on an application which is necessarily made ex parte. There must be exceptional circumstances to justify the exercise of a discretion to extend the validity of a writ beyond the period of twelve months allowed for its service; this is especially so if the effect of renewal was to deprive the defendant of an accrued period of limitation; Easy v Universal Anchorage Co, Ltd [1974] 2 All ER 1104. Exceptional circumstances justifying the exercise of a discretion to extend the validity of the writ might arise:

“Where there has been agreement between the parties, express or implied to defer service of the writ; or where the delay in the application to extend the validity of the writ has been induced; or contributed to by the words or conduct of the defendant or his representative; or perhaps, where the defendant has evaded service or, for other reasons without the plaintiff’s fault, the writ could not have been served earlier even if the application had been made and granted earlier. [Per Megaw J in Heaven v Road and Rail Wagons Ltd [1965] 2 QB 355 at 365]. Such exceptional circumstances may, also exist where negotiations for a settlement between the parties are proceeding at the time of the application for renewal, but not where the application for renewal is made after the negotiations have ceased.”

In Firman v Ellis (The Times, 4 July 1977) F was travelling as a passenger in a car driven by E when it was involved in an accident on 23 May 1973 and F was injured. Negotiations were commenced and F’s solicitor issued a writ on 26 July 1974 but it was not served. Negotiations continued and F rejected an offer. Further medical reports were requested by E’s insurers and F supplied evidence about his employment prospects. On 25 July 1975 the writ expired without having been served. In October 1976 F’s solicitor made another approach for settlement. E’s insurers took the point that since more than 3 years had elapsed since the accident, the action was statute-barred. In March 1977 F applied for the renewal of the writ, but the registrar refused to renew it F appealed and also applied for leave under the Limitation Act, 1975, s 2D to serve another writ out of time. E contended that the application was wholly due to the failure of F’s solicitor to serve the writ within one year, and it would be misapplication of the policy of section 2D if it were to operate as a protection to negligent solicitors instructed by plaintiffs. It was held (QBD Kerr J) that the appeal against the registrar’s refusal to renew the writ would be dismissed; for there had to be good reasons for renewal, particularly if the effect of removal was to deprive the defendant of an accrued period of limitation. Leave was, however, given to serve the writ out of time under the requirements of section 2D of the Limitation Act, 1975, which the plaintiff had satisfied.

The defendant appealed contending that section 2D was to be construed restrictively as applying only to exceptional cases and that, in any event, the plaintiff would not suffer any prejudice if the action were statute-barred for he would have an unanswerable claim against his solicitors for negligence. It was held by the Court of Appeal that the discretion conferred by section 2D was not limited to a residual class of case, or to “exceptional” cases, but extended to all cases in which the three-year limitation period had expired; in such a case the court had an unfettered discretion to allow the action to proceed if, having regard to the matters prescribed by section 2D it considered it equitable to do so; Firman v Ellis [1978] 2 All ER 851, CA.

It may be noted that section 23 of our own Limitation Decree, 1972 (NRCD 54) provides in a limited way, power to extend time for the issue of a writ beyond the period of limitation. Subsection (1) of section 23 states:

“Notwithstanding subsection (1) of section 3 of this Decree where it is proved that the material facts relating to a cause of action were or included facts of a decisive character which were at all times outside the knowledge (actual or constructive) of the plaintiff until two years or more after the commencement of the three-year period relating to the cause of action, the plaintiff may . . . bring his action within twelve months after obtaining knowledge of such facts.”

The plaintiff cannot, however, bring such action without first obtaining the leave of the court on an ex parte application (see sections 13(2) and 24(1). Unlike our section 23(1) the power to grant leave to issue a writ under section 2D of the English Limitation Act. 1975, is very wide because it is stated in that section that:

“(1) If it appears to the Court it would be equitable to allow an action proceed having regard to the degree to which (a) the provisions of section 2A . . . of the Act prejudice the plaintiff . . . and (b) any decision of the Court under this subsection would prejudice the defendant . . . the Court may direct that those provisions shall not apply to the action . . .”

Section 2D (3) also listed those factors which the court should consider in deciding whether to let the time limit be overriden.

Section 3(1) of the Limitation Decree prescribes the time limit of three years within which an action claiming damages for negligence, nuisance or breach of duty shall be brought. It was observed by the Court of Appeal in Musah Nanyame v Alhaji Moro Awudu, Civil Appeal No 4/83 dated April 1984 that the statutory period was not fixed on any objective basis discoverable by logic, and sometimes occasions hardship and injustice. It is for those reasons that a more vibrant and beneficient provision on the lines of section 2D to replace our section 23(1) will be welcomed. After all the relevant provision of the English Limitation Act, 1963, which our section 23(1) adopted is now consigned to the limbo of legal history. Nanyame v Awudu (supra) decided that once the judge has granted leave ex parte to commence an action the defendant was not entitled to set it aside by a motion. The Court of Appeal explained that leave only enables the plaintiff to bring the action and disables the defendant from defeating it by the time bar created by statute. Any other point that can be raised in answer to the claim, including the fact that the plaintiff could not have been unaware of the material facts he alleges could still be raised during the subsequent hearing.

2. SUBSTITUTED SERVICE

Applications for substituted service are a common feature in the courts. The basis for the application (and for ordering it) is that for some reason, personal service cannot be conveniently effected. This inability to affect personal service (where at law there could have been personal service) is the essential consideration of which the court must be so satisfied. Unfortunately it is in this area of exercising discretion where our attitude has been rather lackadaisical. For very often we have allowed substituted service to take care of situations where the plaintiff's real difficulty was that he did not know where to effect personal service on the defendant. The reasons for the plaintiff’s inability to effect personal service on the defendant may be either because he did not know the defendant’s address or that he had left the jurisdiction before the issue of the writ. In either case (and especially if the defendant did not leave the jurisdiction to evade the service of any anticipated writ) substituted service cannot be ordered because as Taylor J (as he then was) said in Bawa v Oyegoke [1977] 2 GLR 412, “where a writ could not be served on a person directly, it could not be served indirectly by means of substituted service.” Bawa’s case also decided that a writ of summons with an insufficient or no address of the defendant is invalid and until amended it will be improper to order substituted service.

To avoid perfunctory for substituted service it is important for the judge to have a close look at the affidavit supporting the application.

These affidavits are wont to contain the statement that the defendant is evading service. Substituted service should not be ordered on such a mere statement without giving the grounds for the statement in the affidavit and also stating the serious efforts which have been made to find the defendant.

3. ORDERS ON PLEADINGS

If there is any matter in which the Court of Appeal is more readily disposed to show its disfavour, then it is the trial judge’s refusal to make such orders on pleadings as will, for instance, avoid multiplicity of actions and enable the real issue in controversy between the parties to be determined finally. Orders on pleadings may take the form of (a) amending pleadings; (b) striking out pleadings; (c) striking out claims; (d) striking out parties or joinder of parties.

(a) Amendment of Pleadings

Order 23, r. 1 enables the judge at any stage of the proceedings to allow either party to amend his indorsement or pleadings, in such manner and on such terms as may be just. The rule further provides that “all such amendments shall be made as may be necessary and for the purpose of determining the real questions in controversy between the parties.” It was explained in Mahama Hausa v Baako Hausa [1972] 2 GLR 469, CA, that this further provision is mandatory. It would seem, therefore, that where such amendment is necessary for the purpose of determining the real issue in controversy then the judge has no option in the matter but to allow the amendment. On other grounds for amendment the discretion still rests with the judge to grant or refuse it. Yeboa v Bofour [1971] 2 GLR 199, CA, which spells out the circumstances when the judge must refuse an amendment must, in my opinion, relate to applications brought on other grounds. By that decision the judge must as a general rule allow an amendment provided that: (i) no surprise results, (ii) it does not enable a party to set up an entirely new case or to change completely the nature of his case, (iii) it is not to add new parties, (iv) it will not do any injury to the opponent's case or prejudice him in some way which cannot be compensated for by costs or otherwise, (v) the application be made bona fide, and (vi) the proposed amendment will not cause undue delay or is irrelevant or useless.

We may here take note of Order 28, r 12 which confers another important power on the trial judge. That rule provides:

“12. The Court or a Judge may at any time, and on such terms as to costs . . . amend any defect or error in any proceedings and all necessary amendments shall be made for the purpose of determining the real question or issue raised by or depending on the proceedings.”

This rule empowers the court to order an amendment to be made of its own motion. The power, however, must be exercised rarely by the court for, as it has been said, the function of judge is to act as a kind of umpire who plays not an active but only a passive role in relation to the raising of the issues for the consideration and determination by the court. It is not the duty of the court to force upon the parties amendments for which they do not ask.  Nevertheless the power contained in the rule is valuable and significant. It enables the court, by persuasion and if necessary by order, to raise the real point at issue      between the parties, and to ensure that its proceedings are free from errors and defects. In Amakom Sawmill & Company v. Mansah [1963] 1 GLR 368, SC, the court pointed at the expediency of the trial judge resorting to Order 28, r 12 so as to meet the ends of justice by amending the figure claimed in the writ to coincide with the figure to which, in the judge’s view, the plaintiff was entitled. In that case the trial judge awarded less damages than he found for the plaintiff because he had claimed for the lesser amount. The Supreme Court pointed out that the trial judge ought to have employed, suo motu, the said rule to meet the situation.

(b) Striking out Pleadings and Striking out Claim or Defence

The rules empower the court or judge to order to be struck out or amended any matter in any indorsement or pleading which may be unnecessary or which may tend to prejudice, embarrass, or delay the fair trial of the action. Further, the court or judge may order any pleading to be struck out on the ground that it discloses no reasonable cause of action or answer, and in any such case or in case of the action or defence being shown by the pleadings to be frivolous or vexatious the court or a judge may order the action to be stayed or dismissed, or judgment to be entered accordingly as may be just—(Order 19, rule 29 and Order 25, rule 4).

Apart from the rules, the court has an inherent jurisdiction to stay or dismiss actions, and to strike out pleadings which are vexation or frivolous, or in any way an abuse of the process of the court, under which it could deal with all the cases included in these rules.

It should be noted that although the rule in respect of striking out the pleadings expressly states that the order may be made “at any stage of the proceedings,” the application should always be made promptly, and as a rule, before the close of pleadings. In Ghana Muslim’s Representative Council v Salifu [1975] 2 GLR 246, CA, the court warned that the jurisdiction of the court under Order 25, Rule 4 must be exercised with extreme caution and that a pleading would only be struck out where it was apparent that even if the facts were proved the plaintiff would not be entitled to the relief sought.

(c) Striking out and Adding Parties

It is provided under Order 16,. rule 11 that:

“. . . The Court or Judge may, at any stage of the proceedings, either upon or without the application of either party, and on such terms as may appear to the Court or a Judge to be just, order that the names of any parties improperly joined, whether as plaintiffs or as defendants, be struck out and that the names of any parties, whether plaintiffs or defendants, who ought to have been joined, or whose presence before the Court may be necessary in order to enable the Court effectively and completely to adjudicate upon and settle all the questions involved in the cause or matter, be added . . .”

The grand design of this rule is to bring all parties to the dispute relating to the subject-matter before the court at the same time so that the disputes may be determined without the delay, inconvenience and expense of separate actions and trials. To achieve this end the rule must be so construed and an amendment allowed.

In Ussher v Darko [1977] 1 GLR 476, CA, it was explained that “the jurisdiction of the court to join a party to an action to avoid a multiplicity of suits under Order 16, r 11 might be exercised at any stage of the proceedings so long as anything remained to be done in the action. It could be exercised even after an admission of liability by one or two possible defendants and even after judgment, though all that remained was the assessment of damages. Whether the application should be acceded to or denied, was a matter for the exercise of the trial judge's discretion and save that such discretion must be exercised judicially and in a manner conformable with justice, no fixed rules existed as to when or how it should be exercised. Such exercise must depend on the facts and special circumstances of each case.

4. DISCOVERY AND INSPECTION

Order 31 deals with the discovery and inspection of documents. By discovery of documents the court may grant leave to a party to see all material documents in the possession of his opponent, and to take copies of the more important ones. It may also order the production by any party to the action, upon oath, of such of the documents in his possession or power relating to any matter in question in the action, as the court or the judge shall think right: and the court may deal with documents when produced, in such manner as shall appear just. It may also order discovery by interrogatories. Failure to obey any order to answer interrogatories, or for discovery or production or inspection of documents attracts very severe sanctions. For under Order, 31 rule 21, the contumacious party shall be liable to attachment. He shall also, if a plaintiff be liable to have his action dismissed for want of prosecution, and, if a defendant to have his defence, if any, struck out and to be placed in the same position as if he had not defended, and the party interrogating may apply to the court or judge for an order to that effect, and an order may be made accordingly.

Let me illustrate how these powers have been used in two English cases. The first is Anton Piller KG v Manufacturing Processes Limited, The Times, 9 December 1975. There P, a German company manufacturing electric motors and generators employed M, an English company as their agents in the United Kingdom. P suspected that M were in secret communication with A’s rivals for the supply to them of P’s drawing and materials and other confidential information. P applied ex parte for (a) an injunction to restrain M from infringing the copyright in P’s drawings; and (b) an order to permit two authorised representatives of P to enter A’s works for the purpose of inspecting all documents relating to the design or manufacture of P’s equipment, and removing all original documents which P had supplied to M. The local judge granted the injunction but refused to make the order asked for. P appealed.

It was held (CA, Lord Denning MR, Ormrol and Shaw LJJ, 8 December 1975) that the appeal would be allowed and the order would be made, such an order would rarely be made and only when there was no alternative way of ensuring that justice was done to the applicant. There were three pre-conditions for making such an order:

(i) there must be an extremely strong prima facie case;

(ii) the damage, potential or actual, must be very serious for the applicant; and

(iii) there must be clear evidence that the other party had in his possession incriminating documents or things, and that there was a real possibility that he might destroy such material before any application inter partes could be made.

All the conditions were satisfied in the present case.

The next case is Harrington v Polytechnic of North London and Others, CA (Sir John Donaldson MR, Griffiths and Brown Wilkinson LJJ), 12 July 1984 (WLR). In this case the plaintiff, a student of the Polytechnic of North London, was prevented from attending lectures by a picket organised by other students opposed to the plaintiff’s political association with the National Front. He issued a writ claiming damages and injunctive relief against the polytechnic and another student P, and on his own behalf and as a representative of the students who had organised the picket. He obtained an interlocutory injunction restraining the second defendant and other students from interfering with his contractual right to attend lectures at the polytechnic. On 1 May 1984 in breach of the injunction, a picket prevented the plaintiff from entering the polytechnic. Photographs were taken of the persons involved in the picket. On 11 May, on the plaintiff's application, the trial judge ordered the polytechnic to disclose to the plaintiff’s solicitors the names and addresses of the twenty-two persons indicated with a cross on the photograph. The polytechnic’s appeal against the order of 11 May was heard and dismissed by the Court of Appeal on 14 May. In order to comply with the court order, lecturers of the polytechnic were instructed to examine the photographs and swear affidavits, either setting out the names of those persons whom they could identify or confirming that none was known. A number of lecturers refused to comply with the polytechnic’s instruction until they had obtained legal advice. On 21 May the judge ordered 14 named lecturers to identify the persons in the photographs. The 14 lecturers’ application to have the order of 21 May discharged was refused. The 14 lecturers appealed contending that the order of 21 May was made without jurisdiction.

Sir John Donaldson MR, delivering the judgment of the court, said that the lecturers relied on the “mere witness” rule in Norwich Pharmaceutical Company v Customs and Excise Commissioners [1974] AC 133. Their position was that they had not witnessed the events of 1 May and they were not concerned with those events. That was a superficially attractive argument but it could not withstand analysis. The plaintiff’s cause of action against the polytechnic was for discovery. It had already been determined by the Court of Appeal that the polytechnic was liable to give discovery and an order had been made which required the polytechnic to disclose all relevant information in its possession. For that purpose information in the hands and minds of its servants and agents was information in the possession of the polytechnic; Anderson v Bank of British Columbia (1876) 2 Ch.D 644. There was clear authority in Dummor v Chippenham Corporation (1807) 14 Ves Gun 245 that servants of a corporation could be required to give discovery on behalf of the corporation and were not to be regarded as mere witnesses. It was not an extension but the application of the same principle to order agents of a corporation to make similar disclosure. The court was satisfied that there was jurisdiction to make an order for disclosure against the 14 lecturers as agents of the polytechnic. The appeals were dismissed. Leave to appeal was refused.

5. ATTACHMENT OF DEBTS (Garnishee Proceedings)

Attachment of debts by garnishee proceedings is governed by Order 49. It was dealt with by Mr. Kom but I will add to his contribution because of its importance as a convenient form of enforcement.

Availability

Where a judgment-creditor discovers that any debt is owed or accrued owing to the judgment-debtor from any other person within the jurisdiction, he may apply to the court for an order requiring that person to pay to him (the judgment-creditor) as so much of the debt as may be sufficient to satisfy the judgement as well as the costs of the garnishee proceedings.  The court’s power to smake an order of this kind is discretionary, and since a garnishee order is basically an equitable method of enforcing a judgment, the discretion will be exercised on a careful judicial basis so that, for example. no order would be made if its effect would be to prefer one creditor against another in a bankruptcy situation. It is important to note that the debt sought to be attached must be actually due to the debtor from the garnishee, though it is irrelevant whether the time of payment has already matured or is in the future: O’Driscoll v Manchester Insurance Committee [1915] 3 KB 499, CA. There is a substantial body of law on the attachability of various classes of debt of which bank current account is the epitome. There is an important area where this procedure is excluded and it is that it cannot be used to enforce payment against the state.

Application

The application for a garnishee order nisi (like divorce, the procedure falls into two sections, the first being in the nature of an emergency freezing of the funds sought to be attached, and the second the substantive determination of the issue) is made ex parte on affidavit. The affidavit may be made by the judgment-creditor himself or his solicitor. The order nisi must in practice be limited to such sum as will (whilst also allowing reasonable costs of the garnishee proceedings) suffice to cover the judgment debt and costs (if awarded), thus avoiding any residue of the judgment-debtor’s bank account being bound by the order. It will be seen that, especially in the case of a limited liability company judgment-debtor, this can be most important since otherwise the finances of the company would be paralysed.

Order nisi

The order nisi must be served personally on the garnishee at least seven days before the date of the hearing. The order also has to be served on the judgment debtor (unless otherwise ordered by the court) so that he has an opportunity to object to the order being made absolute. Until the order is served on him the garnishee is totally unaffected by the order nisi, this being a logical consequence of the proceedings being equitable in nature: Re Standhope Silkstone Collieries Company (1879) 11 ChD 160, C.A. It is important to keep the application as secret as possible so that the judgment-debtor is given no opportunity to withdraw his funds (from a bank) or secure payment (other cases) since once the garnishee has bona fide paid out the debt to the judgment-debtor the attachment would be inoperative in account of there being nothing for the order to operate on. In bank cases the best procedure is to serve the bank not only at its registered office but also at the branch where the account in question is thought to be judgment-debtor withdrawing his funds. Service of the order on the judgment-debtor can be attended to later.

Hearing

Should the garnishee not attend at the appointment for the further consideration of the case, or if he does not dispute the debt claimed to be due from him, the court may convert the order nisi into an order absolute. If the debt is disputed by the garnishee, the question in issue may be summarily determined. or the court may order that there be a trial of that matter.

Order Absolute

The order absolute, orders the garnishee to pay the requisite sum to the judgment-creditor. Any receipt of payment of the sum referred to in such an order is a good discharge from the debt as between the judgment debtor and the garnishee. The costs occasioned by garnishee proceedings are normally recovered by the judgment creditor out of the debt attached by the order and in priority to the judgment debt. A garnishee who arrears even if he admits the debt due normally also recovers his costs. There is, in any event, a wide discretion in the court as to costs.
