BAIL IN MURDER CASES [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 252—259

DANKWA E. V. O

 

INTRODUCTION

AN application by the chief state attorney for the withdrawal of bail which had been granted the accused persons in Republic v Crentsil1 gave Justice F.Y. Kpegah, sitting in a Cape Coast High Court, the opportunity to state when bail can be granted in murder cases. A consideration of the issue is necessary because section 2 of the Criminal Procedure Code (Amendment) Decree, 1975 (NRCD 309)2 (hereinafter referred to as section 96 (7) (a)) introduced a new provision which appears, on first reading, to prohibit the grant of bail in murder cases. It provides that:

"A Court shall refuse to grant bail:

(a) in a case of treason, subversion, murder, robbery, hijacking, piracy or escape from lawful custody.”3

It should be pointed out from the outset that section 96(7)(a) cannot constitute a complete ouster of the jurisdiction of the courts to grant bail in a case of murder. To illustrate, A is charged with murder because he was the enemy of the deceased or because A had once said that the deceased would die one day. By no stretch of the imagination can it be said that the lawmakers intended that bail should be refused to a person charged with murder on the facts of the illustration. The facts simply do not support a case of murder. Varying the facts of the illustration, A is charged with murder but the prosecution gives no evidence to support the charge. By parity of reasoning, section 96 (7) (a) does not oust the jurisdiction of the court to grant bail on the basis of the varied facts. There must be some evidence on which a court can say, "this is a case of murder." The mere fact that the prosecution has preferred a charge of murder against a person does not make the case necessarily one of murder.4 If, despite the charge, the evidence does not establish that the case is one of murder, section 96 (7) (a) does not prevent the court from granting bail to the accused person. The difficulty is with determining the kind or nature of evidence which would make a case one of murder.

The Scope of Section 96 (7) (a)

Before examining the scope of section 96 (7) (a), the facts of Republic v Kwame Crentsil and Okoe Awulley should be stated. The accused persons were arraigned before a district court on a charge of murder. No facts were given in support of the charge, apparently because the prosecution had not completed their investigation. The district court did not grant bail to the accused persons, and remanded them into police custody. Justice Kpegah cannot be faulted for granting bail to the accused persons because there was no evidence to guide him in deciding whether or not it was a case of murder. Subsequently, the prosecution had evidence which led them to apply for the bail to be rescinded. The new evidence was that Kwame Crentsil found his sister (the deceased) in a pool of blood in her mother's house. Crentsil who lived in another house, carried the sister to Okoe Awulley's house. The latter, on seeing the sister, pronounced her dead. Blood stains, which on forensic analysis, matched the blood group of the deceased were found on the wall and door of Crentsil's room. That room was painted the very day on which the sister died. In the view of the prosecution, the accused person could be indicted on this evidence and bail should, therefore, be rescinded.

Resisting the application for withdrawal of bail, the defence argued that bail could be withdrawn only if (a) the condition attached to it were violated or (b) the terms of the bail were not honoured. The court rightly rejected this ground as the sole basis for withdrawal of bail.

Bail could also be withdrawn, the court ruled, when evidence supporting a case of murder is subsequently presented to the court.5 The court stated farther, as Aboagye J had held in Prah v The Republic,6 that for section 96 (7) (a) to operate, a murder charge must be properly laid. Such a charge is so laid if, on an objective assessment 7 of the evidence of the prosecution, a prima facie case of murder can be established. This result is achieved if the evidence is capable of "proving the essential elements of the charge.8 Justice Kpegah ruled that, in the instant case, that standard had not been attained. He saw the crucial evidence of the prosecution in the following light:

"The mere presence of the blood stain in the room of the accused is not enough. The presence of the blood of the deceased in his dress could be explained by the fact of his carrying the body to seek help. The presence of the blood in the room of the accused could also be explained in a similar vein. The accused said his room was painted before the incident. There is no evidence to the contrary. Even if done after the discovery of the deceased's body, it could be a panicky action. I do not think the evidence made available and upon which the accused is being charged takes the case out of the realm of suspicion or mere conjecture.”9

Kpegah J could have rested his ruling on these observations. He had been asked to discharge the bail he had previously granted because the evidence did not justify the continuation of the bail. But in his view, the evidence did not show that he was confronted with a case of murder, and accordingly he rejected the application for the withdrawal of bail. His task should, therefore, have been at an end. However, the learned judge went on to make disturbing comments on why he concluded that the evidence did not raise a prima facie case of murder. Relatedly, he examined closely and accurately the nature or quality of evidence which (1) should warrant the committal of an accused person to stand trial for murder, and (2) lead a trial judge, at the end of the case for the prosecution, to grant bail because there has been no evidence to prove an essential element in the alleged offence, or because the case for the prosecution has been so discredited upon cross-examination that no reasonable jury would act upon it. In showing, as their lordships Taylor, Korsah and Aboagye10 had demonstrated on earlier occasions, that section 96 (7) (a) does not oust completely the jurisdiction of the courts to grant bail in a case of murder, Kpegah J's ruling in the instant case strengthens our understanding of this legislation. But he gives the unfortunate impression, in reviewing the evidence in the instant case, that the quality of evidence before either the bail judge or the committing magistrate is the same as that before the trial judge.10a

The Bail Judge and the Trial Judge

In an important respect, a committing magistrate and a pre-trial judge hearing an application for bail, in a case of murder, perform the same function as a trial judge deciding on whether or not a prima facie case has been made by the prosecution.  They all ask themselves whether there is evidence on all the ingredients of the offence.11 The committing magistrate and the pre-trial judge on the one hand, and the trial judge on the other have different kinds of evidence before them. The evidence before the former has not been subjected to cross-examination. Their duty is to assume that the case for the prosecution is true and then decide, on that assumption, if there is evidence on all the ingredients of the offence.12 They are not in a position either to believe or disbelieve the evidence of the prosecution. A lower degree of evidence will, therefore, pass the pre trial bail stage than that required to uphold a submission of no case at the close of the case for the prosecution.

Proof of the evidence against the accused person takes place at the trial stage. The jury which decides on the guilt or non-guilt of the accused person, or the judge who decides to turn over the case to the jury is confronted with, in a sense, a higher level of evidence. The evidence before them has been subjected to the rigour of cross-examination. Although throughout the ruling Kpegah J shows awareness of his role as a bar judge, as distinct from a trial judge, in parts of his review of the evidence in the instant case, we get the feeling that he is acting as a trial judge. Witness the following lines:

"I do not think these pieces of evidence can be said to be decisive, . . .The evidence as can be seen is clearly circumstantial. The degree of certainty required in such cases before conviction is very high indeed."13

It is also doubtful whether the committing magistrate or bail judge should respectively discharge an accused person or grant bail because some interpretation of the summary of the evidence is consistent with innocence. Kpegah J has no such doubt:

"Some of these cases I have referred to may be dealing with situations of no case to answer after close of prosecution's case. I do not see anything wrong with applying the same test when one is perusing the summary of evidence. If on their own admitted in the summary of evidence, the evidence is widely opened to several interpretations, some of which are consistent with innocence, a decision to commit will be bad so also decision not to grant bail.”

The concern is that this approach would seem to require more than the test whether the evidence supports the ingredients of the offence.

It involves weighing and balancing the evidence in favour of the prosecution and that for the defence. This role is best performed by the trial judge either at the end of (1) the case for the prosecution or (2) the whole case. In our view, the committing magistrate or bail judge should limit himself to the question whether the ingredients of the offence can be supported by the summary of evidence of the prosecution. A positive answer should lead to committal or refusal of bail. It is not being suggested that a bail judge cannot comment on the nature or quality of the evidence. Circumstantial evidence may be so referred to. But, as has been suggested earlier on, he has to assume that the evidence of the prosecution is true, and on that basis decide if it supports a case of murder.14 If it does, bail should be refused. The bail judge should not decide the weight which should be attached to circumstantial evidence.

Against the background of section 96 (7) (a), the temptation of a bail judge to intrude into the arena of the trial judge is understandable. Our national motto is “Freedom and Justice,” and it is commendable for Kpegah J to be “sensitive to the right of the individual to his liberty”15 Because a person may be deprived of his liberty for a long time through denial of bail in a murder case only to be found not guilty when tried, there is the understandable concern that such deprivation should take place only on the strongest of evidence. The danger in this approach is that the bail judge may prejudge the case: if bail is granted, then the case is not one of murder, and the accused person should be set free. Respect for the administration of justice will suffer if, after deciding that a case is not one of murder., a court of concurrent jurisdiction decides later on, on the basis of the same evidence, that it is a case of murder. This may happen when the prosecution, convinced of the strength of its case sets the trial process in motion after bail has been granted to the accused. While concerned about the liberty of the individual, the perspectives of the prosecutor, investigating officers (police) and the lawmaker should nevertheless be sketched. It is not easy to apprehend offenders, and the bail process should not be an escape route for offenders.

Murder is a serious offence, and the offender should be made to feel the enormity of the offence. Denial of bail is one mode of achieving this result. The police will be demoralised if their hard work is brought to naught through overly-stringent requirement by committing magistrates and bail judges.

Section 96 (7) (a) brings into sharp focus the damage in committing an accused person for trial on the basis of only a scintilla of evidence. The summary of the prosecution's evidence may support a case of murder in terms of section 96(7)(a), but it will not necessarily ensure conviction because such evidence may be so discredited at the trial that it will not meet the standard for conviction. As we have seen16 to support a case of murder there must be evidence on the essential ingredients of the offence. But evidence on only one ingredient (out of other ingredients) will amount to a scintilla of evidence. There cannot, and should never, be conviction on such evidence. Accordingly, there is no  justification either to charge, hold in custody, commit to trial, or try a person on the basis of a scintilla of evidence. We, therefore, agree with Kpegah J that State v Bisa17 which requires committal on the ground of a scintilla of evidence should not be followed.

CONCLUSION

Section 96 (7) (a) of the Criminal Procedure Code does not oust the jurisdiction of the court to grant bail simply because the applicant for bail has been charged with murder. A court must decide, on the basis of the evidence before it, whether it is a case of murder. If it is, the court is bereft of power to grant bail. Korsah J captured the substance of this statutory provision in Seidu v The Republic when he said:

“ .  .  . the legislature is ordering this court to refuse bail in all cases where there is evidence . . . in support of the accusation of murder, even if the Court is of the opinion that the accused will appear to stand his trial when released on bail.”18

Bail, which is granted because the available evidence does not support a case of murder, should be withdrawn if additional evidence supporting a case of murder is subsequently brought before it. In the case of a committing magistrate, the accused should be discharged in accordance with section 184 (5) of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960,19 if the evidence cannot support a case of murder. Evidence supports a case of murder if the former is consistent with the definition of the offence under our Criminal Code. A scintilla of evidence cannot pass this test: something far more than that is required to support the offence. The test of evidence which supports the charge is in substance the approach adopted in recent cases,20 and by Justice A.N.E. Amissah.21 It has been restated with commendation by Kpegah J in Republic v Crentsil.

It is important to limit those who are denied their freedom before their guilt or non-guilt is determined. Since the characterisation of the offence as murder will result in the deprivation of the liberty of an individual who has not been proved guilty, and who may eventually be found not guilty, there may be the tendency for judges to require a higher degree of evidence before refusal of bail than if there were not a total ouster of the court's jurisdiction to grant bail. Bail Judges in such circumstances may unconsciously assume that they are deciding on the guilt or non-guilt of the accused, and in the process require more stringent evidence than that warranted at the committal or bail stage.

The difficulty and possible dilemma which face the court in deciding when a case is one of murder can be done away with through a process which takes account of both the liberty of the individual and the protection of society from the serious crimes specified under section 96 (7) of the Criminal Procedure Code. This provision should be repealed. The concerns which dictate refusal of bail and the factors which are to be taken into account in such refusal, as stated in section 96 (5) and (6) of the Criminal Procedure Code should be met if those two subsections are applied to all crimes. We share the sentiments expressed by Aboagye J in Prah v The Republic that:

“I must take this opportunity to advocate the amendment of section 96 (7) (a) of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30), since it can be used to cause untold hardship to innocent persons who are merely suspected of having committed murder. It normally takes not less than one year for a person arrested for murder to be tried and great injustice would be caused if an innocent person arrested for no reasonable cause is to be kept in custody only to be told later that he is innocent. The High Court must be given a discretion to grant bail in all cases when circumstances justify the granting of bail."22
