MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: RE-EXAMINATION AND ADDRESSES [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 105—110

by Sam Okudzeto

 

ON the conclusion of his cross-examination a witness may be re-examined on behalf of the party for whom he has given evidence-in- chief, for the purpose of explaining any part of his evidence given in cross-examination which is capable of being construed unfavourably to his own side. (See section 179 of Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323) and Halshury’s Laws of England (4th ed), Vol 17, para 280). The following limitations however exist: (a) It can only cover issues raised in cross-examination and therefore no questions may be asked which introduce wholly new matters. For example, (i) in the Queen’s Case (1820) 2 Brod & Bing 284) where a witness cross-examined as to whether he had not stated that he was to be the witness for the prosecution. In re-examination the witness can only be asked what induced him to make the statement, (ii) a witness, admitting in cross-examination a conversation with defendant, may be re-examined as to the whole of the conversation but not if he denies that he had any such conversation at all. See Dicas v Lord Brougham (1833) 6 C & P 249, (iii) if cross-examined as to a particular statement only, witness may not be re-examined on other statements in some conversation unconnected with the one spoken to. See Prince v Samo (1838) 7 Ad & El 627. A new matter can only be introduced in re-examination with the leave of the court in which case cross-examination on the new matter will be given. (b) Re-examination unlike cross-examination must not be leading. See Ireland v Taylor [1949] 1 KB 300 at 313, CA. (c) It cannot be used to elicit evidence which should have been dealt with in examination-in-chief. It is also worthy to note that where questions asked in cross examination let in evidence which would not have been admissible in chief, the witness may be re-examined upon it. (Blewett v Tregonning (1835) 5 Nev & MKB 308; Greville v Chapman (1844) 5 QBD 713). (d) Section 73(2) of the Evidence Decree, 1975, states further that a witness cannot be re-examined or otherwise further re-examined as to the same matter raised by the examining party on previous examination without leave granted by the court in its discretion.

The court has much discretion with regard to the conduct of a case. Section 69 of the Evidence Decree states that the court shall exercise reasonable control over the mode and order of interrogating witnesses and presenting evidence. Also section 73(i) subjects such matters to this discretion of the court. The judges therefore have to determine the scope of questions that can be put in re-examination.

The fundamental principle or real purpose of re-examination is to enable a witness to explain or expand upon what he said in evidence in cross-examination. Richard Ducann QC in his book on the Art or the Advocate, states: “If the witness has been badly mauled by the cross-examination then the re-examination can attempt to put Humpty-Dumpty together again.” It is also important to keep in mind that re-examination only arises where a witness has been cross-examined, where therefore cross-examination is declined then no re-examination can take place. Ducann therefore advises counsel not to ask any questions in cross-examination if it is realised that the examining advocate has forgotten to ask about something or has failed to get the answer he wants. If he does ask a question then he allows his opponent a second bite at the cherry. While considering the question of re-examination, in the process of the trial, i.e. examination-in-chief, cross-examination to be followed by re-examination we should note that the re-examination is not necessarily the final drama in the act.  There is the power of the judge to question or call witnesses. This topic was not implicitly included in my address nevertheless I believe, to complete the circle, I ought to touch on it. The judge has a discretionary power to ask a witness any question which he considers necessary. The judge must not however take it upon himself to conduct the examination or cross-examination of a witness. Some decided cases in the UK impose limits on permissible, judicial intervention—Jones v National Coal Board [1957] 2 QB 55: [1957] 2 All ER 155, CA and R v Bateman (1946) 31 Cr App R 106, CCA.

In a criminal case the judge has power to call a witness not called by either the prosecution or the defence without their consent. if he considers that course as necessary in the interest of justice. (See R v Liddle (1928) 21 Cr App R 3, CCA. R v Me Maton (1933) 24 Cr App R 95, CCA), but a judge should not call such a witness after the evidence for the defence is closed, except in a matter arising ex improvise which no human ingenuity could foresee. (See R v Harris [1927] 2 K.B 587, CCA); and only where no injustice or prejudice could be caused to the defendant. (See R v Cleghorn [1967] 2 QB 54- (1967] 1 All ER 996; CA, applying R v Harris [1927] 2 KB 587, CCA.

A judge can also at any time recall a witness who has already given evidence to ask him further questions. Such a witness cannot be cross-examined except with the leave of the judge. (See R v Sulliavan [1923] 1 KB 47, CCA; Fallon v Calvert [1960] 2 QB 201; [1960] 1 All ER 281, CA.)

In civil cases a judge may call a witness to give evidence with the consent of the parties or in the absence of objection. The re-examination in most cases is the last stage before the final speeches or addresses begin. Human imperfection however demands a modification of this rule and in some instances further evidence can be called by the plaintiff or prosecutor in rebuttal. But this may only be done where they have been taken by surprise; where something has arisen in the evidence given by the defence which “no human ingenuity could foresee.” A clear example arose in 1876 where Mr. Hollis a manufacturer of tar and sulphate of ammonia with premises in London was alleged by one Mr. Bigsby, a manufacturer of varnish (Burnswick and Japan Black) that Mr. Hollis’ factory emitted sulphuretted hydrogen which injured the health of himself, his family and the plants in his garden and he sought an injunction to prevent Hollis from continuing to poison the local atmosphere. No suggestion was made to Bigsby in cross-examination that he too emitted sulphuretted hydrogen from his work, but right at the end of the evidence was called for the defendant, an expert who gave evidence that the manufactured of Brunswick Black and Japan Black mineral asphaltum also produced sulphuretted hydrogen. This witness even performed an experiment in court to prove his point which nearly asphyxiated the judge. Mr. Bigsby, who, knowing he used vegetable asphaltum which gave no vapur at all and no mineral asphaltum promptly applied to call evidence in rebuttal. The judge refused the application and also refused  to grant an injunction, saying that not only was Mr. Bigsby responsible for his own misfortune but that he sought to conceal the truth from the court.

Mr. Bigsby appealed to the Court of Appeal which reversed the decision after three days of argument. The court reversed the decision and stated:

“At no period of a cause is it too late to show that confusion and error have arisen from two persons or two things passing by the same name and more especially to show that through such confusion the court had been deceived by a misleading experiment performed in its presence.”

It is my submission that counsel for the defendant contributed to the confusion by failing to cross-examine Mr. Bigsby properly. If he had suggested to Mr. Bigsby that he also produced sulphuretted hydrogen an explanation would have been offered which could have cleared the confusion and therefore evidence in rebuttal could have been avoided.

Most of the blunder in these cases are the fault of lawyers who inadequately prepare their cases, do not study the technical issues of fact involved and, at times, the situation is compounded by hasty and impatient judges who think that lawyers are prone to waste their time. Lack of much exposure of both counsel and judges of course contribute to lack of understanding in most factual errors of this kind.

ADDRESSES

At the close of the case for the defence or where the defence declines to call evidence the procedure is as follows:

(i) Where the defence declines to call evidence then the party beginning will sum up the evidence given by his witnesses and then comment upon it.

(ii) Where the defence calls evidence, however, he will loose the right to have the final word and will then be called upon to address the court. The address is also referred to as “the final speech.” The party beginning will then have the last word or address the court last. The procedure in criminal cases is contained in sections 273 and 274 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30).

(iii) Where the accused is undefended by counsel and states that he does not intend to call any witness as to the facts except himself then he is permitted to address the court if he so desires after making his statement or giving evidence or, when he calls witnesses, then, he sums up his case after calling his witnesses, and counsel for the prosecution will then reply. When the accused is defended by counsel and states that no witness as to the facts will be called except the accused, then at the close of the accused’s statement or evidence, counsel for the prosecution may address the court and counsel for the defence may reply and then call his witness (if any) as to the character of the accused. Where, however, witnesses are called for the defence then counsel for the defence must first address the court and the prosecution will reply.

SUMMARY TRIAL

In a summary trial the prosecutor or his advocate shall be entitled to address the court at the commencement of his case and where the accused has called any witness, other than a witness as to his general character also at the conclusion of the case for the defence.

The accused or his advocate shall be entitled to address the court at the commencement or in conclusion of his case if he thinks fit. (See section 175 of Act 30). The court may grant leave for the accused or his advocate to address the court in reply to evidence adduced by the prosecutor in reply. .

What is the purpose of an address? Address at the beginning of the case is meant to give a summary of the case for either side and give an indication of the mode by which it is to be proved. This procedure of addressing the court at the commencement of the case both for the plaintiff and the defendant is meant to enable the judge and, or, judge and jury where there is one, to be appraised of the issue and have a clear idea of what to expect from the witnesses expected to establish the facts. Unfortunately, this practice is not being continued in our courts and most cases are begun without the judge appreciating what facts to expect and some do nothing more than merely record what transpires in court. The final address is meant to summarise the case for each side, ie the facts but also the law that supports the propositions of the issues set down for trial. Indeed if judges can give guidance the legal issues can be narrowed down and the addresses can aid the judge in giving judgment.

In eighteenth century Britain, counsel who could appeal to the sentiment of the jury had always succeeded in swaying them to their side Lord Cherstefield in a letter to his son in 1746 lead this to say:

“Wherever you would persuade or prevail, address yourself to the passions, it is by them that mankind is to be taken. I bid you can once engage people’s pride, love, pity, ambition, or whichever is their reason can do against you.”

This practice, of course, may not be applicable in present-day England nor in Ghana of today. The important issue, however, is that some cases involve technical issues of fact or even at times technical legal issues in some aspects of law of which the judge may not be very conversant and it is the responsibility of counsel, and I would add that the judge ought to encourage counsel to research and address the court on such issues, then justice can truly be seen to be done.

At the moment because of shortage of materials, ie books dealing with the various specialised fields of law and the lazy latitude of most counsel who are in a hurry to make money, the judges are not being aided by addresses. The practice has also recently developed of judges requesting counsel to submit written addresses. The misfortune of the mode, even though it could ease the burden of recording the address to the judge, does not conform to the rule of the order of addresses. This is because in most instances both addresses are submitted at the same time and therefore the party who is to have the last word, because he is not served with the address of his opponent cannot reply to it. It is also pertinent to mention that an advocate as the term goes is one who speaks for his client and many counsel may be good on their feet and not necessarily in putting those same thoughts in a written form. Perhaps a balance need be drawn in this regard.
