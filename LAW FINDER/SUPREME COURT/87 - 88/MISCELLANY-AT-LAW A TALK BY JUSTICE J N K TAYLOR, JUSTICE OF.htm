MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: A TALK BY JUSTICE J N K TAYLOR, JUSTICE OF THE SUPREME COURT ON THE DILEMMAS OF LAW AND LAWYERS IN CONTEMPORARY GHANA

 

MEMBERS of the Ghana Bar, invited guests, ladies and gentlemen, I appear here by accident.

The Chief Justice, the Honourable Justice Sowah to whom you sent your invitation was unavoidably unable to attend and the statutory official who deputises for him whenever he is unavailable, the Honourable Justice Adade is, I am told, equally in no position to be here and so the Chief Justice has asked me to come as a substitute. I welcome this intervention of providence and I agree to come if you have no objection, for I understand  whoever attends has to give a talk on a topic of his own choice. This means that you primarily select your speakers; however, I am informed the Bar does not object to listening to any speech from me. I am sincerely grateful for the privilege of chatting with you in this manner.

I have listened carefully to the speech of the President of the Ghana Bar Association. In particular I take note of the information he says the Bar is in possession of, concerning corruption in the judiciary. I am sure as a responsible organisation the Bar has made this information available to the Chief Justice. Unfortunately the Chief Justice has not briefed me, and I have been taken unawares. In the circumstance I fear I am in no position to respond to the charges made by  your President.

I have considered the matter of a suitable topic for over a week but I was beset by so many real or probable imaginary difficulties that to reflect my dilemma and the embarrassment in which I found myself I settled on the “dilemmas of law and lawyers in contemporary Ghana” as the subject-matter of my talk. When I speak of lawyers in this connection I include judges and magistrates who have had professional training in the discipline that is “law.”

I wish to commence my talk by pointing out that in Ghana there are many misconceptions and differing philosophies about the true purpose and function of law and a lot of suspicion about the attitudes and motives of lawyers in the performance of their professional duties and indeed such utter misapprehension about the role of the executive authorities in their interaction with law and lawyers, that I believe it is the duty of all who have any pretensions as lovers of law and order and who spurn chaos and anarchy in our society to mount a campaign to clear the confusion and reinstate law and the rule of law as the dominant and desirable factors in our society. This we must do if we aspire to build up a progressive and prosperous country.

With this introduction I propose to discuss first the dilemmas of the law before I round up the topic with my views on the dilemmas of lawyers in present-day Ghana; and in this exercise I do not propose to ignore the dilemmas facing those searching for a new national identity by the aid of the much publicised “on going revolution.”

THE DILEMMAS OF THE LAW

In considering the dilemmas of the law, I feel quite happy that this is a gathering of professional lawyers. Every member of the bar present here is a professional and thus undoubtedly very much learned in the law, and I am consequently saved the needless task of defining what law is and the traditional role of lawyers. I apprehend therefore I can talk about law and assume without any qualms whatsoever that we are not at cross-purposes and that as lawyers we are operating on a wave length at the same meter band. In the circumstances you will understand me when I say that, it is because the traditional role of lawyers as we have understood it in this country for over one hundred  years has suffered reverses that I talk of the dilemmas of the law and lawyers.

 

Some preliminary observations may serve to highlight the nature of the dilemmas I have in mind. Contemporary Ghana is in a state of political flux. A flux that an articulate segment of our society has dubbed revolution. In my view, however, law does not operate in a vacuum and for us as professional lawyers the nature and content of the revolution must be appraised before we can function effectively.

Now every medical practitioner will admit that before a cure can be proffered for a sick person by a qualified doctor the nature of the ailment must be diagnosed and accurately identified. Without this initial exercise no doctor faithful to his calling will attempt to prescribe a remedy. So in my view is the alleged fate of law in contemporary Ghana. Our traditional law is supposed to be sick and we must diagnose its ailment. if it is indeed sick.

Before the British imperial power departed from our shores on 6 March 1957, they left us a concept of law as a mechanism for avoiding the capricious exercise of political and other powers. Law as we have known it for over hundred years guarantees individual right. Anglo-Ghanaian law has always been “proscriptive”. From colonial days until new and ill-defined concepts seeped into our law, our legal attitude has been dominated by the concept that anything not proscribed by statute and not forbidden by the common law and our customary jurisprudence is permitted. A different and to the Ghanaian lawyer a novel approach to law, described by Mr. Justice Apaloo as “socialist legality”1 has been gradually feeling its way into our law a few years before, and ever since the military coup syndrome permeated our society over twenty years ago. It had its uneasy beginnings at the commencement of the Nkrumah brand of Government and attained its apogee in the fervent days of the shortest lived military regime in the kaleidoscopic annals of our history. The lawyers have grudgingly frowned on it. What is this new bride called “socialist legality” which we have been wooing? She would seem to be indigenous in many of the socialist states of the world where “the party” qua party is supreme. William E Odom, a Lieutenant-General of the US Army, hinting obliquely at this socialist creature articulates its legal position in the USSR, the leading socialist country of the world in these words:

“The Party has always been above the law.  In fact one recent Western Study of the role of law in the Soviet Union concludes that law primarily serves to help the party to discipline society, to get society to do what the party dictates. The party however is not constrained by law if it chooses not to be.”2

This view of law is perhaps slightly exaggerated, but not much, for clearly socialist approach takes prescription as its syllogistic premise. However the one undoubted feature of this menacing state apparatus which appeals to poor and undeveloped peoples is that it is in theory ostensibly operated to advance the interests of as large a membership of the unfortunate and allegedly deprived segment of the population as is possible to reach and any hardship as conflicts with its rigid application is pragmatically ignored. Its unrelenting dogma is that only what the law dictates is permitted; all else is as a general rule forbidden particularly if it conflicts with undefined and therefore unarticulated but pervasive party policy. This deeply embeded prescriptive spirit of law is reflected by Anton Chekhov in his story of  “Sergeant Prishibeyev.” The retired sergeant was explaining at his own trial how he dispersed a crowd of villagers. He said:

“By what full right have the people gathered here? I ask ‘Why’? Is it said in the law that people should gather like herds of horses? Where is it written in the law that people are given their will.”

In a jurisdiction where there are no political parties or where the Soviet brand of party prevails this socialist philosophy will equate “Government” with “Party” in Soviet nomenclature and the policy or will of government, even if not expressed in legislation becoming a binding measure for all the citizens. By the standards of the common law the whole concept becomes invidious in cases where the machinery for identifying, the alleged binding policy of the party is ephemeral and amorphous and not subject or amenable to any known process by which it can be discovered before hand. Of course if the policy can be isolated a priori before application this attitude and approach to law will not be objectionable except that it imposes a totalitarian oligarchy on the population.

I do not know whether our history and our customary norms will tolerate and accept with equanimity this alien concept of law. It seems though that all our past constitutional experiments have repudiated it and that every military government without a single exception that has exercised power in this unfortunate Republic of ours has as early as is convenient and practicable set in train pragmatic courses of action calculated to repudiate this totalitarian notion of legality. The future course of law with the rule of law as we have practised it may very well be endemic in our traditional idea of law although only time, and time only will tell.

I have touched on this socialist approach because in periods of acute revolutionary fervour we have not been spared doses of this socialist philosophy.  In this respect, I recall that on 14 March 1983 I gave a talk to the law students of the University of Ghana in a symposium they had organised. In my attempt to instil in the youngmen, intellectual awareness of the virtue of truth and the desirability of discussing national and legal issues in a spirit void of any fear or myopic and narrow-minded considerations, I told them something admittedly critical of the then very popular concept of what was then and even now fashionably called kalabule. I think every Ghanaian lawyer is not unaware of what kalabule is supposed to connote in Ghana. I have my notes of what I told the said university students and I repeat it here for you to appreciate my point. I  said:

“If a person buys something at say ¢100 and sells it at ¢200 when legally the controlled price is ¢150, then the man is virtually a criminal engaged in unlawful trade. If however there is no controlled price, I can see no reason why he should be described as a kalabule person . . . when there is no controlled price, market forces dictate price and calling sellers in such circumstances kalabule people when you have not given them controlled price is meaningless for such alleged kalabule people are obviously traders who buy goods and faithful to the demands of economic laws governing supply and demand try to obtain the highest possible market price for the goods. This has been the practice for thousands of years since men began to trade and yet these so-called kalabule people were alleged to be our principal enemies and effective hindrance to our enjoyment of our well-earned economic prosperity.”

This view of mine which I honestly and candidly expressed and which is in consonance with the common law philosophy of positive law, was subjected to trenchant and vehement attack by our mass-media which accused me of being a champion of kalabule. To this day I am not aware of any logical explanation or traditional legal reason that can conceivably answer the point which I made about kalabule. In spite of that the charge against me was brazenly reiterated by no less a person than a legal practitioner who had joined the new brand of revolutionaries who are more revolutionary than the orthodox revolutionaries and who have been plying the corridors of power in our country for some time now. In an issue of the United Kingdom Magazine West Africa published at the time, the lawyer called me an enemy of the people. He suggested that in view of the opinion I thus expressed about kalabule, I am an unfit person to interpret the laws of the PNDC in my capacity as a Supreme Court judge. Such an attitude must inhibit genuine expression of views by persons who lack the courage to face the emotional reaction of a not well-informed executive.

The English language that I use now, not a bad relic of colonialism, is not an instrument of mathematical precision by which we can measure the substance of our thoughts and quantitatively and qualitatively convey that substance to others, nor is it a language whose nuances can be completely mastered even by the English, let alone those of us living outside the British Isles with a heritage of the rich languages of our proud ancestors. If you rule out disgusting sycophancy and a dishonest motive to appear more loyal to government than any other citizen then the shortcomings of the English medium of communication and a possible incompetent appreciation of the precise meaning of difficult expressions may be a probable explanation of the attack on me and my views.

I have given you this graphic example in order to project into bold relief, the dilemma that faces the law and lawyers in our country. The law and lawyers are at cross-roads. Are we to abandon the jurisprudence of our traditional common law and our own indigenous customary philosophy of law and admit as a substitute the new socialist philosophy of law which like the king’s new clothes is receiving much praise in the mass-media and among a significant segment of the legal fraternity? I suspect that the socialist legality I have discussed here is the premise that founds the jurisdiction and the endeavours of the new comers to our judicial scene the ubiquitous public tribunals said to run alongside the traditional courts on a course so geometrically parallel that as of now the twain never mix. The Ghana Bar Association in resolutions it passed at one of its annual conferences has I understand repudiated the public tribunal’s system in as clear a language as it is capable of mustering for the purpose and its members have been enjoined by the association not to patronise the public tribunals. This injunction which your association has placed on the public tribunals is, I am told not as effective as the association would wish. Some of your members have shown an inclination to repudiate this edict of the Bar Association. They espouse client interests at the tribunals and have thus in accordance with my thesis demonstrated the dilemma of the legal profession by exposing the schism in your ranks to the delight of lawyer and non-lawyer exponents of socialist legality and the embarrassment of your Association. I express these views of mine merely as a statement of a problem and not with sneering synicism or any ulterior motive whatsoever.

This problem facing the Bar Association needs to be settled finally for the peace of the country and orderly growth of our law. Our traditional approach to law is predicated on an independent judicial system suffering no interference from any quarters whatsoever, but subject only to law and constitutionalism. The new socialist legality if I understand it is predominantly neither independent of government nor of the executive view of legality. The dilemma here is poignant: which is it to be? The Bar Association has a strong ally in the view which a very articulate exponent of the traditional approach—one of the eminent members of the judiciary who has now retired expressed almost eighth years ago on 14 October 1980. Apaloo CJ on the occasion of swearing in new judges of the judiciary and offering a rationale for the traditional philosophy of our brand of law said, inter alia:

“A court can only function properly as a constitutional umpire if it is in an independent position. If it is obliged to uphold the policies of the executive either on the basis of constitutional prescription regardless of the constitutional text or because of the mental disposition of the judges, then the court becomes simply a branch, a specialised branch of the executive and it will be unreal to look upon it as an independent tribunal. It would be a sad day for Ghana if our judges were ever to feel so disposed. Such mental attitude in another jurisdiction is christened socialist legality. As a legal concept such philosophy was wholly rejected by the framers of our Constitution.”3

It seems to me that the proliferation of the tribunal system may very well be a reaction to a failure to appreciate the constraints which the rule of law imposes on the judges of the traditional courts. The Attorney-General and PNDC Secretary for Justice would seem to understand the problem and it seems that he appreciates the dilemma of the law and the temporary nature of two streams of parallel law inspired by conflicting jurisprudence running dubiously serene in one jurisdiction. He foresees ultimate merger to resolve the impasse and although the form of the merger is yet to emerge, his views are a hopeful sign for the future of a pure and unadulterated legal system. In a key note address on the “Role of the Public Tribunals in the Ghana Legal System” delivered on 10 July 1987 at a conference of “Chairmen and legal officers of Public Tribunals”, he said, inter alia:

“We think that it will be desirable, if not wise and prudent, to allow them to function for some time parallel to the regular courts to enable them learn from any past mistakes and put then right. There is no doubt given sufficient time to adjust themselves independently of the regular courts, the Public Tribunals will acquire experience to facilitate merging of the two at the top level. It is therefore submitted that the Public Tribunals should continue to function subject to constant or periodic review of its powers and procedures independently of the regular courts until such time that it would be practicable to assimilate the tribunals in the existing judicial system,”4

In spite of all the dilemmas, I am convinced as I survey the legal firmament that the future will not spell doom for the generations that will follow us. I now move to the dilemmas of the lawyers.

The Dilemmas of the Lawyers in their Pervasive Role

The position of lawyers in Ghana society has been very gravely undermined by an insidious and hardly explicable belief, firmly normative that lawyers are liars and therefore a bad lot and that they are paid by their clients to subvert the truth. So pervasive is this belief that it has engendered another strongly held belief quite ludicrous that when lawyers die they are laid to rest in their coffins with their faces downward and hidden contrary to the general practice by which good men not afraid of appearing before God, and who die are laid to rest with their faces upward. In the view of the adherents of this absurd belief lawyers are so dishonest that in their last days, they must for fear of their misdeeds hide their faces when they go to meet their maker. I have already indicated that by lawyers, I do not just confine myself to legal practitioners I include judges and magistrates and all members of the Bench who have had training in law as solicitors and barristers, before their elevation to the Bench.

The very idea that lawyers are paid to sponsor lies is as unfortunate as it is misconceived. That notion so tenaciously held by our people postulates the proposition that the lawyer is employed by his client to subvert justice and by his retainer he endeavours to show that vice is virtuous. This is demonstrably false. It has lingered for far too long a time and lawyers must endeavour to dispel it at once. Our association with law as practised in formal statutory courts is of long standing. As a result lawyers in Ghana have assumed an obligation to uphold ethical standards that have been gradually built up for over 100 years during the interaction of our customary laws with the British common law in the wake of modernising our amorphous indigenous laws5 to conform with equity, natural justice and good conscience, the trinity that dominated our customary laws from 1876 to 1960. The resultant legal system that emerged by the time of our first Republican Constitution ensured the paramountcy of freedom and above all justice as the prime and fundamental objective of our new national state; it offered us prior established laws as the main apparatus for attaining this objective if responsibly utilised. It provided us with an independent bench as the formal mouthpiece of the state in dispensing justice in accordance with law and made an equally independent Bar a central figure as the mechanism for maintaining and operating the system of law so established. In all cases where the system has foundered and in those circumstances where our attempts to establish according to law an enlightened democratic system has failed, the fault can be traced to the frailty of human nature and a garrulous segment of our population bent on exploiting the dilemmas of the lawyers and the difficulties facing the operators of the system.

The lawyer under our system of justice comes face to face with many in-built devices that ensure that he acts as their spokesman to guarantee the fundamental rights of individuals in society. The citizen’s rights from birth and indeed from a date even before birth when he is in the womb may even before he is conceived through infancy to adulthood and old age and his activities before and after coming of age, through marriage, and at every step in his life till death and after death are completely controlled by law and the lawyer is a key figure in this control which is devised to promote the happiness of the individual and the interest of society. Unfortunately it is rather in his effort at ensuring the promotion of the happiness of the people that some of the people paradoxically see something odious and strange in the role he plays in the system when he sponsors an unpopular cause in his professional capacity. This arises when he defends a person accused of a crime of which the public suspect him to be guilty or when he takes a case which he and the people think is bad. Every man and woman in the highest hierarchy of our society exhibit this prejudice against lawyers and some lawyers do indeed find themselves in a dilemma when faced with the problem as to whether to repudiate or espouse such unpopular causes.

This problem leading to the lawyer’s dilemma arises because people fail to distinguish between objective truth and justice according to law. The lawyer is concerned with objective truth and the judges and magistrates deal with objective truth aimed at attaining justice according to law. It is worth noting that justice is something by far larger than truth, and so the inevitable question is what is Truth? I leave the concept of justice unexplained, because as lawyers you all know that it is nothing but fairness dictated and circumscribed by case law, statute and well-known conventional practices of the courts.

The rhetoric question “what is Truth?” may however be simple, if by truth we refer to facts as they appear to us from our own observations and individual knowledge and personal experience of such facts. On this premise the whole concept becomes subjective capable of varying from one individual to another. It is “subjective truth!” The law is very cautious and suspicious of subjective truth, for it defies testimony outside the person with the knowledge and the experience. Subjective truth is an unstable quantity not easy to handle because of its probable variable and problematic content, for, as one English judge rightly points out, the primary evidence of what was passing in the mind of a man is his own testimony.6 You can only get it from him and yet if to serve his interest he is not candid you do not get it.

It is when lawyers and judges deal not merely with “subjective truth” but with “objective truth” that the great dilemmas facing lawyers in their legal profession really surface. If facts are not within a person’s knowledge and experience and he has himself not observed them, how can he tell that they represent the truth in the sense that they are in reality what they purport to be? Consider the position of a lawyer who is consulted by a client. Let us assume the client gives him facts which are to be the foundation of a suit at law. Now, the client may tell him all the facts and even some of the facts may have come to the client from other sources with the attendant danger of distortion in the course of transmission. Let us assume also that the case to be made out to ensure success ought by law to consist of twenty facts. These facts hypothetically represent the truth of the client’s case on the assumption that the said facts are singly and collectively what they purport to be. Now if the client makes available to the lawyer only fifteen of the said facts and either deliberately, dishonestly, or inadvertently omits the remaining five, then although the client has not told an untruth, nevertheless the facts at the disposal of the lawyer will not be sufficient to enable him to arrive at the truth, and the conclusion he draws from the fifteen facts may be false through no fault of the lawyer.

In such a situation to accuse the lawyer of sponsoring falsehood, is, I venture to think not fair or proper. Even if the client purports to give the twenty facts and one or two are not what they seem to be the conclusion based on the proffered twenty facts will also be false. The problem may be compounded by the further fact that the client may not be deliberately attempting to mislead the lawyer. He may himself have been mistaken in his grasp or observation of the said facts: furthermore from ignorance of the requirements of the law, he may have misconceived the importance and relevance of the omitted facts. This is why the law arms the lawyer with the very powerful weapon of cross-examination to enable truth to be elicited and it is equally the reason why the law insists that witnesses must swear to tell the truth, the whole truth and nothing but the truth. Obviously a statement which is literally true but conveys a substantially false impression because of deliberate and significant omissions may very well constitute false representation. As Lord Chelmsford delivering his speech in the House of Lords said in Peek v Gurney, in 1873: “Half the truth will sometimes amount to a real falsehood.”7

The overall picture I have thus delineated shows that no one can guarantee with absolute certainty the infallibility of objective truth, and yet it is want objective truth that the lawyers and judges operate the legal system. The lawyers, judges and magistrates are not themselves present when the facts, the subject-matter of litigation or prosecution occur; yet they must extract these facts from witnesses and draw inferences and conclusions from these facts: if the witnesses are honestly mistaken the inferences may be false: if the witnesses are bent on telling lies the conclusions may also be false and if the presiding judge or magistrate either carelessly or inadvertently having regard to the fact that mankind is not above making mistakes, draw wrong inferences from the facts, then the conclusions, although liable to be corrected by an appellate court will equally be erroneous. Nonetheless if cases are to be decided and litigation conducted or settled by persons who are not themselves witnesses of the facts in controversy then ours is the best system that the ingenuity and the jurisprudence of any society can offer: consequently to accuse those who operate the system particularly the lawyers of dishonesty because of the nature as well as the inherent weakness of the system is to misconceived the whole purpose of the judicial process and to denigrate the accumulated wisdom of many centuries. Surely, it is no part of a lawyer’s duty to pre-judge cases when clearly he is in no position to guarantee the facts in the case.

The lawyer is a necessary adjunct to the machinery for the proper and fair administration of law and justice. The prevalent public disapproval of his role when he defends an unpopular man or cause is not healthy for the law and does not advance the course of justice. This persistent and pervading nature of the error too often reflected in the genuine but misguided view of the average Ghanaian citizen, an error which confuses the lawyer’s duty and obligation with his probable personal belief in the righteousness or otherwise of a case he is handling, can only lead to extraordinary results—results that are not conducive whatsoever, to the necessary maintenance of the integrity of the judicial and legal processes.

It is my well-considered opinion that this belief and erroneous view so embarrassing to lawyers, creates a dilemma and conscience crisis and if not checked or eliminated from the body-politic may deter the good lawyer from representing unpopular causes. And in a country like Ghana we must appreciate that sycophancy and pandering to the official view is fast becoming fashionable and often the common past-time of the failures in our society who walk the corridors of power, kowtowing to officialdom, fawning for favours and largesse and fanning the flames of tendentious popular views which they imagine the wielders of power are probably holding. It seems to me that every leader we have had from the indefatigable Nkrumah, to the revolutionary JJ have not been isolated from these political parasites, and the downfall of many a leader can be traced to over-dependance on their irresponsible and selfish antics. Perhaps we need an institutional mechanism to protect our national leaders from the dilemma posed by the machinations of these shameless ruffians. This is necessary because by the experience of hind-sight it is possible to know the reality that motivates and is behind these merchants of evil and nation wreckers. Consider their past exploits in studied sycophancy and their slogan slinging expertise reminiscent of the horrors of the nazi propaganda machine: note how they attempt to manipulate our leaders: Nkrumah is the Osagyefo who never dies, Busia is the unchallenged champion of our rural folks, Colonel Acheampong is the 13th January man who will catapault us to capture the commanding heights of the economy and more recently the sonorous and endearing appellation JJ was blasphemously translated to Junior Jesus.

We must beware of these elements in our society who will seduce legality and exploit our dilemmas. With their holier than others antics, virtue can easily be denigrated by them. It will then surely degenerate and sustain irreparable damage in the hands of such rascals. The damage they thus cause may take generations to put right, because the popular view it must be remembered is not necessarily right and in the so-called old democracies and even in Ghana, it is not unknown for popular views to be discredited by the passage of time and the way paved for the right but previously unpopular view.

Address by Alao Aka-Bashorun Esq, President, Nigerian Bar Association.

In an interesting speech after which he received a standing ovation, the President of the Nigerian Bar said it had given him a great pleasure for his Association, the Nigerian Bar Association, to be invited to the 1988 Annual Conference of the Ghana Bar Association. Conveying a message from the African Bar Association to the Ghana Bar Association, the President said he owed it a duty to the African Bar Association of which Ghana and Nigeria were members to inform the Ghana Bar Association that fellow members of the African Bar Association were really sorry that the latter Association was about to break up. “For four years,” he said, “the Ghana Bar Association has not been able to host the African Bar Association Conference. We in Nigeria are also involved in the foreign exchange difficulties. Unfortunately the report to be submitted by Ghana Bar at the executive committee meeting of the African Bar Association in August last year could not be debated in view of Ghana’s absence. I have therefore been asked to enquire from Ghana whether it could host the 6th Biennial Conference in March 1989 as promised? Nigeria and Ghana are all involved and I want an assurance that Ghana is prepared to host the conference in March 1989. If Ghana should decline then Nigeria is prepared to take it up.”

Regarding military rule, he said: “Nigeria has been independent for 28 years. Out of this, nine years had been under civil rule and nineteen years under military regime. Ghana from 1966 up to date, four years had been under civil rule and thirteen and a half years under military regimes.

Argentina had been independent for over 108 years, Britain and America over 200 years, but none of these countries had resorted to military rule all these years. They know how to remove a government which has become unpopular. This is interwoven with their political culture. Politicians have their pitfalls and are bound to make errors, but it is not military intervention that can solve the problem, nor the solution being military intervention. This has gone into the political culture of the peoples of those countries. Lawyers in Ghana and Nigeria should be able to point out to the military to confine themselves to their legitimate functions of prevention of outside invasion and defend the territorial water/borders of their countries.

Nigeria is now facing the same problems. The country is heavily in debt incurred by former military men. The military should know that civilians are also patriots and not they alone. The military men who came to power in Nigeria are all now millionaires and one may ask, from where did they get the money?

What Africans should do now is to work towards the elimination of those negative aspects of our culture which tend to invite military interventions. Nigerians are now asking how the problem can be solved. It is for the people to be bold enough to tell the military men to return to the barracks and leave the running of government to the politicians who are trained to do so.”

These are some of the thoughts that we want to share with you.

Thank you.
