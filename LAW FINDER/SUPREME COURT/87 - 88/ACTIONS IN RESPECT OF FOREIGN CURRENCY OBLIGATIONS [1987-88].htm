ACTIONS IN RESPECT OF FOREIGN CURRENCY OBLIGATIONS [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 234—240

FUI TSIKATA

 
 

IN 1979 a Ghanaian businessman entered into a contract in Hungary for the supply to him of goods by a Hungarian state corporation. The goods were delivered to him under the contract.  He made part payment of the purchase price, and issued cheques for US$86,250 to cover the outstanding balance.  The cheques were not honoured. In August 1982, he agreed in Ghana to settle his debt by monthly instalments over a period of eleven months, but he failed to honour this agreement.  Eventually, in July 1984 court proceedings were initiated in Ghana on behalf of the corporation for recovery of the unpaid sum.  The defendant admitted liability for the debt, and the parties were apparently agreed that judgment would have to be entered in favour of the plaintiffs in cedis, rather than in US dollars.

However, while the plaintiff wanted the whole debt to be paid immediately, the defendant sought to be allowed to pay by instalments. It appears that the High Court proceeded by entering a consent judgment in favour of the plaintiff in September 1984 requiring that the entire debt be paid at "the prevailing bank rate."1 Thereafter, on 31 October 1984, it granted an application by the defendant to pay the debt by instalments, giving him a nine-month period within which to pay. At the end of that period, in August 1985, the defendant who had made only the first of the required payments, applied again for a stay of execution and extension of time within which to pay. The legal representatives of the parties agreed to a consent judgment on 27 August 1985 by which the defendant was "to pay the balance now outstanding, ie ¢4,662,062.50" within two weeks.  The defendant first paid ¢3 million of that amount into court and then, after the rejection of a further application by him, he paid in the remaining ¢1,662,062.50.

Subsequently, however, he applied for an amendment of the consent judgment of September 1984, arguing that the exchange rate which should have been applied to determine how much he should pay in cedis, was the rate at that date. The High Court upheld the contention of the defendant (though Ansah-Twum J would have gone further but for the parties' earlier agreement embodied in the judgment of September 1984) and fixed the calculation of the exchange rate at an even earlier date, namely, that of the defendants breach of the contract by non-payment of the purchase price).2

The plaintiff's appeal to the Court of Appeal was allowed by a majority decision (Amua-Sekyi and Abban JJA).  Osei-Hwere JA dissented on the grounds that "the order was made without the defendant's consent at all and also that his counsel was misled by the plaintiffs and their attorney to consent to the agreement."3 He also argued that the Court of Appeal had, in an earlier case 4 followed the then prevailing English authorities, and held that an award of damages must be converted into cedis according to the exchange rate applicable on the date on which the breach of contract occurred.  According to the learned judge, the Court of Appeal was bound by its previous decision, and could not depart from it.

On the other hand, in the leading judgment of the court, Amua-Sekyi JA noted the changes that had occurred in English law, which now permits judgment to be given for a foreign currency sum, and for conversion of that sum into sterling to be made as at the date of payment.  He observed that:

". . . in SOCIETE GENERALE the Court of Appeal drew attention to the fact that our rules of procedure are silent on the matter and . . . we are in those circumstances enjoined by Order 74 of the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954 (LN 140A) to have recourse to the procedure, practice and forms in force for the time being in the High Court of Justice in England."5

Thus the contest was on the one hand between following English authorities which had been overruled in England, but which had been followed in a previous Ghanaian case, and on the other, following the latest word from England.

On the facts of Hungarotex Trading Corporation v Boateng, the following were the principal dates available for the exchange rate conversion, namely, the date(s):

(a) of dishonour of the cheques in 1979 or 1980;

(b) of non-payment of each instalment required under the August 1982 settlement;

(c) of initiation of proceedings;

(d) of the judgment of September 1984;

(e) of non-payment of each instalment required under the judgment  of 31 October 1984;

(f) on which the consent judgment of 27 August 1985 was entered;

(g) of payment by the defendant of the sums into court; and

(h) of payment to the plaintiffs of the cedi amounts.

The table of exchange rate movements within the period covered by these dates, suggests how substantial a difference is made by the choice of date.6

If we look at the matter from the vantage point of ordinary good sense, it seems clear that, particularly within a regime of exchange control restrictions, the proper solution is for the creditor to be paid in the currency in which the debtor agreed to make payment-to the extent that that is consistent with the applicable exchange control laws.  If that cannot be ensured, the next best would be to require that the debt be converted into the currency in which payment is made as at the date of payment; the creditor would get approximate recompense if the debtor paid him the local currency equivalent of the currency bargained for at the rate prevailing at the date of payment.

In The Legal Aspect of Money, first published in 1938, Dr F A Mann argued as follows:

"[T]here is only one solution which is in every respect satisfactory: judgment ought to be given in the foreign money the subject-matter of the obligation, though the judgment debtor should be entitled to satisfy the judgment by the payment of so much of the moneta fori as, at the rate of exchange of the date of actual payment, corresponds to the amount decreed to be payable . . ."7

In the second edition, published in 1953, he repeats substantially the same point and, apparently sceptical of the chances of persuading the English courts, adds that such a "result could be achieved by adding a few simple provisions to the Rules of Supreme Court."8

By 1971, when the third edition was published, Mann had become more forceful in his criticism of the English authorities. "Such results," says the learned author, "are unworthy of a rational legal system . . ."9

Of course, Dr Mann's work has no more standing in our legal system than is demanded by the force of persuasive argument. But that is the same as the legal standing of decisions of English courts, including the House of Lords.

In Hungarotex, was the Court of Appeal bound, as Osei-Hwere JA argued, to perpetuate the irrationalities of the earlier English decisions? He relied on the decision in Societe Generale. In that case, the Court of Appeal overturned a High Court judgment ordering the appellants to pay French francs to the respondent.  The contract of employment between the parties required that part of the respondent's salary be paid in French francs. The appellants were held to have violated the contract by sacking the respondent.  The Court of Appeal varied the judgment of the High Court, and ordered that the appellants pay cedis rather than francs.  Anin JSC refused to follow an earlier decision of Akufo-Addo CJ, sitting in the High Court, in which he awarded damages in foreign currency.  That decision had been reversed, on different grounds, and besides, Anin JSC said, "[t]his court is not bound by the High Court decision.” He then invoked rule 177 of the 7th edition of Dicey’s Conflict of Laws, and the cases cited therein to support the assertion that "it is . . . procedurally wrong for the Ghanaian court to award damages in foreign currency."10

In Societe Generale, the issue was as to whether or not the Ghanaian court could order a party to make payment in a foreign currency. The question about the date on which the exchange rate conversion was to be made was not in issue, and therefore any utterances by Anin JSC on that point would, by the rules of precedent, not be binding even on a lower court.  Besides, the action in Societe Generale was for damages for breach of contract. To the extent that in Hungarotex the plaintiffs were seeking to recover a debt, rather than damages, the actions were different, and the decision in one case does not settle the question in the other. (Of course, there would be good reason to apply one rule to the two situations, if that rule makes sense).

Order 74 of the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954 (LN 140A), on which the majority in Hungarotex relied, reads as follows:

"Where no provision is made by these Rules the procedure, practice and forms in force for the time being in the High Court of Justice in England shall, so far as they can be conveniently applied, be in force in the Supreme Court of Ghana."

There are, in fact, two rules of English law which are being applied on the basis of Order 74.  Firstly, that the court has jurisdiction to entertain an action demanding a payment in foreign currency and to enter judgment in such currency and secondly, that if the judgment debt is to be converted into the money of the forum, the date for determining the exchange rate is the date of payment.

If we look at these two rules unburdened by authority, it is not likely that we would characterise either as procedural.  The first is a question of jurisdiction, and the Court of Appeal has held that Order 74 does not authorise a Ghanaian court to expand its jurisdiction by analogy with an English Court.11 The second rule, relating to the date of conversion, is a matter of the substantive discharge of a money obligation.12 Admittedly, writers on the conflict of laws, including Dr Mann, have characterised these rules as procedural.13 But that characterisation is only justifiable, if at all, in a special sense, as a shorthand way of expressing the point that these rules apply to all actions brought in England to enforce foreign money obligations, regardless of the proper law of the obligation. That is a limited and somewhat arbitrary use of "procedural."14

Rule 7 of section 49 (1) of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372) provides that:

. . . in the determination of any issue arising from the common law or customary law the Court may adopt, develop and apply such remedies from any system of law (whether Ghanaian or non-Ghanaian) as appear to the Court to be efficacious and to meet the requirements of justice, equity and good conscience."

This provision authorises the Ghanaian courts to engage in creative adaptation and incorporation of material from other legal systems.15 That, in our submission, is preferable to a provision which makes "a law passed . . . in the exercise of the legislative [or judicial] power of . . . [any country automatically a] part of the laws of Ghana."16

The decision in Hungarotex at least allows for a more rational development of our rules relating to judgments in respect of foreign currency obligations, though further progress will require a different line of reasoning from that pursued by the court.17

We propose the following basic rules as a starting point:

(1) A Ghanaian court has the power to order the payment of foreign currency by a judgment-debtor to a judgment-creditor.

(2) That power is, of course, subject to prevailing regulations relating to exchange control.

(3) In the exercise of that power, the court may take into consideration similar discretionary factors as it would do on an application for specific performance of a contract.

(4) Where the court chooses to order the judgment-debtor to pay cedis rather than a foreign currency, the exchange rate conversion should normally be as at the date on which the judgment-debtor discharges his liability by satisfying the judgment.

(5) The date on which the judgment debt is thus discharged may, depending on the circumstances, and in particular the conduct of the parties, be any of the following, namely, that on which:

(a) the judgment-debtor makes payment into court,

(b) the sum paid into court is released to the judgment-creditor,

(c) payment of the cedis is made to the Bank of Ghana for subsequent conversion and repatriation to the plaintiff abroad, or

(d) the money is repatriated to the plaintiff abroad.

The general rules as to when and how a debt is discharged will have a bearing on the choice of date. That choice will determine which of the parties will bear any exchange rate losses arising from delays in effecting payment to the plaintiff which are not due to the default of either party.  The relevant question should be whether or not such delay and its consequences could have been reasonably anticipated. If so, the party in breach should bear the loss.
