SEDUCTION ACTIONS IN GHANA: BENYA v LAWANI [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 171—190

AGBOSU L. K.

 

Nearly two decades ago, two teachers of mine posed the following questions:

“It is intriguing that actions for seduction in Ghana are quite frequent, whereas, in England, they are nowadays comparatively rare. Is this because the cultural values of our society cause Ghanaian parents and persons in loco parentis to feel more outraged than English parents when their daughters are seduced? Or is it merely because Ghanaian parents are more mercenary or vindictive?”1.

The learned authors in search of answers to these questions examined some local cases and came to the conclusion that our law on seduction was in considerable need of concise and clear statement. They expressed the hope that their article would stimulate discussion on the subject towards a clarification of the law on seduction. Unhappily the necessary response they had hoped for did not come forth. Perhaps, it is partly due to this lack of precision and clarity in the law that led to the unfortunate decision in Benya v. Lawani,2 a decision which has provoked this article.

The purpose of this discussion is thus to revive the attempt towards the clarification of this aspect of the law in Ghana. In doing so the customary aspect of the tort should be subjected to a close scrutiny. This ought to be done so as to discover the respects in which the conception of the customary law in terms analogous to the common law has influenced the development of the law towards adopting its present posture. But before doing so, the facts of the case under consideration must be presented.

Benya v Lawani

The plaintiff's daughter, at the time of the judgment, was a form three student of the Breman Asikuma Secondary School. During the long vacation she attended a clinic run by the plaintiff’s employer. The defendant, a medical officer, was in charge of the clinic. It was not clear from the evidence, as the plaintiff had alleged, whether or not the defendant had had sexual intercourse with the plaintiff's daughter in the consulting room. Nevertheless as a result of carnal knowledge by the defendant of the plaintiff's daughter, she became pregnant and a child was born.

The plaintiff who is the father of the seduced girl sued for damages in respect of what he called “customary seduction” and some specified special damages. The trial judge, without any indication as to what constitutes customary seduction and without disclosing the basis of her assessment, awarded a colossal sum of ¢300,000 damages excluding ¢30,000 costs in addition to the special damages totalling some ¢53,000 claimed by the plaintiff. Before a closer examination of the merits and demerits of the decision, it might be worthwhile to remind ourselves of the rules relating to the tort of seduction at common law so as to enhance our conceptual clarity from the outset.

Seduction at Common Law

In his introduction to the subject of injuries affecting the family and service relationships, Winfield points out that the tort rules in relation to seduction are still tinged with the archaic idea that those who interfere with a man's rights over his wife or child are meddling with more or less valuable servants of his rather than outraging his domestic feelings.3 This view simply confirms the fact that at common law, a husband's right to a cause of action for the enticement of his wife has, like the action for the seduction of a child, its historical foundations in master-servant relationship and loss of service. Thus Lord, Goddard in Best v. Samuel Fox & Co, Ltd.4 said that all the authorities5 showed that the action which the law gave to a husband for loss of consortium was founded on the proprietary right which from ancient times, it was considered the husband had in his wife.

However, the interest protected by the tort of enticement at common law today is the loss by the husband of the comfort and society of the enticed wife. The action is therefore not based, as in the case of the seduction of a child, on proving loss of service.6 Liability is automatic and is not dependent on carnal knowledge of the wife by the enticer. Thus although the historical roots of seduction and enticement actions are traceable to master-servant relationship and its associated loss of service, it is the latter, loss of service, as an essential ingredient of the tort of seduction that distinguishes it from enticement actions. This is why Lord Campbell in Lynch v. Knight thought that loss of consortium or conjugal society was one that the law might recognise to the wife as well as the husband, but hastened to add that the wife was not the servant of her husband and that the action for criminal conversation did not rest on “the fiction of loss of service as does the action for the seduction of a daughter.”7

Some of our judges also recognise this distinguishing feature of the actions for enticement and seduction at common law. Hence in Dapah v. Madzigbe,8 Francois J, as he then was, said:

"It does not appear that under the common law a right to sue for seduction is one of the benefits a husband acquires on marriage. The reason is obvious. The action derives from the concept of master and servant. If this relationship is not proved as also the consequent loss of service (per quod servitium amisit) the action fails. Our inherited common law, of course, is English. The English do not conceive of their wives as servants.”

It would, however, appear that not enough attention is always paid to the difference between the two torts in the adjudication of cases in Ghana when the term “seduction” is applied to customary situations. This would seem to be one main source of confusion in the law. While the common law draws a distinction between the two torts, actions which might be classified as enticement at common law are labelled together with the inducement and persuasion of a child to leave the home as customary seduction. When for instance, Her Ladyship declared in Benya v. Lawani that the plaintiff was entitled to recover damages at customary law not only for the loss of services but also in respect of his injured feelings, was she applying the common law or customary law principles or the admixture of both of them or simply confusing the common law with the customary law? It is necessary to find answers to these questions in order to clarify the law.

Seduction of a Married Woman under the Customary Law

It would seem that where the protection of the husband and wife relationship is in question, the customary law treats the subject under both public and private law or the admixture of both. According to Sarbah9:

"Where a married woman is seduced, her seducer is bound to pay to the husband as damages a fine or penalty called Brabbu,10 which is for the pacification of the injured husband and is not less than the value of the consawment dowry and all the marriage expenses. . . Moreover, where the conduct of the wife was brought about more or less by the husband's treatment, the seducer liable to a nominal fine.”11

It must be remembered that Sarbah was a lawyer. His choice of words in describing the remedy for the “seduction” of a married woman as distinct from that for the “seduction” of an unmarried woman must therefore be regarded as significant. At the beginning of the same passage quoted earlier, Sarbah states that “if a man seduce [sic] an unmarried woman, he is liable to pay her family damages for the wrong done her and the disgrace brought on her family.” He describes the remedy for the “seduction” of a married woman not simply as damages but as a “fine or penalty.” This would suggest that the seduction of a married woman was regarded by the traditional society, as reflected in the law, as being a more reprehensible conduct than the mere seduction of an unmarried woman. For this reason, it ought to be punished by a fine—a penalty, as indicating public disapproval of that kind of conduct. The law would seem to lean, therefore, more towards the province of the criminal law than private law, or at least, the combination of both.

This view is reinforced by what Danquah had to say on the subject. He states:

“Beyond the fact that ‘Asomo’ is the custom by means of which a person seducing another's wife is made to pay double fees and fines, in respect of the wife taken away or divorced from the former husband, we do not see that there is any differences in the procedure in regard to Asomo and in that for claiming ayefare or satisfaction for simple adultery.”12

Danquah who is also a jurist like Sarbah, employs the term “fines” and not damages, as the prescribed remedy for the “seduction” of a married woman under the customary law. There is also abundant evidence in the very instructive, well researched and learned judgment of Taylor J, as he then was, in Billa v Salifu13 that in most traditional communal subsistent societies, the seduction of a married woman or adultery had been regarded as serious enough a conduct to attract the death penalty. This was particularly so when the wife of a traditional authority was involved. In the relevant portion of the judgment of Taylor J, as summarised in the headnote, it is stated that in Dagomba customary law “an adulterer was punished by the chief and required to pay pan kobiga [vagina money] which was adultery fee.” The learned judge concluded that since the money went to the chief and not to the husband, it was a quasi-criminal remedy and not a claim sounding in damages.

Neither Danquah nor Sarbah did say explicitly that the chief or the elders adjudicating upon such matters received the fines in respect of the seduction of a married woman. Yet the two jurists by their choice of words in the exposition of the customary law, would seem to suggest that as in Dagomba customary law, the remedy for the tort in Akan customary law could be described as quasi-criminal in content. Whatever may be the nature of the action under the customary law, it is clear that liability is automatic provided it has been proved that a valid marriage exists and that the wife left the home as a result of the inducement and persuasion of her seducer. Proof of loss of service is not required.

In this respect, customary “seduction” of a married woman is similar, at least in form, to an enticement action at common law. But in terms of remedies and basis of liability, customary seduciton of a wife is not so identical with common law of enticement as to justify labelling it as seduction. This is bound to cause confusion in the law. The likelihood of confusion is aggravated by labelling together the inducement and persuasion of a child, an unmarried and a married woman in the traditional society as customary seduction. We suspect that the learned judge had fallen a victim to this confusion in the law in Benya v. Lawani. That case involved the seduction of a school girl by a medical practitioner. She held that the defendant was bound to pay damages at customary law, “not only for the loss of services but also in respect of injured feelings of the plaintiff.”14 In assessing damages, she declared that the defendant was “entitled automatically to pay damages at customary law. . .” As we have seen earlier, the common law action for seduction is based on proof of loss of services while liability for enticement is automatic. As will be shown presently, all the authorities show that master-servant relationship and consequent loss of service is not an element of the traditional remedy for inducing or persuading a child to leave the home. Yet in the confused state of the law, the learned judge was apparently applying common law remedies to a customary situation. What then are the essential ingredients of customary “seduction” of an unmarried woman? This must be the next subject of inquiry.

The Seduction of an Unmarried Woman.

Sarbah's exposition of the law and the decided cases show that at customary law, an action for seduction is available to a man whose wife or child has been induced or persuaded to leave and has in fact left the home. Any one standing in loco parentis to a child or an unmarried woman lured to leave the home is accorded a similar right. The term seduction, however, has a recognised legal meaning under the common law, denoting master-servant relationship with consequent loss of service.  What then are the basic ingredients of customary seduction of an unmarried woman?

According to Sarbah:

“If a man seduce [sic] an unmarried woman, he is liable to pay to her family damages for the wrong so done her and the disgrace brought on her family. When the seduction was under the promise of marriage, the liability to damages is not extinguished by any consawment subsequently given by the seducer to her family, who can at any time hold it as satisfaction of the claim for damages for the wrong done their child.”15

We find in this statement of the customary law, no evidence that the seducer's liability is dependent on master-servant relationship or loss of service. In spite of the use of the term “seduce” to describe the tortuous act, two essential elements appear to form the injuria in respect of the seduced single woman: (a) the wrong done her, and (b) the disgrace brought upon the family. It implies that in order to establish liability for this tort at customary law the plaintiff must prove that: (a) the victim of seduction left the home as a result of the inducement and persuasion of the defendants,16 (b) the conduct of the defendant did the victim of seduction a “wrong,” and (c) the whole incident has brought disgrace upon the family.

Several questions remain however, to be answered in order to ascertain what precisely one should be looking for as the basic ingredients of customary seduction of a single woman. What, for instance, constitutes the “wrong” done to the victim for which her family, and not herself, must be compensated? At common law the basis of the parent's claim is the loss of service or services which can be ascertained and specified. Under the indigenous law, does the wrong consist in the act of persuading and inducing the woman to leave the home or should this be coupled with carnal knowledge of her? If so, there would seem to be no sound reason why sexual intercourse between two consenting adults, the female partner of which is unmarried should constitute a “wrong” done to her for which the law must give a remedy, not to the woman said to be “wronged” herself, but to her family. This would be outrageous.

Perhaps the customary law would adopt a more redeeming posture if the age of the female partner were to be taken into account in determining liability. But this has never been clearly articulated in the law. Sarbah wrote simply about the seduction of an unmarried woman and the courts have echoed this without any qualification. It is trite law that the customary law does the reasonable in the circumstances of each case. It would therefore seem not only unreasonable and inconsistent with the tenets of the customary law itself but unconscionable to employ its principles to permit an unmarried woman who has freely consented to sexual intercourse in which she freely participated only to turn round thereafter and inform her family to damnify her sexual partner in damages for the “wrong” done her.

It is in order to avoid such absurd results that Date-Bah and Fiadjoe in their critique of the law made the following submission:

“In any event, on the present state of the authorities, it is submitted that it is not incontrovertible that mere carnal knowledge without pregnancy is sufficient to constitute the customary law tort of seduction of a daughter. Even if mere carnal knowledge should be held to be sufficient to constitute the tort, it is submitted that such carnal knowledge without pregnancy should entitle only the parent or family to sue. The daughter herself should be entitled to sue only when there has been a pregnancy or, at least, where the carnal knowledge was without her consent (i.e. rape). If the law is otherwise, it will afford the seduced girl an opportunity to blow hot and cold.”17

It does not appear that in the case under consideration sufficient judicial notice was taken of the above submission. Hence the learned judge would seem not only to have fallen into the common error of confusing customary law principles with those of English law, but uncritically applied the admixture of customary and common law principles to the case at hand. When Her Ladyship declared that there was no doubt that the plaintiff was entitled to recover damages at customary law not only for the loss of services but also in respect of his injured feelings, she purported to be relying on the authority of two cases. The first one, Asante v. Sarpong,18 concerned the seduction of a seventeen-year old school girl, Lydia, who became pregnant and had a baby. She said in her evidence that she continued to perform the same services as she did before, during the period of her pregnancy. On the strength of this evidence counsel for the defendant contended that as the plaintiff did not lose Lydia's services during the period in question, the action for seduction did not lie. Apaloo J, as he then was, held that it was not reasonable to believe that Lydia would be in a fit physical state to render her father the services she rendered even during her confinement. “Since the notion of service is in itself, a fiction,” said His Lordship, “the loss of service for even one day would suffice to ground an action in seduction. I think the plaintiff lost Lydia's services during her confinement and is entitled to damages against the defendant.”19

It must be pointed out that the authority of this case was wrongly relied upon in the Benya case. The learned judge in Asante v. Sarpong did not state that he was applying the customary law in any way. His decision was premised on proof of loss of service, which as we have seen, is not an element constituting the customary law tort of seduction of a girl or an unmarried woman. Indeed, in Addison v. Acheampong,20 the second case upon which Her Ladyship purported to be relying, Edusei J, as he then was, pointed out that:

“It is not necessary to prove loss of service in actions for seduction in customary law since the seduction of a girl is an invasion of her legal right which gives rise to a claim for damages. The parents of the seduced girl are equally entitled to claim damages because dishonour and disgrace have been brought upon them.”

The use of loss of service as one of the basis for liability in Benya v Lawani is therefore patently wrong. In applying the second element, “injured feelings of the plaintiff”, Her Ladyship failed to make it clear whether or not this ought to be equated with the “dishonour and disgrace” brought on the parents of the seduced girl. It may well be that their injured feelings are predicated upon the disgrace and dishonour brought on the family. No attempt at any explanation was, however, made in Her Ladyship's judgment. It should however be remarked that claims for injured feelings in seduction actions ought not to be encouraged as it would be difficult to find an objective basis for determining liability in such cases. This should be particularly so as lack of conceptual clarity concerning the basic ingredients of the customary law appears to be a contributory factor in the difficulties attending the determination of liability and assessment of damages. No doubt, the learned judge found herself in such difficulties in Benya v Lawani.

Assessment of Damages

Damage at common law is money adjudged to be paid by one person to another as compensation for a loss sustained by the latter in consequence of an injury committed by the former.21 Damages fall under two general heads. The first, described as liquidated damages, is where the amount recoverable by the plaintiff is ascertainable by calculation or is fixed by any scale or some positive data. The second, unliquidated damages, arise in most tort cases, particularly those categorised as being actionable per se, such as for instance, libel, false imprisonment, or as we have seen, in customary seduction. In such cases the quantum of damages would be such as the law would presume to flow from the injury done to the plaintiff.

The basis for the distinction between liquidated and unliquidated damages is that the measure of damages in the case of the former is usually clear and certain while the latter is not so clear and precise. It must be noted, however, that whether or not one is concerned with the latter or the former, the assessment of damages cannot be made arbitrarily. We thus find, for example, the development in our courts, of a practice in personal injury cases, some objective criteria for determining liability and the assessment of damages in a consistent and coherent manner. In claims for pain and suffering resulting from injuries to the person, for example, references are made to the gravity of the injury and the length of time it has been endured or continues to be endured in order to determine the measure of damages. Here, the judge's term of reference is the physical injuries, and sometimes medical reports, all of which he takes into account in determining the quantum of damages. He does exercise discretion, but his calculation is not determined simply by what the victim tells him to be the extent of his pains or sufferings.22 Similarly, on claims in respect of loss of amenities and of expectation of life, the judges do refer to the age and social life of the injured party. If he was, before his injury actively engaged in sporting activities and games, was a member of social clubs, used to love dancing, etc, and is now unable to do so in consequence of his injuries, these ascertainable activities become the main factors in determining the measure of damages.23 It is self-evident then that in spite of the fact that such claims are for “pain and sufferings” in the abstract and intangible in themselves, ocular observation of physical objects serve as a guide to the measure of damages. The task of the judge is made easier by the fact that such damages are tied up to a three-tiered concept of duty, breach and damage as constituting negligence upon which liability is premised. Once this has been established, the judge turns to the developed practice as a guide in assessing damages.

When we turn to customary seduction, however, it would appear that lack of precision concerning the basic ingredients of customary seduction has adversely affected the development of any consistent basis for determining the measure of damages. For example, in Benya v Lawani itself, the learned judge found no difficulty in granting damages for the losses incurred by the plaintiff in respect of clothing and feeding bottles for the baby, extra care for the pregnant daughter and the cloth purchased for her. Being in the nature of liquidated damages, they were specially pleaded and specifically proved. When it came to assessing damages for the customary seduction, “not only for the loss of services but also in respect of the injured feelings of the plaintiff” problems arose.

As the learned judge considered loss of services as a basis of liability in customary seduction, being in the nature of liquidated damages, it should have been specifically pleaded and strictly proved. There was no evidence in the judgment that this was done. Yet Her Ladyship failed to indicate in her judgment, what proportion of the ¢300,000 represented loss of services. Perhaps, the learned judge regarded loss of services as a fiction, as conceived by Apaloo J, as he then was, in Asante v. Sarpong, a case she relied upon for her decision. Even so it would have been helpful if it was made known, the proportion of the award representing that head of damage. Similarly, in regarding injured feelings as another head under which liability would ensue, was she equating injured feelings with the disgrace and dishonour brought on the family? This is an important issue because Asante v. Sarpong24 from which the learned judge implied that injured feelings must be compensated for was presumably decided under the common law and not under customary law. Even should that question be answered affirmatively, it would be enough to point out that disgrace and dishonour, like injured feelings, are not subject to actuarial calculation. Yet some objective criteria must be employed as determining the measure of damages, depending on the circumstances of each particular case. Regrettably this issue was insufficiently addressed in the Benya case. It is conceded that damages are presumed as was pointed out by the Court of Appeal in Yanney v Nyamekye,25 but then, the criteria laid down there and other authorities such as Sarbah and Addison v. Acheampong for instance, are (a) the disgrace and dishonour brought on the family, and (b) the wrong done to the seduced girl. This is basic. As the decision in Benya v. Lawani stands at present, there is no guide as to how the ¢300,000 was arrived at. The proportions representing loss of services, which as we have argued was wrongly included, injured feelings, if that must be equated with the disgrace and dishonour brought on the family, and the wrong done to the girl remain unspecified.

The only statement by which the learned judge sought to rationalise her decision was that “the defendant in view of his position in the society should have set a better example than he has done now.” Is this statement to be understood in terms of exemplary damages, as being vindictive and given not merely as pecuniary compensation for the loss actually sustained by the plaintiff, but as a kind of punishment to the defendant with a view to preventing similar wrongs in the future? It is submitted that this would be going too far.

In Dapah v. Madzigbe,26 Francois J, as he then was, held that the measure of damages for customary seduction should not be equated with the depth of the defendant's pocket. Implied in this holding is the rationale that a man must be compensated for what would reasonably be assessed to have actually been lost by him and the assessment should not depend on what the defendant is or is not capable of paying. To hold otherwise would amount to either punishing the defendant or denying the plaintiff what is fully due to him. That is why the learned judge reduced the amount of damages awarded by the magistrate court at the court below from ¢290 to ¢100. In this respect we may take a cue from the statement of Apaloo J, as he then was, in Asante v. Sarpong. In quantifying general damages, he said:

“. . . I think I am entitled to take into account the plaintiff's injured feeling and the defendant's provocative conduct in denying responsibility for the pregnancy and in imputing unchastity with other men to Lydia. But I do not forget that damages in this case should be compensatory and not penal.”27

We are in respectful agreement with the learned judge that damages should be compensatory and not penal; and this must continually be borne in mind by the judge when assessing damages. Therefore to the extent that the defendant's provocative conduct would tend to aggravate the injured feelings of the plaintiff, it would be appropriate to take that into account in assessing general damages. But such provocative conduct per se may be a sufficient but not a necessary condition for enhanced or aggravated damage.28 Although the case was decided under the common law, the views expressed by the learned judge on the assessment of damages would hold good for the customary law as well.

In the light of what has been discussed, it is respectfully suggested that the decision of Hayfron-Benjamin J, as he then was, in Takyi v Amoyaw29 might not be accepted as a useful guide in the assessment of damages in customary seduction actions. In that case, the seducer was the Headmaster of the Koforidua Girls Secondary School. He seduced an eighteen-year old female student of the school. She became pregnant and delivered a child. In an action for seduction in which the defendant had neither filed a statement of defence nor was represented by counsel, His Lordship in awarding £500 general damages, £200 special damages and £20 costs remarked:

“I consider this as a particularly reprehensible case, as it undermines the confidence of parents in our educational institutions. I am of the opinion that this conduct of the defendant calls for aggravated damages which I assess at £500 . . .”

It is obvious that the learned judge was importing into his criteria for determining the measure of damages questions bearing on morality. It is our humble opinion that it would be safer for the law if the judge draws a distinction between what he considers to be moral or immoral and the loss sustained by the plaintiff. Whether the conduct from which the injury giving rise to liability may be considered as moral or immoral should be immaterial. It must be remembered that adequate provisions exist under our criminal code to deal with those aspects of the plaintiff's conduct in seduction actions which may be regarded as going beyond mere civil wrongs.30

From the facts of Benya v Lawani or Takyi v. Amoyaw, for instance, nothing would prevent the parents of the seduced girls from informing the police or initiating criminal proceedings against the seducers of their daughters. Similarly, the Medical and Dental Board and the Ministry of Education could be informed respectively to take disciplinary action against the respective defendants. It would appear that in those cases where the judge purports to be awarding exemplary or punitive damages, he is often unmindful of the availability of other forms of retribution to which the defendant may be subject. As Mayne and McGregor point out, general damages are directed to the compensation of the plaintiff for the harm done to him but exceptionally they may look to the punishment of the defendant only where his conduct is considered to be wanton, “as where it discloses fraud, malice, violence, cruelty, insolence, or like . . . where he acts in continuous disregard of the plaintiff's right.”31

There was no evidence in the judgment or the record of proceedings that the defendant in Benya v. Lawani conducted himself in any like manner outlined by the learned authors. Indeed, there was evidence in the record of proceedings to show that the defendant had made several attempts to get an amicable settlement out of court through officials of the Social Welfare. These were rebuffed by the plaintiff. There was evidence also in the record of proceedings, that the seduced girl was still paying visits to the defendant who had at least, on one occasion given her ¢10,000 for the maintenance of the baby. He had not, as in the case of Asante v. Sarpong or Addison v. Acheampong for example, denied paternity of the baby. There was nothing to suggest that the defendant's conduct was provocative, malicious or contumelious. According to the record of proceedings, the seduced girl was in form one when she was seduced. At the time of the judgment, she was described as a form three student. This means she had continued her schooling after giving birth to the child. Loss of formal education, one would have thought, must be considered as the most important wrong done to the girl. But if as on the facts of the case she had not sustained such a loss, it should have been a factor to be taken into account in assessing damages. Having regard to the circumstances, it is respectfully submitted that the damages awarded was rather punitive and excessive. The case must therefore not be relied upon as a guide in the future. It is also suggested that exemplary or punitive damages should be awarded sparingly, particularly where the conduct from which the liability ensues, both criminal and civil remedies could be available. For the judge to award exemplary damages in such cases, he would have usurped part of the functions of what should otherwise be left for the criminal law and extra-legal disciplinary institutions and the community itself.

Assessment of Costs

An examination of some Court of Appeal decisions would disclose the development of consistent rules for guiding the courts in the assessment of damages in court proceedings. These rules are so consistent and so frequently applied to interfere with costs awarded in the courts at the lower hierarchy that they could now be regarded as having developed into practice directions to the lower courts. Yet it does appear that sufficient judicial notice has yet to be taken of these rules by the lower courts. The Benya case provides a classic example of such oversight. It has therefore become necessary to raise the issue in order to bring the rules to the attention of both the bench and the bar.

What can be deduced from the decisions of the Court of Appeal over the last ten years or so is that the award of costs in the cause involves the exercise of discretion by the judge. However, this discretion must be exercised judicially. This implies having or exercising sound, wise and sensible judgment especially in relation to practical matters which often do arise in the proceedings. However, some of the judges sometimes inadvertently overlook the rules laid down by the Court of Appeal and award costs out of proportion to the actual costs incurred in the cause. This is due, perhaps to the fact that some of the judges get swayed by the quantum of damages awarded by them or what they might regard as the reprehensible conduct of the losing party. This often leads to arbitrary exercise of discretion with regard to the award of costs as was exhibited by the learned judge in Benya v Lawani.

As would be recalled, costs of ¢30,000 were awarded against the defendant. Yet from the record of proceedings, we found that neither party was represented by counsel. The actual hearing lasted for only one day. Only the plaintiff gave evidence on the basis of which judgment was given. These account for the short written judgment of about one and half pages. As will be shown presently, the costs awarded were in clear disregard of the guidelines and principles laid down by the Court of Appeal to circumscribe the exercise of such discretion.

In Ofori v. Ankrah,32 for instance, one of the issues argued in the appeal was that the 500 guineas costs awarded by the trial judge was excessive as the actual hearing took less than one week. Apaloo JA, as he then was, reducing the costs awarded said:

“. . . the way in which the learned judge exercised his discretion in this case is anything but judicial. I think it is a deplorable exercise of judicial discretion. Considering the nature of work that is involved in this case and bearing in mind the general level of costs in comparable cases, I think inclusive costs of 75 guineas would be reasonable.”

Similarly, in Mackenzie v. Yeboah,33 the same judge reading the judgment of the Court of Appeal in which Siriboe and Sowah JJA, as they then were, concurred, applied the principles laid down in Ofori v. Ankrah and observed that costs of 600 guineas in a case where the actual hearing took only four days, out-of-pocket expenses not exceeding £25 and where the service of a writ in England cost £25, were out of proportion to actual expenses and were not a judicial exercise of discretion.

The two cases show that while it is conceded that awarding costs involves the exercise of discretion, the judge ought to be guided by the general level of costs in comparative cases. In doing so he must take into account the amount of work put in by counsel, the length of time taken by the actual hearing itself, which might be determined by the number of adjournments, the number of witnesses, interlocutory applications, etc. The principles of these cases were re-affirmed five years later in Real Estate Developers Ltd v. Fosua.34 Here the Court of Appeal was considering the circumstances under which appellate courts would interfere with costs awarded by trial courts. It reinforced the view that even though the award of costs had always been discretionary, the exercise of that judicial discretion in the instant case was not based on any known judicial principle and that the trial judge might have taken into deliberation extraneous matters. The Court of Appeal came to that conclusion because, the hearing of the two consolidated suits involving the same counsel for both parties lasted for four days only and some costs had already been awarded in the trial for adjournments at the instance of the defence. On those facts the Court of Appeal concluded that there was no basis for awarding costs of ¢10,000 and ¢150,000 in favour of the first and second plaintiffs respectively. The court proceeded to reduce the costs to ¢2,500 in respect of each suit.

It is submitted that on the strength of the cases reviewed, there was no legal basis for the assessment of the ¢30,000 costs awarded by the learned judge in Benya v. Lawani, where as we have seen, the actual hearing lasted for only one day and neither party was represented by counsel whose works and fees would have to be taken into account in assessing costs in the cause. The Court of Appeal stressed such factors in Acquah v Oman Ghana Trust Holding Ltd.35 One of the issues for determination in that case was whether or not the award of ¢36,000 costs in a simple landlord and tenant case in which the hearing took four days was reasonable. The Court of Appeal emphasising that like all judicial discretions, it ought to be exercised judicially, noted that the case did not involve any complex issues of law or any time consuming research. The Court of Appeal thought that the costs awarded by the trial judge ought to reflect the judge's assessment of the industry put in the case as well as the reasonable expense to which the beneficiary of the award might have been put. On the facts of the case, the court found the sum of ¢36,000 costs so unprecedented and so manifestly excessive that they could only be a most arbitrary exercise of judicial discretion and thus set it aside.

All the authorities then point to the fact that it would not be enough for a judge to say simply at the end of his judgment, as did the learned judge in Benya v Lawani, that “costs assessed at ¢30,000” without more. To be a judicial exercise of that discretion, the judge must disclose the basis of his assessment—that is the factors which he took into account in exercising his discretion in the manner he did. The court made an explicit statement on this in Kusi-Apea v. National House of Chiefs, Kumasi.36 In that case, the second respondent had to travel from Brong-Ahafo Region on several occasions to successfully resist the claim against him in Kumasi in the Ashanti Region. However, the judge refused to award costs in his favour. The Court of Appeal in its judgment said that unless there were very sound reasons for it, a successful party in a suit was entitled to his costs and it was not a proper exercise of discretion to refuse the second respondent his legitimate costs without the High Court assigning good reasons for the refusal. In the light of the authorities, it is submitted firstly, that the assessment by which the ¢30,000 costs were awarded in the Benya case was anything but judicial. Secondly, the lower courts should, in awarding costs, advert to the principles of assessment of costs as laid down by the Court of Appeal decisions on the issue when assessing costs in the cause.

Issues of Policy

Recapitulating the basic ingredients of customary seduction of an unmarried woman under the indigenous law as being (a) the wrong done to her, and (b) the dishonour and disgrace brought upon the plaintiff's family, what then are the underlying social policy considerations of the law? These may be determined only when one understands clearly what is meant by the “wrong” done to the woman and the dishonour and disgrace arising therefrom. Although we have found no definition of the “wrong” in any of the cases, it would appear that it consists in the effects on the woman of the persuasion and inducement on the strength of which she has left the home. In many cases, leaving the home is accompanied with carnal knowledge of the woman by her seducer. Such a relationship might go on clandestinely and may not become publicly known until pregnancy occurs.

It is doubtful whether on grounds of social policy it would be desirable for the law to prevent pre-marital courtship and wooing of the fair sex in a modern society. In modern society, courtship and wooing often precede actual marriage. Yet it seems the policy of the customary law is to discourage such pre-marital relationships. According to Sarbah, quoting Artus’ reference to the customary law in 1625:

“Marriage being the foundation of society, they keep their daughters in cloisters, when they are marriageable, and their son leave themselves to their disposal, never wooing or looking on their wives before they marry them.”37

The statement quoted above clearly shows the extent to which seventeenth century Fanti society frowned upon pre-marital sexual relations. That attitude was reflected in the customary law. The position would seem not to have changed very much in the last decade of the nineteenth century when Sarbah wrote on the subject. He states that “[a] woman living with a man as concubine, mistress, or friend, is not encouraged in the eyes of the Customary Law, which stamps the relationship as immoral, to be remedied as quickly as possible.”38

The customary law of seduction was thus in the past, based on traditional society's conceptions of what is moral or immoral about sexual life. The views held then were closely associated with the way in which it was thought society should be organised. For instance, it is trite learning that the family is regarded as the unit and foundation of society. Its stability, unity and cohesion is thus essential for an orderly organisation of society. Added to this fact is the notion that marriage brings about the union of families. In traditional society therefore, parents and guardians are concerned about the sort of families their sons and daughters get married into. For this reason, particularly in the past, it was the responsibility of parents to investigate the family background of those proposing to marry their daughters. For instance, preliminary investigations as to the existence of witchcraft, diseases such as leprosy and mental disorders, are made about the respective families of the bride and bridegroom.

In traditional societies of the past therefore, it would be out step with social mores and norms for a youngman to seduce an unmarried woman, let alone a married one. Where the woman, became pregnant and therefore became publicly known, this might not only be regarded as outrageous but disrespect for constituted traditional authority and sacred social norms that sustained the health, unity and cohesion of the family and society at large. The question however is whether or not such considerations still hold good for Ghanaian society of this last decade of the twentieth century. When it is said that a man is disgraced, what it means is that he has be lowered in the estimation in which he is held in the eyes of members of the community in which he lives.

The question then is whether or not mere pre-marital sexual intercourse between an unmarried woman or a girl attaining the age of puberty and her male partner without more, can be regarded in modern Ghanaian society as a conduct likely to lower the family of the female partner in the esteem in which it is held in the society? It is very doubtful if this would be the case in Ghana today, at least very unlikely in the cities and urban areas. As to what the position would be at the country-side, it is submitted that the answer would depend on the extent to which Western ideas of civilisation and permissive society has infiltrated the social fabrics of the rural community concerned. It is submitted that one of the most judicious ways of ascertaining the customary mores which might have crystallised into customary law today would be recourse to the procedure laid down under section 50 of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372).39

The courts purport to be applying the customary law. The law must therefore reflect the social mores and norms that determine its essence and content. The social norms of the seventeenth, eighteen and nineteenth centuries which the present posture of the law seems to reflect can certainly not represent norms of today. Indeed when Date-Bah and Fiadjoe point out that seduction actions in England are nowadays, comparatively rare, the reason is that English ideas of sexual life have completely changed from what they were in the last four centuries when seduction actions were common there. It would be a travesty of the true position to suggest that Ghanaians’ ideas about social and sexual life have not undergone some metamorphosis under the impact of Western ideas of civilisation and socio-economic change, quite different from what they were in the past.

It would seem, therefore, that the learned judge was quite mistaken in thinking that by awarding punitive damages against the defendant in Benya v. Lawani, she was bringing the law up-to-date in conformity with social progress. To the extent that such damages are likely to preserve the status quo in their effect, it was retrogressive. It is suggested that in order to modify the law to keep pace with modern developments, the courts should insist that in order to succeed in a seduction action, the plaintiff must prove the following:

(a) that the seduced woman or girl would not have in fact left the home but for the persuasion and inducement of the defendant. This means that where the woman is found to be a very willing partner to the relationship, the action must fail. Here the age of the woman in question, her moral character and the attitude of the parents would be taken into account in determining whether or not the woman herself was a very willing partner to the relationship giving rise to the seduction;

(b) that the conduct of the defendant did the seduced woman a “wrong.” This may be discovered from the effect of the seduction on the woman. Has it, for instance, prejudiced her chances of getting married again as where her seducer is a married man for example? Has it disrupted her formal education or damaged it altogether as a result of pregnancy, etc?

(c)  that the conduct of the defendant had actually brought dishonour and disgrace to the plaintiff's family. The test must be whether or not the seduction has had the effect of lowering in the estimation of the right thinking members of the community to which the plaintiff belongs, the esteem in which his family is held.40 This is where modern ideas about such matters would become relevant for determining the issue.

It is suggested that where the plaintiff fails to discharge the onus required in (a), then it would be unnecessary to consider (b) and (c). It is hoped that when such proofs are required, it would prevent situations like Benya v. Lawani from recurring. What, one may ask, was the loss to the plaintiff and his daughter in that case where the daughter had continued her formal education normally, had a baby—the joy and comfort of most women, and the father obtaining over ¢400,000 an amount sufficient to guarantee her secondary education at least? The law must not lend support to the institution of seduction actions motivated by mercenary and vindictive considerations. In cases of seduction which results in pregnancy and the birth of a child, the welfare of the child must become the primary consideration for the judge. Vindictive and mercenary attitudes on the part of the seduced girl's parents could so seriously affect relations between the parties that the welfare of the child might be sacrificed in the long run. The courts must not encourage this. It is hoped that this discussion will revive comments on this aspect of the law towards its progressive development in keeping with modern trends.
