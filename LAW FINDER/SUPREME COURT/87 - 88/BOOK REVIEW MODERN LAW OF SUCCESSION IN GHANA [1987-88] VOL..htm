BOOK REVIEW: MODERN LAW OF SUCCESSION IN GHANA [1987-88] VOL. XVI  RGL 300—303

 

A K P Kludze. Published by Foris Publications, Holland 1988. No price stated.

OSEW E A

HERE is another well-researched publication from Dr Kludze. His aim was "to write a book which is suitable for legal practitioners but which also satisfies the instructional requirements of law students." This should be borne in mind in reading the review here attempted. The twelve chapters of the book cover practically all the areas which a practitioner would expect: wills, both statutory and according to customary law, succession under the laws of intestacy, including the Intestate Succession Law, 1985 (PNDCL 111), the Marriage Ordinance (Cap 127), the Marriage of Mohammedans Ordinance (Cap 129), and the traditional customary law, not forgetting his own distinction between the "practised customary law" and the "judicial customary law", and some related matters of family law.

The first half of the book takes the reader through discussions and analyses of the Wills Act, 1971 (Act 360) and wills under customary law that are quite satisfactory.  There is no doubt that many will find these very instructive. And the second half which deals with the other forms of succession is even more stimulating, though several of the opinions there stated have been published in the author's earlier book, Ewe Law of Property (1973) and articles in journals published in this country. Many are the comments that Dr Kludze makes on the earlier writers in this field—to make them in the order of prominence in his book, N A Ollennu, K Bentsi-Enchill, J M Sarbah, J B Danquah and R S Rattray.  Some of the views expressed by these writers are, says our author, with respect, wrong; and he then states the arguments in favour of the views he prefers. Some other views of theirs, sometimes supported by decisions of the courts, are, if we may put a phrase of Sir Henry Maine's in our author's mouth, "plausible and comprehensive, but absolutely unverified.”  The rest are endorsed, with reasons. Very many practitioners who read this book will feel invited to reconsider the familiar views on which countless cases have been argued and decided in our courts. That seems to be a really great virtue in a book of this kind. It need not mean that all readers will agree with the views expressed by Dr Kludze. One may, for instance, find these questions teasing. It is said at page 7 that section 40 (1) of the English Law of Property Act, 1925, is "now applicable in Ghana"; but is it? Practitioners are familiar with the rule in Parker v Felgate (1883) to the effect that if a testator was competent when he gave instructions to his solicitor to prepare his will and the will is prepared according to those instructions, the will is valid even if the testator become incompetent at the time of its actual execution. Will they agree with Dr Kludze (pp 25-26) that "our courts should construe the provisions (in our Wills Act, 1971, s 1 (2) to reject" that rule? Also, may it be assumed, in the case of a privileged oral or nuncupative will under section 6 (1) (c) of our Wills Act, 1971, that the required two witnesses “must be present together"? And is the decision in Coleman v Shang (1959) that children procreated in adultery during the subsistence of a monogamous marriage under the Marriage Ordinance were illegitimate “of doubtful validity”? (See pp 101, and 217-218). The author's suggestion (at p 170 and see also p 185) that the provision in section 4 (b) of PNDCL 111 that where there are several houses in an intestate estate the surviving spouse and children have a right of choice of one of the houses in their unfettered discretion “be amended to confer a prima facie right to the matrimonial home on the surviving spouse and children" may well be favoured or even accepted by many lawyers. But is that a practical solution to whatever the problem is that the High Court cannot resolve in its unfettered discretion? Considering the strange things that happen with some of the strangers that come to Ghana, can the law correctly be that "a Ghanaian domicil will not be acquired through a temporary sojourn in Ghana by a foreigner”? (p 176 on s.11(2) of PNDCL 111). More, can it be said correctly, as Dr Kludze says in p 190, that "there will . . . continue to be doubts whether adoption is known to customary law"?  Judging from his own analysis of dicta from the cases in pp 251-252 it does seem that there will not be much difficulty in persuading him to modify that view. It is, however, admitted that throughout Dr Kludze argues his submission with conviction and will usually carry readers with him.

There are many other qualities of this book which your reviewer commends. I mention only a few examples here. Under the sub-heading “Meaning of Particular Words” (p 105) the brief introductory comments on the chosen words will undoubtedly be found helpful by many magistrates and judges, not to mention practitioners and students. Dr Kludze draws attention (p 115) to the seeming inconsistency of several statutory provisions on the commorientes rule in Ghana and makes suggestions for improvement by statutory intervention which are very practical. Then he gives a satisfactory review of the state of the law on samansiw to date (pp 130-160), dealing with the formal requirements therefore and other related matters. And generally his criticisms of several provisions of PNDCL 111 are well grounded, fair and practical (see pp 163, 165, 181-187, 190, 194-195, 196-197, 199/200-203). One hopes that all of them will be carefully considered by those responsible when the time comes for the review of that law (with, of course, other suggestions to which practitioners have drawn attention).

Equal attention may now be given to the short-comings of the book. To start from small beginnings, it appears that at p 9 (3rd sentence) there is an intruding "not" after "may". In the discussion on attestation of wills (p 45) it would have been helpful to cite Order 60, rules 25 and 26 of our Court Rules; and the author's recommendation that the principle of "conscious presence" be adopted by our courts should have been argued a little more. At p 57 there appears a note 95 in which some expected figures do not appear. And do we still have in practice (ie “on the ground") any “Border Guards” in Ghana? (pp 61, 62 and 65). Should we not now consider also whether soldiers engaged in a coup d'etat (not only on restoration of public order thereafter) should be regarded as being on "active service" under section 6 of the Wills Act, 1971 (cf p 65).  In the discussion of privileged wills our author refers to Re Lartey [1972] 2 GLR 488 and states (correctly, I think) that "it (ie the decision) may mean . . . that the 1918 statutory declaration of the meaning of the 1837 Act (in England) applies in Ghana". He finds such a construction ". . . of course, objectionable on the ground that it constitutes an implied compromise of national sovereignty by allowing the English Parliament to legislate for an independent Ghana. Sovereignty should not be compromised indirectly through the interpretation or application of colonial statutes".  It may be wondered whether he was serious here; for not many practitioners will take that view of legislation from the British Parliament. (I have actually cross-checked with ten practitioners chosen at random and while three found the argument interesting, none of them said it was convincing).

There is one thing your reviewer found unfortunate: namely, that Dr Kludze used up so much space (pp 151-156) on a "brief effort to elaborate on ... a simple logical commentary." It was really a reply to a criticism by Dr Dankwa of an earlier article in which our author had expressed the view that . . . "it is a conceivable position that the emergence of private property preceded the making of wills but that wills are rooted in antiquity". I find Dr Kludze's argument more convincing and I consider some of the comments there made justified; but they could have taken fewer pages. Similarly, his discussion of the concept of the family, though admittedly stimulating, is perhaps too long (pp 237-244). There is no doubt that far too many of us have too readily accepted "that every man in a patrilineal community and every woman in a matrilineal community starts a family". The attempt to get practitioners re-think the basis of this is welcome. Earlier, at p 288, it is stated that Ollennu's view that rule number 8 (for succession in intestacies under section 48 of Cap 127) could not apply in Ghana (see The Law of Testate and Intestate Succession, 1966, p 252) "was not correct".  It will be helpful if in the next edition Dr Kludze gives us his reasons, as usual. Next, it is really a merit that this book discusses insolvency, even if only briefly (p 305). It should, however, have been indicated that the Insolvency Act, 1962 (Act 153) is no longer in force. We may all agree that practitioners' concern with estates is mainly their administration. In this book practitioners receive very little assistance, especially as regards insolvent estates. Also even before we come to that, many would welcome a consideration of the requirements of registration of "spouses" under the Customary Marriages and Divorce (Registration) Law, 1985 (PNDCL 112), which unfortunately is not mentioned at all in this book, on the whole question of succession and inheritance.

Finally, in this section, it should be pointed out that there are many printer's errors. In the last line of p 13 "enforceability" is misspelt; at p 18 there is "of" instead of "or" in section 3 of the Wills Act, 1837, quoted; and the English statute, the Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act, 1975 is wrongly described in note 5. At p 76 the year stated to be 1972 in the first line was meant to be 1927; at p 82 the word in the last but one line of the quotation should be “eccentric", in the centre of p 94 “indentified” is incorrect, as also "indefinitely" in p 97 (third down); and is en entre sa mere at p 106 (third time that phrase is there used) correct? “Accured” at p 107 should read "accrued"; "date of the testator" at p 109 should read "death of the testator"; and "Ollennu" is spelt differently in several places, sometimes on the same page, eg. 127.  At p 139 “properly” comes out (in the quotation from Taylor J) as “property”; at p 154, note 94 “citiations” is wrong, as also acknowledgment" in the quotation from Apaloo JA in Awotwi v Abadoo [1977] 2 GLR 375; and the beginning of the last paragraph on p 162 should read "The Intestate Succession . . ." “Private international law” appears in the middle of p 176 without the "O"; "subsection 11 (3) “in p 177 last paragraph, should be without the "11", just as "It" beginning the last paragraph of p 185 should be "If". When we get to p 202 we find "commission" misspelt at the end of the first paragraph; on p 128 we note that “no” in the first paragraph should be "not; in p 222, first line, “until” is misspelt; at p 225 a “will” is intruding in the fourth line of (i); we see that on p 226, note 62, the well-known name "Halsbury's" is incorrectly spelt, in note 63 the title of Ollennu's book is wrongly stated, there appears "al least" for "at least" on p 227, and "formally" at p 235 (end of first paragraph) instead of the intended "formerly."

A few more need to be recorded. On p 237 "exercise" calls for correction; p 242 begins with the wrong title for Ollennu's book referred to above; the word “patri-descent” gets wrongly spelt the second time it is used, at p. 245; and one observes on p 257 (last but one line) that “resource” should be “recourse,” on p 258 (last but one line of the quotation from Danquah) “alienation” is wrongly spelt, on p 260 Amarfio v Ayorkor has changed its “y”  for “v”, and on p 275 the Administration of Estates Act is dated 1963 instead of 1961.  There is also “cases” for “ceases” at p 277.

And there appears a quotation from Margaret Field on p 296 in which the word “unfaithful” can surely not be right, (what man “may dismiss a wife who has not been unfaithful”?). Lastly, on p 303 the word “therefore” must be corrected to “therefor”.  Now, were we talking of merits or demerits?

Your reviewer is happy to be able to say that none of these printer’s errors actually interrupts the enjoyment of the book. The language flows. The arguments are stimulating.  And the submissions are made with agreeable force. Dr. A K P Kludze’s book deserves every minute spent in reading it. I recommend it highly to legal practitioners (including judges and magistrates, all)—and, yes, to law students also; for it is the sort of book which every student will find very helpful while examinations are ahead and which he or she will be happy to own for many years thereafter in practice.
