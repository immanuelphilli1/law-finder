MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: SECOND CONFERENCE OF SUPERIOR COURT JUDGES [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 88—91

 

IN July 1987, the first ever conference of the Superior Courts of Judicature of the Republic of Ghana was held in Accra. As part of the programme of continuing education for judges, law and social change was chosen as the theme of the conference. An aspect of the conference theme which provoked interesting discussion was ethics of the Bench. It is therefore not surprising that an important outcome of the conference was the production of a Code of Ethics for Judges. It was within the framework of that programme of continuing education that the conference of superior court judges was held again in July, 1988. The theme for the conference was proof in law.

In his keynote address, the Chief Justice, Mr. Justice E N P Sowah noted with satisfaction the success of the previous year’s conference. Pointing to the production of a Code of Ethics for Judges as one of its achievements, the Chief Justice said:

“ . . .We were for the first time in the annals of the history of our courts some 112 years ago able to procure for ourselves a written Code of Ethics . . . It is a set of rules which we have ourselves worked out to regulate our conduct. You would agree with me that rules of conduct of this type are better appreciated and honoured in their observance if they are self-made by those whom they will govern than being imposed from some external source.”

No appointment to the Bench at any level, he pointed out, would be complete if it was not accompanied by a copy of the Code. Similarly, no new appointee to the Bench would, in time, be allowed to assume duty until he had read and comprehended the provisions of the Code and expressed his willingness to adhere to them. As to the binding effect of the Code, the Chief Justice observed:

“Not being a statutory legislation, one may question the legal efficacy of the Code. However, since it contains what I have already described as rules of conduct and behaviour, even if a charge as such cannot be preferred for an infraction of the Code, at least conduct not in conformity with the Code cannot now be explained away with the ease of saying ‘I did not know that this is not permissible.

Indeed, speaking for myself and subject to the interpretation by the Supreme Court of these rules, I would say that in most cases a judge who breaks these rules (even though they are supposed to be rules of conduct and behaviour) may render himself liable to the charge of misconduct. The offence of misconduct in my view, criminal conduct as well as conduct unbecoming of a judge and a gentleman are all to be condemned.”

OBJECTIVES OF CODE

The Chief Justice also commented on the popular view held by the general public that conduct which may be permissible in a lawyer or a public servant or a traditional authority will raise eyebrows and sometimes, be deprecated if indulged in by a member of the Bench. This common knowledge, he pointed out, often induced people outside the Bench to dub the judges as snobs when certain offers such as gifts, invitations or even gatherings were declined. In such situations, he observed that:

“. . . the Code achieves a dual objective in that it does not merely instruct those on the Bench what the proper conduct should be in no uncertain terms, but also informs the public about the type of conduct which the peculiar nature of appointment on the Bench regards as an anathema.”

DELAYS IN COURT

Speaking on delays in our courts, the Chief Justice announced that efforts were being made to mechanise our recording system. Continuing, he said:

“Those of you who have been to court No. 1 would have noticed that we are making a modest attempt in our own way to mechanise our recording system. Our primary objective is to work out a system which is such that when a case is tried or judgment given, the typescript should be available for collection within 24 hours. At the moment the system is operating on the basis of 72 hours. There are also some teething errors which we are working upon. Our system will never be effective, or command the respect it deserves if after a judgment a person has to wait for months, or years before a copy can be obtained. This is why we have roped into our exercise in the pursuit of judicial excellence the rapid production of court records and court proceedings.

Our target is to extend the recording system to the Supreme Court, the Court of Appeal and eventually all courts in the country. All these, no doubt, will involve money. We have made a modest request to our government and we are confident that with the commitment of the PNDC to the administration of justice, funds will be made available to enable us to implement our scheme. Ladies and Gentlemen, I am sure you will agree with me that even those who hate litigation will be compelled to admire and respect our court system if we are able to work a system by which court proceedings will be available for those who need them within 24 hours and money spent on such a venture can hardly be described as lost or wasted.”

Turning to the theme of the conference itself—Proof in Law, the Chief Justice pointed out that every court trial involved a concept of proof in one form or the other. In fact if proof were to be eliminated from civil or criminal litigation, there would be no basis for court trials at all. Those involved in administration and who had to deal with disciplinary actions in their offices, he declared, also looked out for proof in such matters. It was for this and many other reasons, he said, that the trouble was taken to invite their learned brothers and sisters in institutions outside the courts and the Attorney-General's Office to attend the conference so that they would all share their experiences together. The Chief Justice then dealt with standard of proof, under the Evidence Decree (NRCD 323) and pointed out that it was divided into two, i.e. the burden of persuasion and the burden of producing evidence. He referred to section 10 (1) under which the former is defined and to section 11(1) under which the latter is delineated. He explained that the burden of proof was crucial when all the evidence was produced. It made itself felt at a later stage than the burden of adducing evidence.

What would appear to have stood out clearly from what he had to say on the subject is that;

“the burden of proof is the determining factor when the tribunal of fact is in doubt. The question of the party which bears the burden of proof is not usually a difficult one, for the fundamental requirement of any judicial system is that the person who desires the court to take action must prove the case to its satisfaction.”

The Chief Justice’s opening address was followed by papers presented by E N Heward Mills, E S Aidoo, Mr. Justice K E Amua-Sekyi, Mr. Justice J N K Taylor, Mr. B J da Rocha and Sam Okudzeto. The first two papers, “Cross-examination” and “Evidence-in-Chief,” respectively presented by Mr. Heward Mills and Mr. Aidoo provoked interesting questions from participants. Suggestions by Mr. Heward Mills that the stature or the imposing personality of the cross-examiner could also be material in turning the scales and the apparent conclusion from Mr. Aidoo’s paper that justice should be done in accordance with the relevant evidence before the court and not what one considers to be the truth, led to debates of a fundamental nature concerning the distinction between objective and subjective truth.

Mr. Justice Taylor regarded such questions as arose as so important that he wrote a preface to his paper on Proof of Criminal Trials. It was so representative of what took place; i.e. lively and active discussion of the issues, that the Review deems it necessary to reproduce it here together with some of the papers presented at the conference for its readers.
