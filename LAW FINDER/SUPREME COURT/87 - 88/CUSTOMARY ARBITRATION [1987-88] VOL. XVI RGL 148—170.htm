CUSTOMARY ARBITRATION* [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 148—170

Kom Enock D

 

INTRODUCTION

IT has often been said that "the law is in the bosom of judges." This often quoted statement means that judges are presumed to know the law and are capable of declaring it at anytime. This, of course, is a legal fiction. It is however particularly true of the law of customary arbitration, because all the law that we know on the subject of customary arbitration is to be found in the decisions handed down by judges from 1883 to the present day.  No attempt has been made to codify these decisions. There is no statute which can be looked at as the primary source of the law governing customary arbitrations. To find the true position of the law one has to go to the Law Reports.  If one is lucky to come across a decision, one must research further to find out whether that decided case has been overruled, reversed or doubted in a later case or cases. 

It must be pointed out at this stage that we have had on our statute books the Arbitration Ordinance,1 and the Arbitration (Foreign Awards) Ordinance,2 both of which were repealed by the Arbitration Act, 1961.3  As will be explained later in this discussion there are differences between customary arbitration and the provisions of these enactments. These enactments were not intended as a codification of the customary principles of arbitration. They were intended primarily to regulate the commercial relations between parties to an agreement where the agreement provides for the appointment of an arbitrator or in certain cases where a court in its opinion decides that a certain dispute should, in the interest of justice, be referred to arbitration, for example, cases which involve the application of technical knowledge to the resolution of disputes. 

Customary arbitration is a method of resolving claims and disputes among members of the various communities in Ghana.  It is a procedure which has been in existence as part of our traditional method of adjudication long before the introduction of Pax Britannica in 1844. 

From the time that the Supreme Courts Ordinance4 which established the first Supreme Court in this country was passed up to the present, this procedure has been preserved as part of our common law.  Thus section 19 of the Supreme Court Ordinance provided that local laws and customs which were "not repugnant to natural justice, equity and good conscience" were to be applied by the courts in suitable cases.  The Native Administration Ordinance5 which defined the jurisdiction of Tribunals, State Councils and Provincial Councils enacted the following proviso:

"Provided that nothing herein contained shall be deemed to affect the power of any native or Native Authority to act extra-judicially as an arbitrator in any dispute of a petty nature in respect of which the parties thereto consent to his so acting under native customary law; but resort shall not be had for the purpose of enforcing the award, to the powers and facilities provided by this Ordinance."

The Native Administration Ordinance was repealed by various State Council Ordinances6 which were themselves repealed by the Chieftaincy Act, 1961 (Act 81).  The Chieftaincy Act, 1961 followed the Native Administration Ordinance by specifically preserving the traditional power of chiefs in customary arbitration.  Section 5 of the Act provided as follows:

"The power of any Chief to act extra-judicially as an arbitrator under customary law in any dispute in respect of which the parties thereto consent to his so acting is hereby preserved.  "

Although this provision was not repeated in the Chieftaincy Act, 1971 (Act 370) which repealed the Chieftaincy Act, 1961 (Act 81), the position of customary arbitration as a recognised method of adjudication was not affected.  All the constitutions which this country has had since independence as well as the various Courts Ordinances, Decrees and Acts have all provided for the preservation of customary law as part of the law of the land.7

Thus our courts have been empowered since 1876 to recognise this mode of adjudication of disputes and conflicts referred to as customary arbitration; and in the process have laid down the principles of law which should regulate the procedure for obtaining satisfaction or justice in the customary sense by resort to the informal procedure of customary arbitration. This is as it should be, since the majority of our people live in rural areas where procedures founded on English common law and Acts of Parliament are unknown. 

It is the modest purpose of this discussion to:

(a) review critically the various decisions which have been given in customary arbitration cases;

(b) examine the two streams of customary and statutory arbitrations with a view to determining the areas where they meet and where they part company;

(c). indicate the extent to which the present law on customary arbitration diverges from what was indeed the traditional customary practice of arbitration; and

(d) assess, in the light of the inroads which the English common law has made into our traditional notions of customary arbitration and the future of customary arbitration as a mode of adjudication in this country. 

B. THE CONCEPT OF CUSTOMARY ARBITRATION

Before we deal with the details of the principles of customary arbitration, it is necessary to dispose of one or two misconceptions regarding the import of the term customary arbitration.  Dr. Elias, writing on the customary judicial process in his learned treatise: The Nature of African Customary Law argued that "customary arbitration" should be described as customary law arbitrament since the word "arbitration" is likely to invoke in the minds of certain people the incidents of "arbitration under English Law and the Arbitration Act, 1889."8  Rather than invoking in the minds of people the incidents of arbitration under English law one would have thought that the word "customary" preceding the word "arbitration" would rather invoke in one's mind the idea that one is dealing not with arbitration under English law but with arbitration according to customary law. 

Professor A N Allott in his book, Essays in African Law,9 used the term "arbitral proceedings" instead of "customary arbitration"; and suggests that a new term should be devised (he does not suggest one) which would bring out the differences between customary arbitration and arbitration under statute law.  It is submitted however that it is not necessary to devise a new term for "customary arbitration."  What is important is for one to know that the word "arbitration" in the expression "customary arbitration" is not the same thing as arbitration under statute law.  The use of the expression "customary arbitration" has become so entrenched in our legal vocabulary (having been used by such textwriters as Sarbah, Danquah and by our courts since 1883) that any change now would create more confusion than if the status quo were preserved. 

When the concept of one language is expressed in another language, two things may happen.  Either (a) the concept may not exist at all in the other language, or (b) the concept may exist in the other language but the characteristics and incidents may be different in both languages.  In this connection, it would be instructive to refer to the example of marriage in England and in Ghana.  The fact that one is monogamous and the other polygamous has never been seen as good reason to look for another expression to describe marriage under customary law.  A marriage is no less a marriage because it describes the Ghanaian situation rather than the English concept.  So it is that "customary arbitration" is no less so because it does not import all the ingredients of the English concept of arbitration.

The English and the Ghanaian customary concepts have, of course, common features such as the settlement of a dispute by an independent third person other than a judge or a magistrate.  It is equally true as will be explained later in this paper that there are also distinguishing features. My submission is that these distinguishing features do not make "customary arbitration" any less an arbitration than marriage would be marriage whether understood in the English or Ghanaian sense. 

At this stage, we may now consider what the ingredients of customary arbitration are—what distinguishes it from statutory arbitration and from what is sometimes referred to as "negotiation for settlement."  

C. CHARACTERISTICS OF A VALID CUSTOMARY ARBITRATION

Since 1883,10 issues or questions of customary arbitration had come before the courts for adjudication in one form or the other. However, it was not until 1959 in the case of Budu II v Caesar11, that Ollennu J, as he then was, spelt out its basic ingredients as follows:

"(i) a voluntary submission of the dispute by the parties to arbitrators for the purpose of having the dispute decided informally, but on its merits;

(ii) a prior agreement by both parties to accept the award of the arbitrators;

(iii) the award must not be arbitrary, but must be arrived at after the hearing of both sides in a judicial manner;

(iv) the practice and procedure for the time being followed in the Native Court or Tribunal of the area must be followed as nearly as possible; and

(v) publication of the award."

We shall proceed to consider seriatim these five essential characteristics.

(i) Voluntary Submission

Ollennu J, as he then was, describing what constitutes a voluntary submission in law referred to an earlier decision of the Court of Appeal in Yaw v Amobie12 where their Lordships observed as follows:

"It is very rare for two people who are quarrelling to meet and agree together that they would submit their dispute to arbitration.  The usual thing is that one party makes a complaint to somebody, the other party is sent for, and if he agrees, the party to whom the complaint is made arbitrates upon the dispute.  Whether a party agreed to submission to the arbitration or not is a question of fact in each case to be determined from the conduct of that party and other circumstances."

A year later Bossman J, as he then was, in the case of Tanor v Dapomah13 severely criticised the Court of Appeal's statement of the law on voluntary submission in Yaw v Amobie and argued that it was difficult to assent or subscribe to it as it conflicted with earlier decisions of the Court of Appeal in Gyesiwa v Mensah14 and Twumasi v Badu15.  He said that following the rule enunciated by Lord Greene MR in Young v Bristol Aeroplane, Co. Ltd,16 he was entitled to choose between the two conflicting decisions of the Court of Appeal and opted to follow the earlier decisions.

By 1962, both Mr Justice Ollennu and Mr Justice Bossman had been elevated to the Supreme Court Bench. On panels differently constituted, they found themselves confronted with the problem of what constitutes a voluntary submission. Both wrote the different judgments which were delivered the same day 21 December 1962. The counsel who represented the parties were the same in both cases.  Ollennu JSC, delivering the unanimous judgment on behalf of Sarkodee-Adoo and Akufo-Addo JJSC in Asare v Donkor17 said:

"It is correct that one of the recognised ways in which arbitration proceedings may be initiated by customary law, is for one of the parties to a dispute to lodge a complaint against his opponent to the would-be arbitrator with a request that he should arbitrate upon it, and the would-be arbitrator then sends for the other party and obtains his consent that the dispute be decided by arbitration. But to amount to a submission to arbitration, it must be shown that when the other party came, upon being sent for, a full explanation was made to him that his opponent had made a request that the dispute should be determined at an arbitration, not in court, and that the person to whom he lodged the complaint should preside over the dispute as the arbitrator.  It is only when the person against whom the complaint is made agreed after such explanation, ie if with full knowledge of the implications he also expresses his agreement to the proposal of the complainant that an arbitration should be so held, that there could be a lawful submission to arbitration by both parties, otherwise not."

Bossman JSC delivering the unanimous judgment of the Supreme Court on behalf of his colleagues, van Lare and Blay JJSC in Paul v Kokoo,18 seized the golden opportunity to explain the stand he previously took in Tanor v Dapomah.19 His Lordship said:

"There is, therefore, that undesirable feature about an arbitration originating in a complaint by one party against the other to a chief, elder, or person who himself ultimately undertakes the investigation and decision of the dispute between the parties.  There is also the feature that when a party attends before a chief, elder, or person because of a complaint made by another against him, it is difficult to draw the line between his attending with the intention of explaining himself or his version of the dispute on the one hand, and his attending with the intention that the dispute might be investigated and decision given thereon, on the other hand.  An attendance following a complaint is an act or conduct which is equivocal and susceptible of two interpretations; whereas when no complaint has been made against him and he and the other disputing party together agree to refer or actually bring a dispute to a chief, elder, or person to decide for them, that act or conduct is unequivocal and more easily susceptible of the only possible interpretation, namely that both have voluntarily referred or submitted their dispute to his adjudication or decision.  Be that as it may, whether because of the considerations above referred to or others not here touched upon, there appears to be a fair amount of authority denying to these arbitral proceedings originating in complaints made initially to the arbitrator by one of the parties, the characteristics of a valid customary arbitration.  "

Five months after the delivery of the two judgments, Ollennu, Bossman and Blay JJSC sat and heard the case of Donkor v Isifu20 and the apparent difference in the law of voluntary submission was resolved once and for all.  Ollennu JSC delivering the unanimous opinion of the Supreme Court said:21

"The mere presence of a party to a dispute at a meeting which purports to arbitrate upon a dispute between him and another person, also present at that meeting, is no conclusive evidence or proof of submission to arbitration. To constitute submission to arbitration in such circumstances there must be evidence that the full implications of the purpose of the meeting was explained to each party, and that with the full knowledge of those implications they each agreed that the person or persons before whom they appeared should arbitrate upon their dispute and give a decision thereon.  The position is fully explained in two recent judgments of this court, namely Asare v. Donkor22 and Paul v. Kokoo."23

This case has been followed by the Court of Appeal in the cases of Mensah v Esah24 and Nyaasemhwe v Afibiyesan.25 So a party relying on customary arbitration must prove first and foremost:

(i) voluntary submission by his opponent,

(ii) show that apart from heeding the call of the arbitrator the proposal to submit the dispute to an arbitration was explained to him and he expressly agreed (as mere physical presence is not enough), and

(iii) adduce some evidence of voluntary submission such as his payment of arbitration fee and/or presentation of drinks. 

Thus in Asano v Taku26 it was held that the defendant's swearing the chief's oath and the plaintiff's reply by a counter oath amounted to a voluntary submission since no one was by law bound to respond to an oath sworn on him and failure to respond to such oath carried no sanctions against an individual declining to respond.  The significance of refusing to respond was to deny the Ohene jurisdiction.

The correct legal position is, therefore, as stated in Gyesiwa v Mensah27 that as to what evidence would precisely satisfy the court that there had been such a submission and such an agreement, that would depend upon the circumstances of the case and upon such evidence as might be adduced to establish that the requisite formalities, if any had been observed in accordance with the customary law.

(ii) Prior Agreement to be Bound

An agreement by the parties to accept the decision of the arbitrator is the second ingredient of customary arbitration.  It appears to overlap with the requirement of voluntary submission, since evidence of prior agreement of both parties to accept an award is also evidence of voluntary submission.  What constitutes a prior agreement, like voluntary submission, is a matter of evidence.  The payment of an arbitration fee in the form of money and, or drinks and paid before the arbitration starts or before the award is published may constitute the evidence of both prior agreement and voluntary submission.  Thus as in the case of Asano v Taku28 the swearing of the Ohene's oath and counter-response by the opponent could amount to prior agreement to accept an award as well as a voluntary submission.  

(iii) Rules of Natural Justice

In Budu II v Caesar,29 Ollennu J held that:

"What appears to have happened was that the arbitrators wanted some preliminary idea of the nature of the lands in dispute before inspecting them, and before hearing the case on the merits; but after the inspection of the land there was no hearing on the merits. I have found that the alleged decision of the arbitrators was never given; but if it has been, it would have been an arbitrary decision based upon no evidence at all."

Where an arbitrator exceeds his agreed terms of reference or proceeds to share the subject-matter in dispute between the disputants the so-called arbitration is null and void ab initio. Thus in the case of Omanhene Kobina Foli v Chief Obeng Akesse,30 the Privy Council held that the learned arbitrator had misconceived his duty by laying down a new boundary line based on consideration of what would be a fair division of the disputed area between the parties and they accordingly set aside the award on the ground that the arbitrator had acted ultra fines compromisi. This decision was followed by the Supreme Court in Nyame v Yeboah31 where it was held that an arbitrary boundary which the Beponhene attempted to fix for the parties if intended to be in execution of the award of the alleged arbitration was misconceived and contrary to settled principles of law. Similarly, in Mensah v Esah32a the so-called arbitrator in a dispute of successorship shared the estate between the disputants. The Court of Appeal held that the mere sharing of the estate detracted considerably from any determination of successorship. 

It is, therefore, clear from the cases that arbitrators are by customary law, required to decide and pronounce on the respective conflicting claims of the parties and not to do what King Solomon 32b threatened to do by dividing the baby, the subject-matter of the dispute.  Such conduct renders an arbitration award null and void.  One other essential rule of customary arbitration is that the decision on the merits must be arrived at after a fair hearing of both parties.  In Budu II v Caesar,33 for example, Ollennu J, as he then was, explaining the rule said that it was a fundamental principle of customary law that no person shall be condemned either in respect of his person or his property without being given a fair hearing.  Also, in Gberbie v Gberbie,34 Amissah JA pointed out that the fundamental principle of natural justice audi alteram partem was recognised as a necessary part of our customary arbitration procedure.  The principle of natural justice of a fair hearing of both sides involves two sub-rules, namely (i) giving equal and enough notice and opportunity to the parties to prepare their case, and (ii) hearing both parties. 

Hearing both parties involves hearing also their witnesses and allowing the parties to tender in evidence, documents in support of their case and be available for cross-examination.  The West African Court of Appeal set aside an award in the case of Inyang v Udo35 because no evidence was taken from the parties. 

The requirement that an arbitration must be conducted in a judicial manner and that the practice and procedure for the time being followed in the Native (now district) court or tribunal of the area must be followed as nearly as possible invokes the second leg of the principle of natural justice, ie that no one should be a judge in his own cause expressed in Latin as nemo judex in causa sua and a judge, for that matter an arbitrator, must not be biased. The courts have declared null and void awards in which an arbitrator sat on his own cause. For example, in Tanor v Dapomah36 the Nkawkawhene, the president of the panel of arbitrators was the husband of one of the disputants.  The counsel for the defendant relied on the case of Mensah v Botchway37 where the arbitrator was related to both parties, ie being a father of one of the parties and a brother to the other, in other words, the arbitration was between a nephew and an aunt.  Bossman J, as he then was, in dismissing that contention distinguished between cases where an arbitrator is related to one of the parties and where he is related to both parties and said:

"But I think it is impossible to find any saving grace about a situation in which a husband is to arbitrate between his wife and a total stranger or third party, or to hold that situation satisfies that test of `the reasonable in the special circumstances of the case'.  The general law of the land has happily sufficiently developed to demand and insist on a higher standard of judicial administration even by the former native courts and tribunals, than was the case 25 years or so ago; so that in a case like State Council of Peki v Adamoo and Others (Div Ct'31-'37,58) Aitken J. was able to follow the decision of Deane CJ, in an earlier case entitled State Council of Peki v. Kwamla Ayim V (unreported) and hold [sic] that the situation disclosed by the record of appeal in the case where the tribunal acted as both prosecutor and judge constituted such a violation of the fundamental principles of natural justice that the tribunal's decision could not be sustained. Even so a situation in which a husband purports to act as a judge in a case between his wife and a third party seems to me to violate the fundamental principles of natural justice.  True, the case was not being tried by a legally constituted court.  It is however in effect a court recognised by customary law—Assampong v Amuaku (1 WACA 192).  The principle that its proceedings must not violate the principles of natural justice must apply in some degree to it."38

Two years later, Bossman JSC had another opportunity to advert to impartiality of an arbitrator in Paul v Kokoo39 where he said:

"It only remains to mention, finally, that in any event the Akwaboahene himself was too involved and interested in the dispute to have been qualified to act as an arbitrator. `Native Custom'—said Griffiths CJ, `generally consists of the performance of the reasonable in the special circumstances of the [particular] case'—see Yerenchi v. Akuffo.40 It can hardly be said that the investigation of the dispute between the plaintiff and the second defendant by the Akwaboahene who himself so readily took up the cudgels against the plaintiff as soon as the parties met before him in his village can be considered reasonable.  The learned judge who presided at the hearing of the appeal in the Land Court was therefore right in his decision that there was no valid customary arbitration award subsisting to estop the plaintiff from prosecuting his action, and his decision setting aside the judgment of the local court should be affirmed."

In Adai v Anane,41 Osei-Hwere J, as he then was, held that the Odikro of Bredi's standing as an arbitrator vitiated the whole arbitration by reason of his avowed interest in the property, the subject-matter of the dispute.  Again in Akakyie II v Ediyie42 the Agonahene who was the grantor of one of the disputants, sat on the arbitration between his grantee who happened to be his linguist and a third person.  The Court of Appeal posed the rhetorical question as to how "it may be asked, could the defendant be expected to receive elementary justice and fairplay from such a biased adjudicator" and proceeded to hold that:

"Clearly the choice of the Agonahene as arbitrator in these circumstances offended against natural justice and the concept of reasonableness in customary law; and the chief was clearly disqualified by interest from playing the role of arbitrator assigned to him by his former linguist."43

The concept of elementary justice and fairplay are variously expressed in our local languages as "a monkey does not participate in matters relating to the forest," or "the corn does not take its case to the court of the fowl."

Recently, Mr. Walter Blege, the Deputy Secretary for Education and Culture and the choirmaster of the "Dumedefor" choir re-arranged an Ewe traditional song which reflects this local idea of fairplay for that choir, and the words read:

"Because of the presence of my enemies under the big tree in the town I shall not go there for my case to be heard.  No! I shall not step there."

No doubt, Kumapim royals and Senior Eddie Donkor and other lyricists may also have their versions.  Be that as it may, the question of what is bias in law has been settled in the famous case of Attorney-General v Sallah44 where Amissah JA delivering the judgment of the Court of Appeal said:

"The proper test to apply in applications like the instant one is `real likelihood of bias.' But every allegation of bias must be looked at within the context of the particular case in which it is made.  What may lead to a real likelihood of bias in one type of case may not have the same tendency in another."

The third and fourth ingredients of the essentials of a valid arbitration may be summarised as follows:

(i)  the award must be a decision on the merits,

(ii) an arbitrator must give (a) enough notice to the parties to prepare their case, (b) give equal opportunity to the parties to state their case fully to the best of their ability, ie to give evidence, call witnesses, be available for cross-examination and tender documents in evidence in support of their case, and lastly

(iii) an arbitrator should not be a judge in his own cause or be affected by bias.

The necessity to conform to the principles of natural justice is buttressed by the fact that there is no right of appeal against a customary arbitration award.  As Ollennu J, as he then was, pointed out in Budu II v Caesar45 "grave injustice would be done if decisions of arbitrators were arbitrary."

(iv) Publication of the Award

An arbitrator is enjoined to publish his award.  Customary law knows no writing, so it is not obligatory that the award must be in writing. Nowadays however, it is common to come across written awards of which signed copies have been supplied to the parties. This is a move in a right direction as in many cases in which an arbitration is relied on both parties give different versions of the award and a written award will stop people wriggling out of the binding effect of an arbitration by giving different versions. The requirement of publishing an award means that the award must be certain, unambiguous and not inconsistent: otherwise it will be set aside.46

EFFECT OF A VALID ARBITRATION

The effects of a valid customary arbitration as can be gleaned from the decided cases are that:

(i) a party to it cannot resile from it;47

(ii) it is binding upon the parties and it will estop a losing party from bringing an action in court against his opponent in respect of the same subject-matter or issue that was arbitrated upon,48 and

(iii) the successful party can bring an action in court to enforce the award.

Section 58 of the Native Administration Ordinance, Cap 76, expressly prohibited the enforcement of customary arbitration awards by providing that "resort shall not be had for the purpose of enforcing the award to the powers and facilities provided by this Ordinance."

Hence Howes J following his earlier decision in Bernasko v Andoh49 said in Adejatu Tomole v Kugbo Aro50 that in effect the section says "you may go to arbitration over petty disputes if you like; but if you do the tribunals will not enforce the award given by the arbitration."

 In Mensah v Takyiampong51 the West African Court of Appeal presided over by Kingdon CJ, affirmed the two decisions of Howes J.  This was the state of the law until the State Councils Ordinance52 replaced the Native Administrative Ordinance in 1952 without repeating the prohibition on enforcement.  

It is our submission that Ollennu J was right when he held in Osae v Apenteng53 that:

". . . there was no machinery by which arbitrators could enforce an award they made and that the only process to enforce an award was an action in a court of competent jurisdiction."

Griffiths-Randolph J added his voice, and perhaps his weight, when he declared in Odartei v Badoo54 that: ".   .   .   the award can be enforced by one of the parties by instituting an action in a court of competent jurisdiction against the other party,  .  .  .  .  "

In Appiah v Okyir55 and Amponsah v Kwatia,56 the High Court and the Court of Appeal respectively held that an arbitration award could be enforced by an action.  The claims were entertained and decisions given.

One is, therefore, surprised at Roger Korsah J's statement of the law in the 1980 case of Republic v Akrokere Sub-Traditional Council; Ex parte Carr.57 His Lordship relied on Mensah v Takyiampong58 which was decided when the Native Administration Ordinance (Cap 76), section 58 which put an embargo on enforcement was in force. His Lordship delivered himself as follows:

"A customary arbitration award could not be enforced even by the superior courts in the sense that the successful party could not invoke the aid of the court to proceed to execution upon it even though it was valid.  The parties were nonetheless bound by it and the award might be used in support of title in an action for declaration of title or as an estoppel to resist an action for such declaration. The attempted enforcement of the arbitral award, the bill of costs, by action before the district court grade II, was therefore wrong in law. There was nothing in section 37 (1) of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372), conferring jurisdiction on the district court to enforce arbitration awards, whether customary or otherwise. The power of the High Court to enforce non-customary arbitration awards stemmed from the Arbitration Act, 1961 (Act 38).  Neither the superior nor the inferior courts had any power to enforce customary arbitration awards in the same manner as a judgment or order by entering them as judgments or orders and ordering executions thereon.  "

His Lordship regrettably did not advert his mind to other decisions of the High Court and Court of Appeal since Mensah v Takyiampong (supra) was decided in 1940.  If he did, surely his decision would  have been otherwise.  In saying that an arbitration award can be enforced one must start by saying what cannot be done. One cannot go into execution straightaway because an award is in one's favour.  This is only possible in respect of a person who obtains judgment before the judicial committee of a traditional council, a Regional or National House of Chiefs.59 In the case of an arbitration award the successful party in seeking to enforce an award must take out a writ of summons and then claim either the costs awarded him as debt due and owing to him, or the delivery to him of certain movable property or in the case of trespass that the defendant has gone beyond the boundary adjudged to belong to him, or that the defendant has felled palm trees etc. or is working in the area determined to be his. 

In all these cases he bases his claim on the arbitration. An endorsement on a writ of summons which merely says or reads "the plaintiff's claim is for the enforcement of arbitration award held before Krontihene" on such and such a date is vague and insufficient and therefore discloses no cause of action. The plaintiff must make a specific claim based on the valid arbitration. If the action is in the High Court or circuit court he must specifically plead the arbitration and lead evidence to prove the essential ingredients of the arbitration. With all due respect, the impression given by Roger Korsah J that the effect of a valid customary arbitration is that it "is only a shield and not a sword" is per incuriam and must be overruled.  A valid customary arbitration is both a shield and a sword.  Section 37 (1) of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372) on which His Lordship so heavily relies does not, it is conceded, expressly provide for the enforcement of customary arbitration award, neither does it, it must be contended, expressly prohibit a claim for a debt, delivery of property or recovery of land or trespass arising out of a valid arbitration award.  It is submitted that if the plaintiff's claim is for debt, contract or tort, the fact that the cause of action arose as a result of an arbitration award is immaterial. Lastly, it must be mentioned that our courts have again and again stressed the point that the burden of proof lies on the person who relies on customary arbitration60 as giving him a cause of action or defence.

ARBITRATION AND NEGOTIATED SETTLEMENT

After the ingredients of a customary arbitration have been examined we must proceed to distinguish it from another species of informal settlement of conflicting claims, ie negotiated settlement.

(i) One of the distinguishing features of a customary arbitration is prior agreement to be bound by an award.  In negotiated settlement, the agreement to be bound is made after the award.  In both, the agreement to be bound may be evidenced by payment of money and/or presentation of drinks. The deciding factor is the time of making the payment and/or the presenting of drinks.61

(ii) Another distinguishing characteristic is that, unlike customary arbitration, a decision in a negotiated settlement need not be on the respective merits of the claims of the contestants. In a negotiated settlement "King Solomon is come to judgment." There is give and take and the main aim is to reconcile the parties by offering them what is fair and reasonable in the circumstances. 

(iii)  And yet another difference is that in the case of customary arbitration the award is binding upon the parties whether or not they accept it. An award in negotiated settlement is binding only if accepted by the parties. Normally on the publication of the award the parties return to signify their acceptance and then pay a fee and/or present drinks.

(iv) Lastly, whilst in customary arbitration a party cannot resile once he has voluntarily submitted himself, in a negotiated settlement a party can resile anytime before he formally signifies his acceptance, since before then he is not in law bound.

A N Allott, in his book, Essays in African Law, wrote about Arbitral Proceedings in Customary Law.  After reviewing the previous decisions leading to Kwasi v Larbi62 he submitted with respect that: "In Akan custom .  .  .   either party is free to withdraw before an award is given or until he has accepted the award."  His reason for so submitting is that "the dichotomy between arbitration and negotiated settlement does not exist" and therefore "it is false to draw a distinction between arbitrations and mere attempts to negotiate a settlement."63

With unfeigned respect to Professor Allott whom we salute for his keen interest in customary law of Ghana, we humbly beg to differ. In Kwasi v Larbi64 (supra) the Privy Council dealing with the right to resile said that it was for the appellants to satisfy the Board that a right so contrary to the basic conception of arbitration was recognised by native customary law.  In this they had failed. 

At the time the decision in Kwasi v Larbi was given by the Privy Council the burden of proving customary law was by the provisions of the Courts Ordinance, Cap 4, section 87 (i) on the person who founded his case upon it, unless proof could be dispensed with because the custom was so notorious that the court take judicial notice of it.65  Since 196066 customary law need not be proved as a fact but is now a matter of law and the courts have since then in numerous cases followed the Privy Council decision that a party to an arbitration cannot resile from it.

Allott placed great reliance on two New Juaben unreported cases.

Fordjwuor v Ayakwa67 and Asantewa v Boayire68 for his contention that a party can resile at any time.  However, both the bench and the bar remain uninfluenced by his opinion.  Francois JA (as he then was) reading the unanimous judgment of the Court of Appeal in Mensah v Esah (supra) said:

"The distinction (between arbitration and negotiated settlement) in my view is important as it may well turn the scales between justice and injustice."

It is our submission that the dichotomy is not false as Professor Allott would want us to believe.

SETTING ASIDE AN ARBITRATION AWARD

Customary law provides no remedy for vacating an award. Therefore a person aggrieved by and dissatisfied with an award has to fall on the machinery provided by the English law.  The procedure is English and the remedy is also English.  One indirect way of setting aside an award is for the aggrieved party to wait and raise the invalidity of the arbitration when the successful party tries to enforce the award or rely upon it in a subsequent litigation between the same parties.69

PROCEDURE FOR SETTING ASIDE AN AWARD

The direct method is to go to court to have it set aside. Under Order 72, rule 13 one can apply to have an award set aside for perverseness and misconduct, and by section 26 of the Arbitration Act, 1961 (Act 38), a court has power to set aside an award where: (i) the arbitrator has misconducted himself or the proceedings or both; and (ii) an award was improperly procured. 

Both Order 72, r 13 and section 26 of the Arbitration Act, 1961(Act 38) talk of "an application."  It is our submission that the application is by originating notice of motion or simply "motion on notice" supported by an affidavit.  In Appenteng Mensah v Alpro Industrial Products,70  Hayfron Benjamin J held that it was clear that where a statute provided for an application to be made to the court but did not prescribe the method, such an application could be made by originating notice of motion.

It may be argued that arbitrators act in a judicial capacity and since they are inferior to the judges of the High Court, an order of certiorari will lie to quash their proceedings if they violate the principle of natural justice or of fair adjudication or exceeded their terms of reference or wrongly assumed jurisdiction.71

There are two stages in an application for an order of certiorari, ie ex parte and notice, the cost is more and it is not as expeditious as by a straight originating notice of motion. It is submitted and advised that the better procedure in setting aside an award is by originating notice of motion.

GROUNDS FOR SETTING ASIDE AN AWARD

The following are some grounds for setting aside an award and fall under three headings, namely award, procedure and bias. 

AWARD

(i) where there is no decision on the merits;72

(ii) where the arbitrator exceeded his terms of reference, ie ultra fines compromissi;73 and

(iii) where the award is ambiguous, uncertain and inconsistent.74

PROCEDURE

(iv) where the procedure of the district court was not  followed;75 and

(v) where no proper judicial hearing took place,76 eg taking evidence in the absence of one party or both, making an award without hearing the witnesses or failing to give a party the opportunity of considering the other party's evidence.77

BIAS

(vi) where the arbitrator sat on his own case; or

(vii) where the arbitrator is guilty of bias. Where an  arbitrator is guilty of having pecuniary or proprietary interest or likelihood of bias, the proceedings will be set aside even though his colleagues who sat with him were not so disqualified.78

It must be remembered that in an arbitration (unlike court proceedings where parties choose the court but not the judge or magistrate) parties choose the arbitrator for weal or for woe, "for better and for worse both as to decisions of fact and decisions of law."79 Hence there is very little or nothing a party can do to set aside an award on that ground. In this respect what Lord Reid said in Republic v The Governors of Brixton Prisons; Ex parte Kwesi Ammah80 is also true of an arbitrator. There His Lordship pointed out that if he had jurisdiction to go right he had jurisdiction to go wrong. Neither error of fact nor law would destroy his jurisdiction.

REMOVAL OF AN ARBITRATOR

There are no reported cases on an application for removal of an arbitrator, but this does not mean that such an application will not be made sometime.  The question that must be considered here is whether such a right is known to customary law.  According to the learned editors of Halsbury's Laws of England,81  (4th ed), Vol 2, para 587, at common law the court has no jurisdiction to remove an arbitrator or umpire but by the provisions of the Arbitration Act, 195082 of the United Kingdom and our local Arbitration Act, 1961 (Act 38) where an arbitrator or umpire has misconducted himself or the proceedings or both he may be removed by an order of the High Court.  It is our submission that if an application were to be made not under the Act but under customary law such an application would on principle have to be entertained because customary law imposes upon an arbitrator the duty to act judicially and to observe the principle of natural justice  of nemo judex in causa sua and audi alteram partem.

It is submitted further that customary law as said by Sir Branford Griffiths CJ in Yerenchi v Akuffo,83 "generally consists of the performance of the reasonable in the special circumstances of the particular case." It will thus not refuse to afford a remedy to a disputant against a misbehaving arbitrator during the proceedings.  It will not make sense to make an award after the arbitration has been completed and after time and energy have been wasted only to set it aside. It will be contrary to plain commonsense for the remedy to be available only when the arbitration is completed and not before and during proceedings. 

EFFECT OF ENGLISH LAW ON CUSTOMARY ARBITRATION

We have mentioned earlier that customary law has no machinery for the removal of an arbitrator, setting aside an arbitration award and enforcement of an arbitration award.  To do any of these a party to a customary arbitration has to resort to the normal courts where the procedure and the remedies are English. The judges (both expatriate and Ghanaian) who in the past sat on these cases were English-trained all having been products of the four honourable Inns of Court in Britain.  It is no wonder that the only distinguishing characteristics left or remaining between statutory arbitration84 and customary arbitration is the existence or non-existence in writing of the arbitration agreement.  All other ingredients as publication of an award, the non-existence of the right to resile, the observance of the principle of natural justice apply equally to both.  English law has as it were guided the footsteps of the customary arbitration to its present position.

Thus it can be said that customary arbitration instead of wearing "batakari" or "kente" now appears in a three piece suit without a bowler hat and an umbrella. 

CODIFICATION OF CUSTOMARY ARBITRATION

In discussing codification of a branch of customary law we are not oblivious of the statement in 1909 made by Osborne CJ (Nigeria) in Lewis v Bankole85 when His Lordship delivered himself as follows:

"I have during thirteen years experience of West Africa been concerned in one capacity or another with several cases in which native customary law has been the subject of judicial investigation; and in nearly every case I have found that there are general underlying principles not difficult to understand, and obviously based on the primitive requirements of the community.  In some instances those principles have been modified, and even departed from, as the result of contact with European methods; indeed, one of the most striking features of West African native custom, to my mind, is its flexibility; it appears to have been always subject to motives of expediency, and it shows unquestionable adaptability to altered circumstances without entirely losing its individual characteristics.  The great danger in applying it in this Court is that of crystallising it in such a way that it cannot he departed from in cases where expediency demands, and where natives themselves would depart from it; ..."

Recently, this country has seen the codification of the customary law of intestate succession, the Ewe son hailed it whilst the Akan nephew condemned it.  One of the benefits of codification is to make the law certain and easily accessible "to all whom it may concern. "

It is our submission that the customary law of arbitration has developed to a stage that it can conveniently be codified without any of the side effects feared by Osborne CJ.  Professor Allott in his Essays in African Law86 drafted a model ordinance of customary arbitration which makes very interesting reading.  He starts with the definition of "arbitral proceedings," "arbiter," after which he deals with adjudicating authority, arbitral proceedings, effect of arbitral proceedings and then enforcement of arbitral awards.  The Model Ordinance is useful as perhaps the first attempt at codifying the rules of customary arbitration.  It unfortunately does not go far enough to deal with some of the problems that one has been encountering in practice involving the principles of customary arbitration.  It's reference to "native courts" is anarchronistic.  It is, however, on the whole, a useful starting point for codification of the principles of customary arbitration.  In enacting a statute to regulate customary arbitration there are two choices.  Either to repeal the Arbitration Act, 1961 (Act 38) and then enact a law embracing both common law and customary arbitration, or enact a law dealing solely with customary arbitration.

The advantages of the first choice is that we shall have one law or statute to refer to.  In addition, since both common law and customary arbitration have common features such as removal of an arbitrator, setting aside an arbitration award and enforcement of arbitration award, it is easy to marry the two forms of arbitration.  It may also be argued by purists that by the merger customary arbitration will lose its identity.  We would, however, humbly advocate a comprehensive arbitration law to which one can turn to find all the answers to the problems relating to arbitration.  One would further advocate that the law of negotiated settlement be embodied in the proposed arbitration law. 

 Lastly, but not the least, we would advocate that all customary arbitration awards and those of negotiated settlements where parties have accepted the awards should be registered with the nearest district court.  Experience has shown that when issues of arbitration come before the courts for adjudication, where the parties agree that there was an arbitration, both parties give different versions of the award and as often happens, each party gets a member of the arbitration panel to support him.  Registration of an award signed by the panel members and the parties will put an end to this practice.  Another advantage of registration is that at the time an arbitration award is being contested, none of the panel members may be alive and parties with dim memories of what took place many years ago may give different versions of the award.  A record of registration of award will serve as a prima facie evidence of what took place. 

CUSTOMARY ARBITRATION IN A CHANGING SOCIETY

Customary arbitration in its traditional setting served a useful purpose. It was a system that served the needs of a relatively simple, unsophisticated society which lived together and saw the problems of the other as the problems of the community to be settled in the interest of peace and harmony within the community.  At a certain stage in its evolution, however, disputes arising within the traditional society were referred to the district commissioner who either referred them to the local chief (or in the most serious cases to the paramount chief) for settlement by means of arbitration, or advised that the matter be referred to the courts for adjudication. The chief regarded the reference of disputes to them by the district commissioner as an honour and therefore did all that was necessary to achieve a fair and equitable settlement of disputes referred to them.  There was also, of course, the further point that the performance of the chief was an important factor which was taken into account by the district commissioner in making his recommendations for "promotion" or the conferment of the chief's or the Governor's medal.

It is a matter of regret that customary arbitration is less resorted to now.  But, with the withdrawal or cessation of the incentives to a fair adjudication referred to, this is not surprising.  Where the adjudication is fair and the award is equitable, the parties to a customary arbitration will tend to be happy to resort to arbitration notwithstanding that there is no right of appeal.  Human nature being what it is, no one would risk being a loser if he has cause to believe that there will not be fair adjudication of his case.  Hence, the present situation where litigants would prefer to send their cases to the regular courts and lawyers frequently advising their clients against resort to customary arbitration. 

 It is our earnest hope that the Law Reform Commission will soon embark on a thorough examination of the advantages of customary arbitration procedures with a view to codifying the law in this respect.
