THE RULE IN ANGU V ATTAH REVISITED [1987-88] VOL. XVI  RGL 281—292

DAVID A NII-APONSAH

 

Some thirty years ago, the legislature of Ghana carried away by its chauvinistic nationalism, and flushed with the then recent victory over colonialism, purported to abolish the rule in Angu v Attah1 which had vexed those nationalists by its supposedly inane "treatment" of Ghanaians' own cherished customary law as "foreign law," whilst treating the received English law as rather being the common law in Ghana. This was regarded as the travesty of the true position since the latter was rather the guest of the indigenous customary law.

It was the celebrated Sarbah who once wrote2 that “. . . Customary Law or usage does not become native law by its having been judicially noticed in a suit."

The courts of Sarbah's day, however, took a reverse view, and proclaimed as Smith J was to do in Hughes v Davies and Another3 only a year before Sarbah's death that "[A]s native law is foreign law it must be proved as any other fact." Rejecting that misunderstood view in his Fante Customary Law, Sarbah had this to say:

"It cannot, therefore, be correct to say, as has sometimes been said, that the native laws and customs are foreign matters which, unless proved, cannot be recognised or noticed by a judge."4

It will be recalled that in Angu v Attah (supra) the Privy Council had stated:

"As is the case with all customary law, it has to he proved in the first instance by calling witnesses acquainted with the native customs until the particular custom have, by frequent proof in the courts become so notorious that the courts take judicial notice of them."

Of this, the honourable Azu Crabbe said in his book John Mensah Sarbah 1864-1910 as follows:

“It seems to follow from this decision that a particular customary law continues to be a question of fact until a firm principle has emerged through consistent decisions in a long line of cases.

The principle in Angu v Attah has only to be stated to realise what a startling proposition it is."5

Unfortunately, one continues reading in vain to identify the author's reason for considering the principle of Angu v Attah to be a "startling proposition."

With so much outrage in the hearts of most nationalists against that mischaracterisation of native customary law as exotic law, it came as no surprise when in 1960 the First Republican Parliament purported to sound its death-knell. One would not be far from right to aver that the rationale behind the purported abolition of the rule in Angu v Attah was precisely the one projected by the leader of that Parliament, President Kwame Nkrumah, in the following words of his address at the formal opening of the Accra Conference of Legal Education and of the Ghana Law School on 4 January 1963. On that august occasion, the late ex-President said:

"African law in Africa was declared foreign law for the convenience of colonial administration which found the administration of justice cumbersome by reason of the vast variations in local and tribal customs. African law had to be proved by experts. But no law can be foreign in its own land and country and African lawyers, particularly in the independent African States, must quickly find a way to reverse this judicial travesty."6

No right-thinking Ghanaian nationalist can fail to agree with the late ex-President on the sentiments he expressed with characteristic eloquence. But enough with sentiments.

Dr Nkrumah objectively and, with respect, correctly diagnosed or isolated the reason for the inception of the rule as being "the vast variations in local and tribal customs."

Five questions therefore arise in respect of the raison d'etre so accurately identified:

1. At the time the sentimental call for abolition of the rule in Angu v Attah, had the vast variations in local and tribal customs ceased to exist?

2. Has the purported statutory abolition brought about a unification or uniformity of the pre-existing variegated indigenous laws since 1960, when the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372), sections 49 and 50 purported to reverse the alleged "judicial travesty?"

3. Did the mere parliamentary edict that it had been abolished in fact abrogate the rule root and branch?

4. Was Angu v Attah consistently and dogmatically applied during its heydays?

5. Has the purported abolition caused any miscarriage of justice anywhere?

These questions will be examined in the order in which they appear above. The irresistible answer to the first question and indeed to the second too is that the "vast variations in local and tribal customs" that gave birth to the rule in Angu v Attah have never ceased to exist either before or after the so-called abrogation thereof. It must be conceded, however, that not only has the abolition provided greater opportunity for development of common legal notions, but that there is also a steady movement towards uniformity.

But until now Akan law has not become assimilated by reason of the abolition into say, Ga, Ewe, Dagbani, Nzema, Dagaare or any other law, though some false starts have been attempted. These and the others continue substantially to remain in their separate and independent compartments. It is impossible to gainsay these facts objectively, although it cannot also be disputed that, not only the administration of all the variants in one system of courts, but also the physical interactions between the various ethnic groups themselves have operated to produce a certain hybrid which is no longer representative of the primordial component customs of which it is made. An example that readily comes to mind is the complexity of Ga Mashi which has become the veritable vortex of most of the various ethnic groups of Ghana. The variations here remain in full cry, hence it is submitted there remains the continued need for the principle in Angu v Attah.

The answer to the third question is that the legislature only dressed up a living body with the shroud of a corpse; and purported to have buried it. This can be gleaned easily from the machinery set up by the law maker, in cases of doubt, for the ascertainment of the particular customary law involved in any one particular case.7 The legislation envisions, inter alia, an examination of the opinions of experts, traditional elders, books by persons specialising in those branches of learning, etc, and at the end of the day what crystallises out of such a search should be classified as "law" and not as "facts" on which a proclamation of the law might be founded.

Thus by mere legislative edict, what in essence is no more than evidence gained from research, is to sell as law. It is submitted, however, that that enactment shows clearly that the legislators had misconceived, and over-reacted to, the analogy drawn between their cherished customary law and foreign law. It was only a metaphor and only an analogy intended to highlight what is to be done in the ascertainment of the customary law. It is submitted that all that Angu v Attah did show was that though the customary law had originated definitely in our soil, the modus,7a for identifying it should be the same as that adopted for the finding of laws that originated from foreign lands. What other approach could be more probative or more objective rather than subjective, and therefore more satisfactory?

The legislators and nationalists seemed to have lost sight of the mere point of comparison of methods and had spent their sentiments upon the mere fact that the one has just been likened to other foreign laws. The fact, however, that one thing is likened to the other does not make the one thing to become the other. Thus, likening Ga customary law to French law so far as its ascertainment is concerned does not make it French law, nor is it intended to say that it is in fact French law. Had the matter been appreciated in this light, it would have been easily realised that the said legislation was pointless. In fact, in its operation it has brought distortions and drawbacks in the administration of justice, as we shall expound subsequently.

The fourth question to be examined is whether or not even during its heydays the rule in Angu v Attah was faithfully and consistently applied. At least, in four Ga Mashi cases the courts showed some inconsistency in the application of the rule in Ankrah v Aryeh;8 Sackey v Okantah;9 Sackeyfio v Tagoe10 and Amarfio v Ayorkor.11

In Ankrah v Aryeh, a case involving Ga Mashi intestate succession, the Privy Council emphasised the rule in Angu v Attah by saying:

"There is not before their Lordships a series of decisions from which any relevant principles have crystallised out in the manner indicated in the case of Kobina Angu v Cudjoe Attah."

In Sackey v Okantah, the majority of witnesses deposed to the prima facie patrilineal character of Ga Mashi inheritance, followed at a rather secondary stage by uterine inheritance; but despite the preponderance of the evidence adduced in that case, the court nevertheless refused to act on it. One may justify the court's refusal by pointing to the fact that one swallow does not make the fully-ripe summer that Angu v Attah had contemplated; therefore a preponderance of evidence in one case does not amount to the "series of decisions" which the rule called for as explained in Ankrah v Aryeh.

Sackeyfio v Tagoe and Amarfio v Ayorkor, two Ga Mashi cases, concerned the right of children to inherit the deceased fathers' estate in the absence of any parent or sibling of his. Although there was not a series of Ga Mashi decisions as contemplated by the Angu’s rule, out of which any principle of matrilineal succession might as at that stage be said to have crystallised, the courts went out on their own motion and relied on Sarbah11a (who did not purport to be an authority on Ga Mashi custom) to impose a matrilineal principle of succession on the plaintiffs, i.e. children of the intestate. The foregoing examples go to show that even in the heydays of the rule the courts, with respect, indulged their own notions of what seemed right to them and did not consistently follow the rule, as the Hon Azu Crabbe noted in his book on Sarbah.

Justification for the Rule

Since the foreign judges administering the law before and after Angu v Attah were not personally menticultured in "the vast variations in local and tribal customs",12 the rule in Angu v Attah was absolutely indispensable if biased or intellectually dishonest impositions on the innate innocence of the foreign judges of our local customs were to be minimised, if not altogether prevented. Now, with Ghanaian judges administering the law, has that danger of taking the judges for a ride ceased to exist? The answer seems still to be negative. With the greatest respect, most of our judges cannot honestly claim a thorough personal acquaintance with more than one system of "the vast variations of local and tribal customs." Where a system other than the one with which a judge is personally familiar comes for application by him, therefore, he will be bound to apply a modus probandi analogous to, if not identical with, the manner of proof stipulated by the rule in Angu v Attah.

What, one may therefore ask, has been the practical effect of the purported abolition of the Angu modus probandi? To say, for instance, that X was a lion in an enterprise did not mean that X was transformed into the leonine beast. The misunderstanding of the nationalists including Sarbah and Dr Nkrumah lay in supposing that the analogy with foreign law actually equates or classifies indigenous law with foreign law. It is submitted that it was the similar methods for their ascertainment which the rule compared, not that it sought to relegate indigenous law to the background or, as Dr Nkrumah would put it, make it "foreign law in its own land and country."

If the opposite view were the correct one, then it would follow that the taking of judicial notice of a local customary law would amount in effect to its indigenisation. But, as Sarbah himself observed: ". . . Customary law or usage does not become native law by its having been judicially noticed."

It is submitted that those who compared customary law with foreign law were not unaware of the possibility of the de facto existence of the former "in its own land and country." What they could not have known fully well was its nature, scope and effect. The fact that they conceded or assumed the possibility of its existence is established by the further fact that they entertained the proof thereof at all. If they had proceeded on the hypothesis that a particular customary law could not possibly exist, the likelihood was that they would not have entertained any offer of evidence to prove it. They would have barred in advance any attempt to have the existence of such a custom proved. Their attitude was that, not being conversant with its existence, nature, scope, effect, niceties, or applicability, they could not be committed to one view or version or the other thereof, unless and until the foregoing facets had been established to their satisfaction in the same way as they would require foreign law of which they honestly assumed ignorance to be established cogently and satisfactorily.

To have construed or understood that honest and indispensable judicial policy as a classification or an identification of indigenous law with foreign law or as a relegation of customary law to the stature of a foreign, uncivilised or undesirable system of law was therefore to have missed the point and the essence of the comparison. As already noted, the essence of the analogy was the similarity of the manner of proof of foreign law and of local custom. But the critics lost sight of this point of analogy. No wonder the nationalists over-reacted to that misleading impression of "treating" our customs as strangers in their own terrain owing to insufficient literature on the rationale, effect, and intendment of the rule in Angu v Attah.12a But the greater wonder is that, in purporting to abolish that rule, the legislature made provisions that can only be honestly adjudged as a concession to the indispensability and continued necessity for at least an analogous modus petendi, discendi atque cognoscendi or manner of discovery.

If the courts were to be able to apply Angu v Attah consistently; they could not dispense with the view of the Privy Council earlier referred to herewith in connection with Ankrah v Aryeh and also in the case of Larinde and Ors v Akifo and Ors13 where the West African Court of Appeal pertinently observed that "[t]he decision of one case in 1892 cannot be said to be frequent proof enabling the courts to take judicial notice of the particular custom." The failure to observe this rule with the fidelity that it required naturally resulted in partial and inconsistent decisions and consequent miscarriage of justice. Attention can now therefore be drawn at this stage to the fifth question relating to the ways in which the abolition of the rule has led to miscarriage of justice.

To begin with, it may be recalled that the effect of the abolition of the rule is that the courts are expected (though unacceptably) to assume the existence and knowledge of the customary laws; nevertheless they are to apply the provisions for ascertainment where they are not sure. However, for certain ethnic groups they are to presume and uniformly apply certain principles, such as those of the matrilineal or the patrilineal systems of inheritances.13a  Because of those hard and fast presumptions which the abolition of the rule has undesirably encouraged, and even tempted the courts to make in such places as Ga Mashi and Fanteland, it is rather dogmatically posited by the courts that no principle of succession can be entertained by them other than the matrilineal system, even though there might be cogent proof of another system operating.

As Professor Kwamena Bentsi-Enchill points out in his major work, Ghana Land Law,14 in Ga Mashi there are some families which inherit patrilineally, others matrilineally and still others in a mixed form. The present author as a Ga Mashi man would endorse that finding of fact without any fear of contradiction. In fact, an elementary and objective knowledge of the nature of that community, i.e. the irrefutable cosmopolitan nature of that community, lends full weight to that conclusion. In spite of this empirically verifiable fact, the courts and some jurists choose, with great respect, to pay obeisance to ill-conceived dogmas that negate the sacred facts about the heterogeneous composition and consequent plurality of our systems of inheritance and also fail to have regard to or insight into the implications of the unique Ga Kpodziemo ritual as to the right of inheritance of a person so “out-doored."15 In this regard, it would be useful for judicial notice be taken of Lord Dunedin's reminder that "the mere citation of authorities is inimical to clear decision" and of the equally pertinent, admonition by Lord Wright that "[a] good judge is one who is the master and not the slave of his authorities?"15a

It is submitted that in the face of the heterogeneity and the unique implications of the Kpodziemo ritual, the following decided cases that slammed the door on the rights of Ga Mashi children were wrongly adjudged (Kwaku v Sacker;16 Larkai v Amorkor;17 Wellington v Quartey-Papafio and Anor;18 Amarfio v Ayorkor;19 Tagoe and Anor v Randolph;20 Amarteifio v Ankrah;21 Sackeyfio v Tagoe22 and Baddoo v Baddoo23) in that they denied the right of children entirely, but were correct in the ultimate result by giving the priority of succession to a full brother of the children's deceased Ga Mashi father. Other misleading judgments were Tetteh v Kotei,24 Addy v Addy;25 Adams v Lamley;26 Kotey v Kotey;27 Honger v Badger and Ors28 and Captan v Yebuah Nukpa.29 They erred because the implications of the Kpodziemo custom completely eluded them as it eluded the great Ollennu himself.

Before leaving off the misapplication of presumptions of law to Ga Mashi and the encouragement which the abolition of the rule in Angu v Attah has given for the continued application of those ill- conceived stare decisis, one should note the following germane observation made by Professor A N Allott in his New Essays in Africa Law:30

"In the old days African communities were self-sufficient integrated societies all of whose members shared a common blood and origin, and were automatically subjected to the same customary law. Certain communities were polyethnic . . . These circumstances no longer prevail. Persons of mixed descent present the first problem . . ."

Who could deny that the foregoing describes the Ga Mashi social milieu as much as any other community which equally now bristles with "persons of mixed descent?"

In the Fante community too, it appears that before and after the abolition of the rule in Angu v Attah, the law has been dogmatically premised on a thorough-going matrilineal principle. With the greatest respect, the celebrated Sarbah who set out on purpose to promote better understanding of his people's customs and institutions in one respect really failed to reflect the true Fante practice envinced by Halmond v Daniel.31

There it was established that, despite the theory by which pedigree was traced matrilineally, in practice on the strength of a countervailing maxim, to wit, "the father dies and leaves his house to the son," there definitely existed a custom whereby possessory title (as contradistinguished with a legal estate) inhered in the son, subject to good behaviour, and that the son enjoyed precedence in the enjoyment of that possessory title. Why that celebrated author failed textually to give as much, if not greater, prominence to that usage as he gave to matrilineal succession is difficult to understand. As a result of Sarbah's unintended misrepresentation of the true facts about the niceties in the operation of the Fante matrilineal system, the application of his writings to his own people has culminated in divesting the majority of entitled persons of their patrimony, whenever issue was joined thereon. Nor has the harm stopped there. It was, with respect, unwisely extended to other systems which were also mistakenly believed, through want of originality and of adequate literature on the subject, to be matrilineal communities as well, despite the warning of an equally learned and authoritative Fante, and historically distinguished as the first African Chief Justice of Ghana, in the following apposite terms from the judgment of Abina Kunkuma v Kwame Achemfo Yeboah.32

"While it may be true that Sarbah’s monumental work recorded general principles of customary laws applicable to the Akan tribes of the Gold Coast, it is acknowledged that there are variations of the customs peculiar to the several tribes."

The continued presumption of a particular system, despite other evidence to the contrary, thus produces pernicious results such as the one produced in Adoko v Larbie;33 where on the inspiration of such an assumption van Lare J, despite the concession of a possessory title made by Fante law to the children of a deceased person, came out with the innocuous-looking but divestive conclusion:

"The plaintiffs may have some Ga connections and may even be Gas on their maternal side, but their late father was a Fante and their rights if any in respect of this farm can only be decided in accordance with Fante custom."

The emphasis is "in accordance with Fante custom" which, because the learned judge apparently presumed to be simply matrilineal, he applied in denying the children of the deceased their rights, whatever they might be, in their own father's estate. This was despite the evidence aliunde of the concession of possessory title to the children as made by Fante law itself. Whose system of law then do the courts, with respect, pretend to apply —a judicial customary law conceivable only to the courts or the systems that command the assent of the native community which according to Lord Atkin (a distinguished judge) gives customs the force of law? As the learned Lord Justice stated in Eleko v Officer Administering the Government of Nigeria.34

"It is the assent of the native community that gives a custom its validity and therefore, barbarous or mild, it must be shown to be recognised by the native community whose conduct it is supposed to regulate."

What is justice then, if Fante custom grants a possessory title to a deceased person's realty to his children, whereas the courts upon encouragement given by the abolition of Angu v Attah should presume Fante customary law to deny children any right in their own deceased father's estate?34a It is strongly submitted that these continue, in full cry, to be burning questions, because the Intestate Succession Law35 has reserved portions of a person's estate to descend according to the customary law of the particular community to which the deceased had belonged. Therefore these questions are still relevant today as ever they were.

A similar injustice would transpire if a blanket application of the matrilineal principle is made to every resident in Ashanti. Thus in Omane v Poku36 such an application was reversed by the Court of Appeal on the ground that it was simply out of place in that case, so far as its unique circumstances showed. If we turn our attention to the application of the patrilineal principle in, say, Adangme and Ewe areas, too, we are able to detect, with respect, similar blind adherence to principle; what Dr J B Danquah called "unbending conservatism for its own sake." In those areas, children37 only succeed; and the inheritance of a female issue of a deceased person does not pass over on the death of that female inheritor to her own children but rather to her agnatically related brothers or nephews. In practice, there are instances where the families of the deceased in these areas have given priority to either brothers or sisters38 of the deceased as against children or even to maternal relations;39 so there are other cases where women's issue have been allowed to inherit the property of their ancestresses. Why the writers from those areas wish to obscure or give a different interpretation to those clearly established instances is very difficult to appreciate. It may, however, be conceded that the expostulations of those writers have their values for education, comment, rectification or re-orientation of the law, if they be not regarded as conclusive authorities.

Since the legal presumptions harboured or conceived for certain areas had existed before the abolition of Angu v Attah, it would not be factually correct or fair to attribute the dogmatic application of the customary law to the abolition of the rule. The most that can be asserted with realism therefore, is that its abolition could give fillip to the imposition of the rules of the customary law in a more doctrinaire and blanket fashion than has the case before, unless the courts exercise greater vigilance. This is where the critical danger lies. The rights of minorities are likely to be prejudiced or disregarded by over-generalisation of the rules of law, in particular, of the customary law for, inter alia, some of the reasons above noted by Professor Allott40 in connection with polyethnic societies.41

CONCLUSION

It may be concluded that amongst the unseen effects of the abolition of the rule in Angu v Attah are:

(a)  There has been a tendency to over-generalise the rules of the customary law to the detriment of deviant systems of families, eg. Ameoda v Pordier;42 Vanderpuye v Botchway.43

(b) The rule itself has shown a remarkable resilience, viability and indestructibility and now rules from its supposed grave and its apparition steals daily into the courts to haunt them.

(c)  It is engendering uniformity but in reality it is an artificial conformity in some cases.

(d)  It is making the application of the law easier but, at the same time, it is prompting juristic indolence or indiligence which cannot but have its ill-effect on the quality of the law available for application by the judges.

As it is well said, an energetic and vigilant Bar is a lever to the Bench. The easy surrender to general principles might conduce to slipshoddiness44 and carelessness, and also undermine the zeal for research and the quest for that true principle which gives the practice of the law its integrity and vitality and also fosters the concern by each to be his brother's keeper.45

With this survey, it is hoped that the courts will guard with greater vigilance against those pitfalls to which man is heir. On no account should the cause of uniformity or conformity override the cause of intrinsic justice for all.
