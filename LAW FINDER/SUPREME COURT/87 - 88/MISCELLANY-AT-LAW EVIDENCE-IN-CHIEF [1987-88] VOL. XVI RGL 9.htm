MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: EVIDENCE-IN-CHIEF [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 95—105

by Aidoo E S, GM

 

A legal practitioner asked to address judges on any matter connected with trials does so, as I do now, with some perturbation of mind. But that is nothing compared with the often clearly visible trepidation with which a witness (whether a party or not) usually enters the box to give evidence in a case proceeding in a court of law. It is easy for those of us who are familiar with the court environment to overlook the hostile and threatening atmosphere which it generates. Some effort should therefore be made by us, both judges and practitioners, to pay some attention to that fact. Another thing we are often reminded of is that in addressing judges, especially when arguing appeals, we should not lecture them. Superior court judges are of course all appellate judges. So this paper will endeavour not to sound as a lecture—we will almost be chatting. And we will not be considering the rules of law on our subject in any detail. Afterall the law, as we are all presumed to know, is in the bosoms of your lordships and ladyships.

Now, a witness who comes to court to give evidence begins by being led through an examination-in-chief. The object here, according to the books, is “to obtain testimony in support of the version of the facts in issue or relevant to the issue for which the party calling the witness contends.” (Speaker’s emphasis.) It is at this point that he narrates all of what he knows of those facts which the counsel who called him considers relevant and otherwise important. And if he has obtained any documents or instruments or other such materials that will support the version of the facts being presented, he tenders those too in evidence to the tribunal. However, in this discussion paper we will concern ourselves not so much with an examination or critique of the relevant laws as with how the examination-in-chief should be conducted by the persons involved in the proceedings of helping in or making the decisions in particular cases.

For in examination-in-chief, counsel’s first concern is: in what order may I call my witnesses so that my client’s case will be presented to its best advantage? Fortunately, at this point the law presents no problem—counsel may call his witnesses in any order that seems likely to be satisfactory to him (and his client). When the witness is in the box, counsel’s next concern shows: how should the witness tell his story? A lot of persons knowledgeable in such matters who comment on this, recommend that the witness should be made to tell his story in a chronological order. No doubt this is true in the majority of cases. There may, however, be now and again an occasion when it will be better not to follow this advice. Then it will show how carefully counsel has prepared. Many are the persons who suppose that examination-in-chief is very easy. It is easy, certainly, to do it badly. And I have seen occasions when counsel has done an examination-in-chief and sat down with apparent satisfaction when several of us at the bar (hearing that witness for the first time, like the judge and/or jury) were not clear what version of the facts was meant to be conveyed, and we have wondered whether the judge and/or jury have got that. Of course it is the case that a witness should be left to give his own evidence, since, as we know, counsel is not allowed to put leading questions in evidence-in-chief. But we also know that at all times it is desirable that the witness should remain under counsel’s control. Merely to say “will you now tell your story?” or “will you now give your evidence?” is not enough. One soon learns in court that the witness who is left to tell his own story does not—and that which he tells becomes a tangled skein. Counsel therefore soon learns that it is wise to take his witness up to the essential points and only then leave him to give his account, taking care to interrupt (although, hopefully, not so as to put him off his stride) in order to bring him back under control so as to maintain his account precisely and chronologically. It is almost essential for counsel to phrase his questions simply and shortly, and take care to put only one question at a time. But, as your lordships and your ladyships very well know, we in practice usually find this very very difficult to do.

Probably the most important concern of counsel is that of leading questions. It is common knowledge that you are not allowed to put leading questions, but you may in general terms do so:

(i) in respect of interlocutory matters—such things as the name, address and description of your witness;

(ii) as to matters which are clearly not in dispute;

(iii) as to matters which have (if the court agrees with you) been already sufficiently proved; and

(iv) to secure an express denial of an allegation.

Fortunately there are many books which deal with leading questions, and so we need not let it detain us.

There are other concerns with which counsel grapples, even though some manage to conceal them from the judge. I shall put some examples in brief questions only at this stage: (1) how many counsel deal with the frequently recurring situation where a witness fails to come up to his proof? and (2) has the other side anything on counsel’s witness? At any rate, is it not wiser to bring out any point which may be adverse to one’s case in examination-in-chief, rather than leave it to one’s opponent to bring it out with greater effect in cross-examination? These apart from the many somewhat uncertain rules of evidence apply to trials in court.

Let me touch on a matter that the books do not often deal with, for obvious reasons. It must be annoying, to the court surely, to flaunt certain mannerisms which are meant to achieve undeserved advantage or merely emphasise the importance, in his own view, of the person doing the examination-in-chief. Sir David Napley in his little book The Technique of Persuasion (1st ed), 1970, p. 7 gives an example by way of advice:

“Do not repeat—as do some advocates—the points the witness gives. The court will have heard the answer for itself, and if it has not done so it will ask the witness to repeat it. Echoes may be fascinating in the Tyrolean foothills, but they are tedious in a court of law.”

Or does the fact that out judges take things down in longhand make such repetitions necessary? There are many other modes of eliciting evidence-in-chief which one may criticise. In order, however, not to be too personal about persons who are still in practice and much in view, I have decided, if you permit me, to quote at some length from an address delivered about 60 years ago before the Association of the Bar of the City of New York by Emory R. Buckner:

“. . .I think the mistake in direct examination which I have most often noticed is the tendency for the lawyer to make himself too much of a witness. You must understand that courts and juries discount our statements because we are supposed to be partisans. We are professionals. If the lawyer conducting the direct examination asks too many leading questions, if his questions are too long, if he talks too much, he is simply using the witness to confirm his statements. What he ought to do is to get the court and the jury interested in the witness. He should create an impression that he as well as the court and jury is very much interested in the story and is following it with equal attention. Lawyers commit this error because of their over-enthusiasm, because they think they can state the case better than the witness, although the fact is they cannot, or because they are conscious of putting on a performance and are thinking more of the effect of their performance than they are of the effect of the testimony of the witness.

A man throughout a trial should try to subordinate himself to his case. It is hard to explain how this is to be done except to advise constantly thinking of that relationship. He cannot do this if he is glancing all the time into the mirror. He must be enthusiastic about his case and his witnesses and not enthusiastic about himself.

He should regard his case as an opportunity to have justice done and not as an opportunity for himself to demonstrate his skill. He should try the case in such a way that the jurymen will say: “That other fellow is a better lawyer but this fellow had the better case.” I think that the greatest compliment a lawyer can receive is to have the jury think that his opponent is a better lawyer than he but that he is fortunate in having the better side of the case.

I cannot emphasise too strongly the importance of the lawyer’s taking himself out of the case as much as possible. He should take out his capacity as a wit. He should take out his gift at repartee. He should take everything out of the case which has relation to his own vanity and should put into the case everything which will help lift his client into the jury box or lift his client up beside the judge sitting in equity for a personal chat.”

(See his paper “The Trial of Cases” published in The Lawyer’s Treasury, 395 at pp 404—405).

With this in the background we are now ready to look at some of the things legal practitioners expect judges to do and some of the things they expect judges not to do, during examination-in-chief of witnesses that counsel has called. But before then let us clear one small cause of confusion, and perhaps mention another. The one to clear is the insistence of several judges that their part in the process is to find out the truth. Finding out the truth is undoubtedly a noble thing. But we have neither the financial nor other resources to undertake that. As one writer in a common law jurisdiction has put it, “trial procedure itself, civil or criminal, is not conducive to the ascertainment of truth and may be said to be inconsistent with it,” aside if the psychological and emotional considerations affecting witnesses and others of the participants. (See the Hon Mr. Justice R W Fox in Well and Truly Tried, Law Book Company Limited, 1982, p. 151). He was speaking of the position in Australia in particular; and I believe we in Ghana have even much less of the relevant resources. In this context he goes on to quote Sir Owen Dixon as once remarking extra-judicially that “the object of the parties is always victory, not abstract truth” (of O Dixon, Jesting Pilate (1965) p. 16). Lord Devlin too seems to concur in this (see his lecture on “The Judge in the Adversary System” published in the The Judge [1979] at p. 54) I do respectfully agree with those statements, although another judge whom I admire much has said to the contrary, that the trail judge’s “object, above all, is to find out the truth, and do justice according to law” (see Denning LJ in Jones v National Coal Board [1957] 2 QB 55 at 63). My contention is not that the search for the truth should always be abandoned. It is that judges should be rather careful in how they decide whether to admit or exclude evidence at this stage with which we are now concerned. However curious a witness’ version may sound it does not help for the judge to say, as I have heard said on at least one occasion, “who will believe this?” Certainly not at the examination-in-chief stage. When Issiah the prophet asked in the Holy Bible “who hath believed our report?” (see Isaiah chapter 53, verse 1) he was, in fact, insisting that his report was absolutely true. In any case where a judge has to reject or exclude testimony at this stage it helps little (except in a very very small minority of cases) to tell the witness giving it and his counsel that “that testimony is simply untrue.” If the judge is then relying in another source of information or a not-too-familiar statement of a rule or some other consideration he should at least be open about it. As Mr. Justice Fox puts it at page 176 of his book:

“Expediency plays an important part in the content and operation of our rules; greater than I had realised. The search for truth is a casualty of the rules of evidence, and the rules of procedure generally. The decree to which this is so also surprise me; the present system pays regard to other values, and it would be helpful if they could all be revealed and evaluated. . .”

It is an interesting advance that the official Commentary on our Evidence Decree, 1975, goes a long way in admitting much of this—but I cannot say that respect for precedents has allowed our judges to follow the guidelines laid down there by the legislature.

The matter I wish to mention is that the rules of relevance, like several rules of procedure and evidence, do present traps to the practitioner, even the experienced one. According to the law, now codified in section 51 of our Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323), all relevant evidence is admissible, subject to the rules of exclusion (or rather the exercise of the judge’s discretion to exclude any), and it is only relevant evidence that is admissible. This section is, as the Commentary puts it, the cornerstone of the law of evidence. But it is not intended to be a stumbling block to anyone. In the minority of cases where real difficulties arise the judges are meant to assist counsel and witnesses (as many of our judges already do) to present the evidence required and not to frustrate them by making odd rulings and adding that “you can always take the case upstairs.” This is clear from the recognition by our lawmakers that there is a:

“. . . basic antagonism in the law of evidence—the desire on the one hand to have all logically probative evidence presented to the tribunal of fact, and on the other hand the need to determine disputes quickly, fairly and finally. Accordingly the law of evidence, in the main, can be seen as one rule admitting all logically probative evidence and a series of rules providing for the exclusion of logically probative evidence for reasons of policy.” (See The Commentary, p. 32.) (Speaker’s emphasis.)

In any trial in court the ascertainment of the relevant facts (as usually pointed at even if not determined, in civil cases, by the parties) begins (as I have already briefly stated) with the evidence-in-chief as far as concerns each witness. Normally what counsel would wish for is that the judge allows his witness to give his version of the facts as pre-determined by counsel. If the judge interferes at this stage the witness is almost always confused. You see, the question of what is relevant to be proved or material to an issue is, as Mr. Justice Fox explains, is one of judgment, and not simply of logic. It is a matter upon which minds can readily differ. The judgment (and in the case of a jury trial it will not be that of the person ultimately deciding the case) must usually be formed when only part of the evidence is in, and of course the evidence called before and after will have been, or will have to be, trimmed away from truth—finding to the procedural and evidentiary requirements of the trial. In short, although defects of human judgment are inevitable and must be accepted, the procedural system, even in this fundamental respect, does not assist the professed desideratum. The difficulty is I believe reflected in the tendency of judges when sitting without juries, and when a borderline question arises, either to admit the evidence, or admit it “subject to objection.” (Ibid, 145.)

He was of course talking of Australian judges. I am adding only that their tendency is commendable. I admit, however, that there will be occasions when most judges must interfere. Afterall the judge is, under the law, in control of all the proceedings (see section 69 of our Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323), which provides that:

“The court shall exercise reasonable control over the mode and order of interrogating witnesses and presenting evidence so as to—

(a) make the interrogation and presentation as rapid, as distinct, and as readily understandable as may be, and

(b) protect witnesses from being unduly intimidated, harassed or embarrassed.”

But I wish to suggest that if a judge feels he must put questions during evidence-in-chief he must carefully word them in order not to put off the witness.

Too much emphasis on speed takes the judge, in my view, out of the section. For example, where the judge says to a witness; “Madam Asempe, you are wasting the time of the court. Don’t you see? Please go to what happened the next day,” he may unwittingly be suggesting to the witness that everything else that happened on the day in question is unimportant; and the witness may, in going to what happened the next day, leave out something that is the basis of understanding much evidence that will follow. Speed is not that important. When I have seen a judge do that I have usually been reminded of the anonymous judge of whom Lord Devlin told in a lecture he gave long ago in Hong Kong. That judge was, from the point of view of the appellate courts, the ideal trial judge—that is to say, he was quick, he was courteous and he was frequently wrong . . .” (See Hong Kong Law Journal’s publication of Law Lectures for Practitioners, 1983, preface by Mr. Justice McMullin).

On the other hand where a judge makes a conscious effort to be helpful to a witness in the way suggested he will soon acquire the reputation of being helpful to counsel too, especially the younger ones. We can all think how that can be done. And, let me add, such a judge will almost always be thinking of the position of non-parties and other witnesses too. The point is this: as between parties, justice in a conventional sense can perhaps be done more often than not. The position of non-parties and particularly their reputations are often put at risk in the process. I am aware of section 68(4) of our Evidence Decree, namely, that a party may object to questions asked by the court and to evidence obtained by the court’s questions at any time prior to the submission of the action to the tribunal of fact for determination. But who has ever heard of that section being invoked or applied? The statement in the Commentary that a judge who abuses his discretion under that section “will be subject to reversal on appeal” is worth noting, but is not very much comfort to counsel who wants to do an examination-in-chief well.

Similar pleas for more care in the exercise of the many discretions conferred by the law on judges to rule on objections to evidence (section 6), to exclude relevant evidence (sections 8 and 52), to apply the rules on onus of proof (sections 10-17) may be made with reference to their timing, that is, during examination-in-chief. It is, as I have indicated, very little consolation to point out that the judge may be reversed on appeal, considering the great cost of and the well-known delays in appeals. True, we have a reported case which supports my plea: the trial circuit judge deferred the exercise of his discretion on the admissibility of a document (on the general ground that a continuance was usually to be preferred to exclusion of evidence) and continued with the hearing of a criminal case until a stage at which he might find whether or not it was judicially exercisable and the High Court judge upheld the decision of the trial judge. (See Republic v Accra Circuit Court; Ex parte Appiah [1982-83] GLRD 32). But who can say how many decisions have, at the trial stage, gone the other way and have not been appealed against? Or if they have been appealed against have simply not been reported.?

A related problem for us is the failure of the judge to give reasons why he exercised his discretion the way he did. Incidentally, I am not interested in long and detailed reasons for a decision on small points. But a short and concise statement of the reason does help practitioners before a judge. In the case to which I have just referred the High Court judge is reported to have said that “there was neither an express nor implied obligation under section 6(2) (of our Evidence Decree) to give reasons for the ruling in the instant case.” And even before then the Court of Appeal, speaking through the Chief Justice himself, had stated that:

“. . . this court wishes to make it plain that in the absence of any requirement by the law that reasons should be given for judicial decisions the mere failure to give reasons cannot invalidate a decision.” (See Azametsi v The Republic [1974] 1 GLR 228 at 246.)

I think, with respect, that both decisions or statements are quite correct, according to the common law. But how helpful are they?

It would be unfortunate if any one here understood that I was pleading for all evidence to be admissible to tender. I have already mentioned the importance of counsel about to examine-in-chief preparing carefully for it. So I am assuming that the questions being raised here will only arise in borderline cases. And what I am saying is that at the stage of evidence-in-chief it is better in those cases to exercise the judge’s discretion in favour of the provisions of section 52 of NRCD 323, especially with regard to the tendering of similar fact evidence. And I entirely agree, by way of example, with this statement in the Commentary at p. 34, namely, that:

“The risks of unfair prejudice and confusion of issues are likely to have more importance in cases tried by a jury. However, even judges can be confused by a multiplicity of side issues or inundated by a mass of detailed evidence of small probative value. Even a judge may be swayed on occasion by emotion rather than reason. Judges and juries alike should therefore be protected from highly prejudicial evidence.”

And what about opposing counsel? His role during examination-in-chief is spelt out in section 6: he should be attentive and ready to make any objection “at the time the evidence is offered.” And even that should be under the control of the judge. Early in my practice I noticed that counsel who were reputed to be good did not raise many objections during evidence-in-chief—they preferred commenting on that evidence at the appropriate time. And that often removed the suspicion that they were trying to shut our relevant evidence that would greatly support their opponent’s case. But if objections are raised then most of us become very concerned with how the judge exercises his discretion in the matter. Readers of Prof J B Thayer’s A Preliminary Treaties in Evidence at the Common Law often remember with apprehension his statement that “the pressures under which a ruling must be made is often unfavourable to clear thinking . . . (p. 266). And let no one think that those pressures are necessarily removed because the judge gives himself or herself an adjournment in order to “consider” (as it is usually put) his or her ruling. That merely prolongs the trial, as far as I have viewed it.

The judicial function has been epitomised by Lord Goff as “an educated reflex to facts.” (See Smith v Littlewood’s Organisation Ltd [1987] AC 241 at 280). By which, according to his further explanation in a lecture given at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, he meant and wished to emphasise that a judge, even if he considers a particular point of law, will examine it in minute detail, and consider it “in relation to a particular set of facts. In this he is assisted by counsel, each of whom has considered the law with care and will advance an argument designed to persuade the court to state the law, even to develop and qualify it, in a way which fits his own client’s case . . .and it (ie. The judge’s vision of the law) is likely to be strongly influenced by the facts of the particular case.” (See the Lionel Cohen Lecture delivered in May 1987. (1987) 22 Is LRI).

Knowing as we all do, the tenacity of first impressions, we should have no difficulty in appreciating why it is so important that when facts are first presented the judge's reaction should not be hasty. Most people will agree that what any person says must be regarded as true until the contrary has been established or a serious doubt has been cast upon it. And at the stage of evidence-in-chief there will usually not be available any such rebutting material.

So how may I sum up? I have said and repeated in several parts that when counsel is conducting an examination-in-chief of his witness before a judge he or she should all along bear in mind that counsel is grappling with a whole lot of problems on his feet—the rules on relevance, on admissibility, on objections, on judicial notice, on exclusion, on onus of proof, on leading questions, all are difficult of application to actual facts in issue and facts relevant to the issue. Especially moments of crisis arise when, for instance, the witness is falling below his proof of evidence. And the judge should endeavour to ask or say only what will help—or keep quiet; and if he has to rule, he or she must always consider the matters I have been trying to describe. This is not just a question of courtesy to counsel or his witness. It is also a necessary recognition of the nature of a trial under the system we operate. C R Williams in the introduction to an article on “Placing the Burden of Proof" published in 1982 had this to say:

“The aim of a trial is to reconstruct the facts of a past event so that the tribunal may then apply the law to those facts. Of necessity, this is a process which can be achieved with only a limited degree of success. Commonly the incident or incidents under investigation were not observed by witnesses, or if observed their testimony is unreliable or conflicting. Evidence other than the testimony of eye-witnesses is often inconclusive or susceptible of more than a single interpretation. In many cases the relevant incident may have occupied but a brief instant of time, an instant which it is extremely difficult to recapture with accuracy.” (See Well and Truly Tried, p. 271).

Finally, Your Lordships and Ladyships, let me refer to what a New Jersey lawyer, Gay Skoloff is reported to have said at the annual meeting of the American Bar Association last year. He advised counsel to encourage their clients to be “creative” when involved in division of assets proceedings. Among other tips, he suggested that “if the wife is claiming that she is entitled to more money, don’t just tell the judge that the husband dines out regularly at expensive restaurants, show him a copy of the menu.” (See the report in the Solicitors Journal, issue of 23 October 1987, at p. 1428). Now, if I produced during divorce proceedings here, for the purpose indicated, a copy of the menu of the New Kokompe Ritz Restaurant, would a Ghanaian High Court judge admit it? and if he did would the Appeal Court judges endorse that?

Thank You.
