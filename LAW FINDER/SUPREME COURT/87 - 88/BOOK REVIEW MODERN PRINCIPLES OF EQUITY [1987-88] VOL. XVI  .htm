BOOK REVIEW: MODERN PRINCIPLES OF EQUITY [1987-88] VOL. XVI  RGL 298—300

 

By A K P Kludze. Published by Foris Publications, Holland, P O Box 509, 300 AM Dordrecht, The Netherlands, 1988, pp xxix and 475.

AIDOO E. S.

This is a big book, the first of its kind in its chosen field, meant for use particularly in Ghana (but generally in Anglophone Africa and in other common law countries).  The author attempts "to write a book which can serve as a textbook for law students whilst also meeting the basic needs of legal practitioners and judges." That is not an easy task.

He has a story to tell which is best done in his own words. “In view of the wide spread assertions that Ghanaian scholars do not write books, I may be permitted to briefly refer to the chequered fate of this book. It is illustrative of the frustrations of Ghanaian authors. The original manuscript was completed in 1976, over a decade ago. Upon its completion I wanted it published by an overseas publisher.  However, the General Legal Council of Ghana decided to support the publication of the book in Ghana . . . As a result, I was persuaded . . . to entrust publication of the book to the Ghana Publishing Corporation. Upon a written guarantee by the Corporation that it would publish the book within eighteen months, I released the manuscript to the Ghana Publishing Corporation . . . The Corporation has failed to honour its commitment. What is worse, the Ghana Publishing Corporation has misplaced my original manuscript . . .  Therefore the book had to be virtually rewritten. The enormity of the task was daunting, and must certainly discourage scholarship."

But the work was done. In its sixteen chapters the book deals with most of the subjects usually covered by standard texts on equity and trusts: maxims, specific performance, injunctions, rectification, rescission, trusts and mortgages. In addition it contains introductory material that gives the history and development of some of these matters, especially with regard to the jurisdiction of the courts. The language throughout is straightforward and clear and each argument is presented so carefully that even where the reader may not be convinced by it he is left in no doubt that our author has helped to clarify the reader's thoughts on the subject.  Some subjects are discussed in more depth than others. This too is by design; for, as Dr Kludze himself explains, “I have either curtailed or expanded the discussion of issues on the basis of their relevance in the Ghanaian and African context. Topics and questions which have engaged, or are likely to engage the attention of the Ghanaian courts have received greater treatment in this book.” And he has done that so well that the balance is impressive. This book contains (in chapters 13, 14 and 15) the only reasonably full discussion of the law of mortgages in Ghana; and the discussion of customary law pledges, old style and modern (in chapter 16) is a thoughtful addition.

Your reviewer obviously thinks that the merits of Modern Principles of Equity are many. A few that readily come to mind may be stated. The author questions (at p. 13) the re-introduction of the “so-called principles of natural justice, equity and good conscience" into our law in a manner that most practitioners will agree with. Why, after that foreign test had been discarded for many years, should the acceptability of the rules of our customary law be subjected again (under section 49 of the Courts Act, 1971) to that test? Next, he does not hesitate much in counselling our courts to depart from English precedents in several cases because, as he clearly demonstrates, our circumstances call for such a departure. Examples are (at p 156) in the discussion of jurisdiction in ordering rectification (at pp 241-247) in his presentation on the denial of rescission for innocent misrepresentation in the case of executed contracts (at pp 295-296), his suggestion that a half-secret trust be held to be valid if the trust is communicated to the trustee at any time before the testator's death; the expression of his hope (at p 307) that the law in Ghana will be changed to remove “this technical anomaly” that a trustee in certain circumstances cannot retire (and even a court cannot release him) from his trust unless a replacement is found for him; and his submission (at p 313) that where a trustee "chooses an adviser   with the requisite expertise, the trustee ought to be able to rely on the expert's advice as a defence to any claim against him for a loss incurred by the trust estate." Several other discussions leading to either suggestions for changes or clarifications in our law are undertaken. Two instances are on misrepresentation (at pp 213-214) that the old common law rule be abolished, “so that a party who is induced by an innocent misrepresentation to enter into a contract may claim damages for that innocent misrepresentation;" and on the possibilities open to beneficiaries in such "dry" matters as tracing the trust property (pp 356-357). Older students and young practitioners should find all those very useful. Indeed, additionally, all practitioners may well consider seriously the advice (at p 459) that our courts should change their attitude in considering customary law pledges.

But, as always, we also have to note the faults. In the early part of the book there is too much Latin sometimes. For instance at p 58. The fact is that very few students are familiar with that language now, unfortunately. There is a slip at p 54 in the discussion of the equitable rule number 11: the person meant (in line 7) is the creditor, not the debtor. Much later, at p 413 (end of line 2) it is clear that “mortgagee” is meant and not "mortgagor" as there now.  It probably would have helped, at p 63, to cite section 18 of NRCD 175 on unconscionability in that discussion. "Pearson" at p 81 (note 62) is not correct. It should be “Pierson” (cf) [1948] Ch 452). And the reference there to Hoggart v Scott (note 68) should be (1830) and not (1930). Which "Lord Buckley" is meant at p 88? Perhaps “Lord Justice” was meant. And what does this (at p 97) mean: “ . . . the English Court of Appeal with Lord Denning and Sachs LJ concurring?” Then at p 99 note 132 says “italics supplied”, but there is no such supply in the section of Act 229 there quoted. The last but one sentence of the last paragraph of p 106 should start with "the lender . . . " and not "the borrower" as there now.  Also, at p 179, there is a reference to a Lord Russell LJ in Joscelyne v Nissen [1970] 2 QB 86 at 98.  The "Lord" should be dropped. And there are many printer's mistakes.  In the last line on the blank page of dedication the word "while" has been put there as "shile". The word “covenant(s)” comes out as "convenant(s)" at pp 111, 154, 359 and  372 (notes 31 and 32).  At p. 117 and in the Table the reference for Doherty v Allman is wrongly printed as (878) instead of (1878) as at p 134; the reference to Hilton v Earl of Granville at p 129 should be 1841 and not 1941; and what is the proper reference for Weeks v Blancy, as at p 192 and in the Table or at p 191?  But, even before most of these, we may note "wherefor" for "wherefore" at p 46, "possessing" for “possession" at p 66, "must" for "much" at p 70 (in the quotation) and after them, uberrimae gets misspelt in note 166 at p 232. "It must" comes out as "In must" at p 181, and at p 226 there is “sustantial” for "substantial", and "substitue" has taken the place of "substitute" in the centre of p 450. Others are "sailing" for “sealing" in note 28 at p 270; "inquires" for "inquiries" in note 42 at p 274, “preferrable" for "preferable" in the middle of p 283; and the reference for Norh v Gbedemah in note 30 at p 457 should be (1926-29) and not (1962-1929).

There are other matters of the "failures" kind that one may draw attention to. At p 78 the statement is made that the "doctrine of mutuality, however, has over the years been whittled away by decisions and its demise is here." But the cases are not discussed until p 80 and subsequent pages where there is no cross-reference to p 78. And at the end of the discussion it is not clear that the cases have, in fact, done any whittling away here. It is admitted however that the author's arguments, all well-known, are convincing; so that one hopes that the Ghanaian courts will follow his advice and "abolish mutuality as a condition for the grant of specific performance."

Next, considering the frequency with which it is discussed whether it is still the law that in applying for an interim injunction the plaintiff has to show "that the evidence before the court shows a prima facie case for relief" (cf Annobil v Annobil (1969) CC 27 per Owusu J) the treatment of that subject by our author (pp 125-127) seems quite inadequate. One hopes that in a subsequent edition he will give us the benefit of careful analyses of at least these cases: Vanderpuije v Nartey [1977] 1 GLR 42, CA.; Agyei- Acheampong v Donkor [1980] GLR 108 and Sussana Baiden v Nana Tandoh and two Others, in fact, decided (after the publication of this book) on 28 February 1989 in the Cape Coast High Court. Also, at pp 175-177 there is a review of many English cases on rectification for unilateral mistake. It is then stated that the defendant may be given "an option for rescission rather than rectification in some cases" (p 175). But there is no indication of why that may be done, only a suggestion (repeated with emphasis at p 177) of how the Ghanaian courts should go: which in effect is that they should be flexible, like the English courts have been. Similarly at pp 182-183 it “is submitted that the Ghana courts should abandon the traditional reluctance of the equity courts to rectify unconscionable contracts." There is nothing in that section to show that our courts have tended to follow that tradition; and one suspects that the reasons for the change there counselled may confuse, rather than help, many magistrates and young judges. It is a pity that the author deals with charitable or public trusts “only in the barest outline" (three and a half pages). Your reviewer thinks that in a book like this more lead on the subject should have been given. Dr Kludze alleges at p 342 that the Ghana courts would apply a lower standard in dealing with breaches of trust, but he does not give an adequate reason for that view. And at p 346, he does not indicate, as he ought to, that the Insolvency Act, 1962 (Act 153) is not yet in force.

To come back to lighter matters, there are several parts in which some explanations are repeated too often, even for students. Examples are to be found at pp 361 and 364 where the familiar definitions of a mortgage are stated more than once in each case. On p 459 one may wonder whether our author's arguments on prima facie presumption of title (from long possession) are consistent with his earlier statement that "the courts seem to have further disabled themselves by not regarding possession as prima facie evidence of ownership" (p. 458). Also, it is not easy to accept Dr Kludze's submissions on the announcement of debts at funerals (p 468) or his suggestion (at p 471) that the rules of evidence should be relaxed to let in parol evidence where a pledge transaction is reduced into writing. Finally, do we still have customary law pledges, that is, after the coming into force of the Mortgages (Amendment) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 37), sections 1 and 2 and the Land Title Registration Law, 1986 (PNDCL 152), sections 72-78 inclusive?

Yet none of these things (ie the long list of printer's errors or the questioning of some of the author's arguments and suggestions) reduces the pleasure one gets in reading this big book. Practically all students of law should be able to follow the language without much effort. Its chief merits undoubtedly include these: That the issues are clarified, the statements are full, and in most cases the discussions are adequate. Dr Kludze has achieved his aim admirably; and his book can be recommended without reservation. Your reviewer is certain that the General Legal Council which gave initial encouragement, Chief Justices Azu Crabbe and Apaloo who showed such personal interest in the project, the Dana Fund for International and Comparative Law, of Toledo, Ohio, USA who gave welcome assistance at a difficult time, the publishers who have arranged for such a neat production, and of course, Dr A K P Kludze himself who persisted through trying times in completing what is now The Modern Principles of Equity — all of these have earned the eternal gratitude of the legal profession in Ghana.
