MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: LEGAL ASPECT OF BLOOD TEST EVIDENCE [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 82—88

by Osew E O

 

THERE is a legal debate in the courts now about our subject: Legal aspects of blood test evidence. The issue is whether or not the courts should order blood test when faced with problems of paternity. The contestants on the one side are presumption and customs and on the other, fact and science. You will in your courts sooner or later be faced with this difficult problem—to order or not to order. Your jurisdiction as spelt out in the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372), s 37 (1) (f), covers “divorce and other matrimonial causes or matters and actions for paternity and custody of children where the law applicable is exclusively customary law.” In practice this will extend to suits of maintenance and affiliated proceedings

The expression exclusively customary law may seem to limit you in your courts on this issue of customary law, but you will find that in arriving at a just decision it will be necessary to resort to the common law and other legislation. And, in a case from Accra New Town District Court to the High Court, Accra, the judge was fettered by this phrase. He refused to order because the issue of paternity with the aid of the result of blood test would not be deciding within the area of jurisdiction vested in the district court. See R v Accra New Town District Court Magistrate; Ex parte Papafio, High Court, Accra, 10 December (1969) digested in (1970) CC 29. Yet rule 7 of section 49 (1) of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372), states, inter alia, “in the determination of any issue arising from the common law or customary law the court may adopt, develop and apply such remedies from any system of law (whether Ghanaian or non-Ghanaian) as appear to the court to be efficacious and to meet the requirements of justice, equity and good conscience.”

I. WHAT THE SUPPORTERS OF LEGAL PRESUMPTION SAY

The legal presumption is that a child born out of marriage contracted under the Ordinance or by customary law is presumed to be the child of the spouses.

(1) That society reveres family life, and that the trespasser is frowned upon, even if he has naturally, though adversely, added to the family, he should not be permitted to take away what he has put in.

(2) That family life would be disintegrated if the trespasser is allowed to fly in like a hawk for chickens in the matrimonial home.

(3) Family life, they argue, must be protected at all costs, even if underneath it is infidelity.

(4) The supporters maintain the child must be the child of the spouses because his interest in paramount; and this interest should override all other interests.

(5) That in societies where bastardy is part of the legal system or custom, the child would face public ridicule with the adverse social and economic consequences in adult life. But the question may be asked whether in Ghana now we have or recognise bastardy?

(6) The supporters maintain that spouses should not be compelled to undergo blood test against their consent; and as for the child how do you secure his consent?

II. SOME CASES SUPPORTING THE PRESUMPTION THEORY

There is a dearth of reported cases in Ghana on blood test evidence. Nyarko v Mansu [1967] GLR 523, an appeal from a magistrate court on custody which came before Archer J (as he then was) at Cape Coast High Court is a classic and most interesting case. The appellant, the woman, claimed that of the four children in the matrimonial home the last two were issues of her adulterous union with a man known to the husband, whom she alleged had become impotent after the birth of the first two senior children. The husband denied this but was not prepared to undergo blood test when his consent was sought by the judge. The woman and the trespasser readily gave their consent. Yet the learned judge, after making a fine case for the test, refused to order the test upon the presumption theory. He however gave interim custody of the two younger children to the mother, because though the husband was wealthy enough to maintain them, he lacked the moral fibre and a good home (the parties had already divorced) to keep the children immediately. In R v Accra West Town District Magistrate; Ex parte Papafio, unreported; digested in [1969] CC 53, the same judge had another opportunity to pronounce on whether the trial district court had jurisdiction to order the test. The answer was negative. Here there was no matrimonial proceedings pending, yet the trespasser had the effontry to snatch back per court, the child he alleged to be his from the matrimonial home. The judge further held that the mother of a child could not be compelled to undergo blood test against her wish; and that it was doubtful whether a mother could give consent on behalf of a child under age.

III. THE EVIDENCE DECREE, 1975 (NRCD 323)

The presumption theory reached a safe haven in the legal system of Ghana per the Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323), section 32 (1) which states:

“A child born during the marriage of the mother is presumed to be the child of the person who is the husband of that mother at the time of the birth.”

Section 32 (2) adds: “A child of a woman who has been married, born within 300 days after the end of the marriage, is presumed to be a child of that marriage.” Section 32 (3) gives the finale. This section applies both to monogamous and polygamous marriages.

Yet section 97 of the same Decree, which deals with privilege against self-incrimination, points to another direction. Section 97(1) provides that in any proceedings a person has a privilege to refuse to disclose any matter or to produce any object or writing that will incriminate him. Section 97(2) also provides that no person has a privilege under subsection (1), where the court thinks that it is necessary to the determination of an issue, to refuse:

(a) to submit his body to examination for the purpose of discovering or recording his corporal features and other identifying characteristics, or his physical or mental condition; or

(b) to furnish or permit the taking of samples of body fluids or substances for analysis. (My emphasis.)

The Decree in section 32 only preserves the presumption theory. It is silent on blood test evidence. Section 97, however, explicitly or impliedly permit blood test where the court finds it necessary in the interest of justice. Some people hold the view that the two sections are in conflict with each other; others think they are not. May be all depends on the section and upon the particular facts of the case before the court. Perhaps section 97 is more inclined to criminal evidence and procedure.

In the complicated case of Daaboa Dagarti v. Donipea, unreported; digested in [1982-83] GLRD 63, CA, though custody was granted to the plaintiff, uncle of the child of six, for being responsible and affectionate and for being consistent and vigilant in pursuing his claim for custody, section 32(1) of the Evidence Decree was not applied, because the receipt of a dowry by a man was conclusive evidence of dissolution of customary marriage.

IV. VIEWS OF THE SUPPORTERS OF BLOOD TEST EVIDENCE

(1) That the fact that a child born at the matrimonial home belongs to the spouses is based on presumption which is rebuttable. The ordinary meaning of presumption is “taking for granted.” The legal meaning is “assuming the truth of a thing until the contrary is proved.”

(2) That society has so progressed from times gone by and so changed in modern times that the traditional view of the marriage bond has much eroded giving way to infidelity between spouses and other matrimonial offences; hence the long-held legal presumption to preserve marriage at all costs, should be relaxed, even if not swept off.

(3) The best evidence should be preferred to any other method of proof, if available.

(4) In a nutshell supporters of blood test evidence are urging the courts to make use of modern scientific achievements in medicine, etc, in deciding cases. This is doing real justice and not resting upon assumed facts.

(5) Adherents of blood test concede that the result of the test is not conclusive. But as is always the case in expert evidence, the final decision rests with the judge or jury.

(6) On the proposition that in these cases the interest of the child should be paramount, supporters of blood test to a large extent do agree, but maintain that the interest of the child should be considered along with the interest of other parties to the case. What is perhaps most important is that justice should be done with the help of all available methods of proof.

In S v S; W v Official Solicitor [1970] 3 All ER 107, the House of Lords upholding an order for the test, held that: In determining, in cases where a legitimacy issue is to be tried, whether or not to order that a blood test of an infant should be made, the High Court was not in general exercising its custodial jurisdiction. Where the interest of the infant was in issue, it was entitled to look at all the evidence available including that of blood test and need not be satisfied before ordering a blood test. The outcome thereof would be for the benefit of the infant. “In my opinion,” said Lord Denning, “when a court is asked to decide whether a child is legitimate or not, it should have before it the best evidence which is available. It should decide on all the evidence and not on half of it. There is at hand in these days expert scientific evidence—by means of blood test—which can in most cases resolve the issue conclusively. In the absence of a strong reason to the contrary, a blood test should be made available. The interests of justice so required.” (See S v McC (formerly S) & M S (Intervening) [1970] 1 All ER 1162 at 1165, CA).

In this case where a white married couple with three sons and the wife had adulterous association with a coloured man resulting in the birth of a girl, the Court of Appeal by a majority ruled against the application of the Official Solicitor that blood test of the infant should not be ordered. But see the earlier case of W v W [1970] 1 All ER 1157, where the majority decided (Denning MR dissenting) that the court could not order an infant’s blood to be tested because, in order to determine the question whether in the exercise of its-discretion, the court should order a blood test, the sole criterion to be applied was what was in the best interest of the child and where the blood test might well show that the husband was not the child’s father, but would not give the child any indication of the identity of his father, a blood test would not be in the child’s best interest.

Denning MR, in a strong dissent at page 1159 in this case where the facts are similar to S v McC & M above cited, had this to say:

“In my opinion where a court is asked to decide a paternity issue, it is in the best interest of everyone that it should do it on the best evidence available. The issue is of such importance and affects so many persons that it should be decided on all the evidence and not half of it . . . Paul's (the infant in the case) best interests are bound up with the interest of all those about him.”

V. THE BRITISH FAMILY LAW REFORM ACT, 1969 PART III

(1) The preamble to this important piece of legislation states, inter alia, “to make provision with respect to the evidence required to rebut a presumption of legitimacy and illegitimacy.”

(2) Section 20 (1): “In any civil proceedings in which the paternity of any person falls to be determined by the court hearing the proceedings, the court may, on an application by any party to the proceedings, give a direction for the use of blood test to ascertain whether such tests show that a party to the proceedings is or is not thereby excluded from being the father of that person and for the taking, within a period to be specified in the direction, of blood samples from that person, the mother of that person any party alleged to be the father of that person, or from any, or any two, of those persons.”

Section 23 (1) (3) reads: “Where a court gives a direction under section 20 of this Act and any person fails to take any step required of him for the purpose of giving effect to the direction, the court may draw such inferences, if any, from the fact as appear proper in the circumstances.”

VI. PROBLEMS TO THINK ABOUT

Before concluding this talk, may I respectfully pose these questions for Your Worships’ consideration in your various jurisdictions:

(1) What will the supporters of presumption say in a situation where the husband does not want the child in issue, or does not have the means of maintaining him, even if he likes him?

(2) What should the court do if there is a reverse of S v McC & M and W v W, where the spouses are Ghanaians with three Ghanaian children and due to the woman’s adulterous association a fourth white skin is born?

(3) What if the minor himself tells the court: “I want as my father the new man and not mama’s husband?” See Nyarko v Mansu [1967] GLR 523.

(4) What about a young inexperienced woman who flirts with two or more men and cannot identify the one who is the cause of her pregnancy?

(5) What about two married couple fighting about the same child, i.e. where husband and wife, on each side, stand on the same platform?

VII. CONCLUSION

(1) Blood test problems will still haunt the courts of Ghana and we cannot overlook their importance in modern proof of cases.

(2) The Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323) ss 32 and 97 apart, there is the need for a comprehensive legislation on the lines of the British Family Law Reform Act, 1969, to resolve the present debate between presumption and blood test evidence in Ghana.

(3) While waiting for such a legislation from the government the superior courts should take steps to develop the law involving paternity.

(4) The Law Reform Commission can take active lead, as it has done in other branches, in finding the solution eventually in statute.

(5) It would also not be out of place to have a national committee on paternity suits to collate views of the public on blood test evidence as a prelude to legislations.

Thank you, Your Worships; and may you have a pleasant career on the Bench.

MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: PREFACE TO “PROOF IN CRIMINAL TRIALS”[1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 91—95

by Taylor J N K, Justice of the Supreme Court

ON Monday, 25 July 1988 when this seminar opened the subject of the elements of trials covering examination of witnesses, cross-examination and re-examination were discussed in a most interesting manner. Mr. E S Aidoo in his invaluable contribution made some statements which understandably provoked discussion and forced a number of participants to rush to the microphone either to support Mr. Aidoo or to express misgivings about the apparent purport of the proposition of law he had enunciated touching on truth in law. You will recall Mr. Kumado’s panoramic assessment and the contribution of Dr Agbosu about the syllogism, a heritage of Greek philosophy and an adjunct of clear thinking in the tradition of Socrates, and his comments concerning the oft-quoted remarks of Holmes about the life of the law being based on experience and not logic. This he would seem to think was perhaps becoming questionable in the light of the discussions.

I recall in this connection that Benjamin Cardozo reminds us that Holmes did not tell us that logic is to be ignored when experience is silent.1a

One cannot but be impressed in the course of the Monday exchange of ideas by the Ananse folklore concerning evidence which Mr. Justice Owusu Sekyere narrated so graphically to demonstrate the essential difference between truth and justice. I do not think we can gloss non-chalantly over Mr. Justice Lamptey’s strictures on the apparently inexplicable stance between our invoicing divine guidance to elicit truth and our dubious but honest insistence on not searching for subjective truth in our courts. These are all manifestations of a perennial dilemma which face and have engaged the attention of courts in all countries for countless generations.

A little over ten years ago, on 17 March 1978 at the High Court here in Accra, I decided a criminal appeal Marhaba v The Republic [1979] GLR 90. It was a case in which the complainant was without doubt assaulted and injured by the appellants and the trial court was compelled by the truth of this undoubted fact to convict the appellants. In apparent confusion, I gave expression to my dilemma, when counsel put forward impressive legal arguments urging me to set aside the convictions. My distress at that time is reflected in these remarks I made in my judgment.a

“In the course of the arguments and after adjournment in the secluded safety of my chambers. I asked myself time and again: These are obviously guilty persons. Is it possible our criminal jurisprudence is so bankrupt that the law can say: Never mind for this and that reason set aside these convictions?”

After experiencing such soul wrenching agony, I allowed the appeals, quashed the convictions, set aside the sentences and discharged the appellants, but because I recognised that the complainant, like Mr. Justice Owusu Sekyere’s squirrel had suffered unjustly, I was unable to acquit them. In the light of our discussion on Monday, I recall trying to justify my conduct by remarking:b

“. . . many persons including even some lawyers misconceive the nature of the judicial process and the legal considerations which the tribunal must apply in a decision as to whether an accused person should or should not be convicted. The crime the subject-matter of a trial may be as clear as a pikestaff, nevertheless the law insists that the procedure and the processes which it has deemed as essential steps to be taken before guilt is pronounced, must be complied with by the administrators and the interpreters of the criminal law. If an essential procedure is blatantly ignored then although a person may be guilty of an act constituting the crime, a conviction cannot be pronounced against him. This means that an acquittal does not necessarily mean that the acquitted person has not committed the act, it may very well mean that his guilt has not been established in the manner which the law demands: conviction therefore means guilt in accordance with what the law demands as essential standards to be followed in establishing guilt, and acquittal means that the proof has either not been adequately established or it has fallen short of the said standards or that the said standards have been ignored.”

Perhaps an example from our Law Reports will illustrate the problem. Where a magistrate after the close of the case for the defence entertained doubt about the guilt of the accused, and on adjourning the case, conducted private enquiries at her bungalow and utilised the facts thus obtained from her bungalow as a basis for convicting and sentencing an accused person, then although the facts might be true, the appeal was allowed and the conviction and offending order set aside.c This is because there is a world of difference between evidence simpliciter and judicial evidence.d And in a court of law proof apart from argument and inference drawn from facts in evidence, using the word evidence in its widest possible meaning as anything that gives reason for believing something, is effected by judicial evidence, judicial presumptions and judicial notice.

Judicial evidence in this context, is evidence as is used in judicial proceedings. In a sense it denotes the means apart from arguments and inferences placed before a court by which the court is informed about issues of facts that are relevant to a determination of matters in controversy in a case before the court whether civil or criminal or otherwise. In a real sense judicial evidence consists of oral testimony of witnesses and it embraces facts within the knowledge of the witness as to documents and things as well as hearsay facts with other facts all of which a court will accept as tending to establish the existence or non-existence of the matters in controversy in a case.e

A realistic treatment of the subject necessarily covers the whole subject of the law of evidences both statutory and the practice developed by the courts for considering whether evidence should be accepted and the weight to be attached to such evidence. The topic is so large that it is impossible to consider it comprehensively and exhaustively in a seminar limited by time. I shall therefore deal generally with testimonial proof leaving inevitably a substantial portion of the subject, which is covered by the hearsay rule, confessions and the judges’ rule, corroboration and accomplices, expert evidence, character and similar facts, the res gestae concept, dying declarations, privilege, silence of the accused and writings and documentary evidence.

There are some matters which it is not necessary at all to prove. The court, for instance, takes judicial notice and does not require proof of two types of facts. The first are facts which are so generally known within the territorial jurisdiction of the court and the second are facts so capable of accurate and ready determination by resort to sources whose accuracy cannot be reasonably questioned that the existence of the two species of facts is not subject to dispute.f Other facts are matters which the law presumes. These have now statutory foundation in the Evidence Decree.g Where a fact or group of facts are prima facie evidence of another fact a rebuttable presumption arises with regard to the existence of that other fact.h Conclusive or irrebuttable presumptions of law are arbitrary conclusions or consequences expressly annexed by law to particular facts and must in fact be regarded more as rules of substantive law rather than rules of evidence. They are termed conclusive presumption because where the basic facts which have given rise to them are found or established no evidence contrary to the conclusively presumed fact may be considered by the court.i The Evidence Decree without exhausting the instances gives five examples of conclusive presumptions:

(1) facts recited in written instruments: section 25,

(2) estoppel by one’s own statement or conduct: section 26,

(3) estoppel of tenant to deny title of his landlord: section 27,

(4) estoppel of licensee to deny title of licensor: section 28, and

(5) estoppel of bailee, agent or licensee: section 29.

The manner in which conclusive presumptions are dealt with in the Evidence Decree would seem to indicate that conclusive presumptions are a species of the wider legal concept of estoppel.

With this rather long preface, I propose to take my contribution on proof in trial of criminal cases, by reminding you that we have operated a criminal justice system in the tradition of statutory courts applying common law principles for over one hundred years. Our present Evidence Decree (NRCD 323) is a consolidation with amendments of the common law principles and statutes relating to evidence. The Decree merely rationalised, simplified and presented in a more coherent and comprehensive form, the rules of evidence in an attempt to assist in the administration of justice.j

Speaking for myself, I cannot see what harm can be done by drawing on the experience of other jurisdictions which operate a similar system of law like ours. I have often wondered at the legal significance of the caveat to renounce apposite and probably persuasive foreign thinking in foreign case law. The prohibition, I understand counsels a prior research into the availability of local case law before recourse to foreign authorities. This is an exercise that can only be meaningfully mounted if our court libraries and our books are up to date.

With our lack of adequate library facilities and with the paucity of local textbooks, this admonition is in my view an invitation that can hardly be accepted and I find that the Bench and the profession have studiously ignored it. If I ignore it in this paper I will be following the current convention and be operating within the spirit if not the letter of the Interpretation Act, 1960 (CA 4).k
