MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: INTERLOCUTORY MATTERS UNDER THE HIGH COURT RULES [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 60—70

 

by Adade N Y B, Justice of the Supreme Court

DURING the life of a case in court many applications are made to the court for such reliefs and directions as the parties think they should be given, while waiting for a final disposal of the case. These may have to do with the mode of service; the nature of pleadings; when to sue or to be left out; the time and place of trial; the issues to be tried; nature of the evidence, and where and when to take it; preservation of the subject-matter of the litigation from destruction, dissipation or wastes, injunctions, etc. Your attention was drawn to many of these matters yesterday when you were addressed on the rules of the High Court. These intermediate matters are seen early in order to speed up the trial itself, and to make for a more efficacious dispensation of justice.

They are dealt with in interlocutory proceedings, resulting in interlocutory orders.

It is not our intention to   deal with all interlocutory matters here, even if we could. Time will not permit this. We have accordingly decided to focus attention on a few of these only, viz. injunctions, stay of execution and some aspects of pleadings. I shall lead the discussion on injunctions and stay of execution, whilst my brother Mr. Justice Osei-Hwere takes the rest.

DEFINITION

The word interlocutory is rooted in two Latin words: inter (between), and loquor, loqui (to speak). Hence interlocutory will, from its roots, have something to do with “speaking between.” An interlocutor we are told by the Dictionary, is a person who takes part in a conversation, a discussion or a dialogue. He “speaks between,” as the action of the discussion swings from person to person. Similarly an interlocutory order will be an order “speaking between” but between the birth of an action and its demise, ie between the institution of an action and the final judgment determining that action. Wharton’s Law Lexicon (14th ed) defines it as “an order made or given during the progress of an action, but which does not finally dispose of the rights of the parties.” He gives, by way of examples, an order appointing a receiver or granting an injunction. In practice, however, an interlocutory order is used in contradistinction to a final judgment, and it matters not whether the order is made before or after the final judgment: see Easton v Nar Valley Drainage Commissioners (1892) 8 TLR 649, citing Smith v Cowell (1830) 6 QBD 75 where an application to appoint a receiver and manager, made long after the final judgment in the case, was held to be a valid interlocutory proceeding. And in any case, applications for a stay of execution are normally made after the final judgment, not before; but these applications are interlocutory proceedings.

INJUNCTIONS

In our rules, interlocutory orders as to injunctions are governed by Order 50. This Order does not deal with injunctions only. It deals also with interim orders to preserve, attach or otherwise control property, whether the subject of litigation or not. By rule 7:

“(1) The Court may grant a mandamus or an injunction or appoint a receiver by an interlocutory order in all cases in which it appears to the Court to be just or convenient so to do.

(2) Any such order may be made either conditionally or on such terms or conditions as the Court thinks just."

Rule 8 gives the procedure by which the application may be made. It provides:

“8. An application for [an order of injunction] may be made to the Court or a Judge by any party. If the application be by the plaintiff . . . it may be made either ex parte or with notice, and . . . it may be made after notice to the defendant at any time after the issue of the writ of summons, and if it be by any other party (ie by the defendant) then on a notice to the plaintiff and at any time after appearance by the party making the application.”

Because these days our courts appear to be sacrificing the rules for what we call broad or substantial justice, I like us to look a bit more closely at what our rules require by way of procedure. In the first place the application may be made by either party—plaintiff or defendant. A defendant may apply.

(a) only on notice to the plaintiff; he cannot do it ex parte; and

(b) only after appearance. A defendant who has not appeared to the writ cannot apply for an injunction.

The White Book (1959 p 1204) tells us further that the relief sought by the defendant must arise in respect of the plaintiff's action or be the incidental thereto. If it is outside the plaintiff's action, then the defendant cannot move for an injunction unless he has filed a counterclaim or a cross-action.

It would seem, however, that in very exceptional circumstances a defendant may apply ex parte. (Order 52, r 3). A plaintiff, however, may apply either ex parte, or on notice.

An ex parte application should be entertained only rarely, in cases of extreme emergency. The plaintiff must make out a case of urgency, eg that the subject-matter will be destroyed, or that it will be mischievous to serve the defendant at that stage. Even so, it seems that the proper course is to grant only an interim order, allowing tine to the plaintiff to serve notice of his motion on the defendant, and adjourn the hearing to a definite date. On the adjourned date, the interim order may be discharged, or it may depending on the circumstances, be continued until trial by an interlocutory order of injunction.

As was observed in Ansah v Ansah [1977] 2 All ER 638 at 642:

“Orders made ex parte are anomalies in our system of justice which generally demands service or notice of the proposed proceedings on the opposite party (see Craig v Kanseen). Nonetheless, the power of the court to intervene immediately and without notice in proper cases is essential to the administration of justice. But this power must be used with great caution and only in circumstances in which it is really necessary to act immediately.

Such circumstances do undoubtedly tend to occur more frequently in family disputes than in other types of litigation because the parties are often still in close contact with one another and, particularly when a marriage is breaking up, in a state of high emotional tension: but even in such cases the court should only act ex parte in an emergency when the interests of justice or the protection of the applicant or a child clearly demands immediate intervention by the court. Such cases should be extremely rare, since any urgent application can be heard inter partes on two days’ notice to the other side. . . Circumstances, of course, may arise when prior notice cannot be given to the other side; for example, cases where one parent has disappeared with the children, or a spouse, usually the wife, is so frightened of the other spouse that some protection must be provided against a violent response to service of proceedings, but the court must be fully satisfied that such protection is necessary.”

The morale is clear. As a rule, do not entertain ex parte applications. Where there is no urgency, the proper mode is for the plaintiff to apply on notice for an interlocutory order of injunction. (See Order 52, r 3).

At this stage it may be in order to say a word or two on terminology. In practice we are used to describing every order of injunction as interim. This is strictly not in order. An interim order is distinguishable from the truly interlocutory order, even though both are interlocutory. Generally the court should not make an order against any party first hearing him. But there may be situations, where, before giving the party a hearing, it may be necessary to take certain steps to protect the property the subject of dispute or otherwise to ensure that the balance is evenly held between the parties in the broader interests of justice. These situations call for interim orders. Thus an interim order is normally made on an ex parte application by the plaintiff, it is limited to a definite and very short time-short, but reasonable enough to enable the plaintiff, exercising due diligence, to serve notice of the motion on the defendant. The “interim” application will then be adjourned to a definite date for hearing “on notice.” It is not proper to adjourn an ex parte application “sine die for service” unless no interim order has been made against the defendant.

As soon as a defendant becomes aware of an interim order against him, he is entitled to move the court, on notice, to discharge it, even before the adjourned date. To quote again from page 642 of Ansah v Ansah:

“If an order is to be made ex parte, it must be strictly limited in time if the risk of causing serious injustice is to be avoided. The time is to be measured in days, ie the shortest period which must elapse before a preliminary hearing inter partes can be arranged, and the order must specify the date on which it expires. (If difficulty in serving the other party is anticipated it may, exceptionally, be permissible to fix a  longer period and to provide in the order that the other party may apply on 24 hours’ notice to discharge the injunction).”

In Ansah v Ansah it was held “quite unjustifiable and improper” to adjourn an ex parte application until the further hearing of the application” inter partes.

On the other hand, a truly interlocutory order of injunction is made on the application of either party, it is always on notice (or should be), and may be limited to a definite date “until trial” or “until final judgment.”

The plaintiff may apply to the court for an injunction any time after he has filed the writ of summons, whether the writ has been served or not. (See Order 50, r 8.) This rule has been interpreted in some places to imply that the court is empowered to deal with the motion thus filed, even though the defendant has not been served with the writ of summons. For instance, in Rhone-Poulence v GNTC [1972] 2 GLR 109 at 116, it is said:

“It is the issue of the writ and not its service which must precede the making of the application. So that if the writ of summons is filed and sealed, and a few hours or minutes are allowed to elapse before the motion is filed, the question of irregularity will not arise, even if all the documents, that is, the writ of summons and the motion for interim injunction, are served on the defendant at the same time. In a situation like this, all that is necessary is that the court must make sure that there are, at least, two clear days between the date of service and the date named in the motion paper for the hearing of the said motion.”

This interpretation, respectfully, fails to draw a distinction between the mere application on notice and service of that notice. The first is catered for by Order 50, r 8 alright; but for the second we must go to Order 52, r 8 viz..

“8. The plaintiff shall, without any special leave, be at liberty to serve any notice of motion or other notice or any petition or summons upon any defendant, who, having been duly served with a writ of summons to appear has not appeared within the time limited for that purpose.” (My emphasis.)

So that although by Order 50, rule 8 the application itself may be made at any time after the issue of the writ, it may properly be served only after the defendant has been “duly served with the writ of summons.” (Order 52, rule 8). It will then not matter whether the defendant has appeared to the writ or not. Otherwise the plaintiff must seek leave of the court to serve the notice of motion. It is for this reason that Order 52, rule 9 makes provision for the situation where it may become necessary, for any reason at all, to serve notice of the motion alongside service of the writ of summons. Rule 9 says:

“9. The plaintiff may, by leave of the Court or a Judge to be obtained ex parte, serve any notice of motion upon any defendant along with the writ of summons, or at any time after service of the writ of summons and before the time limited for the appearance of such defendant.”

When counsel try to prevail upon you to take their applications, make sure counsel's attention is drawn to this. I have already, in passing, mentioned some of the matters which the courts take into consideration when dealing with applications for injunctions. These have been neatly summarised in Frimpong v Obeng II [1974] 1 GLR 16 at 21 as follows:

“It is the duty of a court in dealing with applications for interim order of injunction to consider among other matters the following:

(a) the hardship that would be caused if the application is granted or refused; in other words the relative convenience or inconvenience which might result to the parties from granting or withholding an interlocutory injunction;

(b) whether on the facts before the court it is just and convenient for the preservation of the status quo;

(c) whether damages will afford an adequate compensation for the loss if the application be refused.

Where as in the instant case the respondent disputes the legal title of the applicant to the trees, the court will seldom (however clear the case may in its opinion be) grant an injunction before the applicant has established his legal rights: see Punjabi Brothers v. Namih (1958) 3 WALR 381 at p 382.

The applicant for an interim injunction must show a strong prima facie case in support of the title which he asserts and must make out a probability of the respondent’s case failing. The court however in dealing with such an application does not attempt to determine the rights of the parties.” See Punjabi's case (supra).”

It only remains to comment briefly on one aspect of the practice in this area of interlocutory injunctions. In an action for recovery of land (cocoa farms, houses, etc) in the possession of the defendant, it has been customary, upon making the order of injunction, to appoint the registrar of the court to be the receiver and manager of the land, subject to his expenses and a commission of X per cent of the net income. I have often wondered whether in making these official appointments we have been acting in the best interests of the parties. Registrars are very busy people; and even where a registrar knows what a cocoa farm looks like, let alone how to manage one, he is not likely, to be able to devote such time and attention to its charge as will yield the best dividends for the parties. If the answer to this query is that the registrars delegate their responsibilities, but do not manage personally, then my plea is why not appoint persons other than the registrars?

PERMANENT INJECTION

As a rule, a plaintiff must indorse on his writ of summons all the reliefs he seeks. By Order 2, rule 1, “every action ... shall be commenced by writ of summons which shall be indorsed with a statement of the nature of the claim made, or of the relief or remedy required in  the action . . .” Accordingly, it is the usual practice to indorse a relief for a permanent injunction on the writ of summons. This, however, does not seem obligatory, as it is provided under Order 50, r 14 that:

“In any cause or matter in which an injunction has been, or might have been claimed, the plaintiff may, before or after judgment, apply for an injunction to restrain the defendant or respondent from the repetition or continuance of the wrongful act or breach of contract complained of, or from the commission of any injury or breach of contract of a like kind relating to the same property or right, or arising out of the same contract: and the Court or a Judge may grant the injunction, either upon or without terms, as may be just.” (My emphasis.)

Even without this rule, it would seem that the court has an inherent jurisdiction to grant an injunction if the justice of the case requires it, even though not expressly asked for by the party in whose favour it is decreed. Per Wiredu J, as he then was, in Anthony v University College of Cape Coast [1973] 1 GLR 299 at 311.

STAY OF EXECUTION

It has been our experience that almost every litigant against whom a judgment or order is pronounced, runs back to the court usually out of instinct rather than anything else, to request that enforcement of the order be stayed for a while. A litigant may do this from divers motives: to organise himself to discharge the obligation; to stall execution proceedings for their own sake; to embarrass the judgment-creditor; or simply to make it impossible for the creditor to reap the fruits of his victory. Knowing that motives may be mixed, the rules have set specific guidelines to assist the courts in considering such applications. Where the rules are silent (since rules by their nature cannot cover every conceivable situation) the courts have themselves defined the criteria for granting them.

When an application, is made, the first duty of the court is to ensure that the judgment or order sought to be stayed is capable of being executed. Otherwise a stay is not called for. An application may be necessary while a case is pending, or it may be made after final judgment, whether an appeal is pending or not. In some situations, a judgment-debtor need not ask for a stay. The judgment simply cannot be executed, even though it is executory for example:

(a)  within 7 days immediately following a judgment;

(b) within 7 days immediately following a refusal of the court below to grant a stay;

(c) when the court makes a winding-up order against a company, execution of all judgments against that company is stayed;

(d) upon the filing of an application for a stay. That is when an application is made to a court for an interim order, that court becomes seised of the matter to the extent covered by the prayer contained in the application, and until the court makes an order in the matter it is improper conduct for a legal practitioner to advise the doing of the very act which the application is intended to prevent. For it amounts to flouting the authority of the court. It is not a legitimate exercise of a legal practitioner’s duty either to his client or to the court. See Akufo-Addo JSC in Joseph v Jebeile [1963] 1 GLR 387 at 392; and

(e) where the judgment orders payment of money by instalments, execution cannot issue until after default (Order 42, r 40).

Orders made in the course of a trial for the payment of any sum of money or any costs may be enforced by a writ of execution, save that the court or a judge may, at or after the time of making the order, stay execution until such time as they or he shall think fit. (See Order 42, rule 16(1)(b)). This is fairly straightforward, except that it must be emphasised that this rule relates to orders for the payment of money only, and does not give the court jurisdiction in any other situation. The other rule, not often invoked is Order 42, r 49, that:

“Whenever an action shall be pending in the Court against the holder of a previous judgment of the Court by the persons against whom the judgment was given, the Court may, if it appear [sic] just and reasonable to do so, stay execution of the judgment either absolutely or on such terms as it may think just, until a judgment shall be given in the pending action.”

Thus if A holds a judgment against B, and B sues A, the court may stop A from enforcing his judgment, until B’s action has been determined. Under the same Order 42, r 16 (1) (b) execution of any judgment for the payment of money or of costs may be stayed by the court or a judge, “at or after the time of giving judgment.” This rule maybe invoked whether an appeal has been lodged or not, so long as the judgment or part of it orders the payment of money of any kind. But what if the judgment has no reference to the payment of money? That situation, outside the courts’ inherent jurisdiction, may be covered (be it partially) by Order 42, rule 25. It provides:

“25. No proceeding by audita querela shall hereafter be used; but any party against whom judgment has been given may apply to the Court or a Judge for a stay of execution or other relief against such judgment, upon the ground of facts which have arisen too late to be pleaded; and the Court or Judge may give such relief and upon such terms as may be just.” (My emphasis.)

EXECUTION PENDING APPEAL

The mere fact that a person has appealed does not mean that execution of the judgment appealed from must be stayed. An appeal does not operate as a stay. It follows that an appellant, if he desires a stay, must apply for it. The application goes first to the trial court, and if refused, it may be repeated in the appellate court. An application that has been granted on terms, no matter how harsh and unfavourable to the applicant, cannot be said to have been refused. The applicant cannot therefore apply de novo to the appellate court to have the terms revised. The proper course is to appeal. (See Frans v Oppan & Co Ltd [1984-86] GLRD 35, CA.)

A stay will normally not be granted as to costs. (Compare Order 42, rule 16.) In considering any application for a stay pending appeal, the courts look at a number of factors, depending on the circumstances. These include:

(a) that the substructure of the appeal will not disappear if the appeal succeeds;

(b) that the appellant cannot be adequately compensated in money;

(c) that the appellant will not suffer irreparable loss or injury;

(d) that there are special circumstances which make it imperative that the application should be granted.

All this boils down to the usual professional phrase that the appeal, if it is successful will not be rendered nugatory. To quote from the Jebeile case at page 389:

“Since the respondent’s [landlord’s] right to the possession of the premise depended entirely on whether the applicants tenants owed any rent at the date of the issue of the writ, it was our view that if the applicants were ejected [pursuant to the judgment] and the result of their appeal proved that they did not owe any rent then their ejectment would have been without just cause, and the success of their appeal would be of to use to them whatever. It was upon these considerations that we exercised our discretion in ordering a stay of execution . . .”

And at 390, giving general guidelines, the learned judge continued:

“While we do not wish to say anything that may be interpreted as a fetter on the exercise of the discretion of a trial judge when he considers an application for stay of execution pending appeal we think it necessary in the interest of justice to say generally that when such an application is considered in a case involving, inter alia, the payment of money, the main consideration should be not so much that the victorious party is being deprived of the fruits of his victory, as what the position of a defeated party would be who had had to pay up or surrender some legal right only to find himself successful on appeal. In this respect it is wholly immaterial what view a trial judge takes of the correctness of his own judgment or of the would-be appellant’s chances on appeal, if the position (it is not of course suggested that that is the position in the case before us) is that the victorious party is unlikely to be able to refund amount paid to him, or the defeated party to be restored to the status quo ante, in the event of a successful appeal (and it should not be difficult to determine the likelihood of such an event), then it would be palpably unjust to refuse stay of execution, or, when stay of execution is refused, not to order the judgment-creditor to give good, substantial and realisable security for the refund of the money involved.

Generally speaking it is not our view that the policy of the law in this country should be against staying execution pending appeal especially where the large sums of money are involved, and we would urge that when execution is stayed it should, where the circumstances permit, be on the condition that the judgment-debtor pay into court the amount of money involved, or, when refused, on the condition that the judgment-creditor give security as aforesaid and approved by the judge.

Any situation created by a judicial act, done either inadvertently or callously, which makes it impossible for a successful appellant to recover money paid, or any interest in property or other legal rights surrendered, under a judgment vacated on appeal does a disservice to the course of justice, if only because it undermines public confidence in the administration of justice.”

Per Akufo-Addo in Joseph v Jebeile [1961] G.L.R. 387 at 390.

My advice to you is that whenever you are faced with an application for a stay, refresh your memory from these lines, and you will hardly go wrong. See also Ohene Mensah v Anderson, Civil Motion, 35/82, 2 March 1982, SC, unreported, per Anin JSC, sitting as a single judge; and Ebee v Ebee [1961] GLR 432 at 433. Per Ollennu J (as he then was). Seeing that the courts have full control over their judgments and orders and therefore an inherent jurisdiction to decide when an order should be or should not be effective. I have often wondered whether there is much point in the rules as to stay, particularly so as the courts have “an absolute and unfettered discretion” to grant or refuse a stay, and as to the terms upon which to grant it. (See Halsbury’s Laws of England (3rd ed), Vol 16, para 51, p 35).

In the last analysis; it is all a matter of worldly sense; not to say commonsense, always focussing on your primary task of holding the balance evenly between party and party, and ensuring that one party does not take undue advantage of the other or of the circumstances, to the prejudice of his opponent.

MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: JUDICIAL SAYINGS [1987-88] VOL. XVI 32—35

Disqualification as Director

“Learned counsel made a point of the stigma and hardship that naturally attaches to the order of disqualification [as a director under section 186 of the Companies Code, 1963]. But it is a case of balancing the interest of the individual against the well-being of the public. In the bustle of the pursuit of our various vocations, the law stands a silent sentinel as protector of the public. The public needs protection for their investments or credit, whether as shareholders or members of the public at large, from over zealous and fraudulent business adventurers and even from well meaning but misguided directors” per Mensa Boison JA in In re West Coast Dyeing Industry Ltd; Adams v Tandoh, Court of Appeal, 7 March 1985; digested in [1984-86] GLRD 131.

Fraud

“Fraud, like cancer, calls for swift remedy. It must be uprooted. Therefore, when fraud is brought to the court’s notice and there is credible evidence to support it the court is obliged to deal with it swiftly and decidedly” per Osei-Hwere JA in In re West Coast Dyeing Industry Ltd, Adams v Tandoh, Court of Appeal, 7 March 1985: digested in [1984-86] GLRD 131.

Judicial Attitude to Fraud

“Clearly because he [the trial judge] had other less disquieting grounds on which to set aside the judgment, he must have thought he should in the greater interest of justice pass over the charge of fraud in a non-committal fashion.

I am afraid the revolutionary changes which have taken place in this country and which have breathed some new lease of life into some of our well-known notions and conceptions of legality, do not permit this approach to adjudication. Whatever the previous legal position may have been in regard to deciding cases in the interest of justice in such a manner as to give as little pain as possible to parties in litigation, I think the subsisting enabling provision of section 1(1)(c) of PNDCL 42, which enjoins the judiciary to eradicate corrupt practices from the body-politic as a matter of state policy, do militate against this passive attitude of glossing over corrupt and dishonest conduct of parties in a suit. In my respectful opinion in order to eradicate such corrupt practices and other dishonest conduct and thus fulfil our constitutional mandate, judges at trials are in duty bound to make pronouncements on the merits of fraud charges which form the issue or part of the issues in trials before their courts” per Taylor JSC in Dahabieh v Turqui, Court of Appeal, 21 April, 1988, Civil Appeal No 73/86, to be digested in [1987-88] GLRD.

Legal Justice and Emotions

“The facts demonstrate clearly the benefit all the parties would derive if the family is allowed to intervene in the overall promotion of unity and the avoidance of scars that cannot be hidden and wounds that never heal, both perpetual sources of irritation and disharmony in the family. The legal machinery functions as a cold analytical mechanism yielding no place for emotive considerations. Humanitarian expansiveness too readily becomes subversive of the legal order. However in the intimate enclave of the family unit, humanitarian issues can be aired and resolved, uninhibited by legal rules” per Francois JSC in In re Sackitey (Deceased) Dzamioja v Sackitey, Court, of Appeal, 14 July 1988, Civil Appeal No 142/82, to be digested in [1987-88] GLRD.

Two Heads are Better than One

“When many minds participate in considering a problem, so many subtle influences dictate the attitude of each participant that it is not unusual for the parties to come to diametrically opposed views. In such circumstances, no matter how right an individual considers his opinion to be, he must nonetheless have the intellectual discipline to appreciate that no one has a monopoly of wisdom and that what he so jealously holds to be absolute truth may very well not be the real truth. The much quoted aphorism: two heads are better than one, is not an idle precept” per Taylor JSC in Kwaye v Bisi & Ors, Supreme Court, 8 November 1988, Civil Suit No 16/88, to be digested in [1987-88] GLRD. Quotable-Quote.

Hardship

“The great difficulty in such cases as the present is to free one’s mind from the influence exercised upon it by a knowledge that a party is suffering from hardship arising from a fault not perhaps his own. Yet it is a most dangerous thing to stretch the law to cover individual hardship” per Hector Macleod J in Bordoh v Aryebbir, Full Court, 20 November, 1883 cited with approval by Taylor JSC in Darke IX v Darke IV, Supreme Court, 30 July 1985; digested in [1984-86] GLRD 59

Judicial Precedence and Injustice

“The customary rule [that the head of family cannot be sued for accounts] . . . is productive of injustice and provides a potent shield for the breach of fiduciary duties. After all, the law must adapt itself to changing social conditions and those precedents are inapplicable to modern conditions. There is some question whether doing this amount to judicial legislation. Refusing to follow an obviously unjust precedent cannot rightly be construed as judicial legislation. We have constitutional authority to refuse to be bound by a precedent which injures the innocent, benefits the guilty and puts a premium on blatant breach of fiduciary duty. To do otherwise, would be an exhibition of judicial inertia wholly indefensible in our day and age” per Apaloo CJ in Hansen v Ankrah I, Supreme Court, 21 March 1985; Civil Appeal No 2/83, to be digested in [1987-88] GLRD.

Chiefs must not Done Legalistic Garb

“I think it cannot be over-emphasised that if our revered chiefs would resist the temptation of trapesing the maize of legal rules and procedures and restrict themselves to the sphere where they tower above everyone else, they would be providing greater service to Ghana and advancing the customary law” per Francois JSC in Pomaa and Others v Fosuhene, Supreme Court, 17 June 1987, Chieftaincy Appeal No 3/86 to be digested in [1987-88] GLRD.

Benefit from a Contract

“It always comes ill from the mouth of a man who has benefited from a contract to resist the performance of his obligation on the ground that the contract was illegal. It is particularly odious where that man takes the point only after he has attempted deliberately to perpetrate a bare-faced deception on the court and has been found out” per Asare-Kwapong J in Appiah v Nkrumah, High Court, Koforidua, 17 June 1986; digested in [1984-86] GLRD 141.

Proof in Law

“Whilst no court will treat reliance upon pleadings as pedantory or mere formalism but will accept their function as ascertaining with precision the matters on which the parties differ and the points on which they agree and thus arrive at a certain clear issues on which both parties desire a judicial decision, yet I venture to say that it is the duty of the defendant to negative by his evidence (or by some other proof in court) the case which the plaintiff has alleged against him in the witness-box” per Osei-Hwere JA in Patu-Styles v Amoo-Lamptey, Supreme Court, 16 January 1984; digested in [1984-86] GLRD 138.

Judicial Feelings

“I unhesitatingly reject without any reservation whatsoever [the] emotional approach to legal problems . . . I am not prepared to ignore the implication of clear legal provisions unless doubts on the said implications are planted in my mind by the cogent or even plausible argument. In the absence of that, I will true to my oath, give effect to statutory provisions uninfluenced by any extraneous considerations . . .” per Taylor JSC in Patu-Styles v Amoo-Lamptey, Supreme Court, 16 January 1984; digested in [1984-86] GLRD 138.

The Life of the Law has not been Logic

“Holmes has given us the classic phrase ‘the life of the law has not been logic . . .’ But I am yet to read that an illogical reasoning is a virtue at law” per Adade JSC in Bisi v Tabiri alias Asare, Court of Appeal, Accra, 18 July 1984; digested in [1984-86] GLRD 109.

Acceptance of a Party’s Case

“To accede to learned counsel’s argument will be to lay down a rule that a court must be able, at all times, to accept the whole of a party’s case as pleaded, or else it must dismiss it. Such a rule has never been, and, hopefully, will never be, the basis of the administration of our laws” per Adade JSC in Bisi v Tabiri alias Asare [1984-86] GLRD 109.

23 June 1988
