KUBI AND OTHERS v DALI: A NEW SOURCE OF TAX REVENUE? [1987- 88] VOL. XVI RGL 240—249

Mills, J.E.A.

 

OFFICERS of our tax collection agencies, now desperately searching for every legitimate means of increasing their tax collections, should be grateful to the Court of Appeal for drawing attention to one source of tax revenue that has so far remained untapped.

In Kubi v Dali,1 the plaintiff, a 45-year old trader and a fare-paying passenger on the defendants' vehicle, was seriously injured in an accident while travelling to the market where she had intended to sell her goods.  Apart from the serious physical injuries that she sustained, all her goods were completely burnt.  Finding the defendants guilty of negligence, the trial judge awarded the plaintiff a global sum of ¢281,076 broken down as follows:—

¢

1. special damages   ..          ..           ..           5,500

2. goods destroyed   ..          ..           ..          51,576

3. loss of actual earnings      ..           ..          13,000

4. loss of future earnings      ..           ..          180,000

5. permanent incapacity       ..           ..            20,000

6. pain and suffering            ..           ..             8,000

7. loss of amenities of life    ..           ..              3,000

Total         ..          ..           ..           ..           281,076

On appeal, counsel for the defendants contended, inter alia, that having regard to the evidence, the amounts of ¢13,000 and ¢180,000  awarded by the trial judge for "loss of actual earnings" and "loss of future earnings," respectively, were excessive.  In an unanimous decision, the Court of Appeal dismissed this contention by counsel.

In the view of Abban JA, who delivered the judgment of the court, there was sufficient evidence to support the award of ¢13,000 for loss of actual earnings and there was, therefore, no justification for disturbing the trial judge's finding. On the question of the ¢180,000 awarded for loss of future earnings, while the learned judge felt that the five years' purchase used by the trial judge was on the low side, he nevertheless ruled that, in the absence of any wrong application of principles by the learned trial judge in arriving at his final figure, it would be improper to disturb his finding on the sole ground that if the Court of Appeal had tried the case in the first instance, it would have used a much higher number of years of purchase.

That in dismissing counsel's contention, Abban JA was only applying the principles of law that have for long been accepted as indicating the correct line of approach to be adopted by our courts in such matters, cannot be disputed. What is, however, noteworthy about this case, and should therefore excite the interest of judges, lawyers and taxmen, is the following excerpt from the learned judge's judgment:

"However, as I pointed out earlier on, when I was dealing with the award of ¢13,000 under loss of earnings, the learned trial judge, even though he rightly took into account exigencies of life and other relevant factors when scaling down, he unfortunately omitted to consider one other essential element.  That is, in scaling down the awards under 'loss of earnings' and 'loss of future earnings' the learned trial judge failed to take into account the tax liability of the plaintiff.  The income tax laws of this country impose an obligation on all income eaners of a certain category to pay taxes on their earnings. The plaintiff, without doubt, falls  under that category, and the fact that she stated in her evidence that she had not been paying taxes did not absolve her from liability. Thus the amount of damages awarded for loss of earnings should take into account the income tax which the plaintiff would have had to pay if she had continued to trade or work: see British Transport Commission v Gourley [1956] AC 185, HL. If compensation for loss of earnings were to be assessed without any dimunition for income tax, an injured plaintiff would be  receiving more than he would have received if he had not been injured and so would be put in a more beneficial state. This could only mean that the court would be calling upon the defendant in such a situation to pay the plaintiff more than what the plaintiff would have received if he or she had remained able to carry on his or her trade.

Obviously this will be against all principles. In the present case, since the learned trial judge erred in not taking into account the tax liability of the plaintiff in awarding the ¢13,000 as her loss of earnings for three months and the ¢180,000 as her loss of future earnings, these figures should be reduced by the income tax exigible on them."2

Noting that, "it is impossible to assess with accuracy or precision what deductions should be made by reason of the tax liability,"3 the learned judge estimated the tax exigible at one-third of the total amount awarded for loss of earnings, actual and prospective, and ordered that the amount of ¢64,333, representing the estimated tax, be paid over to the Commissioner of Income Tax.

VALIDITY OF RULINGS

It needs to be pointed out that the failure of the trial judge to take into account the tax element in awarding damages for loss of earnings, actual and prospective, is not an isolated case.  Indeed, to the best of the present writer's knowledge,4 our courts have never taken the tax  element into consideration in awarding such damages.  The reason may well be that they have never been invited to do so . But be that as it may, does it then mean that Abban JA applied the wrong legal principles in arriving at his ruling?  It is submitted that in attempting to answer this broad question, we need to find answers to the following three related questions: firstly, is there any legal authority for the proposition that the tax element be taken into account in awarding damages for loss of earnings? secondly if so, how is the tax to be assessed? and thirdly, is it the defendant or the tax authorities who should  "benefit" from the tax deduction?

(i) The Tax Element in Damages Awarded for Loss of Earnings

As indicated by Earl Jowitt in the case of British Transport Commission v Gourley,5 until 1933 the question whether the tax position of the injured party should be considered in assessing damages for loss of earnings had never been raised in the English courts. The reason as the learned judge surmised, was probably that:

"No doubt in the old days tax was so small that it may have been thought not worthwhile [sic] to take account of it.  In the great majority of running-down cases the question would not have arisen and in those days the amount of damages was assessed by a jury under appropriate directions from the judge."6

The first case in which this question came up for decision was that of Fairholme v Firth and Brown Ltd7 in which the managing director of a company had been wrongfully dismissed.  In awarding damages, du Parcq J refused to take the tax position of the plaintiff into account in assessing damages on the ground that:

"I should be reluctant to give a decision which would seek to alter an inveterate practice unless I were convinced that the practice is inconsistent with principle, and unjust, and I am not so convinced in this case.  On the contrary, I am of opinion that it is right in principle to have no regard, in assessing damages as between master and servant, to the servant's liability to the Crown which is truly res inter alios acta."8

Even though in the next case of M'Daid v Clyde Navigation Trustees,9 Lord Sorn decided to deviate from this "inveterate practice" on the ground that to ignore the tax element "would be to act in a manner which was out of touch with reality,"10 English courts11 continued to follow this questionable practice.

BRITISH TRANSPORT COMMISSION v. GOURLEY

It was this case, decided by the House of Lords in 1955, which effectively put an end to the "inveterate practice" of the English courts.  The facts of this case were rather simple.  An eminent civil engineer was gravely and permanently injured through the negligence of the servants of the defendants, British Transport Commission.  Though he was 65 years of age and made a remarkable recovery, he remained very much handicapped and his earnings, actual and prospective were considerably reduced.  In the Queen's Bench Division of the High Court, he was awarded, inter alia, £37,720 for loss of earnings, actual and prospective.  In awarding this figure, however, the learned trial judge, following the "inveterate practice" adopted in Billingham v Hughes12 refused to take the tax element into account.  The learned judge, however, at the request of the defendants made an alternative assessment of £6,695, representing the sum which he would have awarded if he had taken the plaintiff’s tax liability into account. This decision was affirmed by the Court of Appeal.

On appeal to the House of Lords, the question that came up for determination was whether the judge ought to have taken the tax position of the plaintiff into account in assessing damages for loss of earnings. The House of Lords ruled, by a majority of six to one,13 that the trial judge ought to have taken the plaintiff's tax position into account and therefore reduced the award to £6,695.

Earl Jowitt took the opportunity to lay down the following as the correct principle to be applied by the courts in awarding such damages:

"My Lords, it is I think, if I may say so with the utmost respect, fallacious to consider the problem as though a benefit were being conferred upon a wrongdoer by allowing him to abate the damages for which he would otherwise be liable.  The problem is rather for what damages is he liable? and if we apply the dominant rule, we should answer: ‘He is liable for uch damages as, by reason of his wrongdoing, the plaintiff has sustained.’

I cannot think that the risk of confusion arising if the position be taken into consideration should make us hesitate to apply the rule of law if we can ascertain what that rule is.  Nor should we be deterred from applying that rule by the consistent or inveterate practice of the courts in not taking the tax position into consideration in those cases in which the courts were never invited to do so."14

The rationale behind this principle, as the learned judge went on to explain, is that:

“. . . to ignore the tax element at the present day would be to act in a manner which is out of touch with reality.  Nor can I regard the tax element as so remote that it should be so disregarded in assessing damages. The obligation to pay tax-save for those in possession of exiguous incomes - is almost universal in its application.  That obligation is ever present in the minds of those who are called upon to pay taxes, and no sensible person any longer regards the net earnings from his trade or profession as the equivalent of his available income.  Indeed, save for the fact that in many cases-though by no means in all cases—the tax only becomes payable after the money has been received, there is, I think, no element of remoteness or uncertainty about its incidence."15

It is worth pointing out that the force of these arguments advanced by the House of Lords in support of its decision was so strong that Lord Tucker (who voted with the majority) was compelled to make the following admission:

"My Lords, having now heard this point argued three times-twice in your Lordships' House and once in the Court of Appeal-I am persuaded that the decision in Billingham v Hughes, to which I was a party in the Court of Appeal, was erroneous."16

It can thus be seen that Abban JA's ruling on this issue was not only the result of the application of the correct principle in such cases but also a trail-blazing effort on his part to set the records straight by putting our judges on the right course.  The learned judge, therefore, deserves our commendation for his "pioneering" effort.

(ii) Assessment of Tax Exigible

Even for tax officers, who are specially trained for this exercise, the assessment of tax on actual earnings, based on current tax laws, is not easy.  The correct assessment can only be made where the tax officer is in possession of all the necessary details about the taxpayer, such as, incomes from other sources, expenses, details about the tax-payer's family and life insurance payments, to mention only a few.  This explains why income tax assessments cannot be finalised until the taxpayer has filed a full and detailed income tax return for the appropriate year.  The assessment of tax on future earnings would certainly pose more serious problems for even the most efficient and knowledgeable tax officer. This is simply because the tax officer would have to determine a person's tax liability on the basis of future tax  levels and rules which even he cannot predict.  In the light of this, little imagination is required to appreciate the difficulties that judges (who are not trained tax officers) are likely to face in determining the tax payable on loss of earnings, actual and prospective.

Earl Jowitt clearly had this in mind when he warned that:

"It would, I think, be unfortunate if, as the result of our decision, the fixation of damages in a running-down case were to involve an elaborate assessment of tax liability . . It is impossible to assess with mathematical accuracy what reduction should be made by reason of the tax position, just as it is impossible to assess with mathematical accuracy the amount of damages which should be awarded for the injury itself and for pain and suffering endured."17

Lord Goddard alluded to the same difficulties when he stated that:

"We were not informed what the calculations were which had resulted in this figure.  But in considering special damage in these cases the rate of tax to be taken must, as it seems to me, be the effective rate of income tax, and, if necessary, surtax, which would have been applicable to the sums in question if they had been earned.  That rate depends on the combination of a number of factors that may vary with each case - allowances, reduced rates, surtax rates, other income of the claimant or his wife, charges or reliefs.  The task of determining it may not always be an easy one . ."18

But the difficulties inherent in this exercise should not deter judges from performing their judicial functions.  As Earl Jowitt explained:

"It will no doubt become necessary for the tribunal assessing damages to form an estimate of what the tax would have been if the money had been earned, but such an estimate will be none the worse if it is formed on broad lines, even though it may be described as rough and ready."19

Lord Goddard offered a similar advice when he stated that:

". . . in complicated cases it is to be hoped that the parties, with the help of accountants, will be able to agree [sic] figures. If not, the court must do its best to arrive at a reasonable figure, even though it cannot be said to be an exact one."20

Once again, it is evident that in estimating the tax exigible at one-third the total amount awarded for loss of earnings, Abban JA was only trying to make the best out of a rather difficult situation, even if the result was rough and ready.  The learned judge can, therefore, not be faulted on his estimate of tax exigible in as much the same way as judges cannot be faulted for their estimates of awards for such items as physical incapacity, pain and suffering and loss of amenities of life.  It is submitted that in view of the difficulties and problems already outlined, no accountant or tax officer, if he had been called upon to assist the court in the case, could have provided, on the spur of the moment, any more accurate estimate of the taxpayer's tax liability than Abban JA's efforts yielded.

(iii) Who is to BENEFIT from the Deduction of the Tax Element?

The question that needs to be answered is simply this: Is the tax element to be used to reduce the amount of damages payable by the defendant, or should it be paid over to the commissioner of income tax, as directed by Abban JA?  If the answer is the former then it means that damages awarded for loss of earnings, whether "gross" or "net", are not taxable in the hands of the plaintiff.  If it is the latter, then it means that such damages are not tax exempt.

Position under English Law

It would appear that under English law it is generally accepted that sums awarded as damages for loss of earnings are not taxable in the hands of the plaintiff. Indeed, in British Transport Commission v Gourley,21 Earl Jowitt appears to have lent his support to this interpretation of the English law when he remarked that: "[I]t was agreed by counsel on both sides—and I think rightly agreed—that the respondent would incur no tax liability in respect of the award of £37,720, or alternatively of £6,695."22 But the same judge was later to express his doubts about the legality of their interpretation when he stated that:

"I express no opinion as to what the answer ... would have been if it had been the subject of a judicial decision.  There may well be a difference between actions for personal injuries and actions for wrongful dismissal in regard to the obligation of the plaintiff to pay tax on the amount received, and cases on the one topic may therefore be a dangerous guide to follow on the other."23

Whether this interpretation of the English law is legally supportable or not, it needs to be pointed out that its application produces certain undesirable results, a major factor which clearly influenced Lord Keith's decision to dissent from the majority opinion in the case of British Transport Commission v Gourley. As the learned judge stated:

"I feel great difficulty in the view that the incidence of taxation on an injured taxpayer should be any concern of the wrongdoer and should be used to minimize an award of damages in his favour. To many it may seem somewhat hard that the more tax a man has paid before he meets with an accident the less damages relatively will he recover from the person who has injured him."24

Position under Ghana Law

Though the question as to whether damages awarded for loss of earnings are taxable or not was not specifically addressed by Abban JA in his judgment, the inference to be drawn from his ruling that the tax element deducted be paid over to the commissioner of income tax is that they are taxable under Ghana law.

This ruling, it is submitted, is an accurate interpretation of our law.  That any income of any person accruing in, devised from, brought into, or received in Ghana in respect of gains or profits from any trade, business, profession, vocation or employment is taxable is clearly stated in paragraph 1 of the Income Tax Decree, 1975 (SMCD 5). This provision therefore covers any income from any of these sources, whether actual or prospective received by the taxpayer.  Does it make any difference that this income is received by the taxpayer as part of damages on account of injuries sustained by the taxpayer?  To be able to answer this question it would be helpful to refer to the relevant provision of the Income Tax Decree, 1975, which provides as follows:

"(1) There shall be exempted from tax: . . .

(g) Capital sums received by way of retirement on account of old age, or retirement on account of sickness or other infirmity of mind or body, or as death gratuities or compensation for death or injuries."25

It is clear from this provision that the type of compensation exempted from tax is the kind awarded to the taxpayer for the injuries actually sustained, such as physical deformity, incapacity, pain and suffering and loss of amenities.  Damages for loss of earnings are awarded as a consequence of the injury but not for the injury itself, and are therefore not tax-exempt.  Abban JA's ruling on this score, it is submitted, is a true reflection of our laws.

CONCLUSION

That Abban JA applied the correct principles of law in arriving at his rulings in this case, it is hoped, has been amply demonstrated in the foregoing discussion. The judgment in the case, no doubt, imposes an additional responsibility, albeit onerous, on our judges. However, the fact that in awarding damages for loss of earnings our judges have to temporarily assume the role of tax officers should not deter them from performing their judicial functions.  Afterall, very often our judges are called upon to don the mantle of doctors, engineers, surveyors and accountants, to mention only some few professions.  And yet, in spite of their lack of training in these areas, they have performed their tasks with considerable success. As Abban JA has shown, assuming the role of temporary tax officers should pose no serious problems for them.  In any case, as Lord Goddard26 advised, in complicated cases they can fall upon tax officers or accountants for assistance.
