MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: EXECUTION [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 53—60

by Enoch D Kom, LLB (Lond) FGA of Gray’s Inn Barrister-at-Law. SAG

 

 

INTRODUCTION

The method of execution depends largely on the reliefs claimed and granted by a trial court. Matters of execution come before the courts in the following manner:

(i) stay of execution of a judgment;

(ii) interpleader summons in cases of execution by a writ of fifa;

(iii) setting aside an auction sale of property;

(iv) application for garnishee order;

(v) summons to show cause,

(vi) application for writ of possession and delivery

(vii) attachment for contempt; and

(viii) leave to go into execution.

1. Stay of Execution

There are two types of applications for stay of execution and these are:

(i) stay of execution coupled with an application to set aside a default judgment; and

(ii) stay of execution pending an appeal.

The contents of the supporting affidavit in each case are different.

In the first case the applicant must depose in his affidavit:

(i) why he failed to either enter an appearance, or file a defence or to attend court on the date fixed for hearing;

(ii) facts and/or points of law to show that he has a good defence to the action so that the vacation of the default judgment will serve a useful purpose; and

(iii) facts to show that it would be unjust for the case not to be heard on its merits.

These are matters which, should in the normal course influence a judge to vacate a judgment and stay execution. There are many local decisions on the setting aside of default judgments and stay of execution, but a recent decision of the Court of Appeal in the case of A T Lutterodt v Mensah Nyarko [1984-86] GLRD 34, CA is of interest. There Adade JSC said:

“The defendants’ conduct following the judgment of the High Court has been reprehensible and provocative in the extreme bordering on contempt of the court, and even of the judicial system itself. These matters were commented upon and justifiably taken into account by this court in its ruling of 5 February 1985. The impression, however, should on no account be created that the defendants are being short-shrifted and denied a hearing on the merits simply because they had indulged in ill-advised extra-judicial methods to attain their objectives. True, there must be an end to litigation. But it is equally important that these courts do not, in the process, sacrifice the need to give all parties a fair hearing on the merits—from which it arises that so long as his opponent can be adequately compensated in costs, an applicant must in fairness be let in to be heard on the merits of his case. It will not be fair, however, if the respondent stands to suffer irreparable damage from the grant of the application.”

In the case of stay of execution pending an appeal the supporting affidavit must contain:

(i) the grounds of appeal to be relied upon at the appeal court;

(ii) facts to show (a) that greater hardship would be caused to the judgment-debtor by the judgment not being stayed than to the judgment-creditor; (b) that the judgment of the appeal court will be rendered nugatory in the likely event of his winning the appeal if the judgment-creditor goes into execution.

In that case the trial judge must balance the hardship to the judgment-debtor and his likelihood of winning appeal, and the judgment-creditor being deprived of the fruits of his legal victory. The principle which will guide the court is laid down succinctly in Joseph v Jebeile [1963] 1 GLR 387, SC and explained further in Abudulai v Agyei [1976] 1 GLR 185 at 192:

“It is a well-settled principle of practice that the only grounds on which, under the rules, the court would stay execution of a judgment or order for payment of money are either that there are special circumstances which render it inexpedient to enforce the judgment or order or that the judgment-debtor is unable from any cause to pay the money. In the case of a stay of execution pending appeal, it has never been the practice of the court to deprive a successful litigant of the fruit of his litigation by locking up funds to which prima facie he is entitled pending an appeal: see Monk v Bartram [1891] 1 QB 346, CA. But where a party is appealing, exercising his undoubted right of appeal, the court ought to see that the appeal if successful is not nugatory: see Wilson v Church (No 2) (1879) 12 ChD 454 at p 458, CA. Becker v Earl’s Court, Ltd (1911) 56 SJ 206, CA is an authority for the proposition that to grant or refuse a stay is in the discretion of the court and that the court will grant it where the special circumstances of the case so require. As a general rule a stay will always be granted where it is shown that if the damages and costs were paid there will be no reasonable probability of getting them back if the appeal succeeds: see Atkins v Great Western RY Co (1886) 2 TLR 400, such as where the judgment-creditor is a person resident out of or about to leave the jurisdiction, see: Wootton v Sievier (1913) 30 TLR 165, or where the judgment-creditor is indisputedly [sic] a man of straw.”

Trial judges must remember that if stay of execution is refused by them the judgment-debtor has another chance to have as it were, a second bite at the cherry (see LI 1001 and LI 1002 amending respectively Order 58 r 7, and LI 218, r 27). Often when the second application is made at the appellate court, neither the judgment of the court below nor the ruling dismissing the application for stay of execution is ready. These are necessary to support the second application and an appellate court would be happy to know why the judgment-debtor lost the action and on what grounds his application for stay was refused.

2. Sheriff, Interpleader Summons, Order 57

Here I must limit myself to the discussion of rules 7 and 8 of Order 57. These read:

“7. If the  claimants  appear   in pursuance of the summons, the Court or a Judge may order either that any claimant be made a defendant in any action already commenced in respect of the subject-matter in dispute in lieu of or in addition to the applicant, or that an issue between the claimants be stated and tried, and in the latter case may direct which of the claimants is to be plaintiff, and which defendant.

8. The Court or a Judge may, with the consent of both claimants or on the  request of any claimant, if, having regard to the value of the subject-matter in dispute, it seems desirable so to do, dispose of the merits of their claims, and decide the same in a summary manner and on such terms as may be just.”

Often what happens as soon as the claimant and judgment-creditor appear in court is that the judge orders that the claimant should issue a writ against the judgment-creditor and thus another litigation is set in motion which may take years to decide.

Meanwhile the judgment-creditor waits and prays and the judgment becomes more and more worthless. Some of these cases of interpleader can be dealt with summarily under rule 8, e.g. where a car, television, sound system, fridge or a house is fi fa—ed the documents of purchase will show clearly whether the claimant’s claim is genuine or is a delay tactics being orchestrated on behalf of the judgment-creditor. In such cases ordering a writ of summons to be issued, pleadings to be filed and summons for directions to be taken, deprive the judgment-creditor of the fruits of his victory. More use must be made of rule 8 to dispose of such claims in a summary manner.

It is interesting to note that for very good policy reasons, an appeal against a decision or judgment in an interpleader suit is interlocutory, i.e. 14 days within which to appeal. (See Order 57, r 11 (4), RT Briscoe v Amponsah (1969) CC 100, CA and Agati v Agbenaku [1978] 1 GLR 14, CA).

3. Setting Aside an Auction Sale, Order 51, Rule 18

Where there was no interpleader or there was one which has however been dismissed the judgment-debtor may apply to set aside a sale of his property sold in execution of a judgment debt and costs; Order 51, rule 18 requires a judgment-debtor:

(i) to bring his application within 21 days from the date of sale. Extension of time to bring the application can be sought for and granted under Order 64, r 4;

(ii) must show by his supporting affidavit that there was a material irregularity in the conduct of the sale; and

(iii) must show that he has sustained substantial injury by reason of such irregularity.

If these three requirements are not fulfilled an application must not be granted.

Lastly, it must be pointed out that the right “to apply to have a sale set aside, is only for the judgment-debtor and all persons whose interest in the property is identical with, or subordinate to that of the judgment-debtor. It has no reference to persons who claim title adverse to that of the judgment-debtor; such persons cannot suffer any injury by reason of the sale”.  See Akyeawaah v Poku [1960] GLR 110 at 112.

4. Garnishee Order (Attachment of Debt) Order 45.

Where a third person has funds due to a judgment-debtor then the judgment-creditor may apply by motion ex partes for a garnishee order nisi to issue against such a person. The supporting affidavit must state:

(a)  that judgment has been obtained;

(b)  that to the deponent’s knowledge and belief a named person or company is indebted to the judgment-debtor;

(c)  that it is still unsatisfied, and the amount that remains is still unpaid;

(d)  that such debtor to the judgment-debtor is within the jurisdiction of the court; and

(e)  that all debts owing or accruing from such third person to the judgment-debtor be attached to satisfy the outstanding judgment debt together with costs of the garnishee proceedings.

On the day fixed for hearing the court may order such third person to appear and show cause why he should not pay to the judgment-creditor the moneys due to the judgment-debtor which he holds, or so much of it as may satisfy the judgment debt and costs.

5. Summons to Show Cause, Order 69

This method of execution is now less used either because it is revolting for a person to be imprisoned for non-payment of debt or because of the difficulty in the judgment-creditor proving the means of the judgment-debtor. Be that as it may, Order 69, rule 12 specifically mentions or states matters which a judge must take into consideration before making an order committing the judgment-debtor to prison and these are:

(1) that the judgment-debtor has then or has had since the making of the judgment, sufficient means to pay the money directed to be paid by him, or part thereof, and he refuses, neglects, has refused or neglected to pay the same;

(2) that with intent to defraud or delay his creditors or any of them he has made any gift, delivery, or transfer of any property or removed property from the jurisdiction of the court making the judgment;

(3) that the debt or liability in respect of which the judgment has been made, has been contracted or incurred by him by fraud or breach of trust;

(4) that forbearance of the debt was obtained by him by fraud; and

(5) that the debt or liability was wilfully or recklessly contracted or incurred by him without his having at the same time a reasonable expectation of being able to discharge it.

Imprisonment in execution of a judgment has been abolished in the UK over a quarter of a century ago and in view of the human rights involved our courts must be wary of granting this relief to a judgment-creditor.

6. Application for Writ of Possession and Delivery, Orders 47 and 48

This is a method of execution whereby the judgment-creditor is put into possession of an immovable property or a movable property is delivered to the judgment-creditor. In both cases the application is by motion ex parte. In the case of a writ of possession it is necessary that all persons in actual possession must be served with the notice of the proceedings or at least notice of entry of judgment in favour of the plaintiff. In the case of Mosi v Bagyina [1963] 1 GLR 337, SC, it was held that where the defendant took part in the proceeding it was not necessary to serve him with the notice and that the persons to be served were:

(i) those who did not appear at the trial and therefore did not know of the result of the action, or

(ii) where a third party was in possession of the land, the party against whom an order for possession had been made.

7. Attachment for Contempt

Attachment for contempt is a method of going into execution or compelling a losing party to satisfy the judgment by doing an act (other than the payment of money) or abstaining from doing anything. (See Order 42. rule 7).

There are two ways of doing this: either under Order 44, rules 1 and 2 or under Order 59, rules 21—27.

The first issue is when does a successful party use Order 44 procedure and when does he use Order 59 procedure? The answer is where the application for attachment is being made to the court that made the order or judgment which is violated then the procedure is that laid down in Order 44.  Where the order or judgment flouted is that of an inferior court which has no power to commit for contempt of its own judgments or orders, then Order 59 procedure must be used.

(i) Order 44 Procedure

Under this Order the party in whose favour the order or judgment is made must apply for leave on notice to the losing party. The supporting affidavit must state:

(a) the nature of the judgment or order, whether it is for recovery of movable property or payment of money, abstaining from doing an act;

(b) the date of the order or judgment and whether the time for compliance has elapsed, Dake v Dorwu [1971] 2 GLR 75.

(ii) Order 59 Procedure

The procedure under this Order is two tier. First there is an application by motion ex parte supported by:

(a) an affidavit,

(b) statement, and

(c) a certified true copy of the judgment or the order which the defendant disobeyed.

The statement must contain:

(a) the name and description of the applicant;

(b) the relief sought; and

(c) the grounds on which relief is sought.

Where the applicant does not exhibit a certified true copy of the order or judgment he must sufficiently explain his failure to do so in the supporting affidavit. See Republic v Volta Region Chieftaincy Committee; Ex parte Asor II [1972] 2 GLR 273 at 279—281.

When leave has been granted the application must now be repeated on notice to the contemnor(s). The following are the documents to be served on the contemnor:

(a) a motion on notice pursuant to leave having been granted;

(b) a copy of the motion ex parte;

(c) a copy of the supporting affidavit; and

(d) a certified true copy of the order or judgment disobeyed.

These documents are to be personally served on the alleged contemnor and not on his counsel or solicitor: see Order 59, rr 21 and 25. On the hearing of the motion the court may direct any issue of fact in dispute to be tried in the same manner as other issues of fact are tried—rule 26.

8. Leave to go into Execution, Order 42

There are some cases in which a person who has been given judgment must seek leave of the court before going into execution and these are:

(a) where six years have elapsed since the judgment or date of the order, or any change has taken place by death or otherwise in the parties entitled or liable to execution; Gyebu v Lagos (1957) 2 WALR 138 at 143; Adjeiola v CFAO [1971] 2 GLR 11;

(b) where a husband is entitled or liable to executions upon a judgment or order for or against a wife;

(c)  where a party is entitled to execution upon a judgment of assets in future; and

(d) where a party is entitled to execution against any of the shareholders of a joint-stock company upon a judgment recorded against such company, or against a public officer or other person representing such company.

Where an application for leave is refused or dismissed a second application can be made founded or based on new materials or facts which were not before the court in the first application: see WT Lamb & Son v Ridder [1943] 2 KB 331.
