MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: PROOF IN CIVIL CASES: SOME THOUGHTS ON LAND SUITS [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 110—116

 

Amua-Sekyi K E, Justice of the Supreme Court

THE practitioner in Ghana leaves the law school persuaded that there are but two standards of proof—proof beyond reasonable doubt, said to apply in criminal cases, and proof on a balance of probabilities in civil suits. No sooner does he start practice than he is confronted with a third which, he is told, applies to actions for declaration of title to land. It is this supposedly third standard of proof that I intend to examine in this paper. But first, what do we mean by a balance of probabilities or, as the Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323) now calls it, a preponderance of the probabilities? Section 12 (1) of the Decree defines it as "that degree of certainty of belief in the mind of the tribunal of fact or the court by which it is convinced that the existence of a fact is more probable than its non-existence."

In land suits where the claim is laid in trespass, the plaintiff is required to prove one of two things: either that he is the owner of the land, or that prior to the trespass complained of he had exclusive, long and quiet possession of it. Where the plaintiff relies on title as the basis of his action for trespass, or sues for a declaration of his title to the land, a higher standard of proof is demanded from him than that required for proof of possession. What our courts demand from him is, in fact, proof beyond reasonable doubt, but this is often couched in the form that the plaintiff must rely on the strength of his own case and not on the weakness of his opponent's case. It was not a mere slip of the tongue when in Abotche Kponuglo v Adja Kodadja (1933) 2 WACA 24, where the respondent had claimed damages for trespass and an injunction against further trespass, the Privy Council said at page 25, "Has the respondent discharged the onus which rests upon him of demonstrating beyond reasonable doubt that the title to the disputed land is in him?"

In our courts, the statement that in an action for declaration of title to land, the plaintiff must rely on the strength of his own case and not on the weakness of the case of his opponent is associated with the name of Webber CJ, Sierra Leone, and the case in which he is generally regarded as having laid down the rule is J M Kodilinye v Mbanefo Odu (1933) 2 WACA 336, decided by the West African Court of Appeal at its sitting in Lagos, Nigeria, on 18 June 1935. The other members of the court were Kingdon CJ, Nigeria and Butler Lloyd J. However, a case earlier in time is Amodu Rufai v Ricketts and Others (1933) 2 WACA 95, on which Dean CJ, Gold Coast sat with Kingdon and Webber CJJ, in delivering judgment in Lagos on 28 August 1934.

The pronouncement was a reaction to Order 39, rule 1 of the Nigerian Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules which was in the same terms as Order 40, r 1, Second Schedule of the Gold Coast Courts Ordinance (Cap 4). These provided that the court may in any suit without the consent of the parties, non-suit the plaintiff where satisfactory evidence shall not be given entitling either the plaintiff or defendant to the judgment of the court. The question that arose was whether the rule was to be universally applied or limited to some cases only.

In Amodu Rufai the plaintiff sought a declaration of title to land being the property of one Saogun, deceased. Although he pleaded that the land was situated at a place called Pakodo Ikorodu he did not offer any evidence as to the extent of Ikorodu land or how Saogun came by his portion. He relied on three judgments which, he said, affected the land, but failed to show that the land in dispute was within the lands covered by those judgments. When the matter came before the West African Court of Appeal, Webber CJ said at page 96:

"This question of the absence of evidence as to the extent of the land in respect of which the present land in dispute is alleged to be part seems to me to be fundamental obstacle to the plaintiff's success in this claim."

In dismissing the appeal Webber CJ said at page 97: "This is a claim for a declaration of title to land and the onus of proving ownership is on the plaintiff. The result depends on the strength of the plaintiff's and not on the weakness of the defendant's case." It seems that the reference to "the weakness of the defendant's case" was made because it had been urged on appeal that the judgment of the High Court dismissing the claim was against the weight of evidence, implying that if the evidence of the plaintiff on the one hand and that of the defendants on the other were properly weighed the balance would favour the plaintiff.

In Kodilinye the plaintiff sought a declaration of title to land in Onitsha Province. The trial judge found that there was little to choose between the rival traditional histories and that the evidence on both sides was "conflicting and somewhat confused" and dismissed the action. On appeal, the point was taken that having regard to Order 39, r 1 the plaintiff ought to have been non-suited. The answer of Webber CJ, was that the rule did not apply to actions for declaration of title to land. He said from pages 337-338:

"I do not think that this rule should be applied in this case which seeks for a declaration of title. The onus lies on the plaintiff to satisfy the Court that he is entitled on the evidence brought by him to a declaration of title. The plaintiff in this case must rely on the strength of his own case and not on the weakness of the defendant's case. If this onus is not discharged, the weakness of the defendant's case will not help him and the proper judgment is for the defendant. Such a judgment decrees no title to the defendant, he not having sought the declaration. So if the whole evidence in the case be conflicting and somewhat confused, and there is little to choose between the rival traditional stories the plaintiff fails in the decree he seeks, and judgment must be entered for the defendant."

Two cases in the same vein are Nana Darku Frempong II v Nana Owusu Brempong II & Others (1952) 14 WACA 13 and Ededem Archibong V v Ntoe it a (1954) 14 WACA 520. In the first case the trial judge found that both parties were in actual possession of parts of the land in dispute and that the defendants had made several grants of land only one of which had been contested by the plaintiffs; but on relying on Kojo Addo v Kwadwo Wusu (1938) 4 WACA 96 he made a declaration of title in favour of the plaintiffs on the ground that they had been more vigilant than the defendants in asserting their rights. In the West African Court of Appeal Mr Bossman, counsel for the defendants argued that the trial judge had misdirected himself as to the real issue in the case and that the question which ought to have been asked was, "The burden of proving their title to the land is upon plaintiffs. Have they in fact discharged it?" and he submitted that it was the rule enunciated by Webber CJ in Kodilinye which was applicable to the case before the court, not the principles laid down in Addo v Wusu. The court consisting of Foster-Sutton P, Coussey and Manyo-Plange JJ agreed and allowed the appeal. In the second case, one of three communities which were in occupation of an island sought, as against the other two, a declaration of their title to the whole and failed. When on appeal, it was urged that the judgment was against the weight of evidence; the court per Coussey JA answered at page 522 that:

". . . the onus is on the plaintiff throughout to prove the title which he seeks to have confirmed. That onus never shifts. It is not sufficient for the plaintiff in such an action to show possession and argue that the defendant has proved no better title."

There are at least three qualifications to the rule in Kodilinye v Odu. The first is where the plaintiff leads satisfactory evidence but the court is disabled from granting him relief because the boundaries of the land in respect of which the declaration is sought have not been clearly ascertained. The point arose in the Gold Coast case of Kwadzo v Adjei (1944) 10 WACA 274, in which judgment was delivered in Accra on 14 November 1944. The action had been commenced in the Akome Native Tribunal, had gone to the Avatime State Council on appeal, and then to the Provincial Commissioner's court. The plaintiff had obtained a declaration of title even though the boundaries of the land had not been ascertained with any degree of accuracy. Allowing the appeal, the West African Court of Appeal consisting of Kingdon and Harragin CJJ, and Coussey J remitted the case to the Provincial Commissioner's court with a direction that that court shall re-hear the case entirely de novo with the assistance of a plan showing the land in dispute. Another opportunity for a departure from the rule came on 5 October 1954 when the court delivered judgment in Lagos in the Nigerian case of Amata v Modekwe (1952) 14 WACA 580, where both the plaintiffs and the respondents had tendered in evidence plans which the court found were inaccurate. Coussey JA, who read the judgment of the court, said from pages 58—582:

"The burden of proof is on the plaintiff . . . once the western boundary as it is demarcated by the plaintiffs is rejected, as it must be, there is not and has never been any evidence as to where this boundary is . . . In these circumstances the proper order is in my opinion one of non-suit of the plaintiff."

The other members of the court concurred and the order was made.

The second qualification arises from the much-misunderstood maxim, "Possession is nine-tenth of the law." The maxim does not mean that if there is evidence which shows that the party came into possession of the land illegally this may be ignored; rather, that if the origin of the possession is uncertain, then, provided he can show that it is of long duration and was uncontested until the dispute arose, the law assumes in his favour that it was lawful.

Two cases may be cited to illustrate this. In Abinah v Kennedy [(1921) FC'20-'21, 211] a house which originally belonged to the family of the plaintiff was sold by public auction in execution of a judgment debt and bought by one Niblett to whom a certificate of purchase dated 1883 was issued. The certificate was so vaguely worded that in the opinion of the court it was valueless. Somehow this certificate came into the possession of the husband of the defendant who had lived in the house, effected repairs and received rents for some 30 years until his death. It was held on these facts that Kennedy must have bought the house from a person who had a right to sell. In Aidoo v Adjei [1976] 1 GLR 431, CA, it was proved that Millers, a company which later merged with another to become the United Africa Co, Ltd, had in 1911 entered upon and developed a plot on Jukwa stool land. There was no trace of any document of sale although it was alleged that one had been signed. Millers' successor, UAC Ltd, had remained in undisturbed possession of the land until 1960 when they sold the land to the plaintiff. In 1972 when the plaintiff was disturbed in his possession of the land he brought an action against the trespasser and the Jukwa stool and obtained a declaration of title to the land.

Thus, where the plaintiff was in exclusive, long and quiet possession before trespass, the law shifts the burden of proof onto the defendant, thus permitting the plaintiff to take advantage of weaknesses in the case of the defence. In Kwaku Dua III v Kwamin Tandoh (1926) PC 1874—1928, 109, where the stool of Sefwi Bekwai, which was in possession sued the stool of Sefwi Wiawso for declaration of title to land, the Privy Council had this to say at p 110 on the burden of proof:

"Their Lordships are clearly of opinion that the facts proved and admitted establish that the Bekwai people are in possession of the disputed land, and this fact throws upon the respondent the burden or proving his title. The trial did not proceed strictly upon this line, but the plaintiff in the first instance called evidence to support the right of the Bekwais, and the defendant answered; this is however, immaterial. The real question is, Did the evidence rebut the prima facie title of the Bekwais shown by possession.?"

Conformably with this view, the West African Court of Appeal and the Privy Council in Ebu v Ababio (1956) 2 WALR 55, affirmed the decision of the Land Court in favour of a stool in possession for a declaration of title to land, even though the traditional evidence adduced on its behalf was unsatisfactory. Again, in Kwesi Yaw v Kwaw Atta [1961] GLR 513, when it was suggested that the traditional story of the plaintiff who was in possession, required corroboration by other witnesses to be acceptable, Adumua-Bossman J answered at page 517, "What stronger corroboration can you have than the concrete and cogent fact that the plaintiff and his people are in effective occupation and control at the date hereof?" And finally, in Ricketts v Addo [1975] 2 GLR 157, CA, where the title of the plaintiff's family to a portion of the land was held to be good but defective as to the rest, a declaration of title to the whole was made in their favour upon proof of long possession.

The third qualification arises from the second and it is that where the plaintiff fails to prove title but shows that he is entitled to possession and occupation of the land, the proper order to make is not one dismissing the action but one guaranteeing to him the use and occupation of the land until such time as his adversary proves a better title. Two cases which illustrate this are Eyo Ita v Etubom Asido (1935) 2 WACA 339 and Emegwara v Nwaimo (1953) 14 WACA 347. In the former, the plaintiff who claimed the land as having been bought by his predecessor from a third party failed to prove this, but the evidence showed that it was acquired from the defendants and had with their knowledge and consent remained in the possession of the plaintiff for over 50 years without any acknowledgement of right in the defendants. The West African Court of Appeal consisting of Kingdon and Webber CJJ and Butler-Lloyd J allowed the appeal against the grant to the plaintiff of a declaration of title and substituted a right of occupancy in accordance with custom. In the latter, the precise nature of the plaintiffs' title to the land was in doubt, but the evidence showed that they were entitled to exclusive and undisturbed possession of the land. On appeal a declaration of title in their favour was set aside, but the injunction restraining the defendants from trespassing on the land was sustained.

To conclude, most of the land suits in our courts have, as plaintiff, a person who claims title to land suing, and as defendant, a person in possession of the land. Such a defendant need not, and usually does not, seek any relief in the proceedings, being content with things as they are. Kodilinye v Odu says that in that event the plaintiff must rely on the strength of his own case, that is, prove his title, and not rely on the weakness of his opponent's, that is, lack of title in the defendant; so that if the plaintiff fails to prove that he is entitled to have a declaration made of his title to the land, the action ought to fail and the defendant left in possession of the land. In Kwamina Kuma v Kofi Kuma (1934) 2 WACA 178, upon proof that the ancestors of the defendant had been in possession of the land for six generations without acknowledging the title of the plaintiff, a declaration of title in favour of the plaintiff was set aside and a non-suit entered. In Abakam Effiana Family v Mbibado Effiana Family [1959] GLR 362, CA, the plaintiff family sought to prevent the defendant family from re-building a house which had fallen into ruins, claiming that the land belonged to them. Delivering the judgment of the Court of Appeal, Van Lare JA said at page 364:

"On the plaintiff's own admission it was established that the defendants had been in long undisturbed possession and occupation of the land, including that portion of the land on which the controversial house had been built, and were therefore entitled to the full protection of the law against anyone except a person who could affirmatively prove a better title. It is clear that the onus upon the plaintiffs in this case was heavy in the extreme."

The court accordingly restored the decision of the trial native court dismissing the action. And in Azagba v Negov [1964] GLR 450, SC where the plaintiffs claimed that their ancestors had put the ancestors of the defendants on the land as caretakers some five hundred years before the suit, the action for a declaration of title failed. Thus, the rule enunciated by Webber CJ applies whenever the defendant was in exclusive, long and quiet possession of the land before the suit. Where he was not, the case falls to be determined on a balance of probabilities, by which test the plaintiff's possession, if of long duration, may prove decisive; for even where a plaintiff in possession fails to satisfy the court that he is the owner of the land he may be granted the right of occupancy according to custom if he proves his entitlement to same.
