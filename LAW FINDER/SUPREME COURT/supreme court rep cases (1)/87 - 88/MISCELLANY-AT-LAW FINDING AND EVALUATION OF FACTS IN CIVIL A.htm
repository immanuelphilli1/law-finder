MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: FINDING AND EVALUATION OF FACTS IN CIVIL ACTIONS [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 116—122

 

by da Rocha B J, Barrister-at-Law

ONE of the most important elements of proof in a civil action is the finding which the trial judge makes on the facts. A fact cannot be considered as proved unless the trial judge makes a favourable finding .in respect of it. Civil actions brought in our courts are tried by a judge sitting alone without a jury. He is the judge both of the law and of the facts. He must consider all the facts presented to him and then apply the law. There are three stages, first the trial court must make findings of the primary facts, secondly, it must draw the proper inferences from the facts and thirdly, it must apply the law.

The primary facts in an action are facts which are proved by the oral testimony of witnesses or by the production of a thing, for example, a document. Where there is no dispute as to the facts the trial court will simply accept them in so far as they are relevant to the resolution of the issues before it evaluate them and apply the law. In the vast majority of cases, however, the facts are disputed. Confronted with disputed facts, a trial court must decide which version it accepts as true and which it rejects. In other words it must make proper findings of facts. The finding of facts is a duty which a trial court must not evade in any case where conflicting facts are presented which require resolution. Where a trial court fails to make findings of fact or makes the wrong findings it is disabled from applying the correct law and therefore giving a right decision

This duty to make proper findings of facts has been stressed in a number of cases decided in our courts. The principle which emerges from these decisions is that whenever the question, “what are the facts found?” cannot be answered with precision, then the judgment of the trial court ought to be held unsatisfactory. As Van Lare JSC put it in Quaye v Mariamu [1961] GLR 93, “It is a trial judge’s duty to make up his mind one way or the other on the primary facts and when he has made up his mind he should state his findings and then proceed to apply the law.” See also Addison v A/S Norway Cement Export Ltd [1973] 2 GLR 151; Adjin & Co v Kumaning [1982-83] GLRD 13.

It must be emphasised that the duty of the trial court is not merely to make findings of fact but to make proper findings of fact. This means that findings of fact must be based on evidence which is properly before the court. Findings of fact which are not based on or supported by evidence before the courts are wrong and cannot stand. How wide or how narrow should be the coverage of the trial court in making findings of facts? It depends on the facts and circumstances of each case. There are, however, at least two imperatives. First, the court must consider all the evidence for both sides so that it can ascertain the areas of conflict if any. Secondly, wherever there is conflict the court must resolve it. It may turn out that the court does not accept both versions of conflicting facts as true. In that case this must be clearly stated and the person who bears the burden of proof of the particular fact must fail.

In carrying out these imperatives a trial court may have to make specific findings on many separate facts. On the other hand a favourable or adverse finding in a particular fact may well imply the same finding in respect of other related facts. The question is how much can be left to inference. In Quaye v Mariamu for example, the appellate court set aside the judgment of the trial court and ordered a re-trial. The appellate court listed facts on which the trial court should have made findings but did not. In Reindorf v Amadu [1962] 1 GLR 508, the complaint made against the judgment of the trial court was that it had not made definite findings of fact on relevant important issues. The trial court did not deal with the specific fact. In a short judgment the Commissioner who tried the action said he was more impressed by the evidence led by the plaintiffs and their witnesses and that they had proved that they were the owners of all that land known as Dome land as shown on exhibit C by their possession and occupation, and trespass had been committed. The traditional evidence by the co-defendant and their witnesses was inconsistent and unreliable and in his view did not show any evidence of occupation. He visited and inspected the land in dispute and the physical features as the pillar marks supported the case for the plaintiff. Korsah CJ and Sarkodee Addo JSC refused to set the judgment aside and order a new trial on the ground that the statement “clearly shows that the court accepted the evidence of the plaintiffs and their witnesses.” Further they said that the learned Commissioner having come to the conclusion that the traditional evidence led by the co-defendant and their witnesses was inconsistent and unreliable and also that it did not show evidence of possession and occupation, there was no need for him to discuss what each witness said, having found that the plaintiffs were in possession and having accepted their traditional evidence, he had made the basic finding upon which he came to the conclusion that the plaintiffs were entitled to the declaration they sought.

Azu Crabbe JSC, as he then was, dissented from this view. In his opinion the trial Commissioner had not made adequate findings and he listed at least ten facts on which he thought findings should have been made but were not made.

Cases involving traditional evidence and the occupation of land may perhaps present some problems. Traditional evidence can be long and is usually given by more than one person. Is it proper for the trial court simply to say that it accepts one version of traditional evidence in preference to another, or must it make specific findings on every aspect of traditional evidence of which testimony has been given? Similarly in the case where the possession of lands is in issue, is it enough for a trial court to hold that one party is in possession or must specific findings be made in respect of all the facts which establish possession?

It is not easy to give a straight answer to these questions. Every case must be considered on the basis of the facts presented and the peculiar circumstances. The cases do suggest however, that a trial court must make findings on all primary facts of which evidence has been given and proof of which is necessary to resolve a material issue in the action.

One very important aspect of the fact finding process is the assessment of the credibility of a witness. If a trial judge does not believe a witness he must naturally make adverse finding in respect of facts which are sought to be to be proved by that witness’ evidence. By what criteria is a trial judge guided in assessing the credibility of a witness? Section 80(2) of the Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323) provides a fairly good guide. The section gives a list of matters which may be considered in deciding on the credibility of witness;

(a) the demeanour of the witness;

(b) the substance of the testimony;

(c) the existence or non-existence of anything testified to by the witness;

(d) the capacity and opportunity of the witness to perceive and recollect or relate any matter about which he testifies;

(e) the existence or non-existence of bias, interest or other motive;

(f) the character of the witness as to traits of honesty or truthfulness or their opposites;

(g) a statement of conduct which is consistent or inconsistent with the testimony of the witness at the trial;

(h) the statement of the witness admitting untruthfulness or asserting truthfulness.

It will be seen from this list that assessing credibility of a witness is partly objective and partly subjective. The demeanour of a witness for example may not affect different judges in the same way. Any decision based on demeanour is largely subjective. It is important not to over emphasise this subjective element in assessing credibility. Demeanors of witnesses can be great importance in some cases but it can also mislead and it is always necessary to consider demeanour against the background of all the evidence. An over reliance on demeanour may result in important aspects of the evidence being overlooked or not being given their proper weight. It is necessary to bear in mind that:

(i) a witness may be a good actor and  may lie plausibly with a straight face;

(ii) that the mere fact that a witness has not been shaken in cross-examination does not mean that he is necessary speaking the truth. A bold person may be consistent and assured in giving his answers but he may nevertheless be lying or may simply be wrong, a timid person may be hesitant and confused but he may be speaking the truth;

(iii) that there are many factors which affect a person’s demeanour and which may show him in a relatively unfavourable light even though he may not be lying, eg. fright, shyness, fatigue, illness;

(iv) that a person may make genuine mistakes of perception and may make  honest assertions on the basis of such mistakes;

(v) that there are many cases which cannot be fairly determined purely on the credibility of a witness.

This is particularly so where there is documentary evidence or other evidence which is independent of the oral testimony of witnesses, eg. the actual position of objects or the measurements of a physical object which is in existence and available. Speaking of demeanour reminds me of father Andrew. In my student days there was a trial at the Old Bailey which attracted much attention. Two notorious gangsters were charged for being involved in a violent fight in Soho. One of them called as his witness an old retired Anglican clergyman. This witness almost seventy years old and venerable looking so impressed the court by his demeanour that his testimony was believed and as a result the accused who called him was acquitted. Later the conscience striken father Andrew confessed that he had given false evidence. Apparently he was in very reduced circumstances and could not resist the inducement offered to him to come and tell a false story which secured the acquittal of the accused for whom he gave evidence. The case raised quite a storm in the London press. I do not know the sequel to father Andrew’s confession but it goes to show how misleading demeanour can be.

Or take the case of a witness who gave evidence at an inquiry in Accra. He was an old man from Kumasi very smart and impressive in appearance who spoke with impressive lucidity. He was giving evidence against a magistrate who had been accused of accepting a bribe. In cross-examination he was quite firm and unshaken. The accused informed his counsel that the man was one of a breed in Kumasi known as professional witnesses who for a consideration would swear to anything. They were usually very neat in appearance and had very good memories. It was bluntly put to the witness that he was a “professional” witness which he indignantly denied. His evidence being that he was the go between for the payment of the bribe to the magistrate. He was asked whether he thought it was right to play such a role. His startling answer was “Ye ye daa!” The members of the Committee of Inquiry were not, of course, pleased by this answer and it affected their assessment of his credibility.

When the primary facts have been found the trial court must then evaluate or draw the proper conclusions from them. This is really or should be a logical process which is more objective than the earlier process of fact finding. Here demeanour is irrelevant. The facts accepted by the court are before it and it must draw conclusions which are reasonable.

Azu Crabbe JSC stated the principle in these words in his dissenting judgment in Reindorf v Amadu: “In reaching a decision a judge sitting without a jury has a dual function to perform. The first function is the establishment of particular facts, that is the perceptive function. The second function is an evaluation of the actual facts which have been found.”

As already observed, evaluation is really a process of deduction based upon given facts. The problem is that sometimes it is not easy to draw a clear line between a simple fact and what is an inference from that fact. Since evaluation is an exercise in deduction it can be carried out by any intelligent person given the facts. The legal issues raised by the deduction can, of course, only be appreciated by a person trained in the law.

When the judgment of a trial court goes to an appellate court, how does the latter court treat the trial court’s findings and evaluation of facts? If the trial court failed to make any findings at all its judgment may be set aside and a new trial ordered. The appellate court “can only do justice between the parties if it is satisfied that the foundation has been laid by the trial judge whose duty it is to resolve the primary facts.” When the trial court does make findings of fact the well established rule is that the appellate court will not lightly disturb such findings. The burden of showing that the trial court was wrong in his decision as to the facts lies on the appellant and the appellate court will not disturb findings of facts made by the trial court especially where in a conflict of testimony the demeanour of witnesses who have been seen and heard by the trial court is a material element in consideration of the truthfulness of statements made.

The appellate court, however, can and does (when the need arises) disturb the findings of fact made by a trial judge if such findings are not sustainable because the evidence on record does not support it and the trial court overlooked obvious matters which render the finding untenable, eg conflicts in the evidence or where the preponderance of probabilities is against the findings.

In Appiah v Takyi [1982-83] GLRD 1, the Court of Appeal did not hesitate to disturb a findings of fact made by the trial judge. One of the issues in this case was whether the prices in a contract of sale was ¢3,200 or ¢5,000. The trial judge found that it was ¢5,000 on the oral testimony of a witness but there was documentary evidence which supported the contention that the price was ¢3,200. The Court of Appeal set aside the findings of the trial judge and held that the price was ¢3,200.

In Addision v A/S Norway Cement Co. Ltd the trial judge found that there was no contract between the plaintiff and the defendants and said he could not decide what a sum of ¢80,000 mentioned in a document represented. The Court of Appeal held that there was ample evidence from both parties to support the existence of an oral agreement and the sum of ¢80,000 was named pursuant to that contract. Where there is ample evidence on record on record to support a fact which was not considered by the trial court the appellate court may make its own findings. In the case of Adjin & Co v Kumaning the issue of estoppel by long possession and acquiescence was raised. The trial court did not however consider the issue. On appeal the Court of Appeal found that there was more than sufficient evidence before the trial court to support the plea estoppel.

In Atadi v Ladzekpo [1982-83] GLRD 15, the Court of Appeal held that (i) where documentary evidence was tended to make an oral testimony improbable, it would be wrong for the trial court to reject the documentary evidence and to rely on demeanour unless there was good cause for doing so; (ii) whenever the testimony of a party on a crucial issue was in conflict with the testimony of his own witness it was not open to a trial court to gloss over such conflict and make a specific finding on that issue in favour of the party whose case contained the conflicting evidence. The trial judge must direct his mind to the conflict in evidence and weigh the different versions. If he did this he may, having regard to the particular fact in issue and the circumstances of the case make a favourable finding notwithstanding the conflict.

The duty of the appellate court may be summed up in words of Lindley MR in Coglan v Cumberland [1898] 1 Ch at pp 704—705 that:

“Even where . . . the appeal turns on a question of fact, the Court of Appeal has to bear in mind that its duty is to rehear the case, and the court must reconsider the materials before the judge with such material as it may have decided to admit. The court must then make up its own mind, not disregarding the judgment appealed from, but carefully weighing and considering it; and not shrinking from overruling it if on full consideration the court comes to the conclusion that it is wrong.”

Before I conclude, I should like to raise some few matters which are not matters of law but which I consider important because they affect the judge’s ability and capacity to make the right findings and therefore draw the correct inferences from the facts. The matters referred to in section 80(2) of the Evidence Decree as criteria for assessing credibility require that a judge’s perception and reasoning should be acute. Anything which tends to impair such perception and reasoning must necessarily impair the quality of the judgment. It is common to see in judgments such remarks as “I am impressed by the demeanour of the witness, the “evidence of X had the ring of truth.” Such a statement pre-supposes a careful perception of the witness and his testimony. We must, however, frankly ask ourselves how acute a judge’s perception can be if most of the time his face is glued to the record book in the drudgery of recording the evidence in longhand. The answer seems obvious to me. Or take a manner in which proceedings are recorded in different pages of a record book so that at the end of the whole case the judge has to hop from one page to another to put together the evidence of the facts on which he must make his findings. Furthermore, the longer the interval between the time evidence is taken and the time judgment is given, the greater the risk that the impressions created may have faded or even become distorted.

These problems were adverted to last year, and I am sure that they will be raised by other speakers as well as myself. I think the time has come for a fitting solution to be found. In the case of recording evidence if the most modern gadgetry cannot be procured, it is surely not beyond our means and resource to employ competent shorthand writers for each judge so that the Bench can be freed from the drudgery of recording in longhand. I am informed that the public tribunals have shorthand writers. Why can’t we? Maybe it is a matter of “ask and ye shall be given” in which case I urge your lordships to ask and to ask loudly and persistently.

I believe also that the work load carried by a judge may seriously affect his perception. Something should be done to ensure that a judge is not so overloaded with work that sheer fatigue blunts the acuteness of his perception.
