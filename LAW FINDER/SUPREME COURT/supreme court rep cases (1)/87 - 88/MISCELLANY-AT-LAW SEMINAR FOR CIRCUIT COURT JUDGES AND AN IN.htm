MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: SEMINAR FOR CIRCUIT COURT JUDGES AND AN INDUCTION COURSE FOR NEWLY APPOINTED MAGISTRATES [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 35—41

 

FEATURING prominently in the continuing education of judges programmes of 1987 were a seminar held for circuit court judges from 24 to 27 February 1987 and an induction course organised for newly appointed magistrates between 11 and 22 May 1987. The majority of the talks and lectures were given and delivered by some of the most experienced judges and legal practitioners. They dealt principally with procedure and interlocutory matters. Coming as they did from the bosom of their lordships themselves and the most experienced legal practitioners, those talks and lectures could serve as useful guides to members of the Bench themselves as well as those of the Bar. The Review is happy to present below as many of those papers as space would permit.

THE ART OF WRITING JUDGMENT: BY SOWAH E N P, CJ

On 31 December 1981, the PNDC upon assumption of power suspended the Constitution, 1979, and in its place declared that it would rule by Proclamation. Section 9 of the said Proclamation provided that notwithstanding the suspension of the Constitution all courts in existence immediately before 1981 shall continue in existence with the same powers, duties and functions as under the existing law subject to the Proclamation and the laws issued under it. There are other provisions under section 9 which are not of the present moment under discussion.

Delivery of judgment is in my view the most important function of a judge. The duty of an adjudicator, be he magistrate, a circuit judge or a High Court judge, is to hear evidence, determine the issues upon the evidence and finally pronounce his decision or judgment. The decision is binding upon the parties and their privies until a higher court overturns it. People usually resort to the courts because they want to affirm or protect their rights, properties and proprietory interests. They come to court to seek various forms of redress. The parties at your level are only interested in the justice of your judgment. Very soon you will be faced with greater responsibility as proposals have been made for the enhancement of your criminal and civil jurisdictions. Whatever be the nature of the action before you, whether it be simple or complicated, the parties are only interested in the judgment and not in the length of the reasons for the decision.

This brings me to my topic, “Writing of Judgments.”

(a) Language

All our judgments are delivered in English, unfortunately. I say unfortunately, because it would have been much easier to give judgment in our own native languages. However, the official language of this country is English and proceedings in court are conducted in English. Therefore our first duty is to acquire reasonable command of the English language. If a judge can attain a higher standard of English so much the better for his judgment and advancement. It should be the aim of every judge to endeavour to acquire a very high command of English.

Lord Denning, in his book, The Discipline of the Law, admonishes the profession thus:

“To succeed in the profession of law you must seek to cultivate the command of language. Words are the lawyers’ tools of trade . . . when you have to interpret a section in a statute or a paragraph in a regulation, you have to study the very words.”

These admonitions addressed to the legal profession ought also to be needed by judges, who afterall are also members of the legal profession. A judge hears arguments of counsel and has the onerous duty of deciding which argument appeals to him and to convey his decision in words. The book goes on to give reasons why words are so important:

“They are the vehicle of thought. You think in words, not in symbols. There is no other means available. To do it convincingly, do it simply and clearly (the emphasis is mine). If others find it difficult to understand you, it will often be because you have not cleared your own mind upon it. Obscurity in thought leads to obscurity in language.”

You will therefore appreciate that it is a sine qua non of your profession as a judge, to possess an adequate fund of language.

How do you acquire this asset? There is only one way and it is to read. You must make yourselves at home with the great masters of the English language through constant reading. I am aware that books are expensive and there are no libraries worth the name in the regions. Nevertheless where there is the will, there is a way.

(b) Style

I will observe that style is the bedfellow of language. Your style makes reading of your judgments interesting or dull. Each one of us has his own style of writing, and I urge that you stick to that style which suits you and to improve upon it, if need be. Style has been described as “the dress of thought” and like in everyday life “a modest dress, neat but not necessarily gaudy will true critics please” (Samuel Wesley). Some judges do like to write after the style of Shakespeare, Macauley or Hastings. It is important that whatever style you adopt, must lead to your judgments being intelligible and interesting. Some judges like to be long-winded and rambling in their judgments and so, wrongly, think that by writing pages and pages of judgment they display their knowledge of the law. If you can do so without obfuscation, all well and good; stick to that.

I prefer to be brief though, not, of course, at the expense of clarity and lucidity; and if I can convey my thoughts in a sentence or two, I will rather than write multifarious volumes of words which are full of sound and fury yet signifying very little. That, of course, is my style which I learnt at school. I had an old English school master whose popular adage was, “Oh my soul, be brief” and he said so after he had marked our English essays. Make your style interesting. It is certainly boring, particularly in a civil judgment, to read thus: “PW1 said this . . . and PW2 corroborated PW1, PW3 supported the plaintiff. Turning to the defence DW1, DW2 stated so and so.”

An appellate court is not much interested in the recitals of the evidence which you have in any case recorded but it is very much so in the evaluation and appraisal of that evidence and the mental process by which the court arrives at its findings and conclusions.

There are a few cases where the demeanour of witnesses may be important and may be good guide for preferring one piece of evidence to the other resulting in the judge believing one party and disbelieving the other. Perhaps I should mention that I have always been sceptical of the process whereby a judge uses demeanour as a yardstick for his judgment. Some witnesses may be so timid or frightened by the atmosphere of the court that their answers appear incoherent and convey an impression of telling lies, yet they may be speaking the gospel truth. Such witnesses when told that they are liars become very aggrieved by being ranked under that nomenclature or as a prefix to his name. Judges are not omniscient, they are all liable to the ordinary human frailties including fallibility of man. Therefore if a judge should find it necessary to say of a witness that he or she is a liar, the judge must support his description from the evidence before him. He should not say under privilege of immunity that which he dares not say outside court premises.

You will permit me with a quote from Cardozo. He says:

“Often clarity is gained by a brief and almost sententious statement, at the outset of the problem to be attacked. Then may come a fuller statement of the facts, rigidly pared down . . . as opposed to those that are decorative and adventitious. If these are presented with due proportion and selection, our conclusion ought to follow so naturally and inevitably as almost proof itself.”

(c) Settling the Issues

Before a judge begins to hear evidence he should get counsel to settle the issues upon which he is being invited to make a determination. If the parties have no counsel, the judge must himself be clear what issues he is to determine. Settling the issues both save the time of the court and stops counsel from useless and unnecessary cross-examination.

There is a wrong notion here in Ghana that in cross-examination counsel can ask any questions whether it be relevant to the issue or not.

To illustrate the point that I am driving at; the complainant in a criminal case gave evidence for the prosecution against his steward boy who had been charged with theft of his property. Counsel’s first question without laying any foundation whatsoever in cross-examination was whether or not a certain woman was not complainant’s girl friend? Naturally, the witness protested and asked for the protection of the court. Counsel insisted that he was cross-examining and was free to ask any question. Throughout that cross-examination there was not the slightest attempt to link the lady with theft. It was an attempt to embarrass the gentleman in the presence of his wife. Such cross-examination is an abuse of counsel’s privilege and the processes of the court, and should be discouraged. Cross-examination should go to the matters in issue and should not be an instrument of oppression of a witness.

Reverting to the subject of our discussion, namely, the settlement of issue, it is the duty of a judge having heard the evidence to proceed by discussion to find how far relevant evidence resolves each issue. A learned judge’s function all the time is to ascertain where the preponderance of credible evidence lies and upon it make his finding of facts.

(d) Issue of the Law

It is for the judge to know his law not by carrying every law in his head but by knowing where to find it. It is most unfortunate that there are no libraries worth speaking of in almost all the regions. Judgments of the Court of Appeal and the Supreme Court are not available to you. Every effort is being made by the administration to get these disabilities modified. It is hoped that very soon appellate court registries will compile judgments of the Court of Appeal and Supreme Court for distribution to judges and lawyers. Our Law Reports are nearly eight years in arrears.

Having said all this there is still the need for judges to refresh their minds with the basic law of the country. I have no doubt that you are all aware of the doctrine of stare decisis, that is, a previous decision of the superior courts on the point is binding upon all inferior courts. You know also that under the Constitution and the PNDC Proclamation the Supreme Court decisions are binding on all courts including the Court of Appeal Decisions of the Court of Appeal are binding on the High Court and inferior courts and so the chain of obeisance goes down the steps of the hierarchy of courts.

In applying law the court sometimes may find that there are two or more conflicting authorities on the point before it. If there is a conflict between a decision of the Court of Appeal and of the High Court, the Court of Appeal decision should prevail. If they are decisions of courts of concurrent jurisdiction, the judge must discuss them in his judgment and decide which decision to adopt giving reasons for his preference. It is not for the court to decide which point is correct. The court should leave that for a higher court to resolve. Having chosen your preference on issue of law, you then apply it to your findings of facts. But there is the rub; for if the application of the law is wrong the bottom of the decision falls apart unless a higher court finds that upon proper application of the law court’s findings can be supported.

Finding of fact must therefore be clear and unequivocal, for law cannot be applied in vacuo.

(e) Summary: The Mechanics, Style and Delivery of Judgment

There are no laid down principles for writing judgments. They must bear the stamp of the judge. At your level, however, I venture to think you can learn a lot from those who have travelled longer on the same road as you are now in the process of doing. In writing judgment, the judge’s first duty in his preface is to give a short narrative of the parties, the issue which has brought them to court, and the statements of claim and defence; but he must not reproduce the whole statement of plaintiff’s claim and statement of the defence. This is most unnecessary, because they are part of the record. It is time consuming and unnecessary wastage of stationery.

The judge then must set out the issues both of fact and law. There may be ancillary issues, but if they do not affect the determination of the main issues, they must be briefly disposed of. The judge should concentrate on the main issues and proceed to attack the issues arising, firmly making his own findings and applying the law, and coming to a conclusion one way or the other. There can be no drawn match in a court of law.

(f) Rulings

Before I am done, I will touch briefly on rulings as they are of some importance in the determination of substantive matters.

(1) They should be brief and concise and to the point, never be clouding the issue.

(2) The principle of law applied should be clear, if necessary supported by an authority from a higher court.

(3) There is no need to cite long passages from judgments of superior courts. All you need is to give the title of the case and its citation.

(4) What you must not do is to appear to give your decision on the whole action when you have not taken evidence. I regret to say I have seen this happening, even in the rulings of some in our superior courts.

Finally, I will leave you with the advice of an American judge in what he described as the ABC of legal writing: accuracy, brevity and clarity. There should be no prevarication and ambiguity in your judgments. Have no doubts about your judgments and leave the appellate court to do its best or worst. We all learn from our mistakes.

PROOF IN TRIALS OF CRIMINAL CASES [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 213—228

Taylor J. N. K.

INTRODUCTION

THE topic is dealing with the process of the judicial system by which it can be established whether a criminal offence has indeed been committed or not by a person accused of committing it. It is therefore necessary to know what is involved in the concept of crime and the legal means of establishing it. A criminal case in Ghana is essentially concerned with the acts or omissions of individuals in the state. These acts or omissions are proscribed by law and for that reason as sanctions against persons indulging in them. There is a liability for those contravening the prescribed law to be punished by death, imprisonment, fine1 or be subjected to other measures calculated to discourage the said acts or omissions.

These proscribed acts or omissions are in criminal jurisprudence technically labelled as actus reus. The fundamental principle in criminal law is that as a general proposition, without fault or intention or in-advertence the application of punishment provisions and other measures are considered inexpedient. Therefore to establish a crime, unless a statute specifically provides to the contrary, the act or omission must be established together with the intention accompanying the commission of the act. The said intention -the mental element or the knowledge of the wrongfulness of the act is popularly known to the students of the law as mens rea. It is often said that the mens rea and the actus reus must coalesce before a crime can be said to have been established and this idea is institutionalised in the latin aphorism actus non facit reus, nisi mens sit rea, properly translated this means "an act does not make a man guilty of a crime unless his mind be also guilty." It is thus not the actus which is reus but the accused and his mind respectively.2

In consequence of this concept of crime, proof in a criminal case at a trial is legally defined as the establishment of the facts in issue relevant to the statutory ingredients of the crime by admissible evidence of a requisite degree of belief concerning the said facts, in the mind of the tribunal of fact or the court.3 The term tribunal of facts or the court is used to highlight the distinction between jury trials and trials without a jury. For in jury trials, the jury are the tribunal of fact and where there is no jury questions of fact are determinable by the presiding judge.3a

Four matters call for comment on the definition of a "proof" proffered here. The first is the term statutory ingredients which is inextricably connected with the second matter -facts in issue, the third is admissible evidence which is also closely connected with the fourth, requisite degree of belief.

STATUTORY INGREDIENTS

The structure of our criminal legislation from the date when statutory courts dispensing criminal justice started to operate in this country is geared to deal with conduct specifically defined as crime by the statutory law of the country. The result is that our courts are not bound by the definition of any offence in a foreign country.4 This, of course, does not mean that our courts should be cut off from the accumulated learning and experience in other jurisdictions operating a comparative legal system like ours.5. It does mean however that nothing is a crime unless a statute in Ghana makes it so and the principle here is therefore nulla poena sine lege.6

This brings us to the second matter, namely, facts in issue. The facts in issue in a criminal trial are all those facts which the prosecutor, invariably the Republic, must prove in order to succeed together with any further facts which the accused must prove in order to establish a defence which is open to him.7 In a criminal trial there are generally only three types of facts that might emerge at various stages of the proceedings. The first are the facts which the prosecution might give before the commencement of the actual trial, indicating the material they intend to prove by admissible evidence.  Secondly, facts which the accused might, if he is minded to do so, lead in his evidence in his defence. Finally, there are facts which on a consideration of the respective facts led in evidence by the prosecution and the defence, the presiding judge or magistrate finds as representing in his opinion the actual facts.8 If these last category of facts tend to establish the truth or otherwise of the proscribed act constituting the ingredients of the offence as defined by statute, then they are the facts in issue. And the facts in issue in a criminal trial as in all other cases are only provable by admissible evidence. The whole question of proof therefore is in all cases completely covered by the law of evidence. What are all these cases which the law of evidence is designed to cater for?

Generally speaking, there are about four classes of cases where the law of evidence is applicable. These classes are criminal cases, quasi-criminal cases, civil cases and quasi-civil cases. Quasi-criminal cases cover cases like enquiries in connection with misconduct of persons in public or private organisations where the result of the enquiry may end up in the dismissal, suspension or other punishment of a private nature. Civil cases involve claims by plaintiffs against defendants for damages in respect of civil wrongs or injury and directed at settling the differences between the parties by the payment of damages or restoring the parties to the position they would have been in but for the civil wrong done or the injury suffered. It does not involve punishment. Instances of quasi-civil cases are the cases of commissions or committees of enquiry charged with responsibility to enquire into matters of national importance and directed at merely informing the convening authority by their reports of the relevant facts which the authority needs.

Theoretically, in the absence of specific statutory provisions, there are similarities between the rules of evidence applicable in establishing facts in all these cases, but in practice there are relevant differences depending on the category of case involved.9 With regard to criminal trials there are a number of provisions largely made statutory by the Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323) which are peculiar to criminal cases. For instance, as a general proposition, barring statutory exceptions, the rules of evidence cannot be waived by the agreement of the prosecutor and the accused. Again the standard of proof is higher than in civil and a great number of other cases.10 There are also special rules governing the admissibility of complaints,11 the reception of unsworn evidence, the compellability and competence of the accused, and there are special provisions regarding the hearsay rule under which dying declarations, confessions and depositions can be received in evidence.12

The whole purpose of these special and peculiar rules in the criminal justice system is to enable the proof of the ingredients of a crime to be attained at a very high level of relevancy by very superior species of judicial evidence. Judicial evidence in criminal trials consists of the testimony of hearsay, documents, things and facts which a court will accept as evidence of facts in issue in a given criminal case. When the evidence is so acceptable then it is often referred to as admissible evidence. And it must be evidence of such a nature as to make the tribunal of facts to feel sure that the ingredients of the offence have been proved. This is the requisite degree of belief in the quality of the evidence proffered in proof and it is now statutorily described in the Evidence Decree confirming the traditional description as "proof beyond a reasonable doubt."13

The effect of this rather very high standard of proof is that if inadmissible evidence is improperly accepted and a conviction has resulted therefrom, an appellate court would uphold the conviction only on being satisfied that if the offending evidence were excluded it would not affect the conviction, and there would be no substantial miscarriage of justice.14 When counsel objects to the reception of evidence on the ground that it is inadmissible, he should do so at the time the evidence is offered15 and the court must record and rule on the objection16 and although the appellate court has a discretionary power to allow an objection not raised at the trial to be raised on appeal, it would only do so if it has good reason to go upon, in the absence of a good reason the objection would not be entertained.17 Improper rejection of evidence is subject to the same consideration and where the rejection had resulted in a grave miscarriage of justice, the conviction would be quashe18 and if the appellate court is satisfied that owing to exceptional circumstances, the interests of justice require that there should be a re-trial, the court may order a re-trial upon such terms as it may think fit.19

One essential feature of proof in criminal trials is that it is proof solemnly undertaken before a tribunal of competent jurisdiction. The preliminary or committal proceedings before a district court is held in a court room which may not necessarily be open to the public. This is no doubt a precaution to ensure privacy and unnecessary publicity in case an accused person is discharged for want of evidence to warrant his committal for trial. For this purpose, it is provided that if the ends of justice will be best served by so doing the court in committal proceedings may order that no person shall have access to, or be or remain in the room or place without the court's express permission.20 This is specially so, since in committal proceedings formal evidence is not received. In summary trials, however, the room or place at which the magistrate or judge sits is a court room open to the public and a conviction consequent on an enquiry held by a district magistrate not in court but in his residence at which evidence was received and used for the conviction was held not to be sustainable on appeal.21

THE BURDEN OF PERSUASION

The essence of a criminal trial is proof of facts by the production of admissible evidence. The fundamental principle governing "proof" generally in a criminal trial is that he who asserts a fact has the initial obligation to produce evidence in order to establish a requisite degree of belief concerning the fact he asserts in the mind of the tribunal of fact or the Court.22 This is known in the law of Ghana as the burden of persuasion and it is often referred to in the books as the burden of proof 23 In appropriate circumstances, the said burden of persuasion may require a party to raise a reasonable doubt concerning the existence or non-existence of a fact or that he should establish the existence or non-existence of a fact by a preponderance of the probabilities or standard of proof beyond a reasonable doubt.24

STANDARD OF PROOF

The burden of producing evidence therefore involves the obligation of a party to introduce sufficient evidence up to a standard of proof so as to convince the tribunal of fact or the court to avoid a ruling against him on the issue.25 In a criminal trial the said standard of proof or the burden of producing evidence when it is on the prosecution as to any fact which is essential to guilt, requires the prosecution to produce sufficient evidence so that on all the evidence adduced at the trial a reasonable tribunal of fact or court could find the existence of the fact beyond a reasonable doubt.26 In jury trials the jury determines questions of fact and in non-jury trials the court, ie the presiding judge or magistrate decides all questions of fact.27 When once a tribunal of fact has decided a question of fact, then strictly speaking it is not the province of an appellate court to determine the sufficiency of the evidence produced at the trial in proof of the fact. The duty of the appellate court would then be confined to a determination as to whether in say, the case of a conviction, there was any evidence in the trial which could support the conviction.28

On the issue of the standard of proof and therefore sufficiency of evidence in proof of offences, it is not uncommon at criminal trials for conflicting evidence to be given concerning the issues by the prosecution and the defence. The applicable principle of law is that where the conflict is in respect of material facts or issues then the court is bound to give good reasons for preferring the prosecution's version as against the defence and in the absence of such good reasons, it seems a conviction will be quashed by an appellate court.29 Where, of course, the conflict is on immaterial issues, it makes no difference to the conclusion of the court and a conviction will not be interfered with.30 Material issues therefore are necessarily the issues which will substantiate a conviction if decided in favour of the prosecution and sustain an acquittal if determined in favour of an accused. At the commencement of any trial, the accused generally pleads either guilty or not guilty.

On a plea of guilty all the facts constituting the ingredients of the offence are prima facie presumed to be admitted and there would therefore be need to lead evidence in proof of the charge unless there shall appear sufficient cause to the contrary31 in which case the plea of guilty should be rejected by the court and a plea of not guilty entered for the accused and evidence in support of the charge is then led in the normal way.32  If the plea of guilty is accepted, it is not permissible for the court to proceed to pass sentence without hearing the facts which the prosecution had proposed to give in evidence in support of the charge.33 On a plea of not guilty, all the facts founding the ingredients of the offence as can be gathered from the charge and the statutory definition of the offence are in issue and these would normally be made up of the actus reus and the mens rea. As Lord Goddard CJ once put it:

"whenever there is a plea of not guilty, everything is in issue and the prosecution have to prove the whole of their case, including the identity of the accused, the nature of the act and the existence of any necessary knowledge or intent."34

In this respect, it must be reiterated that it is the facts in issue that must be proved by admissible evidence and that evidence of rumours of the commission of a crime do not constitute evidence of the commission of the said crime and consequently evidence of rumours circulating in the country about the complicity of an accused person is no proof of his complicity in any crime.35 By the practice of the courts a plea of guilty is not acceptable in a murder case and this invariable practice and convention has now been made statutory. The statutory procedure in guilty pleas in murder cases is to enter a plea of not guilty and to proceed to hear evidence in the normal way. It would seem that the principle is applicable in all capital offences carrying the death penalty and the policy justification for this must rest in the severity of the sentences entailing as it does the ultimate punishment known to the law and implying that an injustice committed by a wrong conviction can never be rectified after the sentence has been carried out. See section 199(5) of Act 30.

In proving the guilt of an accused person, the traditional concept institutionalised by the decision of the House of Lords  in the famous Woolmington appeal by the Lord Chancellor is that subject to the defence of insanity and to statutory exceptions, the burden of proof resting on the prosecution to prove the guilt of the accused person beyond reasonable doubt does not shift. It appears however that the rule is apparently not as stringent as the statement would seem to imply and in appropriate circumstances the burden of persuading a tribunal of fact that a person is guilty of a crime or wrong-doing could shift.36 In a charge of bigamy involving an accused person already married under the Marriage Ordinance who contracted a marriage under customary law, the prosecution proved the celebration of the first marriage. They then proved the identity of the accused as one of the spouses to the Ordinance Marriage. The accused then merely alleged that his first marriage was, in fact, invalid, a course reminiscent of the subsequent caveat of Ollennu J. as he then was, in Majolagbe v Larbi (supra). Sir Crampton Smyly CJ sitting in the Full Court at Sekondi in affirming his conviction said:

"In the present case, the prosecution have proved the marriage and the identity of the parties and under our . . . law . . . the accused is bound to establish the invalidity. He has not done so. Conviction affirmed."37

Furthermore, in certain circumstances mostly provided in statutes a prima facie evidence of overwhelming significance may be produced by the prosecution and this may shift the evidential burden of proof on an accused person. So where a person is charged with dishonest receiving and is proved to have had in his possession or under his control anything which is reasonably suspected of having been stolen or unlawfully obtained and he does not give an account to the satisfaction of the court as to how he came by it, the property may be presumed to have been stolen or unlawfully obtained and the accused may be presumed guilty of dishonest receiving in the absence of evidence to the contrary.38 Here on proof of certain matters the onus shifts to the accused. A comparative case of the onus shifting to the accused also arises under section 47 (1) of Act 64 as amended.

The evidence led before a court in proof of charges against an accused person, must generally be adduced in the presence of the accused person. Where, however, the offence is punishable by fine or imprisonment not exceeding three months (whether with or without a fine) and it is not a felony, then the magistrate or the judge has power to dispense with the personal attendance of the accused, provided the accused pleads guilty in writing or appears by advocate. If in such circumstances instead of issuing a warrant for his arrest to be brought to court evidence is admitted in the absence of the accused, then the absence is an irregularity depriving the court of jurisdiction.39 We apprehend though that if on expunging the evidence led in the absence of the accused, enough admissible evidence will be available to support the conviction and the exercise will not occasion a substantial miscarriage of justice, then the conviction in such circumstances will not be disturbed on appeal.40

By the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Code, on a plea of not guilty, the magistrate or the judge is mandatorily required to hear evidence in proof of the charge.41 In a summary trial he has no discretion to dismiss the charge on the ground that it is defective without hearing the evidence. If the charge is in fact defective he has ample powers under the Criminal Procedure Code to make an order for the alteration of the charge by way of amendment or by the substitution or addition of a new charge, as he considers necessary in the interests of justice to meet the circumstances of the case.42 If he fails to do so and dismisses the charge, his decision would be set aside on appeal and a new trial may be ordered.43

WITNESSES: THEIR COMPETENCE AND COMPELLABILITY

Proof in law as Ollennu J, as he then was, in effect points out in Majolagbe v Larbi44 is attained by judicial evidence, which consists of the testimony of witnesses, hearsay statements, documents, things and facts,45 including facts not in issue but which show the existence of other facts in issue. The term judicial evidence therefore comprises the following five species of materials:

(a) testimony of witnesses on matters within their knowledge;46 or (b) hearsay statements admissible under special conditions;47 or (c) things tendered by witnesses; or (d) documents tendered by witnesses;48 or (e) facts not in issue given by witnesses but which are relevant to the existence of other facts which are in issue.49

TESTIMONY OF WITNESSES

Before any person is considered competent to give evidence in proof of a criminal charge he must in general, and subject to any enactment or rule of law take an oath or make an affirmation in the prescribed form that the evidence he is proposing to give shall be true.50 The court, however, may at any time, if it thinks just and expedient and for reasons to be recorded in the proceedings, take without oath the evidence of any person who declares that the taking of an oath whatsoever is according to his religious belief unlawful or who by reason of immature age or want of religious belief ought not in the opinion of the court to be admitted to give evidence on oath. Such evidence when recorded in the proceedings is treated as if it had been taken on oath.51

It is to be noted that for evidence to be taken without an oath, whether due to immature age or want of religious belief the court must record the reasons for doing so, and so in Duncan v Wood52 where the evidence of a person of immature age was taken without oath and no reasons were recorded for so taking the statement, Francis Smith J, sitting in the Divisional Court in Cape Coast quashed the conviction, proceeding under statutory provisions virtually on all fours with the present statutory law governing the matter. As far as religious belief is concerned, the oath is not confined to the christian religion and it may be administered in any form whatsoever which is recognised by the deponent's religion.53

It would seem that to permit a person to give evidence without being sworn or without making an affirmation, the statutory conditions spelled out in section 57 (2) of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372) must be strictly complied with. It must therefore be shown by the witness or otherwise that either the taking of an oath is according to his religious belief unlawful or that he has no religious belief. Thus under the comparative provision contained in section 1 of the English Oaths Act, 1888, it was held that the court had a duty to see that the statutory conditions were strictly complied with and that where the objection of a witness was on grounds other than the two statutory grounds, or though not objecting to an oath, the witness considered the oath not binding on him, and refused to state what form he considered binding, he could not be allowed to affirm.54

In the light of the prevailing statutory provisions a competent witness is one eligible to testify and therefore liable to be called to give evidence, but a compellable witness is one who is not only qualified to be called as a witness at a trial but is obliged by law to testify. In the circumstance a witness may thus be competent without being compellable.55 The principal ground of incompetency is one due to defect in intellect of the proposed  witness. For this reason no witness is competent who is  incapacitated by tender age, drunkenness or unsoundness of mind from giving rational testimony. The incapacity however is predicated on the inherent want of understanding and appreciation of the testimony caused by the defect in the reasoning faculty.56 Consequently, a lunatic is competent during a lucid interval, a drunken person on his return to sobriety, and a child of tender years if he understands the duty to tell the truth. The position therefore is that every person who is capable of expressing himself so as to be understood either directly or through interpretation by one who can understand and who also understands the duty to tell the truth is a competent witness.57 Formerly the husband or wife of an accused was generally not competent to give evidence for the prosecution without the consent of the accused, but since the coming into force of the Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323) all pre-existing laws and statutory disqualifications for witnesses have been abolished.59

The oath requirement is generally a condition precedent for the admissibility of the evidence of witnesses, and so any statement made by a witness without oath or affirmation is not considered as evidence in trials of criminal cases.60 There are however very important exceptions to this rule. One such exception is that an accused person in a  criminal trial may make a statement in his own defence without first taking an oath or making an affirmation and without being subject to examination of all the parties.61 Such a statement is admissible to the same extent as if it had been made under oath and affirmation and subject to examination by all parties entitled to cross-examine.62 The fact that the statement was given without oath or affirmation or that there was no possibility of cross-examination, as for instance, in case of statements from the dock goes only to weight and credibility of the statement and may be subject to comments by the court, the prosecution or the defence.63 Secondly, a voluntary confession statement of an accused person which is not made on oath is admissible in evidence as an exception to the hearsay rule.64 Another exception applies by practice to children of tender years. In criminal proceedings the unsworn evidence of such children is admissible if the court is satisfied that though they do not understand the nature of an oath, they are possessed of sufficient intelligence to express themselves so as to be understood and they understand the duty to tell the truth. Such evidence however needs corroboration.65 Furthermore a witness merely called to produce a document need not be sworn.66 An advocate when required merely to explain a case in which he had acted as counsel or to give evidence of the terms of a compromise between parties to litigation in which he had acted need not be sworn and it is customary for him to speak from his place at the bar.67 A judge sitting at a trial and a member of the jury in a criminal case are incompetent to testify in the trial68 but if a judge merely appears to explain a case in which he had presided, it would seem that he need not be sworn.69 Of course the judge can ask questions of the witnesses, and by the practice of our courts, it is unthinkable for an objection to be raised to his questions, but the Evidence Decree in section 68 (4) permits it now. Under normal circumstances the prosecution calls its witnesses to prove its case and the defence if it chooses or where by law an onus is cast on it also leads evidence to rebut the case made by the prosecution or to discharge its onus. The judge, however, is not a mere umpire and has statutory discretion at any stage of a criminal trial to call any person as a witness or re-call and re-examine any person already examined70 but this statutory power of the judge to call a witness suo motu at the close of the prosecution's case must according to case law be exercised judicially to prevent injustice, eg to clarify an obscure or ambiguous point in the prosecution's case.71 This is particularly so where the judge calls a witness after the defence had closed its case. In such a situation, undoubtedly the judge has a discretion under statute to call a witness not called by either the prosecution or the defence and without the consent of either of them provided that in his opinion that course was necessary in the interest of justice. Such a witness however, it has been held, could not be examined or cross-examined either by the prosecution or the defence without the leave of the court.72

The discretion of the court to call such a witness has also by the decision of our courts been limited to cases where something had arisen ex improviso on the part of the accused which no human ingenuity could foresee or where the prosecution had been taken by surprise and the evidence was allowed to rebut a defence belatedly raised.73 The apparent decision of Andoh J in Nyarko v The Republic [1974] 1 GLR 206 that the prosecution or the defence could not cross-examine the witnesses of the court without leave would seem to be no longer good law in view of the specific provision subsequently made by the Evidence Decree that all parties may cross-examine witnesses called by the Court.74

The judge at a criminal trial has complete control over the proceedings. He is enjoined by law to exercise reasonable control over the mode and order of interrogating witnesses and presenting evidence so as to make the interrogation and presentation as rapid and distinct as will be conducive to a ready understanding of the case.75 And he must exercise such control as will protect witnesses from being unduly intimidated, harassed or embarrassed.76 It is not unknown for a witness called by the prosecution or the defence to give evidence adverse to the party calling him. Before the Evidence Decree reformed the law, the legal position was that a party calling a witness was not permitted at all to impeach his credibility unless the witness was treated as hostile and the effect of treating a witness as hostile was said to make his evidence in favour of the party calling him to be nugatory.77

The prohibition against impeaching ones own witness has now been abolished by section 81(1) of the Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323). Under that provision the credibility of a witness or any part of his testimony may be attacked or supported by any party including the party calling the witness. There is a further new provision in the Decree that where before he calls a witness a party has reasonable grounds to believe that he will want to attack the credibility of the witness or any part of his testimony, the party shall out of the presence of the witness notify the court and every other party of his expectation, except that if the witness is a party, the witness need not be notified.78 So obviously an accused person need not be notified by the prosecution that they intend to impeach his credibility. This question of impeaching the credibility of witnesses highlights a perennial problem in relation to the evidence of witnesses, as to how the court is to treat a statement or a witness previously made which is conflicting or inconsistent with his testimony in court. The case law on the subject inherited from English law in R v Harris79 would seem to be that once it is proved that a witness has made a previous statement the contents of which are shown to be inconsistent with his evidence in court, the effect of his testimony on oath is said to be negligible. Akainyah JSC reading the unanimous judgment of the Supreme Court in Poku v The State80 pointed out that this English principle enshrined in R v Harris (supra) was strict but not absolute; quoting the English Court of Criminal Appeal case of R v Golder he said:

"[I]n this country it would expose the administration of criminal justice to ridicule if the testimony of a witness on oath were rejected outright because he is alleged to have made a previous unsworn statement which is in conflict with his evidence without carefully considering his account of the circumstance under which any such statement was made."81

And in Lanquaye v The Republic82 the court noted that the principle in R v Harris was rather too widely stated. It said that:

"The evidence of a witness on oath cannot be ignored simply because he has made on a less formal occasion another statement inconsistent with the sworn testimony. There may be very good reasons for making the unsworn statement and ... such a wholesale rejection, if it is the law of England cannot possibly be the law of Ghana."

The principle governing inconsistent statements as is expounded in the Poku and Lanquaye cases has now been institutionalised in the said section 81 (1) of the Evidence Decree and a sworn testimony of a witness which is inconsistent with his prior unsworn statement is only liable to be rejected according to the circumstance of the  case.83 It is obvious that the principal source of evidence in criminal cases for the purpose of proving or disproving guilt is the testimony of witnesses. Without a witness or witnesses therefore it is impossible to prove any case in court, and the fundamental principle of evidence in criminal cases as is contained in section 60 of NRCD 323 is that subject to statutory exceptions a witness may not testify to a matter unless sufficient evidence is introduced to support a finding that he has personal knowledge of the matter84 and the relevant evidence in proof of personal knowledge may, but need not consist of the testimony of the witness himself.85 Moreover if no objection is raised against his evidence by any party, a witness may testify to a matter without any proof that he has the requisite personal knowledge.86

The effect of this statutory provision in section 60 of the Evidence Decree enjoining personal knowledge of matters for the legal validity of the evidence of a witness on those matters is that, in the absence of personal knowledge then following objection to the evidence by or on behalf of the person against whom the evidence is proffered, the evidence on those matters is inevitably inadmissible and in law could not be the foundation for a finding of guilt.

As is obvious from a consideration of the structure of our Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30) and the provisions of the Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323), it is oral testimony by witnesses and not evidence by affidavit or depositions which is central to our criminal justice system. There are two exceptions. The first is admission by an accused person even if it is contained in an affidavit, provided it satisfies the well-known requirements of voluntariness as is expounded in Ibrahim v R87 by Lord Sumner. The second is a statutory provision meticulously formulated in section 124 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act. 30).The provision is so important that its terms deserve careful study. It is as follows:

"124. (1) Whenever the High Court or a Circuit Court is satisfied that the examination of a witness is necessary for the ends of justice, and that the attendance of such witness cannot be procured without such delay, expense, or inconvenience as, in the circumstances of the case, would be unreasonable, the Court may dispense with such attendance and issue a commission to any District Magistrate, within the local limits of whose jurisdiction the witness resides, to take the evidence of the witness.

(2) The Magistrate to whom the commission is issued shall proceed to the place where the witness is or shall summon the witness before him, and, after satisfying himself that sufficient notice has been given to the parties to the proceedings, shall take down the evidence of the witness in the same manner, and may for this purpose exercise the same powers, as in the case of a trial." It is provided that depositions so taken may subject to all just exceptions be read in evidence in the case by either party and shall form part of the record and furthermore that it may also be received in evidence at any subsequent stage of the case before another court.88

Generally speaking, with few exceptions, the jurisdiction of our courts in criminal cases is territorial and does not extend to foreign countries unless a statute specifically extends it.89 Furthermore our orders and court processes are intra-territorial. A judge in Ghana purporting to act under section 124 of Act 30 can therefore claim no power to order a magistrate in a foreign country to proceed to any place to take evidence under section 124 of Act 30. It seems therefore that apart from admissions of accused persons which may be contained in an affidavit and deposition taken in Ghana under the strict provision of sections 124-127 of the Criminal Procedure Code, evidence by affidavit in criminal trials is not known to our law, and it will be legally wrong to found any conviction on such evidence.

The essential duty which the Republic undertakes when it prosecutes an accused person is to lead admissible evidence by competent witnesses to establish the guilt of the accused person beyond reasonable doubt. To do this the prosecution must lead evidence to establish to the requisite degree of proof, every material ingredient of the offence. All witnesses therefore, essential to the unfolding of the narrative on which the prosecution is based must be called by the prosecution, whether in the result the effect of their testimony is for or against the prosecution.90 The effect of this view of the duty of the prosecution is that where a material witness was available who would be able to dispose of the case one way or another and he was not called, then the prosecution must be considered to have failed to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt,91 and a convicted person would be entitled to have his conviction quashed.
