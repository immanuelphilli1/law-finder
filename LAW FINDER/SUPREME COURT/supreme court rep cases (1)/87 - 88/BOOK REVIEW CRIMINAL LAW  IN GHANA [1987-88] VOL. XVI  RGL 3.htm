BOOK REVIEW: CRIMINAL LAW  IN GHANA [1987-88] VOL. XVI  RGL 303—304

OSEW E A

 

MR Justice Twumasi has fulfilled a long-felt need in the administration of law and justice in Ghana generally, and particularly, in the field of the criminal law, by writing and publishing the first comprehensive textbook on the substantive criminal offences in the country since the founding of the Law School over 20 years ago.

He is most qualified in the field, because of his long background in the practice and administration of the criminal law. He was a legal practitioner, a prosecutor at the Attorney-General's Office; and he has been on the bench for a long time and at all levels: from a District Magistrate via the Circuit to the post of a High Court Judge. He is, therefore, a principal actor in the legal drama, not a commentator.

Criminal law in Ghana, in its 24 chapters, covers all that is necessary to know about this branch of the law. The topics discussed include the theory and general principles of the law and particular offences. Treason and subversion, with the first treason trial in the country (1963) and the subversion trial (1972), are vividly and dramatically narrated. Murder, insanity, stealing in all its forms, bigamy, contempt of court, criminal libel, corruption and extortion, are also given secure places. Economic crimes, like offences against the currency, customs, smuggling, price control and hoarding are lucidly explained. Chapter 24 contains offences such as trial by ordeal, slave-dealing and motor offences, which are usually not found in criminal textbooks but in separate traffic regulations.

Mr. Justice Twumasi gives flesh to the skeleton of our Criminal Code (Act 29) which makes drab and hard reading. He has made easy the reading and study of the criminal law by compiling in one book, offences that are scattered in various enactments, acts, decrees and legislative instruments.

The book is uniquely written with originality of approach. The author informs the reader from local background. Most of the illustrations and cases are locally decided; but he does not hesitate to fall on English and Commonwealth cases to supplement where necessary and where there are no local authorities.

Having been published in 1985, recent cases are not covered. This is not the fault of the author, since the manuscript was with the publishers for years before seeing print. The publishers also pass the buck to the economic and other constraints in Ghana which fetter prompt publication of good works. This, however, does not detract from the fact that Criminal Law in Ghana is a monumental work as rightly described by veteran publisher.

The publishers inaptly describe this book as volume one; yet it is a comprehensive work on the substantive law on the subject. According to the author there is another book to appear (not necessarily volume two) as supplementary to the present book which will cover criminal procedure and evidence.

The 634-page book with good print, blue cover and a portrait of a prisoner with handcuffs and key spread across his face behind the prison bars, is published by the Ghana Publishing Corporation, Tema.  The hard cover sells for ¢1,980.00 and the soft cover for ¢1,495.00.

The Judge’s book stands out as a castle in criminal jurisprudence in Ghana. He has laid the foundation for others to build upon.

The readership extends to all and sundry: members of the bench, legal practitioners, “pocket lawyers”, law tutors and students, the police, taxi and “trotro” drivers, sociologists and criminologists.
