MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: ANNUAL CONFERENCE OF THE GHANA BAR ASSOCIATION 1988 [1987-88] VOL. XVI 7—32

 

VARIOUS functions and events involving the Ghana Bench and the Bar occurred almost unnoticed by the public during the year 1988. The exception was the Annual Conference of the Ghana Bar Association held at Sekondi-Takoradi on 4 October 1988. Public interest in the \conference was aroused by the controversial opening address delivered by Mr Peter Ala Adjetey, the National President of the Ghana Bar Association. In his speech, he referred to corruption by few members of the Bench. This aspect of his speech engendered such controversy receiving wide publicity in both the local and the world press, including the BBC that the Review deems it wise and desirable to present in full the text of the President’s speech. We also have the pleasure to present the full text of the speech made by Mr Justice J N K Taylor, who deputised for the Chief Justice, Mr Justice E N P Sowah, on that occasion. Also included in the package is the talk of Mr Alao Aka-Bashorun, the National President of the Nigerian Bar Association who was a special guest of the Ghana Bar Association at the 1988 Annual Conference.

Address delivered by Mr Peter Ala Adjetey, National President of the Ghana Bar Association at the Opening Session of the Annual Conference of the Ghana Bar Association held at Takoradi on Tuesday, 4 October 1988.

Mr Chief Justice’s Representative (Mr. Justice J N K Taylor), My Lords, Nananom, Your Excellencies, Distinguished Guests, My Fellow Members of the Legal Profession:

Although you have been welcomed to the twin city of Sekondi-Takoradi by the Regional President of the Western Regional Branch of the Ghana Bar Association in the person of Mr. D D Gwira, I wish, on behalf of the General Council of the Bar, to welcome all the distinguished guests who have found the time to attend the opening session of our 1988 annual conference and have done us such great honour by their attendance. We realise that for many of you it has taken some effort to travel all the way from your usual places of residence to Takoradi to grace this occasion with your presence. We are grateful to you for the honour that you have done our Association.

During the year, we have lost a few members of the Association including Alex Asante-Tannor, C T Yebuah, William Ofori-Atta and George Commey Mills-Odoi. Let us be up-standing and observe a minute’s silence in their memory.

The past year has been an extremely busy and eventful year and I intend as usual to set out as briefly as I can some of the things that the General Council of the Bar has tried to achieve with varying degrees of success during the year under review. In the first place, the national officers of the Bar have tried during the year to keep up with their programme of visits to regional Bars. We took the view, however, that in view of the financial burden necessarily imposed on regional tours, it would be desirable to leave it to regional Bars to invite the national officers to visit them in their regions. I cannot say that this policy has worked to our entire satisfaction. Hardly any regional branch has found it convenient to invite the national officers to visit it and those regional branches that were visited by members of the General Council or by the national officers found themselves receiving such members only because programmes initiated by the General Council has to be implemented at the regional level. In the light of this experience the national officers may consider drawing up their own programme of regional tours which will be financed almost entirely from the coffers of the national secretariat of the Bar. It is important that the national officers of the Bar should be in constant touch with our members at the regional and local levels so that problems that arise at these levels can be discussed and attended to, so to speak, in situ. The importance of regional tours by the national officers, therefore, cannot be too strongly emphasised.

In accordance with our policy of seeking to achieve results in all our endeavours by consultation, co-operation and conciliation, in the course of the year, we have held meetings with several officers of government. As usual, we have held very fruitful discussions with the Chief Justice on the administration of justice in the country generally. The administration of justice in this country continues to be plagued by the absence of a judicial council. We had assumed that a functioning judicial council would be in place within a few months of the 1987 annual conference, but this was not to be. The absence of a functioning judicial council and of security of tenure for judges over the past couple of years has done incalculable harm to, and undermined, all efforts aimed at strengthening the judiciary and thereby enhancing the quality of justice administered by our courts. The reason for this is obvious. Not many lawyers of competence, ability and integrity are willing to become members of the judiciary when they are liable to be dismissed at the whim of the executive without being given an opportunity of answering any charges that may be made against them.

The conditions of service of judges in this country are simply a disgrace. It is generally felt that judges do not receive a living wage and that their salaries are inadequate to enable them to live in dignity and to maintain their families as they should. This state of affairs is calculated to undermine judicial confidence and ultimately to undermine the administration of justice itself. A strong judicial council able to fight for and maintain the independence of the judiciary and assure reasonably tolerable conditions of service for judges is an indispensable prerequisite to the maintenance of the rule of law in this country.

It grieves me to say that the Bar continues to receive reports which show that some judges and magistrates are engaged in a course of conduct calculated to undermine the efforts of the Bar to secure for the bench reasonably comfortable conditions of service. The behaviour of some judges both on and off the bench falls far below what is expected of them. It has been said time and again that a judge, like Caesar’s wife, should be above suspicion. Reports reaching us indicate that a small minority of judges and magistrates, in spite of all appeals, continue to engage in corrupt practices, even sometimes descending so low as to bargain over the amount they should receive by way of bribe to give judgment in the giver’s favour. It is criminally disgraceful that justice should be sold to the highest bidder. We call upon such judges and magistrates to put an immediate end to their career of corruption before draconian steps are taken against them. The judiciary and the legal profession are self-disciplining institutions. It was society that gave them the power to discipline themselves. If this power of self-disciplining is not properly and effectively used by the judiciary and the legal profession, society, by which I mean those responsible for making the laws of the State and for exercising executive power, will take away the right or power of self-disciplining given to the legal profession and the judiciary and persons from outside these institutions will enter the scene to discipline members thereof. I consider it right as National President of the Ghana Bar Association to draw attention to this danger and to express this concern.

The General Council also held useful discussions with the honourable Attorney-General and PNDC Secretary for Justice. We were greatly heartened by the discussions with the Attorney-General. Unfortunately, the optimism which was engendered in us by our discussions with the Attorney-General has not manifested itself in positive results or achievements. We hope that in the ensuing year the honourable Attorney-General will be much more successful with the employment of his powers of persuasion with his cabinet colleagues so that what he, like us, wish for the administration of justice in this country would find expression in appropriate legislative action. I shall deal with another aspect of our consultations with the Attorney-General later in this address.

The General Council of the Bar, after a number of abortive attempts, was also able to hold very frank discussions with the Acting Director of Prisons and his deputies. The discussions centred mainly on the conditions of prisoners, the physical conditions of the prisons in this country and matters relating thereto. Anyone familiar with the physical surroundings of our prisons will bear testimony to the generally unsatisfactory and filthy conditions of our prisons and particularly of police cells and other places of confinement. We tried to draw attention to these unsatisfactory and undesirable conditions and to point out that conditions in prisons should not be dehumanising.

When, several years ago, in a debate in the Parliament of Ghana, the life of which was cut rudely short by some members of our armed forces, I tried to impress upon the government of the day the necessity to improve conditions in our prisons; members of parliament and especially members of the government party, almost laughed me to scorn. My words and admonition, however, proved to be prophetic, for within a few months all of us heard on the radio that parliament had been dissolved and that all members of parliament and all ministers and members of the government were to report to the nearest police station for their own safety. Of course, we all know what happened to them when they reported at those police stations. They found themselves in the very prisons, the conditions of which I had tried to get them to improve to no avail and needless to say they were not particularly amused by the conditions in which some of them had to live or exist for several years.

Everywhere in the civilised world, attempts are being made to improve conditions of prisoners and generally to reduce the prison population by resorting to custodial sentences only where it is absolutely necessary to do so. This, of course, requires co-operation of the Bench and all those concerned with the administration of justice. We suggested the need to have a national parole board in this country to examine automatically and periodically the cases of all those who are in prison or some form of confinement whether as a result of an order of a court of competent jurisdiction or as a result of executive fiat with a view to deciding whether they should be released on parole or otherwise.

We followed the meeting with the Acting Director of Prisons, with a meeting with the PNDC Acting Secretary for the Interior at which meeting were present not only the Acting Chief Director of the Ministry of the Interior but also the Acting Director of Prisons. We stressed the need to reduce the number of police barriers in the country to a minimum since these barriers were more calculated to harass innocent citizens than to prevent the commission of serious crimes. We also discussed the illegal activities of members of the Civil Defence Organisation or the so-called People’s Militia and the need to contain their revolutionary zeal within limits and to prevent them from harassing innocent citizens and using the weapons freely made available to them for the purpose of settling private scores. We again also raised the question of the establishment of a national parole board as well as other issues which will be dealt with later in this address.

In my address to the annual conference held in Accra in 1987, I indicated that substantial progress had been made towards making the publication of a house magazine or journal for the Ghana Bar Association a reality. At that time I said that the date for the publication of a Ghana Bar Bulletin was a matter of weeks rather than months. As it turned out, I was wrong. There were more problems to be surmounted than I had imagined, not the least of which was the problem of money. As you know, the Ghana Bar Association cannot boast much by way of financial resources. Our annual subscriptions are too low and are paid, in any case, by only some 25 to 30 per cent of our membership in spite of the efforts that we make to get members to pay their subscriptions. The General Council has decided that subscriptions should be raised substantially and this matter would be brought before this conference. Be that as it may, we had serious problems in raising the necessary capital to fund the publication of the Ghana Bar Bulletin. As you all know, we were able eventually to overcome these seemingly insurmountable difficulties and on 16 May 1988, at a ceremony at the British Council Hall in Accra, which was reasonably well-patronised, the Ghana Bar Bulletin made its debut in the arena of professional publications in this country. We still have  some teething problems, the most important of which is the problem of distribution, but we think that these problems will in due course be solved so that our house magazine will be available the length and breadth of this country and world-wide. On this occasion it is only proper for me to congratulate and thank all those whose efforts made the publication of the Ghana Bar Bulletin a reality. We are not yet out of the woods and we ask all persons of goodwill to support this worthwhile publication in a practical way. In a climate in which we are effectively denied access to the media except on matters which are regarded non-controversial, it is important that an organisation such as ours should maintain its own organ of communication.

As you will recall, we had expected that a seminar on civil procedure and legal aid sponsored by the International Bar Association would be held in Accra in June this year. We had expected that this seminar would have been followed by the 6th Biennial Conference of the African Bar Association also in Accra, in the same month. Unfortunately, owing to a number of factors, it was not possible for either the seminar or the 6th Biennial Conference of the African Bar Association to come on as planned. Following consultations with the International Bar Association and some officers of the African Bar Association and of its constituent organisations, it has been decided that the International Bar Association seminar on civil procedure and legal aid, will be held in Accra probably at the Kwame Nkrumah at Conference Centre on 20 and 21 March 1989, followed by the 6th Biennial Conference of the African Bar Association probably at the same venue in Accra from 22 to 25 March 1989. It is expected that the theme of the 6th Biennial Conference of the African Bar Association will be “Human Rights in Africa” and a panel of distinguished speakers both from and outside Africa is being invited to address the conference on various topics under that general theme. The first draft programmes for the seminar and the biennial conference, the organisation of which the Ghana Bar Association is largely responsible, are almost ready and, as soon as approval is given for them, they will be made available to members of our Association and to members of the African Bar Association and all those concerned so that registration for the seminar and the conference can start in earnest.

The situation with regard to human rights in this country has improved considerably since the hectic and almost chaotic days of 1982 and 1383 but it still leaves much to be desired. Probably one should not really expect otherwise in a military administration and as long as laws like the Habeas Corpus (Amendment) Law, 1984 (PNDCL 91) and the Preventive Custody Law, 1982 (PNDCL 4) remain on our statute books, human rights violations are likely to continue without any chance of their being effectively challenged. As things stand now anyone in this country is liable to be picked up at any time of the day or night at the behest of the executive or agencies of the government and can literally be kept in detention or confinement in whatever conditions the executive thinks fit without being brought to court for trial and without anybody being able effectively to do anything about it. Such a situation is inherently subversive of human rights and creates temptations for agencies of the government to violate the human rights of citizens.

The fact that human rights violations may not be as common now as they were before does not derogate from the potential threat to human rights of the existence on our statute books of the two laws I have mentioned and laws of a similar hue. Specifically the human rights of some of our members have been violated in the course of the year. Several of our members have been summarily arrested and detained without so much an explanation for their detention. One or two of them have been released from such detention but as long as a single person is unfree the rest of us are unfree and we cannot therefore say that we are happy with the existing situation.

At the 1987 annual conference, I mentioned that the Ghana Bar Association, believing that they should not engage merely in negative criticisms but offer constructive advice, accepted an invitation from the Board of Public Tribunals to submit suggestions to the Board on measures aimed at enhancing the administration of justice by the public tribunals. This was in fact done in June 1987 when the Association submitted a memorandum to the Board of Public Tribunals on this matter. Up to now the Board of Public Tribunals has not communicated to the Association what it intends to do with the suggestions which it invited itself from the Association and no change appears to have taken place in the manner in which public tribunals are run and in their jurisdiction.

It is unnecessary for me to go through the exercise of setting out again the reasons for our Association’s opposition to the existence of the public tribunals in their present form, It is perhaps pertinent to recall that at the Call Dinner held last year the Attorney-General indicated that the public tribunals system would be reviewed to allow for some forms of appeals to the traditional courts. This promise has not yet been honoured and we call upon the Attorney-General to make good his promise for which he received thunderous applause at the dinner, as early as possible. We of the Ghana Bar Association are concerned with ensuring that there is one system of justice for all people in this country, not two as obtained at present: one in which there are rules which are known before hand and the other in which there are no rules previously ascertained or ascertainable.

It is necessary that seven years after the coup or the events that brought the present government into power, the system of public tribunals should be dovetailed into, or merged with the existing traditional legal system. I understand that in the Soviet Union this process did not take more than four or five years and the USSR is a vast country, several times the size of our own country and with several times our population. Instead of bringing about this merger, however, we understand that district tribunals are also going to be established. We can only protest against this step. If we are asking for investors to come into this country, we must be aware of the fact that all investors will be concerned with the safety of their investments and therefore necessarily with the type of justice that is administered by our courts and the extent to which our courts and the extent to which our courts are free and independent.

I consider it necessary in this address to advert to a matter which may appear to some to be of relative insignificance but which the Bar Association considers as important and significant. I am referring to the recent call by certain sections of the media for the introduction of corporal punishment or flogging as a sentence to be awarded to offenders against our laws. I have even heard it said that in a recent television programme the Acting Director of Prisons was said to have expressed the view that the introduction of corporal punishment could ease over-crowding in our prisons. The People’s Daily Graphic has taken up the matter and in two editorials has advocated the introduction of corporal punishment. It is strange that in this day and age, in a society which professes to be civilised we should be advocating the introduction of barbaric and degrading forms of punishment.

The naivety of the argument that corporal punishment should be meted out in our schools and by parents on their children and by implication that this could be extended to adult members of the population as a punishment for the commission of offences is self-evident. What this argument does is to equate our adult population with children.  South Africa is said to inflict corporal punishment in adult black members of the population but that is only because the apartheid regime regards all black people as children fit to be flogged in the same way as children are flogged in schools for various types of misdemeanour. To inflict corporal punishment on an adult citizen is to violate his human rights because infliction of corporal punishment involves the application of cruel and degrading punishment which is not permitted under international human rights treaties and other conventions. It is perhaps not insignificant that Ghana has not yet seen it fit to ratify the African Charter on Human and People’s Rights promulgated by the Organisation of African Unity of which Ghana is a member. This Charter makes the imposition of cruel and degrading punishment an offence against international law.

If our prisons are over-crowded, surely the reasons for this are, obvious. In the first place, most of our prisons were built scores of years ago for a population that was about one-third what it is now. It must only be expected that they cannot accommodate the prison population that would necessarily be increased by the increase in the general population of the country all other things being equal. In the second place, too many custodial sentences are imposed these days especially by the public tribunals. The answer to this is two-fold. In the long run more prison accommodation must be made available but as an additional measure, resort to custodial sentences should be had more infrequently than hitherto. It may be that crimes are on the increase but if they are, the reasons can be found in the existing economic and social conditions of our times. The solution should be a social and humanitarian rather than a draconian one. Draconian measures cannot solve any problems in the long run. A call for the introduction of corporal punishment is a call for a march into the dark past to which we all hope we shall never return. It is, in fact, a call to a form of recidivism from which we must recoil with horror. The Ghana Bar Association calls upon the Ghana Government to reject the short-sighted calls made on it to introduce corporal punishment as a way of punishing adult members of this country.

The world is new awash with acronyms and Ghana has not been left behind in this development. We have our own ERP or Economic Recovery Programme. We are also all familiar by now with SAP or Structural Adjustment Programme, and recently we are becoming familiar with PAMSCAD or Programme of Action for Mitigating the Social Costs of Adjustment. Over the past several years this country has been going through the throes of an economic recovery programrne and a structural adjustment programme as part of the conditionalities of the International Monetary Fund without which we cannot obtain much-needed assistance from that world financial institution.

The implementation of the structural adjustment programme has meant the imposition of the severest economic hardships on the people of this country and these hardships are being experienced by all, rich or poor alike, except probably for a very few breed of Ghanaians. The living conditions of most of us have deteriorated beyond description. The structural adjustment intended to improve the economy has had the effect, unintended or not, of institutionalising poverty. The signs of this poverty stare us in the streets of our main centres of population.  Begging by Ghanaians which was relatively rare, has now become an everyday sight.

The government itself no doubt, realises the severe economic and social hardships imposed on the people of this country by their relentless implementation of the structural adjustment programme. On 29 August 1988, in his speech at the opening of the 24th Biennial Convention of the International Federation of Women Lawyers at the conference hall of the Kwame Nkrumah Conference Centre in Accra, Mr D F Annan, a former Court of Appeal judge and now a member of the PNDC, said that while Ghana appreciated the congratulations and praises of the western industrialised nations for the success that Ghana has achieved in its structural adjustment programme yet what we would appreciate more would be more assistance and that praises alone were not enough. He is said to have repeated this on a subsequent occasion at another venue. He also added that the success Ghana has achieved in the process of structural adjustment has been achieved at a certain cost. He did not elaborate.

The cost or price which had to be paid for the so-called success of our structural adjustment programme is the drastic reduction in the living standards of most Ghanaians. So severe are our economic constraints that Ghana is in danger of becoming an “Oburoni wawu,” nation where the majority of the populace rely on second-hand clothing imported from abroad to cover their nakedness. Very few persons in paid employment especially in the public service can afford to wear clothes other than second-hand clothing. The cost of education has sky-rocketed to an extent in which only the very few privileged members of our community can afford to send their children to primary schools let alone secondary schools and universities. Over the years, the physical structures of our schools have deteriorated to an extent unknown in our previous history and in spite of the much vaunted junior secondary school scheme introduced about a year ago,  hardly any improvement has taken place in the physical environment of our schools. The implementation of the junior secondary school project has taken the form of merely changing the names of existing middle schools into junior secondary schools except in very few cases.

This country seems to have abandoned any attempt at formulating, let alone, implementing a housing policy for several years now. The State Housing Corporation which was intended to provide houses for those not sufficiently economically well-off to be able to provide their own accommodation has now become a veritable building contracting firm ready only to build houses for those who can pay in advance the millions of cedis necessarily involved in building the simplest dwelling houses in this country at the present time. The State Housing Corporation is, in other words, ready to help only those who can already help themselves and totally unprepared or ill-equipped to assist those who need their assistance most in securing accommodation. If we are to sleep rough and at the mercy of the elements today so that our children may be able to live comfortably in mansions tomorrow, then our children may indeed never be born to be able to enjoy such life.

We have now been told that an entirely new programme which is to be implemented, for the first time anywhere in the world in Ghana, going by the name of PAMSCAD or Programme of Action for Mitigating the Social Costs of Adjustment, has been launched, the purpose of which is to attempt to mitigate some of the harsh effects of the structural adjustment programme and to attempt to introduce in a reasonable manner, programmes of social reconstruction and provision of social amenities generally. It is said that under this programme Ghana will receive assistance from various international organisations to tackle some of the very serious social problems that we have in this country—problems created by the disintegration of our basic social infrastructures and the disappearance of any semblance of a welfare state in this country.

It cannot fail to be noted that since the introduction of the economic recovery programme and its concomitant structural adjustment programme, a number of countries in the western industrialised world have been reported as having offered various types of assistance to Ghana. In the main, this assistance has taken the form of loans carrying interests of varying decrees. Straightforward grants have been the exception rather than the rule. It must be obvious to anyone that if we continue at the present rate at which we are contracting loans, we may find ourselves in a position in which, in attempting to solve the problem of indebtedness, we end up by accumulating more debts and being incapable or unable to disentangle ourselves from the strangle-hold of indebtedness which will persist and fall upon the shoulders of our grand-children and great grand-children.

While it is not a bad thing to be assisted when in distress, such assistance must be really substantial and not cosmetic; it must be calculated to solve the problem at its roots rather than to create more problems in its train. The type of assistance which can help this country out of its difficulties should take the form of straightforward grants. The western industrialised countries, and especially the United States, Britain and France should advance from the stage of rhetoric and declarations of intent and take positive action towards the relief of indebtedness in the developing world in general and Ghana in particular and one of the ways this can be done effectively is by the writing off of debts. If there are legislative or other obstacles to such a process, surely such obstacles can be removed if the will to do so exists. If we continue at the rate at which have been devaluing our currency from a few cedis to the dollar in 1981, to the present rate of some ¢300 to a dollar at the forex bureau rate, the time would not be far-off when it would take a basketful of cedis to buy a loaf of bread as did happen once in one western country.

We may now turn our attention for a few minutes to the political situation in this country. Last year we had occasion to criticise, not only the content of the proposals for district level elections in this country, but also the basis of legitimacy of the proposals themselves. We said, and this must be repeated here, that provisions or proposals for government at the district level form part of the constitutional framework of any country and that to implement such proposals before a constitution has been promulgated for the country, amounts to putting the cart before the horse and depriving the exercise of any basis of political legitimacy. We had occasion also to criticise the large proportion of nominations of members of the district assemblies that the proposals contained. It must not be forgotten that nominations were an odious feature of colonial rule and it is amazing that after 30 years of independence we should be harking back to colonial devices for keeping the local population in line. No one, of course, paid any heed to our objections and we have been told that the district level elections will take place sometime in November this year. Voters’ registers have been compiled on the basis of qualifications which has not been approved by the people either through their elected representatives in a constituent assembly or in a referendum. It is difficult to imagine what the basis of legitimacy of these proposals is and one can only say that they amount to nothing short of an imposition, however benevolent, beneficent or well-intentioned. In democratic constitution-making, good intentions alone are not enough.

The question that may well be asked at this stage is, after the district level elections what next? Obviously steps ought to be taken to make provision for government at the regional level and at the centre. Whatever may have happened with regard to district level government, we ask, in the name of democracy, that action be taken within a few months to appoint a broadly representative committee to devise the framework of a constitution for this country. It is suggested that such a committee should be given say about six months to complete its task and thereafter its proposals should be submitted to an elected constituent assembly which itself should be given a limited time within which to promulgate a constitution for a civilian democratic representative government in this country.

Comparisons, they say, are odious. This country has had a military government since 31 December 1981. Nigeria has had two military government since 31 December 1983. Nigeria has gone through the process of appointing a constitutional drafting committee which has completed its work and has handed over its proposal to an elected constituent assembly which itself is seriously engaged in the work of hammering out a constitution for a civilian democratically elected government. Although we do not necessarily agree with everything that has been done in Nigeria (eg some of us fundamentally disagree with the tieing up of the hands of the constituent assembly as to wait it can and cannot do and the limitation of the number of parties to two), we respectfully say that the people of this country are not entitled to anything less than what the people of our sister West African country of Nigeria are receiving. It is difficult to see why it should take a vest country like Nigeria four years to do what it is taking our country almost twice as long even to comtemplate.

As I have said, this country has been under military dictatorship since 31 December 1981. Some may say that it has been a benevolent dictatorship; others may say that it has been a repressive dictatorship. There is room for difference of opinion here. But whatever it is, whether benevolent or repressive, it is a dictatorship. That is one of the reasons why it is possible to have laws like PNDCL 4 and PNDCL 91 on our statute books.

During the period that this Government has been in power, a number of measures have been taken which have resulted in a number of Ghanaians going into exile for political reasons. Some of such persons have been convicted in absentia and it has become extremely hazardous for them to return to this country. The Ghana Bar Association is of the view that the time has come, after nearly seven years of ruling by the PNDC, for the government to declare a general amnesty for all Ghanaians whether for those that have gone into exile or not, so that all may live in peace and devote their energies towards the re-building of our country. There are some Ghanaians who have been in exile for some nine years, while others have been in exile for some seven years. From all indications, many of them are suffering great deprivations in their countries of asylum and it does the country no good to have so many of its able citizens languishing in exile in foreign lands. The Ghana Bar Association calls upon the PNDC to demonstrate political magnanimity and maturity by permitting all such Ghanaians to return to their native land.

We are not by this saying that if Ghanaians have committed offences against the ordinary laws of this country they should not be proceeded against. That is a totally different matter. Until the rule of the erstwhile AFRC and the present PNDC it was not possible under our law to try a person for a serious criminal offence in absentia. It is strongly urged that our law should be so modified that the conviction of all persons sentenced in absentia would automatically lapse upon their return to this country so that they can be tried afresh rather than that, upon their return to this country they would have to show cause why their convictions should not stand. We cannot, and ought not to live in this country in an indefinite state of fear and uncertainty.

The words inscribed in our national Coat of Arms are “Freedom and Justice.” A cynic may well ask: Where is your freedom and where is your justice? On 4 July 1852, an escaped negro slave in the United States who became a leading editor, politician and abolitionist called Frederick Douglass, in a speech on “The Meaning of the 4th of July for the Negro” ended with the following words, which again some cynics may say are largely as true today as when they were uttered nearly a hundred and fifty years ago:

“What to the American slave is your Fourth of July? I answer, a day that reveals to him more than all other days of the year the gross injustice and cruelty to which he is the constant victim. To him your celebration is a sham; your boasted liberty an unholy licence; your national greatness swelling vanity; your sounds of rejoicing are empty and heartless; your denunciation of tyrants, brassfronted impudence; your shouts of liberty and equality, hollow mockery. . .”

Let us do nothing now that will justify a Ghanaian cynic in independent and free Ghana, at the end of the day, to utter words similar to these words of desperation and utter loss of confidence in the system spoken of so many years ago by an American negro slave.
