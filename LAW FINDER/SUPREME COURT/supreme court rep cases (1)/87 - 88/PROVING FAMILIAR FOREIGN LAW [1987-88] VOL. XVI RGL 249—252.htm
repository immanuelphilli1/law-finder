PROVING FAMILIAR FOREIGN LAW [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 249—252

FUI TSIKATA

 

*

ACCORDING to the English rules of private international law,

(a) a foreign rule of law has to be pleaded and proved for the purposes of court proceedings as though it were a question of fact;

(b)  this normally involves calling an expert to give oral testimony, though affidavit evidence will sometimes suffice;

(c) legislation, law reports, textbooks and other literature on the foreign law are only admissible if (and only to the extent) tendered as part of the evidence of the expert witness; and

(d) these rules apply to proof of the law of common law countries (subject to statutory exceptions) to the same extent as to proof of the law of other legal systems.1

If the Ghanaian legal system adopts the same rules, certain paradoxes will emerge. Where, by private international law rules, an issue is to be determined in Ghanaian adjudication by reference to the English legal system, the relevant English rule could not be invoked as such unless an expert on English law were called to give oral testimony. However, if Ghana law were applicable, the Ghanaian court could, of its own motion (and would normally) refer to English textbooks and other writing, to inform itself of the English common law rule in order to determine whether to pronounce it as Ghanaian law.

One way of getting around the problem is by arguing that because the common law is part of the law of Ghana, no common law rule can be regarded as a foreign law. This would have the consequence that rules of American, Nigerian, Kenyan and English law (to take a few examples) neither need to be pleaded nor proved by expert testimony. Even though the result would be sensible, this route would be technically incorrect, and would produce its own difficulties.  Different common law jurisdictions differ in the formulation of specific rules, even as they share a broad approach and usually have many identical rules,1a Indeed, the English rules on proof of foreign law assume differences between common law jurisdictions: there would otherwise be no basis for requiring that other common law rules be proved by a special process. The implication of accepting that argument would be that every rule of every common law jurisdiction would be a part of the Ghanaian legal system. That would be impossible where, on a particular point, there is a conflict of rules as between common law jurisdictions. It would be difficult, on the other hand, to make a principled argument to the effect that the common law as determined in England should have a special status in an independent legal system.

The better route, in our submission, is to invoke the rules of judicial notice: these allow the judge, in relation to matters which are well-known, or capable of relatively certain determination, to make a finding by his or her own study of appropriate works of authority (sometimes aided by the parties' provision of relevant material), without the necessity of recourse to testimony.2 The statement in Dicey and Morris that the "courts take judicial notice . . . of notorious facts, but not of foreign law,"3 is immediately followed by two pages of “exceptional cases”, some of which threaten the existence of the “general” rule.

In Jadbranska Slobodna Plovidba, Split v Oysa Ltd,4 an argument arose as to whether English or Ghanaian law (specifically the Companies Code, 1963 (Act 179), section 13) applied as the proper law of a contract providing for arbitration in England, between a Yugoslav company and a “Ghanaian business venture”. The High Court held that English law was the proper law. (It did hold alternatively, that the same result would have been arrived at in the particular case, if Act 179 were applicable. But that holding is not the subject of our present discussion).

In determining what were the applicable rules of English law, it does not appear from the report that the court was assisted by oral testimony of an expert witness: a number of English cases were cited and applied in the normal way Ghanaian courts do when invoking a common law rule. This was, with respect, the correct course to follow, though it is not what the English private international law rules require in their most authoritative formulation.5 What if the law to be applied is contained in legislation rather than in cases? Common law jurisdictions share principles of statutory interpretation which make it possible for lawyers trained in one jurisdiction to have a reasonable understanding of others’ legislation without having to resort to an expert6

And what if the foreign law is the customary law of another African country:  Yoruba7 or Hausa law, or even Ewe law as it applies in Togo, or the law of the Kassena-Nankani people across Ghana’s border with Burkina Faso?  Of course, the resolution would have been quite simple in colonial times: all customary law (whether of communities within the colony and the protectorates or beyond) was, in the relevant sense foreign and had to be proved as a fact before the colonial courts.  Even then, judicial notice could be taken of particular rules which had become “notorious” by constant proof.8 Since 1960 the ascertainment of the customary law of various communities in Ghana has been recognised as a question of law.9

The relevant legislation does not, however apply to the customary law of communities outside Ghana.  Nonetheless, various communities in Ghana probably share more rules with their neighbours beyond the national borders than with others within. The reasoning which leads to the invocation of the doctrine of judicial notice for common law systems is also applicable to rules of customary law, at least those of our immediate adjoining neighbours.  Indeed, we submit that the argument could legitimately be extended to cover customary law systems on the African continent; there are broadly similar approaches and there are many principles which are common to the various customary law systems.

We propose therefore that courts should explicitly apply the doctrine of judicial notice to the rules of other common law and customary law jurisdictions, where these are relevant for the determination of issues before them.  In order to prevent unfair surprise to any party to a dispute, and to enhance the quality of the forum’s findings on a foreign law, there should be flexible requirements placing responsibility on the parties to give such notice of a foreign law and legal materials upon which they might wish to rely, as would enable other parties to make an adequate response. That the courts can thus exercise their power of judicial notice is supported by the following passage in the Commentary on the Evidence Decree, 1975:

“To the extent that foreign law is in issue it is a fact which must be proved like any other fact. When the foreign law can be determined accurately and readily from a source of unquestionable accuracy, that foreign law may be judicially noticed.”10

Finally, consideration could be given to the provision, through administrative and judicial channels, of facilities for securing help from institutions in the foreign country to guide the forum's determination. This would involve diplomatic negotiation and local legislation to give effect to whatever agreement was thus reached. In formulating detailed positions on this matter, the principal concerns would be a search for accurate and authoritative determination of the foreign rule, without inflicting undue expense on the parties or causing inordinate delay in the proceedings. These suggestions are relevant for the, proof of foreign law generally, whether or not familiar to Ghanaian lawyers.
