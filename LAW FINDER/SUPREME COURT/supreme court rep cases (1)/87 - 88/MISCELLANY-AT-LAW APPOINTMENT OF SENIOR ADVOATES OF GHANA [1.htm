MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: APPOINTMENT OF SENIOR ADVOATES OF GHANA [1987-88] VOL. XVI 1—6

AGBOSU L K

 

THE Review is happy to draw the attention of its readers to the fact that the General Council of the Ghana Bar Association has in 1988 implemented: its decision to honour Ghanaian lawyers of distinction by elevating such lawyers to the status of Senior Advocate of Ghana. The appointment is to be made on annual basis upon an advertisement in the news-media inviting qualified lawyers to apply for elevation to the status of Senior Advocate of Ghana. The basic criteria for elevation are (i) the applicant shall be a lawyer of good standing; (ii) he shall have been enrolled as a lawyer for at least fifteen (15) years; (iii) he shall have been in active legal practice and or occupation; (iv) he must possess sound knowledge of the law, professional competence, integrity and high general compartment; and (v) he must maintain or belong to a chamber with a reasonable quantity of the basic tools of the legal profession, principally by way of legal material or law books to ensure sound practice and afford training to young members of the profession.

The distinguished lawyers appointed for the first time as Senior Advocates of Ghana are:

Mr Jonathan Arthur

Mr Bernard J da Rocha

Mr E Brite-Gaisie

Mr Enoch Davidson Kom

Mr Peter Ala Adjetey

Mr Joseph Kwesi Agyemang

Mr Charles B Kwesi Zwennes

Mrs Joyce A Bamford-Addo

Mr Seth Y Bimpong-Buta

The elevation of Mrs Joyce A Bamford-Addo and that of S Y Bimpong-Buta, both lawyers in the public service is particularly significant. Whilst Mrs Bamford-Addo, the Director of Public Prosecutions, is the first Ghanaian woman lawyer to be appointed as a SAG, Mr. S Y Bimpong-Buta, the Editor of the Ghana Law Reports and Review of Ghana Law, is not only the youngest of the group of nine, but the first Ghanaian locally trained lawyer to be appointed a SAG. All the other eight distinguished lawyers were trained in the United Kingdom.

In a short but an impressive ceremony attended by members of the Bar, the Bench, the Diplomatic Corps, the Acting Attorney-General, Mr E G Tanoh, the Under Secretary for Justice, Mr Martin Amidu, some other secretaries of state and other government functionaries, the SAGS were admitted to the Degree and Status of Senior Advocate of Ghana at the Ghana Law School on 23 December 1988.

The Review takes this opportunity not only to warmly congratulate all the nine elevated lawyers but to urge them to maintain the high professional standards and competence for which they have been justifiably honoured, the Review is proud of them and wishes all of them well. We present below some of the speeches and comments made at the ceremony.

Speech delivered by the Chief Justice, Mr E N P Sowah at the First Ceremony for Admission to the Degree and Status of Senior Advocate of Ghana on Friday 23 December 1988, at the Ghana School of Law:

My Lords, Your Excellencies, Ladies and Gentlemen,

I would like to congratulate the persons who have today and for the first time in the history of this country—been admitted to the degree and status of SENIOR ADVOCATE OF GHANA. And I hope that I can presume to do so on behalf of all of you.

By the creation of the degree of Senior Advocate, Ghana has, as it were, embarked upon a journey through an unknown territory and these people have taken upon themselves to lead the way—to act as path-finders. It is a very heavy duty that they have assumed and I wish to share with them my thoughts on how they should do it.

I notice from the regulations governing the admission, conduct and practice of Senior Advocate of Ghana that they will have certain privileges. Some of these are:

(1) A Senior Advocate of Ghana (SAG) shall be entitled to use the letters SAG after his or her name.

(2) A Senior Advocate of Ghana shall have the front left hand strap and the pocket at the back of the left side of his black gown made of a prescribed red material instead of black.

If you want to know what I think of these privileges, my reply is, “What privileges!” And even the main privilege, which is: “A Senior Advocate shall rank before all other lawyers in Ghana,” does not amount to much compared with his obligations and responsibilities. In other words the privileges that a person acquires by being admitted to the degree of Senior Advocate are nothing compared with the responsibilities that he assumes. What are those responsibilities? The responsibilities of a leader, those responsibilities which are generally unwritten yet by which a leader is, in the last analysis, judged.

The role of a leader is one of self-sacrifice. “Greater love hath no man than this, that a man lay down his life for his friends.” Does that sound bombastic and high-saluting? Perhaps; but it is the final maxim and is applicable at whatever level one operates.

How does it apply to a Senior Advocate? Thus: For a person in that position, the principle is not so much what you can gain from the profession as what you can give to the profession. In all things, at all times, you must think first of what the profession would gain before considering what you would gain. For your practice is no longer yours, but Ghana’s.

I want to look at one or two of the ways in which the Senior Advocate should (notice I say should, or must I say “shall,” with apologies to the constitutional authorities?) contribute towards the development of the profession.

What comes into my mind immediately (because it is topical and I have personally been involved in it) is the legal aid scheme. Senior Advocates must be seen to be actively participating in it, fighting the cause of the poor. For far too long, the impression has persisted that the poor get a raw deal in the courts because they have not the means to engage counsel and even where counsel is engaged for them by the state, it is sadly believed that they get second-class service, because it is only inferior lawyers who accept such briefs. The better lawyers will not accept them because the fee paid by government is too small

Well, ladies and gentlemen, the time has come for us to erase that unfortunate impression and our Senior Advocates are in the best position to help us do so. They must be seen to be working for the indigent and under-privileged so that the poor can also say they had the services of a first class lawyer. To be able to do this, they have to sacrifice their time and devote a substantial part of their profitable practice to it. I am not asking you to lay down your lives here, but the principle is the same. There must be equal justice in our courts and in the country.

To be able to participate fully in the legal aid scheme, you obviously need assistance from others. And that brings me to my next point. The legal profession in Ghana is not properly organised, among other things, because the system of pupilage is not well developed. There are virtually no law firms, nor, except for a few cases, are there any chambers with a well-organised system for taking pupils. The effect is that many young lawyers come out and wander about trying to pick up experience and make a living as best as they can. It is for them a struggle for existence and, because the struggle is so intense, they pick up some bad habits.

On the other hand (that is on the side of the senior lawyers) what one finds is that because they have no juniors, they are far too often asking for adjournments. It should be possible for well-organised chambers to be represented in several courts at the same time, so that they do not ask for adjournments anywhere. If you will permit me, I shall share my experience with you in that respect. I had more than six pupils and juniors in chambers, they are all well known and now include High Court judges, former presidents of the Bar, eminent lawyers—some are amongst the recipients of this degree.

You may retort that things are not what they used to be. In my view this is not quite correct. Every junior in chambers knew that he got two-thirds of the fee which had been charged on the brief whether it was paid or not; and that was an incentive for hard work. There was therefore no need for the request of adjournment. At any time the courts were occupied by pupils and juniors from La and Kpobi Chambers now known as Lynes Quashie-Idun & Company.

One of the qualifications for admission as Senior Advocate is that “He must be in active legal practice and/or occupation and, if in private practice, be in a position to take in pupils in his chamber.” I do not think that that is adequate. He may be in a position to take in pupils; but will he do it after he has been made Senior Advocate? In my opinion that qualification should be tightened up a little more so that before a person is admitted he should have developed a well-organised system of pupilage in his chambers.

I will wish that the acquisition of the degree and status of Senior Advocate of Ghana by these gentlemen will not lead to the creation of an elitist club of lawyers They more than any practitioner, must conduct themselves with courtesy and modesty to the bench. They should be shining examples of what law and justice stand for in our society. They should be mindful of what Roscoe Pound said over 70 years ago. He said “The sporting theory of justice is so rooted in the profession in America that most of us take it for a fundamental tenet” Warren Burger, a former Chief Justice of America interpreted Roscoe’s theory as “Lawyers instead of search for the truth and justice, often tended to seek private advantage forgetting that they were officers of the court with a monopoly of legal services that mandated duties to the public as well as to their client.”

Perhaps the sporting theory is not only endemic to America, it is also found very much in operation in this country. Ghanaian lawyers are equal to any in common law Black Africa, but as is always the case a handful of members of the profession can inflict considerable harm, which is out of proportion to their numbers, on the public image of the profession. I may add that this also goes as well to those on the Bench.

Senior Advocates of Ghana I wish you well, Thank you.

Speech by Mr Bannerman-Williams, President of the Greater Accra Branch of the Ghana Bar Association

My Lord Chief Justice, My Lords, spiritual and temporal, The Honourable Acting Attorney-General, the Honourable Deputy Secretary for Justice, Your Excellencies, Regional Secretary, Members of the Bench and Bar, Senior Advocates to be, Distinguished Guests, Ladies and Gentlemen:

We are gathered here this morning to add to the pages of history; the history of the honourable profession of the law in Ghana. We do so by doing honour to some of the men of distinction in the legal profession, men to whom honour is due.

When applications went up for the degree and status of Senior Advocates of Ghana, a few friends asked me whether I had applied; I told them that I did not think I was yet qualified. Little then did I know that circumstances would for the time being incapacitate the President of the Bar and so impose on me the singular honour of performing the noble and enviable duty of conferring the degree and status of Senior Advocate on some of the most senior members of the Bar including the President himself, on behalf of the General Council of the Bar.

My joy is indeed great and my pride beaming, for this great honour circumstances have bestowed on me. I am indeed very grateful. My Lord Chief Justice, records have it that one hundred and eleven years ago, in 1877 to be precise, the first 10 lawyers were enrolled in the Supreme Court to practise law in the courts of Ghana, then called the Gold Coast. Eight-six years later, that is, in 1963 the first 9 locally trained lawyers were called to the Bar in Ghana. Today, 25 years after, the first 9 lawyers of the Ghana Bar Association have qualified for the degree and status of Senior Advocates of Ghana. These are significant milestones which make this day one of moment for the legal profession, and we are justly proud.

On behalf of the General Council of the Ghana Bar Association and on my own behalf, therefore, I salute these honourable lady and gentlemen of distinction for their remarkable achievement of being the first ever to qualify for the degree and status of Senior Advocates of Ghana. Honourable lady and gentlemen, it will be presumptuous on my part to remind you that your new status is one of honour, dignity and responsibility and may I charge you to hold it as such. Permit me also to remind you of the immortal lines of Shakespeare uttered through the lips of Mowbray in Richard II, and I quote:

“The purest treasure mortal times afford

Is spotless reputation; that away

Men are but gilded loam or painted clay

A jewel in a ten-times barred-up chest

Is a bold spirit in a loyal breast

Mine honour is my life, both grow in one

Take honour from me and my life is done.”

Although you have no forebears, you being the pioneers in this new status, yet, it is the wish of the General Council of the Bar that you cherish your new status like an enviable heritage handed down to you by men of excellence.

At this stage, may I draw your attention to what a former President of the Ghana Bar Association, in the person of Mr Johnny Quashie-Idun, said on 31 March 1976 on the occasion of the centenary celebrations of the Supreme Court of Ghana, I quote,

“. . . the Ghana Bar Association is always proud to recall the names of John Mensah Sarbah, Kobina Sekyi, J E Casely-Hayford, the Honourable Thomas Hutton Mills, Charles Bannerman, Sir Emmanuel Quist and Dr J B Danquah—their records in our law books and commentaries on their achievements are more eloquent than anything that can be said on this occasion. We must emulate their noble principles and not exhibit fear, even when we are not ‘threatened’ or ‘hindered.’

These were indeed great men whose lives were dedicated to the service of our nation and who contributed in no small measure towards the achievement of our independence and our national self-confidence.”

We wish to let go forth that a new torch of the honourable profession of the law in Ghana has this day been handed over to you; a torch which you must so bear aloft that when the time comes for you to take your exit, as indeed one day you would, it could be said of you that you were remarkable torch-bearers, men who knew no rule of life but duty and service to mankind, men who had no ambition but the greatness of their nation and profession and men who looked for no better reward than that of inner satisfaction of a task nobly performed.

Once again, on behalf of the General Council of the Ghana Bar Association, I congratulate you on your achievement. We pray for God’s guidance and protection in your new roles.

Thank you.
