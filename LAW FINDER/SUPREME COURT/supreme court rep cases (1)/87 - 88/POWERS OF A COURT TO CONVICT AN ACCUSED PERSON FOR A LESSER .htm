POWERS OF A COURT TO CONVICT AN ACCUSED PERSON FOR A LESSER OR INCLUDED OFFENCE OTHER THAN THAT CHARGED [1987-88] VOL.  XVI RGL 260—281

Amidu, Martin A.B.K.

 

INTRODUCTION

OUR system of criminal justice, a British colonial legacy, is conceptually based on the general principle of fairplay and fair trial. With the understanding of such basic requirements, the system of criminal justice is founded on the premise that law must be certain, easily ascertainable and accessible to the body-politic.  Hence an act or omission which is not proscribed by law shall not be punished as a crime.1 For this reason to safeguard the rights of the citizenry and to ensure fair trial and fairplay, both the Criminal Code, 1960 (Act 29) and the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30), apart from proscribing and defining acts which would amount to a criminal conduct, also prescribe penalties incidental thereto.

The Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, for example, deals with charges or indictments,2 the rule against duplicity,3 double jeopardy,4 the statement of particulars of offence, defects in such particulars and consequences incidental thereto.5 All these rules are provided as safeguards for ensuring certainty, fairness and lack of surprise in the trial of criminal cases.  For the same purpose, the Criminal Code, 1960 classifies acts into various categories with corresponding penalties. In such classification the legislature would seem to have deliberately categorised offences according to their nature, gravity and similarity. Those crimes which are so similar, interconnected and interrelated that their constituent elements or component parts consist of one and the same conduct are grouped together in one class, so that offences of lesser degrees of gravity are included in a major one. A classic example is provided under chapter 2 of the Criminal Code, 1960, dealing with the offence of criminal homicide and similar offences.6

Thus where an accused person is charged or indicted for an aggravated offence, there is no element of surprise or uncertainty when the aggravated offence is not proved but a lesser offence included in the major ingredient of the aggravated offence is proved.  An accused person in such circumstances can be said to be aware of the lesser offence because in preparing his defence for the aggravated offence, the essential elements of the offence of a lesser degree must have been considered.7 In consequence, both the common law and statute have for the purpose of avoiding duplicity of trials provide for circumstances where a trial court may return a verdict or give judgment finding an accused person guilty of an offence of a lesser degree or an included offence.8

 

Hence in some systems of criminal justice administration an accused person could not be convicted for a misdemeanour on an indictment for a felony even though all the ingredients of the misdemeanour may be present in the charge of felony and vice versa.  This is based on the fact that a felony and a misdemeanour are different things and that there can be a conviction for the other only when it is expressly provided for by statute.9 It is however permissible for a person to be convicted for a less aggravated felony or misdemeanour on an indictment charging a felony or a misdemeanour of greater aggravation, provided that the indictment or charge contains words apt to include both offences.10 Thus criminal codes of countries whose legal systems have their origin in the common law, sometimes provide expressly for circumstances when a court may convict for a lesser offence.11 Most often these statutes or codes classify offences into felonies, misdemeanours, indictable and non-indictable offences, but make general provisions for conviction in the alternative for lesser or included offences, so that trial courts have to determine in accordance with the rules of substantive criminal law and procedure when and in what circumstances the provisions for conviction for alternative lesser or included offences may be applicable.

Some of these statutes provide for a number of specific cases such as the circumstances under which when a person is charged with a full offence and an attempt is proved, a court may convict of attempt; where on a charge of attempt, the prosecution proves the full offence the court must convict of the latter offence; stealing charge receiving proved-conviction for receiving, etc.12 Such statutes apart from specific provisions also contain an omnibus clause which appears to cater for cases not caught by the special provisions.13 One such provision is section 154 of our Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, which provides:

"154. (1) When a person is charged with an offence consisting of several particulars, a combination of some only of which constitutes a complete lesser offence, and such combination is proved but the remaining particulars are not proved, he may be convicted of the lesser offence although he has not been charged with it.

(2) When a person is charged with an offence and facts are proved which reduce it to a lesser offence, he may be convicted of the lesser offence although he was not charged with it."

Such omnibus provisions within the context of specific provisions have given rise to problems of interpretation as to their scope and effect.14 The problem appears to be compounded in some jurisdictions whose criminal systems of justice are founded on the Anglo-Saxon system of jurisprudence where the procedural requirements enabling a trial court to convict an accused person depends on whether or not the accused person is tried by a jury, assessors or by a judge sitting alone. This is because the division of offences into indictable and summary offences, felony and misdemeanour carries with them particular modes of trial unless otherwise stated.15 The mode of trial appears to determine the type of verdict or judgment that can be given by the trial court.16 Following the powers of a trial court to return a particular verdict or give judgment on a charge or indictment depending on the mode of trial, the question arises as to when a trial court can substitute a lesser offence for an aggravated one to enable a conviction for the lesser offence.  For example, can a jury trying an accused person of an indictable felony convict of a misdemeanour as a lesser offence under the Ghanaian omnibus provision as misdemeanours can only be tried summarily?  At what stage and in what mode of trial in Ghana can a trial court invoke the exceptional and special powers to convict an accused person of a lesser offence in an aggravated one? The wording of section 154 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, for instance, has given rise to the question as to whether an appellate court can, suo motu exercise the powers conferred on the trial court, and in what circumstances?

It would appear that section 154 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, has never been a subject of interpretation and consideration by the appellate courts in Ghana until the decision of the Court of Appeal in Sarimbe alias Olala v The Republic17 in which it was held that an accused person could be convicted of assault on a charge of murder and that the Court of Appeal had jurisdiction to substitute a conviction of assault for one of murder by virtue of the said section 154. This decision brings into focus for the first time, the necessity to discuss the scope and effect of section 154 of the Criminal Procedure Code having regard to similar provisions in other jurisdictions having an analogous system of jurisprudence.

An attempt will be made in this discussion to show that viewed in the context and the totality of the Criminal Code, and the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, section 154 of the latter Act is of limited application and ought not to be interpreted to mean that an accused person can be convicted of any lesser offence other than the one charged. It would be contended that a broader interpretation of section 154 of the Criminal Procedure Code will run counter to the legal requirements of certainty and advance warning to the accused to enable him prepare his defence. The accused, it will be further contended, will in such circumstances be taken by surprise and the foundation of fairness as required by the Anglo-Ghanaian criminal justice system would be eroded.

HISTORICAL PERSPECTIVE

The administration of criminal justice in Ghana and Nigeria was governed in the colonial era by criminal codes under ordinances of the Gold Coast and Nigeria. Starting from 1914 and probably earlier, the ordinances of the Gold Coast and Nigeria dealing respectively with criminal procedure made similar provisions for convicting accused persons for lesser or included offences.18 The Criminal Procedure Code of Nigeria (Cap 43) for instance contained the following:

"179. (1) In addition to the provisions hereinbefore specifically made whenever a person is charged with an offence consisting of several particulars a combination of some only of which constitutes a complete lesser offence in itself and such combination is proved but the remaining particulars are not proved he may be convicted of such lesser offence or may plead guilty thereto although he was not charged with it.

(2) When a person is charged with an offence and facts are proved which reduce it to a lesser offence he may be convicted of the lesser offence although he was not charged with it."

The current Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30), as we have already indicated contains the same provisions in section 154 (1) and (2) except for the words emphasised above.

The main difference between section 179 of the Nigerian Code and its Gold Coast counterpart, section 141 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1951 (Cap 10) was that the words: "in addition to the provisions hereinbefore specifically made," do not appear in section 141 of the Gold Coast Ordinance, and the words: "minor offence," is used in place of "lesser offence" in the Gold Coast Ordinance. In view of the similarities of the lesser or included offence provisions in both jurisdictions and the fact that the West African Court of Appeal was the appellate court for the two colonies, an analysis of cases decided by the West African Court of Appeal in respect of these provisions may be of assistance to our understanding of section 154 of the Criminal Procedure Code.

 

A review of the case law on this aspect of the law before 1960 shows that the provision has seldom been the subject of application by the superior courts and the appellate courts have interpreted it only on few occasions.  The general position before 1960 would seem to be that a trial court could not invoke the lesser offence provision unless the aggravated offence with which the accused was charged could be reduced to the lesser offence.  It was not the case that the offence for which the accused was convicted or had to be convicted was less serious than the offence charged; but that the offence charged could be reduced to the lesser one.19 Thus in the case of a murder charge it has been held that the courts had discretion to convict the person of manslaughter in the absence of a specific provision in the Criminal Code making it possible to convict him of the lesser offence of manslaughter.20 At least it has been held by the West African Court of Appeal in the case of R v Gyan21 that on a charge of manslaughter under the Criminal Procedure Ordinance of the Gold Coast an appellate court could in the exercise of its powers under sections 141 of the Criminal Procedure Ordinance and 11 (2) of the West African Court of Appeal Ordinance (Cap 31) enter a substitute conviction for the offence of intentionally causing bodily harm.22 In contradistinction with the decision in R v Gyan the same court differently constitute held in the case of R v Noku of Yusa 23 that on a charge of murder the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Ordinance of Nigeria did not enable the court to convict the accused of committing an act intended to cause grievous bodily harm.

Apart from criminal homicide and related offences, the West African Court of Appeal decided in the case of R v Amos Kanu24 that an accused person who was charged with the offence of "fraudulent false accounting"- a felony punishable by seven years imprisonment, could not be convicted of the offence of false accounting by public officer," a misdemeanour punishable by imprisonment of two years. The reason for the decision was that the aggravated offence could not be reduced to the lesser offence.  Relying on the decision in R v Kanu, the Supreme Court of Nigeria held in R v Adokwu that upon a charge of conspiracy to cause a certain person to be charged with the offence of unlawfully giving an injection though knowing that he was innocent contrary to section 125 of the Nigerian Criminal Code (which carries imprisonment for seven years or more), the accused person could not be convicted of the lesser offence of conspiracy to effect an unlawful purpose under section 578 (6) of the Code. It would appear from the above decisions that before 1960, a trial court could only invoke the provisions enabling it to convict an accused person of a lesser offence when the lesser offence was embodied in the aggravated offence and could be reduced to the lesser one. If this view is correct then it is submitted that the lesser offence must be one for which the trial court is capable in law of convicting the accused person.  Otherwise an appellate court cannot apply the provision on appeal.

RECENT DECISIONS OF THE GHANA COURT OF APPEAL

As indicated earlier, the reported cases of the superior courts of Ghana show that the provisions of section 154 (1) and (2) of the Criminal Procedure Code have seldom been the subject of interpretation by the Court of Appeal.  Consequently it has been presumed that the Court of Appeal in Ghana may adopt decisions of the West African Court of Appeal on the interpretation of section 179 of the Criminal Procedure Ordinance of Nigeria which is in pari materia with section 154 of our Criminal Procedure Code.25 On 11 October 1985 and 17 April 1989, the Court of Appeal interpreted and considered the scope and effect of the above quoted provisions in the cases of Sarimbe alias Olala v The Republic26 and Moses Tsinowope v The Republic27 respectively.

In Sarimbe alias Olala the appellant fought his seven months old pregnant wife. She died the next day. The pathologist who performed the autopsy attributed her death to shock and intracranial haemorrhage.  He was prosecuted for murder on the grounds that he intentionally caused the death by unlawful harm.  The appellant denied the charge but admitted having slapped his wife.  He was convicted and he appealed.  In the light of the medical evidence, the Court of Appeal found that the offence of murder was not established.  The prosecutor then invited the court to substitute a verdict of manslaughter.  The court on its own motion, however, went on to hold that since on the evidence, the appellant admitted slapping the deceased, he was clearly guilty of assault within the meaning of section 84 of Act 29.  Consequently, in the words of the court, "in exercise of the court's power under section 154 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30), a verdict for assault would be substituted for the one of murder."

In the case of Moses Tsinowope, the appellant was indicted for the murder of one Amadu Tunde.  The facts of the case are that the appellant was determined to recover from the deceased person, money which the deceased had taken from the appellant's girl friend. As the appellant was walking with him towards his house for the money, he stopped at a spot and refused to go further. The appellant held his shirt and tried to drag him along and in the process, the deceased slapped him.  The appellant retaliated with a slap which felled the deceased in a supine position. The deceased was led away. The deceased was later found in his room lying on the ground in a coma from which he eventually died. A witness for the prosecution gave evidence at the trial that shortly before the appellant came and confronted the deceased, three girls had quarrelled and fought with the deceased that same evening.  The pathologist who performed the autopsy attributed his death to massive subdural haemorrhage which could have been caused by injury such as a blow on the head, a fall, a hit on the head with a club or a hard object.  At the close of the case for the prosecution the defence made a submission of no case which was overruled by the trial judge.  At the conclusion of the trial, the jury returned a verdict of not guilty of murder but guilty of manslaughter. The rejection by the court of the submission of no case on behalf of the appellant was the main grievance on the appeal.

In the light of the medical evidence, the Court of Appeal found that the offence of manslaughter was not established as the evidence did not show that the slap of the accused resulted in his death.  The court held, therefore, that the trial judge erred in putting the appellant to his defence on the charge of murder.  The court went on to hold further that the error of the trial judge in putting the appellant to his defence on the charge of murder did not necessarily occasion any miscarriage of justice in view of section 154 (2) of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30).  The Court of Appeal then proceeded to convict the appellant of assault on the grounds that if the learned trial judge had directed the jury on the issue of assault there was no doubt that the jury would have returned a verdict of guilty of assault.

In coming to the conclusion which it did the Court of Appeal sought to draw a distinction between sections 154 and 271 of Act 30 by stating that section 271 of Act 30 inferentially, disentitled a judge from withdrawing the charge from the jury if he was of the opinion that there was evidence that the accused had committed any offence of which he could lawfully have been convicted on the indictment upon which he was being tried.  In other words, if the accused person could be convicted on any offence related to the charge upon which he was being tried, then he must be put to his defence.  As the court pointed out, manslaughter for instance, was related to murder, therefore where the evidence did not support a murder charge, but one of manslaughter a trial judge was obliged to put the accused to his defence.  In the opinion of the Court of Appeal, notwithstanding section 271 of Act 30, when facts were proved which called into service section 154 (2) of Act 30, then on a submission of no case, a trial judge had to invite the jury to enter a verdict of not guilty on the charge in the indictment and call on the accused to defend himself on the lesser offence with which he could not have been convicted on the indictment.

On the authority of these cases, it now appears to be a proposition of law that: (1) where an accused person is indicted for murder and the prosecution fails to prove the offence or the alternate lesser offence of manslaughter, a trial court can, if facts are adduced which support the offence of assault, convict the accused person of that offence; (2) where the trial court erroneously convicts the accused person of manslaughter, the Court of Appeal can invoke the provisions of section 154 (2) and convict the accused of assault if the facts adduced support the offence of assault; (3) the jury in a trial of murder or manslaughter is entitled to be directed to consider returning a verdict of guilty of the summary offence of assault.

In other words, as a legal proposition of general application in the courts of Ghana, excluding the Supreme Court, the position now appears to be that: (1) in the application of the provisions of section 154 of the Criminal Code a trial court can, not only convict of a lesser offence but for a different offence of which the person has not been charged but which nonetheless is a lesser offence of an aggravated one; (2) the Court of Appeal has jurisdiction based on section 154 to substitute a conviction for a lesser offence other than the one charged, notwithstanding the fact that the trial court has no jurisdiction to try the lesser offence upon the indictment or the charge laid before it.

It would however appear that considering the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, dealing with convictions for lesser or included offences;28 the discretion of the judge to consider if there is a case to answer29 in a trial by jury at the conclusion of the prosecution's case; the duty of the judge in a trial by jury;30 the duty of the jury in such trials;31 the jury's exclusive right to consider and return their verdict;32 the duty of the jury to return a verdict on each charge unless otherwise ordered by the court;33 the imperative duties of the judge in respect of action on the verdict;34 and section 12 (2) of the Courts Act, 197135 (which empowers the Court of Appeal to substitute a verdict which the jury could have given at the trial), the decisions of the Court of Appeal in Sarimbe alias Olala and Moses Tsinowope cannot, with due respect, be said to represent an authoritative statement of the law of Ghana.

JURISDICTION

The appellate jurisdiction of the Court of Appeal in criminal matters is limited to appeals from decisions of a circuit court in cases tried on indictment and appeals from judgments, decrees or orders of the High Court.36 The High Court has original jurisdiction in almost all criminal matters.37 In practice, however, the High Court seldom tries cases summarily because such cases are rarely brought before it. The original jurisdiction of the court is therefore exercised mainly in respect of the most serious offences triable on indictment. It stands to reason that most of the criminal cases that come on appeal to the Court of Appeal are indictable offences.  As indicated earlier, the division or classification of offences in the Criminal Code carries with it definite modes of trial unless otherwise stated.  Even in the case of offences that can at the option of the prosecutor be tried either summarily or on indictment, the choice of the mode of trial carries with it certain definite procedural and legal requirements for arriving at a verdict.38

In view of the fact that the bulk of the criminal appeals before the Court of Appeal are indictable offences, it is a sine qua non that in exercising its appellate powers the court must comply strictly with section 12 (2) of the Courts Act.  That section requires that where an appellant has been convicted of an offence and the judge who tried him or the jury could on the indictment or charge have found him guilty of some other offence, and on the findings of the trial judge or the jury it appears to the Court of Appeal that the trial judge or jury must have been satisfied of facts which proved the appellant guilty of that other offence, the court may, instead of allowing the appeal, substitute for the verdict found by the trial court or the jury a verdict of guilty of that other offence, and pass such sentence in substitution for the sentence passed at the trial as may be warranted in law for that  other offence.

In trials by jury the duties of both the judge and the jury are clearly spelt out in the Criminal Procedure Code.  It is the responsibility of the jury after the summing up by the judge to consider their verdict and where the jury is unanimous in their opinion, the judge shall give judgment in accordance with their opinion when the accused is found not guilty the judge must record a judgment of acquittal while if he is found guilty the judge must pass sentence on him according to law.  The verdict is the province of the jury and the judge has no option in the matter.  In trials with assessors even though the judge ultimately decides the verdict he must consult the assessors for their opinions after the summing up.  The Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, does not provide for the procedure to be followed on a submission of no case, but it can be inferred that it is the judge's duty to decide on that submission and he can uphold the submission, without requesting for opinions from the assessors.39 In that eventuality the assessors will have to be discharged if the accused is acquitted. It follows from the foregoing that after the verdict, either on a submission of no case or at the close of the case for both sides, the Court of Appeal can only substitute a verdict which the court could have given at either stages of the trial.

The decisions of the Court of Appeal in Sarimbe alias Olala and Moses Tsinowope presuppose that the provisions of section 154 can be invoked by a trial court, even in a trial with a jury to convict for a lesser offence notwithstanding the jury's responsibility for returning the verdict on-the offence charged on the indictment or one in respect of which he could lawfully be convicted on the indictment upon which he is being tried.40 Secondly, that when a jury returns a verdict of not guilty in favour of an accused person on a submission of no case, the jury will not be functus officio but can proceed to consider a charge of assault which could not be lawfully tried on the indictment.  Should it be accepted upon the basis of section 271 of Act 30 that upon a return of a verdict of not guilty on a submission of no case the jury ceases to be seized with any indictable offence, then the only reasonable interpretation to be placed on the Court of Appeal's decision in the case of Moses Tsinowope will be that after the trial judge had put the accused to his defence, he would have to continue the trial alone without the jury.  Such a result will, it is submitted, be rather preposterous as the accused cannot be said to have had a fair trial and an adequate opportunity to prepare his defence.  He would in such a case have been tried on indictment, up to the point when his submission of no case is upheld, and summarily for assault when the jury would have become functus officio.41 In view of the fact that the jury cannot convict of just any offence outside of the indictment, a verdict of guilty of assault by a jury will clearly be ultra vires section 271.

AUTREFOI ACQUIT

The better and rational view would appear to be that if after the close of the case for the prosecution, the latter has not satisfied the requisite evidential burden to warrant calling upon the accused person to enter into his defence, either for murder or manslaughter, the judge is obliged, upon returning a verdict of not guilty by the jury, to acquit and discharge the accused person of the charge on the indictment. The judge is at liberty to advise the prosecution to consider charging the accused person with the lesser but different offence of which he has not been charged and could not be convicted of on the indictment but of which some cogent evidence may have been produced against him.  It should be pointed out that the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, section 271 does not empower the court to convict an accused person of assault on an indictment for murder. This means that where an accused person is subsequently tried and convicted on the lesser offence of which he was not originally charged, he cannot plead "autrefois acquit" at his trial.  The basis for this submission is that by the definition of murder and manslaughter under sections 47 and 51 of Act 29 respectively, before a person can be convicted of murder or manslaughter the prosecution must prove that some person has been unlawfully killed.  In the absence of establishing that fact, it is unnecessary, and in fact, impossible for a trial court to consider whether the person alleged to have been killed was killed by the accused under any of the circumstances mentioned in sections 47 and 51 of Act 29. Thus in R v Nta 42 the appellant was charged with murder and acquitted on that charge.  He was subsequently charged and convicted of the offence of committing an act intended to cause grievous bodily harm.  Counsel for the Crown submitted that on a charge of murder the accused could have been found guilty under section 58 of the Criminal Procedure Code of Nigeria for committing an act intended to cause grievous bodily harm. Since that conduct formed part of the charge of murder, and as the appellant had been acquitted on the charge of murder and had not been convicted of an act intended to cause grievous bodily harm of which he stood in jeopardy on the charge of murder, he was entitled to rely on a plea of "autrefois acquit" and that his conviction was wrong. The West African Court of Appeal held that on a charge of murder the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Ordinance of Nigeria did not enable the court to convict the accused person of an act intended to cause grievous bodily harm. As the accused was not in jeopardy on the lesser charge at the first trial, he could not plead "autrefois acquit" at the trial.43 The court also held that on a charge of murder an accused person could not have been in jeopardy of conviction for rescue, nor for serious assault. In fact, R v Nta is often referred to as an authority for the proposition that on a trial for murder a court cannot convict of serious assaults.

CLASSIFICATION OF OFFENCES

It is for good reasons that the legislature classified homicide offences under a particular PART of the Act.  Offences of causing harm are classified under a different PART and it would appear that even when a person is charged with murder and the prosecution fails to prove murder or manslaughter he cannot normally be convicted of causing harm on the same charge.  For even though the offence of murder embraces causing harm, that offence cannot under our law, be reduced to causing harm because murder or homicide by definition must involve the death of a human being.  When no death occurs no offence of homicide-murder or manslaughter would have been committed. The intention to kill is that which makes the offence of murder more serious than manslaughter and hence reduces the former to the latter when that intention is lacking.  It follows therefore that without death which is the central ingredient of criminal homicide, no criminal homicide can be reduced to another offence-not even of causing harm.44

It is an accepted principle of law that the legislature does not do things in vain and the expression of intent in section 52 of the Criminal Code, 1960, shows clearly that the legislature intended only manslaughter to be a reduced offence of murder.  Even though the provisions of section 154 of Act 30 was enacted subsequent to section 52 of Act 29 the provisions of the earlier Act being specific enactments, they cannot be said to be affected by the subsequent enactment of the general provisions in section 154 of Act 30-generalia specialities non derogant.45 The capital nature of the offence of murder has led to the curtailment of the powers of the jury to convict for other lesser offences  than that charged in the indictment for murder and this explains why section 569 (2) and (3) of the Criminal Code, Ch. 51 of Canada, and the Criminal Law Act, 1967, section 6 (2) of the UK also clearly spell out the related offences for which a jury is capable of returning a verdict on a charge of murder.

It is submitted that even though the Court of Appeal in the Tsinowope case employed the words "related" and "lesser offence" to distinguish the legislative intent in sections 271 and 154 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, the two words are not mutually exclusive and a related offence of lesser aggravation will almost invariably be a lesser offence.  It is further submitted that when a lesser offence is not related to an aggravated offence, then the lesser offence will not be a reduced offence of the aggravated one but a different minor offence in comparison with the prohibition provided by the statute for punishment.46

It is our view that the legislature did not intend to give licence to Ghanaian trial courts to convict for any lesser or included offence on charges brought before them.47 If this were so then the wording of section 154 (1) would appear to be unnecessary as section 154 (2) could easily have catered for that.  Reading section 154 (1) and (2) together, it would appear that section 154 (2) is supposed to complement section 154 (1) and to avoid any ambiguity.  It is in this respect that the observations of Mr Justice Bairamian of the Supreme Court of Nigeria in the case of R v Adokwu as to the apparent superfluity of section 179 (2) of the Criminal Code of Nigeria seems to be relevant. The learned judge in analysing the effects of section 179 came to the conclusion that:

"It may be that section 179 (2) is superfluous altogether; or it may be that there are other cases to which it might apply but which does not come to mind.  Be that as it may, I am of opinion that subsection (2) ought not, any more than subsection (1), to be applied except with . . . limitations."48 In the United States of America where the lesser offences provisions are intended to be wide in scope, the wording of sections 444 and 445 of the Code of Criminal Procedure admits of no ambiguity.49

Returning specifically to a conviction for assault on a charge of murder, it ought to be pointed out that even though the offence of rape or defilement of a female under the age of ten years or between the ages of ten and fourteen years embraces assault, it is not permissible for a trial court to convict an accused person of assault under any of the three charges "unless the assault involved an element of indecency."50 The procedure for handling offences of rape and the defilement of a female under the age of ten as provided for under section 159 of the Criminal Procedure Code enables the court to handle these felonies.  The defilement of a female between the ages of ten and fourteen years which is a misdemeanour is allowed to be handled alongside those felonies.  Nonetheless when felony has been committed the different but lesser offence of assault, which is a misdemeanour can only be invoked when there is an element of indecency.51 One may therefore ask whether in one of the most serious capital offences under the Criminal Code the legislature would have intended that the misdemeanour of assault should be a lesser offence for the purposes of a conviction. The answer, it is submitted, is no.

FAIR NOTICE

It is trite law that a person cannot ordinarily be convicted of a different offence other than that charged, because an accused person must be told with certainty of the offence he has committed to enable him prepare his defence. Thus normally, an accused person who is charged with extortion cannot be convicted of corruption and vice versa; one charged with stealing cannot be convicted of fraud by false pretences and vice versa; one charged with extortion cannot be convicted of robbery and vice versa; and a person charged with rape or defilement of female cannot be convicted of kindred offences and vice versa, etc.  These offences, however, have so much in common that multiplicity of trials on the same facts, could result.  Legislatures, therefore, sometimes make some definite exceptions to the general rule to enable some or all of these offences to be dealt with on an indictment charging one and not the other or vice versa.  Thus the Ghanaian legislature devotes sections 155 to 161 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 to defining different offences which ought to be dealt with on the same charge or indictment. It is our view that the legislative intent was to conform to the general principles of legality by giving advance warning and ensuring fairplay to enable conviction for offences which ordinarily could not be dealt with on the same indictment. For instance, rape is a first degree felony whilst defilement of female under ten years of age is a second degree felony; defilement of female between ten and fourteen years of age is a misdemeanour but they can be dealt with on an indictment charging rape or defilement of female under the age of ten.52

If the Court of Appeal's decisions in Sarimbe alias Olala and Moses Tsinowope are right, then there would seem no need for the legislature, in view of section 154 of Act 30 to enact section 159 of Act 30, as a trial court has a discretion to convict for the lesser offence even though a different one. The same thing could be said for the offence of robbery and extortion.53 That the legislature has made specific provisions for these offences means that it is aware that a reduced offence may not necessarily be a lesser offence of the aggravated one charged on the indictment.

In view of the fact that sections 154 to 161 are all dealt with by the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, under the sub-heading "conviction for offence other than charged," section 154 ought to be read in the context of that whole sub-heading.  In the context of that sub-heading, it is submitted that one cannot escape the conclusion that section 154 being a general provision within the context of specific provisions was intended by the legislature to be limited to offences that are reducible to lesser offences included in the aggravated ones charged. It is in the light of this that we think Mr Justice Bairamian's analysis of section 179 of the Criminal Code of Nigeria is correct and relevant in considering section 154 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960.  This is in accord with the cardinal principle of our Anglo-Ghanaian adversarial system of adjudication that a trial should be fair, and fairness requires sufficient notice of the offence alleged so that the accused person may be able to defend himself.

The Court of Appeal in the Moses Tsinowope case appears to have substituted a conviction of assault for the jury's verdict of manslaughter, ostensibly on the basis that the jury had power to have returned a verdict of guilty of assault on the indictment charged.  This would appear to be based on the powers of the Court of Appeal, in special cases as provided for in section 12 (2) of the Courts Act, 1971,54 to reduce the main charge to a lesser one. Having demonstrated that the application of sections 154 and 271 of Act 30 was palpably misconceived, because after an acquittal under section 271 the jury will be functus officio, it follows that there was no legal basis for the court substituting for manslaughter the conviction of assault ostensibly under section 12 (2) of Act 372.  This position, it should be noted, appears to contradict the decision of the West African Court of Appeal in R v Gyan where the court acting on the invitation of counsel for the prosecution to invoke its powers under sections 141 and 11 of the West African Court of Appeal Ordinance substituted a conviction for intentionally causing harm for one of murder.  It is nonetheless our view that the court in R v Gyan failed to consider the specific issue of the scope and effect of section 141 in its context.  If this was done the court might have come to the conclusion as it did in its considered opinion in Awuah V R— 54a that section 141 did not apply to appeals.

The facts of Sarimbe alias Olala are similar to the Moses Tsinowope case but the Court of Appeal in Sarimbe alias Olala said: "in exercise of the court's powers under section 154 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30), a verdict for assault would be substituted." It is our view that section 154 of Act 30 deals with situations where a trial court may exercise a discretion to convict an accused person of a lesser offence of an aggravated charge.55 It is not a provision applicable or a discretion exercisable by an appellate court.  An appellate court's powers to substitute a conviction, it is submitted, are based on section 12 (2) of the Courts Act, 1971, in the case of the Court of Appeal and upon sections 330 of Acts 30, and 19 of the Courts Act, 1971, in the case of the High Court.

The correct interpretation to be placed on the combined effect of section 154 of the Criminal Procedure Code and sections 12 and 19 of the Courts Act would appear to have been made in the decision of the West African Court of Appeal in Awuah v COP.  To that case the district magistrate, Kumasi, convicted the appellant of corruption by an agent contra section 419A of the Criminal Code.  On appeal to the Supreme Court the judge, invoking the provisions of sections 141 and 319 (1) (a) (ii) of the Criminal Procedure Code,56 substituted a conviction of corruption by a public officer contra section 394 of the Criminal Code of the Gold Coast.  The West African Court of Appeal held that section 141 of the Criminal Procedure Code had no application to proceedings on appeal. It is submitted that in view of the fact that the court in Awuah v COP gave a considered judgment on the application of section 141 while the decision in R v Gyan which was subsequent in time was premised on the assumption of the applicability of the same section on appeal, the decision in Awuah v COP ought to be given more weight in considering the scope and effect of section 141 of Cap 10 and therefore, of section 154 of Act 30.

It is further submitted that the only occasion where the Court of Appeal may have to consider section 154 of the Criminal Procedure Code is when there is an appeal from a decision of the circuit court or the High Court, convicting an appellant of a lesser offence on a charge of an aggravated offence. In such a case the Court of Appeal's duty is only limited to interpreting that section to see whether the conviction is appropriate as a lesser offence within the meaning of the section.  The High Court similarly has such an interpretative duty in hearing appeals from the district court or circuit court if the provision has been invoked to ground a conviction.57 The High Court, unlike the Court of Appeal, can convict of a different, lesser or equal offence than that charged at the trial of first instance.58

In Awuah v COP Jackson J of the divisional court was satisfied with the evidence given by the prosecution and its corroboration but conceded that the charge should have been brought not under section 419A but under section 394 of the Gold Coast Criminal Code since the appellant who was a member of the Asantehene's Court "B" on his view of the evidence, was not an agent at all but a public officer who had accepted a bribe in respect of the duties of his office.  This was why the learned judge relying on sections 141 and 319 (1) (a) (ii) of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cap 10) altered the finding of the court below, and convicted the appellant of the offence of corruption by a public officer contrary to section 394 of the Criminal Code.  The West African Court of Appeal while holding that section 141 of the Criminal Procedure Code had no application on appeal, refused to quash the conviction on the grounds that section 319 (1) (a) (ii) of the Criminal Procedure Code was by itself sufficient authority for altering the conviction under section 419A to one under section 394.

IN THE WORDS OF THE COURT

"the powers conferred by the section are very wide, and we can find in the Code itself no restrictive or qualifying provisions which could have the effect of narrowing them, though they should not, of course, be invoked where such a cause would cause any injustice."

In the opinion of the West African Court of Appeal the particulars of the offence under section 419A, as set out in the charge gave the appellant the fullest possible notice of the facts with which he was charged and of the real substance of the charge and even though the actual charge was laid under a wrong section, it could not possibly be said that he had no full notice of the details of the accusation against which he was to defend himself.

The provisions of section 319 of the Criminal Procedure Code (Cap 10) are similar to section 330 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 as amended.  Thus in Asamoah v R,59 the High Court sitting as an appellate court from the decision of a district magistrate, grade II, refused to quash the conviction of the appellate who had been convicted of selling a cake of new sunlight soap at 15p instead of at the controlled price of 10p fixed by the Price Control (Maximum Prices) Order, 1969 (E.I 135 of 1969) contrary to sections 1 and 2 of the Control of Prices Act, 1962, even though at the time of the offence, Act 133 had been repealed and had been replaced by the Price Control Decree, 1972.  By E.I 17 of 1972 made pursuant to the Price Control Decree, 1972, the controlled price of a cake of sunlight soap was fixed at 14p instead of 10p.  In framing the charge, Act 113 was quoted instead of NRCD 17.  In the opinion of the court, by the provision of section 330 (1) of Act 30, as amended by the Criminal Procedure (Amendment) Act, 1965,60 and by reason of the fact that the appellant's conviction had not occasioned any miscarriage of justice his conviction should be upheld.  In fact the provisions of section 330 of Act 30 as amended by Act 261 empowers the High Court when it considers that no substantial miscarriage of justice had actually occurred or that the points raised consists of a technicality or procedural error or a defect in the charge but there is evidence to support the offence alleged in the charge or any other offence of which the accused could have been convicted upon that charge, to dismiss the appeal.  In doing so the court can alter the finding of the trial court for the different offence for which the accused could have been convicted in the charge laid in the statement of offence. Notwithstanding this provision, it is submitted on the authority of Awuah v COP that even the High Court cannot substitute a lesser offence for an aggravated one unless the lesser offence can be reduced from the aggravated offence and the trial court on the facts proved could have convicted the accused of the lesser offence.  Furthermore in altering a conviction for a different offence for which the accused could have been tried, the legal requirement of fair notice and lack of surprise should have been met by the substance of the charge.61

CONCLUSION

In conclusion it must be emphasised that the principle of legality requires certainty in the criminal law so as to enable the citizenry order their lives accordingly. It would be safe to assume in principle, that in responding to law society as a whole takes into consideration the interpretation which the courts would place on statutory provisions and order its life accordingly.  Where the conduct of the courts in interpreting their powers under a criminal statute is consistent with a certain meaning or consequences arising from a breach of the statute the citizenry is entitled to rely on the consistent attitude of the courts in ordering its affairs.  We support the American position that the legal environment of a statute as a means of understanding legislation must include public response to the statute.61a Thus for over twenty-five years, in murder cases, the summing up and direction by judges to the jury have never included the possibility of returning a verdict of causing harm, assault or a similar offence.

The learned author of Criminal Procedure in Ghana in a commentary on the case of R v Noku states that "it is not clear what the position under Ghana law would be because there is no authority either way that on a charge of murder the court may convict of causing harm."  He however concludes that "it would appear that opinion favours the argument that it is not open to the court to convict of causing harm on a charge of murder."62 This conclusion was based on the consistent trial of cases of wounding leading to death.  Be that as it may, we think that the general principle and argument contained in the commentary is valid and we humbly support them.

The attitude of the Court of Appeal in Sarimbe alias Olala v R and Moses Tsinowope v R appears to give the impression that the wording of section 154 is so plain that it ought to be accorded its ordinary meaning without more. However as demonstrated in this paper section 154 read in its context and the totality of the Criminal Code and the Criminal Procedure Code cannot have an ordinary meaning which can be mechanically applied.  The conduct of the appellate courts for the past twenty-five years clearly indicates a consistency which does not support the Court of Appeal, suo motu, to take an accused person by surprise and impose on him a conviction for assault on an indictment for murder.  We have demonstrated that the Court of Appeal is not clothed with such a power under section 154.

It is our hope that the Court of Appeal will in the future recognise the sudden dislocation and deprivation of liberty which decisions such as those in Sarimbe alias Olala and Moses Tsinowope entail and consider the generality of the law on the subject-matter including making recourse to decisions of other jurisdiction in assisting it and inviting counsel to address it on the issue before coming to a decision which the subject can regard as just and fair.
