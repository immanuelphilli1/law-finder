MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: PRE-TRIAL—HIGH COURT (CIVIL PROCEDURE) RULES [1987-88] VOL. XVI RGL 70—82

Da Rocha J. B. (SAG)

 

BETWEEN the time proceedings are initiated and the time an action comes on for trial there are many important processes. These processes which may conveniently be described as pre-trial matters cover a fairly wide range of subjects. In this paper I will consider the most important of them and my emphasis will be on those areas where the court has powers and discretion which it may be called upon to exercise. The matters I will consider are (i) parties and causes of action; (ii) issue and service of writ; (iii) appearance and default of appearance; (iv) pleadings; and (v) summons for directions.

Parties and Causes of Action under Order 15 (LI 1129)

The two subjects of parties and causes of action are so closely related that it is convenient to consider them together. Every action begins because one person, the plaintiff seeks a relief or remedy against another person, the defendant. Usually there is one plaintiff suing one defendant, but in appropriate circumstances there may be more than one plaintiff or more than one defendant. Any person who is not under disability may sue or be sued in his own right or in a representative capacity. A person is under disability if he is an infant, i.e. under the age of eighteen years or if he is a patient, i.e. by reason of mental disorder or infirmity, he is not capable of administering or managing his own affairs.

Every person under disability must sue by his next friend or defend an action by his guardian ad litem and the title of the suit must disclose this fact. If an infact sues without a next friend the court as soon as it becomes aware of this fact must stay all further proceedings until a next friend is added. The defendant in such a case may apply to the court for an order that a next friend should be added or the writ set aside. Similarly if a patient sues alone without a next friend the court must stay proceedings until a next friend is added and the defendant may apply for a stay of proceedings until a next friend is added. The same considerations apply in the action as though he were of full capacity. Proceedings will be stayed until a guardian ad litem is added. It is not proper for the lawyer of a client under disability to sue or defend or continue proceedings without a next friend or guardian ad litem. Any lawyer who does so may incur personal liability for costs. (See Geilinger v. Gibbs [1897] 1 Ch. 479; Yonge v. Toynbee [1910] 1 KB 215.) Proceedings in this context cover everything done from the time the action is initiated until final judgment is satisfied or enforced.

Every application on behalf of a person under disability whether as a plaintiff or a defendant in an action at whatever stage of the action must be by his next friend or guardian ad litem. (See The Shoesmith [1938] 2 KB 638). An order is not required for the appointment of a next friend or guardian ad litem but the lawyer acting for such a person must certify that he is a fit and proper person and that he has no interest, adverse to those of the person under disability. A next friend or guardian ad litem should not have any interest in the action adverse to that of the persons under disability. A near relation or a friend of the family is usually preferred. He must also be a person of substance capable of prosecuting the action and defending it in the interest of the person under disability.

It is mandatory that a next friend or a guardian ad litem should act by a lawyer. (See Order 15, r 12 (4)). Sometimes an order of the court may be required for a next friend to be replaced in cases where a party becomes a patient after proceedings have begun.

Joinder of Parties

In a simple case one person sues claiming a relief or remedy against the other. Sometimes, however, two or more persons may join in one action as plaintiff to sue one or more defendants. Two or more persons may be joined as plaintiffs or defendants where (if separate actions were brought by or against each of them) some common question of law or fact would arise (in all the actions) and the relief or reliefs claimed are in respect of or arises out of the same transaction or series of transactions. In the case of plaintiffs, for example, several plaintiffs may be joined where they suffer injury or damage as a result of a tort. Similarly in an action for recovery of land all joint owners and all joint covenantees will join as plaintiffs. Where an action is founded on a contract made with many persons jointly they must all join in the action as co-plaintiffs.

As far as defendants are concerned, a plaintiff may sue any number of persons who are severally or jointly and severally liable to him on the same contract or tort or on separate causes of action arising out of the same subject-matter. The important thing is that the subject-matter or complaint against the several defendants must involve the same facts and arise out of the same circumstances. In actions for recovery of land all those who are actively in physical possession of the land should be made defendants. If many persons are jointly liable on a contract they must all be joined as defendants in any action to enforce the contract. If any of the contractors liable is not joined as a defendant, those who are actually sued may apply for a stay of proceedings until the person not joined has been joined.

Two or more plaintiffs may make their claim in the alternative where there is a doubt as to which of them is entitled to the relief claimed. In the same way two or more persons may be sued as defendants in the alternative where the plaintiff has reasonable doubt as to the persons from whom he is entitled to claim a remedy, eg where two defendants blame each other. Where defendants are sued in the alternative they may also be sued severally at the same time. Defendants must not however be sued jointly and in the alternative. Claims in the alternative are usual in cases involving principal and agent, and in actions for negligence. See Bonsu v. Bonsu [1971] 2 GLR 242; Aegis Shipping Co, Ltd v. Volta Line Ltd [1973] 1 GLR 438 and Omane v. Poku [1972] 1 GLR 295.

 

Misjoinder of Parties

It is a matter of great importance that an action should be properly constituted by having before the court all the persons who are proper parties or whose presence is necessary for the proper adjudication of all matters in controversy. The rules provide that misjoinder or nonjoinder should not operate to defeat a cause or matter. The court is given very wide powers at any stage of the proceedings either of its own motion or upon application to add, substitute or strike out parties. If a person who ought to be made a party has not been added the court may order him to be added. If a person has been wrongly added the court can order his name to be struck out and another person substituted. In exercising this power the court must always take care to ensure that neither party is prejudiced. The overriding consideration is whether a party will suffer injustice or prejudice which cannot be compensated for in cost. A person can be added as plaintiff only with his consent. Leave is readily given to correct a genuine mistake as to the name of a party if the court is satisfied that there is no reasonable doubts as to the identity of the person intending to sue or intended to be sued. (See Order 15, r 6, GIHOC v. Vincenta Publications [1971] 2 GLR 24; Lartey v. Bannerman [1976] 2 GLR 461; Robertson v. Akrama II [1973] 1 GLR 445 and Ghana Muslims Representative Council v. Salifu [1975] 2 GLR 246).

Representative Action

Where numerous persons have the same interest in any proceedings one or more of them may sue or be sued on behalf of the rest. It is essential that the person or persons who are to be represented and the person or persons representing them should have a common interest in the same subject-matter and the reliefs or remedies claimed must be beneficial to all of them. (See Bedford v. Ellis [1901] AC 1; Market & Co, Ltd v. Knight SS Co, Ltd [1910] 2 KB 1021 and Banahene v. Himah [1963] 1 GLR 323).

The class of persons sought to be represented must be defined in the title to the action very clearly. If any member of the class of persons sought to be represented has to be excepted for any reasons this must also be clearly indicated. No leave is required to bring a representative action but a representative plaintiff or plaintiffs may if they wish, apply for a representative order. A representative plaintiff is in control of the action until judgment. After judgment a representative plaintiff cannot deprive any member of the class represented of the benefit of the judgment. Once judgment is given all members of the class represented are bound by it and cannot take any further action.

If a representative action is properly constituted, every member of the group is bound by the judgment even if not named as a party. If, however, a party alleges fraud or collusion or that the court was deceived into believing that the group was fairly representative then he may have the judgment set aside. (See The Calgary [1901] 2 Ch 652 and Commissioner of Sewers v. Gellatly (1876) 3 ChD 10).

Third Part Proceedings

A person may also become a party to an action through what is known as third party proceedings. In such proceedings a defendant to an action who claims to be entitled to contribution or indemnity in respect of the same action against someone may bring in such person as a “third party” by serving a third party notice. When the third party is served with the notice he becomes a party to the action with the defendant serving the action being treated as a plaintiff and the third party being treated as a defendant. When the action is tried the court may give judgment to the effect that the third party may either make a specific contribution or wholly or partially indemnify the defendant in respect of any judgment given in favour of the plaintiff against the defendant. (See Order 16 (LI 1129)).

Causes of Action

Every plaintiff must have a cause of action to justify his invoking the jurisdiction of the court. A cause of action has been given the following judicial definitions: “a factual situation the existence of which entitles one person to obtain from the court a remedy against another person.” (See Letang v. Cooper [1965] 1 QB 232 at 242). “Every fact which it would be necessary for the plaintiff to prove if traversed in order to support his right to judgment of the court.” See Read v. Brown (1888) 22 QBD 128 at 131: see also Spokesman (Publishers) Ltd v. Attorney-General [1974] 1 GLR 88, CA. Simply stated a cause of action arises whenever a person’s legal rights are infringed or threatened with infringement and he seeks a remedy or relief or a declaration of his right from the court. A person cannot successfully maintain an action if he has no cause of action. Indeed, it is an abuse of the process of the court for a plaintiff to initiate proceedings when he has no cause of action.

The rules permit a plaintiff in the same writ to join more than one cause of action against the same defendant. (See Order 15, r 1 (LI 1129)). The rules as to joinder of parties and joinder of causes of action are rules of convenience. Where joinder will delay or embarrass the defendant or otherwise cause inconvenience, the court has the power at its discretion, to do any of the following: (i) order separate trials; (ii) confine the action to some of the causes of action and exclude others; (iii) order the plaintiff or plaintiffs to elect which cause of action shall be proceeded with; (iv) order which plaintiff should proceed and which should be struck out; and (v) prevent a defendant from being embarrassed or put to expense by being required to attend proceedings in which he has no interest. (See Order 15, r 5 (1)).

The Writ of Summons

Assuming that there are proper parties and there is a reasonable cause of action proceedings may be commenced by the issue of a writ. A writ must bear the title of the action. This consists of the names and addresses of the parties. If the plaintiff sues or the defendant is sued in some special capacity, this must be indicated on the writ. (See Orders 2, 3, 4 and 5 (1). Every writ of summons must have an indorsement on it of the plaintiff’s claim. The indorsement is a concise statement which should disclose the plaintiff’s cause of action and the relief or remedy sought by him. The distinction between an ordinary writ and a specially indorsed writ is of no practical importance because of the provisions of the new Order 14 (LI 1129).

A writ of summons is in force for twelve months from the date of issue inclusive. It may, however, be renewed if the defendant is not served within the time. An application for leave to renew must be made before the twelve months expire. The court may grant leave to renew the writ for six months from the date of renewal. A renewed writ must be marked with the date, month and year of such renewal. The renewal of a writ can be a matter of importance particularly in a case where the plaintiff’s claim may become statute-barred after the twelve months. In actual practice it may be more prudent where there is difficulty in serving a defendant to apply for substituted service before the twelve months expire. (See Order 8). A writ must be served on the defendant. It must be served personally unless the court otherwise orders. Order 9 makes provision for service in particular cases and Order 10 provides for substituted service. Service in Ghana is effected by bailiffs of the court, who must always furnish a certificate of service. In all situations where the rules require that there shall be service the court must make sure that such service has been effect before proceedings begin.

Appearance and Default of Appearance

When a defendant is served he must enter an appearance. By entering an appearance the defendant gives notice that he has received the writ and provides his own address if acting in person or that of his lawyer at which service may be effected. A defendant who intends to take part in the proceedings must enter an appearance. If there is default of appearance the plaintiff may apply for judgment in default of appearance. He may obtain final judgment or interlocutory judgment depending on the nature of his claim. If the plaintiff’s claim is for a liquidated amount he may ask for final judgement. If the claim is for general damages or in detinue, the plaintiff may obtain interlocutory judgment and amount of damages or the value of the goods will be assessed later. Where the action is for recovery of land and there is default of appearance then the judge may enter judgment that the person whose title is asserted in the writ shall recover possession of the land. In such a case the solicitor of the plaintiff must give a certificate that the claim is not based on a mortgage transaction. Where the action is based on a mortgage transaction the plaintiff must obtain leave of the court before entering judgment in default of appearance. In all other cases where there is default of appearance the plaintiff upon filing an affidavit of service and a statement of claim, if not filed with the writ, may proceed with the action as if the defendant has appeared.

A judgment in default of appearance cannot be entered against a person under disability. Where a person under disability fails to enter appearance the plaintiff must apply to the court for an order that some proper person should be appointed guardian ad litem to appear and defend the action. A judgment obtained in default of appearance may be set aside or varied at the discretion of the court. The party seeking to set aside the judgment must in an affidavit show that he has a defence to the action if the judgment was regularly obtained. It is not necessary to obtain leave to enter late appearance before applying to have the judgment set aside. An application to set aside must be made promptly and within a reasonable time. The court always prefers to pronounce a judgment on the merit or by consent. It therefore readily sets aside judgments obtained in default. (See Evans v. Bartlam [1937] AC 437 at 480.

Pleadings: Order 19

Pleadings are the written statements which the parties in an action serve on each other and in which each sets forth in summary the material facts on which he relies in support of his claim in defence. The term pleading includes the statement of claim, defence, counter-claim, reply and defence to counter-claim and pleading subsequent thereto and particulars of pleadings. It does not include a general indorsement on a writ. What is the purpose of pleadings? Pleadings provide a method for stating and resolving questions of fact or of mixed law and facts in dispute between the parties to the proceedings. They clearly define the issues or questions in dispute between the parties which the court is called upon to decide. Pleadings give fair and proper notice of each party’s case to his opponent so that the latter can meet it. By the pleadings the court is able to see the precise matters in issue between the parties which the court must resolve. The limits of the action are thus settled and it cannot go beyond the bounds of the pleadings except after amendment properly made. The pleadings also provide a permanent record from which the nature of the claim and defence and the issues and questions raised in the action decided may be ascertained. This makes it possible in future actions to prevent litigation by the parties and their privies upon matters already adjudicated between the parties.

In any dispute the court must focus its attention on the pleadings for the whole case must necessarily revolve around them. Each party can make his case as is disclosed by the pleadings and no departure will be allowed except after amendment. It is obvious that the quality of a party’s pleadings has a lot to do with whether he wins or loses his case. There are certain cardinal rules of pleadings, which must always be borne in mind. A failure to observe any of them can lead to serious damage to a party’s case. These rules are that:

(i) A party is bound by his pleadings. He cannot plead one set of facts and at the trial base his case on different facts. Any attempt to depart from one’s pleadings will give rise to objection. If for any reason, eg mistake, discovery of new facts, it is necessary to depart from the pleadings an amendment must be made.

(ii) A party must plead the material facts on which he relies for his claim or defence but not the evidence by which those facts can be proved. (See Order 19, r 4).

(iii) A party must specifically plead all matters which show the action or counter-claim not to be maintainable, or that the transaction is void or viodable in point of law. Every ground of defence or reply which if not raised would be likely to take the other side by surprise must be pleaded, eg fraud, statute of limitation, release payment performance, illegality. (See Order 19, r 16).

(iv) Every allegation of fact in any pleading which a party does not admit must be denied expressly or by necessary implication. A failure to deny will be deemed to be an admission except as against a person under disability.

(v)  it is not enough to make general denials. A defendant must in his defence specifically deny every allegation of facts in the plaintiff’s statement of claim which he disputes and a plaintiff must in his reply specifically deny every allegation of fact in a defendant’s counter-claim which he disputes. Only damages need not be denied because they are always deemed to be in dispute.

(vi) Denials must not be evasive but must answer the point of substance.

(vii) A party must be consistent in all his pleadings. No allegation of fact inconsistent with previous pleading will be allowed except by way of amendment. For example, a plaintiff cannot in his reply raise any new grounds of claim or make any allegation inconsistent with his statement of claim.

The court has wide powers to enforce the rules of pleadings and strike out or amend pleadings or to stay or dismiss proceedings which are scandalous, frivolous or vexatious or are likely to cause delay or disclose no cause of action or defence or otherwise constitute an abuse of the process of the court. This power arises under Order 19, rule 29 and Order 25, rule 4. The power must be exercised with great caution because its effect is to deprive a party of the right to a full trial. It will not be exercised except in a very clear case. (See Hubbuck & Sons Ltd. v. Wilkinson [1899] 1 QB 86 at 91 and Dyson v. A-G [1911] 1 KB 410 at 419). The court will, however, stay or even dismiss proceedings so as to prevent parties from being harassed and put to expense by frivolous, vexatious or hopeless litigation. (See Riches v. Director of Public Prosecution [1973] 1 WLR 1019 at 1021).

Where the only ground on which an application is made is that the pleading discloses no reasonable cause of action or defence no evidence is admissible. The court looks at the pleadings alone. (See Republic of Peru v. Peruvian Guano Co. (1887) 36 ChD 489). Affidavit evidence is, however, admissible where the application is based on other grounds, eg it is frivolous or likely to cause delay or an abuse of the process. The power will not be exercised if it entails a minute examination of the facts and merits of the case in order to see whether the plaintiff has a cause of action or the defendant, a genuine defence.

Amendment of Pleadings

A party is bound by his pleadings but the rules allow him to amend his pleadings by deletion, substitution or addition. Indeed sometimes a litigant may find it necessary to revise his whole pleadings in order to re-state or reframe his case before the action proceeds so that the real issues in dispute may be brought out. All amendments must be made which bring out the real question in controversy between the parties or which correct any defects or errors in any proceedings. However, negligence or carelessness may have been the first omission and however late the proposed amendment, the amendment should be allowed if it can be made without injustice to the other side. There is no injustice if the other side can be compensated by way of costs. (See Tildesley v Harper (1878) 10 ChD 393).

There is no kind of error or mistake which, if not fraudulent or intended to overreach the court, the court ought not to correct if it can be done without injustice to the other party. Courts do not exist for the sake of discipline, but for the sake of deciding matters in controversy. As soon as it appears that the way in which a party has framed his case will not lead to a decision of the real matter in controversy, it is as much as a matter of right on his part to have it correct, if it can be done without injustice as anything else is a matter of right. (See Cropper v. Smith (1884) 26 ChD 700 at 710-711.)

A party may without leave of the court amend any pleadings of his once at any time before pleadings close. If he does so he must serve the proposed amendment on his opponent who may apply to the court to disallow the amendment. In practice most amendments are made with leave of the court. Amendments may be made at any stage of the proceedings. The power of the court to grant or refuse leave to amend is discretionary. The discretion must be exercised so as to do what justice may require in each particular case and upon such terms as the court may direct. Application for leave made before trial are more readily granted. In such an amendment, the plaintiff may add a new cause of action and the defendant a new ground of defence. Leave to amend is not so readily granted at the trial. If the amendment is really necessary and it will not cause injustice to the other party it will be allowed subject to costs and the case being adjourned to enable the other side to plead to the amendment. (Even where the amendment is such as to change completely a plaintiff’s cause of action leave to amend may be given on proper terms.) (See Budding v. Murdoch (1875) 1 ChD 42.)

In spite of its very wide powers the court may nevertheless refuse an amendment in the following circumstances:

(i)  Where the necessity for the amendment was apparent long before trial but was not asked for. The later the stage in the trial when an amendment is sought the more reluctant the court may be to grant it unless there is very good reason for doing so.

(ii) Where the amendment is not bona fide and the court is not satisfied as to the truth and necessity for the proposed amendment, eg an amendment seeking to introduce for the first time an allegation of fraud which was not pleaded originally, or amendment in a defamation action to add a plea of justification which was not made originally and the defendant cannot satisfactorily explain why there has been delay in pleading justification.

(iii) Where an amendment seeks to withdraw an admission, unless it is clear that the admission was inadvertently made or new facts have come to light to justify the withdrawal. (See Associated Leimore Ltd. v. Associated Newspapers Ltd [1970] 2 QB 450.)

(iv) Where the proposed amendment is immaterial and or trivial or technical and will not really improve the case of the party making it. (See Sinclair v. James [1894] 3 Ch 554.)

(v) Where the amendment would change the action into one of a substantially different character which should be more conveniently dealt with in another action. (See Budding v. Murdoch (1875) 1 ChD 42.)

(vi) Where a cause of action sought to be introduced arose after the date of the writ. (See Ettelby v. Federated European Bank Ltd. [1932] 1 KB 254.)

An amendment of the writ or pleadings duly made with or without leave takes effect from the date of the original document which it amends, not from the date when the amendment is made. The action continues as if the amendment had been made from the very beginning. What stood before amendment is no longer material before the court (See Warner v. Sampson [1959] 1 QB 297.)

Leave to amend must be given only and to the extent that the proposed amendment has been properly and exactly formulated. Where the amendment is of such a nature or of such length that making written alterations in the document will render it difficult or inconvenient to read, a fresh document should be prepared. The amended writ or pleading must be indorsed with a statement that it has been amended, specifying the date on which it was amended, the name of the judge by whom the order allowing amendment was made. If the amendment is made without leave the rule under which it is made must be shown.

It is essential that when leave to amend is given the amendment should be properly indorsed and made part of the record of the court. The court will not treat any pleading as amended. Every pleading must be amended in fact. Generally an amendment will not be granted after judgment has been entered except for the purpose of  correcting accidental slips or omissions. (See Attorney-General v. Corporation of Birmingham (1880) 15 ChD 423 and Robertson v. Reindorf [1971] 2 GLR 289).

Default of Pleadings

A plaintiff may deliver his statement of claim with the writ of summons. If he does not do so then he must file it within ten days after appearance. A defendant must file his defence within fourteen days from the time limited for appearance or from the service of the statement of claim whichever is later. The time limited for filing a statement of claim or a defence may be extended by consent in writing or by order of the court. If the plaintiff defaults in filing a statement of claim the defendant may apply to the court to dismiss the action with cost for want of prosecution. If no statement of claim has been filed by the time the application is heard the action will be dismissed. Where the defendant defaults in filing a defence the plaintiff may apply to the court for judgement in default of defence— final judgment where there is liquidated demand the interlocutory judgment, where there is a claim in detinue or for general damages.

Where the action is for recovery of land the plaintiff who applies for judgment in default of defence must file a certificate if represented by counsel or an affidavit if acting in person, that the claim is not based on a mortgage transaction. If the claim is based on a mortgage transaction, leave of the court must be obtained before judgment is entered. A judgment in default of pleading cannot be entered against the state except by leave of the court to be obtained by application of which leave must be given.

Closure of Pleadings.

Before an action can proceed to trial the pleadings must close. Pleadings are deemed to be closed (i) at the expiration of seven days from the delivery of defence or reply, and (ii) where a pleading subsequent to reply is ordered after the expiration of the period limited in the order. Where pleadings are closed there is an implied joinder of issue on the pleadings last delivered. This means that material statement of facts in the pleading last delivered shall be deemed to be denied and put in issue. The rule as to implied joinder of issue does not apply to a counter-claim. If a plaintiff does not deliver a reply to a counter-claim then at the end of fourteen days from the service on the counter-claim he will be deemed to have admitted the statement of facts contained in the counter-claim.

Summons for Directions: Order 30

Not later than seven days after the pleadings are deemed to be close the plaintiff must take out a summons for directions. If he fails to do so the action may, on the application of the defendant, be struck out for non-prosecution. The purpose of the summons for directions is to enable the court to examine thoroughly the issues in an action and prepare the ground for trial. For the purpose the rules give very wide powers to the court to give directions as to the course the proceedings should take. Order 30, rule 2 gives a list of matters in respect of which the court may give directions or make orders, eg discovery and inspection of documents, and how particular facts may be proved at the trial. If the powers of the court are properly exercised on the hearing of the summons for directions the course of the proceedings can be greatly facilitated. Matters like ordering inspection of documents, making orders to plans, ensuring that the pleadings are in order—whether the matter can be disposed of by legal argument or not may be taken at the summons for directions stage.

Unfortunately, the practice now is to treat the summons for directions as a rather perfunctory exercise. Most of the time what all the judges ask is whether the issues are agreed. If both counsel say yes, he proceeds to give a date or direct that the registrar should fix a date.

Sometimes the judge may not himself have read the papers. The result of all this is that some lawyers are actually advocating the abolition of the summons for directions.

It is necessary that judges recognise and use the powers they have under Order 30 of the Rules. An important prerequisite is that the judge must himself study all the pleadings so that he can be sure first that they are in order; secondly that there are triable issues raised by the pleadings and thirdly that these issues have been properly set out. If the pleadings are defective the judge must be ready to deal with them in the proper manner, eg by using his powers under Order 19, rule 29. Sometimes it is apparent on the fact of the pleadings that the facts are not seriously in issue and that the matter can be dispossed of by legal argument. In such a case a judge should not hesitate to so order. It saves much time and expense and lightens the judge’s own burdens.
