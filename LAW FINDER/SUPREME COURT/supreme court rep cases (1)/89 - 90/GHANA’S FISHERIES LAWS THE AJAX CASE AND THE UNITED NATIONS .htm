GHANA’S FISHERIES LAWS: THE AJAX CASE AND THE UNITED NATIONS CONVENTION ON THE LAW OF THE SEA [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 289—306

Sarpong G. A.

 

*

INTRODUCTION

In December 1982, the international community under the auspices of the United Nations concluded the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (hereinafter referred to as the Convention).1 The Convention which comprises some 320 articles and nine annexures governs virtually all matters concerning the oceans. It establishes a twelve-nautical mile territorial sea, which is a zone of absolute sovereignty for coastal states subject only to the right of innocent passage; the concept of archipelagic waters whereby sovereignty over the waters within the islands are granted the archipelagic states; and the doctrine of the continental shelf as a natural prolongation of land territory into the sea domain is also recognised under the Convention. It, however, puts a limit of 350 nautical miles as its maximum permissible extent. The Convention also makes provision for a contiguous zone extending up to 24 nautical miles from the baselines for customs, fiscal, immigration and sanitary control purpose. The Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) concept extending up to a limit of 200 nautical miles from the baselines is crystallised under the Convention which seeks to accommodate coastal states’ rights to the resources of the EEZ with the rights of all other states to the enjoyment of the traditional high seas freedoms like navigation, over flight and the laying of submarine cables and pipelines.

Beyond the EEZ is the high seas which is for the use of all states subject to a reasonable user requirement. An innovation under the Convention is the attempt to give practical effect to the sea-bed resources in this area as the common heritage of mankind by the creation of the International Sea-Bed Authority whose commercial arm, the Enterprise, will, in conjunction with states and other institutions, mine sea-bed resources for the benefit of mankind as a whole. This has been a rather controversial aspect of the Convention and a stumbling block to its acceptance by certain states. This controversy, however, is not the concern of this article which focuses mainly on the relevant conventional rules governing fisheries.2 The Convention also addresses other issues of global concern. These include measures for the prevention and control of marine pollution, marine technology transfer, scientific research and a comprehensive dispute settlement mechanism.3

A record 119 states signed the Convention when it was opened for signature at Montego Bay, Jamaica. It comes into force twelve months after ratification by 60 states. As at April 1991, 47 states including Ghana had ratified the Convention.4 Ghana having ratified the Convention, is obliged, under international law, to ensure that her maritime affairs conform to the texts of the Convention. The Ajax case5 which involved the arrest, detention and eventual prosecution of certain foreign vessels and their crew at the public tribunal for illegal fishing in Ghana’s territorial waters, raises certain jurisdictional questions as regards the conformity and, or consistency of the tribunal's judgment with the relevant applicable rules of international law. The article examines the Ajax in the light of applicable conventional texts and Ghanaian legislation on the subject. At the end of the analysis, certain conclusions and, or relevant lessons are drawn.

The Ajax Story

On 1 March 1989, this Ghana Navy Ship GNS Achimota, while on patrol duties in the eastern part of Ghanaian territorial waters, sighted a Greek fishing vessel Ajax I (FVAjax I) approximately ten nautical miles south of Ada poaching.6 Under orders from GNS Achimota, FV Ajax I and its crew were arrested and escorted to the Tema Harbour at 0345 hours on 2 May 1989. On berthing, FV Ajax I’s log, passports and other documents of its crew (eighteen Ghanaians and five Greeks) were seized. A search on the vessel revealed that it had fifteen cartons of shrimp, 50 sacks of assorted fish, and another five cartons of assorted fish. A report was submitted by GNS Achimota to the Eastern Naval Command on the incident.

The matter was eventually reported to the Bureau of National Investigations (BNI) for investigation. After the necessary investigations, the (BNI) referred the matter to the office of the special prosecutor for appropriate action. In the Ajax7  the accused8 were charged with doing acts with intent to sabotage the economy of Ghana contrary to sections 9(1)(f) and 16 of the Public Tribunals Law, 1984 (PNDCL 78). The particulars of the offence were that the accused:

“between January and March, 1989 in Ghana, Togo and elsewhere, by . . . various acts caused or facilitated the illegal fishing within Ghana’s territorial waters without a licence by Ajax I, Ajax II and Ajax IV for large quantities of shrimps and other assorted fish on a number of occasions with large catches of shrimps and other assorted fish [they] discharged or had discharged at Lome, Togo for sale in Togo and elsewhere and kept the proceeds to [themselves and for the] Ajax Fishing Company Ltd, of Piraeus, Greece which acts were done with intent to sabotage the economy of Ghana.”

On 16 June 1989, the tribunal ordered the accused to pay $650,000 plus ¢4,000,000 to the state being compensation, costs and reparation for the offence of fishing in Ghanaian waters pursuant to the Public Tribunals (Amendment) Law, 1989 (PNDCL 213). The accused complied and the tribunal subsequently decreed:

“The accused are now free to leave Ghana subject to our recommendation that they be deported. Towards this end the Ministry of Interior and the Commissioner of Police (CID) should take charge of their passports and facilitate their departure.

The fishing vessel and the crew at the harbour should also he released to go. In this regard the Ghana Navy should escort them out of Ghana’s territorial waters in order to avoid them taking any advantage to fish. The fishing vessels Ajax I, II and IV are all banned from entering Ghana's territorial waters and any future breach of this order shall constitute a contempt order of this tribunal and [justify] the seizure of any such vessel and confiscation thereafter.

Concerning this, a distinction should be made between the crew and those accused, i.e. the third and the fourth accused who shall depart to Lome by road. The ¢4,000,000 be paid to the security co-ordinator as directed by the tribunal.”

Conformity with International Law

Ghana has signed and also ratified the Convention. The Convention’s texts thus constitute a norm or benchmark against which the validity or otherwise of Ghana’s municipal acts in the maritime sphere ought to be measured. To assess the conformability of the Ajax with international law the following issues may be raised: was the arrest of the vessel and its crew permissible under international law? Did the tribunal have jurisdiction to try the accused? Was the conviction and sentence passed by the tribunal in conformity with the conventional norms? The resolution of the foregoing issues calls for an analysis of the relevant provisions of the Convention on fisheries and applicable Ghanaian legislation. The analysis is conducted under two main broad headings: regulatory competence of the coastal state; arrests and punishment for violations.

Regulatory Competence of the Coastal State

Under traditional international law, the coastal states’ rights of exploitation and regulation of fisheries was limited to the territorial sea. Under the Convention, however, coastal states’ fisheries regulation rights are applicable in the EEZ as well. The authority of the coastal state to enforce those regulations in the EEZ is in nature equivalent to that in the territorial sea. The coastal state has jurisdiction over all fisheries activities whether carried on by nationals or by foreigners; and can apply its legislation governing these activities to nationals and foreigners alike.9 Indeed, under article 19 of the Convention any fishing activities by a foreign vessel in a coastal state’s territorial sea is deemed prejudicial to the peace, good order or security of the state involved, and coastal states have the power, under article 21, to enact legislation, inter alia, for the prevention of the infringement of its fisheries laws. This regulatory competence extends to all foreign vessels except warships and other vessels operated by states for non-commercial purposes.

In the EEZ, the right to determine the allowable fisheries catches is the sole preserve of the coastal state which is also required to adopt measures that would prevent over exploitation. This it may do in co-operation with relevant competent international organisations. The coastal state also has competence in determining its capacity to harvest the living resources of the EEZ. It is, however, enjoined to permit other states access to the surplus of the allowable catch when it does not have the capacity to harvest the entire allowable catch.10 Nationals of other states fishing in the EEZ are obliged to comply with the regulatory measures imposed by the coastal states. These regulatory measures are provided under article 62 of the Convention.11

It must be pointed out, however, that access of other states to the surplus of the allowable catch can be gained only through arrangements with the coastal states. The coastal state is enjoined to do so having regard to articles 69 and 70 (which grant landlocked and geographically disadvantaged states (LLGDS)) rights to participate in EEZ surplus exploitation. In granting other states access to its EEZ, the coastal state should be guided by factors like the significance of the living resources of the area to the economy of the coastal state concerned and its other national interests; the requirements of developing states in the sub-region or region and the need to minimize economic dislocation in states whose nationals have habitually fished in the zone or which have made substantial efforts in research and identification of stocks.

It is important to note that the determination of the total allowable catch is the sole competence of the coastal states. Even though the coastal state is enjoined, as noted above, to permit other states to participate in the exploitation of the surplus, the Convention is silent as to what happens in the event that a coastal state refuses other states access to her surpluses in the EEZ. Being no more than agreements to agree, the articles are inherently weak and cannot enure to the benefit of LLGDS in the event of a conflict12 Indeed, there is no doubt that competence over fisheries in a coastal state’s EEZ is its exclusive preserve.

This view is reinforced by the Convention’s provisions on dispute settlement which exempts fisheries disputes in the EEZ from compulsory jurisdiction. Under article 297(3), disputes concerning the interpretation or application of the Convention with regard to fisheries are to be settled in accordance with section 2 (on compulsory procedures entailing building decisions) except that the coastal state shall not be obliged to accept the submission to settlement of any dispute relating to its sovereign rights with respect to the living resources in the EEZ or their exercise including its discretionary process for determining the allowable catch, its harvesting capacity, the allocation of surpluses to other states and the terms and conditions established in its conservation and management laws and regulations. It is submitted that in the light of the foregoing that the Convention grants coastal states sovereignty over the living resources of its territorial seas and the EEZ. Hence any vessel that illegally fishes in these waters is subject to the criminal jurisdiction of the coastal state. The crew of the FV Ajax I were caught illegally fishing in Ghana's territorial waters. There is no doubt therefore that they were subject to the criminal jurisdiction of Ghanaian courts. The same conclusion would have been arrived at had they been caught fishing in Ghana’s EEZ. In fact at the material time, Ghana had enacted legislation aimed at giving effect to the provisions of the Convention relating to the delimitation of the territorial sea, the contiguous zone, the EEZ and the continental shelf in order that these provisions of the Convention shall have the force of law in Ghana.13 The Law which was passed pursuant to Ghana’s ratification of the Convention in 1983 sets out these maritime zones and their limits in accordance with the Convention's provisions and vests the resources therein in the State14 It remains to be considered whether or not the conviction and sentence passed by the tribunal were in conformity with the provisions of the Convention.

Arrests and Punishments for Violations

The coastal states’ jurisdiction over fisheries applies to its own nationals and foreigners alike. Any person found violating the fisheries laws and regulations may be arrested by the competent authorities of the coastal state, tried and punished by its domestic courts. However, states do not have unfettered rights and/or discretion as to the mode of the exercise of their criminal jurisdiction. Indeed, the Convention imposes limitations as to the nature and/or type of punishment that may be meted out to a vessel and/or its crew who are apprehended for violating a coastal state’s fisheries laws. Article 73, the relevant provision on the subject provides that arrested vessels and their crew shall be promptly released upon the posting of reasonable bond or other security. In addition, penalties for such violations may not include imprisonment, in the absence of agreement to the contrary by the states concerned or any other form of corporal punishment. The Convention further obliges coastal states to promptly notify the flag state, through appropriate channels, of the action taken and of any penalties subsequently imposed. In its judicial proceedings, the coastal state is obliged to impose financial penalties only and to refrain from any, and/or unnecessary detention of a vessel or its crew. This idea was introduced for fear that unreasonably prolonged detention of fishing vessels would be detrimental to the fishing industry. The Convention does not provide for the amount of financial penalties that may be imposed or deposited by the coastal state as a bond. Such matters obviously fall within the discretionary competence of the coastal state. The danger is that if the sum of money demanded is small, the captain or owner of the vessel is highly unlikely to appear before the tribunal of the coastal state. Judge Shigeru Oda suggests that the amount of the bond or other security might in practice be fixed by each coastal state at an amount equivalent to the fine that might be imposed by its courts at a later stage. The difficulty here is that there are no uniform international standards as to the fines to be imposed.15

Thus even though states appear to have absolute sovereignty over the living resources in their maritime zones, there are limitations as to the kind of punishment that they can impose for violations: they are obliged to release promptly offending vessels and their crew upon the posting of a bond and can only impose financial penalties.16

In the light of the foregoing, Ghana's handling of the Ajax is troubling in several respects. From the available facts and record on the trial, the crew’s passports were taken from them and they were prevented from leaving the country until after the trial. There is no indication that any bond or other security was demanded from the crew for their release as provided under the Convention. Indeed, there is no doubt that the same would have been furnished for their release in view of the promptness with which the fines were paid.17 There is also no indication that the flag state was promptly notified of the arrest and detention of the crew. Assuming this was the case, this would also amount to a violation of the Convention.

Penalties for violation under the Convention in the absence of agreement by the states concerned, are not to include imprisonment or any other form of corporal punishment, yet the accused were recommended for deportation by road. Equally contentious was the tribunal’s order banning the affected vessels from entering Ghana’s territorial waters, a breach of which shall constitutute a contempt of the order of the tribunal and justify the seizure of any such vessel and confiscation thereafter. This order of the tribunal, it is submitted, offends against the Convention’s provisions on innocent passage. Under the Convention, so long as a vessel does not engage in any of the list of activities provided under its article18 it has the right of innocent passage through the territorial sea of a coastal state. Even though a coastal state possesses the right under the Convention to suspend temporarily in specified areas of its territorial sea, the innocent passage of foreign vessels for security considerations,19 it has no right to impose a permanent ban on a vessel from entering its territorial sea. Such a ban is obviously a violation of the freedom of the seas which is the cornerstone of the Convention.

Thus by extending the scope of its award beyond pecuniary penalty, the tribunal's pronouncement fell foul of international law. Besides, the tribunal’s $650,000 award against the vessels and crew would appear ultra-vires judging from the ¢500,000 prescribed under the law.20 In a sense, the tribunal's award forcefully brought home the fact that the ¢500,000 fine limit under the law is unrealistic in this day and age of sophisticated technology when factory vessels of immense tonnages could be employed to exploit resources worth thousands of dollars. A more realistic approach to the subject would be to set a minimum in terms of the permissible fine and allow the tribunal to determine upward limits having regard to the circumstances of each case. In this regard, factors such as the value of the catch found on board a vessel at the time of arrest, the frequency or degree of violation by the offending vessel and/or other vessels; and the importance of the living resources to the economy of the coastal state should be taken into consideration.

The law also provides that regulations made pursuant to it may prescribe a penalty for an infringement thereof of a term of imprisonment not exceeding fifteen years and may also require the forfeiture of anything used in the commission of the offence21 The inconsistency of this provision with the Convention is obvious.22 Apart from these flaws, the law regrettably made no provision for the specific authority and/or competence to be exercised within Ghana's declared maritime zones.23 The issue was left to the PNDC to resolve by means of regulations. At the time of the Ajax no such regulations had been made. However, the law as noted, made the Convention's provisions on the territorial seas and the EEZ applicable in Ghana.24 The tribunal was thus equally obliged from the municipal standpoint, to apply the provisions of the Convention. The Ajax’s shortcomings from the international law standpoint thus applies mutatis mutandis when viewed purely from the domestic law viewpoint. Presumably the nature of the law and the absence of any regulations on the subject must have contributed to the errors on the part of the tribunal. Ghana being a party to the Convention, is obliged by its treaty obligations to enact legislation governing fisheries. The resort to PNDCL 78 as a medium for the enforcement of fisheries rights violations would thus appear to run counter to the conventional norms. It is in this regard that the recent specific legislation on fisheries is to be welcomed. The remainder of this article is thus devoted to an examination of the Fisheries Law, 1991,25 in the light of the relevant rules of international law.

The Fisheries Law, 1991

The law which was passed on 30 January 1991 and repeals the existing legislation on the subject is a fairly comprehensive piece of legislation governing fisheries in Ghana’s riverine system or “coastal waters.” The latter is defined as the area of the sea declared by an enactment as the territorial sea, EEZ or continental shelf of Ghana. The law thus, by implication, applies throughout Ghana’s declared 200 miles zone.26 This is consistent with the provisions of the Convention.27 The importance of the law must be viewed from the standpoint of the importance of fisheries in the Ghanaian economy. It is a source of employment, food, raw material for industry and a foreign exchange earner. In 198828 for example, the total fishery landings from inshore vessels (other than tuna) amounted to ¢2,030,906,000 while distant water vessels landed ¢4,411,550,000 worth of catch. Tuna catch for that year amounted to ¢8,124,378,000.

The law prohibits the building of any motor fishing vessel without licence. The design of such vessel shall be subject to approval by the Shipping Commissioner to whom the surveyor (who in every situation must be employed to supervise its construction) shall make periodic reports. On completion of its construction, a vessel must be issued with a seaworthiness certificate by the Commissioner before it could be offered for sale either within or outside Ghana.29 The law bans the importation of vessels into Ghana without prior approval. Imported vessels should not be more than five and seven years old respectively for motor and tuna vessels. Contravention of the fore-going provisions of the law attracts a fine not exceeding ¢500,000 or to a term of imprisonment not exceeding two years or both and in addition the court may order the forfeiture of the vessel in respect of which the offence was committed.30 The law also bans the operation without licence of any fishing craft within the coastal waters of Ghana for purposes of fishing or in connection with any fishing activity.31

Contravention of this provision attracts sanctions. In the case of a foreign-owned or registered vessel, the fine shall be not less than $150,000. Locally owned and/or registered vessels are liable to not less than ¢50,000 or more than ¢100,000 fines while the exigible penalties in the case of canoe fishermen is ¢50,000 and ¢100,000 minimum and maximum respectively. The court may in addition to such fines order the forfeiture of the fishing craft, catch or other apparatus or a combination of any other involued in the commission of the offence.32

The law makes provision for the manning of motor fishing vessels. It specifies the various categories of personnel and their requisite qualifications on the basis of tonnages for various fishing vessels.33 As in the preceding sections, violations of this provision attracts a penal sanction of a fine of not less than ¢100,000 or a term of imprisonment not exceeding six months or both. The law also provides for various fishing zones and prescribes the various gear and markings for vessels. Penalties are also provided for the violation of these rules.34 An innovation under the law is the establishment of the Fisheries Monitoring, Control, Surveillance and Enforcement Unit (the Unit).35 It is charged with the responsibility for the monitoring, control and surveillance of all fishing operations within the coastal waters and riverine systems of Ghana; and the enforcement of the law and regulations thereunder. It is to comprise of personnel from the Ghana Navy, Ghana Air Force and Department of Fisheries. From time to time, the services of personnel from other government agencies and departments may be solicited to assist the Unit in the performance of its functions. Personnel of the Unit are accorded powers and privileges of policemen. Their functions, inter alia, are to:

a. arrest any person or fishing craft where there is a reasonable suspicion that any act has been done by the person or in relation to the craft in contravention of any of the provisions of this law or regulations made thereunder;

b. board any fishing craft to inspect any licence or other document issued in relation to such fishing craft;

c. board any fishing craft to search and examine any fishing gear or other apparatus carried on or in such fishing craft;

d. require the owner or person in charge of a fishing craft to exhibit his fishing gear or apparatus or catch; and

e. enter any premises ashore where there is reasonable suspicion of any contravention of this law, and inspect such premises, the catch, fishing gear or other apparatus of fishing craft involved in such contravention.

Any person who obstructs and/or hinders the personnel in the performance of their functions shall be guilty of an offence and liable on summary conviction to a fine not exceeding ¢300,000 or in the case of a foreigner $10,000 or its equivalent or to a term of imprisonment not exceeding twelve months or both. Personnel of the Unit are granted immunity from suit in the performance of their duties.36 A technical Committee37 is established as a co-ordinating body. It is also to advise the head of the Unit on matters relating to the implementation of government's fisheries policies, particularly in areas of monitoring, control and surveillance of fisheries resources and the enforcement of the law. A fisheries advisory council (the Council)38 is established under the law. It is to advise the secretary on all matters affecting the fishing industry as well as matters relating to the implementation of government policies on the development, management and utilisation of Ghana’s fisheries resources.

The law gives the secretary powers by legislative instrument to make regulations on a wide range of issues affecting fisheries.39 The infringement of these regulations attract pecuniary penalties, imprisonment and/or confiscation of the means employed in the commission of the offence.40

On the whole, the law, backed by the required regulations, should provide a viable legal and/or institutional base for the conservation, management and utilisation of Ghana’s fisheries. The creation of the Unit is a welcome inroad into the enforcement system. In view of its composition, it should provide a much more coherent and co-ordinated approach towards fisheries law enforcement. There is, however, the need to equip the Unit with the appropriate means and tools for its enforcement role. This has become more urgent in view of the expansion of Ghana’s coastal waters 200 nautical miles out to the sea.41

The police powers accorded members of the Unit should enable them to extend their enforcement role into other equally important areas such as pollution control. However, a look needs to be taken at two aspects of the fisheries law - namely, punishment for violations and indemnity for personnel of the Unit.

Punishment for Violations and Indemnity for Personnel of the Unit

As observed earlier the Convention prescribes financial penalties for fishery law violations.42 For a country like Ghana that has ratified the Convention, the latter is the norm or benchmark against which the validity of Ghana’s laws may be tested. Those provisions of the law which prescribe imprisonment and/or confiscation of fishing gear for violations thus clearly offend the conventional norms. Ghana would thus have to amend these provisions and bring them in line with the provisions of the Convention.43

Under section 29 of the law, no action shall be brought against the head of the Unit, the Director of Fisheries or any person authorised to assist the Unit in respect of any act done in good faith in the performance of their functions. This provision was probably inserted to ensure the smooth performance of the functions of personnel of the Unit. The Law does not provide any guide as to what amounts to good faith. Presumably this implies the common law notion of the concept whose determination is the province of the courts. Therefore an initial plea of good faith cannot bar a course of action in the law courts. In any event, personnel of the Unit have the same powers and privileges of police officers. The provision therefore is unnecessary and ought to be expunged from the law. It must be emphasised that even though the instant piece of legislation lies within the domestic competence of Ghana, since the law covers the EEZ and the continental shelf where other international actors are at play, the validity of such provision is dependent on international law.44 Such a provision is likely to be frowned upon by the international community. Even though fisheries regulations matters are the sole competence of a coastal state like Ghana it must be done in accordance with the conventional norms. It is not in the interest of Ghana that she be branded as a violator of international law.45

CONCLUDING REMARKS

The Convention grants coastal states like Ghana sovereign rights over their fishery resources. Ghana is blessed in fisheries. There is thus the need for sound and effective legal and institutional mechanisms for the development, management and utilisation of our fisheries resources for the well-being of the people. This, however, must be done within the framework of the applicable conventional norms. Even though under the Convention determination of fisheries issues in a coastal zone is purely within the domestic competence, there is an exception in terms of penalties for violations. Here the Convention prescribes financial penalties as the norm. A review of the Ajax and the other relevant Ghanaian legislation has shown that certain Ghanaian Acts and legislative provisions conflict with international law. Pacta sunt servanda: and the Convention’s provisions are the norms and benchmark against which the validity or otherwise of such Acts may be measured.

There is thus the need for a review of Ghana's legislation with emphasis on monetary fines. In this regard, minimum levels of fines only need be prescribed; the upper limits could be left to our tribunals to determine having regard to the circumstances of each case- The establishment of the Unit is a welcome inroad into the enforcement regime under our law. There is, however, the need to provide the Unit with the appropriate tools for its enforcement role. The provision of legal rules per se are not enough; they must be backed with the appropriate enforcement mechanism. The provision of the right tools and/or equipment for the Unit would be a worthwhile investment. For, apart from helping to conserve resources, the fines imposed could be a valuable source of maintenance of the Unit.
