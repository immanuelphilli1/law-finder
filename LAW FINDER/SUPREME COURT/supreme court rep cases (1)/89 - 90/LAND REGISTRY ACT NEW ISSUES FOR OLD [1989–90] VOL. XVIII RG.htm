LAND REGISTRY ACT: NEW ISSUES FOR OLD [1989–90] VOL. XVIII RGL 307—317

Woodman Gordon R.

 

 

*

THE volume of litigation turning on the Land Registry Act, 1962 (Act 122) evidences the statute’s practical importance.  It also demonstrates the difficulty of establishing regular, full compliance with the procedures necessary for its efficient operation by both the officials administering it and private parties affected by it.  This note discusses the issues raised in two recent cases where the application of the Act was accompanied by doubts and controversy.  The joint outcome of the cases was to resolve one long standing issue, to develop the law on others, and to reveal troubling possibilities of problems in certain new issues.

The Doctrine of Constructive Registration: An Issue Resolved

In Odametey v Clocuh1 a written conveyance had been made on behalf of the Akumajay stool of Accra in favour of the plaintiff in 1970.  She had presented it for registration in the same year.  It had still not been registered by 1976, when a representative of the stool made a written conveyance of the same land in favour of VB, who secured registration of this instrument. Still in 1976, VB then made a written conveyance in favour of the second defendant, who registered that instrument also.  As a result of events on the land the plaintiff sued for a declaration of title, damages for trespass and an injunction.  The second defendant in his defence claimed title.  He asserted that the plaintiff’s title was ineffective, relying on the Land Registry Act, 1962 (Act 122), s 24(1), which, as far as is relevant here, provides that “an instrument ... shall be of no effect until it is registered.”

In reply the plaintiff relied upon the doctrine of constructive registration.  According to this, if an instrument had been duly presented for registration, and the registrar had refused or neglected without lawful ground to register it, in equity it would have been deemed to have been registered from the date on which it was presented.  The Supreme Court held by a majority that the doctrine was not part of the law, and gave judgment for the defendants.

In the principal judgment, Taylor JSC reviewed the history of the doctrine through the cases.  These have been discussed at length in earlier volumes of this Review.2  This note will not attempt to rehearse or assess fully the arguments, but merely to comment briefly on this latest (and possibly terminal) stage of the judicial debate on the issue.

Taylor JSC noted that the doctrine was first propounded in Ntem v Ankwandah3 by Apaloo CJ in a judgment concurred in by the other members of the Court of Appeal.  Taylor JSC argued first that this holding was contrary to three decisions of the Court of Appeal which should have been recognised as binding upon the court.4  Secondly, he contended that it was contrary to the words of section 24(1).  In a discourse on the judicial use of the words “void,” “ineffective” and “invalid,” Taylor JSC argued: [The simple use of these words does not really make any difference whatsoever in all cases where at the end of a trial the conveyance in dispute remained unregistered.”  Finally, Taylor JSC noted that subsequent decisions had weakened the authority supporting the doctrine.  It had been rejected by the Supreme Court in Nartey v Mechanical Lloyd Assembly Plant Ltd, reversing a decision of the Court of Appeal which had adopted the doctrine.5  This may not have been the strongest of authority, since the majority of the Supreme Court held there that the unregistered instrument had been made by a grantor who was not entitled to make the grant: thus even if it had been deemed to have been registered it would have been ineffective, and therefore a decision on the doctrine of constructive registration was not vital. However, Taylor JSC had emphatically stated in that case that an unregistered conveyance was “ineffective and invalid to confer rights and impose obligations.”6 Furthermore, in Ayitey v Mantey,7  Francois JA (as he then was), a member of the court which had decided Ntem v Ankwandah, had confessed to “a lack of confidence in the correctness of the decision.”

The Supreme Court was not unanimous on this issue.  Amua-Sekyi JSC stated that he “fully supported” the decision in Ntem v Ankwandah.  However, he considered it inapplicable in the present case because the instrument had in fact been registered before the conclusion of the trial.  The implication of this development (which will be reverted to below) was in turn undermined by the view of Amua-Sekyi JSC that the grantor had ceased to hold title to the land before 1970.  Hence he ultimately reached the same decision as Taylor JSC, although for quite different reasons.  Francois and Wuaku JJSC stated that they agreed with the judgment of Taylor JSC, the former thus impliedly repeating his earlier repudiation of the doctrine.  The judgment of Adade JSC, which followed immediately that of Taylor JSC, reads in full: “I agree only in the general conclusion that the appeal fails and ought to be dismissed.”  Adade JSC seems in these words to distance himself from the particular arguments and holdings of Taylor JSC. But the reasons for his concurrence in the decision are unstated.  Nevertheless, it is clear that the particular arguments of Taylor JSC were accepted by a majority of the Supreme Court. Ntem v Ankwandah may not be quite dead, but it seems unlikely to be restored to vigour.

A Customary Law Grant Prior to a Registrable Instrument

Another point of divergence between Taylor JSC and Amua-Sekyi JSC related to a different, but also important aspect of the Land Registry Act, 1962.  Grants of land according to the formal requirements of customary law are not made by instrument, and are therefore not registrable under the Act nor dependant on registration for their validity. It was claimed by the plaintiff in Odametey v Clocuh that she had received a customary law grant of the land in 1962; and by the defendants that VB had received a customary law grant in 1960 from a representative of the stool.  The instruments executed on behalf of the stool in favour of the respective parties each recited a prior customary law grant, but they also contained operative words of grant on behalf of the stool as owner or as “absolute and beneficial owner.”  These contradictions were reflected in the parties’ respective claims.

Taylor JSC held that both parties in their pleadings and evidence had effectively repudiated claims to titles based on prior customary law grants. Furthermore, the legal authority of the respective alleged customary law grantors to act on behalf of the stool was questionable. Consequently, the judgment of Taylor JSC was directed exclusively to the question of the effectiveness of the documentary conveyances, with the result already noted.  Amua-Sekyi JSC held that the customary law grant of 1960 to the second defendant’s vendor had been valid. Consequently, the customary law grant of 1962 to the plaintiff and the documentary grant to her of 1970, even assuming that it had been validated by constructive registration, had been ineffective, because the grantors had no longer held title at the times they were made. The view of Taylor JSC was the view of the majority.

Consideration of the course of events, including the course of the case, prompts the suggestion of certain propositions: (1) A customary law grant, if validly made by a legally competent party, transfers a good title without writing.(2) After such a grant, no grantor may validly transfer, whether by a customary law grant or by a common law documentary conveyance, any of the rights which have been transferred to the grantee under that first customary law grant. (3) If a grantor makes a common law documentary conveyance in favour of a party who has already received a grant under customary law, then (i) in so far as the instrument purports to transfer rights already passed by the customary law grant, it is ineffective, although it would seem that it may be construed as a written confirmation of that grant; but (ii) in so far as it purports to transfer any rights previously retained by the grantor, for example by granting a more extensive interest than the prior grant, it is effective. (4) If a titleholder makes a common law documentary conveyance in respect of land which has previously been the subject of an invalid customary law grant, but not of a valid grant, then the documentary conveyance is effective.  In the present case, Amua-Sekyi JSC considered that the issue fell to be decided by propositions (1) and (2).  Taylor JSC, because he held that the parties were bound to rely only on their documentary conveyances, considered it a matter of proposition (4), the primary issue being that of priority between two instruments.

Constructive Notice Through Registration: A New Potential Effect

Registration not only saves the instrument from ineffectiveness.  It confers an additional advantage, since section 25(1) of the Act provides that “registration of any instrument shall be deemed to constitute actual notice of the instrument and of the fact of registration to all persons and for all purposes...” This was employed in constructing one of the grounds of decision in Nartey v Mechanical Lloyd Assembly Plant Ltd.  In that case the plaintiff claimed title by a documentary grant from the representative of the Agbawe family of La.  The defendant-company argued that this grant was ineffective, because only the La stool was competent to grant land in the area where the land in issue was situate.  The plaintiff’s instrument had been registered.  So had many other grants of land in the vicinity made by the Agbawe family.  Taylor JSC held on the basis of section 25(1) that these registrations must be deemed to have given the La stool notice of the fact that the Agbawe family was persistently asserting the right to make grants of land in this area. This served to establish one of the elements of acquiescence which, Taylor JSC held, estopped the stool and those, like the defendant-company, who claimed through the stool.8

This suggests that the effects of section 25(1) could be far-reaching:  Its obvious and unproblematic application occurs where there is a grant of land which has already been the subject of a registered grant.  Any prospective purchaser may be expected, as part of the usual investigation of title, to conduct a search at the Land Registry.  Today the registry plots the subject-matter of every registered instrument on a map of the area. Thus it is simple in practice to discover all the registered instruments relating to a particular portion of land.  A purchaser can hardly complain of being deemed to take with notice of any such prior registered grant.  In the present case, this was the position of the defendant-company.  But to hold the La stool affected by notice implies more.  It implies that the holder of land, whether or not he is making grants, should from time to time search the register in case some unauthorised person is purporting to grant it away.  This conclusion follows from the sweeping terms of section 25(1).  It may be desirable that this practice should be adopted by land holders generally, but it is certain that it has not yet been.

There is perhaps one further question which may be worth consideration.  Is the fictitious knowledge established by section 25(1) enough to provide not only inferential knowledge that others are under a mistaken belief as to the grantor’s right to deal with the land, but also the element of a fraudulent intention to lead them into acting on the faith of that belief?9

Administration of the Act: New Issues and Problems

The evidence showed that the dispute in Nartey v Mechanical Lloyd Assembly Plant Ltd had raged in the offices of the Land Registry before it reached the courts.  The plaintiff had received a documentary conveyance, which he had registered, from OK acting on behalf of the Agbawe family of La. Three years later his grantor, together with another person also acting as a representative of the Agbawe family, executed a statutory declaration stating that the plaintiff’s instrument had “lapsed,” and that “fresh proposals [had] been noted in favour of [the defendant-company.”] They then executed a conveyance to the defendant-company (exhibit F), which was presented to the Land Registry and registered.  At around the same time they sent a letter to the Chief Lands Officer asking that exhibit B should “be lapsed” in favour of the defendant-company. The Land Registry thereupon “re-plotted” the land in the name of the defendant-company.  When the plaintiff discovered what had happened he petitioned the Chief Lands Officer for his grant to be re-plotted. This was done, and the Chief Lands Officer wrote to OK and the defendant-company saying that exhibit F was null and void.10

The Court of Appeal held in relation to these events:

“The attitude of the Lands Department in their unilateral decision in deleting the plotting of the land conveyed in the defendants’ exhibit F was unjustified. The Land Registry Act, 1962 (Act 122) indeed lays down the procedure for resolving conflicting interests.  This was never followed through.  By their default the defendants’ conveyance remains unregistered.”

The court then applied the doctrine of constructive registration to hold that exhibit F must be deemed registered as from the date of its presentation to the Land Registry.

In the Supreme Court these events received little discussion.  Taylor JSC determined the conflict between the two instruments on the basis that both had been registered, but that the plaintiff’s had been registered first.  He referred to section 26 of the Act, which provides, inter alia, that “[a]n instrument . . . shall, so far as regards any land affected thereby, take effect . . . as against other instruments affecting the same land .  . . from the date of its registration.”  Consequently, it was concluded, the plaintiff’s instrument took priority over the defendant-company’s.

Only Amua-Sekyi JSC discussed these events as possibly having a substantive effect on the decision.  He held:

“Non-registration being a defect which can be cured, its absence [sic] will not deprive a party of the protection of the courts.  In a proper case the courts can order that a document which has been registered be removed from the register and one which has been refused registration be registered.”

In so far as the first sentence reiterates the doctrine of constructive registration, it is a view which was rejected by the majority of the court, as has been noted.  The second sentence seems, with respect, to be correct as a general proposition. The holder of an unregistered instrument will be protected by the court by the enforcement of his right to register it (provided it is not an instrument which may be lawfully refused registration).11 But the argument does not show that the particular actions of the Land Registry officers in this case were lawful and effective.

It is submitted that the determination of the legal effects of these actions is a problem only because of a failure to distinguish clearly between the processes of registration and plotting.  Registration is generally mandatory upon a land registrar once an instrument has been presented for registration.12 Registration may be refused only if the instrument is “made in contravention of, or is null and void by virtue of, any enactment”; or “it contains any interlineation, blank, erasure or alteration not verified by the signatures or initials of the persons executing” it.13  Under the statutory provisions now in force it may not be refused on the ground that it appears to conflict with another, previously executed instrument, or that it appears to the registrar that the grantor was not entitled to deal with the land as he purported to do.14  On a refusal based on the ground that it contravenes or is avoided by an enactment, the parties to the instrument are entitled to a formal hearing and determination of the issue before the Chief Registrar of Lands, from whom an appeal lies to the High Court. Presumably this was the procedure to which the Court of Appeal referred.  But it would appear to have a limited scope.

The Act provides no means of cancelling a registration once it has been effected.  No doubt a court might declare that a purported registration was void, if for example, it had been procured by fraud in the registration process. But the absence of any extensive possibility of refusing registration, and of any statutory provision for cancellation, is accounted for by the absence of any possibility for registration to remedy a defect in an instrument. Registration has two effects:  It terminates the operation of section 24, which prior to registration has deprived the instrument of any effect which it would otherwise have had. It constitutes actual notice.  Neither of these consequences entails the validation of an otherwise invalid instrument. An invalid instrument may have to be registered if presented, and cannot be “de-registered.” But this is immaterial because it continues to be invalid.

“Plotting” on the other hand appears to be a purely administrative procedure introduced by the Lands Department. As an aid to searches in the Land Registry it would seem to be a valuable initiative, for which land purchasers and users must be grateful. But nothing in the Act supports the conclusion that the procedure affects the legal status of an instrument.  There is no ground for holding that legal consequences follow from the “plotting,” refusal of plotting or “re-plotting” of an instrument or the land which it affects.

While the Land Registry may not have at law the power to affect instruments by plotting, it may still have a significant possibility of affecting the priority of rights to land. This emerges from part of the judgment of Amua-Sekyi JSC in Odametey v Clocuh. It has been mentioned that the plaintiff’s instrument was presented for registration in 1970, but wrongly not registered, while the two instruments on which the second defendant's title was based, executed in 1976, were registered in that year. Amua-Sekyi JSC would have been prepared to apply the doctrine of constructive registration in favour of the plaintiff. But he found this unnecessary because, by the end of the trial, the plaintiff’s instrument was no longer in the category of those affected by the doctrine, that is, instruments presented for registration but wrongly not registered.  Apparently the plaintiff’s instrument was eventually registered during the course of the trial.  It might have been imagined that this was too late to affect the priorities. However, section 26(2) of the Act (the “period of grace” provisions) enacts that, if an instrument is presented for registration within a specified period from its execution, it is to take effect as against other instruments from the date of its execution, not from the date of its registration. Amua-Sekyi JSC held: “[P]rovided section 26(2) was complied with (and this may fairly be assumed in favour of the plaintiff) [the plaintiff’s instrument] took effect from the date of its execution, that is, in 1970.” The implication is that, if the Land Registry wrongly fails to register an instrument which is presented promptly after its execution, the Registry retains the power to register it at a much later date, with retrospective effect, so that it gains priority over other instruments which were registered years earlier.

This may provide an opportunity to remedy the wrong done to those whose registration is initially refused without justification. But such a step may be regarded as less than perfect justice by those who have taken grants during the period of delay without any knowledge or means of knowledge of the existing instrument, and who find their rights postponed to another’s, possibly years after their own grants were duly registered.

Taylor JSC commented on the problems arising under the Act when conflicting grants were made in respect of the same land, and suggested a reform:

“Consideration should be given by the Law Reform Commission to a provision by which researchers in the Land Registry are supplied information on all applications for registration already with the Lands Department on the date of their inquiry.  A time limit not exceeding say six months for registration after which applications lapse should be considered. And the registration when ultimately effected in cases where the applicant has done all that is required of him on the date of his application can be made retrospective to the date of his application, provided no application prior to his, is at the registry.”

The Land Registry has been attempting to take a more active role in the clarification and securing of titles than is allowed for by the legislation under which it operates.  It may be thought that the need for this has become even more pressing by reason of the fact that the registry’s scope of operation as contemplated by the Act has never been fully realised, the most far-reaching of the powers provided for in the Act having been withheld.15 Nevertheless, for a full-scale adjudication on titles and the consequent state guarantee of security on registration, it will be necessary to wait for the implementation of the Land Title Registration Law, 1986 (PNDCL 152).  One conclusion to be drawn from the experience of the less ambitious Land Registry Act, 1962, is that the efficient, speedy performance of official functions is essential to the success of a land registration statute, but difficult to achieve.
