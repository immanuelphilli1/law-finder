SEMINAR ON THE TREATMENT OF OFFENDERS: THE ROLE OF PAROLE IN THE ADMINISTRATION OF CRIMINAL LAW  [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 148—155

Danyagre J S

 

*

Introduction

There appears to be a consensus amongst academics, government advisory bodies and legislators that high levels of incarceration are ineffective and inhumane and that disenchantment with institutional penal programmes generates enthusiasm for community care.  Secondly, the maintenance of any prison system is as much a direct cost to society as crime itself.  The cost of imprisonment may also be measured in terms of its damaging social, psychological and interpersonal correlates. This human cost of imprisonment as it is sometimes called has been variously identified.  The most comprehensive and moving statement is contained in the 1971 American Commentary on Prisons by the American Friends Service Committee:

“We submit that the basic evils of imprisonment are that it denies autonomy, degrades dignity, impairs or destroys self-reliance, inculcates authoritarian values, minimises the likelihood of beneficial interaction with one’s peers, fractures family ties, destroys family economic stability, and prejudices the prisoner’s future prosperity in his economic and social status.”  (American Friends Service Committee 1971, p 33).

In other words, irrespective of whether imprisonment fails to reform (or even possible fosters criminality), it can easily be viewed as a destructive, divisive and damaging instrument of social policy.  Although not everyone would be prepared to subscribe to the view that all these are present all the time, there is sufficient literature including both academic commentaries (American Friends Service Committee, 1971; Cross, 1971;  Mitford, 1984;  Hawkins, 1976, Sommer, 1976; King and Morgan, 1979) to suggest that the human cost of imprisonment is far from negligible.  One can therefore understand the PNDC’s serious concern about our prison condition and the efforts which are being made to ameliorate these conditions.  It is very important that our penal policy attaches some importance to community based corrections.  This is because they offer the humanitarian advantage of eliminating the unfavourable consequences or incarceration; a reformative impact no less than imprisonment and large fiscal savings.  A certain number of our offenders may instead be retained in the community as safely, as effectively and at much less expense to the taxpayer.  Such community based approach will provide a surer foundation for development in the field, make better use of our limited resources both human and financial and could in the long run lead to a reduction of the pressure on the prison system.

Aim

The aim of this paper is to outline the duties, functions and the role of the parole system in the administration of criminal law.

I will however initially, attempt to review the working of a parole system. Ghana's penal system is a colonial relic. This paper is therefore based on the British parole model. We can however make alterations to suit our local conditions and needs. The parole system is an important and innovatory penal measure.  Any convict or prisoner with good conduct serving a determinate sentence is eligible for release after serving one third of his/her sentence or six months, which ever expires later. The basic philosophy of parole is that:

"Long periods in custody, far from fitting offenders for re-entry into society, are likely progressively to unfit them; and that some prisoners who do not of necessity have to be detained for the protection of the public are more likely to be made into decent citizens if before completing the whole of their sentence, they are released under supervision with the liability to recall if they do not behave."

(Prisons and the Prisoner, 1977, para 108).

Release, if granted, is on licence and under the supervision of a probation officer until the prisoner's earliest possible date of discharge or release. Parole is seen as a privilege and a favour, not a right.  The practical operation of it is that a man sentenced to six years' imprisonment is entitled to good conduct remission of one-third of his sentence (two years) and is able to earn parole. He could then be released after serving two years of his sentence. His next two years are spent on licence under supervision (by a probation officer) and his last two years at liberty, no longer liable to recall. For a person serving a six-month sentence, parole is irrelevant. With a nine-month sentence, the character becomes eligible after serving six months and can also be automatically released on remission.

Justification for Parole

Parole  is justified on the following grounds:

(a) Releasing convicts from prison who have maximised their training and whose continued imprisonment is neither beneficial to themselves nor to society.

(b) It is a rehabilitative or therapeutic device. The conditions of the parole licence compel the parolees to comport themselves. This eventually leads them to be useful and law-abiding citizens. Research has shown that only a minority of prisoners on parole re-offend and are recalled to prison.

(c)  It is a powerful control device in prison. Prisoners are compelled to behave well. Otherwise they would not normally be recommended for parole by the prison administration. In other words, parole ensures institutional conformity.

(d) Helps flush out prisoners from the system and consequently, the burden of overcrowding and maintenance costs are minimised.

Selection for Parole

There are three stages in parole decision-making. The first stage involves the local review committee. This is the first formal stage in the process of selection for parole. The committee is made up of (i) the Officer-in-Charge of the prison, (ii) a member of the Board of Visitors, (iii) a probation officer, and (iv) an independent member (with no connection whatsoever with the judiciary or law enforcement agencies). The task of the committee is to review each eligible inmate who wishes to be considered for release on licence, and to recommend to the Secretary of State (Secretary for the Interior in the case of Ghana) if they consider him/her to be suitable or unsuitable. The second stage concerns the parole board. The board is appointed by and is responsible to the Home Secretary. It advises the Secretary on the release on licence and recall of prisoners who have been referred to it, on the conditions of the licence and the variation and cancellation of these conditions and on other matters connected with release on licence or recall which are referred to it.

The Board must consist of:

(i)  a person who holds or has held a judicial office;

(ii)  a psychiatrist;

(iii) a person who has knowledge and experience of the supervision of after-care of discharged prisoners;

(iv) a person who has made a study of the causes of delinquency or the treatment of offenders (criminologist).

The case of any person referred to the Parole Board may be dealt with by any three or more members of the Board. The Board  is required to report annually to the Home Secretary on its functions and the Secretary presents the report to government (Parliament).

The recommendations of the local review committee are considered by the parole unit at the Home Office (Ministry of Interior in the case of Ghana). The dossiers of  those who have been favourably recommended are referred to the Parole Board, together with the dossiers of any other prisoners the Secretary of State would be prepared to release. The Secretary of State may in certain cases, where a local review committee has recommended parole, release the person on licence without reference to the Parole Board.  Criteria for Selection for Parole

Parole is not granted automatically or solely for good behaviour in prison, nor is a prisoner paroled if there appears to be a substantial risk of the commission of a further serious offence.  Parole is normally granted only if the prisoner demonstrates by his/her attitude and response in prison, that he/she wants to be a law-abiding citizen and if there appears to be a prospect, that with the help of his/her supervising officer he/she will be able to do so. Account is also taken of the prisoner's past record, circumstances of offence, social background, reports by the probation service and his/her likely circumstances on release.

Conditions of Parole Licence

A person subject to a licence must comply with the conditions specified in the licence.  The conditions of the licence are determined in consultation with the Parole Board. There are a number of standard conditions set in the licence:

(a) The parolee is required on release to report to the supervising probation officer without delay.

(b)  He must keep in touch with him as instructed, expect to receive visits from him at home.

(c)  Inform him at once of changes of address or changes or loss of jobs.

(d)  Be of good behaviour and lead an industrious life.

(e) Must not travel outside the country without the prior permission of the probation officer.

Care, however, is taken to ensure that difficult conditions which cannot be complied with are not included in the licence.

The ultimate sanction for failing to comply with the conditions of a parole licence is revocation of the licence and recall to prison.

Prediction for Parole

Prediction instruments provide a means of classifying offenders into risk groups by examining to what ettent characteristics, such as the number of previous convictions, and age, are correlated with subsequent reconvictions. It is then possible to assign to individuals or groups of individuals a predicted probability of reconviction according to the combination of their characteristics. Criminologists use prediction scores for two main purposes. The first is to identify those people most at risk of committing or continuing to commit offences, and the second is to assess the effectiveness of penal treatment. In the latter case, prediction scores, arrived at by classifying offenders according to their risk of committing offences on the basis of information known about them before they are allocated to a particular treatment, provide a base-line against which their reconvictions after treatment can be assessed. The main variables normally taken into account during the assessment of prisoners for parole are the value of any property stolen, the number of associates, escapes from prison custody, number of previous convictions, the number of previous imprisonments, interval at risk since the previous conviction, experience of probation, age when first convicted, marital status, living arrangements, employment position, and time spent in the last job held.

The Effect of Parole in the Administration of Criminal Law

There is some evidence that incarceration, certainly if it is prolonged, may well have an adverse effect upon criminal behaviour and actually promotes lawlessness. The thesis that imprisonment itself is criminogenic dates back at least to the time of John Howard, who called prisons "seats and seminaries of every idleness and vice" and of Jeremy Bentham, who suggested that "an ordinary prison is a school in which wickedness is taught by surer means or can ever be employed for the inculcation of virtue" (Bentham, 1864, p. 351). In more recent years, this view has been taken up by (Clemmer 1940, pp 299-316) who suggests that, through a process of "prisonisation" a prisoner tends to become assimilated into the culture of the penitentiary and thereby subject to influences which would deepen his criminality and progressively unfit him for re-entry into civil life. And, although the implied causal relationship between exposure to prison and increased criminality has been questioned (Hawkins, 1976, chap 3), there is some evidence that longer prison sentences are positively associated with an increased probability of reconviction (Eichman, 1966, Juman and Dickover, 1969). The most impressive comes from Florida where following the United States Supreme Court's decision in Gideon v Wainwright, as many as 1,252 convicted and imprisoned offenders had to be released because it was judged they had been improperly convicted without a counsel. This permitted a comparison to be made between the early releases and a matched group of prisoners released at the same time but at the end of their sentences. The early release - prisoners showed a significantly lower recidivism rate of 13.6 per cent. Compared with the controls' reconviction rate of 25.4 per cent. (Eichman, 1966). In addition, there is now an overwhelming research consensus that prisons, like all other penal strategies, have negligible behavioural impact. For example, early British views of the effectiveness of penal treatment talked about the "interchangeability of penal measures." (Walker, 1965, pp 257-259, 1971, pp 120-122) and consistently negative research findings (Hood and Sparks, 1970, chaps 6 and 7).

The weight of evidence suggests, then that prisons simply do not work as instruments for reforming criminal behaviour. The ineffectiveness of penal treatment has opened up a great void in penal philosophy, and in practical terms the lack of authoritative and guiding penal purpose makes the continued use of imprisonment in its present form both problematic and cause anxiety.

The worst feature of our penal system is overcrowding. The impact of overcrowding is not limited to living quarters. Rather it has a "knock-on" effect throughout the prisons and affects workshops, recreation, visiting and educational facilities. In such a situation, the fulfilment of the aim of the prisons service to help prisoners lead "a good and useful life" becomes even more remote.  The prison population is a little over ten thousand as at now. This figure does not mean that there is an alarming rate of growth of crime in this country. It is simply a case where certain offences which should not attracts a prison sentence do so. Prison statistics show that the major offence which attract sentence of imprisonment is stealing. A little over two-thirds of all prison inmates are convicted on stealing charges. This group of offenders are followed by persons convicted of "economic sabotage" offences. This, accounts for about 6.0 per cent of inmates. The third category consists of inmates convicted for "unlawful entry" an average of 4.50 per cent; then inmates convicted on "conspiracy to commit crime" 4.30 per cent. Offenders convicted of assault constitute approximately 3.50 per cent, "other offences” and "causing harm" represent about 3.0 per cent respectively. Persons convicted on robbery charges account for 2.0 per cent. Fraud 3.0 per cent. Murder, drugs and debts 1.3 per cent. These statistics tend to reflect the attitude of the courts towards certain type of offences. The statistics also show that, except for a few murderers, armed robbers and economic saboteurs, we cannot really talk of the so-called dangerous offenders in our prisons as in other countries.

Attempts to reduce the prison population have included efforts to take people out of the prisons as well as schemes to decongest the prisons. Examples of the former include the introduction of remission, government amnesty and pardon and the latter, the opening up of prison camps. These alone are not enough. The introduction of the parole system will benefit both the prisoner and the society. Under the parole system a good number of our prison inmates will be flushed out from the system, thus reducing overcrowding, maintenance and generally improving conditions in the system. It is estimated that between two and three thousand inmates can be paroled yearly, a great relief to the Prisons Service.

Justice to the offender is attempting to offer him an opportunity to change his life style while avoiding double-think and brutalisation. But equally important is justice to the community in offering it protection, freedom from exaggerated claims and value of money.
