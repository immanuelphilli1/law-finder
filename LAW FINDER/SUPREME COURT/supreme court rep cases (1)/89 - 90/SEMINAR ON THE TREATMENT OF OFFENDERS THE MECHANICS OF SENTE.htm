SEMINAR ON THE TREATMENT OF OFFENDERS: THE MECHANICS OF SENTENCING [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 139—148

Nyavor Eric

 

*

Mr Chairman, distinguished guests, whatever view we take of the laws—whether seeing them as protecting a particular class of people, that is the law makers or as what God has ordained—they are geared towards order in society and the society is made up of human beings. That being the case, there are bound to be conformists and non-conformists due to the one great talent that the maker has endowed the human being with, that is, the ability to think for himself.  Some people say there is the eleventh commandment, that is "Thou shall not be caught."  It is those who break this law who are charged before the public tribunals or what has become known as the traditional courts which are then called upon to decide whether they are guilty or not. Once found guilty, the tribunal or court has the duty to impose the appropriate punishment or sentence as prescribed by law.

To justify conclusions on sentences, it has always been the usual practice to refer to the causation of criminality like the mental and physical constitution of persons, their hereditary tendencies, physical defects, motives and character, environmental conditions, moral weakness and others like poverty and unemployment. Indeed, an Italian, Cesare Lombroso has attributed causes of criminality to hereditary tendencies and physical defects like a heavy jaw, a sloping forehead, scanty beard and left handedness, while in Ghana, it may even be attributed to evil forces, especially an old fair coloured aunt or grandmother. In respect of juvenile delinquencies, lack of proper supervision and broken homes have been identified as the major causes. We must by all means not forget the causation aspect in deciding on the appropriate sentence.

Honourable Chairman, in imposing a sentence, the tribunals and the courts have a lot of discretion except in a few cases like murder for which there is no discretion but to impose the death sentence. This discretion must be exercised judiciously. The mechanics of sentencing in Ghana have been dictated by the theories of punishment known to the law, retributive, deterrent/preventive and reformative.

Retributive

The theory of ethical (moral) retribution otherwise called retributive justice holds that wrongdoers are punished in accordance with a moral law that requires or at least permits it; there is supposed to be some necessary moral connection between wrongdoing and punishment. Retributive justice is a refinement of the primitive urge to take revenge for injury. Philosophers have classified the notion of retributive justice into two propositions, negative and positive. The negative side is that people ought not to be punished unless they have done a wrong which must mean a moral wrong. On the positive side is the theory that wrongdoers ought to be punished, either as a revenge or vengeance.

Deterrent/Preventive

Some philosophers believe the whole of morality is concerned with maximising human happiness or welfare, looking to the future and not to the past. Bentham, the father of this theory, believes that "[A]ll punishment is mischief.  All punishment in itself is evil. It ought to be admitted in so far as it promises to exclude some greater evil."  The reason for a sentence, according to this theory, is either its effect upon the person punished or by     serving as a warning to others with like minded tendencies as the convict. Thus people are not sentenced to death by shooting by firing squad for armed robbery but that armed robbery should not be committed. This is the desire to use the criminal trial by way of denunciation, a means of reinforcing society's feeling that the conduct is bad.

Reformative/Corrective

The exponents of this theory of punishment believe the whole purpose of punishment should be to reform a criminal. The advocates of this theory believe that there is the possibility of reforming a criminal since they also believe crime is the result of the social inadequacies and that the human being's character can be changed. There is the proposition also that the ultimate justification of any punishment is not that it is retributive of the particular crime, thus the punishment must fit the crime.

Mr Chairman, it is a difficult task to sit in judgment over others and it is a heavier burden to sentence others.  The difficulty arises from the fact that the sentencing authority is often faced with the problem of exercising a discretion on the basis of the best choice from the theories outlined above. Experience has shown that most officers faced with these problems have opted out for the retributive or deterrent theories. In his book, Principles of Sentencing (2nd ed), 35-37 D A Thomas states the position thus:

"The weight given to the various factors probably varies with the type of offence and with the opinion of the judge. Judges however, are not good at explaining their own motivations. Sentencing is not an exact science and great difference of approach exist between different judges and Benches."

The mechanism of sentencing therefore differs from one judge or magistrate to the other.  Apart from the above known theories, sentencing may even depend on the background of the sentencing power, his likes or dislikes.  There was a judicial officer who was reputed to have used the dice to determine the length of imprisonment to be imposed on a convict. There are other officers who have a phobia for a particular offence because of a past experience.  For example, a judicial officer who had been a victim of theft obviously

hates thieves. His sentence is, therefore affected by this and not by any of the theories of punishment outlined above. Some officers too allow their sentences to be determined by whether the person pleads guilty or not. Though this is unfortunate, it is true. These officers are the lazy ones who always want a short cut. To them, a "plea of not guilty" is a taboo and is immediately followed by a remand in prison custody for the accused to think. If he pleads guilty, he is likely to receive a more liberal sentence than if he is convicted at the end of a full trial. This practice must by all means be frowned upon.

Sentencing at the Public Tribunals

The public tribunals were set up by the Public Tribunals Law, 1982 (PNDCL 24). The latter enactment was however repealed and replaced by the Public Tribunals Law, 1984 (PNDCL 78). Permit me to digress a little on the history of the tribunals. The tribunals did not come to be established in a vacuum. They emerged through the objective demands of the revolutionary process. There was the need to involve the ordinary man, in the administration of justice, there was the need to demystify the administration of justice and the need to strip it of the artificial strappings of the orthodox judiciary. Section 16 of the Public Tribunals Law, 1984 (PNDCL 78) is the provision that deals with punishment or sentence. It provides five types of sentences, namely:

(a)  death for offences specified by the PNDC to attract the death penalty such as robbery;

(b) where on the unanimous decision of a public tribunal, it is satisfied that the offence is so grave as to demand the death penalty;

2 section 16(2) of PNDCL 78 states, any person convicted by a public tribunal shall be liable to serve a term of imprisonment of at least three years;

3 section 16(3) states that where having regard to the special circumstances relating either to the offence or the accused, the tribunal is satisfied that the imposition of the minimum sentence shall be too harsh, it shall impose a lesser term of imprisonment;

4 a public tribunal under section 16(4) may in lieu or in addition to any sentence above sentence a convict to undertake communal or manual labour; and

5 any person convicted by a tribunal for having illegally or dishonestly acquired any property is liable on conviction to forfeit it to the state.

So far as the district and community tribunals are concerned, they cannot impose the death penalty or a sentence of more than two years' imprisonment or a fine in excess of ¢25,000.

Section 17 of PNDCL 78 talks about punishment of young offenders. Subsection 3 states, no tribunal shall impose a sentence of imprisonment on a person under twelve years of age. Section 17(4) states further:

"17. (4) No Public Tribunal shall impose a sentence of imprisonment on a person who is a first offender and has attained the age of twelve years but is under the age of eighteen years unless the Tribunal is of the opinion that no other method of dealing with that person is appropriate."

The last provision on sentencing to consider is section 18 of PNDCL 78. Section 18(1) states:

"18.(1) Where a Public Tribunal imposes a fine on any person or forfeits his recognisance, the Tribunal may make an order—

(a) allowing the person time to pay the amount of the fine or the amount due under the recognisance; or

(b) directing that the amount be paid by instalments of such amounts and on such terms as may be specified in the order."

The tribunals therefore, exercise a lot of discretion in sentencing and I venture to say it has a much wider discretion than the “traditional” courts.

How this Discretion has been Exercised

I have already talked about the history and the need for the establishment of the tribunals. At the time of their establishment, it must be remembered that certain offences were being committed at a very alarming rate with remarkable impudence. They include:

(1)  white collar crimes like falsification of accounts, fraud, altering forged documents;

(2) commercial crimes like over and under-invoicing; off-shore banking, money laundering investment fraud;

(3)  capital flight offences like smuggling, illicit dealings with the country's natural resources like timber, minerals, etc;

(4)  robbery, extortion, and what has been labelled as ritual murder; and

(5)  drug trafficking.

Again this country of course is undergoing socio-economic transformation. The country therefore, needs peace to attract investors and these investors are only prepared to invest in a place which they consider has the least risk. At present, when the whole world is waging a relentless war against drug trafficking, Ghana would appear ridiculous should our sentencing policy deal leniently with such offenders.  Let us take the case of our primary natural resources.  On timber fraud cases for instance, it is to be noted that researchers have discovered African mahogany used in Europe is logged in Ghana where the rain forest is severely threatened. According to Simon Rietbergen who carried out his research in Ghana for the International Tropical Timber Organisation, 80 per cent of the rain forest already disappeared. In such a situation, our sentencing policy should be able to protect national interest by executing such punishment on those involved in the fraud so as to serve as a deterrence to others.  The public tribunals in such cases have mostly applied the theory of deterrence as a mechanism for sentencing convicts. To quote Salmond J:

"Nothing is more effective to stamp out crime than a long term of imprisonment. That may sound harsh, but we have to remember . . . the ordinary people and their likes must be protected. And in these circumstances, it does not wring my withers at all to be told how awful it is that a comparatively young man should be shut up for a long time" (Hunt (1958)) The Times, November 26.

Shakespeare's position is, desperate diseases must be fought by desperate means or not at all.  The tribunals have sometimes used the provisions of section 16(3) of PNDCL 78 to let the punishment fit the crime. Special circumstances surrounding the accused such as his background is taken into consideration in considering sentence. Thus if a businessman who has ¢1 million to his credit steals ¢5,000 from a poor civil servant, he is not likely to receive the same punishment as a poor man who steals from a rich man. The tribunals have also used the same section to impose fines and sentence convicts to do communal or manual labour.

Effect of Sentencing Policy

It does appear that both the traditional courts and the tribunals have tended to place more emphasis on custodial sentences than the other forms of punishment like probation, fines, bonds to be of good behaviour and communal or manual labour. The result is that our prisons now contain many more inmates than they were intended to, and the prisons service is now calling upon us to decongest them. For example, at the last count, the Sunyani prison had a total lock-up population of 669 inmates whereas the place was constructed for a maximum of 400 people.  It was estimated that cl.4 million would be needed for prisoners' uniform and the feeding cost of its inmates was put at el.8 million per month. In the present system however, a prisoner is expected to serve one-third of his sentence before he may be set to work, that is if a person is sentenced to 30 years' imprisonment with hard labour he must stay in confinement for ten years before he is allowed to do any work outside, thus a lot of manpower is lost.

Sometimes, the imposition of sentences of deterrence have even had negative effects. In 1987, the Central Regional Public Tribunal sentenced a convict who was reputed to be the Chairman of the Central Regional "Banker-to-Banker" Association to a term of six months' imprisonment plus a fine of c2 million or in default a further ten year's imprisonment with hard labour for operating clandestine lotto commonly called "banker-to-banker."  This was the last of such cases prosecuted not because the practice ceased but it is alleged that some operators were prepared to pay any amounts demanded to the security agencies to avoid prosecution.  When this convict was released, he rode in an open car dressed in all white waving a white handkerchief through the principal streets of Cape Coast to a party held in his honour.

Our prisons have long ceased to be places for reform.  Instead, with due apologies, they have been more of a training ground for future criminals. For example, in September 1988, the Western Regional Public Tribunal convicted, Theophilus Boaman, a  prisons officer for attempting to smuggle Indian hemp and akpeteshie to inmates of the Sekondi Central Prisons.  How can such an officer help to reform prisoners in this custody? Sentencing policy must be dynamic and respond to the socio-economic demands of our society.  We must also remember that whatever sentences we impose today, how we use our discretion may not be viewed in the same way by the future generation as society changes.  The first known recorded punishment is the one imposed on the serpent, Adam and Eve in Genesis 3 vrs: 14 to 19.  The story is well known but I am sure some of us consider that punishment today as being too harsh on at least Adam and Eve on the grounds that the temptation they faced was too much for them.  Again, consider the fate of Moses.  In spite of his obedience to Yahweh, in spite of all he went through leading recalcitrant tribe out of slavery, his punishment was "you will see Canaan but your legs shall not step there" for failure to control his temper.  How many of us today will impose the same sentence more so when the people who provoked Moses to anger entered Canaan?  I have already said our sentencing policy must reflect our efforts in social and economic development.  Society is changing and there is the need to have look at our sentencing policy to determine whether they meet the end of justice.  By and large, most of our convicts are those hit by the antithesis within the society, that is to say, the product of an unequal, corrupt and careless socio-economic and political system; the victims of the unjust order.  And it is this group of convicts who need reformation.  And in reforming them, we must not make them paupers and thus create more social problems.  The devil, they say, finds work for idle hands.

In sentencing therefore, we must make sure that we do not expose them unnecessarily to the hardened criminal in our prisons. Instead, a way ought to be found to train them to become useful citizens in the future.  This goes for relatively minor offences and it is for such offences that increasing use must be made of section 16(4) of PNDCL 78 to make convicts subject to manual or communal labour. In the process, the convict himself learns a profession while he pays his debt to society by contributing towards its development.

Suggested Reforms

In his address to the conference of Chairmen and Legal Officers of the Board of Public Tribunals on 10 July 1987, the Hon Mr G E K Aikins, Attorney-General and Secretary for Justice notes:

“It seems to me fines, payment of compensation and liability to police supervision are fair and proper.  In the Commonwealth and other countries, a new type of punishment has been evolved and it is working very satisfactorily for the benefit of both the offender and society.  It is the community service order.  After conviction, the offender is not sentenced to imprisonment or fined, instead he is ordered to render service at a specified place for specific  periods.”

This is a point worth considering.  As stated earlier, the tribunals already have power to give such an order and increasing use must be made of it. There are yet other ways, laid down in the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30) such as probation. More use will have to be made of probation officers within the Department of Social Welfare. It is not every convict who must be sent to jail or sentenced to a fine. In some cases, a period of probation, may be enough.  If a youngman of about 20 years pleads guilty to a charge of possessing Indian hemp and his explanation is that he boils the hemp and drinks it every morning as a cure for his asthma and since he started he has seen remarkable improvement in his condition, what sentence do we give him in our present-day  circumstances?

Finally, attention must be drawn to the provision of the Public Tribunals (Amendment) Law, 1989 (PNDCL 213) which makes provision for the offer of compensation or restitution of property. This may be said to be similar to the American system of plea bargaining.

Section 1(b) states:

“ . . . where a person is charged with any offence the commission of which caused economic injury, loss, harm or damage to the State or any agency of the State, the Public Prosecutor may ascertain from the accused whether he admits the offence and is willing to offer full compensation or make restitution for the injury, loss,—harm or damage caused.”

And subsection 3 thereof states:

“ . . . the Tribunal, if satisfied that the accused is able and willing to pay compensation or make restitution, may in lieu of passing sentence on him make an order for compensation or restitution as the case may be.”

Every society is judged by the way it treats its non-conformists. In so doing, we must remember for every crime, there is a past and for every criminal, a future.
