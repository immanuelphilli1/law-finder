RULES OF PRACTICE OR PROCEDURE [1989-90] VOL XVII RGL 261—272

 BIMPONG-BUTA S. Y., EDITOR*

 

1. INTRODUCTION

It is provided by the Supreme Court Rules, 1970 (CI 13), r 66(1) that:

“66 (1). Where a party to an action before the Court fails to comply with any provisions of these Rules or with the terms of any orders or directions given or with any rule of practice or procedure directed or determined by the Court, the failure to so comply shall be a bar to the further prosecution of the action unless the Court considers that the non-compliance was not wilful and that in the interests of justice the non-compliance should be waived.”

It is plain that rule 66(1) of CI 13, vests the Supreme Court with jurisdiction to waive non-compliance with any provisions of the Rules or the terms of any orders or directions given by the court or any rule of practice or procedure where:

(a) the applicant is a party to an action before the court and

(b) the court considers that the failure to comply with the Rules or rule of practice or procedure was not wilful and that in the interests of justice the failure to comply should be waived.

The vexed question is: what is the scope of the jurisdiction or power given to the Supreme Court by rule 66(1) - especially in relation to rule 13(1) and (2) which also provides:

“13. (1) The appellant shall, within three weeks of being notified that the record is ready, file with the Registrar a statement of his case based on the grounds of appeal as set out in the notice of appeal.

(2) Where the appellant does not file the statement of his case in accordance with the provisions of the preceding sub-rule the appeal shall be deemed to have been struck out and the Registrar shall so inform the parties.”

It is clear that under rule 13(1), where an appellant fails to file a statement of his case within three weeks of his notification that the record of appeal was ready, the appeal shall be deemed struck out under rule 13(2). Does rule 66(1) empower the Supreme Court to waive non-compliance with rule 13(1) and thereby restore on the list for hearing, an appeal which had been deemed struck out under rule 13(2)? That is the question which came before the Supreme Court in Darke XII v Dei XI.1

2. THE FACTS IN DARKE XII V DEI XI

The plaintiffs in this case successfully sued in the High Court, Accra for a declaration that a judgment obtained by the defendants in the High Court, Ho was a nullity and therefore a subsequent judgment of the Court of Appeal founded on it was equally null and void.  An appeal by the defendants from the High Court decision was dismissed by the Court of Appeal.  The defendants again appealed to the Supreme Court from that decision and fulfilled all the conditions of appeal.  Subsequently they were informed by the Registrar of the Supreme Court that the record of appeal was ready.  However they failed to file the statement of their case within three weeks of being notified that the record of appeal was ready as mandatorily required by rule 13(1).  Consequently, the registrar informed the parties that the appeal had been struck out in accordance with rule 13(2).  The defendants therefore brought an application under rule 66(1) for an order, inter alia, to “relist the appeal.”  In other words they sought an order that their non-compliance with rule 13(1), should be waived under rule 66(1) and the appeal be heard on the merits.

3. THE ISSUES

Two issues arose for determination by the Supreme Court.  First, whether the defendant-applicants had given good cause for not complying with the rule 13(1)?  In other words, whether the non-compliance was not wilful and that in the interests of justice it should be waived. Secondly, whether as contended (on a preliminary objection by the plaintiffs) the Supreme Court had no jurisdiction under rule 66(1) to extend the time for filing the statement of claim and thus restore an appeal struck out under rule 13(1).  In support of the second issue, it was argued by the plaintiff-respondents that the words “a party to an action” in rule 66 meant that the rule was applicable only to matters brought before the court in the exercise of its original jurisdiction2, that the words “a party to an action” in rule 66(1) were distinct from “a party to an appeal” as used in some other parts of CI 13 and therefore “appeals” did not fall within the definition of “actions” in rule 66 and therefore were not affected by rule 66(1).

4. DETERMINATION OF ISSUES

In resolving the first issue, ie the merits of the application, the Supreme Court by a majority decision,3 dismissed the defendants’ application for waiver for non-compliance with rule 13(1) because the applicants failed to prove that their non-compliance with rule 13(1) was not wilful and that in the interests of justice, it should be waived.

It must be stressed, however, that even though the Supreme Court by a majority of four to one dismissed the application on its merits, it nevertheless by a majority of three to two4 affirmatively held that the Supreme Court had jurisdiction under rule 66 of CI 13, to waive non-compliance with rule 13(1) and enlarge time within which to file a statement of case and thereby restore for hearing an appeal deemed struck out for non-compliance with rule 13(1).

In support of that decision, Adade JSC observed that the issue as to the power of the Supreme Court under rule 66 to enlarge time under rule 13(1) had previously been argued and affirmatively decided in a number of cases including the Yendi Skin Cases.5 His lordship in further support, referred to the following definition of “Action” in the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 19546: “Action” means “a civil proceeding commenced by writ or in such other manner as may be prescribed by rules of court, but does not include criminal proceedings . . .” His lordship thus held that since the matter before the court, ie the appeal by the defendants, was a civil proceeding commenced in the High Court by a writ, it was an “action.” As Adade JSC put it:

“I do not believe that it has ceased to be an action simply because it is on appeal.  After all it is passing through the court system.  Until it has exhausted its life within the system, it continues to be an action.”

Similarly his lordship Amua-Sekyi JSC (despite dismissing the application on its merits) ruled in favour of rule 66 vesting the Supreme Court with power to extend time for filing statement of case under rule 13(1) when he said: “. . . in all those cases, where a notice of appeal has been duly filed the court has power under rule 66 to waive non-compliance with the rules governing appeals.”  And even though his lordship Ofori-Boateng JA also dismissed the application on its merits, he held that the Supreme Court had power under rule 66 to extend time under rule 13(1).  His lordship referred to the various legal definitions of “action.”7  He thus concluded that “action” within the meaning of rule 66 connoted both original and appellate proceedings.  In the words of his lordship Ofori-Boateng JA: “The only reasonable way of interpreting “action” [in rule 66] is to give it its commonsense meaning, ie its connotations as an original and appellate proceedings simultaneously.”  His lordship further held that the use of the words “these Rules” and “any” in rule 66 meant that the rule was applicable to any provision in CI 13.  Ofori-Boateng JA summed up the majority’s interpretation of rule 66 when he said:

“I reject the submission of counsel for the respondents on the interpretation of ‘action’ under rule 66 which he suggests ousts the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court exercised in favour of an applicant to give him an extension under rule 13(1).”

This conclusion was a clear rejection of the minority view (per Osei-Hwere and Edward Wiredu JJSC) on the question as to the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court under rule 66 to extend time under rule 13(1)—and more specifically, the meaning of the words “a party to an action before the Court” in rule 66 as including “a party to an appeal.”

5. CONTRARY DECISION IN In RE OPPONG(Decd)

It is interesting to observe that soon after the majority in Darke had handed down its interpretation of rule 66 on 8 July 1991, ie on the same day, the Supreme Court (differently constituted) in In re Oppong (Decd)8 was again called upon to deal with the same legal issue founded on the same basic facts as in Darke. Thus in In re Oppong (Decd), an application for grant of probate was dismissed by the High Court, Kumasi. An appeal from that decision to the Court of Appeal was equally dismissed. The applicants were granted special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court.  After fulfilling all the conditions of appeal, they were informed by the registrar that the appeal record was ready.  However like the applicants in Darke they failed to file their statement of case within three weeks of their notification that the appeal record was ready as mandatorily required by rule 13(1) of the Rules.  They were accordingly informed by the registrar that the appeal had been struck out under rule 13(2).  The applicants therefore filed the instant application before the Supreme Court in the exercise of its discretion under rule 66 for an extension of time to file their statement of case under rule 13(1).

On the issue of jurisdiction, the Supreme Court by a three to two majority decision,9 held (contrary to the earlier decision in Darke) that it had no jurisdiction under rule 66 to extend time within which the plaintiff-appellants might file a statement of their case under rule 13(1).Wuaku JSC who delivered the leading judgment of the majority said:

“In my judgment rule 66 is meant to apply to action taken to invoke the original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court and is not meant to confer jurisdiction on the court to restore to the list an appeal struck out under rule 13(2).”

In his very short concurring opinion, Osei-Hwere JSC also said:

“I agree with the full opinion expressed by my brother Wuaku JSC and, like him, I hold the view that the motion ought to be dismissed as this court has no power to extend time.” In the same vein, Edward Wiredu JSC in support of the majority on the issue of jurisdiction also said: “I would adopt the opinion I have already expressed in Darke XII v Dei XI this morning and dismiss the application for want of jurisdiction.”

6. CRITICISM OF THE DECISION IN In RE OPPONG (Decd)

The contrary majority decision of the Supreme Court in In re Oppong (Decd) needs to be closely examined.  First the court did not even consider the issue of whether or not to depart from the previous majority decision in Darke which was binding upon it under article 116(3) of the Constitution, 1979.  Although his lordship Wuaku JSC in his opinion rightly took the view that in a subsequent case the Supreme Court would be entitled to depart from a previous decision “when it is very clear that the previous view or decision was given per incuriam,” no attempt whatsoever was made to show that the previous decision of the Supreme Court in Darke given earlier on the same day, was given per incuriam.  The omission is understandably excusable because it appears the attention of his lordship (who was not on the Darke panel but who delivered the leading majority opinion in In re Oppong (Decd)) was not drawn to the fact that the issue of jurisdiction founded on rule 66 had been affirmatively decided by the majority in the Darke case.  That explains why his lordship in delivering the leading judgment of the majority said:

“In my judgment, it seems to me that there had never been any previous occasion when the issue was seriously taken and thoroughly argued and a considered and well-reasoned decision given thereon.”

However, their lordships Osei-Hwere and Edward Wiredu JJSC (who joined Wuaku JSC in giving the contrary majority decision in In re Oppong (Decd)) had dissented from the majority decision in Darke that the Supreme Court had jurisdiction under rule 66 to extend time for filing statement of case under rule 13(1).  That notwithstanding, their lordships also made no attempt to reject the previous binding decision in Darke as wrongly decided.  The inexorable conclusion is that the majority decision in In re Oppong (Decd) was given per incuriam for failing to consider the binding decision in Darke.

Secondly, it was conceded on all sides that the most important words or phrase in rule 66 were and still are: “a party to an action” who must have failed to comply with “any provisions of these Rules.”  If as decided by Darke “a party to an action” includes by definition an appellant before the Supreme Court, then by the use of the words “any provisions of these Rules” the Supreme Court would be entitled under rule 66 to waive non-compliance with rule 13(1).  However, if as decided by In re Oppong (Decd) the words “a party to an action” does not include an appellant but is referable only to a person who has commenced an action by invoking the original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court under Part IV of the Rules, then the Supreme Court cannot exercise its jurisdiction under rule 66 to extend time under rule 13(1).  That is the crux of the matter.

7. PROFFERED INTERPRETATION OF RULE 66(1)

It is respectfully submitted that since the CI 13 itself did not define what is meant by “action” in the context of the Rules, the better approach is to construe the word “action” in its known legal meaning as connoting both an original and appellate proceeding as rightly pointed out by Ofori-Boateng JA in Darke.  Since the word “action” has acquired a definite legal meaning as including both an original and appellate proceeding, it appears the majority in In re Oppong (Decd) were (with the utmost respect) in error in giving it a restricted meaning as involving only an original action commenced by a writ.

In any case there is one very important factor which admittedly was not brought to the attention of their lordships but which nevertheless clearly negates the interpretation placed on the words “a party to an action” by the majority in In re Oppong (Decd).  The interpretative clause in CI 13, ie rule 70, defines a “party” as including “any party to an appeal or other proceeding and his counsel.”  It is therefore clearly illogical for the legislature, ie the Rules of Court Committee, to define the word “party” in rule 66 as including an appellant, ie "a party to an appeal" and for the majority in In re Oppong (Decd) to hold that the Rules of Court Committee did not intend that the phrase "a party to an action" should include an appellant.

It is therefore suggested that since rule 70 of CI 13 defines “party” as including "any party to an appeal or other proceeding and his counsel” his lordship Osei-Hwere JSC was (with the greatest respect) clearly in error when in his dissenting opinion in Darke he said: “The Supreme Court Rules themselves seem to recognise that ‘a party to an action’ is not the same as ‘a party to an appeal’.”  Furthermore Osei-Hwere JSC in Darke contrasted the words “a party to an appeal” in rule 63(1) with the words “a party to an action” in rule 66.  The said rule 63(1) provides for the calling of new evidence by “a party to an appeal before the Court.”  On the basis of this contrast, his lordship Osei-Hwere JSC said:

"If it was the intendment of rule 66 to cover `a party to an appeal' there was no reason why the word `party' should not have been employed simpliciter but that it should be qualified by the words`to an action'."

It is also suggested that his lordship’s construction of the words “a party to an appeal” in rule 63(1) as not including “a party to an action” is hardly defensible because rule 63(1) itself provides that “a party to an appeal before the Court” would not be entitled to adduce new evidence in support of his original action,10 unless the court allows such evidence in the interests of justice.  Further rule 63(2) also provides that the adduction of such new evidence would not be allowed by the court unless such evidence was unavailable to the party at the hearing of the original action.11 The reference to the words “original action” in rule 63(1) and (2) amply supports the conclusion that (contrary to the opinion of Osei-Hwere JSC, in Darke) “a party to an appeal” may be construed as including “a party to an action.”

It must also be pointed out that CI 13 provides not only for the hearing by the Supreme Court of civil and criminal appeals (Part II and Part III respectively) but also the hearing of an action (invoking its original jurisdiction—Part IV) and petitions (challenging the election of a President—Part VI).  It is in this light that rule 72 of CI 13 (the provision as to the date of commencement of the Rules) draws a clear distinction between the right of any person “to bring an action” on the one hand, and the right “to appeal” on the other.  The provision in rule 72 does not, with respect, necessarily support the view that “a party to an action” in rule 66 excludes “a party to an appeal.”

In any case, an inference could legitimately be drawn from the 1985 Supreme Court decision in Darke IX v Darke IV12 which supports the construction of the phrase “a party to an action” in rule 66 as including an appellant, ie “a party to an appeal.”  In this case the Supreme Court by a four to one majority decision,13 allowed the appeal from a decision of the Court of Appeal – granting leave to the appellants to apply for a review under section 3(2)(a) of the Courts (Amendment) Decree, 197214 on the grounds that the application (which was late by 48 hours) was statute-barred under rule 7(1) of the Supreme Court Rules, 1970.  It was therefore held that the Court of Appeal had no jurisdiction to grant the application which ought to have been filed within fourteen days from the date of the decision sought to be reviewed.

More importantly, the Supreme Court held that non-compliance with rule 7(1) could not be waived in the exercise of its jurisdiction under rule 66 because the court’s discretionary power under the rule was to be exercised only when the appeal itself could be said to be properly pending before the court.  It was thus held that where the appeal had been filed out of time - by only 48 hours - that constituted a fundamental defect - affecting the very foundation of the appeal.

Consequently, there was, notionally, no appeal before the court for which the court might exercise its jurisdiction under rule 66.  It follows logically that if (as in the subsequent 1991 decisions in Darke and In re Oppong (Decd)) the appeal in the 1985 Darke decision had been filed within time, the Supreme Court might have exercised its discretionary jurisdiction under rule 66 to waive non-compliance with the rules.  This is the clearest binding authority yet that the words “a party to an action before the court” in rule 66 includes an appellant, ie “a party to an appeal.”

8. CONCLUSION

We may conclude that in the context of the Rules, the interpretation placed on rule 66 by the majority in the 1991 Darke decision appears to be the better view.  Besides, In re Oppong (Decd) suffers from the disability of being a per incuriam decision for failing to consider the earlier 1985 and 1991 binding decisions in Darke.  It is also very interesting to note that even though the issue of the Supreme Court’s jurisdiction under rule 66 was in Darke affirmed by a majority decision in favour of the applicants, the application for extension was nevertheless by a majority decision dismissed on the merits. However, in In re Oppong (Decd), the issue of the court’s jurisdiction under rule 66 was by majority decision disaffirmed against the applicants but the application for extension of time was also by a majority decision granted.  In any case in the subsequent 1992 decision in Botchway v Appiah,15 the Supreme Court unanimously dismissed, on the merits, an application for an extension of time within which to file a statement of case as required by rule 13 of CI 13.  Unlike the earlier conflicting decisions in Darke and In re Oppong, the court in Botchway v Appiah did not specifically refer to the issue of whether or not it has jurisdiction under rule 66 of CI 13 to grant the application.  The court simply assumed jurisdiction.  It considered and dismissed it on its merits - holding unanimously per Adade JSC that16:

“Rule 13 of CI 13 requires the parties to file their statements within specified times, and this rule must be obeyed.  If an extension of time should be sought, it must be for sound and convincing reasons, sufficient to induce the court to sympathise with the applicant and to exercise its discretion in his favour. It is not enough merely to say ‘I have delayed. I want an extension of time.’  Parties, especially appellants, must show by conduct and attitude that they are interested in the appeals they have filed . . .”

It must be said that the conflicting decisions by the Supreme Court on the same legal issue, ie the true interpretation to be placed on rule 66 leaves litigants and their legal advisers bewildered. It is suggested that this is one area of the law of procedure which ex abundanti cautela calls for immediate intervention by the Rules of Court Committee.  Rule 66(1) —a most welcome rule which empowers the Supreme Court to waive non-compliance “with any provisions of these Rules”—should be amended so as to reflect the true legislative intention, ie that the Supreme Court has jurisdiction under rule 66 to enlarge time under rule 13(1) or any other relevant rule in respect of any original action or appeal pending before the court.  Until then litigants and their legal advisers shall remain bemused and rightly question the efficacy of some decisions of the Supreme Court.*
