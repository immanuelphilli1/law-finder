SUPERVISORY JURISDICTION OF THE SUPREME COURT [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 381—385

By Bimpong-ButaS Y Bimpong-Buta S. Y

 

  

 S. Y.

I—INTRODUCTION

The law is well established that the High Court is vested with jurisdiction - statutory and at common law - to issue prerogative orders: habeas corpus, certiorari, mandamus, quo warranto, prohibition, etc in the exercise of its supervisory jurisdiction over inferior courts or tribunals.1 The Supreme Court is vested with similar supervisory powers over all other courts in Ghana.2 It can issue such prerogative or supervisory orders directed at not only inferior courts but even superior courts such as the High Courts and the Court of Appeal.3  Thus the Constitution, 1979, art 119 provides:

"119.  The Supreme Court shall have supervisory jurisdiction over all other Courts in Ghana and over any adjudicating authority; and in the exercise of its supervisory jurisdiction shall have power to issue such directions, orders or writs including writs or orders in the nature of habeas corpus, certiorari, mandamus, prohibition, and quo warranto as it may consider appropriate for the purposes of enforcing or securing the enforcement of its supervisory powers."

II—AMBIT OF THE SUPERVISORY POWER

What is the ambit of the supervisory jurisdiction of the Supreme Court?  In Republic v High Court Registrar, Kumasi; Ex parte Yiadom,4 the Supreme Court held that its supervisory jurisdiction under article 119 of the Constitution, 1979, consisted of two parts:  Firstly, it has the supervisory powers generally over all courts in Ghana.  In exercising that jurisdiction, each case is to be considered on its merits and due regard being paid to all the relevant circumstances.  Secondly, it has general supervisory powers extending to and including granting of prerogative writs or orders.  It was held that the scope of these prerogative writs or orders was governed by precedents and pronouncements by the courts as to when these writs could properly be issued.  Thus the court re-echoed the well-established principle of law that the writ of certiorari would be issued to quash not only jurisdictional errors but also to correct errors of law apparent on the face of the record.  Where the error of law complained of is latent and not patent, the proper procedure is to appeal.  The court also held that its supervisory jurisdiction did not extend to giving directives by way of advice to the applicant, a queenmother, to enable her enforce her rights against the respondent for allegedly posing and exercising the functions of a chief when not previously nominated and enstooled as such in accordance with customary law.

An important issue which often crops up in an application for prerogative orders such as certiorari is this: Can a party apply for an order of certiorari when he also has the right to statutory appeal?  Can he first apply for an order of certiorari without first resorting to the statutory right of appeal?  In its recent decision in Republic v High Court, Accra; Ex parte Quarcoo5 the Supreme Court endorsed the application in the Supreme Court of Order 59, r 3 of the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954 (LN 140A).  That rule enjoins the High Court not to grant leave to apply for an order of certiorari before the expiration of the time limited for an appeal or the determination of the appeal.  This is to ensure (as the court put it) that "conflicting decisions are avoided and finality of judgments assured." The inference to be drawn from this decision is that a person is not in law precluded from applying to the Supreme Court for a prerogative order merely because he has the statutory right of appeal from the decision complained of.

III—PROCEDURE FOR SUPERVISORY JURISDICTION

The question is: what is the proper procedure for an application for a supervisory order by the Supreme Court?  Can a person bring an application for a supervisory order when a suit for a similar relief is pending before the lower courts?  The answer to these questions were given by the Supreme Court in its November 1991 decisions in Ex parte Quarcoo (supra) and Republic v High Court, Accra; Ex parte Puplampu I.5  In Ex parte Quarcoo, the Supreme Court found that apart from the applicant's pending application for certiorari - challenging the decision of the High Court as being null and void, the applicant was simultaneously pursuing a substantive suit in the High Court seeking the same relief.  The Supreme Court in its ruling ordered the applicant to discontinue one of the pending actions - failing which it would proceed to dismiss the application for certiorari pending before it.  In the words of the Supreme Court per Amua-Sekyi JSC:  "In this court, we have time and again refused to entertain applications for orders of certiorari whenever it has come to our notice that the applicant is also seeking relief in another court." In so ordering, the Supreme Court reiterated paragraph6 of its Practice Direction issued on 15 June 1981 which reads 7:

"It is also to be noted that where a cause or matter can be determined by a superior court, other than the Supreme Court, the jurisdiction of the lower court shall first be invoked. The Supreme Court may dismiss any such cause or matter, with punitive costs to be paid personally by counsel or by the party responsible for bringing such cause or matter to the Supreme Court in the first instance."

In Ex parte Puplampu I the applicant sought in the Supreme Court for an order of certiorari to quash the decision of Lutterodt J (as she then was) - refusing to quash by certiorari the decision of the National House of Chiefs in respect of the Ada chieftaincy dispute.  The application was dismissed by the Supreme Court on the grounds that the trial judge had acted within her jurisdiction.

The Supreme Court took the opportunity to lay down the proper procedure to be adopted in an application for its supervisory orders. It observed that the Constitution, 1979, art 119 had conferred on it supervisory jurisdiction without indicating the procedure for the exercise of that jurisdiction.  It also observed that the Supreme Court Rules, 1970 (CI 13)—regulating the practice and procedure in the Supreme Court do not contain any provision governing the practice and procedure for grant of prerogative orders. The Supreme Court however held that "the procedure for the various prerogative orders by the High Court are well known and followed at times with some modifications."  The Supreme Court referred in particular to the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954 (LN 140A), Order 59, r 3 which provides:

"3. Leave shall not be granted to apply for an order of certiorari to remove any judgment, order, conviction or other proceeding for the purpose of it being quashed, unless the application for leave is made not later than six months after the date of proceeding or such shorter period as may be prescribed by any enactment . . .”

It is evident from the above rule, that the applicant for prerogative order must challenge the judgment or order complained of within or not later than six months from the date of the giving of the order or judgment and he must seek the prior leave of the court before filing the application on notice for the grant of the prerogative order.  In Ex parte Puplampu I the Supreme Court observed that the practice so far in the Supreme Court was for the applicant to come by motion on notice without the prior leave of the court. The Supreme Court disaffirmed that practice.  It held that the time factor of six months within which to challenge the order must be observed by the applicant.  Consequently, it held that "any application to be brought whether ex parte or on notice must be made within six months no matter for what purpose the application is brought."  To ensure that time does not run out against an applicant who decides to appeal instead of first applying for a supervisory order, the Supreme Court held unanimously per Wuaku JSC that:

“What the applicant should have done if he had wanted to pursue the matter by praying for an order of certiorari, is to apply for it at the time he lodged his appeal and then to have the certiorari application stayed until the appeal was heard and disposed of.”

IV—CONCLUSION

It appears from the Supreme Court decisions in Ex parte Quarcoo Supra and Ex parte Puplampu I (supra) that the practice and procedure in the High Court relating to applications for prerogative orders are applicable in the Supreme Court subject to necessary modifications.  An applicant also has the choice of deciding whether to apply for a supervisory order or to appeal if he has the statutory right of appeal.  If he files an appeal, he cannot obtain a prerogative or supervisory order from the Supreme Court until the appeal is determined.  However, he can apply for a supervisory order after the expiration of time limited for an appeal.  In any case he cannot apply for a supervisory order without the prior leave of the court. Legal practitioners and litigants must also take note that in line with the decision in Ex parte Puplampu I, the Supreme Court would henceforth not entertain an application for a supervisory order without first obtaining the leave of the court.*

Postscript.  The decision of the Supreme Court in Ex parte Puplampu I – requiring the bringing of a prerogative application within six months from the decision complained of has recently been seriously doubted in Republic v High Court Accra; Ex parte Darke XII, Supreme Court.  30 March 1992, Civil Motion No 46/91 (to be reported in 1992 GLR) where Osei-Hwere JSC said:  “Three of my brothers who took part in the Puplampu decision expressed their disenchantment with the statement [as to the six month’s deadline] which aims at prescribing dogmatically an adjectival rule of law within the province of the Rules Committee...

The question of time is so fundamental that it may initially go to a court's jurisdiction.  Accordingly any stipulation as to time must be expressly stated.  As CI 13 is silent on the issue of time for making an application to this court for an order by way of certiorari, I agree with the general consensus that it can be brought at any time although, the grant being discretionary, it may be refused because of unreasonable delay for making the application.”  It appears Ex parte Puplampu has been overruled by Ex parte Darke XII (supra) on the issue of time limits within which to bring the application..
