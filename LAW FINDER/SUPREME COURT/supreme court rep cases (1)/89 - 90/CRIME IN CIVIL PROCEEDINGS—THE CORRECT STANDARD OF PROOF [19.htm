CRIME IN CIVIL PROCEEDINGS—THE CORRECT STANDARD OF PROOF [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 370—380

Dr. Nii Lante Wallace-Bruce

 

INTRODUCTION

In the case of Ampadu v. Dankwah1 his lordship Abakah J posed this question: "what is the standard of proof required to sustain fraud in civil proceedings?"  In answering the question, his lordship relied on In re Agyepong (Decd.); Poku v. Abosi2 where it was held that fraud in civil proceedings required a higher standard of proof than an ordinary civil matter; there must be proof of criminal deception.  Applying this standard, the learned judge came to the conclusion that in the case before him, the allegation of fraud had not been proved.  The purpose of this Comment is to discuss the law relating to the proper standard of proof in civil proceedings where there is an allegation of a criminal offence.

THE LAW IN ENGLAND

Under English law it is well established that only two standards of proof are recognised. In criminal cases, the prosecution must prove the guilt of the accused person beyond reasonable doubt.  In civil cases the standard is the balance of probabilities or preponderance of probabilities.  Needless to say that the criminal standard is higher than the civil one.  The English courts have not looked favourably on attempts to create a third standard lying between the criminal and the civil standards.3  Until 1957, it was not clear what the correct standard of proof was in civil proceedings where a party alleged that a crime had been committed.  On the one hand, a line of authorities held that the appropriate standard  was the criminal one—beyond reasonable doubt.4  On the other, there were authorities which held that the normal civil standard of balance of probabilities was the appropriate one.5

In 1957 the English Court of Appeal had the opportunity to bring the debate to an end. It decided in favour of the civil standard  or more accurately, a standard within the civil one. In Hornal v Neuberger Products Ltd,6 the plaintiff, a precision engineer, in an action for damages for breach of warranty or, alternatively, for fraudulent misrepresentation, had alleged that the director of the defendant-company had in the course of negotiations for the purchase of a used capstan lathe, stated that it had "been Soag reconditioned."  Soag was a reputable firm of toolmakers and if the statement had been made, the director must have known that it was untrue because the machine had not been reconditioned at all.  In the court of first instance the judge applied the balance of probability test on the claim based on fraud and held that the statement had been made.  But he noted that if the criminal standard of proof had been applied he would not have been so satisfied.  On appeal, the Court of Appeal agreed with the learned judge below.  The case is significant because it is generally accepted as having settled the issue on the correct standard of proof.  There are important dicta in the judgment worth drawing attention to for the purposes of our discussion.  Denning LJ (as he then was) for example, stated:

"The more serious the allegation the higher the degree of probability that is required: but it need not, in a civil case, reach the very high standard required by the criminal law."7 Hudson LJ also stated:

"Notwithstanding the existence of some cases where the point appears to have been argued and decided in a contrary sense, I think the true view, and that most strongly supported by authority, is that which the judge took, namely, that in a civil case the balance of probability standard is correct."8

And finally in a statement which has often been quoted, Morris LJ stated as follows:

"But in truth no real mischief results from an acceptance of the fact that there is some difference of approach in civil actions.  Particularly is this so if the words which are used to define that approach are the servants but not the masters of meaning.  Though no court and no jury would give less careful attention to issues lacking gravity than to those marked by it, the very elements of gravity become a part of the whole range of circumstances which have to be weighed in the scale when deciding as to the balance of probabilities."9

Two clear principles are discernible from the holding and the fore-going dicta.  Firstly, the correct standard of proof in a civil proceeding where a crime such as fraud is alleged, is the applicable standard in civil actions generally, that is: proof on the balance of probabilities, and not the higher criminal standard of proof beyond reasonable doubt.  Secondly, there is no absolute standard as such in either case. However, the degree of probability must be proportionate to the subject-matter.  In the words of Denning LJ (as he then was), "the more serious the allegation the higher the degree of probability that is required . . ."10

The case of Hornal v Neuberger Products Ltd (supra) which settled the law in England has been followed in a number of cases.  In In re Dellow's Will Trusts11  where both spouses were found dead in their home from coal gas poisoning, the issue before the court was whether the husband or the wife had died first.  As the evidence was not conclusive, the husband being the elder, was presumed to have died first. Thereupon, the issue became whether the wife had feloniously killed the husband.  As Ungoed-Thomas J pointedly put it:  "There can hardly be a graver issue than that . . ."12

His lordship held on the evidence that the wife had feloniously killed the husband.  In so holding, Ungoed-Thomas J applied the civil standard of proof, relying as his authority on Hornal v Neuberger Products Ltd.  The result was that neither the wife nor anybody claiming through her could claim the husband's estate. Another civil case in which there was an allegation of criminal conduct but the civil standard of proof was applied is Post Office v Estuary Radio Ltd13  The sole issue was whether Red Sand Tower in the Thames estuary from where the appellant operated a wireless transmitting station without a licence from the Post Master General, was part of the United Kingdom or the territorial waters adjacent thereto.  Although the claim was for a civil remedy in a civil action, it was necessary to prove an offence under section 14 of the Wireless Telegraphy Act, 1949. It was held that the proper standard of proof was the civil standard of the balance of probabilities.  The Court of Appeal rejected a submission by Estuary Radio Ltd that since the acts relied upon constituted a criminal offence, the onus lay upon the Post Office of proving them beyond reasonable doubt.  In rejecting that submission, the Court of Appeal followed its decision in Hornal v Neuberger Products Ltd.

Again in The Michael14 the Court of Appeal affirmed the trial judge's holding that it was the owners of the ship to prove the absence of complicity upon "a clear balance of the probabilities."  In that case the vessel with a cargo of soda ash on a voyage from the United States to Venezuela sank in 1973 and became a total loss.  It was alleged by Lloyds' underwriter that the vessel had been deliberately sunk with the knowledge and consent of its owner's. Alternatively, they pleaded that the owners' initial claim for a loss by perils of the seas had been put forward fraudulently or recklessly on the ground that the owners then knew or strongly suspected that the vessel had, in fact, been deliberately sunk.  Finally, but more comparatively, is the recent House of Lords decision in Khawaja v Secretary of State for the Home Department.15  The appellants, one Indian and the other a Pakistani, were alleged to have entered the United Kingdom by practising fraud or deception on immigration officers and that they were therefore "illegal entrants." They were ordered to be detained pending summary removal. The House of Lords held that the correct standard of proof was that which generally applied to civil proceedings but the degree of probability should be proportionate to the nature and gravity of the issue.  It was emphasised, especially by Lord Scarman and Lord Fraser of Tullybelton, that because the liberty of the individual was at stake the degree of probability required should be high to the satisfaction of the court.  Applying that standard, the House of Lords held that the evidence proved overwhelmingly that the Pakistani had obtained entry through deception on the immigration officers and so his appeal was dismissed.  The appeal of the Indian was allowed as the evidence failed to prove that he was guilty of deception.

The significance of Khawaja v Secretary of State for the Home Department (supra) is that the House of Lords clearly indorsed16 the approach taken by the Court of Appeal in Hornal v Neuberger Products Ltd (supra).  The law in England can thus be stated as follows:  where there is an allegation of a crime such as fraud in a civil proceeding, the correct standard of proof is that used generally in civil matters, namely, balance of the probabilities.  However, due regard has to be paid to the serious nature of the allegation - a higher degree of probability is to be applied within the general civil standard.

THE LAW IN AUSTRALIA

It is interesting to note that Australia actually took the lead in this area of the law.  As far back as 1938, the High Court of Australia (the highest judicial body) laid down the law in Briginshaw v Briginshaw;.17 In that case the appellant-husband's petition for divorce on the ground of adultery was dismissed by the trial judge because he was not satisfied that adultery had been proved beyond reasonable doubt.  The High Court of Australia held that the appropriate standard of proof was the civil standard. However, due regard had to be given to the seriousness of the allegation. As Dixon J (as he then was) pointed out:

"It is often said that such an issue as fraud must be proved clearly, unequivocally, or strictly or with certainty. This does not mean that some standard of persuasion is fixed intermediate between the satisfaction beyond reasonable doubt required upon a criminal inquest and the reasonable satisfaction which in a civil issue may, not must, be based on a preponderance of probability. It means that the nature of the issue necessarily affects the process by which reasonable satisfaction is attained.  When, in a civil proceeding, a question arises whether a crime has been committed, the standard of persuasion is, according to the better opinion, the same as upon other civil issues. But, consistently with this opinion, weight is given to the presumption of innocence and exactness of proof is expected."18

The Australian position was put beyond argument by the High Court of Australia in Rejfek v McElroy.19 In that case the appellants had sued the respondents for the rescission of a contract to purchase a milk run in a suburb of Brisbane, and for damages, because of fraudulently untrue representations inducing the contract. The trial judge, sitting in the Supreme Court of Queensland, gave judgment for the defendants because he was not satisfied beyond reasonable doubt.  Having examined the leading authorities on the subject, the High Court held that in the absence of statutory provisions to the contrary, proof in civil proceedings of facts amounting to the commission of a crime, had only to be established on a balance of probabilities. It was stated:

"No matter how grave the fact which is to be found in a civil case, the mind has only to be reasonably satisfied and has not with respect to any matter in issue in such a proceeding to attain that degree of certainty which is indispensable to the support of a conviction upon a criminal charge."20

The court referred to its earlier decision of Helton v Allen21 in which it made it clear that the criminal standard of proof was inappropriate in determining facts amounting to a crime, such as fraud, in civil proceedings.  The decision of the High Court of Australia has since been followed in a number of cases including, Edelsten v Deputy Commissioner of Taxation (NSW)22 (a bankruptcy proceeding), and by the Australian Broadcasting Tribunal in its landmark decision finding media tycoon, Alan Bond and his television companies, no longer fit and proper persons to hold television licences.23

THE POSITION IN GHANA

Coming back to the question which was posed by his lordship in Ampadu v Dankwah (supra) it is clear that the standard applied in that case is at variance with the legal position in both England and Australia.  It is thus imperative to closely examine the authority on which that case was based.  In In re Agyepong (Decd); Poku v Abosi,24a K, the head of family of A who had died intestate, issued a writ in his capacity as head in the High Court, Sekondi. K sought a declaration that: (a) A's self-acquired and family properties possessed by him at the time of his death became vested in him (A); (b) the marriage of P (claiming to be the lawful wife of A and already granted letters of administration) was not in accordance with the Marriage Ordinance, Cap 127 (1951 Rev) and that she should be restrained by injunction from interfering with the properties of A; and (c) P should surrender the properties and render account. More relevant to our inquiry, K contended that the grant of letters of administration to P was irregular and fraudulent because, firstly, P had falsely described herself as a wife married under the Ordinance, and secondly, P did not disclose that A had other wives and many children. The trial judge ruled that P had been fraudulent in her application for the grant of letters of administration and therefore ordered the revocation of the grant. The Court of Appeal allowed the appeal concluding that the evidence established conclusively that at the time of the marriage of P and A, the latter had divorced all his other wives. Jiagge JA, the learned president who delivered the leading opinion of the court, dealt with the issue of the standard of proof and stated the law in these words24b:

"Fraud must not only be pleaded but clearly and distinctly proved by the man who alleges it. The standard of proof required is stated by Denning LJ in Bater v Bater [1951] P 35 at 37 as follows:  'A civil court, when considering a charge of fraud, will naturally require for itself a higher degree of probability than that which it would require when asking if negligence is established.'  Fraud in civil proceedings requires a higher standard of proof than any ordinary civil matter.  The burden of proof is on the man who alleges fraud and there must be proof of criminal deception . . .  The allegation of fraud to nullify a marriage under the Ordinance after the death of one of the parties to the marriage requires strict proof. There can be no relief unless such fraud is proved strictly and clearly."25

Her lordship Jiagge JA concluded: "The respondent and co-respondent failed miserably to prove the fraud alleged, and there can be no revocation of the grant of letters of administration without such proof."26  Jiagge JA, in the above dictum cited as her authority, the dictum of Denning LJ (as he then was) in Bater v Bater.27a  With all due respect to her lordship, it is our submission that her statement of the law is contradicted by the leading authorities both in England and Australia.  In Bater v Bater (supra), a wife appealed against a commissioner's dismissal of her petition for divorce on the ground that the charges of cruelty made by her were not proved beyond reasonable doubt. A careful reading of the Court of Appeal's decision would disclose that Bater v Bater could not be relied upon as authority for a general proposition that a crime such as fraud requires "proof of criminal deception" in a civil proceeding. Although the court dismissed the appeal, the learned judges seemed to say that the term "beyond reasonable doubt" was not used by the commissioner with the same strictness as it is used in criminal jurisdiction.  Denning LJ explained that if the commissioner had put the case higher and said that the case had to be proved with the same strictness as a crime was proved in a criminal court, then he would have misdirected himself because that would be the very error which the Court of Appeal had corrected in Davis v Davis.27b  It would be adopting too high a standard.  The divorce court, he said was a civil court, not a criminal court, and it should not adopt the rules and standards of a criminal court.

In a dictum which has been frequently cited in many cases,28 but which directly contradicts Jiagge JA's formulation, Denning LJ stated:

"It is true that by our law there is a higher standard of proof in criminal cases than in civil cases, but this is subject to the qualification that there is no absolute standard in either case.  In criminal cases the charge must be proved beyond reasonable doubt, but there may be degrees of proof within that standard.  Many great judges have said that, in proportion as the crime is enormous, so ought the proof to be clear.  So also in civil cases. The case may be proved by a preponderance of probability, but there may be degrees of probability within that standard.  The degree depends on the subject-matter.  A civil court, when considering a charge of fraud, will naturally require a higher degree of probability than that which it would require if considering whether negligence were established.  It does not adopt so high a degree as a criminal court, even when it is considering a charge of a criminal nature, but still it does require a degree of probability which is commensurate with the occasion."29

Further and more curiously, is the fact that in In re Agyepong (Decd); Poku v. Abosi (supra), the Ghana Court of Appeal did not address itself to the cases of Hornal v Neuberger Products Ltd and Rejfek v McElroy (supra). As we have already seen, those cases not only clarified but indeed settled the law in England and Australia respectively. Moreover, those cases have been followed in a number of cases.  Serious doubt must now be cast on the value of In re Agyepong (Decd): Poku v Abosi as authority on the question of the correct standard of proof in civil proceedings where there is an allegation of criminal conduct such as fraud, arson or even felonious murder.

CONCLUSION

From the foregoing discussion it can be stated that the correct standard of proof in civil proceedings where there is an allegation of a crime, or some other grave matter, is: unless there are statutory provisions to the contrary, proof on the balance of probabilities that is the general civil standard. In the Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323) it is called "a preponderance of the probabilities."30  However, the degree of probability must be proportionate to, or commensurate with, the nature and gravity of the particular allegation in question.  Thus where there is, for example, an allegation of minor assault, the degree of probability need not be very high.  On the other hand, where the allegation is that of fraud or arson, the degree of probability must necessarily be very high so that the tribunal of fact will be certain that the allegation has been proved.  However, the standard of proof will in all cases be within the general civil standard.  The courts in England and Australia have held consistently that the criminal standard of proof is inappropriate.  We should bear in mind the warning of Lord Scarman in the case of Khawaja v Secretary of State for the Home Department:

"There is no need to import into this branch of the civil law the formula used for the guidance of juries in criminal cases.  The civil standard as interpreted and applied by the civil courts will meet the ends of justice."31

Finally, it should be observed in Ampadu v Dankwah (supra) that the evidence before the court was so unsatisfactory that in our view, if the High Court at Koforidua had applied the standard of proof as stated in cases such as Hornal v Neuberger Products Ltd and Rejfek v McElroy (supra), the result would have been the same.  In Ampadu v Dankwah, his lordship ruled that three of the particulars of the alleged fraud were not "proved at all." Regarding the other three particulars, his Lordship was not satisfied because (a) the family of the deceased became divided after his death and there was no head of family up to the time of the hearing; (b) the sort of meetings said by the defendants to have taken place; and (c) action taken by the defendants to obtain the letters of administration.  In the result, the plaintiffs' action alleging fraud was dismissed.  Of course, the decision of the Court of Appeal in In re Agyepong (Decd.); Poku v Abosi is binding on the High Court until overruled.
