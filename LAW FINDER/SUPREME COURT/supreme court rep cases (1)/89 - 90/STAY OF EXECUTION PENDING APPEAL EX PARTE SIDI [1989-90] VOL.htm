STAY OF EXECUTION PENDING APPEAL: EX PARTE SIDI [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 273—288

Dr. Agbosu L. K., Assistant Editor*

 

INTRODUCTION

One of the ways in which a judgment-debtor could frustrate a judgment-creditor’s hope of reaping the fruits of a judgment is by using the rules of court and procedures established over the years to delay payment as long as possible.  In this regard one of the most effective weapons in his arsenal would be the use of the appeal system within the hierarchy of the courts.  He would appeal against the decision and subsequently apply to the court to be granted a stay of execution pending the determination of the appeal.  When such an application is granted, depending on the terms of the grant, payment of the substantive award together with costs in the cause could remain suspended until the determination of the appeal.  Having regard to the acknowledged delays in our adversary system of adjudication, this weapon could be effective indeed where the judgment-debtor decides to exhaust the appeal system including recourse to the review powers of the Supreme Court which are nowadays frequently restored to.

It is in order to avoid such injustice that the courts usually grant stays of execution pending appeals on terms, balancing the interest of both parties on two firmly established principles, among others. These principles are: firstly, that a successful party ought not to be prevented from enjoying the fruits of the judgment, and secondly, that an appeal, if successful, must not be rendered nugatory.  However, in a recent decision of a strongly constituted Supreme Court in Republic v Court of Appeal, Accra: Ex parte Sidi,1 there were statements made accepting the contention by a judgement-debtor, that the terms imposed on him in a grant of stay of execution pending appeal being inconvenient and difficult for him to fulfil, should be viewed as a refusal of the application and therefore entitling him to repeat it at the Court of appeal under the Court of Appeal Rules.2 This would seem to run counter to the main stream of authorities established over the years.  The statutory enactments governing the determination of applications for stay of execution pending appeals and the case law developed thereon as well as the literature on the subject are well-known to legal practitioners.3  The purpose of our comment is therefore not to describe them in detail but to highlight some of the pertinent questions raised by the Supreme Court decision in Ex parte Sidi.

EX PARTE SIDI: A SEQUEL TO GHASSOUB v. BIBIANI WOOD COMPLEX LTD.

Until the Court of Appeal decision in Ghassoub v Bibiani Wood Company Ltd,4 it would seem, no one had ever questioned seriously the terms on which the judges have been granting stay of execution pending appeal in the exercise of their discretion over the years.  Provided the judge had exercised his discretion judicially by balancing the interests of both parties, the application was accepted to have been granted, the nature of the terms imposed notwithstanding.  A classic example is provided by the decision of Apaloo J, as he then was, in the celebrated case of Saunders v Awudu.5 There, the plaintiff recovered judgment for £6,000 damages against the defendant who had applied to the court to be granted a stay of execution pending the determination of the appeal he had filed against the decision.  The ground for the application was that the successful judgment-creditor had no visible means and it might not be possible to recover the sum from her if the appeal was successful.

The learned judge granted the application on condition that the whole of the judgment debt of £6,000 together with the costs of 150 guineas should be paid into court within ten days from the date of the order; and from it £2,000 should be paid to the successful party. This should however be done on condition that the plaintiff should provide sufficient security for its refund if the appeal succeeded. Granting the application on those terms, the learned judge said:

“There can be no doubt that considerable hardship will be caused to the plaintiff and her children if this application is granted in its present form. There is also the equally well-established principle that unless very good grounds exist, a successful litigant ought not to be deprived of the fruits of his judgment merely because of the pendency of an appeal.  What I conceive I have to do in this case is to balance the plaintiff’s right to her entitlement to reap the fruits of her judgment, against the equally well settled right of the defendant, that the appeal, if successful should not be nugatory.6

It should be noted that the financial circumstances of the plaintiff did not prevent the court from granting the stay on condition that payment of the whole amount be made into court.  We find a grant of stay pending appeal on similar terms by the Supreme Court in the oft-quoted case of Joseph v Jebeile.7 There, the Supreme Court ordered the judgment-debtor to pay the whole judgment debt of £1,450 into court pending the outcome of the appeal.  The authority of this case has never been questioned on any ground that the terms were “onerous,” “inconvenient” or “difficult” for the applicant to fulfil.  What the decisions show is that the interests of either party require that the amount adjudged to be payable should be adequately secured so that during the pendency of the appeal neither party could take advantage of the other.  Payment into court in the interim would seem to be the most reasonable compromise the courts have been resorting to all along.

However, in Ghassoub v Bibiani Wood Complex Ltd, the Court of Appeal, without any reference to previous authorities and without any in-depth analysis of the relevant authorities or enactments governing the determination of applications for stay pending appeal, gave a new twist to the rules and was concurred in by the Supreme Court in Republic v Court of Appeal, Accra; Ex parte Sidi8 In that case the court below ordered, in an application for stay pending appeal, that three-quarters of the judgment debt of ¢150,366.36 together with the costs of ¢8,000 should be paid to the plaintiff-judgment-creditor and the remaining one-quarter, to be paid into court.  This in effect meant that the whole of the judgment debt was to be paid by the applicant.  Counsel for the applicant repeated the application at the Court of Appeal contending that there was an issue of law to be determined on the appeal as to whether service on a visitor in a solicitor’s office was service on the solicitor.  He also claimed that the application at the court below was virtually refused.  There was nothing in the judgment of the Court of Appeal indicating the reasons given by the counsel for the applicant for stating that the application was virtually dismissed.  In its judgment, the Court of Appeal said:

“We think this is a proper case to grant a stay since there are arguable points in the appeal as to whether service on a visitor in a solicitor’s office is service on the solicitor.  The stay we have granted is in respect of the whole judgment debt of ¢150,366.36 and the costs of ¢8,000 . . . in the court below the court awarded costs of ¢1,000 when the applicant’s motion was virtually dismissed.”

It is clear therefore that the stated reasons for granting the application was because of the arguable points in the appeal.  It was the unusually laconic terms in which the three-quarters of a page judgment of the Court of Appeal was couched in the Ghassoub case that provided the basis for the controversy which ensued in Oppan v Frans & Co Ltd9 and which spilled over to the Supreme Court in Ex parte Sidi.

In the Oppan case, the plaintiff obtained judgment against the applicant in the total sum of ¢130,500.  Dissatisfied with the result the defendant appealed to the Court of Appeal and subsequently applied to the High Court praying to be granted a stay of execution pending the determination of the appeal. The court ruled that execution would be stayed on condition that the whole of the judgment debt and costs be paid into court two months from the date of the ruling.  Apparently encouraged by the decision in the Ghassoub case upon which he rested his case, the defendant repeated the application at the Court of Appeal claiming that the conditions imposed by the High Court were onerous, inconvenient and virtually amounted to a refusal of his application. For these reasons, he thought he was entitled to repeat the application under rule 28 of the Court of Appeal Rules, 1962 (LI 218) as amended.

The grounds for the application apparently seemed novel.  The Court of Appeal thus justifiably, suo motu raised the question as to its competence.  In one of his characteristic lucid judgments in which Edusei and Boison JJA concurred, Adade JSC, sitting as an additional judge of the Court of Appeal, declared the application to be incompetent.  In doing so, he examined the effect of rules 27 and 28 of the Court of Appeal Rules, 1962 (LI 218)10 as amended by the Court of Appeal (Amendment) Rules, 1975 (LI 1002) on the competence of the application.  For purposes of quick reference and clarity, we reproduce them below as follows:

“27. An appeal shall not operate as a stay of execution of proceedings under the judgment or decision appealed from except where the court below or the court otherwise orders . . .

28. . . . where under any enactment an application may be made either to the court below or to the Court it shall be made in the first instance to the Court below, but if the Court below refuses to grant the application, the applicant shall be entitled to have the application determined by the Court.”

It is crystal clear from rule 27 that where a judgment is declared in favour of a party with consequent award of a sum of money to be paid to him by the defendant; the former could take steps prescribed by law to execute the judgment so as to reap the fruits of the judgment, an appeal by the defendant notwithstanding.11 But where the latter is desirous of preventing the immediate exercise by the former of his acknowledged right to execute the judgment, he would have to come under rule 28 of LI 218. As Adade JSC rightly pointed out in his judgment, rule 28 as amended by LI 618 clearly gave the judgment-debtor the right to apply to the Court of Appeal for stay if a previous application to the High Court was refused.  Surely, it cannot be disputed by any rule of logic that the refusal or the dismissal of the application by the High Court is the condition sine qua non for repeating it at the Court of Appeal within the meaning of the rules. As Adade JSC rightly observed in Bisi v Tabiri alias Asare,12 Holmes has given us the classic phrase “’the life of the law has not been logic’. . . but I am yet to read that illogical reasoning is a virtue at law.” It must also be observed that as the High Court has concurrent jurisdiction with the Court of Appeal in these matters, a subsequent application to the latter after it had been dismissed or refused by the former could not be by way of appeal, but a fresh application.13

The problem faced by the applicant in the Oppan case therefore was to prove that his application at the High Court was dismissed and therefore was entitled to repeat it at the Court of Appeal as a fresh one in accordance with the rules. The applicant sought to discharge this obligation by claiming merely that as he found the conditions imposed by the alleged grant to be onerous, inconvenient and difficult to fulfil, the application was virtually dismissed. As he put it, “to pay the whole judgment debt into court as ordered by the High Court would mean [that] I have to sell my landed property as I do not have the liquid cash for this colossal sum, and this will create great hardship on me.” But as there was in existence a long standing formidable array of authorities backed by practice indicative of the fact that stays of execution pending appeals have been granted over the years on terms, apparently much more onerous than that about which the applicant was complaining, the task confronting him was obviously formidable. But he found solace in the Ghassoub case.

Adade JSC, rightly in our humble opinion, concluded that the effect of rules 27 and 28 was that the jurisdiction of the Court of Appeal could not be invoked until the condition precedent of the High Court refusing the application had been satisfied and that the mere fact that a defendant-judgment-debtor might find payment inconvenient or onerous could not be construed as a refusal of the application by the court.  The application, he pointed out, would have been granted on terms; for as long as the order subsisted the plaintiff-judgment-creditor was prevented from going into execution. His lordship referred to the solid authorities of Joseph v Jebeile and Saunders v Awudu to reinforce his argument. He pointed out convincingly that the £6,000 together with 150 guineas ordered in 1962 to be paid within ten days by the applicant in the latter case might be equivalent to some one million cedis in 1983 and therefore about ten times the amount involved in the case before the court in 1983.14  We should add that the terms imposed in the grant of stay in Joseph v Jebeile and many other cases could be seen to be far more stringent than the one complained of in the Oppan case.  Yet no one has ever suggested that those orders were either invalid or amounted to a refusal of the application.

Adade JSC therefore had respectable authoritative precedent and sound logic behind him when he concluded that stay of execution at the High Court meant stopping, delaying or imposing any fetters on the judgment-creditor to obtain an appropriate writ of execution under the provisions of the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954.  He found support in Fitouri Krekchi v United Africa Co Ltd15 in which the West African Court of Appeal used the terms, “stay of execution” and suspending execution interchangeably. But if it is agreed that the effect of any grant of stay of execution whether on terms or not, does suspend action by the judgment-creditor to have his debt paid until the appeal is determined, can it be said in reality that the grant on terms which compel the applicant to pay the whole or part of the amount awarded in the interim, “suspends,” or delays payment? The determination of this issue became crucial in Ex parte Sidi which will be the next subject of our comment.

THE NEW TWIST TO THE RULES ON GRANT OF STAY

In Republic v Court of Appeal, Accra; Ex parte Sidi, the plaintiff was awarded ¢1,000,000 damages and costs of ¢5,000 by the High Court, Tamale, in a libel suit against the defendant.  The latter appealed to the Court of Appeal against that decision and subsequently brought an application for stay of execution pending the determination of the appeal.  The High Court in granting the application followed tradition by staying execution on condition that the defendant shall pay the judgment debt and costs in the cause into court within three months of the order with liberty in the plaintiff to go into execution if the defendant failed to comply with the order.

Apparently encouraged by the decision in the Ghassoub case, the defendant repeated the application in the Court of Appeal contending that the terms were harsh and onerous and therefore should be construed as a refusal.  The Court of Appeal followed the reasoning in the Oppan case and held that the application had not been refused but had been granted on terms and that if the defendant was dissatisfied with those terms, his remedy lay in an appeal against those terms. Being dissatisfied, he brought an application before the Supreme Court for, inter alia, certiorari to quash the decision of the Court of Appeal and for mandamus to order the Court of Appeal to hear the application.

Granting the application unanimously and severely criticising the decision in the Oppan case, the Supreme Court held that when an applicant in Ghana applied for stay of execution of a judgment debt, he was asking the court to suspend the actual payment of the debt pending the outcome of the appeal. If he was, in effect, asked to pay the whole of the judgment debt, whether into court or not, he would justifiably consider it an abuse of language and an indulgence in technicalities to argue that he had been granted his application for stay. Consequently, an alleged grant having attached to it onerous conditions stultifying the substantive expectation of an applicant was a refusal. Hence the High Court order that the whole of the judgment debt of one million cedis be paid into court within three months was, in the circumstances of the case and within the context of Ghanaian conditions, tantamount to a virtual dismissal of the application because the average Ghanaian could not fulfil them and was therefore a “refusal ab initio.”

As the court put it, per Taylor JSC, in “the case of a judgment debt,” [the applicant] “wants the court to suspend the actual payment of the said debt pending the outcome of the appeal.”  He stressed:

“Clearly if within three months, the applicant was unable to obtain the colossal sum of ¢1,000,000 to be paid into court, then the order for stay would lapse and the judgment-creditor would be entitled to go into execution.  How in such a situation it can justly be said that a stay of execution was granted is in my respectful view difficult to appreciate.”

These views were apparently expressed as a criticism of the reasoning by which the Court of Appeal in the Oppan case came to the conclusion that the application in question was granted on terms.  With the greatest respect, and in humility, the Supreme Court would seem to have been at cross-purposes with the Court of Appeal here.  The premises of the Court of Appeal in the Oppan case was to conceive of a “grant of stay” as restricting or delaying the exercise of the right by the successful judgment-creditor to go into execution within seven days after the award, in accordance with Order 42.  Surely, whether or not the judgment-debtor, on the orders of the court on terms, pays the whole or part of the amount awarded into court, it does not thereby free the judgment-creditor from the fetters that limit his freedom of action to recover payment.  In this sense then, a stay would have been granted despite the conditions which might compel the former to pay some money into court.  The Court of Appeal’s conclusions in the Oppan case thus follow logically and validly from that premise; and it must be conceded that such conclusions were consistent with all the authorities that have expounded the law on the issue ante-dating the Chassoub thesis. Of course, it is conceded that the corollary of the Oppan premises might be true of the Ex parte Sidi premises in some cases.  This must be so because so long as the plaintiff is prevented from going into execution so would the defendant’s obligation to pay be suspended. That was the premise of the Supreme Court. However, this is not a necessary condition for maintaining that the two premises are one and the same thing.  The primary reason for this conclusion is that the cases do not support the premise of the Supreme Court in Ex parte Sidi.  If they did, there would have been no place for cases such as Saunders v Awudu; Joseph v Jebeile, etc, just to mention a few.  Applications for stay in cases of that nature would all have been regarded as having been refused.  If we are to accept the Supreme Court’s premise, which conceives of a stay as “suspending” payment by the judgment-debtor simpliciter, and not, as the Court of Appeal regarded it in the Oppan case, as the restricting or suspending the exercise of right by the judgment-creditor to go into execution, then all the cases in which the defendants had been ordered to pay the whole or part of the judgment debt into court in the interim would now be regarded as having been overruled and no longer good authority.  Certainly, the Supreme Court could never have intended to stretch its logic that far.  That is why it held that to obviate protracted litigation the court would order the judgment debt and costs to be stayed upon more favourable terms pending the hearing and the determination of the appeal before the Court of Appeal.  The court thus went ahead and granted stay on condition that the defendant paid into court ¢200,000.  It is submitted that as the condition imposed involved the payment of money before the determination of the appeal the grant was in conformity with previous authorities but was inconsistent with the Supreme Court’s own premise that conceives a grant of stay as a “suspension” of payment by the applicant.  Whatever be the case, we believe that the Supreme Court did not, by the decision in Ex parte Sidi, seek to abolish grants of stay on terms that require the judgment-debtor to pay the whole or part of the judgment debt before the determination of the appeal.  But the Supreme Court itself had never made this clear in its judgment.

It is submitted, with respect, that the Supreme Court had confused the premise of its interpretation of “stay of execution” in Ex parte Sidi, with those of the Court of Appeal in the Oppan case.  This mainly accounted for the different conclusions in both cases. We submit further that for reasons to be elucidated below, the Oppan premise is to be preferred not only because it is supported by precedent, experience and practice, but it provides a sounder basis for balancing the interest of the parties.

Another aspect of the Supreme Court decision which deserves comment is its disagreement with the decision in the Oppan case that the mere fact that a defendant-judgment-debtor might find payment inconvenient or onerous could not be construed as a refusal of the application by the court and that the application would have been granted on terms.  In terms of the Supreme Court’s logic that where the applicant is required to pay the judgment debt, then payment would not have been “suspended” and therefore a stay would have been refused, the Court of Appeal would have been wrong.  But in the light of precedent and experience and the way in which the courts have, over the years treated the grant of a stay on terms as a grant and not a refusal, particularly because the successful party’s right to go into execution would have been suspended, the Court of Appeal would be right.

But the Supreme Court’s decision was not only based on the logic of “suspension” of payment by the applicant into court, either in whole or in part.  It held additionally that where the alleged grant had attached to it onerous conditions that stultified the substantive expectation of an applicant, that would amount to a refusal.  In the particular case at hand the applicant’s claim that the payment of the one million cedis plus the ¢5,000 costs within three months was onerous and harsh and therefore amounting to refusal was acceded to by the Supreme Court.  This would seem to be an introduction of a new principle into the rules for determining applications for stay of execution pending appeals. Innocuous as it seems, it could be dangerous!  It could undermine the objective basis for determining applications in exercise of the judge’s discretion in the light of the facts and circumstances.

Firstly, it must be pointed out that the courts do not exist to satisfy the expectations of applicants.  They do exist to do justice as between the parties.  In doing so they apply the law to the evidence before them regardless of the expectations of the parties.  Each party’s expectation is that the court should give a decision favourable to him.  In this regard their expectation would normally be in conflict.  Hence a decision one way or the other on an objective criteria would result in satisfying the expectations of one or more of the parties but “stultifying” the expectations of the losing party or parties.  Stultification or satisfaction of either litigant’s expectations by the court’s decision thus comes about only as a coincidence and not by design.  Therefore a stultification per se of a party’s expectations by a decision of a court of law based on the evidence before it cannot be a valid basis for faulting the decision.

With regard to applications for stay of execution, we do not, with the greatest respect, consider it right that the subjective view of a judgment-debtor that his expectations are stultified because he finds terms imposed by the judge in an application for stay (using his discretion judicially) difficult to fulfil and inconvenient and therefore, such grant on terms amounts to a refusal of the grant, should be sustained by a court of law.  There is the danger that acceding to such pleas without caution would lead the courts into abandoning their objective criteria for adjudication and overlook in the process the interest of the injured party who has been awarded damages in reparation for his injury or wrong.

In our adversary system of adjudication, the personality of the parties, their financial standing or positions in life have never been allowed to play a role in the adjudicative process in so far as the determination of liability is concerned.  When the judge determines liability either for breach of contract or a breach of duty in negligence, or for the commission of some other torts or for money had and received or in debt, he does not take into account the financial standing of the parties in determining liability.  The objective of the law is to put the person wronged or injured in the position he would have been in if the injury sustained by him had not occurred.  Once liability has been established, unless the judgment-debtor has been legally declared bankrupt, he is liable to pay, his financial standing, difficulty or inconvenience in payment notwithstanding.

Unhappily, the Supreme Court did not provide any tangible reason why although the financial standing of the parties play no role in determining liability, it should become relevant in determining applications for stay of execution.  If the decision of the Supreme Court remains as the correct law it would give a right to every litigant to repeat his application for stay at the Court of Appeal on his own subjective views that the terms imposed by the High Court are onerous, inconvenient and difficult for him to fulfil and therefore amounting to a refusal of his application by the High Court.  This would in effect mean the abolition of grants on terms that require judgment-debtors to pay part or the whole of the money into court. Under such circumstances, how would the court now protect the right of the judgment-creditor to reap the fruits of the judgment?  Would his expectations not be “stultified”?  It would be unfortunate if the financial circumstances of each individual litigant should now become part of the criteria to be considered in determining applications for stay of execution pending appeal.

In any case, the financial position of the defendant, as could be inferred from the judgment, was a determining factor in the Supreme Court’s decision.  Yet nowhere in the judgment was any evidence produced to show the financial position of the applicant.  He merely claimed he would find it difficult to pay the judgment debt within the time specified.  Should the court only accept the applicant’s claims on their face value?  Supposing the judgment-debtor had insured against the kind of liability involved and which became known to the court, would the decision have been different?  What happens where in another case, a similar amount was awarded with a grant of stay on similar terms but to be paid by an acknowledged wealthy litigant?  Would the Ex parte Sidi principle apply to treat the application as having been refused?

In the Oppan case for example, the applicant did not state that he was incapable of paying.  He claimed that to pay the whole of the judgment debt of ¢130,500 into court would mean that he would have to sell his landed property as he did not have the liquid cash for the amount involved.  In the light of the Ex parte Sidi principle, the Court of Appeal should have acceded to such a plea, merely because he would want to pay in a manner that was convenient to him; and in the process the victorious party’s interest overlooked.  In our humble opinion, this new twist to the rules on application for stay is fraught with dangers!

We must continually bear in mind the fact that our procedures are time consuming and expensive.  So long as the judgment debt remains unpaid, the judgment-creditor is deprived of the amount awarded to him.  The damage to him remains unrepaired.  While he is totally prevented from investing it profitably, its value continues to fall rapidly as a result of internal weaknesses in the economy, aggravated by the fluctuations in the international financial system to which our economy is almost inextricably linked, but over which we have no control whatsoever.

Thus Amua-Sekyi JSC, when comparing the fluctuations in the value of the pound sterling and that of the cedi in a recent case, made the following apposite observations:

“If I may say so without hurting national pride, our currency, the cedi, is even less stable than sterling now is.  It is best described as a falling star ever plunging downward into the abyss.”16

While the value of the amount awarded continues to dwindle in the manner described above, the judgment-debtor holds on to that amount and is free either to invest it profitably without accounting to the judgment-creditor when his appeal fails or could divert it or dissipate the funds which could become unavailable by the time the appeal is determined.  Either way, it would be at the expense of, and to the detriment of the judgment-creditor.  Of course, similar arguments could be made in favour of the judgment-debtor because his appeal might succeed.  That is why it has been the tradition of the courts to balance the interests of the parties on objective basis by granting stay on terms.  These factors were rightly recognised by Francois JSC in his judgment in the Ex parte Sidi case when he noted:

“In our conditions where the value of the cedi has assumed complexion at odds with economic realities, following foreign practice may not be in the interest of litigation in this country.  Terms that require payment of substantial amounts of money into court or to the bank, pending the result of an appeal may insidiously subvert justice.  It is this aspect of the distortions that the economy of this country has been reduced to in real terms, on the power of money, its availability and its effect on justice, that I would agree that a fresh look be taken of the Court of Appeal decision in Oppan v Frans & Co Ltd.”

We agree with his lordship that distortions in the economy and their effects on the value of the cedi must, among other things, be taken into account in determining applications for stay of execution. But we are at a loss as to why the scales should be tipped in favour of the judgment-debtor.

We are mindful of the warning by the Supreme Court in Joseph v Jebeile per Akufo-Addo JSC that:

“Any situation created by a judicial act, done either inadvertently or callously, which makes it impossible for a successful appellant to recover money paid . . . under a judgment vacated on appeal does a disservice to the course of justice, if only because it undermines public confidence in the administration of justice.”17

But that warning has to be counter-balanced by the equally well-established principle that the successful party must not be deprived of the enjoyment of the fruits of his litigation. As a compromise, the Supreme Court advised in Joseph v Jebeile that:

“. . . when execution is stayed it should, where the circumstances permit, be on the condition that the judgment-debtor pay into court the amount of money involved, or, when refused, on the condition that the judgment-creditor give security as aforesaid and approved by the judge.”18

It could be noticed from the above quotation that the application would have been granted not (refused) on the condition that the judgment debt be paid into court.  But where the application is refused and the judgment-creditor becomes entitled to go into execution, then he is requested to give security to the satisfaction of the court, for the return of the money if he should finally lose the appeal.  Therefore a grant of stay on conditions, no matter how onerous cannot be interpreted as a refusal.  The remedy open to an applicant, as decided by the Court of Appeal in the Oppan case is to appeal against the so-called onerous terms and not to repeat the application in the next higher court.

CONCLUSION

In the light of the above, and in the interest of justice, we agree with the suggestion made in the judgment of Francois JSC that we adopt the English practice of “the losing party paying the judgment debt on security from the winning party of repayment if the appeal is successful.  Besides, securing undertakings by counsel are also resorted to.”  That suggestion is in accord with what our courts have been traditionally accustomed to doing all along. We should add that in such cases, the judgment-creditor should be compelled and made to understand that where the appeal succeeds he is liable to refund the money received together wit not be earlier than the statutory grace period of seven days normally allowed the judgment-debtor.  This position is justifiable because as soon as a judgment is declared against a party and awards are made, his liability to pay arises, his financial circumstances notwithstanding. It is an obligation imposed by law. Until declared bankrupt according to law these obligations cannot be changed to suit his personal “convenience” or financial circumstances.

From this view point, it follows that the controversial question which arose concerning the remedy of a party who is displeased with the conditions imposed in a grant of stay would become irrelevant.  Where stay is refused, the losing party would be free to repeal this application at the Court of Appeal.  Where the stay is granted, either at the Court of Appeal or at the High Court on terms that require payment of the amount with security as suggested, the obligation to pay would arise without a right of appeal against the terms and the difficulties which Amua-Sekyi JSC rightly pointed out such appeal would give rise to would become irrelevant.
