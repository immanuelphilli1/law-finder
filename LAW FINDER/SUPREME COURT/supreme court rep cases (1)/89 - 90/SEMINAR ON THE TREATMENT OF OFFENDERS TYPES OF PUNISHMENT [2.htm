SEMINAR ON THE TREATMENT OF OFFENDERS: TYPES OF PUNISHMENT [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 167—173

Senah K A

 

Ever since human kind outlived the "state of nature" the quest for law and order as a necessary precondition for functional social existence has been—and continues to be a passion.  Thus as we survey human societies over the ages, we find a great variety of social control mechanisms. My task in this paper is to identify the types of punishment which various societies or epochs have meted out to wrongdoers. As we survey the penologies of various societies it is clear that punishment resorted to in society whether it be the so-called primitive, barbaric or civilised, are based on two-fold considerations: (a) the assumed damage of the anti-social act to the group; and (b) the measure of repayment that the social group exacts.

Thus the taboos of primitive man and the criminal codes of civilised societies are both based on the doctrine that punishment can be found to fit the crime. It is evident, however, that what punishment fits what crime invariably depends on a people's concept of desirability, their level of socio-economic development and on their theories of crime and responsibility.

Crime and Punishment in Early Societies

In these societies, men had precarious hold over their future. Thus they were constantly in fear of the ravages of the unknown and the supernatural. Nothing stood between them and the powers of darkness except the well-beaten path of custom. To use the anthropologist's phrase, "traditional man's custom is his luck." In this society, transgression of the customary codes thus exposed the offending individual to untold woes and besides rendered his whole social group liable to the vengeance of the gods, for responsibility was collective. In this society also, crime and sin were identical. Hence to supplement the individual’s fear of violating the prescribed modes of conduct, on account of the vengeance of unseen powers, there is his certain knowledge that his group will revenge itself upon him for rendering the groups liable to destruction by spiritual forces.

Types of Crime

Early traditional societies had two classes of crimes though they are indistinguishable in certain circumstances: public crimes and private crimes. The former are those which touched the whole social group and the latter were wrongs against private individuals. Given the collective orientation of traditional societies, its members would aid in the repression of public crimes; the adjudication of private crimes was left to the injured parties and their kinsmen. Because traditional societies thought that the most serious disasters to society came from the intervention of supernatural agencies, it was natural for them to regard as the most heinous crimes those which violate the customary rules for placating these supernatural forces and which exposed the whole group to their relentless wrath.  Of this class of offences, by far the most important was the violation of some fundamental taboos which governed the common code of conduct.

Although there are many taboos in early civilisations, perhaps the most respected, since it furnished the basis for the whole system of relationship in tribal society was the taboo against endogamy and incest. Therefore its violation was most severely punished. The other major public crime of early days was witchcraft or sorcery which consisted in using magical powers to bring disaster upon the group and to secure individual revenge or advantage, in effect betraying the group to the mercy of the evil spirits. Naturally, the presence of such a person as a sorcerer in the group was a source of ever present and due cause for panic in the social body.

Beside the two most important public crimes two others—cowardice and treason—were usually deemed of sufficient importance to attract group attention. Of the crime considered a matter of private revenge on the part of the individual or his clansmen there were four or five never overlooked by the injured person or his relation. These were murder, adultery, theft, slander and assault.

Public Crimes

In early tribal societies the punishment for committing public great offences such as endogamy or witchcraft was carried out with severity; the offender was physically eliminated. He might be hacked to pieces or brutally exterminated or even in some cases eaten up; G P  Murdock in his book, Our Primitive Contemporaries, provides us with an example:

Among the Tasmanians, if a member by his action gained the ill-will of the tribe, he had to stand while they hurled spears at him. These he took as best he could by contortions of the body—a defence at which the Tasmanians were adept through long practice.

When the nature of the offence is appraised from the viewpoint of traditional people, their fury is easily explainable.  The fierceness of the punishment is the result not of a desire to inflict pain upon the offender but of eagerness to get rid of him and thus remove the danger of the group being harmed by the powers of darkness. There is also a desire to placate the gods and prove that the group would not tolerate wrongdoing. The punishment of a coward was usually a gross humiliation, public ridicule or corporal punishment rather than death. Other public crimes may be punished by banishment or ostracisation.

Private Crimes

Indeed, in tribal societies, it is difficult to separate public crimes from private ones especially when it comes to punishment. In those societies, every person belonged to a unitary group, a clan or lineage with mystical solidarity. Here, it was thus a religious duty for each clan to avenge summarily any wrong against any of its members. The basis of this blood feud retaliation is the well-known lex talionis. "eye-for-an-eye, tooth-for-a-tooth." Thus a man who murdered a member of another clan exposed every member of his clan including himself to the same fate. His clan may, however, sue for peace by giving away a member of the clan.

In some cultures, the literalness of this method of retaliation with the punishment made exactly to fit the crime is astonishing. Hobhouse quotes one illustration from the Leges Henrici Primi:

“A man who has killed another by falling on him from out of a tree is himself put to death in exactly the same method - a relative of the deceased solemnly mounting a tree and, much as one would say at his own risk, descending upon the offender."

This exactness of retaliation was even carried to animals and inanimate objects as for instance the killing of the ox that had gored a man or the burying or breaking of the weapon which had been used in a murder.

Early Modern Society

Although punishment of wrongdoers in early modern society represented an enlightened departure from that of early tribal societies, punishment nonetheless maintained its brutality. Indeed, down to the close of the eighteenth century, the almost universal method of punishing crime was some type of corporal punishment.  Let us examine a few of these.

Flogging

This was universally employed to preserve family, domestic, military and academic discipline. In the Mosaic code, however, flogging was also prescribed as a method of punishment for crime:

“. . . the judge shall cause him to lie down, and to be beaten before his face, according to his fault, by a certain number.

Forty stripes he may give him, . . .” (Deut. Chap. 25:2-3).

Parental flogging is also encouraged in Proverbs 23: 13-14:

"Withhold not correction from the child: for if thou beatest him with the rod, he shall not die.

Thou shalt beat him with the rod, and shalt deliver his soul from hell.”

For all these purposes, flogging continues down to the twentieth century. As a method of punishing criminals, it still persists in some penal institutions, especially in juvenile reformatories. Before the development of prisons at the beginning of the nineteenth century whipping posts were common in America and England.

Mutilation

As in early tribal societies, lex talionis was also fashionable in early civilisation. A justification of mutilation as a punishment appears to have been based upon the desire to prevent the repetition of a particular crime. Thus thieves and counterfeiters had their hands cut off, liars and perjurers had their tongues torn out; spies had their eyes gouged out, etc. Egyptians and Assyrians punished rape by castration.

Branding

The branding of prisoners of war, slaves and criminals was common in late Oriental classical societies. The Romans branded criminals with some appropriate mark upon their forehead. Branding was also common in France and America. In England, the letters used in branding suggested the crime committed. Murderers were branded with an "M"; thieves with a “T”; vagrants with "Y"; idlers with an "S" meaning slave and fighters and brawlers with an "F" (for fraymaker).

Stocks and Pillory

In early modern times, the stocks and pillory were used as a method of corporal punishment.  The stocks held the prisoner sitting down with his feet and hands caught in a locked frame. The pillory on the other hand held him standing with his head and hands similarly caught. When these were employed and not accompanied by any other mode of punishment, their operation was chiefly psychological as it exposed the culprit to public ridicule. But if the prisoner and his offence were unpopular, he was likely to be pelted and stoned, whipped or branded while he held fast in these engines of torture.

Confinement

In the early 1830s, prisoners were often confined in their cell with both their hands and feet fastened by heavy chains to the sides, ceiling or floor. It was not uncommon for prisoners to be chained in a reclining position upon bars of iron and left in such a position for days or weeks. Prisoners were also chained together at work or in cage wagons at night. They may also be cuffed to their cell doors as is the practice in some modern penal institutions. So much for corporal punishment.

Capital Punishment

Capital punishment is the infliction of the death penalty, taking the life of a criminal convicted of a serious crime. At different times in history and even today, there have been and there still are very different ideas as to what crimes deserve this extreme penalty. While the method of executing the death penalty today are confined to a few standard devices such as beheading, hanging, electrocution, use of lethal gas, and firing squad, capital punishment in the past was executed in a great variety of ways no longer considered "humane" in civilised society. Let us identify some of these.

Flaying and impaling was widely practised in the ancient Orient and medieval Europe. This brutal punishment meant just skinning the victim alive and then placing his body upon a sharp stake where he remained until he died. Another method of administering the death penalty in the Orient was the exposure of the individual to gradual death from insect bites. The victim might also be thrown to lions or other ravenous beasts or poisonous reptiles. Other methods of administering the death penalty include drowning, stoning, casting from a high rock or precipice, poisoning, burning at the stake or in oil, starvation and crucifixion.

Institutionalised and Non-institutionalised Punishment

Although treatment of offenders in contemporary society a far cry from what obtained in the earlier periods, there is no doubt, however, that varying degrees of the elements of early forms of punishment still linger on. Today, apart from capital punishment perhaps the commonest form of punishment of offenders is through institutionalised and non-institutionalised methods. The latter include fines, probation and confinement to a geographically defined area.

Of the institutionalised punishments, imprisonment is the norm. Although modern penological view holds that offenders are sentenced to prison as punishment and NOT for further punishment, penological practices of many countries including Ghana, are yet to come to this realisation. Today, convicts do not only suffer what Gresham Sykes calls the "pains of imprisonment," but also they suffer physical deprivation: people are sentenced to atrocious years of imprisonment and with hard labour; people are made to carry their own excreta; they are made to sleep on bare and wet floors; they are given spartan diets and are sometimes denied medical treatment.

Specialised Institutions

Apart from the prisons, some countries have used specialised institutions such as asylum to punish or at least, to contain non-conformist behaviour. In some countries people who are constantly deviant or are anti-establishment are declared mentally sick and are either sent to asylum or are banished into internal exile. In the USSR such banished persons are made to work while they undergo proper ideological re-orientation.

Conclusion

In this paper, I have attempted to give a panoramic view of the nature and types of punishment meted out to wrongdoers over the ages. Although the discussion is by no means exhaustive, when one considers the methods of torture, the modes of inflicting corporal punishment and the varieties of death penalty, we may well doubt whether any other member of the animal kingdom has ever shown such cruelty and brutality as has homo sapiens in dealing with the criminal group. Let us end by re-echoing what we all know already: modern penological thinking is humanistic in orientation.  It emphasises reform and rehabilitation and not safe custody and punishment.
