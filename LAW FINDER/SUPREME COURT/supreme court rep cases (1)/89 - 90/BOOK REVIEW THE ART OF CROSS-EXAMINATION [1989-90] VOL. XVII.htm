BOOK REVIEW: THE ART OF CROSS-EXAMINATION [1989-90] VOL. XVIII RGL 419—422

 

By Nat Heward-Mills, Barrister-at-Law, of the Middle Temple. Published by Financial Training Publications Ltd, Holland House, 140-144 Freston Road, London W10 6 TR, 1988, vii, pp 119. And A Handbook of Cross-Examination also by Nat Heward-Mills. Published by Sedco Publishing Ltd, P 0 Box 2051, Accra, Ghana, 1991, pp 86.

BIMPONG-BUTA S. Y.

THE first book, The Art of Cross-Examination was launched on 8 April 1988 at an impressive ceremony in the Supreme Court Buildings, Accra, by his lordship, Hon Mr Justice E N P Sowah, the then Chief Justice of Ghana. And on 18 January 1992, almost four years later, the author came out with another publication, A Handbook of Cross-Examination, a booklet described in its preface as a “companion volume to the author’s work, The Art of Cross-Examination.” The second publication, sub-titled A Pocket Book of Cross-Examination was also launched at another impressive ceremony at the Ghana School of Law on 18 January 1992 by his lordship Hon Mr Justice Francois, Justice of the Supreme Court. Your reviewer was present at both launching ceremonies and is therefore more than happy to have the opportunity of reviewing the two books.

In his Foreword to The Art of Cross-Examination, the Hon Mr Justice E O Ayoola, the then Chief Justice of The Gambia said at vii-viii:

“The author has tried to present his work on cross-examination as the lively subject that it is . . . The author has used examples from real cases and drawn from his personal experiences at the Bar . . . The refreshing thing about Mr Heward-Mills’ book is that by the manner of the presentation of the subject, it is a book that professionals and laymen, young and experienced practitioners will find interesting and useful.”

And in his preface to A Handbook of Cross-Examination his lordship Mr. Justice Francois wrote in a similar vein:

“This booklet . . . demonstrates the subtleties and skills that must accompany this art.

It is a handy, practical book that should find a place on every lawyer’s shelf.  It packs in its pages the experience of a lifetime in the author’s chosen area of expertise.”

The above glowing tributes obviously provoke two intertwining questions: First, what are the contents of these two books, and secondly, what are the central messages sought to be conveyed by the author? The Art of Cross-Examination begins with an Introduction which pleasantly exposes the reader to one of the fundamental objects of cross-examination: to expose a seemingly honourable, reliable and over-confident witness as a liar and an unreliable person whose word is not worth the paper on which it is written. To achieve this, the author in one sentence in the Introduction sums it all at p 3:  “Failure to know one’s case well enough before cross-examining could lead to the suppression of truth and consequently to undetected injustice.” The author in chapters 1 and 2 suggests a very practical way of knowing one's case: preparation. The subject is discussed under two sub-headings—remote and immediate. The author states what he means by remote preparation thus at p 5:

“It is quite clear that the cross-examiner should be as knowledgeable as possible on most subjects, if not all. At any rate, as soon as he is given the brief he must start to study around the central theme.”

As to the immediate preparation, the author advises the cross-examiner to study the case thoroughly; he must draw up a plan of action but that must not include coming to court with an already prepared list of specific questions. The author recommends a more effective way: the cross-examination must be built around chosen topics or themes.

In chapter 3, the author deals with the all important issue of cross-examining a party to an action: the plaintiff, the defendant, the accused and their counsel. The author admonishes against the cross-examiner losing his temper when cross-examining the opposing party and he must not initiate his cross-examination by asking damaging questions. Rather as stated at page 36, the cross-examiner must start asking seemingly innocuous questions and “reserving the bomb-shell till a time when it can only leave the witness exasperated.” Chapters 4 and 5 deal with the biased and unbiased witness. As to the way to handle the handwriting expert, the author impresses on the reader (at p 85) the fact that there is no magic about the handwriting expert and that it could be shown by cross-examination, that his conclusions are in fact, unscientific and based on half truths.

The Art of Cross-Examination ends with chapter 6—Conclusion. Under that heading, the author writes at p 92, “My intention in writing this book was to expose the budding advocate to my own brand of cross-examination.” It must be said that the author does so admirably and convincingly. And at pp 92-93 the author makes some observations “for the general guidance of the beginner.” He must be very confident and avoid asking unnecessary and purposeless questions; he should not exhibit overbearing attitude and shout at the witness; he should cross-examine on important matters; the cross-examiner needs not follow the order in which the evidence-in-chief was given and finally the cross-examiner must be alert and ready to deal with new matters arising in the course of cross-examination.

As earlier pointed out, the subsequent booklet, A Handbook of Cross-Examination, was written by the author as a companion to the earlier book. Pages 1 to 18 cover briefly: the definition and nature of cross-examination, how to handle prosecuting and opposing counsel, the trial judge and the audience and the subject-matter of cross-examination; pages 19-25 deal with the importance of Discovery, Inspection and Interrogatories in cross-examination; and pages 31 et seq deal with what the author describes as “certain prerequisites”: (i) logic -ability to think clearly which is an essential tool in cross-examination; and (ii) the need to be well-versed with the provisions of the Evidence Decree, 1972 (NRCD 323), particularly sections 62(1), 63(1), 70-72, 75-76, 81-84, 96-97, 117-120, 134-135. Pages 49-61 cover the need for adequate preparation (a point well- emphasised in the earlier book) and finally pages 61-79, deal with how to cross-examine the witnesses of the parties.

It must be said that both The Art of Cross-Examination and its companion A Handbook of Cross-Examination are lucidly and simply written. Each proffered mode of cross-examination is well-illustrated by civil and criminal cases in which the author was himself the cross-examiner. It makes the reading lively, entertaining and practical. It is highly instructive to the budding cross-examiner and very refreshing to the experienced trial lawyer.

On the debit side, The Art of Cross-Examination has no index of subject-matter, a defect which is cured in the subsequent publication—A Handbook of Cross-Examination. On the other hand, whereas the former is divided into six numbered chapters which deal with clearly defined topics, the latter has no numbered chapters even though the sections of the book indicates an arrangement of the book as numbered 1 to 14. There are, however, virtually no typographical errors-a clear evidence of excellent proof reading.

Every author knows that writing even an article, let alone a book, is a time-consuming exercise. It requires a lot of endurance, patience and perseverance. Mr Heward-Mills therefore deserves more than hearty congratulations for finding the time even as a busy legal practitioner to write The Art of Cross-Examination and A Handbook of cross-Examination. Every lawyer in countries exercising common law jurisdiction like Ghana, must definitely read Mr Heward-Mills’ incisive and lucidly written contribution to the art of cross-examination.
