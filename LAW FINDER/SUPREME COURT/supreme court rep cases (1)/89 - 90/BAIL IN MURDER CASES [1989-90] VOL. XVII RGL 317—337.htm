BAIL IN MURDER CASES [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 317—337

Hon Martin A B K Amidu

 

The powers of both a pre-trial and a trial court to grant bail in murder cases fall under the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30), as amended by the Criminal Procedure Code (Amendment) Decree, 1975 (NRCD 309). Section 96(7)(a) of the Code provides:

“(7) A court shall refuse to grant bail—

(a) in a case of treason, subversion, murder, robbery, hijacking, piracy or escape from lawful custody.”

This provision has been a subject of divergent judicial decisions since the judgement of Taylor J (as he then was) in Okoe v The Republic.1 The controversy would appear to have reached its high watermark in Brefor v The Republic2 when Taylor J (as he then was) joined issue with Osei-Hwere  J (as he then was) over his decision in Donkor v The Republic.3a The controversy concerns the two diametrically opposed views as to whether or not the said section 96(7)(a) places a complete bar on the ability of a court to grant bail, inter alia, in murder cases. The argument was generated by a consideration of the legal effect of article 15(3)(b) and (4) of the suspended Constitution, 1969, which enjoined a court to grant bail in all cases to unreasonable delay in trying or prosecuting a case3b

Section 96(7)(a) was enacted after the suspension of the Constitution, 1969. Article 15(3)(b) and (4) thereof provided:

“(3) Any person who is arrested, restricted or detained, . . .

(b) upon reasonable suspicion of having committed, or being about to commit, a criminal offence under the laws of Ghana, and who is not released, shall be brought before a Court within twenty-four hours.

(4) Where a person arrested, restricted or detained in any circumstance as is mentioned in paragraph (b) of the immediately preceding clause is not tried within a reasonable time, then, without prejudice to any further proceedings that shall be brought against him he shall be released either unconditionally or upon reasonable conditions, including in particular such conditions as are reasonably necessary to ensure that he appears at a later date for trial or proceedings preliminary to trial.”

It is to be noted that there was a verbatim re-enactment of the 1969 provision in article 21(3)(b) and (4) of the Constitution, 1979. One would have thought that since these provisions of the Constitution, 1979, were subsequent in time to the enactment of section 96(7)(a) of Act 30 the provisions of the Constitution overrode the general provisions of Act 30 as amended. The spirit and letter of the constitutional provisions would appear to be inconsistent with and repugnant to the limitations sought to be placed on the discretion of the court. The overriding condition for granting or withholding bail in all criminal matters, one would have thought, is whether there has been unreasonable delay in trying or commencing proceedings preliminary to trial.

The recent decision of the High Court in Republic v Crentsil4 appears to decide otherwise. It also purports not only to lay down the circumstances under which both a pre-trial and a trial court may grant bail in murder cases but also the standard of evidence or the evidential burden which the prosecution must discharge to avoid an adverse ruling against it in an application for or against bail. The decision further purports to fill in what the learned judge considered to be a lacuna left by previous decisions premised on section 96(7)(a) dealing with murder cases. It is therefore necessary to discuss how the decision in Republic v Crentsil helps us to appreciate the previous decisions and the law on the subject.

The facts of the case as stated in the ruling are that, the accused persons were charged with the offence of murder. They were put before a district court. The district court without either hearing or recording the facts remanded the accused persons in custody. This was to enable investigations to be continued for the prosecution to be able to tell the court something on the next adjourned date of 17 May 1988, a very short time. This was also to give the court the opportunity to consult the authorities and come out with a decision on the question of bail. The accused persons being dissatisfied with the refusal of the district magistrate to grant them bail, applied to the High Court for bail. In his affidavit supporting the application the accused averred that he had been charged with the murder of his sister and denied the charge. There was no affidavit in opposition. The court being sensitive to the right of the individual and his liberty granted him bail. The court considered it dangerous and reprehensible for a court to remand an accused in custody in circumstances where the prosecution had either chosen not to reveal to the court the grounds for seeking to have him remanded for investigations to continue or chosen not to give the facts of the case to enable the court to properly exercise its discretion. After bail was granted, the police continued their investigations and found traces of blood stains on the wall in the bedroom of the accused and which had been freshly painted, and also on the door to this room. The blood stains were scrapped and sent to the forensic laboratory for analysis of the blood sample. The laboratory analysis revealed the various blood samples to be human blood of group AB type which matched that of the deceased. On the basis of the forensic laboratory report the Republic applied to the court to rescind the bail earlier granted on the grounds, inter alia, that the first accused person was involved in the matter or knew more of the murder than he had told the police. Consequently, the accused should be taken into custody to assist the police in their investigations.

Upon the foregoing facts, the High Court in its ruling treated the matter as one requesting for rescission or withdrawal of bail in a murder case simpliciter under section 96(7)(a) as distinct from an application under section 96(5)(b)5 which justifies a refusal of bail pending investigations. It will therefore be helpful for a proper appreciation of the ratio decidendi of the case to set out the relevant portions of the affidavit in support of the Republic’s motion as quoted by the learned judge in his ruling:

“4. That in the course of investigations, paint samples scrapped on the door and room of the first accused, which were freshly painted, on the day in question, were sent to the police forensic laboratory for examination.

5. That the result showed that the paint contained human blood and is identical to that found on the door of both the deceased and first accused person. Forensic laboratory report is exhibited herein as A.

6. That the first accused person in his cautioned statement to the police stated that he painted the room the same day but prior to the time he discovered the deceased in the mother’s room in a different house.

7. That with the report of the forensic laboratory it is evident that the first accused person was involved in the matter or knew more of the murder than he had told the police.

8. That it is very necessary that he should be taken into custody to assist the police in their investigations.”

One is not fortunate to have a similar recitation of the respondent’s affidavit in opposition to the application but the ruling states that:

“This application was resisted on the main, that the court having exercised its discretion in favour of the accused to grant him bail, the same court could not be asked to rescind bail and that the application was improper and not maintainable.”

The court then set out the legal argument advanced in support of this precise and exclusive ground for opposing the application— “that the court cannot rescind or withdraw bail properly granted unless the conditions attached had been violated or that the bail had not been honoured.”

The issues that appear to have been thus joined on the application for withdrawal of bail was whether notwithstanding a non-violation of the terms of conditions of bail properly granted the court can rescind that bail to enable an accused person to be taken into custody to assist the police in its investigations. It was held by the court that even where the conditions attached to bail properly granted had not been violated a court had power to withdraw or rescind bail where new facts emerge which would make the earlier grant of bail injudicious6 or a direct violation of the mandatory provisions of section 96(7)(a) of Act 30 by virtue of the courts inherent power to set aside any such illegal or void decision. After making this finding on the issue raised in the respondent’s affidavit in opposition the learned trial judge proceeded to reformulate the grounds and the relief sought by the prosecution on which issues had been joined. He said:

“I will now turn to the more substantial issue which in my view are raised by this application. I have earlier in this ruling quoted section 96(7)(a) of Act 30 which peremptorily prohibits the grant of bail where an accused person is charged with murder. The question is under what circumstances can this provision be said to hold sway and extinguish the High Court’s power to grant bail?”

The learned trial judge then held that upon the evidence disclosed by the application, a charge of murder had not been properly laid against the accused to warrant the rescission of the bail earlier granted. He held further that in considering whether or not the evidence on an application for withdrawal of bail satisfied the requisite degree of proof, the test must be an objective one of whether the evidence in support of the application was such that a court would be of the opinion that there was a case for the accused to answer. The burden of proof, according to the court, in such a case was the same as that placed on the prosecution both at the committal proceedings, and at the close of the case for the prosecution at the trial itself. The prosecution must prove “that they have evidence to establish all the essential elements of the charge.”

The case of Republic v. Crentsil which was clearly an application brought under section 96(5)(b) on the ground that “it is very necessary that the accused be taken into custody to assist the police in their investigations” was thus by a process of judicial imposition of the real issues in dispute between the parties, made to assume the hallmark of a standard case of bail in murder cases.7 In view of the fact that the issue of bail in murder cases  has been a recurrent issue in our courts, we would proceed to examine this case as a specie of those cases and thus contribute to the on-going debate on the subject. We would then conclude by examining whether the case of Republic v Crentsil can in fact be said to have decided any legal issues on bail in murder cases.

PROPER CHARGE OF MURDER

The gravamen of the learned judge’s refusal to rescind bail in the Crentsil case was that there was no proper charge of murder laid upon the evidence. The evidence in his lordship’s view did not establish all the essential elements of the charge. Consequently there was no case of murder to call in aid section 96(7)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30), as amended by section 2 of the Criminal Procedure Code (Amendment) Decree, 1975 (NRCD 309).

In coming to the conclusion that there was no proper charge or murder laid upon the evidence, the court stated that there were two circumstances in which a court might consider the issue to pretrial bail: Firstly, on the presentation of the facts to the court in a summary trial and secondly, in the summary of evidence in a trial on indictment. In both cases the test was the same. The test we are told was also the same but in a limited sense, at the close of the case for the prosecution. The decision of the learned trial judge can thus be reduced to the proposition that: the issue of whether a proper charge of murder has been laid would depend on (a) the presentation of the facts at a summary trial, or (b) in the summary of evidence in an indictment either at the committal proceedings at a bail court or at the trial itself.

It is however trite learning that a district court is incapable by virtue of section 39(1)(b) of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372) of trying the indictable offences of murder and manslaughter. Therefore it has no jurisdiction to grant bail even when the facts presented purport to be given at a “provisional” summary trial or on a provisional charge of an indictable offence which is yet to be appropriated for summary trial by the Attorney-General.8  The only limited sense in which a district court can be said to be seized with a proper charge of murder is where a district court is exercising its committal jurisdiction under sections 181 to 197 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30). That is in deciding whether on the bill of indictment and summary of evidence furnished by the prosecution to the court and the accused, there is a case for the accused to answer to warrant his being committed to stand trial. In the exercise of this committal function the district court may decide whether the accused should be admitted to bail pending committal or pending his appearance before the trial court after committal.9 Without furnishing a district court with the bill of indictment and the summary of evidence, that court cannot be said to have before it a charge of murder on which an evaluation and objective analysis of the summary of evidence can be made to determine whether the charge is proper or improper to warrant the grant or refusal of bail. In such a case a district court’s jurisdiction to determine whether there is a case to answer as expounded by the learned judge in the Crentsil case would not exist.

In spite of the fact that the learned judge expounded correctly the two circumstances in which an accused person’s application for pre-trial and trial bail in a court may be considered, he failed to address the fact that the application before him was neither one arising out of the presentation of the facts by the prosecution in a summary trial nor one arising out of the refusal of bail by a district court during or after a consideration of the indictment and summary of evidence. The proceedings of the district court at which the accused was remanded in custody show that even though a charge was read and explained to the accused in Fanti, his plea was not taken and the facts were reserved. A ruling was made by the district court ostensibly upon an oral application by counsel for the accused for bail. There is no indication whatsoever that the accused persons were charged with the offence of murder. The only remote indication of the charge before the district court was a statement by his lordship that:

“In his affidavit supporting the application for bail, the accused denied the charge and explained how he came to discover the dismembered body of his younger sister.”

In his lordship’s view there was no affidavit in opposition and the respondents were deemed to have admitted the denial of the accused. The fact, however, still remains that the ruling of the learned High Court judge does not disclose whether, in fact, the charge denied in the affidavit was one of murder or a non-capital offence of manslaughter, causing harm by means of offensive weapon or other offence involving unlawful harm not caught by section 96(7)(a) of Act 30. It is thus difficult to appreciate the High Court’s ruling that the accused person was charged with murder albeit an improper charge before the district court as disclosed by the affidavit in support of and in opposition to the application for withdrawal of the earlier bail. Admittedly counsel for the respondent could have argued that on the facts disclosed in the affidavit in support of the application the accused could properly be charged with murder in which case the power of the court to grant bail had been ousted. The bail earlier granted had been overtaken by events. It should therefore be rescinded in view of the new facts. But such a submission by counsel for the Republic would by no means be evidence that the accused was charged with murder. On the contrary it shows that the Republic could in its discretion charge the respondent with murder or any other related or similar offence. It is our submission that the only appropriate conclusion to draw from the affidavit of the Republic and the arguments in support thereof is that the accused persons were put before the district court for the purpose of being remanded in custody to enable the police to continue with investigations. The application before the High Court for withdrawal of bail was upon new facts disclosed by police investigations while the accused was on the court’s bail and it had become “very necessary for the accused to be taken into custody to assist the police in their investigations.”

It is further submitted that the facts disclosed by the ruling of the High Court did not raise the issue of a proper charge of murder. His lordship’s own ruling did not find that a bill of indictment and summary of evidence were furnished to ground a charge as required by law to enable the district court consider whether or not the charge was proper. The district court was not therefore in a position to exercise its discretion to remand the accused on a murder charge. This may explain why the district court remanded the accused to enable the prosecution to tell the court “something” within a very short time. The district magistrate also undertook to look up the authorities (within the short time of remanding the accused) on bail in the circumstance it found itself.10 It is also suggested that even if the district court purported to be trying a murder case in refusing bail, the decision of the district court was challengeable not by an application for bail to the High Court but by an application for certiorari. In arriving at this conclusion one may take cognisance of the fact that the High Court has on a number of occasions entertained applications for bail from district court on the basis of charge sheets purporting to charge accused persons with the indictable offence of murder. Prah v The Republic11 and Seidu v The Republic12 have often been quoted as authority for the proposition that the peremptory requirements of section 96(7)(a) can only be evoked when the charge laid before a district court is a proper charge of murder. Both cases were quoted with approval in the Crentsil case. A brief discussion of those cases will assist our appreciation of the Crentsil case.

In Prah v The Republic the Republic argued that section 96(7)(a) prohibited the grant of bail by the court. It was held that the claim of the applicant deposed to in their affidavit in support of their motion for bail was impliedly admitted by the respondent by not filing an affidavit in opposition. It was in consequence held that the applicants had not been properly charged with murder and since the application was not opposed on the merits it would be granted. The application would thus appear to have been granted on the technical basis of default of pleadings which made it impossible for the court to go into the real merits of the application.

In Seidu v The Republic the applicants were granted bail by the district court. The Republic successfully applied to the High Court for an order of certiorari. After nine months of police investigations without discovering any material evidence to create a nexus between the applicants and the murder, the applicant applied to the High Court for an order of certiorari to quash the order granting bail. The issue before the court was whether (a) despite the mandatory provisions of section 96(7)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30) as amended, the court could in the absence of evidence supporting the charge of murder, grant bail and (b) who had the right to decide whether a case was one of murder or some allied crime. It was held, inter alia, that any person preferring a charge under a criminal statute must be quite sure that the offence charged was within the letter of the law. It was unbelievable that the legislature by section 96(7)(a) of Act 30 intended the courts to refuse bail to persons against whom no proper charge of murder had been preferred. The assumption was that the legislature had not intended what was inconvenient or unreasonable. Those whom the legislature intended section 96 of Act 30 to cover were those against whom, the charge of murder preferred could be said by the court to be properly supported by the evidence which the prosecution professed to have got and not against persons whom there was no legally acceptable evidence connecting them with the charge of murder. The provisional charge of murder against the applicants, it was further held, was unknown to the criminal law of Ghana even though that phrase was often heard in and around our courts without justification and explanation. Consequently, the court decided that there was no proper charge of murder preferred against the applicants.

In both Prah v The Republic and Seidu v The Republic one of the main grounds for granting bail was that there was no proper charge of murder preferred against the applicants. These two cases did not specifically decide the issue of a purported charge of murder before a district court which was not on indictment. It is however submitted that any murder charge brought before a district court which is not by virtue of the service of a bill of indictment and summary of evidence as required by the procedure for indicting an accused person is not a proper charge of murder. Any decision taken thereon by a district court could be quashed by certiorari. It is the duty of counsel or the accused to submit  and request that he should be discharged by the district court.13 The failure of counsel to raise a preliminary objection to the jurisdiction of the court below does not confer on that court a bail jurisdiction from which a High Court may exercise a reviewing power of granting or refusing bail. The grant of bail by a High Court will mean that the accused was properly before the court below and could appear before it for his case to be properly disposed of. This definitely introduces a contradiction because if the charge was improper the accused ought to be discharged and not granted bail. The only method to effect such a discharge in our view is to come by way of certiorari to have the decision and proceedings of the court below quashed. A person cannot be on bail on an improper charge of an offence.14 It is consequently submitted that the learned bail judges in both Republic v Crentsil and Prah v The Republic erred in assuming that in the absence of a proper indictment of the accused of murder, the district court had jurisdiction to hear a case of murder based on an ordinary charge sheet. Such a charge sheet would be unknown to the process of pre-trial adjudication on indictment in our jurisprudence to justify recourse to section 96(7)(a) of Act 30. Osei-Hwere J (as he then was) appreciated this in Donkor v The Republic. In that case the applicant was charged before a district court with subversion contrary to section 1 of the Subversion Decree, 1972 (NRCD 90) and remanded in custody. In granting bail to the accused the High Court held that the charge being one of subversion the district court had no jurisdiction to entertain it. In coming to that conclusion the learned judge stated that an inferior court was not presumed to have any jurisdiction except that which was expressly provided and the civil courts both superior and inferior were completely divested of jurisdiction to try an offence under the Subversion Decree, 1972. The vaunted expression “provisional charge” whatever its legal basis and claim of convenience, in the opinion of the judge, could not be proffered as a means of conferring on a district court jurisdiction to which it had no claim. Regrettably, however, all that the judge could do was to grant bail even though he had, in fact, declared the proceedings before the district court to be illegal, ultra vires and void. Since the accused in all these cases were not discharged, one may ask whether they would not have been liable to bench warrants if they failed to appear at the next adjourned date before the district courts which had been declared to have no jurisdiction. It is our submission that in such cases counsel is entitled to use the process of certiorari instead of an application for bail because the facts would clearly not be a proper case of bail but excess of jurisdiction.15 On the foregoing, we submit that it can be said with certainty that despite the powers of a district court to commit an accused person for trial on indictment for murder, neither the Criminal Code nor the Criminal Procedure Code confers on a district court jurisdiction to grant pre-trial bail on a charge of murder simpliciter.

We are however of the view that a district court or a circuit court, as the case may be, has jurisdiction to assist in the arrest and detention of murder suspects for the purpose of preventing such suspects who “may interfere with any witnesses or evidence, or in any way hamper police investigation.” Once a suspect is brought before a district court on any process or after being arrested without warrant and the Republic adduces evidence to show that the suspect may do any of the acts quoted above the court may remand the suspect. He need not be charged if in fact police investigations have not yet established the requisite degree of evidence for the preferment of a proper charge. It is for these reasons that we are of the view that the decision in Republic v Crentsil that a case of murder had not been objectively made out on the application because the affidavits before him did not disclose an objective evidence of murder was, with due respect, erroneous. On the judge’s own ruling bail is to be objectively determine on the facts presented in a summary of evidence in committal proceedings or a trial on indictment. None of the two conditions existed in the Crentsil case to warrant the reliance of the court on affidavit evidence to test or analyse the facts presented or the summary of evidence.16

A MURDER CASE

The court in Republic v Crentsil also held, among other things that a bail court was the proper body to decide whether or not a murder charge preferred against an accused disclosed a case of murder to warrant his being remanded in custody as required by section 96(7)(a). Roger Korsah J whose decision in Seidu v The Republic was adopted by the court in the Crentsil case had earlier held that the issue as to whether a case was one of murder or some other crime had to be determined objectively by the court. He said that although the prosecution had to decide what charge it would prefer against a suspect, it was the duty of the court to decide whether the case was one of murder or some other crime by considering the evidence in support of the charge. A case would not be considered a murder case until it had been decided to be so by the court having jurisdiction to try it. He decided therefore that the legislature was by the provision of section 96(7)(a) ordering the court to refuse bail in all cases where there was evidence in support of the accusation of murder. Taylor J (as he then was) had in Okoe v The Republic raised the issue whether the section could operate to oust the jurisdiction of the High Court in a situation where a man was held ostensibly on a murder charge in a case where the police had no evidence whatsoever, even of a prima facie nature, to connect the man with the alleged murder. He did not feel called upon to decide the issue at the time, whether this hypothetical situation would be a murder case within he contemplation of section 96(7)(a).17 Roger Korsah J found the court to be the appropriate body to answer that question on the appropriate occasion provided by Seidu v. The Republic. The bail court in Republic v. Crentsil did not only apply the decision of Roger Korsah J but went on to indicate the evidential burden on the prosecution at the bail court. There must be a prima facie case or a case to answer else bail would be granted on the basis that the charge was not properly laid. Roger Korsah J on the other hand anchored his decision on the fact that a provisional charge of murder was unknown to our laws.18 He said that if the legislature had said in every case where a charge of murder was preferred against the person the court shall refuse bail, he would concede that the power of the court would have been completely ousted. In Brefor v. The Republic, Taylor J, as he then was, finally answered the question left open in Okoe v The Republic by deciding that the court was the proper body to decide whether an accusation was a case of murder after examining the bill of indictment and the summary of evidence and not the affidavits.

The issue of who is to decide whether a case is one of murder to oust the jurisdiction of the court for purposes of bail would appear to have arisen because of the wording of section 96(7)(a), ie: “in a case of murder” instead of “on every charge of murder.” Under section 96(7)(a) the requirement that a court shall refuse to grant bail in a case of treason, subversion, murder, etc appear to be so fluid as to open itself to the different interpretations placed on the words “in a case of” by the prosecution and the courts.19 It is arguable that a case of murder is one in which the jury  has returned a verdict of murder after the summing up. The trial court in the sense used by Roger Korsah J in Seidu v The Republic will not be a bail court but the trial court which can decide whether a case is one of murder or some other crime within the jurisdiction of the court and the jury. A decision by a pre-trial court, sitting as a bail court, that on the evidence a case is not one of murder would amount to acquitting the accused person by a subterfuge at bail court and thus usurping the powers of the trial court in murder trials. A bail court’s duty is not to find out whether there is a case for the accused to answer but whether in view of section 96 of Act 30 an accused ought to be granted bail. The merits or otherwise of the charge, indictment or case is not in issue at that stage. Once the bail court begins to determine by extensive analysis of evidence whether or not there is a case for the accused to answer or a prima facie case has been made out, it assumes the duties and functions of a trial court which it is not.20 In this sense we agree with Dr E V O Danquah that the learned trial judge in Republic v Crentsil had exceeded his functions as a bail court in the analysis and examination he undertook to determine whether the accused was properly charged with murder.21 As we noted earlier, we are of the opinion that even if the bail court were the proper body to decide what a case of murder is, the learned judge erred in deciding the case without a bill of indictment and summary of evidence. Taylor J raised the issue of a man against whom there was no evidence on a charge of murder, even of a prima facie nature and held in Okoe v The Republic that it was not the province of a bail court to pronounce on the guilt of an accused person. By the same reasoning we submit that it is not the province of a bail court to pronounce on the innocence of an accused person as the court tried to do in Republic v Crentsil.

We are of the view that the legislature, intended by section 96(7)(a), that a court shall refuse to grant bail on every charge of murder. We have argued that the interpretation placed on the words “in a case of murder” and “in a murder case” also pose problems of finding out the intention of the legislature. The High Court itself cannot agree on the interpretation of section 96(7)(a) of Act 30. The appellate courts have not borken the impasse.22 The law therefore cannot be said to be unambiguous in its present state. Our view of section 96(7)(a), we must admit, could have the tendency of working hardship on accused persons who are eventually found not guilty. But our criminal justice system has conferred on the Attorney-General, a quasi-judicial officer, the power of deciding whether or not to indict a party. Sections 55 and 58 of Act 30 contain safeguards to ensure that the power is exercised only by responsible officers of the court. There is no basis to suggest that if the Attorney-General and those to whom power is delegated to sign indictments are true to the honourable profession of the law a party would be indicted capriciously.23 Be that as it may, it is our humble suggestion that the foregoing discussions show that our law on bail, particularly section 96(7) if it is still operative, needs to be reviewed. In doing this recourse could be had to the Bail Act, 1976, and the Magistrates’ Courts Act, 1980, both of Britain and of other common law jurisdictions.

Relief Sought by the Republic in Crentsil Case.

The foregoing discussions in as far as they relate to the case of Republic v Crentsil have proceeded on the assumption that the accused person had been properly charged with murder and therefore bail ought to be withdrawn or rescinded. The facts as deposed to in the applicant’s affidavit and quoted extensively by the court show clearly that the relief sought was not premised on a charge of murder. Admittedly, paragraph 7 of the Republic’s affidavit which the court curiously chose to emphasise and upon which the whole ruling was premised stated that the accused person knew more about the matter or the murder than he had told the police. That paragraph cannot be interpreted to mean that the accused person had committed the offence of murder. In fact the paragraph speaks of knowing more about the matter or the murder. It was thus at best highly speculative which explains the definite request made in paragraph 8 of the affidavit that it was very necessary that the accused person “be taken into custody to assist the police in their investigations.”

It is trite learning that a court must try cases and give judgment upon the facts disclosed on the pleadings and upon the issues joined on those facts.24 A court may make comments on issues not specifically raised but which are collateral to the main facts and issues. When a court fails to make a decision or render judgment on the facts and issues in the pleadings but on speculative and hypothetical facts and issues the judgment can be said to be a nullity.25 The court could be said to be in breach of the audi alteram partem rule as the parties would not have been heard on the facts and issues brought for adjudication.

The learned judge in the Crentsil case thought it regrettable that Taylor J (as he then was) did not feel called upon to decide the hypothetical issue of a man held ostensibly on a murder charge in a case where the police had no evidence whatsoever, even of a prima facie nature to connect him with the alleged murder. But there is no doubt that Taylor J realised, rightly, that the facts deposed to in both affidavits for and against the application in Okoe v The Republic did not call upon him to decide the hypothetical issue as to whether the case would be a murder case within the contemplation of section 96(7)(a). Anglo-Ghanaian jurisprudence requires that courts decide real and existing disputes or accrued causes of action and not speculative or hypothetical cases. Unfortunately the learned bail judge in the Crentsil case missed Taylor J’s appreciation of the duty of judicial self-restraint which enjoins judges not to decide hypothetical issues. When the opportunity presented itself in Dogbe v The Republic26 Taylor J, as he then was, did in fact decide that the provisions of section 96(7)(a) of Act 30 as amended by NRCD 309 were mandatory and imperative. They ousted the hitherto unquestionable, ancient and time-honoured jurisdiction of the courts to grant bail to persons accused of crime when the case was, inter alia, even of murder. As the case did not fall within the constitutional purview of “unreasonable delay” in trying the accused person he dismissed the application. In Brefor v The Republic, Taylor revisited the issue of when bail might be granted in spite of section 96(7)(a). He was content to look at the bill of indictment and the summary of evidence and decide without any elaborate analysis which might prejudice a decision at the trial that prima facie, it was a murder case. Unfortunately in Republic v Crentsil the court’s attention was not drawn to Taylor’s decision expressing his views on deciding hypothetical issues.

It is our submission that the issue before the court for decision was whether or not upon the facts disclosed by the Republic’s affidavit the accused person ought to be taken into custody to assist the police in their investigations. The Republic’s case was that new facts had emerged which warranted the rescission of bail. The court unfortunately failed or refused to decide the issues required of it by the application. The court cannot therefore be said to have decided a case of bail in murder cases as the decision in that respect was speculative, hypothetical and offends against Anglo-Ghanaian criminal justice and adversarial system of dispute resolution. The purported decision is not, with respect, an obiter dictum as no ratio decidendi can even be discovered on the issues raised by the Republic.

Applicability of Section 96(7)(a)

In Brefor v The Republic Taylor J (as he then was) posed by way of obiter, the question whether on the coming into force of the Constitution, 1979, on 24 September 1979, section 96(7)(a) of Act 30 could co-exist with article 21 (3)(b) and (4)(a). He conceded that this problem would be raised in sharp relief with the coming into force of the Constitution. He however hazarded the opinion that the most casual consideration of the problem would reveal that it was impossible by any process of interpretation to contend that the constitutional provisions repealed section 96(7)(a) of Act 30. However, we are inclined to agree with the decision of Osei-Hwere J (as he then was) in Donkor v The Republic that the constitutional provision in respect of bail for unreasonable delay did not survive the Constitution, 1969. The only provisions which survived were those specifically saved by the Proclamation, 1972. We share the views of Osei-Hwere J that article 15(3)(b) and (4)(b) cannot be said to be rules of law which survived the Constitution. It is also our view that section 96(7) did not survive article 21(3)(b) and (4)(a) of the Constitution, 197927 because that provision is inconsistent with the spirit and letter of the fundamental human rights provisions of the said Constitution. The fundamental human rights provisions of the Constitution evinced an intention not to discriminate against persons suspected of the commission of a crime. It therefore promulgated article 21(3)(b) and (4)(a) making unreasonable delay a mandatory condition for which an accused person who is arrested, restricted or detained and is not tried shall be granted bail. By purporting to place a bar in special cases on accused persons from being granted bail, section 96(7)(a) discriminates against those suspects or accused persons. Article 21(3)(b) and (4)(a) is a specific provision which is subsequent in time to section 96(7)(a) of Act 30, which is a general provision.28 The latter provision should therefore be deemed to have been amended. Should our submission be the correct view, then it can be said that with the abrogation or suspension of the Constitution, 1979, the provisions of article 21(3)(b) and (4)(a) of the Constitution not having been specifically continued in existence by the Proclamation, 1981 is inoperative. It stands to reason that the only statutory provisions applicable to bail are section 96(1) to (6) of the Criminal Procedure Code as amended by NRCD 309. The court in Republic v Crentsil would thus have been in error even in purporting to decide on section 96(7)(a) in a case or on a charge of murder.

CONCLUSION

Both the decision of the High Court in Republic v Crentsil and Dr E V O Danquah’s article reviewing that case are premised on concern for the liberty of the individual. A court of law has a duty to maintain the scales of justice evenly between the state and the individual. A court should not unjustifiably refuse to decide whether the state should in  a particular case hold a person whom it reasonably believes is necessary to assist the police in investigations. If a court does this it will be disabling the state from discharging its duty of ensuring the safety of the life and property among the body-politic. The well-intentioned desire to uphold and secure the liberty of the individual may well produce anarchy in such circumstances. Offences may be committed with impunity resulting in some cases, in incapacity or loss of limb, life and property without any one being brought to book because the police cannot be heard on their reasons for wanting to take suspects into custody for investigations or to prevent interference with witnesses, etc.

It must be a credit to Taylor J (as he then was) that while defending and upholding the liberty of the individual from what he perceived to be undue encroachment by the rather powerful state apparatus he never in any of his landmark judgments on bail forgot that the liberty of the individual can thrive only in an atmosphere where judges follow the taught traditions of the common law in holding the scales evenly between the individual citizens and the State. It would be a sad day in legal history of Anglo-Ghanaian jurisprudence when the liberty of the individual becomes the clarion call for anarchy or a means of seeking judicial self-glorification per se. Mr. Justice Cardozo’s admonishment to judges of the common law tradition not to be knight-errants ought to be remembered by all those whose business it is to judge. In pointing out the difference between “le phenomine Magnaud” and justice according to law, he said:

“The judge, even when he is free, is still not wholly free. He is not to innovate at pleasure. He is not a knight-errant, roaming at will in pursuit of his own ideals of beauty or of goodness. He is to draw his inspiration from consecrated principles. He is not to yield to spasmodic sentiments, to vague and unregulated benevolence. He is to exercise a discretion informed by tradition, methodized by analogy, disciplined by system, and subordinated to ‘the premodial necessity of order in social life’.”29

Be that as it may, it is our submission that the various interpretations by both the courts and legal writers on the current mandatory bail provisions of this country point to the fact that there is the need to reform the present law on bail to take account of its inadequacies and ambiguities. As this country moves towards a Fourth Republic, the law on bail must be looked at and reformed to achieve the pronounced object of the government in ensuring that existing laws are amended to guarantee to the individual the maximum enjoyment of his fundamental human right of liberty to the person from undue interference by the State. It is our hope that this Case Comment would contribute to any future efforts at constitution-making and of the law reform in reviewing the existing law to bring it into harmony with international norms on human rights.

[Editorial Note.] The provision requiring grant of bail where the accused “is not tried within a reasonable time” in article 21(3)(b) and (4) of the Constitution, 1979, has been re-enacted verbatim as article 14(3)(b) and (4) of the Constitution, 1992. Thus the author’s call for the reform of the law on bail in murder cases is yet to be answered and the debate is still open.
