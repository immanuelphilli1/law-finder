MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: TRIBUTE TO THE HON MR JUSTICE J N K TAYLOR RETIRED SUPREME COURT JUDGE [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 4—19

 

JUSTICE J N Kobina Taylor, undoubtedly the most confident, courageous, the boldest, and perhaps, the most industrious and certainly one of the ablest of Ghana’s judges retired from the Bench as a Supreme Court judge on 30 March 1990. That was at the mandatory retiring age of 65 years. This ended some 21 years of a distinguished career on the bench.

Kobina Taylor is by birth a full blooded citizen of Ghana. In the period that providence has spared his life he has had a varied and chequered career by which he has acquired a great deal of experience. He started from very humble beginnings as an off-spring of a young elementary school educated Fanti clerical officer and an illiterate and conscientious Ga mother. His life has been a struggle. A statement about his life at a symposium on Human Rights in Changing Society sums up his early and adult life. He himself said:

“I have been an engineering student, a clerk in UAC, a secondary school headmaster, an impoverished private student in the United Kingdom, a dish-washer in restaurants in England, a labourer, railway worker in England, a law graduate, a barrister, a crown counsel, state attorney, Director of Public Prosecutions, Acting Attorney-General and Commissioner for Justice, a High Court judge and now a judge of the Supreme Court in this Republic."

As can be seen from this statement, Justice Taylor is a man of many parts and varied experiences. He attained adolescence during the colonial period. He lived through the Nkrumah regime, the military rule of the NLC, the Second Republic of Dr Busia, the Acheampong oligarchy of triple councils, NRC, SMC I and SMC II, the short AFRC interregnum, the Limann Administration and the PNDC era. During that time span he had, among other things, been counsel and chairman of a number of commissions and committees of inquiry and had taken part in Cabinet discussions and decisions in his capacity as Ag Attorney-General and Commissioner for Justice during the NLC era.

Versatile as he is, it would require a book on his life and times to adequately assess his immense contribution not only to Ghana as a whole but more particularly in the field of administration of law and justice in Ghana.1

However his immense contribution both as a State prosecutor in the Attorney-General's Department and a superior court judge for a period of 31 years cannot be lost on the people of Ghana—particularly Honourable Members of the legal fraternity in Ghana. It is conceded on all sides that he was a successful and a distinguished judge and an eminent jurist. His integrity and honesty both in his personal and public life were publicly acknowledged—and in fact he passed the acid test—when he willingly and fearlessly presented himself before Parliament for vetting and approval as Justice of the Supreme Court in compliance with article 127(1) of the 1979 Third Republican Constitution. By his judicial decisions and pronouncements he exhibited the sterling traits of being forthright, candid, consistent, just and uncompromising on principles he believes in, particularly when they have to do with the freedom, liberty and the fundamental human rights of the individual. His concern for the weak, the poor and young offenders, his objection to piecemeal prosecution of a person suspected of crimes as constituting oppression by the State against the individual, his abhorrence of dishonesty, injustice and unfairness are most welcome traits reflected in his numerous incisive and erudite judgments, rulings, articles and public speeches.

He was a judge who had deep respect for judicial precedents but at the same time his lordship did not shy away from rejecting judicial precedents - however hallowed and exalted—if convinced that they were demonstrably wrong: see for instance his landmark decision in Nkrumah v Manu [1971] 1 GLR 176, where his lordship, as a High Court judge, criticised as wrongly decided the Court of Appeal decision in Attiase v Abobbtey (1969) CC 149. And in paying tribute to Justice Taylor, the Review cannot fail to draw attention to his monumental decisions such as Billa v Salifu [1971] 2 GLR 87 and his powerful, well-reasoned and highly illuminating dissenting opinions such as that in Kwakye v Attorney-General [1981] GLR 944. If in Billa his lordship very much enriched our knowledge of Dagomba customary law, in Kwakye he did show deep concern for respect for individual human rights. Equally in his dissenting opinion in the Supreme Court decision in Appiah v The Republic [1987-88] GLRD 88 he championed the rights of the accused against the oppressive State apparatus. As his lordship put it: "we in the traditional courts have been obliged for over a century to strain hard to uphold a balance that will ensure that the accused has justice and the State apparatus is not used oppressively against him."

In recognition of his immense contribution to the development of the law and his rare qualities, the Ghana Bar Association for the first time in its history organised in his honour a farewell ceremony on 30 March 1990. In his introductory remarks, Mr Sam Okudzeto, chairman of the ceremony on that occasion pointed out that in honouring Mr Justice Taylor, the Bar Association was not setting a precedent to be followed on the retirement of every judge. The case of Kobina Taylor was a special one in recognition of his rare qualities and contribution to the development of the law. He stressed that it was only judges who could measure up to such standards that would be honoured in a similar manner. The Review was represented on that occasion and we are happy to present to its readers tributes by the National President of the Bar Association, Mr Anthony Mmieh and the reply by Kobina Taylor.

Tribute by Mr Anthony K Mmieh, National President, Ghana Bar Association

A little over five years ago, I had the pleasure and singular privilege, as the then Vice-President and acting President of the Ghana Bar Association, to stand in for the then National President, Mr Peter Ala Adjetey, SAG, who was then out of the country, and on behalf of the entire membership of the Bar to pay tribute to Mr Justice Fred Kwasi Apaloo, a legal giant, a distinguished jurist and the Chief Justice, who was retiring from the Ghana Bench after 25 years of legal excellence and distinction.

Today, it has fallen on my lot again to perform yet another such task of honouring and paying tribute to another giant in the legal firmament of this country. This time it is Mr Justice J N Kobina Taylor, a justice of the Supreme Court, who retires at the mandatory retiring age of 65, after almost 21 years on the Bench, and having declined to accept an ad hoc extension of one year to stay on. As in all such cases, we do so with mixed feelings. We are naturally sad and disappointed that so soon the judiciary, indeed the people of our dear country, Ghana, has to miss the agile, fertile, prospective and imaginative brain of Mr Justice Taylor. Some of us, until now, were of the erroneous view that Mr Justice Taylor was in his late fifties.

It may be appropriate and opportune at this time to once again call on the government of the PNDC to take another look at its law which enables and gives power to the government to extend the tenure of office of a judge who reaches the compulsory retiring age of 65 for such periods and from time to time as it may deem fit. The Ghana Bar Association has already expressed its views on this law. The Association thinks that this does not augur well for, but rather undermines, the integrity and independence of the judiciary. Is it not possible that a judge who is desirous and wants badly the extension of time may lose his independence and independent judgment in the belief, even if mistaken, that there is a Big Brother somewhere who is watching every step he takes and scrutinises all his legal pronouncements and that to retain his position he must of necessity play ball? Admittedly, such a person is not worthy of being a judge. But, the inescapable truth is that such a possibility exists. The Ghana Bar Association thinks that the government should be called upon to provide and ensure uniform and compulsory retiring age for all judges and that it should put a stop to the selective extension of tenure of judges.

I have already indicated that it is a day of mixed feelings. The brighter and happier aspect of the ceremony this morning is to acknowledge and honour legal brilliance, excellence, scholarship and hardwork. This, we hope, will inspire all other judges, and those who aspire to become one. Shall we say this affords the Association the rare opportunity to pronounce judgments on our judges? It must be said that we do not intend it to become a wholesale affair and a matter of course. We shall not honour a judge because of the personal views we hold about him and only on account of friendship and relationship. It needs to be emphasised that we do and shall do so only on account of legal excellence and distinction. Afterall, the Bible enjoins us in Philippians 4: verse 8 that:

". . . whatsoever things are true, whatsoever things are honest, whatsoever things are just, whatsoever things are pure, whatsoever things are lovely, whatsoever things are of good report; if there be any virtue, and if there be any praise, think on these things."

I must issue the caveat that in honouring Mr Justice J N Kobina Taylor, we are not trying to deify him. Far from that. He is only human. He has his personal shortcomings, convictions and style. We grant him that. It is for his total contribution to the legal development and jurisprudence in this country that we are publicly acknowledging today, and hope that his achievements may be the beacon to other members of the judiciary.

My Lord Chief Justice and honourable members of the judiciary, permit me, and although without meaning any disrespect to any of you, to say that Mr Justice J N Kobina Taylor, in my considered view, is the Lord Denning of Ghana judiciary. I wonder what pair the two of them would have made were they to sit together as judges. May be, it would be like the attempt now being made to compare the exploits of Rocky Maciano and Mohammed Ali in world boxing. It is simply a question of two giants.

Who then is the man Mr Justice J N Kobina Taylor? Shakespeare says that some are born great, some acquire greatness and others achieve greatness. To my mind, it is the last that is true and apt about Mr Justice Taylor. Admittedly, Justice Taylor has a royal blood in him because of his maternal link with the James Town stool of British Accra in the Ga-Mashie state. But he had had to work strenuously and assiduously throughout life to achieve the greatness we all acknowledge in him and have come to take for granted.

Mr Justice Taylor was born in Accra on 24 March 1925. He began his formal schooling in the then Accra United School in 1931 where he stayed until 1936 and left for Nigeria. He was in St Thomas' Catholic School, Kano, from 1937-40 from where he proceeded to St Gregory's College, Lagos, in 1941 up to 1945. He returned to Ghana and went to Achimota College where between 1946-47 he read for Inter BSc (Engineering). The next few years saw him in the teaching profession. Between 1948-50 he was a tutor in Science, Mathematics and Latin at Odorgonno Secondary School, Accra. In 1950 he became and served for two years as Headmaster of the now defunct West Africa College in Accra.

In 1953 he left for the United Kingdom where he attended the University College, London 1956-57 and Council for Legal Education 1954-58. He was called to the English Bar (Lincolns Inn) in 1958. He also experienced life between 1953-55 as a Carriage Electric Lighting Assistant of the British Railways. He returned to Ghana in 1959 and was employed as Assistant Crown Counsel in the Attorney-General's Office and remained in that office until 1969 when he was elevated as a justice of the High Court. He went through the ranks in the Attorney-General's Office and served in various grades as State, Senior and Principal State Attorney and Director of Public Prosecutions. In 1969, he acted as the Acting Attorney-General and Commissioner for Justice. In 1980, after 11 years as a High Court judge, Mr Justice Taylor was appointed as a Justice of the Supreme Court, from where he retires today.

The varied experiences that Mr Justice Taylor went through helped to shape his legal brain, conception, attitude and his contribution to the development of law in our country. No one, even with a casual association with our judiciary, can deny Mr Justice Taylor his due. He is no lazy judge and never tolerated counsel who were ill-prepared and who never studied their brief. He read and was conversant with the case dockets that were placed before him. He believed in clarity of reasoning, and sometimes looks like an advocate rather than a judge. But this no doubt, was to enable him understand better the issues and cases that he had to pronounce upon. To him, there was no small case. All were equally important and he brought zeal, scholarship and hardwork to bear on his judgment, be it a simple marriage custom in Dagomba or the controversial AFRC trials and the provisions of the Constitution, 1979. He is a fearless man who never shies away from expressing his independent views, quite a number of times, in dissenting judgments and in very strong language. He is not the one to compromise on a principle for the sake of convenience or advantage. He hates the abuse of power, totalitarianism and dictatorship in any form. His judgments bear ample testimony to this. He believes in judicial independence and fights for it. Such a man as I have sought to describe, is bound to evoke controversy and turn the apple cart once in a while and Mr Justice J N K Taylor is no exception. It is the combination of all these that have gone to make him the great judge he is.

It is true that legal historians will one day be in a better position to assess Mr Justice Taylor. But for the moment, permit me to quote from some of his judgments and pronouncements. In Mensah v GFA (the Vipers Case) (Supreme Court, 24 January 1989; digested in [1989-90] GLRD 2) he said:

"It seems to me that we have an obligation as custodians of legality, to build in this country and foster in our people, a culture of respect for law and order. When individuals or quasi-state organisations or institutions evince a tendency to avoid and evade the law by recourse to questionable strategies, we must expose their antics and not encourage them to think that it is permissible to have recourse to ingenious methods to pre-empt the requirements of the law. If we fail to do this, the actions of such individuals or institutions will serve as examples to the dishonourable elements in our society and erode the confidence of the law abiding segments of our population in the ability of the judiciary to champion the course of justice."

And in an address delivered at the Annual Conference of the Ghana Bar Association in Kumasi on 4 October 1988, he stated:

". . . in a country like Ghana we must appreciate that sycophancy and pandering to the official view is fast becoming fashionable and often the common past-time of the failures in our society who walk the corridors of power, kowtowing to officialdom, fawning for favours and largesse and fanning the flames of tendentious popular views which they imagine the wielders of power are probably holding. It seems to me that every leader we have had from the indefatigable Nkrumah, to the revolutionary J J have not been isolated from these political parasites, and the downfall of many a leader can be traced to over-dependence on their irresponsible and selfish antics. Perhaps we need an institutional mechanism to protect our national leaders from the dilemma posed by the machinations of these shameless ruffians. This is necessary because by the experience of hind-sight it is possible to know the reality that motivates and is behind these merchants of evil and nation-wreckers. Consider their past exploits in studied sycophancy and their slogan slinging expertise reminiscent of the horrors of the Nazi propaganda machine, note how they attempt to manipulate our leaders: Nkrumah is the Osagyefo who never dies, Busia is the unchallenged champion of our rural folks, Colonel Acheampong is the 13th January man who will catapault us to capture the commanding heights of the economy and more recently, the sonorous and endearing appellation J J was blasphemously translated to Junior Jesus.

We must beware of these elements in our society who will seduce legality and exploit our dilemmas. With their holier than others antics, virtue can easily be denigraded by them. It will then surely degenerate and sustain irreparable damage in the hands of such rascals. The damage they thus cause may take generations to put right, because the popular view, it must be remembered, is not necessarily right and in the so-called old democracies and even in Ghana it is not unknown for popular views to be discredited by the passage of time and the way paved for the right but previously unpopular view." (See his "Dilemmas of Law and Lawyers in Contemporary Ghana" (1987-88) 16 RGL 19 at 30.)

My Lord Chief Justice, in conclusion all that we can say is that he is a great judge and that we are all the poorer for his departure. We are happy, however, in the knowledge that it is at the ripe age of 65 and not by radio. On behalf of the Ghana Bar Association and on my own behalf we wish Mr Justice Taylor his well-deserved earned retirement.

Thank you.

Reply by Hon Mr Justice J N K Taylor

President of the Ghana Bar, my Lords, members of the Bar, Ladies and Gentlemen, I am overwhelmed by all the nice things said about me. After over 30 years of my association with the Bench and the Bar I believe this is the first time a farewell party of this sort has been held by the Bar in honour of a retiring judge. I can think of two instances involving Mr Justice Amissah and Mr Justice Apaloo, but as far as I know the functions in their case were not sponsored by the Bar.

When I was told of this farewell function, I was not told the form it would take but suspecting I may have to say a few things I put pen to paper about 11.30 pm yesterday. If I am to say nothing I will hold my peace, if I have to say something then you will have the result of my effort last night. I had quite some difficulty in selecting a theme, but after reflection, I recall what on 9 January 1986 on the occasion of his retirement from the Judicial Service after a very distinguished career on the Bench, Mr Justice Apaloo CJ said. This provided me with my topic: "Courage and Honesty." On that occasion, Mr Justice Apaloo CJ said, inter alia:

"The one great quality, I would wish to see in my colleagues is courage—that they should be in a position to defend to the death positions that they believe to be right...moral uprightness aside, I cannot think of a more important quality in a judge than this."

(The emphasis is mine.)

I think courage is an essential virtue that must be cultivated by all lawyers whether they are on the Bench or at the Bar. The story one hears of lawyers refusing to take up clients' cases and advising them to give up their rights because their opponent is a powerful government functionary is regrettable and unfortunate. It seems that under a constitutional government it is not difficult to display courage on the Bench or at the Bar especially as constitutional guarantees of judicial independence make it easy to be fearless. It is under military regimes that courage and fearlessness in members of the legal and judicial fraternity become meaningful. In this connection, I venture to think that I am in a unique position to offer some advice because of my peculiar experience.

I have been in the public service of Ghana for more than 30 years and have had the advantage of serving in sensitive positions under all regimes, civilian and military that have administered (I nearly said bedevilled), the affairs of this country since independence. I was Assistant Crown Counsel and later a state attorney under the Nkrumah regime; under the NLC, I was Director of Public Prosecutions and acted as Attorney-General and Commissioner for Justice and thus took part in taking important cabinet decisions; I was senior counsel for the committee that probed the assets of Dr Kwame Nkrumah our first President; I served under the NRC, the Busia regime and the AFRC as a judge of the High Court and I was the Chairman of the committee that investigated the assets of Dr Busia and his ministers. Under the Limann administration and the government of the PNDC I have been a Supreme Court judge from which position, I am now retiring.

I can tell you from the experience I have thus gathered, that in working with military regimes I discovered, what I believe is little known outside restricted government circles that soldiers who execute successful coups and take over governments are very anxious to do the right thing; they often strain hard not to do anything that can be said to be unlawful. It seems to me that the majority of lawyers are not aware of this characteristic of the military mind which in my view can be favourably harnessed to the advantage of this Republic. The result of the failure to grasp this, is that at virtually all the periods of military rule, there would seem to be hostility between the military establishment and the legal profession. But as I brace myself to leave the judicial scene, I feel I should draw attention to this aspect of the soldier-lawyer relationship in the hope that a true appreciation of this may promote better understanding in the future.

I do not think there is any government in the world which can do away with law and lawyers and be a viable regime. The trouble with our country is that some members of our profession have questionable experience and find it difficult to survive in the hurly burly of true professionalism. They make up for their failings by lobbying in the corridors of power for positions they cannot efficiently hold.

They are at times quite articulate although empty and they succeed in convincing powerful persons to sponsor them. They give dangerous advice and cause confusion in the country. I remember in 1973, during the NRC administration, Mr Justice Abban in a commendable display of judicial independence granted an ex parte motion for leave to apply for certiorari to quash the decision of the military tribunal. When the applicant applied for certiorari, the Director of Public Prosecutions raised a preliminary objection to the jurisdiction of the High Court to entertain the motion on the ground that by legislation, namely section 20 of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372) and section 4(7) of the Subversion Decree, 1972 (NRCD 90), the jurisdiction of the High Court had been ousted.

When the preliminary objection was overruled, the government was highly disturbed and in a discussion I had with members of the military, they thought the courts were against them and that the High Court was playing politics and had entertained the application to embarrass the government. I had to explain that the High Court was acting in this manner “to fulfil and not to destroy the law” as was said by Smith J in Balogun v Edusei ((1958) 3 WALR 547). I told them that the court was anxious to find out whether the military tribunal which is a tribunal with limited jurisdiction was operating within the jurisdiction given it by NRCD 90, so that it would be called to account if it was not doing what NRCD 90 enjoined it to do. They were surprised at the explanation as they said they did not realise that that was what the court was attempting to do because they had been given conflicting and contrary advice.

Our hope for the future lies in our building up for ourselves and the generations that will come after us a culture of respect for law and the acquisition of behavioural norms which promote a national approach to the solution of our problems. Man is so superior in the scale of living creatures that by an intelligent and unemotional examination of his problems with a trust in God he can resolve the greater portion of the problems. One instrument forged over centuries by the collective genius of mankind for the solution of human problems is the law; and lawyers and judges are a key to the solution. The abandonment of legality if accepted and indulged in nonchalantly will pose a threat to the orderly foundation of our society. Law—the law that is no respecter of persons is the only bulwark of a happy and prosperous society.

One superior advantage which law has over other rules as authority to be obeyed is that its content is based on a priori foundation and very often has some acceptable moral and equitable ingredients. For this reason it has two very desirable dimensions. Firstly, it is available to be resorted to even before the emergence of the event which requires its application, and secondly its content is generally not outrageous and intolerable and it is consequently neither variable nor capricious.

The superiority of law having regard to its qualitative content has been appreciated by many wise men long before the christian era. In his Politics, the great Greek philosopher, Aristotle gave consideration as to whether law or the rule of the individual is preferable. The answer he gave over 2,000 years ago is worth reiterating for it is refreshing. He said, inter alia:

"To invest the law  . . . with authority is it seems to invest GOD and reason only; to invest a man is to introduce a beast, as desire is something bestial, and liable to be corrupted by passion. We may conclude then, that the law is reason without passion and it is therefore preferable to any individual."

Under the influence of this Aristotelian view, the architect of the great American principle of judicial review was to proclaim in his landmark decision in the celebrated case of Marbury v Madison in 1803, the fundamental ideal of "a Government of laws and not of men."

History is replete with instances in which man and not law had dictated the conduct of citizens in society. An example readily remembered is the monarchical extravagance which produced Magna Carta in 1215 and which ultimately led to King Charles of England losing his head. We see this in many of the oriental states, ancient and modern and quite recently in 1988, in one contemporary manifestation that cannot possibly fail to impress itself on any keen observer of the world scene. I refer to the extraordinary proclivities of the late Ayatola Khomeni who speaking in his personal and presumably religious capacity and apparently contrary to Iranian law and universal moral standards, sentenced in his absence, a British subject Salman Rushdie to death. Rushdie, the writer of the famous book The Satanic Verses is not an Iranian and is neither resident in, nor has any connection with, Iran. Furthermore, he had not been tried in any court, in any country for any offence.

Roscoe Pound in an illuminating article in the Columbia Law Review in 1913, referred to Kipling's description of the court of the oriental despot in a language that pre-enacted in an earlier age, the fantastic and preposterous claim in modern times of the erstwhile leader of the Iranian clergy. As he put it:

"By the custom of the East any man or woman, having a complaint to make or any enemy against whom to avenge has the right of speaking face to face with the King at the daily public audience ...The privilege of open speech is of course exercised at certain personal risk. The King may be pleased and raise the speaker to honour for that very bluntness of speech which three minutes brings a too imitative petitioner to the edge of the ever ready blade.”

You see in Kipling’s description here a concept of law as a capricious instrument for ensuring the paramountcy of individual passion. But law as we have operated it in our courts ensures that the laws regulate the conduct of all in the society without any exception whatsoever. This concept can be traced in Anglo-Ghanaian jurisdiction in its embryonic stage to some of the writings of Bracton. Henry of Bratton (Bracton) was a judge of the Kings Bench in the reign of Henry III. In his great work entitled: De legibus et constitudinibus Angllae, there runs an apt quoted passage:

“The King himself ought not to be subject to man, but subject to GOD and the Law, for the Law makes the King. Let the King then attribute to the law attributes to him, namely dominion and power, for there is no King where the will and not the law has dominion,”

Bracton’s words ring true today and through the ages as they were when he first wrote them. Thus when on one Sunday morning, 10 November 1608, King James I, in a discourse with all the judges of England and the Barons of the Exchequer, maintained that it was treason to entertain the idea that he was under the law, Lord Coke, the Chief Justice, in the celebrated case of Prohibition del Roy (See Proclamation (1611) 12 Coke 74, 76) contradicted the monarch and quoted Bracton’s words: Quod rex debet esse sub homine sed sub des et lese.

It is worth noting that individuals like Coke have in the exercise of judicial power blazed a bold trial characterised by intellectual integrity and an inflexible faith in law as a safeguard of human happiness. As a judge, Coke came face to face with executive powers of the highest possible order. He made it abundantly clear even to James I, in a decision in the well-known case of Proclamations that in spite of his being King of all Britain, nevertheless he could not by Proclamation “make, a thing unlawful which was permitted by the law before.” (See 7 Coke (1609), 63-65.) His basic philosophy reflecting the Bracton’s tradition in this landmark decision in 1611 was that the King had no prerogative but that which the law and the land follows.” And he insisted that the judges and not the King were the only authorised interpreters of the law. (See Nicholas Fullers Case (1608) 12 Col 41.) Such spirits do not survive autocracy and demagogy and so Coke’s career as a judge came to an abrupt end when true to his conscience, he gave as it were a bold and fearless dissenting opinion in the best tradition of the exercise of judicial responsibility in the case of Commendans in 1616. (1616 Hobert 140-166)

In that case the question which was put before all the judges in England, was whether in a case pending before them, which the King thought “to concern him either in person or profit,” they could be required to stay proceedings till the King could consult with them. With the exception of Coke all the judges answered “Yes.” Coke’s answer defying fawning sycophancy and in the best exercise of judicial power under law was that “when that case should be, he would do that which befit for a judge to do.” Shortly after this answer in 1616 Coke was removed as Chief Justice.

To us in Ghana, this case must be reminiscent of a situation admittedly not exactly parallel, but somewhat not dissimilar which occurred here in our court almost 350 years after the experience of Lord Coke. On 9 December 1963, in the Special Criminal Division of the High Court here in Accra, the court presided over by Korsah CJ with van Lare and Akufo-Addo JJSC, acquitted three persons: Tawiah Adamafio, Ako Adjei and the late Cofie Crabbe of a charge of conspiracy to commit treason contrary to sections 23(1) and 180(1) of the Criminal Code, 1960 (Act 29) (see State v. Otchere [1963] 2 GLR 463). It is apparently the consensual view of the legal profession that the accused persons were acquitted upon legally sound and unimpeachable grounds. By the Special Criminal Instrument, 1963 (EI 161), however, the acquittals were, inter alia, declared null and void and re-enacting Coker’s experience of 1616, the Chief Justice and his panel members were removed as judges.

The reason for the removal of Korsah CJ and his colleagues as seeped through to us at the Law Office at the time, was that the Chief Justice should have consulted the President, Dr Kwame Nkrumah, for the sake of the security of the State before acquitting the accused: and so the well-known aphorism that history repeats itself found fulfilment on our shores in an echo of the 1616 experience of Lord Coke. One can only hope that our own judges in the world of Coke like Korsah, van Lare and Akufo-Addo have set examples for us which we of this generation must emulate and transmit to the generation of lawyers who will come after us. This is one advice which I wish to pass on to my brethren on the Bench and my professional lawyer colleagues at the Bar.

Before I am done, I wish to seriously make mention of a matter which we should endeavour never to gloss over if we aspire to win the confidence of the Ghanaian public in our brand of justice. There are many persons who believe rightly or wrongly that we sell justice in our courts to the highest bidders. Quite clearly, at times this auction accusation is made by irresponsible litigants who have lost their cases and are smarting under a misconceived grievances; but there surely are also great number of persons who have not lost any cases and who nevertheless make like accusations. This pervasive charge of corruption needs to be tackled boldly and honestly. I am unable here and now to offer a solution, but I think its ramification should be firmly appreciated so that it can urge us to think out solutions.

In Ghana, the average person who pays for a favour in litigation informs his close friends “in confidence” of what he has done. The close friends as well inform their close friends of what has happened, also “in confidence”. The tale empemistically called rumour repeatedly told “in confidence” continues and progresses through a wide spectrum of the society and the alleged taker soon gets an ignominous name. He ends up with a notorious reputation and people shake their head and carry on a whispering campaign any time his name crops up. Ultimately his evil deed does catch up with him, and as night follows day he finally pays the price when he least expects to do so.

I think the maxim “honesty is the best policy” has stood invoilable for thousands of years and our judiciary and legal profession should be guided by it. It is the most important side of the judicial function and that is what Justice Apaloo CJ had in mind when in the quotation of his to which I made reference at the commencement of this address, he daubed “Moral Uprightness” as the most important quality in a judge.

I thank you.

The Review proudly salutes the Hon Mr Justice J N K Taylor as one of the outstanding and eminent judges of Ghana. His departure from the Ghana Bench (but happily not from Ghana legal scene) is undoubtedly an incalculable loss not only to the Ghana legal profession but also to the Commonwealth and other countries exercising common law jurisdiction. The Review and all the staff of the Council for Law Reporting wish him a very happy retirement and long life.
