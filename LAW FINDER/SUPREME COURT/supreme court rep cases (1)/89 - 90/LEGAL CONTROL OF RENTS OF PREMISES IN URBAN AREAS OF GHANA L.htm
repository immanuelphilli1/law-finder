LEGAL CONTROL OF RENTS OF PREMISES IN URBAN AREAS OF GHANA: LESSONS AND PROSPECTS* [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 118—137

Kotey Nee-Ashie  E.

 

*

INTRODUCTION

Concretising the right to shelter has been one of the central  issues of development.  Most countries are or have been to varying  extents, confronted with this problem.  In the rapidly urbanising  countries of the developing world - with their rapidly increasing  populations - provision of affordable housing has assumed particular importance. Various measures have been adopted towards this end.  In some countries massive programmes of public housing have been undertaken. Several planning and housing finance measures have also been adopted. Another widely used device has been the introduction of rent control schemes. 

This article examines the attempts by the law maker to control the rents of premises - particularly residential accommodation through the instrumentality and processes of the law.  In this article we shall specifically be concerned with the control of rents of premises in urban Ghana.  Our focus will therefore not include the rural areas.  This is because the nature of the housing sector and the rental situation in the urban areas is substantially different from that in the rural areas.  The Living Standards Survey,1 showed that whereas about 60 per cent of households in Accra and 46 per cent of households in the other urban areas lived in rented accommodation only eight per cent of households in the rural areas lived in rented accommodation. It must also be pointed out that we shall be dealing specifically with housing rent.  We shall therefore not be concerned with rents of business, commercial and industrial premises which may not raise the same issues as housing rent.

A system of rent control involves a legal limitation of the amount of rent which landlords can lawfully charge and receive from their tenants. This is often complemented with provisions designed to offer greater security of tenure to tenants by restricting the circumstances under which a landlord can lawfully evict a tenant. This is because the legal restrictions on levels of rent are unlikely to be effective if a landlord retains all his common law rights to recover possession of his premises.  For example, at common law, a periodic monthly tenancy (which according to Alameddine Bros v Paterson Zochonis2 is the type of tenancy commonest in the country) may be terminated by a month's notice.  In a situation of housing shortage a tenant's ability and resolve to insist on paying the legally stipulated rent is likely to be very weak if he can be ejected after just a month's notice.  Section 17 of the Rent Act, 1963 (Act 220) therefore provides that in addition to terminating or being entitled to terminate at common law a landlord can only eject a tenant if one of the conditions specified therein is satisfied.3  These include non-payment of rent, breach of a lawful covenant, acts or omissions of the tenant constituting nuisance or annoyance, waste or neglect, and reasonable requirement of the premises by the landlord for himself or a member of his family. The termination of leases and the eviction of tenants is a complete subject in itself and nothing more will be said about it.4

Rent control has been resorted to in situations of housing shortage-in situations where the demand for housing outstrips supply.5  This has been the situation in urban Ghana at least for the last half century.6  Rent control is therefore an attempt to deal with aspects of, or ameliorate the full impact of the housing situation on the tenant by insulating him against the rent which would otherwise be determined by market forces. It is therefore an instrument of social policy.  In Ghana it has also had what Killick describes as an egalitarian objective, ie a mechanism for protecting the urban poor.7  Before embarking upon an examination of the legal attempts to control the level of rents of premises, and the lessons that can usefully be drawn from the Ghanaian experience, we will locate the problem in context by outlining the nature of the housing situation and the rental accommodation market in urban Ghana.

The Housing Situation

The first point that must be noted about the housing question in urban Ghana is the rapid increase in the population of the country in the last half a century or so. The population has increased from 4,118,000 in 1948 to 6,726,615 in 1960 to 8,559,313 in 1970 and to 12,296,081 in 1984. It is now estimated at about 14 million.  The annual rate of growth of the population is now estimated at about 3.2 per cent. Even more phenomenal is the increase in the number of people living in the urban areas. The number of people living in urban Ghana (defined by the censuses as a town of 5,000 or more ) rose from 538,000 in 1948 to 1,551,000 in 1960 to 2,472,456 in 1970 and 3,934,796 in 1984. Thus by the definition employed in the 1984 census, 32 per cent of the population lived in urban areas.

A look at the growth of some of the major cities and towns  may even be more instructive.8  Between 1960 and 1984, the populations of Accra and Kumasi more than doubled, while that of Tamale increased three-fold.  An examination of the causes of this rapid rate of urbanisation goes beyond the compass of this article.9  Suffice it to note that, it is due to the general population growth caused by a continuing high fertility rate and a considerably reduced mortality rate, and rural-urban migration caused by socio-economic development biased in favour of the urban areas.10  The population of the urban sector is estimated to be growing at an annual rate of four per cent while that of the rural areas is estimated to be growing at a rate of 2.6 per cent. 

A second factor is the situation of housing scarcity in the urban areas.  The rapid growth in the urban population coupled with inadequate resources for housing provision and bad planning and therefore a slower rate of growth in the housing stock has led to a shortage of housing in the urban areas.11  This has been attested to by Busia in Sekondi-Takoradi,12 by Acquah in Accra13 and the social survey of Kumasi carried out in 1960.14  In 1954-55, when a United Nations Technical Assistance team visited Ghana to examine the housing question the population of Accra was approximately 162,000 and the number of households 26,260.15  There were 2.71 households per house and the average number of rooms per household was 1.79.16  A comparison of the 1948 and 1960 census figures shows that the number of persons per dwelling increased from 10.5 to 18.5 in Accra, from 10.4 to 21.3 in Kumasi, and from 13.6 to 18.3 in Takoradi.  The 1960 population census also showed that more than a third of the population lived twenty or more to one house and that there was a 25 per cent inter-censal increase in the number of persons per house.  The 1970 census showed that of 172,087 houses in urban Ghana, 88,488 (about 50 per cent) housed more than ten people, and 24,312 (about 20 per cent) housed more than 20 people.  The 1970 census also showed that the number of persons per house was 15.7 in Accra, 22.1 in Kumasi, 12.06 in Tamale, 20.3 in Takoradi and 6.06 in Tema.  It further showed that the number of households per house in urban Ghana was 3.423.  The 1984 population census showed that the average number of persons per house was 15.1 in Accra and Nungua, 15.6 in Teshie, 20.9 in Kumasi, 9.5 in Tema, 13.9 in Tamale and 17.4 in Sekondi and Takoradi.  It was estimated that 70,000 housing units were needed in the country in 1986 just to keep pace with population growth.   There is an accumulated national deficit of 250,000 housing units,  that for Accra-Tema alone being 41,000.17  The present annual requirement is 100,000 housing units while current production is about 28,000.  About one-third of this requirement is in the urban areas. 

A third contextual factor is the size and importance of the rental accommodation market.  The preliminary report of the Living  Standard Survey, 1988, shows that over 60 per cent of households  in Accra rented their accommodation while less than ten per cent  are owner occupiers.  For the other urban areas of the country  the figures are about 45 per cent and 20 per cent respectively.18  A significant proportion of households in the urban areas therefore  rent their accommodation. 

Another feature of the housing situation and rental accommodation market is that about 80 per cent of the housing stock is built and owned by private individuals.  Only about seven per cent of the housing stock is publicly owned.19  Attempts to boost  housing production and to bring a measure of public ownership and participation into the housing sector through the activities of  public bodies like the State Housing Corporation, Tema Development  Corporation, SSNIT, REDCO, APPROTECH, etc have had little effect20 on what remains an essentially private and free enterprise activity.  Most landlords are therefore private individuals.  The attempt by some of these public bodies to enter into the housing sector and particularly the rental accommodation sector has been stultified by mismanagement, incompetence, inefficiency, corruption and other economic and socio-political factors.21

A further significant factor is that the housing finance sector is not developed.22  There is therefore not much assistance to the housing sector.  Those who engage in building have to finance their operations largely from private resources. The few who are able to attract some funding from the formal financial sector have to pay about the same rate of interest as loans for trading and other commercial activities.  Such loans, are at any rate, often inadequate to finance the whole project.  Building is therefore an essentially private activity funded largely from private and informal sources.

Another contextual factor - which is highlighted by the figures on the number of persons and households per house, is that at the middle and lower ends of the residential accommodation market, tenants do not rent a whole house or a complete flat.  These tenants rent a room or two in a house - the other rooms being occupied by other tenants and/or the landlord.  Utilities like kitchen, bathroom and toilet facilities are shared.  Preliminary results of the Living Standards Survey, 1988 show that 85.4 per cent of tenants in Accra and 65.5 per cent of those in other urban areas occupied less than a complete house or flat;23 and research conducted in Kumasi in 1980 showed that 70 per cent of households occupied just a single room.24

From this, admittedly brief, survey of the outlines of the housing situation and the rental accommodation market in urban Ghana, the picture that emerges is one in which population growth and urbanisation has led to quite a severe housing crisis, as the rate of housing production has failed to keep pace with population growth and urbanisation.  Equally important: it is clear that private individuals are still the key players - attempts at socialising the market through public building initiatives having largely failed.  It is against this contextual background that we shall examine the attempts to use the law to control rents. 

Attempts at Legal Rent Control

Attempts at controlling the level of rent through the processes of the law was introduced into this country with the enactment of the Defence (Rent Restriction) Regulations of 1942 when war time conditions shot up the levels of rent and the prices of other commodities. Since then the output of the legislative machine in this area has been enormous.  But to what effect? Two main approaches have been adopted by the law-maker since independence.  The first has involved the assessment of rent of individual premises based on a number of factors by rent officers.  Under this approach the rent control legislation itself does not specify any rent for any type or class of premises.  The second approach has involved the legislative fixing of standard rents for particular types or classes of premises particularly at the middle and lower end of the rental accommodation market.  The first approach is represented by the Rent Act, 1963 (Act 220) which has been the parent rent control statute since 1963. 

Act 220

Act 220 applies to all premises25 except government buildings and leases of SHC, TDC and similar premises.26  Act 220 is therefore of general application in the sense that here is no limitation on the value or type of premises, the part of the country, or the income of tenant to which it applies.

Act 220 attempts to control the level of rents by providing criteria by which to determine what rent can lawfully be charged—the recoverable rent.27  Generally, Act 220 provides for assessment of the recoverable rent when there is disagreement between the landlord and the tenant.  An application for assessment may be made by the landlord or tenant to a rent officer.28  The Act also makes provision for applications and assessments by the Minister.29

In the assessment of the recoverable rent, certain factors must be taken into account.30  These include the rateable value of the premises, the value of the land on which the premises are situated, the amount of annual rates or a proportionate part thereof, the estimated cost of repairs or maintenance of such premises, the current interest rate charged by the banks, the obligations of landlord and tenant, the amount of recoverable rent for like premises and the justice and merits of each particular case.  And in one case the Court of Appeal held that the process of assessment must be a judicial process.31   So both parties must be heard, each must have the right to cross-examine the other and his witnesses. They must have access to and the right to comment upon documents and information which are used in the assessment.

Act 220 also provides that it shall be a criminal offence for any person to demand or receive more than the recoverable rent notwithstanding any agreement to the contrary.32  It further makes it a criminal offence for any person to demand more than one month's rent advance in the case of a monthly tenancy or more than six months' rent in advance in the case of a tenancy of more than six months as a condition for the granting, renewal or continuance of a tenancy.33  The phrase "as a condition for the grant," has meant that this provision is often circumvented.  The demand or receipt of goodwill—a practice quite common in commercial leases - is also made a criminal offence.34

Act 220 also requires that periodic monthly tenants be provided with rent cards within seven days of the commencement of the tenancy.35  It further requires that written receipts are issued by landlords for payments of rent made by a tenant.36

The machinery for the enforcement of Act 220 centred around the rent officer.37  The rent magistrate38 and other law enforcement agencies also help.39 The assessment of rent is, in the first instance, performed by the rent officer.40  Appeals from the decision of the rent officer lie to the rent magistrate,41 i.e. the district magistrate of the locality.42  The decision of the rent magistrate is final though it is subject to appeal on questions of law.43

This then was the position of the law from 1963 to 1973.  The law generally failed to control the level of rent.  A different approach was therefore adopted.  This involved the legislative specification of fixed, standard rents for various types and classes of premises.

NRCD 158

The first piece of legislation to adopt this approach is the Rent (Amendment) Decree, 1973 (NRCD 158).  This Decree was not of general application.  Unlike Act 220, it applied only to the cities of Accra, Kumasi and Sekondi-Takoradi.44  It also applied to "residential premises" only.45  Perhaps, partly because not much attention was paid to this Decree, the judicial decisions do not indicatethat lawyers and the courts got involved in the interesting issues that would have been involved in determining when premises have been rented for residential purposes only in a country where many people like traders, bakers, kenkey sellers, tailors, hairdressers, etc carry on their business or part of it on the premises where they reside.  The Decree was also targeted at the lower end of the residential accommodation market.  It defined premises as a single room of the type and size specified in the First Schedule of the Decree, and a tenant as a person whose income did not exceed ¢1,000 and rented a single room only.46  Under section 1 and the First Schedule of the Decree the rent specified for various types and sizes of room were as follows:

Sandcrete or Landcrete:                   12ft x 12ft         =       ¢7.50 per month

"                       "                        12ft x 10ft         =      ¢6.50   "    "

"                       "                        10ft x 10ft         =      ¢5.50   "    "

Wooden or swish                        12ft x 10ft         =      ¢4.50   "    "

Iron Sheet                                  12ft x 10ft         =       ¢3.50  "    "

The Decree further provided that where the dimension of the room rented varied from those specified in the First Schedule a rent proportionate to that payable for the specified size and type was payable.47  Contravention of any of the provisions of the Decree was made a criminal offence.48  NRCD 158 therefore amended the provisions of Act 220 in only a limited number of cases and applied to the cities only. 

AFRCD 5

The Rent Control (Amendment) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 5) largely continued the approach adopted by NRCD 158 by fixing standard rents or upper limits for most premises.49  But it went further. Unlike NRCD 158, AFRCD 5 was general in application.50  It applied to the whole of the country and to all residential tenants, irrespective of their income or the type or size of premises they were renting.51  The Decree provided that the rent chargeable for different types of premises in different parts of the country shall not exceed that specified in the Decree.52  The figure for one room accommodation with shared amenities in the regional capitals and Tema was ¢20 per month and ¢16 per month for other parts of the country.53 The rent payable for a "two-roomed self-contained terraced house" shall be ¢150 per month or 27 pesewas per square foot which ever is lower;54 for three-roomed self-contained semi-detached ¢175 per month or 30 pesewas per square foot which ever is lower55; and for a three-roomed self-contained detached house ¢200 per month or 34 pesewas per square foot which ever is lower.56  The rent for self-contained houses of more than three bedrooms was to be negotiated by the parties who shall refer the matter to the rent officer in case of disagreement.57  Again contravention of any of the provisions of this Decree was made a criminal offence.58 

It must be noted, however, that though AFRCD 5 fixed the upper limit of rent chargeable it afforded much more leeway to rent officers to determine the recoverable rent - using the upper limits as guidelines  than existed under NRCD 158.  Things continued very much as before with the law and the people largely dancing to different rhythms.

PNDCL 5

The next significant piece of legislation in this long-running saga is the Rent Control Law, 1982 (PNDCL 5). It applied to residential premises only.59 Though it adopted the second strategy noted above for the lower end of the rental accommodation market, perhaps in tune with the legislative mood of the times, its approach was revolutionary.  It started by slashing rents of all residential premises except those below ¢20 per month to half of that payable as at 31 December 1981.60  It also provides that rent for one room should not exceed ¢20 per month61 and for a chamber and hall ¢50 per month.62  Where the rent payable for premises exceeds ¢1,000 a month the landlord is to pay 50 per cent of the said rent to the state as tax.63  Section 6 of the law was quite revolutionary.  It provides that contravention of the provisions noted above rendered the landlord liable to forfeit the premises to the state.64  The law also requires that every landlord shall issue each tenant with a rent card65 and further that no action or complaint by a landlord should be entertained if the said rent card has not been issued.66

PNDCL 138

The last in this fairly long line of legislation aimed at controlling the level of rents of residential premises is the Rent Control Law, 1986 (PNDCL 138). 

This law combined both approaches by specifying fixed standard rents for the lower end of the market67 and providing for assessment of rent of other accommodation.68  For the lower end of the market it provides that the rent payable for one room of a size of 12ft x 10ft with shared amenities shall be:69 (a) sandcrete ¢180, (b) landcrete ¢140; and (c) swish ¢120.  Rent for rooms (generally known as chamber and hall) of say 12" x 10" per room, shall be70: (a) sandcrete ¢250, (b) landcrete ¢200, and (c) swish ¢180.  Where the dimensions of the rooms are larger or smaller than that specified above, a proportionate rent shall be paid.71  The secretary in charge of housing may by legislative instrument amend these provisions.72 As usual criminal sanctions attach to infringement of these provisions.73  The provisions relating to the issuing of rent cards and the registration of tenancies are retained.74  A landlord who has not registered his tenancy shall not demand or receive rent.75 

Until the enactment of PNDCL 138 the machinery for the enforcement of rent control has been that established by Act 220, ie through rent officers, rent magistrates and other law enforcement agencies, like the police and the courts.  PNDCL 138 departs from this.  It provides for the establishment of rent and housing committees for districts which are specified in the Second Schedule to the law.76  A rent and housing committee shall consist of one representative of the local CDR, a rent officer, a representative of landlords in the district, a representative of tenants and two other persons of integrity and good standing appointed by the District Assembly.77

The functions of the rent and housing committee include the hearing and the determination of (a) allegations of breach of covenant or any law in any landlord-tenant relationship, (b) applications for assessment of rent, and (c) applications for ejectment of tenants or recovery of possession.78  For the purpose of assessing the amount of recoverable rent the rent and housing committee is to take into account the same factors that the rent officer is required to consider under Act 220.79  Decisions of the rent and housing committee are to be enforced through the public tribunal system.80  Any person aggrieved by a decision of the rent and housing committee may appeal to a community or district public tribunal.81  The law seeks to oust the supervisory jurisdiction of the superior courts over these committees.82

Critique

The second approach - ie the fixing of rigid standard rents or the specification of upper limits is considered by some as a better or certainly more suitable and effective method than the first approach which, it has been noted, proceeded on the basis of assessment and determination of rent for individual lettings.83  It has been argued that the specification of rigid rents or fixed upper limits simplifies the enforcement of the rent control mechanism because it enables tenants to know what the landlord cannot lawfully charge.84 

This approach is however questionable.85  It tends to be too rigid.  Rent is legitimately determined by a number of factors which may vary from tenancy to tenancy.86  Thus though two premises, houses, flats or rooms may be of the same size and type, they may be in different neighbourhoods or locations, may have different amenities, or be in different states of maintenance or repair or may be on different floors.  Various leases and agreements may also offer varying degrees of security to the tenant.

Secondly, some of the pieces of legislation which adopted that approach introduced too many categories and distinctions.87  But the study of the operation of law in society demonstrates that distinctions between different classes of cases inevitably makes the law difficult to apply.  This is because arguments about borderline cases arise and, the provisions of the law can be avoided or thwarted by the simple expedient of moving out of the specified category.  For instance, NRCD 158 was to apply only to tenants who rented a single room and had an annual income of not more than ¢1,000.  First, the policy objective of the requirement that the tenant should be renting just a single room is not clear. Secondly, determining what is one-room and more crucially, whose income does not exceed ¢1,000 was unlikely to be easy in all situations.88 Again, PNDCL 5, as has been noted, provided that where a landlord charges more than ¢1,000 a month for premises then he shall pay 50 per cent of the said rent to the state as tax.  A landlord who charged ¢1,500 would therefore only end up with ¢750.  It therefore meant that a landlord who wanted to charge anything between ¢1,000 and ¢1,999 was better off charging ¢1,000 in which case he was not liable to this tax.

The strongest criticism of the standard rent approach, however, is that it is out of step with the concrete realities of our economic situation.  Although most of these pieces of legislation provided for periodic reviews of the amount of rent specified and the law can generally be amended, most of the specified rents were not reviewed for considerable periods of time.  Thus, the First Schedule of NRCD 158, which came into force in 1973, was not amended until the Decree was repealed in 1979.  During that period inflation rose from an annual rate of 17 per cent in 1973 to 73 per cent in 1978.  From 1976 to 1978 the annual rate of inflation was more than 50 per cent.  Furthermore, from 1973 to 1978 prices in the economy as a whole increased twelve-fold and yet landlords of premises controlled by NRCD 158 were still required to charge a fixed specified rent.  Between 1979 and 1981, when AFRCD 5 was in operation, the rate of inflation averaged more than 50 per cent per annum and the consumer price index for these years shows that during the period prices increased more than three-fold.  Again, landlords were required to charge not more than the rents specified in AFRCD 5.  Between 1982 and 1986 when PNDCL 5 was in operation the consumer price index indicates that prices within the economy increased more than three-fold.89  And yet again rent of certain types of premises were not to exceed certain specified upper limits which were not varied during that period.  And since 1986 when PNDCL 138 came into force prices have about doubled.  But the rent of certain types of premises remain the same.  When law is obviously ridiculous people tend to ignore it and feel justified in doing so.

This then has been the history of the legislative attempt to deal with the rent element in a complex and multi-faceted housing question.  These legislative efforts have not been entirely successful in achieving the objectives for which they were enacted.90  To the extent that relief for tenants through the holding down of rents and greater security of tenure has been the primary aim, the spate of legislation and the present crisis in the housing rental sector - indeed the whole housing sector - is proof enough that all is indeed not well.

Indeed, it is arguable that these laws have aggravated the situation by discouraging investment in the rental housing sector.91  The rate of return on investment  particularly at the lower end of the rental accommodation market is so low as to eliminate investment in that sector as a purely commercial proposition.92 It is also likely that the maintenance and repair of the existing housing stock has been adversely affected by the legal controls.93  Furthermore, some landlords have withdrawn their premises from the rental sector particularly because of the immense difficulty involved in lawfully securing the eviction of a tenant.

Lessons and Prospects

There has been a rather misplaced confidence in the miraculous capacity of law to solve problems and achieve policy objectives.94 Measuring the effectiveness or impact of law is, of course, no easy matter.95 As Woodman reminds us law may have no simple, single and well-defined objective.96  The effect or impact of a piece of legislation may also be direct or indirect, intended or unintended. The time-frame within which the effectiveness of law is judged should also be chosen very carefully.  One should not evaluate the effectiveness of a piece of legislation within too short a period of time.97 

Having entered all these caveats, however, we think it can legitimately be asserted that these laws have been largely unsuccessful in controlling the level of rents.

Admittedly, one reason for this has been the inadequacy and ineffectiveness of the machinery and bureaucracy provided for the enforcement of the law.  But, it is submitted, the more important  reasons - the underlying causes - are to be found in the nature of the housing situation in the urban areas, the mode of housing production and technology, and the characteristics of the rental housing sector. The general economic and social situation characterised by fairly high levels of inflation, high interest rates, and shortages of building materials have adversely affected the housing industry and created a situation in which the tendency for non-compliance has been enhanced. Corruption on a fairly widespread basis and disenchantment with and alienation of a political, social and economic scheme of things which has largely failed to satisfy the aspirations of most people has further undermined their confidence in the law and hence, their will to obey.98  Law, it has been observed, is a confidence-trick.90  In these concrete circumstances legal attempts at controlling the level of rents is unlikely to be successful. 

Where then do we go from here?  There would seem to be no blue-print or magic wand.  Indeed, a central thesis of this paper has been that the law does not provide any magic solution and - presumably "lawmen" are not magicians.  The housing question in urban Ghana is a complex multi-dimensional problem.  Admittedly, it has legal aspects - particularly those relating to land tenure and administration and its rules relating to access to land, security of tenure, mortgages, estate administration and compulsory acquisition of land, planning and zoning.  But it also has economic, demographic, social, planning, architectural and technological dimensions.  At the end of the day it is only an increase in the housing stock and therefore an improvement in the housing situation that offers any hope - and that would require concerted and intensive, integrated multi-disciplinary action.  Legal palliatives designed in the abstract to deal with one aspect of this complex problem are unlikely to make much of an impact.  Law, like 'lawman,' has its limitations.
