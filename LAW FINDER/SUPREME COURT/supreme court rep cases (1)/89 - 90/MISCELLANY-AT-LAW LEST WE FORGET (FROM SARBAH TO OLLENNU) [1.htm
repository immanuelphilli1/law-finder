MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: LEST WE FORGET: (FROM SARBAH TO OLLENNU) [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 32—42

 

By Mr E D Kom, FGA, SAG

Rudyard Kipling, the great English poet, whose mortal remains now lie in Westminster Abbey, London, wrote a poem “lest we forget.” This poem found its way into the Methodist Hymn Book as No. 889. In this poem, Rudyard Kipling reminded his fellow Englishmen and women of some facts of history they should always learn to remember.

In the days of the Israelites, the prophets from time to time reminded "the chosen people" of certain facts of history and they always prefaced their message with: "thus sayeth the Lord of hosts."

We have long bearded prophets going about in multi-coloured flowing gowns but who do not seriously perform functions as their predecessors in title. In this day and age, the duty of reminding people of their responsibilities is done sometimes by advertisement, eg “Grow what you eat and eat what you grow.” Another example is “slow down” "change to bottom gear," "remain in bottom gear," "thank you,” which one finds when descending the Kwahu Hills. I would like to remind the general public and particularly, the members of the learned profession of certain historical facts "lest we forget."

I feel this must be done and I am supported in this feeling by what is contained in St Luke's Gospel as follows:

". . . Woe unto you also, ye lawyers! for ye lade men with burdens grievous to be borne, and ye yourselves touch not the burdens with one of your fingers.

Woe unto you! for ye build the sepulchres of the prophets, and your fathers killed them.

Truly ye bear witness that ye allow the deeds of your fathers: . . .

Woe unto you, lawyers! for ye have taken away the key of knowledge: ye entered not in yourselves, and them that were entering in ye hindered."1

Dick Butcher in Shakespeare's play Henry VI suggested that the first thing they must do was to "kill all the lawyers" In Thomas More's UTOPIA, lawyers were conspicuously absent.

A story is told of a father who took his son to a cemetery on All Souls Day. The son saw an inscription on a tomb: "Here lies the remains of a christian and a lawyer." The boy asked the father why are two people buried in the same grave? No doubt, the little boy could not think of a christian being a lawyer or a lawyer being a christian.

Many of our people think like the little boy and it is a wide-spread belief that if a lawyer dies, he is put into the coffin face downwards so that he cannot look at his maker in the face. I held this view until as national secretary of the Ghana Bar Association. I was invited to see a colleague, the late Myles Abadoo of Cape Coast put into his coffin.

This erroneous view about lawyers is from time to time watered by virulent attacks on them by a section of the press and individuals. An interesting one was by Sergeant Alolga Akatapore, published in the West African magazine in an interview when the sergeant, then a member of PNDC was returning from Moscow, USSR. He said: "All lawyers are rogues and thieves except a few ones.” I do not know whether the sergeant was quoting from the Talmud where lawyers are referred to as "oppressors and robbers." In view of the foregoing statements about lawyers, I feel, like Rudyard Kipling, that I must remind my countrymen and women of certain facts lest we forget. Are lawyers in fact nothing but obstructionists or the bad people some say they are?

I would like to start from Sarbah and end with Ollennu or from 1900 to 1987. Sarbah has a Hall of the University of Ghana, Legon, named after him, and long before that, Mfantsipim School, true to its motto "Dwen Hwe Kan," "think ahead" named one of the houses after him as far back as 1930. Who was this Sarbah? And what did he do to merit such recognition?

Fortunately for us there are two good and readable books on John Mensah Sarbah: Gold Coast Men of Letters, by Magnus Sampson published in 1937 and the other, John Mensah Sarbah, his Life and Works, by the Honourable Mr Justice Samuel Azu Crabbe, published in 1971. If Mr Justice Azu Crabbe had the opportunity to erect a tombstone for Sarbah I think his inscription would be: "Here lies a lawyer, an educationist, a patriot, a nationalist and a legislator" because in his treatise he put the life and work of Sarbah into these compartments.

This compartmentalisation of the life and works of Sarbah was adopted by the Honourable Mr Kojo Botsio, then Minister of Foreign Affairs, when on behalf of Osagyefo, Dr Kwame Nkmmah, he declared open the Hall of Residence in Legon named after Sarbah. I believe I am in good company if I look at Sarbah's life and work through the same spectacles. The first remark I would like to make is that Sarbah saw the acquisition of knowledge of law not as an end in itself but as a means to an end, ie service to mankind in whatever situation he found himself.

He readily came to the assistance of the Wesleyan Boys’ High School when the headmaster threatened to close down the school. He re-named the school "Mfantsipim" and designed the school's motto "Dwen Hwe Kan." Whenever the school ran into financial difficulties he assisted by paying the salaries of the staff from his own pocket. He bought a pipe organ for the Wesleyan Church in Cape Coast in memory of his father and brother.

The late Opoku Acheampong, a lawyer, emulated what Sarbah did. He also bought musical instruments for the Presbyterian Church, Akropong Akwapim and built from his own resources the Okuapeman Secondary School. Dare any lawyer do any of these things today without the fear of being labelled by the local press as "hardworking and successful," and being served with an assets declaration form to appear before the Citizen's Vetting Committee, now Office of the Revenue Commissioner the next day because of his life style?

As a jurist, Sarbah wrote Fanti Customary Laws in 1897 and later Law Reports. These works have since their publication been referred to by the courts, in some cases the statement of law was adopted, in others it is said to be too wide a proposition. Later textwriters like Danquah, Ollennu, Bentsi-Enchill and also Kludze have all used it as a primary source material. No writer on our customary law can do without Sarbah's Fanti Customary Law as a starting point of his thesis and this will be so for a long time to come. With the efluxion of nearly a century, we may not now agree with everything Sarbah wrote and this is what it must be.

In 1906, as a nationalist, he published the Fanti National Constitution to demonstrate that the crown colony rule contravened a fundamental principle of British traditional justice and fairplay and to encourage his people to study their own history and institutions. He therefore, traced "the broad outlines of Akan-Fanti communities and explained the principles controlling and regulating them.”

As a patriot his memorable achievement was his opposition to the enactment of the Lands Bill of 1897. The purpose of the proposed law was to vest in the Crown all waste lands, forest lands and minerals. Waste land was defined as a "land of which, for 30 years before the commencement of the ordinance, no beneficial use had been made for cultivation or habitation or for collecting or storing water or for any industrial purpose."

Lest we forget, may I draw your attention to a recent law, Compulsory Letting of Unoccupied Rooms and Houses Law, 1982 PNDCL 7 s 1 which provides:

“Any unoccupied room in rental premises or unoccupied house whose owner refuses to rent the said room or house or whose owner cannot be found after reasonable efforts have been made to trace him, shall be identified and rented under this Law to persons who do not already have a place to live in.”

In February 1895 the editor of the Gold Coast Independent newspaper described the proposed Lands Bill of 1897 as "a thin end of the wedge" and therefore said if the Bill was passed into law “it would be a flagrant violation of the most elementary principle of justice, a despotic use of power, a crucial wrong and an unjustifiable oppression of the people in this country.”

Sarbah prepared a strongly worded petition for the chiefs and people of Cape Coast which they sent to Her Majesty’s Secretary of State for the Colonies. Sarbah’s opposition to the Lands Bill of 1897 led to his formation of the Aborigines’ Rights Protection Society (ARPS). The chiefs and ARPS briefed Sarbah as counsel for a fee of 400 guineas appear on their behalf before the Legislative Council, Accra, and to argue their case of opposition to the Bill. This was followed by a delegation of three, J W Sey, T F E Jones and George Hughes to present the objection of the chiefs and people and ARPS to the Secretary of State for the Colonies at Downing Street, London.

In August 1898, Her Majesty’s government bowed down to the objection of the chiefs and people to the Bill. Sarbah before then wrote a letter rejecting the 400 guineas counsel’s fee, and I quote a paragraph of that letter written to the Vice-President of the ARPS:

“I do not spurn or refuse the very handsome retainer of four hundred guineas, but in serving my country, the land of my birth, within her borders, I seek no reward; nor expect any remuneration; and did I ever dream of any recognition for such humble services which I have performed, the fact that, at such a crisis my countrymen selected me to plead their cause, is in itself a solemn honour which will not be unremembered or unappreciated by me. I shall always treasure the confidence which in this instance, my countrymen have reposed in me.”

Many people had written or spoken about this kind gesture of Sarbah and I, therefore, beg to add my voice. If Sarbah had been a rotarian, I would have said he observed the rotary principle of “Service above self.” The 400 guineas was used in purchasing a printing press which published the Gold Coast Aborigines and the Gold Coast Nation.

Sarbah was the first native of the Gold Coast to be enrolled as a barrister, others before him, like George Blankson, James Brew and Charles Bannerman, were solicitors. Sarbah served his profession, his people, his country and his church. No one was therefore, surprised, or begrudged him when he was awarded the most distinguished order of the Companion of St Michael and St George (CMG). He, as it were, set the pace in all sphere of human endeavours for his contemporaries and generations of lawyers yet unborn to follow.

Some contemporaries of Sarbah took the challenge seriously. J E Casely Hayford in his books Gold Coast Native Institutions published in 1903 and United West Africa published in 1919, dealt with traditional institutions as having great merit, sound and firm basis upon which a colonial superstructure can be erected.

Traditional institutions, according to him, must not be obliterated as “primitive and backward.” Joseph Ephraim Casely Hayford, the man of vision and faith, was the topic for the 1975 J B Danquah memorial lectures by Professor F H Ofosu Appiah. It was the eighth in the series organised by the Ghana Academy of Arts and Sciences.

In those lectures he painted the late J E Caesly Hayford as a nationalist, politician, patriot, a scholar, statesman and last but not the least as a Pan-Africanist. Casely Hayford, as a member of the Legislative Council and as the editor of the Gold Coast Leader from 1902-30, used the Council debates and his articles in the press to advance the cause and ideals of Pan-Africanism. He saw a vision and had faith in what he saw.

From Sarbah and Casely Hayford one must naturally go to Peter Awoonor Renner, the author of Renner’s Law Reports, a work which enjoyed great prestige but less known and used today. When municipal council elections were introduced, lawyers were the leaders of the Ratepayers' Party and Maambii Party. One remembers with delight names like Hutton Mills, Frans Dove, Kobina Sekyi, Kojo Thompson and Dr J B Danquah who were all active participants in the politics of their time.

It is therefore not surprising that in Busia's parliament there were 35 lawyers out of 140 MPs and in Limann's there were 28 out of 140 MPs. As a professional group lawyers in the past dominated the politics of this country. Not because lawyers were power hungry or because they had arrogated to themselves the divine right to rule, as is being alleged these days in certain circles. Lawyers are trained to defend the rights and liberties of the individual. As long as there continue to be lawyers in this country any attempt to trample upon the rights and liberties of the people will find them in opposition. This is so, not only in Ghana but also in other parts of the world. In other spheres of human endeavour the presence of lawyers is very much felt even in the church. This reminds me of Dr J B Danquah in whose memory the Ghana Academy of Arts and Sciences has instituted an annual lecture.

Dr Danquah has been appropriately described as the "Doyen of Gold Coast politics." In his life he strove single handedly and fearlesssly for the maintenance of the rule of law and the observance of human rights. The judgments of the Court of Appeal in the cases of In re Okine,2 In re Amponsah3 and In re Dumoga,4 where Dr Danquah referred to 35 authorities as far back as 1758; and In re Akoto5 in which his arguments were rejected, is a living and fitting memorial to him. It is ironical that he suffered and died in detention without trial, the evil he stood against in our society.

May he rest in peace.

Despite his active involvement in politics he had time to write extensively on many subjects such as the African History and Culture. Dr Danquah also had time for legal practice and it was a great joy for us as junior members of the Bar to hear him in the Supreme Court crossing swords with his arch rival, Mr Geoffrey Bing QC, the then Attorney-General. His authorities were brought in suit cases from his own library.

Please, permit me to tell you of an incident. Barely three months after my enrolment, I  found myself appearing against Dr Danquah in a land appeal case before Mr Justice Ollennu. Mr Ollennu called Mr Elegba, the stenographer and dictated his judgment. I  lost. In the afternoon when I came to chambers, Mr Bentsi-Enchill called me and told me that Dr Danquah had telephoned him to tell him that even though I lost the appeal my performance was very good indeed and that I should keep it up. Few are the seniors who would congratulate a junior when they are the winners and the junior, the loser. Such was Dr Danquah that he gave honour where honour was due.

I think I would be ungrateful if I do not mention the founder of Naoferg Chambers, Kwamena Bentsi-Enchill, a lawyer of great distinction, author of Ghana Land Law, a Supreme Court Judge, Commissioner for Stool Lands Boundary Settlement Commission and Professor of Law in the University of Zambia.

His past pupils in Naoferg Chambers are Kingsley Nyinah, E N Moore, the late Chief Biney, the late Sampson Baidoo, G K Andoh, the Ombudsman, A W Acquaah and I happened to be his last pupil in chambers when he left to take up an appointment as a lecturer in the Ghana School of Law under the directorship of Professor Lang in 1959. After his long sojourn in America and Zambia he came to deliver the Danquah Memorial Lectures in 1971 intituled Institutional Challenges of our Times.

In those lectures he, like J E Casely Hayford, was of the view that our indigenous institutions can be used as the basis for a democratic government. He was perhaps the first protagonist of finding a democratic system that is peculiarly Ghanaian which other African countries may copy.

As a justice of the Supreme Court he will be remembered for his dissenting judgment in Adwoa Pokuaa v State Insurance Corporation [1973] 1 GLR 335 where he attacked and condemned the name driver clause in motor insurance policy. Little did anybody know that he would die as a result of a motor accident caused by an uninsured road contractor.

All that I have been trying to do is to point out that lawyers in the past, advanced frontiers of knowledge, they were leaders of thought and they played a conspicuous role in all spheres of human endeavour.

Let's not allow the era of these great men to come to an end. I am thinking of such great men as Nii Ama Ollennu, who in recent times was an author, writer, honorary professor of the University of Ghana, Fellow and past President of the Ghana Academy of Arts and Sciences, patron to a host of voluntary organisations, eg Boys Scout, Girls Guides, Deaf and Dumb, Red Cross Society. Above all he was an elder of the Presbyterian Church of Ghana and a lay preacher. He was one time President of the Christian Council of Ghana and the Director of the Ghana School of Law.

In Accra two circles are named after lawyer Danquah and Obetsebi Lamptey, two Halls of Residence of our universities are also named after lawyers, Sarbah and Casely Hayford. Of the BIG SIX of the late forties, with the exception of Kwame Nkrumah, the rest were lawyers, and it is even said of Kwame Nkrumah that he was enrolled as a student in Lincoln's Inn and sat his Roman Law paper.

Like Rudyard Kipling, I want to draw attention to the events of that era from Sarbah to Ollennu lest we forget.

Please, forgive me if I sound like a prophet of doom. I think, and I may be wrong and I humbly stand to be corrected, that we are not doing enough. We have not shown that excellence the lawyers in the past have shown. We are now not leaders of thought in the country. We are not exhibiting that caring attitude for the man on the Nima tro tro (bus) nor championing the cause of those disadvantaged and under disability. Last but not the least one does not nowadays see what the late Akufo-Addo termed "sartorial elegance" on the part of lawyers.

In recent times allegations and accusations of fraudulent financial deals, involving large sums of money have been made against some lawyers, resulting in serious and embarrassing consequences to some of them and shall I say to the profession as a whole. As usual the mass media gave these stories the maximum publicity. Ten or fifteen years ago a lawyer could say like Samuel:

"Behold, here I am: witness against me before the LORD, and before his anointed: whose ox have I taken? or whose ass have I taken? or whom have I defrauded? whom have I oppressed? or of whose hand have I received any bribe to blind mine eyes therewith? and I will restore it to you.

And they said, Thou hast not defrauded us, nor oppressed us, neither hast thou taken aught of any man's hand."6

The practice of law in the English speaking world is described as a "learned and noble profession." Before and after the introduction of the Institutes by Justinian, the practice of law by the "jurisconsults" of Rome was a learned and noble affair. It was in order to preserve the dignity of the practice of the law that on 17 January 1969 the Legal Profession (Professional Conduct and Etiquette) Rules, 1969 (LI 613) was passed and signed by Edward Akufo-Addo and E S Aidoo. Rule 4(1) of the rules provided:

"A lawyer shall not practice unless he is a member of professional chambers or the pupil of such a member."

Membership of professional chambers under the rules has been clearly defined as:

“(a) having the lawyer's name exhibited at the chambers and that lawyer having the right to use the chambers for the conduct of his practice; and

(b) that lawyer having the services of the clerk who is the clerk of the chambers.”

To me this means a fixed place of business where the processes of the court can be served on a lawyer. What we now have is Mobile Chambers. In some lawyers' brief cases are letterheads, rubber stamp and receipt books and they operate from anywhere especially from the open garages of the Cocoa Affairs Courts.

If the late Akufo-Addo were to visit the Cocoa Affairs any morning to see how legal practice is conducted his heart would bleed. The Cocoa Affairs Courts and their precincts are like the STOCK EXCHANGE MARKET where money changes hands openly and at incredible speed. Surely, we have fallen short of rule 9(11) of LI 613 which provides that: "it is the duty of a lawyer to maintain the honour and integrity of his profession."

The juniors complain that seniors are not willing to take them in chambers and seniors complain that there are no vacancies; so we have "The egg and the chicken" problem, ie vicious circle. If the biologists have not yet found a solution to the problem of the egg and the chicken, that which came first, the Bar Association must find a solution to the problem of mobile chambers because it is affecting the “honour and integrity” of the profession as a whole.

A client saw me at the Cocoa Affairs and asked me "lawyer, do you also come here?" When I replied that I was going to Mr Justice Alhaji Ammah's court the client said again, "If there is a High Court here then it must be removed." My Lord, the Chief Justice, whilst the Bar Association finds a solution to the mobile chambers may I appeal to your Lordship to move the two High Courts from Cocoa Affairs.

LI 613 talks of the honour and integrity of the legal profession and yet one finds a lawyer moving several applications for letters of administration prepared by court clerks and letter writers who collect the bulk of the fees and give the lawyers ¢1,000 per motion and he accepts it. As if this is not bad enough, when the judge asks a question, the lawyer begins to look for the client whom he sees for the first time, or the lawyer quickly goes to consult the letter writer. Thank God, not many lawyers engage in this practice but I think the honour and integrity of the profession should be maintained. I am not advocating monopoly but if court clerks and letter writers know how to prepare letters of administration, then by all means they must move the application in court.

Mr Chairman, My Lord the Chief Justice, in view of the limited number of good chambers, poor library facilities in chambers and at court buildings and the delay in the publication of Law Reports, may I humbly suggest that continuing education for all lawyers in private practice should be seriously considered.

There should be symposia, workshops, lectures and seminars organised for lawyers by the Bar Association and attendance should be made compulsory for all lawyers in private practice. Failure to attend a minimum number of times should attract a sanction to be imposed by the Bar Association. To this end, I further suggest that the Conference of the Bench, Bar and Faculty should be revived. This used to be an annual affair held early in the legal vacation at Legon. Compulsory continuing education for practitioners is a sure way of improving the services we offer or render to the public.

I, speaking for myself, heartily welcome the creation of the degree and status of Senior Advocate of Ghana. My fervent prayer and hope is that the awardees may not consider themselves as retired army generals who have no more battles to fight. They must be “still pursuing, still achieving.” They must set high professional and ethical standards for others to emulate.

In this way the introduction of SAG will be beneficial both to themselves and all concerned. I, therefore, respectfully suggest that it be laid down that every Senior Advocate deliver an inaugural address at such time and place as the Council of the Bar may determine. By this other members of the profession may benefit from the vast experience they have acquired from their many years of practice at the Bar.

The question I want to pose is, should we acquiesce in the era coming to an end or are there lawyers who are prepared to take up the challenge from where others have left off?

"Let us all be up and doing with a heart for any fate. The lives of these great men remind us. That we can make our lives sublime and departing, leave behind us footprints on the sands of time."

Yes, Sarbah, J E Casely Hayford, Peter Awoonor Renner, Hutton Mills, Frans Dove, Kobina Sekyi, Kojo Thompson, Dr J B Danquah, Bentsi-Enchill, Akufo-Addo and Ollennu have left indelible footprints upon the sands of time. Lastly:

"The tumult and the shouting dies, the captains and the kings depart, still stands thine ancient sacrifice, a humble and a contrite heart, Lord God of host, be with us yet, lest we forget—lest we forget."
