THE ACTION FOR BREACH OF PROMISE TO MARRY IN GHANA: NEW LIFE TO AN OLD RULE [1993-95] VOL XIX 41—68

MENSA-BONSU H J A N

 

*

INTRODUCTION

The common law action of breach of promise to marry was part of the received law of Ghana. It remains a part of our received law, although it has rarely been used.1 For many years, it had been considered disreputable and has since been abolished in England and Wales.2 The agitation to abolish that action has a long history. Before the 1930s, the agitation for the abolition of this cause of action had begun in England and the United States of America. This led to a spate of abolitions in many states of the Union beginning from 1935.3 It remained discredited and by the time it was abolished in England, it had such few friends that it died a quiet and unremarked death.4 In other common law jurisdictions, efforts have been made not to abolish it, but to reduce its negative aspects by giving it a statutory basis.5 Predictably, neither of these efforts has had any influence on the issue in Ghana.6 Therefore, the action as it exists in Ghana, is essentially a reflection of the original common law position.

In the heated debates that preceded the abolition in both the United States and England, the argument was put forward that the action for breach of promise to marry served no social purpose other than to reward "gold-diggers.”7 It was said that many women used the threat of that action to procure money from men who fell into their traps. If the man did not want the embarrassment of a law suit he would try to avoid it by agreeing to a  [p.42] settlement. Hence, the images that have been floated around are those of the vicious and avaricious young woman setting out to entrap and extort money from a well-meaning and respectable man who had made a mistake of judgment. The argument further suggested that the virtuous woman who had been truly wronged was too decent to expose such intimate aspects of her life to the glare of publicity whilst the one who did not want the match, anyway, was enabled to cash in on the rules.8

Writing with passion, most contemporary writers threw mud on the plaintiffs and whitewashed the defendants. The very essence of the cause of action was questioned since its remedy lay solely in damages.  It was thought to be unthinkable to decent people that damages could be regarded as adequate compensation for one who had genuinely lost a marriage match. With poetic fervour, one writer railed that:

"The action . . . applies the most prosaic of remedies to the most sentimental of complaints. The ashes are weighed on the cold altar after the sacred flame has gone out. Tender confidences, whispered protestations, the passionate phrases of love letters, all the mysterious signs and symbols which love dotes upon are carefully put together . . . to establish a transaction as though the wooing of the human heart were like bargaining for a pair of lungs. From this phase of life's tragedy, poets and romancers turn with a shudder. But to such sufferers as seek the courts our common law imparts a consolation which ought at all events to expel the last lingering passion from the breast of the suitor.”9

Lest these sentiments be deemed to be the product of the male imagination, the fact must be noted that many of the leading abolitionists in the state legislatures in the United States were female.10 Feminist theorists also believed that the notion of female-dependency upon which the action was premised was repugnant to the emerging image of the woman, independent of, and equal to, the man.11 For this reason, they supported the efforts to abolish the action.

Although this cause of action survives in Ghana, it has been put to very little use. This may be because the prospect of announcing that one has been jilted, must send fear through the hearts of most Ghanaian women and wound [p.43] the pride of most Ghanaian men. Yet, many fact-situations indicate that this cause of action could be a useful device in Ghana. A recognition that these fact situations merit the application of this action would not be setting the clock back as far as the status of the Ghanaian woman is concerned. On the contrary, it would be an indication that women are more confident about taking their destiny into their own hands and protecting their interests when their rights are infringed. It would be as well to remember that although women tend to be the plaintiffs, it is not an action reserved for women.12

The purpose of this article is therefore to discuss the action and the modern uses to which it could be put within the Ghana legal system. To this end, the first part of this article deals with the nature of the cause of action itself at common law; the second part discusses the treatment of the action in the Ghanaian authorities and the policy issues therein; the third part tackles the possibilities that exist within the social system for the vindication of rights by this cause of action; and the final part concludes the discussion with suggestions which may be of use to the Ghanaian courts and practitioners of the law.

I. THE COMMON LAW ACTION

The cause of action known as breach of promise to marry has murky antecedents. It is unclear whether it is a contract or a tort. Some writers proceed upon the premise that it sounds in contract law.13 The Ghanaian courts are of this category. Others maintain that it is a tort.14 For others still, it is a hybrid because it straddles both branches of the law.15 Certainly, the various camps assign good reasons for their positions. However, it does appear that owing to the fact that each side emphasises the aspects most reminiscent of either tort or contract, the nature of the action has become even more confused. It would seem that the differences in opinion are dictated by whether one is looking at its constituent elements, or its effect. As one writer points out, there is no real cause for disagreement because "[I]t is ex contractu in form but ex delicto and penal in nature and substance."16 Therefore what it really is, is not of much importance.

[p.44]

Elements of the Action

(i) The Promise

There must be evidence supporting a mutual exchange of promise.17 The marriage promise may be proved by a verbal promise of marriage.18 The promise may be conditional, in which case there must be evidence of fulfilment of the condition before an action for the breach would lie.19 Where there is a general promise of marriage, an intention to perform within a reasonable time would be imputed.20

(ii) What Constitutes a Breach?

An action for breach of promise to marry arises when a person makes a promise of marriage to another and refuses to perform. The refusal could be by conduct as evidenced by marriage to another,21 or by an express refusal upon a request for performance.22 The refusal could be communicated to the plaintiff herself or himself, or to a third party for the purpose of being communicated to the plaintiff.23 When, unknown to the promisee, the promisor is already married, or marries another after the promise has been made, the breach arises by reason of the fact that the promisor has no capacity to contract another marriage because of a subsisting one.24 An action for anticipatory breach may also be brought for the repudiation of a conditional promise even before the fulfilment of the condition precedent.25 Where a breach is established, damage is presumed.

As in a regular contract, the promise could be held to be unenforceable if there is evidence that it was made in circumstances that raise issues of public policy. Therefore, where the promisee knows that the promisor is already married, the promise would be unenforceable, even if intended to take effect after the death of the promisor's spouse.26 The law finds repugnant the idea [p.45] that another would be waiting in the wings, and, presumably, hoping and praying for the death of the promisor's spouse. Questions of immorality apart, who knows what crimes could be spawned by the seeming longevity of that spouse?

The promise is equally void if, either party being married, the promise is conditioned upon the divorce of either party.27 Yet again, it is clear that such an agreement would encourage divorce between a couple who probably could, without the interference of third parties, work out their marital problems.28 Although this rule enables the defendant from taking advantage of his or her own wrong, ie protects the promisor from being sued, it disables the plaintiff from benefiting likewise.29 This result can be justified on the ground that a contrary position would be tantamount to enlisting the aid of the courts in prosecuting an immoral claim. Therefore a person who enters into such an agreement bears the risk of the other party reneging on it after the divorce has been obtained at that party's urging. Thus such a promisee would be denied a claim if the promisor exploits the situation to obtain his or her freedom and thereafter, marries another. However, where the promise is renewed after the divorce, it would support the action.30 At least that would support the theory that a party without marital ties may properly make such commitments.

(iii) Defences

Owning to the nature of the action, there are very few defences once there is evidence that a promise of marriage was made. Whilst it is a defence to show that the marriage would have been against the law,31 it is no defence to show that the marriage would have been unhappy if the marriage had taken [p.46] place because the promisor had fallen out of love with the promisee.32 Indeed, even where there is evidence that the plaintiff had no affection for the defendant, it would only go in mitigation of damages.33 The disapproval of the promisee by the promisor's family is no defence,34 nor is the disapproval of the promisee's family by the promisor.35 In other words, not liking a party's family is no reason to renege on the promise to marry.

Other factors that operate as defences include the plaintiff's unchastity, illness of the defendant and an agreement to release in consideration of payment of compensation, ie an agreement to pay off the promise. The unchastity of a plaintiff is a defence36 because the nature of the relationship under contemplation is such that the defendant is entitled to expect chastity from the plaintiff. However, it operates as a defence only if the fact is concealed from, or unknown to the promisor at the time he makes the promise.37 Where the unchastity is known to the defendant, it only goes in mitigation of damages.38

Neither the physical nor the mental illness of a plaintiff or a defendant constitutes a good defence.39 The only exceptions to this are when the [p.47] defendant's illness is of such a kind that there is the possibility of death within a short period of time,40 or it is dangerous and contagious in nature.41 Infirmity of a permanent kind such as epilepsy,42 or impotency,43 or injuries44 have been held to be only mitigating factors.

(iv) Damages

Where an action is upheld, damages are at large45 and are awardable in three categories—compensatory, aggravated and punitive damages. All these are awardable at the discretion of the jury,46 or by the judge in the jurisdictions where jury trials for civil cases have been abolished. In consequence of the jury discretion at common law, the factors influencing the award have developed on a case by case basis. Before the action was abolished critics alleged that factors such as the looks of the plaintiff often played a role in the measure of damages. Thus a plaintiff who was good-looking, it was said, would be granted huge damages, but "if she (was) unattractive, they [the jurors] often [had] a sneaking sympathy for the man”47 and would consequently award lesser amounts.

There are various heads of damages that could be classified as compensatory: mental distress,48 loss of reputation,49 loss of economic advantage that [p.48] the marriage would have conferred,50 social disgrace, injured feelings, expenses incurred in preparation for the wedding,51 pecuniary loss arising out of the engagement such as employment given up in anticipation of the marriage,52 loss of opportunity for contracting another marriage because of the engagement to the defendant53 (particularly if it was a long engagement), loss of future prospects of marriage54 (particularly if something has happened to render her undesirable as a wife for another), injury to health55, etc. All these are awardable to put the plaintiff in the position she or he would have been in, if the agreement had been performed.56 In addition to compensatory damages, aggravated damages may also be awarded for the improper conduct of the defendant before the breach. Where there is evidence that the plaintiff was seduced as a result of the engagement, it aggravates the damages. So also does the fact of non-disclosure of venereal disease on the part of the defendant.57

The defendant’s conduct during the trial of the suit may also be the subject of the award of aggravated damages. Unfounded allegations of unchastity is treated as “adding insult to injury.” Thus, although the unchastity of the plaintiff is a defence if unknown before the promise was made,58 the defendant raises it at his own peril.59 If the allegation is not proved, or if found to be wholly untrue, the damages awardable would be aggravated. The reason for this is that the defendant would have shown himself to be, not only dishonourable in trying to wriggle out of an obligation, but also dishonest in attempting to justify his behaviour by attacking the plaintiff’s character.

A court may also award punitive damages. However, punitive damages are not awarded often unless the defendant has shown himself to be a thorough rascal, either by his motivation for the engagement, or in the [p.49] manner in which it was broken. The usual grounds for awarding this genre of damages are fraud and malice. In Drobnich v Bach,60 the man's motive in getting engaged was to enable him seduce the plaintiff and therefore the whole engagement was fraudulent. This was also the case when a man entered into an agreement to marry in order to have access to the woman's money.61

Evidence of malice is also a factor that calls for the award of punitive damages. The malice could relate to the manner in which the breach occurred, or the motivation for entering into the match. In Trammel v Vaughan,62 the match had been entered into for the defendant's vanity. He had done it to show his friends that the plaintiff was accessible to him and that he could marry her if he chose to. So malicious was this motive that the court thought it was an appropriate case for the award of punitive damages.

In awarding punitive damages for the manner of breach, the rationale is that certain modes of behaviour in communicating the breach are unacceptable. Going out of one's way to cause pain when one is also in breach of an agreement is evidence of malice. This is because a decision not to go ahead with a planned marriage, if made in good faith, would be made with the knowledge that it would cause pain. Therefore, evidence of "wantonness and ruthlessness and an intention to unnecessarily injure feelings,"63 makes a good case for the award of punitive damages.

Damages may however be mitigated by the operation of certain factors. The rationale for this is that although breaching such an agreement is indefensible in all but the most serious situations, there are certain factors that render the conduct of the defendant less reprehensible than it would otherwise be. The fact is therefore acknowledged by mitigating the damages. For instance, a plaintiff is entitled to claim damages for loss of reputation. However, where the plaintiff had little by way of reputation, this fact has to be reflected in the measure of damages.64

Again, owing to the fact that this action is based on a failure to marry, the plaintiff's lack of feeling for the defendant is a material fact that goes to mitigate the damages. Mount v Boget65 is illustrative of the point under discussion. The defendant alleged that the plaintiff did not care for him. He alleged this because the plaintiff had told him that he looked like "a Maypole with an apple stuck on top and like a liberty Pole with an umbrella on it"; [p.50] that he was soft in the head; a fool; "a double-tongued Dutchman" whose company she was ashamed of; that he was "good for nothing except making money"; that she "would not have him if he was worth the whole of New York or if every hair on his head was hung with diamonds." In consequence, the trial court instructed the jury that if she truly held these opinions, then she had escaped a curse and not lost a benefit.66

Other factors such as the violent opposition of the plaintiffs family to the match67; infirmity of a permanent nature of the defendant,68 or the plaintiff69; disease of a genetic kind in the plaintiff's family of which the defendant becomes aware after the engagement,70 etc. In general, it is fair to draw the conclusion that anything that would render the plaintiff an unsuitable spouse would be admitted in mitigation of damages.

Rationale for the Cause of Action

The rationale for permitting such a cause of action at common law lay in the conception of marriage in the Anglo-Saxon society. Marriage was a sacred duty and therefore a failure to honour a promise of marriage was a serious matter. In those days, marriage was a spiritual matter and under the jurisdiction of the ecclesiastical courts. The ecclesiastical court would therefore compel a man who had made such a promise, to go through with it.

When the ecclesiastical courts were abolished, jurisdiction passed to the common law courts which could award only damages as remedy.71 With the inability to order specific performance, a different rationale for sustaining the action was needed. Justifying the award of damages, the court held in Baker v Smith72 that:

"[T]he engagement to marry is not merely (sic) a spiritual matter and this action is not to compel [sic] marriage upon the contract, but to recover damages for not doing it ... and here is a temporal loss [p.51] and therefore a temporal action doth lie.”73

In thus perceiving the failure to honour the promise not just as a spiritual injury but also as a loss capable of being measured in damages, a firm basis had been laid for the intervention of the common law. In what then, lay the woman's loss?

The loss lay in the fact that marriage represented economic security for a woman. It carried social status since it was considered the most honourable estate for a woman in a patriarchal society. Virginity was also a prized possession because: "[A] woman who was no longer chaste had lost something both of psychic value and of value on the marriage market. She was 'damaged goods.'"74 To be deprived of these valuables by a faithless lover was therefore a quantifiable loss for which the award of damages would be appropriate.

Another justification for the action was that it enabled a woman who had been seduced, and who had borne an illegitimate child, to bring claims against the father of the child. Since at common law an illegitimate child was not entitled to the support of the father, it became a convenient means by which the rules could be circumvented. The damages thus obtained became a means by which the mother could maintain herself and her child at a time when the labour market was virtually closed to women.75

Furthermore, where the change of mind had occurred so late that expenses had been incurred towards the preparation for the wedding, someone had to bear the loss. Sometimes substantial outlay of expenditure would have occurred because the plaintiff would have purchased a trousseau and other things which would be of use only if the marriage had taken place. These situations raised legal issues as to the proper party to bear the loss. It was not fair to let it lie where it fell since it would lie too  heavily on the party who had done nothing to create the situation. For all the above reasons, therefore, it made sense to consider the breach an actionable wrong.

Any Use in Modern Times?

Much of the criticism that has preceded abolition efforts have centred on the negative aspects of the action. It has been blamed for creating a market in extortion. It has also been derided because the fact of appearing to soothe wounded feelings with money has been thought to be ridiculous."76 Yet again, [p.52] the image of the economically-dependent woman is considered an affront to the gains that the feminist movement has made in this century.

All these criticisms have some merit, but they are not unique to this cause of action. One of the biggest problems in insurance is fraudulent claims. Yet no one suggests that insurance should be abolished because of it. As to soothing wounded feelings and compensating loss of reputation with money, actions for defamation do no less. For those worried about the image of women, this action does nothing that alimony suits do not do. The economically independent woman who does not depend upon marriage for social status is still very much in the minority. In any case, depriving a person who has been treated this way of a cause of action leaves a big gap in the law. Why should people whose deepest feelings have been trifled with, have no recourse when a bruised toe would be actionable under the same system of law?

Critics of this cause of action also claim that the action works unfairness to defendants since it proceeds upon the assumption that the marriage would have been an advantage to the plaintiff. Since the defendant could have been a bad husband or wife, or the marriage could have been unhappy, the argument goes, the plaintiff may have been saved from much unhappiness. This may be true but the same presumption underlies all other contracts. The law has never operated upon the assumption that the breach of an obligation may have, in fact, been in the plaintiff's interest. At all times, the relevant question is whether one party to an agreement voluntarily entered into, is refusing to perform. Furthermore, the criticism is promised upon such speculation that the law would be better off not embarking upon such an inquiry. Who is to know whether a seemingly irresponsible man would not mature into a model husband and vice versa?

The value of the engagement, as a period of transition to the married state, has also been set up as a reason for discouraging liability for breaking it off. It is said that the couple get to know each other better and so marriages that would have been unhappy are averted. Whilst this holds some truth, the possibility of a suit would also be a check on those who are apt to take advantage of the relationship during that period. It is the defendants who have abused the engagement by initiating sexual activity and enjoying other benefits attached to marriage, and not the plaintiffs who have sought redress for such abuse, that deserve criticism.

An assessment of the cases from some of the Commonwealth jurisdictions indicate that this action served a real purpose even if liable to abuse. It is true that those cases of extortion that created social anxiety and led to efforts to abolish the action did not end up in the law reports. However, those that did were worthy of intervention by the law even if the motives for bringing the suit were not honourable ones. There is no evidence that even in business, motives for taking legal action are always honourable ones and strictly for business reasons only. Since when has the law concerned itself with the [p.53] plaintiff's motivation rather than the existence of an actionable wrong?

For the feminists whose sense of propriety is violated by this cause of action, there is good reason why its continued existence should not be frowned upon. There is nothing wrong with the law playing referee, even among equals. Despite the battle for the equality of the sexes, the fact remains that in matters of sexual morality, women are judged more harshly than men. Therefore a woman who constantly changes partners does not command as much respect, or marriage value in society, as one who changes less frequently. Certainly this is true of most African societies. Again, marriage is still prized among African women although it may not represent the economic security that it once did. Persons who remain single beyond a certain age implicitly lose their seniority in the extended family to younger ones who are married; and therefore to be denied the opportunity is to have been denied something of extrinsic worth. It is also true that generally a woman's virginity does not command the premium that it once did on the marriage-market, and therefore there is little question as to "damaged goods", but this is not altogether true of muslim societies. Although motherhood outside marriage is not frowned upon as severely as it used to be in European societies, it is not the ideal state of respectability either. It is also an undeniable fact that it decreases one's chances of ever getting married. Can it seriously be argued that a woman who suffers this because of a broken promise of marriage has suffered no wrong for which the law should provide a remedy?

Whilst the payment of damages would not restore lost reputation, it would be more of a check on those who lead other people on and then abandon them at will, or desert them when the material benefits have been exhausted. It is an undeniable fact that keeping a woman engaged for a long time and jilting her deprives her of other prospects of marriage that she would have had. Furthermore, there can be little argument about the emotional injury that is suffered and the scars that are left behind by the fact of having been jilted. There must be a legal recourse for such behaviour. It is the existence of a cause of action that signals the law's disapproval of such behaviour as unacceptable. In the absence of such an action, one writer observes:

". . . the man may use his immunity from suit as a means of injury, the scope of which is only limited by his own and his counsel's imagination. His whole courtship may be insincere, his promise to marry only a subterfuge to obtain meals, money, presents, services in his business, and even sexual relations."77

For those actions founded on the deception of the plaintiff by an already married defendant, the memorable words of Denning LJ (as he then was) in [p.54] Shaw v Shaw78 should leave no doubt that this action is as respectable in the law as any other:

"Every man who proposes marriage to a woman impliedly warrants that he is in a position to marry her and that he is not himself a married man; and he reaffirms that warranty when he afterwards goes through a form of marriage with her ... To take the familiar words of the banns of marriage, he warrants that there is no 'cause or just impediment' why he should not marry her. Every day thereafter of their married life he continues the warranty; he warrants that their marriage was valid and that there was no impediment to it."79

Couched in the language of warranties, it is clear that a person who proposes to sue on the warranty or take advantage of this cause of action is no worse than is any other person seeking recourse in the courts.

The usefulness of this action is further demonstrated by the fact that even in those States in the USA where it has been abolished, it has become necessary to so interpret the statute that abolished the action, that "ineffectual marriages"80 have been thereby excluded.81 This would not have been necessary if the action had not been abolished by statute. However, ineffectual marriages are not the only situations deserving of legal protection.

In those States where it was not abolished, it has shown its strength even in a post-industrial society such as exists in the USA. Bradley v Somers82 illustrates the point here about the fact situations that require judicial intervention. The parties met at a time when both of them were married to other people. They agreed to divorce their spouses in order to get married. A date was set for the wedding and the woman incurred a lot of expenditure in preparation for the event. During the wedding ceremony, the groom refused to sign the marriage licence on the ground that he was in love with someone else. The priest tried to reconcile them but failed. Consequently, he was obliged to announce to the wedding guests that the wedding had been [p.55] cancelled. The plaintiff subsequently brought this action, claiming damages for embarrassment as well as for nervous shock. She also claimed special damages for the expenses incurred in the preparation for the wedding, and for psychiatric care. Would a system of law that had no response to a fact-situation such as occurred in the instant case be worthy of its name?83 Whichever way one looks at it, this cause of action has suffered the "Give-a- dog-a-bad-name-and-hang-him" syndrome.

II. THE GHANA EXPERIENCE

As already stated, the cause of action still survives in Ghana. It was raised in the High Court in the case of Acheampong v Acheampong84 where the plaintiff, the customary law wife of the defendant, brought action for a breach of promise to marry her under the Marriage Ordinance.85 Taking a preliminary objection, counsel argued that at customary law a wife did not have a right of action against a husband and that even if she did, she could not maintain such an action. No authorities were cited to support these propositions.86 The court presided over by Amissah JA overruled both grounds of objection. He stated that:

"The incidents of a marriage under the Ordinance are quite different from those in a customary marriage. And if a woman has given consideration for the promise to transform the one under custom into the other under the Ordinance, it would be harsh to stop her from recovering for the breach."87

Beyond acknowledging that a cause of action might exis, his lordship did not go further. However, he stated explicitly that public policy would defeat the action but that he needed to hear more arguments on this score.88

The next case to take this up was Afrifa v Class-Peter.89 This was an [p.56] appeal against the award of damages for breach of promise to marry the respondent. The evidence as to whether there had been a valid customary marriage was somewhat unsatisfactorily assessed. However, the trial court concluded that the parties were merely engaged under customary law and therefore the action was the appropriate one. This was affirmed on appeal. It appears that despite the finding that the formalities of the customary marriage had not been completed, the court was anxious to find that the promise made was as regards the Ordinance marriage.90 Writing the opinion of the court, Francois JA (as he then was) was not in doubt that the action was maintainable on the ground that the incidents of the customary marriage and the Ordinance marriage were different. There was no discussion of the appropriateness of the action since the court took it for granted that the issue raised no legal point worthy of further discussion. Owing, however, to the fact that on the evidence, the formalities of the customary marriage had not been concluded, the strength of this authority is weakened.

The issue of customary marriage and the action for breach of promise to marry was the central issue in Aning v Kingful.91 After stating that the parties were married under customary law, the judge went on to say that they were also engaged to be married under the Ordinance. This was as a result of the presentation of a Bible and ring. That meant that there was a further promise by the man that the marriage would be converted into a christian monogamous marriage under the Marriage Ordinance.

In holding that the action would lie, Amuah Ag J quoted as his authority, the holding in Appiah alias Acheampong v Acheampong92 that such actions are not contrary to public policy. The conclusion however that the facts in that particular case did not offend public policy is no indication that all of such actions are in accord with public policy.

The learned judge's uncertainty as to whether this action was appropriate in these circumstances did not evaporate, even with his citing of authority. In what amounts to a recognition that the marriage had broken down beyond reconciliation, Amuah Ag J, stated that:

[p.57]

". . . it does seem to me that it is marriage under the Ordinance that she is yearning for. Once the marriage under the Ordinance has failed, it is not likely that the customary marriage will be pursued by both of them. At least from the look of things he does not intend to take her as his wife any more."

This is a kind of pronouncement that no common law judge had to make, because in the culture from which the action evolved, the parties did not first go through a prior marriage. After making this pronouncement, he then proceeded to award damages. If a marriage develops differences to the extent that the parties can no longer live together, is the award of damages an appropriate remedy?

Many are the troubling questions that this case, as well as subsequent ones involving customary marriage, raises in the particular circumstances in which the cause of action was upheld. The parties were married under customary law. This raised serious issues of a general societal nature, yet the case was treated as if the only issue at stake was whether there was sufficient evidence of the promise.93 However, the real issue at stake was whether a promise to convert a potentially polygynous marriage into a monogamous one was enforceable at law. It is true that in the particular social context, marriage under the Ordinance commands greater respect among certain sections of the Ghanaian society, but does that create a legal obligation to honour a promise so made or pay damages?94 What is the implication in the wider society of cases such as this one? Not the least troubling, is the fact that at the time Aning v Kingful was decided, it had become possible to petition the High Court for the dissolution of a customary marriage.95 This being so, a further question that needs to be posed is this: Does a fact situation that would be adequate grounds for divorce, also support a breach of promise of marriage? Does divorce not make a presumption of marriage, whilst the action for breach of promise makes a presumption of not having been married? Can [p.58] one be both married and unmarried at the same time?96 It is conceded that there are situations in which one could operate in more than one capacity. However, marriage relates to status and therefore, one either is, or is not.

The issue is made the more difficult by a consideration of the remedies. In an action for a breach of promise, damages are the appropriate remedy. It is however not so for divorce either under customary law or the Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971.97 If a court cannot grant damages for divorce, then the issue is this: should a person who is not interested in maintaining a marriage under customary law be entitled to damages when they would not be available to others seeking divorce from the courts? The same consideration goes for injured feelings. If the injured feelings of the plaintiff are a basis for the award of damages, why are deserted wives not entitled to damages for their injured feelings?

The head under which damages would be awarded in such circumstances is also problematic. Is there social disgrace in not being married under the Ordinance?98 Does the woman lose the worldly advantage that would have accrued to her as the wife of the defendant — when she is already his wife under customary law? It is conceded that certain Christian sects such as the Catholic Church impose penalties of a religious nature upon persons not married according to the rites of the church. However, if the refusal of the defendant to undergo the religious ceremony constitutes unreasonable behaviour, it ought to go in support of the sole ground for divorce — that the marriage has broken down beyond reconciliation — and not one of an action for damages.

Further questions need attention if action is to be upheld under these circumstances. Is a marriage under the Ordinance of more economic value than a customary marriage (because the loss of an economic benefit is at the very foundation of the action)? Arguably, before the promulgation of the Intestate Succession Law, 198599 this was so as there were rights of [p.59] succession upon intestacy.100 However, since the rights were contingent upon intestacy, those economic rights did not automatically accrue to one married under the Ordinance. Therefore to award compensation to one based on the loss of such prospects is somewhat ludicrous—especially since there would be no evidence of the comparative longevity of the two parties.

What interest does the action in these circumstances protect anyway? Apart from contributing to instability in marriage by promoting discontent on the part of females for having been denied the title "Missus", does it serve any positive ends?101 Is the action a viable option for the one who, despite her disappointment, is willing to carry on with the relationship or only for the one who has lost interest in the match anyway? Does society regard such a plaintiff any more as "damaged goods" than it does divorced women? If not, why are such women entitled to damages when divorced women are not granted damages for lost reputation? What about the opinion of right thinking members of the Ghanaian society as regards this situation? Would such a woman be regarded as having been jilted, or divorced?

Furthermore, if the plaintiff granted sexual favours to the defendant, is that not the customary duty of a wife? Why should the defendant have to pay damages for exercising his rights as a husband under customary law? Is a promise to marry under the Ordinance tantamount to a promise not to exact one's customary law rights as a husband? Should that be considered an aggravation of the damages because the plaintiff has been "seduced?" If a child had been born to the couple, would right-thinking members of society consider the child as born outside wedlock and the mother as an unweded mother? These troubling questions are not easily answered. Certainly not by the approach taken by the court.

It is also argued that upholding actions for breach of promise when the parties are married under customary law seems ill-advised. The courts have over the years laid down the essentials of a customary marriage.102 Ought they to uphold actions for breach of promise to marry in circumstances that make inevitable the conclusion that customary marriage is not real marriage? How does that reflect on the majority of Ghanaians who are married under [p.60] customary law only?103 Since a wedding ceremony, whether religious or merely civil, is an expensive affair in Ghana would poverty be allowed as a defence?

Since it is now possible to register customary marriages,104 this precedent would present serious theoretical problems if it was followed. It would be strange if a plaintiff, wielding a certificate evidencing her marriage under customary law, could by that means also obtain damages on the ground of not having been married. What if the action were brought by a plaintiff, married under the Ordinance but not under customary law? Would the action be countenanced? If not, is this not because of the perception that marriage under the Ordinance is inherently superior to customary marriage? What about a plaintiff married according to Islamic rites and registered under the Marriage of Mohammedans Ordinance105 but who claimed the right to be married in a civil ceremony under the Marriage Ordinance because of a desire for a monogamous marriage? Would that have been treated differently? These are questions that require some reflection because they involve serious issues of public policy.

There is the further issue of the presentation of the ring and Bible. While this is consistent with an imputed desire to contract a Christian marriage,106 there is no such logical progression to holding that it refers to marriage under the Ordinance. A marriage under the Ordinance can be contracted in a civil or religious ceremony. It therefore does not automatically follow that a promise to marry in church is anything more than a church blessing. If this sounds like academic hair-splitting, so does the legal meaning of the presentation of the Bible and ring to the average Ghanaian man who makes such a presentation.

Further, if a man does not wish to be monogamous, is it right to compel him to undergo such ceremony for fear of having to pay damages to his wife? Would she still remain his wife after he has been sued? Why should the decision to be monogamous not be voluntarily made? Should a society such as ours, that has found it impolitic to make a frontal attack on polygyny through legislation, enforce monogamy by the back door? Other than creating opportunities for prosecutions for bigamy, are any real societal ends served by such an attitude? Is it really true that marriages under the Ordinance tend to be more stable than other types? Without any sociological [p.61] evidence, ought the court to lend its authority to the perpetuation of such myths?

If the fact had also been borne in mind that it is not a cause of action for women only, extra care would have been taken in the creation of the precedent. Suppose the plaintiff in the instant case had been the man rather than the woman, would the court's attitude have been different? If, for instance, the man had alleged that the woman, by her unchastity, had made it impossible for them to convert their marriage to a monogamous one, would the action not have been dismissed with heavy costs? Would he not have been advised that his proper remedy lay in divorce? This is not an impossible scenario since the action is founded on the unilateral breach of an agreement to marry. Clearly, the application of the rules to this particular fact-situation was unfortunate in that it raised more questions than it attempted to answer.

It is clear that the courts have a leadership role in this regard. If there is a misapprehension in the society as to the status of the customary marriage, it is the court's duty to set the record straight through their decisions. To go along with attitudes born or those misconceptions is for the court to abdicate its social responsibility. Such actions should be disallowed on grounds of public policy.

III. PUTTING THE ACTION TO WORK ANEW

What has been said so far is apt to create the impression that the present writer disapproves of this cause of action. Such an impression would not be farther from the truth. What is being pointed out here is that it was resurrected from the dust of centuries for a particularly undeserving cause. Its application not only discredited the customary form of marriage, but also enabled a plaintiff to claim damages for a failed marriage.

There are some uses to which this cause of action could be put to serve a social need. Its primary use as an action for jilted lovers could find use in situations where an absence of legal relief would be unjust.107 Although it is a common law cause of action, there is no reason why in an appropriate case, it could not be used for a failure to contract a customary marriage. There is less of a danger of fabricated evidence than under the English system where an agreement to marry often takes place between the couple only. At least here, the court would not be left in the position of having to choose between the word of the plaintiff and that of the defendant.

A recent case has more than ever, shown the necessity for the courts to intervene in situations that are peculiarly Ghanaian. In Djarbeng v Tagoe108 the High Court per Ampiah J (as he then was) held that a valid promise of marriage could not be proved because what was put in evidence did not [p.62] measure up to the requirements of customary law. The facts were that the defendant had an amorous relationship with the plaintiff resulting in the birth of a child. The defendant then left the country for the United Kingdom and wrote letters to the plaintiff informing her that he had made up his mind to marry her. There was also an allegation (described in the report as "mere") that at an earlier paternity ceremony, the defendant's family had agreed on his behalf that he would marry the plaintiff. On these facts, the learned judge held that there was no evidence of a promise to marry either under the Marriage Ordinance or customary law.

This is a conclusion that is hard to support even on his lordship's own reading of the case. Even if no weight was attached to the promise made on behalf of the defendant (and rightly so), a like attitude to the defendant's own letters cannot be justified. The judge placed much weight on the traditional modes of contracting marriage, stating that when a man desired to marry a woman, he applied to the family of the woman through his own family for their consent. Therefore any other mode could not be proper. For this reason his lordship held that what had transpired did not amount to a promise because the family of the man was not involved. This seems a strange conclusion to draw, judging by the fact that there was an allegation of a “promise" having been made by the defendant's family on his behalf. If it was a family involvement his lordship was looking for, there was ample evidence of it. In a system of law which permits marriage ceremonies to be performed in the absence of the parties to the union, it seems strange that so much emphasis should have been put on the fact of the defendant's absence. His family could have given those assurances if he had instructed them to do so. Therefore his absence per se was not of dramatic importance.

Secondly, if a valid promise of marriage could be made only through the man’s family,109 then what was wrong with the promise made at the paternity ceremony? If it is true that custom does not permit a man to make valid promises of marriage on his own account, then there is no basis for rejecting the family’s assurance at the paternity ceremony. If, on the other hand, promises made by the family are not binding on the man, then his own assurances must be binding. To deny that a man may give his own assurances, and also that such assurances may be given on his behalf, is [p.63] tantamount to saying that a man can never make a valid promise of marriage. One cannot have it both ways. Therefore, one of those two versions of the customary law must be wrong.

This was indeed, a proper case for the action of breach of promise to marry. The law should not allow persons who make promises with no intention of fulfilling them to get away with their misdeeds. In an area as important as marriage, a reliance of the plaintiff on such assurances deserves legal protection because a contrary position could only breed chaos. Whilst it is conceded that the mere fact of the birth of a child does not amount to a promise, the law should not idly sit by, if subsequent promises of marriage go unhonoured.

The emphasis placed by the court on the customary procedure would also result in denying male plaintiffs a claim; for after all, in our culture, it is the family of the man that applies for the hand of a woman, and never vice versa. Therefore, following from his lordship's reasoning, only females may bring actions under these rules—a position contrary to all common law authority. In any case, how many men go to the homes of their prospective spouses these days, in the company of members of their family, in order to make promises of marriage? Is the imposition of such a requirement not merely creating a way of escape for people who make promises which they subsequently regret, to back out without liability?

It is in the interest of the society that women to whom such assurances are given, be encouraged to set themselves apart for the men who give such promises. The law should not encourage women to have "several irons in the fire" in order to ensure that they would not end up with the shorter end of the stick. A society that provides no avenue for legal redress when a man has "wasted a woman's time" should seriously rethink its position or risk having to put up with the problems that are likely to result from young women keeping a string of suitors in tow.

The action addresses the situations in which a woman would have been made a fool of, or would have been exploited by another by the promise of marriage. For instance, there are situations in which a man makes a promise to marry a woman but is unable to afford the expenses involved in the performance of the customary rites. In reliance on the promise, the woman agrees to cohabit with him. If after a time, it becomes clear that the failure to perform the rites is wilful, ought there not to be a cause of action for this woman? Should the man be able to obtain the services of a "wife" by making promises which he has no intention of fulfilling? If at the time he made the promise, he had every intention of honouring it, should he be able to change his mind after another has relied on the promise to her detriment?

Another fact-situation is where a man decides to marry a woman and initiates action by presenting the customary "knocking fee.”110 If he [p.64] subsequently changes his mind and fails to complete the full requirements of the marriage, is this not an appropriate case for permitting the action? If for no other reason than that such a step by the man would have prematurely taken the woman off the marriage-market, and thus cost her other opportunities to procure a match. It would also have ridiculed her in the eyes of her family, as well as ridiculing the family—particularly if this happens in a small community. Are these not situations which are currently rampant but which appear to have no remedy under the law?

On the side of males, there are also fact-situations which deserve legal redress. It is not an uncommon occurrence that in consideration of marriage a man assumes financial responsibility for a woman — often involving substantial expenditure, especially where the woman is undergoing apprenticeship to learn a trade. Where the woman changes her mind about marrying the man and begins to consort with other men or to conduct herself in such a way as to make marriage with her impossible, it is submitted that a cause of action for breach of promise to marry should lie.111 The fact that a man would feel free to hold a woman to a promise of marriage or claim damages is also an acknowledgment of equality that mere sloganeering would not achieve. Although, on grounds of public policy, the action should not be available for, or to those who enter into these kinds of arrangements with parents on behalf of very young children, no such consideration exists where the woman is old enough to know her own mind.

One more circumstance in which this action should be available pertains to married men who contract other marriages. It is common knowledge that these bigamous associations exist at such a level as to have become almost respectable in the society. Many men contract monogamous marriages and then, either through ignorance or deliberate act, purport to contract other customary marriages. Where the man's status is concealed from the woman, it ought to be possible for her to maintain an action. It is important to note at this point that in Ghana, very few women go through such marriages without some knowledge of the man's status. However, this occurs mostly because of ignorance of what a previous marriage under the Ordinance means to the legality of any subsequent marriage. Since it is the party who gives the warranty who is in breach, this should not affect liability unless the "victim" could be presumed, by reason of special circumstances, to have knowledge of such lack of capacity.112 Where a caveat is entered by another to stop the proposed marriage, it would be unjust if the law offered no legal [p.65] remedy. For in this case, not only would expenses have been incurred, but the social humiliation would also be substantial.

There are two distinct situations here: that of one married under the Ordinance and therefore without capacity to contract a fresh marriage, and that of one married under customary law. In the latter situation, the problem arises if the fact of a previous subsisting marriage is fraudulently concealed from the promisee. These raise identical issues, but they would be discussed severally since the former marriage is bigamous113 whilst the latter is lawful.

Where the person is monogamously married, the point would be that the relationship contracted with the plaintiff is void. For this reason an action would lie. Therefore a person who leads another into such an ineffectual marriage fails to "marry" and should be liable if the other party seeks a remedy at law.114 Although this action, like the provisions on bigamy, have never stopped anyone intent on contracting such a marriage, it is important to recognize the existence of a remedy for those willing to make use of it.

Where the other party is married under customary law, there are two possibilities. A female defendant would be liable because she does not have the capacity under customary law to contract more than one marriage at a time. However, with a male defendant it would be enough to show that the plaintiff knew of the marital status and agreed to contract the marriage. Since the man retains capacity to contract further marriages, no injury would have been done where the woman knew of his marital status. Promises of the man to divorce his wife should also be treated as at common law and held to be contrary to public policy.115

Evidence of concealment would raise different issues from those discussed above. The otherwise lawful nature of the marriage notwithstanding, the fraud perpetuated on the promisee at the time of the promise by reason of the defendant having a subsisting customary marriage with another should found the cause of action. This situation must be distinguished from the situation in the Aning v Kingful case. At least in that situation, there is no social opprobrium, as when a woman undeservedly earns the name of "husband-snatcher" by marrying a married man. There is certainly a measure of loss of reputation involved which would be deserving of an award of damages. Therefore where a decree of nullity would be available to one who purported to marry under the Ordinance, this action should be available to one deceived into a polygynous union.

[p.66]

Efforts to provide a remedy for the "victims" of such unions has achieved new emergent proportions. This is because since the passage of the Intestate Succession Law, 1985,116 it has become possible for one to suffer grave injury if fraudulently lured into such a situation. Without such an action, the only options left to such a woman is divorce (with the possibility of non-maintenance) or remaining in a union one considers unacceptable and the complications that would arise upon the death of the man.117 Should this not be an appropriate case to remedy if the misled party decides to withdraw from the relationship?118 It is likely that the existence of such remedy would discourage men who believe that it is appropriate to conceal one's marital status in order to procure a woman's consent. Without this belief that once a woman is presented with a fait accompli of a polygynous set-up, there is nothing much that she could do about it, at least a disincentive would have been created. Surely this would be a useful social service if the courts would perform it.

A further advantage that this cause of action has in this country is that the cause of action resides in the victim of the breach, and not in another person. Therefore, in the case of a female plaintiff, it gives her a say in whether to subject her personal life to public glare. This guarantee is not available to the one whose life forms the subject of an action for seduction because the right of action is in another.119 The existence of this action is also a much-needed affirmation that at some point in time, a woman is capable of maintaining a challenge to those who would exploit her and then treat her with contempt. There is yet again the possibility that the potential plaintiff would be an independent woman whose father could with little credibility claim damages for loss of services, etc.120 Besides, this action is not limited to any age and so any person with a grievance may maintain an action and not be denied redress for a wrong that is undeniable. In modern Ghana, the action has psychological value for women and should be appropriately exploited.

[p.67]

IV. SUGGESTIONS AND CONCLUSIONS

The proposals made above are not as revolutionary as they are apt to be perceived at first blush. A casual look around would reveal how many men and women could have resorted to this action if its portents had been better appreciated. The fact that very few people litigate issues relating to matters involving intimate details does not mean that such actions are considered foreign, or that they would continue to be regarded as such. There are many factors, not the least of them being the availability of counsel who appreciates the merits of the cause of action, why very little use has been made of it. In addition, the propaganda that has accompanied the abolition of that cause of action has served to render it disreputable in the common law world.

In this paper, an attempt has been made to establish the cause of action as a legitimate one for wider application. This has been done through an exposition of its nature and incidents. This was followed by an analysis of the criticisms that have led to its abolition in some common law jurisdictions. Lastly, the common fact-situations in which this action would serve the ends of justice have been described. The hope is that more of such "victims" would be encouraged to seek legal remedies instead of putting up with their loss, or remaining in marriages procured by fraud, only because of the possibility of losing out completely.

The difficulties inherent in upholding the action when the parties are already married under customary law have been noted. It is suggested that unless the situation involves a concealment of a subsisting previous marriage, the action should not be upheld. Holding a person liable for making a promise to marry monogamously when he had already contracted away his capacity for doing so should be treated as a distinct situation from when no such disability exists. In the one case, it involves fraud, but this is not necessarily so where it involves a subsequent change of mind. Where it involves the latter situation, the remedy should be divorce so as to avoid the down grading of the customary form of marriage in order to uphold this cause of action.

The circumstances under which a promise of marriage would be inferred must also be given considerable thought in order to solve two problems: forestalling the situations of blackmail which have discredited this action, and discouraging unscrupulous persons from taking advantage of others. The mere fact that it enables men to be blackmailed is not a good enough reason to neglect the action, since the contrary situation enables women's affections to be abused with impunity. If the possibility of blackmail is known, that should encourage more openness and honesty in intimate relationships. On the whole, it is better for society to hold people to promises made—even of marriage—and to declare the parameters within which one may change one's mind without causing undue hardship to another.

From the foregoing discussion, it is clear that there are possibilities within [p.68] the current social set-up for the vindication of rights. Owing to the difference in culture, it should be easier for the Ghanaian courts to control the incidence of abuse. The mere possibility of the existence of abuse, however, should not operate against the application of the action, for, which rule of law is not capable of abuse? On a more important level, however, it should reduce the powerlessness of women in intimate relationships. People should be encouraged not to let losses lie where they have fallen—especially in deserving cases. The law may not be helpful in preventing breaches of faith, but there is no doubt that raising the cost of a breach may assist in that regard.
