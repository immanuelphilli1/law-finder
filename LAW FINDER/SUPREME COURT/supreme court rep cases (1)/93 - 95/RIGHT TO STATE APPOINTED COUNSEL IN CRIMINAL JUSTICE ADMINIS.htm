RIGHT TO STATE APPOINTED COUNSEL IN CRIMINAL JUSTICE ADMINISTRATION UNDER THE CONSTITUTION, 1992, RGL 159—181

MARTIN A B K AMIDU

 

*

INTRODUCTION

The right to counsel as a requirement for fair trial is one of the fundamental human rights guaranteed and entrenched under the Constitution, 1992. Yet the right to counsel has an uncertain place in our criminal justice system.  In spite of their rhetorics on fundamental human rights and the rule of law, both the bench and the Bar appear married to the colonial system of criminal justice where an indigent accused or a person who has lost his liberty cannot have a right to state appointed counsel to assist him at the pretrial or the trial proceedings to obtain his liberty. The only exception concerns trials for the capital offence of murder. This is the case in spite of new developments in the metropolitan colonial country during and after the colonial period. This conservatism of the judicial and legal minds in failing to recognise the modern democratic principles of the rights against self-incrimination, to legal aid and due process may explain why the fundamental human rights provisions on the liberty of the individual, respect for human dignity, equality before the law, fair trial and the mandatory constitutional requirement of state appointed counsel by the courts for purposes of the defence or prosecution of constitutional matters have not been enforced by the courts or invoked by lawyers in this country.1

The wind of change in respect of this important fundamental principle of state appointed counsel for indigent accused persons does not appear to have blown through our courts and law chambers. Yet section 110 of the Courts Act, 1971 empowers the superior courts, and under certain conditions inferior courts, to assign a lawyer by way of legal aid to indigent persons in any proceedings before them where the interest of justice so requires.2 In the [p.160] industrialised common law jurisdictions such as the United States of America and England which have less elaborate provisions on the right to state appointed counsel for indigent accused persons who have been deprived of their liberty, the courts have developed fundamental principles to include the pauper's right to state appointed counsel.  In the United States, for instance, the Fifth, Sixth and Fourteenth Amendments have been interpreted either separately or together to afford this fundamental right to a fair trial.3  In England the courts have developed the concept of right to counsel and state or court appointed lawyer for serious offences apart from murder.4  The situation is now largely governed by the Legal Aid Acts of 1974 and 1982.

In 1931 the Court of Criminal Appeal in England held that on an indictment for rape, a prisoner ought to be provided with assistance of learned counsel.5 Similarly in 1942 Humpherys J observed that it was desirable for a prisoner charged with a sexual offence to be represented by counsel.6  It is now the nature and complexity of the crime that determines whether an accused ought to be assigned a state appointed counsel, and a request for one by the accused is not a necessary condition for the assignment of such counsel.7 And in 1942 the Supreme Court of United States in Powell v Alabama8 unequivocably declared that the right to the aid of a counsel was of a fundamental character.

[p.161]

In Gideon v Wainwright9 the Supreme Court reaffirmed the principle that in an adversary system of criminal justice any person who was too poor to engage the services of counsel could not be assured a fair trial unless counsel was engaged for him by the state. In so deciding the Supreme Court over-ruled Betts v Brady10 which subsequent to Powell's case had held that the right to state appointed counsel was not fundamental to a fair hearing.11

These developments in criminal jurisprudence are in recognition of the powerful resources of the state apparatus vis-à-vis the weak position of the individual or group of citizens and their liberty. A casual acquaintance with state power in developing countries would indicate the fundamental importance of protecting the individual against misuse or abuse of the state apparatus against the individual's freedom and liberty. It is not therefore surprising that the Constitution, 1992 provides for the right to counsel in pre-trial proceedings in article 14(2). The right to counsel at the trial is provided [p.162] for in article 19. Article 294 then appears to be all-embracing and controlling as it mandatorily requires and stipulates that a person is entitled to legal aid in connection with any proceedings relating to the Constitution if he has reasonable grounds for taking, defending, prosecuting or being a party to the proceedings which seek to enforce any of the provisions of the Constitution.

When the provisions of the Constitution dealing with the protection of the liberty of arrested or accused persons at the pre-trial or trial proceedings are read alongside the proceedings of the 1992 Consultative Assembly, it becomes evident that the Consultative Assembly was so concerned with safeguarding the fundamental rights of our people against arbitrary arrests, detentions and trials that it expanded and liberalised the ambit of those rights.12

The summation of the five minutes speeches of each member of the assembly which largely dwelt on fundamental human rights and independence of the judiciary finds expression in what is now the Constitution, 1992.13

It has therefore become imperative that the will of Ghanaians as enshrined in the fundamental human rights provisions on liberty of the person, respect for human dignity, equality before the law, fair trial, and the provisions of article 294 which make it mandatory for state or court appointed counsel to assist persons to defend their rights under the Constitution are not allowed to be violated by the conservative attitude of the Ghanaian courts and the Bar.

The purpose of this paper is therefore to demonstrate that at both pre-trial and trial proceedings an accused person in forma pauperis is entitled as of right under the Constitution, 1992, to state appointed counsel. It would be argued that in view of the provisions of article 294 and other related provisions of the Constitution, it is the duty of the state and its courts to ensure that suspects or accused persons in forma pauperis are assigned counsel at the pre-trial and trial stages.  Failure to assign counsel to such persons who are compelled to defend their fundamental human rights under the Constitution should render the pre-trial or trial proceedings a nullity as being violative of the fundamental human rights provisions and article 294 of the Constitution. It is argued that neither phraseology "right to counsel of his own choice" nor "right to lawyer of his choice" mean that the accused person must pay for a counsel of his choice. What in our opinion ought to be the dimension of the right to assistance of counsel has been briefly stated. While the discussion of the nature and quality of the right provided under article 294 is beyond the scope of this article, it is however our hope that this [p.163] paper would provide a basis for debate which could address the consequential issues of the nature and quality of the assistance to be provided by state appointed counsel.

PRE-TRIAL RIGHTS TO STATE APPOINTED COUNSEL

Rights to Counsel while in Custody

The provisions of the Constitution, 1992 on the protection of personal liberty stipulate in article 14 the circumstances under which a person may be deprived of his personal liberty under the Constitution and in accordance with procedure permitted by law.14  Article 14(2) provides that:

"(2) A person who is arrested, restricted or detained shall be informed immediately, in a language that he understands, of the reasons for his arrest, restriction or detention and of his right to a lawyer of his choice."

A person who is charged with an offence (an accused) is required under article 19(2) to be informed immediately in a language that he understands, and in detail, of the nature of the offence charged; be given adequate time and facilities for preparing his defence; and be permitted to defend himself in person or by a lawyer of his choice.15 In addition to these provisions for the protection of personal liberty and fair trial, articles 17(1) and (2) stipulate respectively that all persons shall be equal before the law and that a person shall not be discriminated against on grounds of gender, race, colour, ethnic, religion, creed or social or economic status.16

We are of the opinion that the importance of article 17(1) and (2) of the Constitution for our present purposes lies in the fact that both articles 14(2) and 19(2) are designed to ensure that the state, as a generic person, does not have an undue advantage over the individual in the defence of his person and liberty.  It is also to ensure that as between individual citizens, economic and other powers do not give advantage to the one over the other.

It must be acknowledged that beginning with the landmark appellate decision in Okorie alias Ozuzu v The Republic17 interpreting the almost identical provisions of the Constitution, 1969 the Ghanaian courts have held that a person who was lawfully arrested or detained must be informed of his right to consult counsel of his own choice as required by article 15(2) of that Constitution, otherwise confession statements under caution obtained from such a person would not be admissible in evidence at his trial.  In his lordship's judgment this right to be informed of the right to counsel of a [p.164] person's own choice was so fundamental that it was irrelevant that an infringement of that constitutional right did not occasion a miscarriage of justice or that counsel did not raise it at the trial. His lordship, after rejecting the two confession statements obtained in breach of the constitutional provision, came to the conclusion that there was sufficient evidence aliunde to justify the conviction appealed against. In arriving at that decision on the fundamental nature of the right to be informed of right to counsel of a person's own choice, his lordship relied on the United States Supreme Court decision in Miranda v Arizona18 and the principles underlying it as persuasive authority.19

Azu Crabbe CJ's exposition of the law in respect of article 15(2) of the Constitution, 1969 was followed by Taylor J (as he then was) in Republic v Akosah.20 He held that in spite of the suspension of the Constitution, 1969 the provisions of article 15(2) of that Constitution were by virtue of sections 2(1) and 3(2) of the National Redemption Council (Establishment) Proclamation, 1972 a rule of law applicable to two incriminating police statements voluntarily made by the accused without being informed of his right to consult counsel of his own choice. His lordship also relied on the principles enunciated in Miranda v Arizona, particularly the judgment of Warren CJ as applicable to pre-trial statements taken from arrested, restricted, detained or accused persons.21 In this case the objection to the admissibility of the incriminating statements was raised by counsel for the accused when making a submission of no case after the close of the case for the prosecution. Since the statements were the only statements against the first accused in the case, the objection was upheld and he was not called upon to make his defence.

In Marhaba v The Republic22 Taylor J (as he then was) in spite of an outburst against the Criminal Procedure (Amendment) Decree, 197523 held that even though at the time the accused's statement was taken the rule of law requiring compliance with a person's right to be informed of counsel of his own choice was in force, he could not by virtue of the retroactive nature of [p.165] the Decree exclude the statements taken in violation of the appellant's right to consult counsel of his own choice prior to the making of the statement. On the strength of section 34 of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372) however, he held that the trial magistrate who had been promoted to the circuit court during the hearing of the case did not have the authority of the Chief Justice to act as a district magistrate to complete the part-heard case involving the accused. The conviction and sentence of the appellants by the circuit court were all therefore a nullity.

The cases reviewed above show how the breach of constitutional guarantee of right to counsel of one's own choice in cases of arrest, restriction or detention have been invoked to protect the right to personal liberty. It is however clear that in all these appeals, the accused persons were represented by retained counsel of their own choice. In the courts below where all the accused were unrepresented by counsel, they were convicted in spite of breaches of their fundamental right to a fair trial and their rights to counsel which could have enured to their benefit.  In interpreting the Sixth Amendment of the American Constitution in Miranda v Arizona the majority of the Supreme Court held that the provision that "in all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right. . . to have assistance of counsel for his defence" implied that if the police proposed to interrogate a person they must make known to him that he was entitled to a lawyer and that if he could not afford one, a lawyer would be provided for him prior to interrogation.24  The state has the burden to prove that a defendant knowingly and intelligently waived his right to retain a state appointed lawyer.25 The willingness of a person to accept a state assigned lawyer, in our opinion, is the determining factor of whether counsel is of the defendant's own choice within the meaning of the Constitutions, 1969, 1979 and 1992.

The High Court has been entrusted with the exclusive original jurisdiction under article 33 of the Constitution, 1992 to protect fundamental human rights and freedoms. It is provided that  under article 33(5):

"(5) The rights, duties, declarations and guarantees relating to the fundamental human rights and freedoms specifically mentioned in this Chapter shall not be regarded as excluding others not specifically mentioned which are consider to be inherent in a democracy and intended to secure the freedom and dignity of man."

It is our submission that the only basis on which the High Court can ensure the protection of rights "inherent in a democracy and intended to secure the freedom and dignity of man", is to accept those decisions of the US and British courts which embody those inherent democratic principles. The basic idea in those decisions is that the right of counsel of one's choice is not limi- [p.166] ted to self-paid counsel. As we have attempted to show, "assistance of counsel" as used in the Sixth Amendment of the American Constitution and the phraseology "right to counsel of his own choice" as used under the Constitutions 1969, 1979 and 1992 respectively do not connote right to self-retained counsel. The intendment as correctly demonstrated in the American decisions is that the state as a generic person and the individual must be equal before the law in terms of their legal defence capability.26

In Powell v Alabama the US Supreme Court noted that even the intelligent and educated layman could not understand the skills of the science of law. This applies with greater force in a country such as ours where the ratio of population of educated laymen to illiterate population is very low. The United States has a higher literacy ratio than most developing countries within the Anglo-American common law jurisdiction.  Its jurisprudence, however, still requires the services of state appointed counsel as a way of protecting their fundamental human rights and freedoms. This requirement must therefore be of even greater importance in a developing country such as ours with a largely illiterate population which has very little knowledge of how the law works both in practice and in theory. The only logical approach would be to follow Miranda v Arizona in holding that articles 14(2), 294 and the relevant provisions of Act 372 cast a duty on the state to provide a lawyer to incarcerated persons who are willing to accept one before any statements are taken from them. Otherwise the right provided for by the Constitution would be meaningless to the majority of the urban poor and the ignorant rural population.27

Right to Counsel at Preliminary Hearing of Indictable Offence

Our Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 requires every offence  tried on indictment to be preceded by committal proceedings in the nature of a preliminary hearing by a district court.28 The procedure to be followed at the preliminary hearing is provided for in sections 181 to 197 of Act 30. Even though the preliminary hearing takes place before a court of law, they are pre-trial proceedings and not proceedings in the trial itself which take place [p.167] before a circuit court or High Court with a jury or assessors.29 The issue therefore is whether the right to counsel, with the accustomed incidents of consultation and opportunity of preparation for the trial, is a sine qua non at the preliminary hearing in view of the fact that these proceedings take place before a court.  By the practice of our courts a person indicted for murder is assigned counsel for his defence at the trial. There is however no practice or decided case which requires that a person indicted for murder must be represented by counsel at the preliminary hearing. This has been so in spite of the fact that the conduct of an accused at the preliminary hearing can facilitate his conviction.30

An examination of the procedure to be followed at the preliminary hearing may assist us to determine whether under our constitutional provisions the right to counsel is mandatory to a fair trial and therefore of fundamental character to the liberty of the individual. The prosecution is required to furnish the court and the accused with a bill of indictment stating in writing the charge against the accused and a summary of evidence, comprising a list of the witnesses whom the prosecution proposes to call at the trial together with the summary of evidence to be given by each witness. The bill of indictment is to comply with the provisions of sections 201 and 202 as to form and content.31 The bill of indictment and summary of evidence are to be signed by the Attorney-General or a person authorised by him to sign on his behalf.32 The prosecution is permitted to address the court in explanation of the charge against the accused and a reply may be made by or on behalf of the accused. The addresses are not to be recorded but the accused may make a statement in accordance with section 187.  Under Act 30 the court does not hear evidence of witnesses and their cross-examination. The duty of the court is to examine the bill of indictment and summary of evidence and decide whether there is a case for the accused to answer. If the court is of the opinion that there is a case to answer, it shall commit him for trial. The accused is to be discharged if the court is of the opinion that there is no case for him to answer.33 The committal court is also empowered to assist the accused to secure the attendance of his witnesses, if any, after he has identified them to the court.34 In committing the accused for trial the court is to admit him to bail or remand him into prison custody pending the day, time and place when and where he is to appear before the trial court to [p.168] answer the indictment preferred against him.35 Where the charge is one in respect of which an accused has an election to be tried by jury or assessors, the court is to ask the accused to make his election.36

In spite of the fact that the court is not to hear witnesses and their cross-examination at this stage of the proceedings, there is no gainsaying that most accused persons would not appreciate what the form and content of an indictment is under the Criminal Procedure Code. The legislature has also recognised the fact that the accused may be unable to understand the charge and to address the court in reply to the prosecution's address in explanation of the charge. It has therefore provided that a reply may be made by the accused or on his behalf.37  Surely it is envisaged that the person to make the reply on behalf of the accused is a lawyer who has a right of audience before the court. It has also been held that in order for a committal court to commit an accused for trial the prosecution's evidential burden disclosed on the face of the indictment and summary of evidence is to establish a prima facie case. The evidential burden is the same as that placed on the prosecution at the trial to sustain a finding that the accused has a case to answer.38 The accused is also at liberty to persuade the court to grant him bail before committal. An accused who does not understand the implications of a trial by a jury or assessors cannot be said to have made an intelligent choice as to the two modes of trial.

Finally, a statutory statement made by an unrepresented accused pursuant to section 187 of the Criminal Procedure Code has been held by the Supreme Court in State v Banful39 not to be automatically admissible and that the same principles of voluntariness apply to its admissibility as are applicable to extra-judicial confessions. The prosecution has the burden of proving that they were made voluntarily in the sense that the previous inducement did not still operate on the mind of the accused at the time of making the statutory statement or the so-called judicial confession.40

The right to counsel we have indicated includes the accustomed incidents of consultation and opportunity of preparation to secure one's liberty before or at the trial. It is our opinion that the requirements of the preliminary hearing as discussed above makes it imperative that the right of an accused to his liberty, equality before the law and a subsequent fair trial can only be adequately safeguarded when an indigent accused is provided with a state appointed counsel at this stage of the proceedings. This will enable counsel [p.169] not only to protect the interest of the accused at this stage but also give him adequate opportunity to familiarise himself with the case, consult with the accused and to prepare for the trial.

We have not come across any decided case in this country where the issue of the accused's right to counsel at the preliminary hearing has been raised to set aside a hearing or a trial. The decision of the United States Supreme Court in Coleman v Alabama41 may therefore be of immense assistance and guidance for our purposes.  In that case the court held that the Sixth Amendment right to counsel applied to a preliminary hearing where the state law allowed such a hearing. The Alabama statute allowed for a preliminary hearing in which witnesses were to be called, examined in-chief by the prosecution and cross-examined by the defendant. Nonetheless the decision of the court particularly its recital of the third and fourth steps which counsel could take at the preliminary hearing are instructive:

" . . . Third, trained counsel can more effectively discover the case the state has against his client and make possible the preparation of a proper defence to meet the case at the trial. Fourth, counsel can also be influential at the preliminary hearing in making effective argument for the accused on such matters as the necessity for an early psychiatric examination or bail."

The Legal Aid Act, 1974 of England as amended by the Legal Aid Act, 1982 also supports this view of the right to counsel. It also shows the importance of the indigent's rights to counsel for purposes of bail.42

The decision in Coleman v Alabama, taken in conjunction with the right to legal aid as defined under article 294 of the Constitution, 1992 supports the conclusion that the right to counsel includes its concomitant incidence of consultation and opportunity for preparation for trial even at the preliminary hearing stage.

THE RIGHT TO ASSISTANCE BY STATE APPOINTED COUNSEL AT THE TRIAL

The recognition by our appellate courts of the dangers facing an unrepresented accused person is amply demonstrated in a series of appellate judgments. In Avegavi v The Republic43 the Court of Appeal per Anin JA (as he then was) stated that it was necessary in practice for the trial judge to warn an unrepresented accused that he was going too far in his cross-examination of the prosecution witnesses and thereby exposing himself to impeachment as to his credit and antecedents. The three appellants in the case were [p.170] unrepresented by a lawyer in a trial for robbery. They elected to give evidence on their own behalf. They each denied the charge and also denied ever making the confession statements attributed to them. The third accused during cross-examination stated that he did not know if Sgt Hanu concocted the statement which he said the third accused had made. He also stated that he never begged Sgt Hanu or anyone that he had done wrong. He stated further that: "I say Sgt Hanu had lied to the court in what he had said about me . . ." The prosecution at that point and in the presence of the jury applied for leave to cross-examine the first and third appellants as to previous convictions because the third appellant had attacked the character of Sgt Hanu, a prosecution witness. Leave was granted to prove the previous convictions of the appellants. Previous convictions for stealing were duly proved on the next adjourned date. They were convicted. On appeal it was argued that the trial court had no justification to permit the cross-examination of the first and third appellants as to their previous convictions and for the proven convictions to be revealed to the jury. The Court of Appeal upheld the submissions and quashed the convictions. One of the grounds for quashing the convictions was that because the jury were erroneously apprised of the previous convictions of the first and third appellants before the summing-up and consideration of their verdict, there was a substantial miscarriage of justice and a denial of a fair trial for all the appellants.

In Adusei II v The Republic44 the accused was charged on two counts of publishing false news contrary to section 208 of the Criminal Code, 196045, and deceit of a public officer contrary to section 251 of the same code. The accused who pleaded not guilty was represented by counsel on the day his plea was taken. On that day and at subsequent hearings a senior state attorney was responsible for the prosecution. On the day the prosecution opened its case and until the trial was completed, the accused was unrepresented by counsel because the court had refused, after several adjournments at the request of counsel for the accused, to grant a further adjournment on the day the prosecution opened its case. In attempting to meet the evidential burden placed on the prosecution the senior state attorney called witnesses to prove the mens rea of the accused by giving evidence to the effect that the accused knew that the contents of his publications (his letters) were false. By such false publications he had intended to deceive a named public officer. The senior state attorney also led evidence to show that the accused had a disposition to commit crimes of a similar nature as he had on previous occasions made similar reports against some of the prosecution witnesses to their detriment. The senior state attorney did not however produce any evidence to show that the accused was charged with any offence in connection with those reports. In the course of the trial the [p.171] trial judge summarily committed the second defendant witness into custody for what he felt was perjury without an opportunity for the witness to explain his conduct. The trial judge also not only admitted evidence of the accused's character but relied on such evidence in his judgments. The accused was consequently convicted and sentenced on both counts. On appeal to the High Court against his conviction and sentence his counsel submitted, inter alia, that the trial judge erred in admitting and relying on the evidence of the accused's character; and that there was a miscarriage of justice as the trial judge was influenced in his judgment by the evidence of the accused's past conduct. It was held, allowing the appeal, that on the facts of the case the evidence of the accused's character was inadmissible evidence and should have been rejected.  It was further held that:

"It is, without doubt, the role of a judge in the fair administration of criminal justice to assist an accused person who is not represented by counsel in putting his defence before the court ... It is not for him therefore to put impediments in the way of the defence of such an unrepresented accused person."45(a)

The court held further that where an unrepresented accused person cross-examined a prosecution witness in a way that left open his character for attack, the court should come to his aid and warn him of the dangers of cross-examination.

In the course of his judgment his lordship referred to the senior state attorney's justification for leading similar fact evidence at the trial and stated that the attacks on the appellant's character by the prosecution were unwarranted and highly prejudicial to his fair trial and the judge's admission of those attacks and his reliance upon them in his judgment amounted to a substantial miscarriage of justice.46

In Banda v the Republic47 the accused was charged with the possession of Indian hemp without lawful excuse in contravention of section 47(1) of the Pharmacy and Drugs Act, 1961 (Act 64). The accused who pleaded not guilty was represented by counsel on the first day of the trial. During counsel's cross-examination of the first prosecution witness he applied for an adjournment to enable him apply for a certified true copy of evidence which the witness had given in a previous abortive trial. After a number of adjournments the court decided to proceed with the trial in spite of the fact that the appellant told the court that he had seen his counsel in the premises [p.172] of the court earlier in the morning. The trial judge proceeded to the end in the absence of the appellant's counsel and without the appellant being afforded the opportunity to cross-examine the first prosecution witness. He was convicted and given an enhanced sentence after considering his past record. On appeal to the High Court it was submitted, inter alia, that the court ought to have afforded the appellant the opportunity to engage a fresh counsel or should have been permitted to cross-examine the witness himself; and that the trial judge had failed in his duty to assist the appellant in the trial especially since he was unrepresented by counsel. It was held, among other things, that:

"It is undoubtedly, one of the recognized functions of a judge in a criminal trial that he should assist an accused person, who is not represented by counsel, in putting his defence before the court ... But the duty of the trial judge to assist the accused does not mean that he should vacate his role of the 'impartial umpire' and enter the arena of contest by assuming the cross-examination of the witnesses on behalf of the accused himself or that he should show uninhibited paternalism towards the accused."48

The contradictory role of a judge assisting an accused in cross-examination while at the same time maintaining his role of an umpire in our adversary system of criminal justice is clearly exposed in the different perceptions of the court's role in the Adusei II and Banda cases. In our opinion such contradictory judgments of the High Court could have been avoided by adherence to the fundamental democratic principles of fair trial which require that an indigent accused person ought to be provided with the services of a court appointed counsel if he is willing to accept one.49

However, in Adu v The Republic50 an application for adjournment and for a certified true copy of the previous proceedings by a new counsel engaged by the accused was disregarded by the trial judge who proceeded with the trial. The appellant was consequently unrepresented and he was convicted and sentenced. His counsel therefore appealed against the conviction and sentence and applied for bail pending appeal. Granting the application for bail the High Court held that the provisions of sections 70(1) and 175(1) of Act 30 accentuated the time-honoured right of an accused person to engage his own counsel to defend himself. If the court therefore wilfully excluded or prevented the accused's counsel from representing him that would amount to a denial of justice. It was held further that on the facts the trial judge must have been aware that his refusal to grant an adjournment [p.173] would work injustice to the appellant, he therefore exercised his discretion wrongly.

Those cases were decided when the Constitution, 1969 was suspended.  However, the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372), s 110 which was enacted pursuant to the Constitution, 1969 particularly article 171 dealing with legal aid, empowered the courts, under certain conditions, to grant legal aid to indigent accused if the interest of justice so required.51 These provisions survived the suspension, and later, the abrogation of the Constitution, 1969. The courts have, however,  never exercised the power to appoint counsel for indigent accused in the interest of fair trial or justice as required by the law. It does not appear that lawyers have thought of the importance of this limited provision on the right to counsel. They have therefore failed to raise the issue of an indigent's right to counsel in the relevant cases. The right to counsel continues to be limited to murder trials which originates from a long standing practice of the courts and not a creature of statute.52  Even if section 110 of Act 372 were not on our statute books, we would still be of the view that the attitude of the Ghanaian courts and lawyers in refusing to extend or fight for the extension of right to counsel for indigent accused person is unsupportable by principles inherent in fair trial in civilised democracies.53

CONSTITUTIONAL RIGHT TO COUNSEL

Even where statutory power or rule of law supporting the assignment of a lawyer to an indigent accused person who has asked for or whose case warrants one does not exist, there would seem to be nothing preventing our courts and lawyers from invoking the constitutional right of an indigent to be provided with appointed counsel in the defence or prosecution of matters relating to the enforcement of the Constitution. There is no decided case in which either a court or a counsel has raised the fundamental issue of the right of an accused person to state or court appointed counsel to defend and enforce his fundamental constitutional right to protection of personal liberty, respect for human dignity, equality before the law and fair trial.54 Article 294(1) and (2) of the Constitution, 1992 which is similar in content with equivalent articles in the Constitutions, 1969 and 1979 provide as follows:

"294.(1) For the purposes of enforcing any provision of this Constitution, a person is entitled to legal aid in connection with any [p.174] proceedings relating to this Constitution if he has reasonable grounds for taking, defending, prosecuting or being a party to the proceedings.

(2)  Subject to clause (1) of this article, Parliament shall, by or under an Act of Parliament,  regulate the grant of legal aid."

It is our submission that any deprivation of a person's right to personal liberty, equality before the law or a fair trial automatically gives to that person the right to enforce those fundamental rights under the Constitution. This, as article 294(1) and (4) clearly shows, includes the pauper's right to court or state appointed counsel whether he asks for it or not. This constitutional provision overrides any statutory provisions as to legal aid for an unrepresented and indigent accused. As clause 2 of the article shows, even Parliament cannot regulate the grant of legal aid in matters relating to the enforcement of these rights. The foregoing submissions are further reinforced by article 19(2) of the Constitution which mandatorily requires that an accused be provided with the services of an interpreter without payment by him. If at such preliminary stage of a criminal proceeding the state is required to pay for such a service, then there seems to be no tangible reason for denying the accused the right to state appointed counsel. This view is further fossilised by the mandatory provision of counsel to persons who reasonably require one for purposes of enforcing their rights in relation to a provision of the Constitution.  If this were not so one may ask what the need is to inform a person arrested, restricted or detained of his right to a lawyer of his choice.55 The requirement that a person charged with an offence is to be informed in detail of the nature of the offence charged; to be given adequate time and facilities for the preparation of his defence; and to be permitted to defend himself in person or by a lawyer of his choice56 stems from the recognition of the fact that a large percentage of the population is illiterate and do not therefore read or understand the English language. In such a situation the services of counsel are indispensable to a fair trial. But where the accused has not the wherewithal to defend himself, the right would become meaningless and, hence the need for state appointed counsel. Such a situation is also true of the intelligent and educated layman who has sometimes no skill in the science of law. The framers of the Constitution, 1992 are rational and intended what is responsible. The framers of the Constitution cannot therefore be said to have ignored the fundamental right to a lawyer in defending rights threatened to be invaded under the Constitution. It is for this reason that provision is made under article 294 in such unambiguous terms for the enforcement and defence of fundamental human rights.

[p.175]

Our Constitution is modelled largely along the lines of the United States Constitution even though ours contains unnecessary details. It would therefore be instructive to refer to the United States constitutional case of Gideon v Wainwright57 where the right of an accused person to be provided with state appointed counsel was discussed. Black J delivering the opinion of the Supreme Court made the following comments on Betts v Brandy:

". . . The fact is that in deciding as it did—that 'appointment of counsel is not a fundamental right, essential to a fair trial'—the court in Betts v Brandy made an abrupt break with its own well-considered precedents. In returning to these old precedents, sounder we believe than the new, we but restore constitutional principles established to achieve a fair system of justice. Not only these precedents but also reason and reflection require us to recognise that in our adversary system of criminal justice, any person haled into court, who is too poor to hire a lawyer, cannot be assured a fair trial unless counsel is provided for him. This seems to us to be an obvious truth. Government, both state and federal, quite properly spend vast sums of money to establish machinery to try defendants accused of crime. Lawyers to prosecute are everywhere deemed essential to protect the public's interest in an orderly society.  Similarly, there are few defendants charged with crime, few indeed, who fail to hire the best lawyers they can get to prepare and present their defenses. That government hires lawyers to prosecute and defendants who have the money hire lawyers to defend are the strongest indications of the widespread belief that lawyers in criminal courts are necessities, not luxuries. The right of one charged with crime to counsel may not be deemed fundamental and essential to fair trials in some countries, but it is in ours . . . This noble ideal cannot be realised if the poor man charged with crime has to face his accusers without a lawyer to assist him . . ."58

The judgment of Sutherland J in Powell v Alabama is also so important to the fundamental right of the accused to state assigned counsel that we deem it wise to quote it in extenso to guide both the bench and the Bar in construing articles 14, 17, 19 and 294 of the Constitution, 1992. He said:

"The right to be heard would be, in many cases, of little avail if it did not comprehend the right to be heard by counsel.  Even the intelligent and educated layman has small and sometimes no skill in the science of law. If charged with crime, he is incapable, generally, of determining for himself whether the indictment is good or bad. He is unfamiliar with the rules of evidence. Left without the aid of Counsel he may be [p.176] put on trial without a proper charge, and convicted upon incomplete evidence, or evidence irrelevant to the issue or otherwise inadmissible. He lacks both the skill and knowledge adequately to prepare his defence, even though he has a perfect one. He requires the guiding hand of counsel at every step in the proceedings against him. Without it, though he be not guilty, he faces the danger of conviction because he does not know how to establish his innocence."59

(Author's emphasis.)

We wish to commend the decisions in Gideon v Wainwright and Powell v Alabama to the Ghanaian Bar and bench as a contrast to the Ghanaian decisions on the right of the poor accused person.  Responsible members of the bench and Bar are aware of those cases. The reasons for not advocating these rights may be due to the perception of both the bench and the Bar as to their role in developing fundamental rights or principles of a constitutional nature. This is the only context in which one may understand the absolute lack of initiative by both the bench and the Bar to extend the scope of the right to counsel in murder trials to include other criminal offences where the accused reasonably requires such assistance as provided for under previous Constitutions and section 110 of Act 372. Inspite of the presence of Act 372 on the statute books since 1971 its relevant provisions have not been employed to advantage.

The perception of the courts particularly the Supreme Court, as the highest court of the land in constitutional adjudication and as engaging in considerations of competing policy interest of society to strike a balance that maximises the enjoyment of liberty and freedoms by individuals, do not appear to have taken root in this country.60 Under the Fourth Republican Constitution the role of the courts, particularly the Supreme Court, in purposive evaluation of conflicting policy consideration to ensure good governance cannot be abdicated. A mechanical approach to constitutional [p.177] adjudication and interpretation as happened under the Constitutions, 1960, 1969 and 1979 will retard rather than enhance an organic growth of a Constitution whose interpretation must of necessity depend on the needs of society at a particular epoch in history.61

DIMENSION OF THE INDIGENT'S RIGHT TO COUNSEL

We have argued that an indigent accused person has the right to state appointed counsel at both the pre-trial and trial proceedings. Even though this right inhered under our Constitutions since 1969, the courts had behaved as if they never existed. Consequently, there are no decided cases on the scope of the indigent's  right to counsel and its accustomed incidents in our criminal jurisprudence.  Having established that the right existed and that it has been given constitutional backing in the Constitution, 1992, we propose to examine briefly what the scope of the right ought to be.

The cases we have discussed in connection with the indigent accused's right to counsel deal with felonies and not misdemeanours.  Our Criminal Code and Criminal Procedure Code62 classify the mode of trial as being summary or on indictment.63 Summary offences are not necessarily limited to misdemeanours even though trials on indictment deal exclusively with felonies.64 Generally speaking a summary trial as the name implies is an expeditious mode of trial and reserved for less serious offences triable by a district court judge or at times a circuit court judge sitting alone. Trials on indictment are generally reserved for the more serious offences and is more formal.  It is tried by a circuit court or a High Court judge sitting with a jury or assessors. However, by the scheme of our Criminal Procedure Code a summary offence does not connote a misdemeanour or a less serious offence. It is an offence which the enactment creating it makes punishable on summary conviction and provides no other mode of trial.65  Thus, even though robbery is triable summarily by the circuit court, the High Court or [p.178]  public tribunal, the penalty for the offence is life imprisonment or death.66 Where the enactment creating the offence does not state the mode of trial but the maximum penalty on first conviction does not exceed six months, it must be tried summarily.67 On the other hand an offence must be tried on indictment if the penalty upon conviction is death or it is one declared by law to be a first degree felony.68 In addition to the foregoing, there are several offences which may be tried by one or the other mode of trial because they have not been specified to be triable either summarily or on indictment.69 The manner in which to proceed with a trial in this third category of offences is left to the discretion of the Attorney-General.70 Thus a second degree felony may be tried either summarily or on indictment — the minimum penalty to be imposed notwithstanding.71 The general understanding that summary offences are offences of a less serious character while indictable offences comprise all offences of a serious character is not applicable to our Criminal Code and Criminal Procedure Code.72 This being the case it does not appear that the distinction between felonies which gives rise to right to counsel and misdemeanours which do not, can find a place in our criminal jurisprudence.73

Its is our opinion that since the courts in upholding the fundamental human rights and freedoms of the individual are compelled to enforce rights not specifically stated in the Constitution but which inhere in a democracy, the proper dominator of the right to counsel ought to be the decisions of the United States Supreme Court in Argersinger v Hamlin74 and Scott v Illinois.75

In Argersinger, the petitioner, an indigent accused was charged with carrying a concealed weapon under Florida law. The offence is punishable by imprisonment of up to six months, or a fine of $1,000 or both. The trial was before a judge without a jury and the petitioner was unrepresented by [p.179] counsel. He was sentenced to 90 days imprisonment. He petitioned the Supreme Court of Florida by habeas corpus, alleging that he had been deprived of his right to counsel. He was consequently unable as an indigent layman properly to raise and present to the trial court good and sufficient defences to the charge against him. The Supreme Court of Florida by a majority of four to three held that the right to court-appointed counsel extended only to trials "for non-petty offences punishable by more than six months' imprisonment." The petitioner petitioned the Supreme Court of the United States for certiorari which was granted reversing the decision of the Supreme Court of Florida. It was held that the right to counsel might well be necessary for a fair trial in petty offences prosecution because the legal and constitutional questions involved in a case that actually led to imprisonment even for a brief period were not only less complex than when a person could be imprisoned for six months or more. The requirement of a fair trial might necessitate the presence of a lawyer even in prosecutions for offences less serious than felonies. The Supreme Court was of the opinion that even on arraignment where an accused pleaded guilty, "a problem which looms large in misdemeanour as well as felony cases", was that the accused needed counsel so that he might know precisely what he was doing in terms of the prospects of going to jail or prison.  This also enabled him to be treated fairly by the prosecution. To avoid the obsession for speedy dispositions of misdemeanours regardless of the fairness of the result, the court concluded that the problems associated with misdemeanour and petty offences often required the presence of counsel to ensure the accused a fair trial. The court however did not consider it necessary to answer Powel J's query whether the right to counsel is applicable to situations where there is no prospect of imprisonment.  It's decision thus appears to be limited to the situation where loss of liberty is involved as in the case under consideration in which the petitioner was sentenced to jail.  The court stated, quoting its previous decision in Baldwin v New York76:

 "  . . . the prospect of imprisonment for however short a time will seldom be viewed by the accused as a trivial or 'petty' matter and may well result in quite serious repercussions affecting his career and reputation."

It could be said that Argersinger applies the "actual imprisonment" standard to determine whether there is a constitutional right of an indigent to court appointed counsel.

In Scott v Illinois76a the court refused to apply the Sixth and Fourteenth Amendments right to appointed counsel beyond the actual imprisonment standard put forward in Argersinger. The petitioner in that case, an indigent [p.180] was convicted of shoplifting and fined $50 after a summary trial in an Illinois state court. The penalty for the offence was set by the relevant statute at $500 fine, or one year in jail, or both.  The petitioner's conviction was affirmed by the Supreme Court of Illinois in spite of the petitioner's contention that a line of cases culminating in Argersinger v Hamlin require the state provision of counsel whenever imprisonment is an authorised penalty. The court was of the view that the Sixth and Fourteenth Amendments did not apply to a petitioner who was charged with a statutory offence for which imprisonment upon conviction was authorised but not imposed.

The decision in Scott was a close 5-4 majority decision and it may well be that in compelling circumstance the courts may require appointment of counsel under the Sixth and Fourteenth Amendments for misdemeanours and petty offences.77

Under the legal Aid Act, 1974 and the Legal Aid Act, 1982 of England the right to appoint counsel is not limited to felony cases. Indigent accused persons are allowed the right to counsel in summary trials emanating from hybrid offences under the Acts.78  In view of the fact that the mode of trial in Ghana is not dictated by the seriousness of the offence we are inclined to suggest that our courts ought to be guided by the actual imprisonment standard in deciding the scope of the right to appointment of counsel under articles 14, 19 and 294 of the Constitution, 1992.  Where no imprisonment will result and therefore personal liberty is not impaired, the right to state-appointed counsel may not be reasonably required under article 294 for the enforcement of the fundamental right to personal liberty and fair trial under the Constitution.

CONCLUSION

We have in this country a Legal Aid Law79 which sets up a Legal Aid Board to administer a legal aid scheme under the Law.  We have not in our discussion adverted to its provisions because legal aid under the Law is a privilege and not a right. The cumbersome process and requirements  involved do not make it useful to many indigent arrested, restricted, detained or accused persons as it is dependent on an extra-judicial application to a Board by the affected person.  His case may well have been disposed of [p.181] before it is granted.80 Our previous Constitutions, and the present Constitution, 1992 confer a right to counsel on an indigent accused. The right to counsel as we have attempted to show is a reasonable requirement for an indigent person who has to enforce his fundamental right of personal liberty, respect for human dignity, equality before the law, fair trial and his right to legal aid under article 294. The prospect of imprisonment may well result in quite serious repercussions affecting the indigent citizens's fundamental human rights and freedoms and ought to be defended vigorously by the courts under the Constitution.

It is our hope that the vision of the framers of the Constitution to secure the rule of law and liberty for our people will be relentlessly pursued in granting the right to appoint counsel to indigent accused by the courts who have for long claimed to be bastions of human rights and liberty.
