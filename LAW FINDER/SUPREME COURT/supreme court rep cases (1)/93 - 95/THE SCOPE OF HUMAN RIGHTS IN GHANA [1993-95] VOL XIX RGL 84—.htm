THE SCOPE OF HUMAN RIGHTS IN GHANA [1993-95] VOL XIX RGL 84—104

HON JUSTICE J N K TAYLOR

 

*

The history of mankind has been dominated by a struggle for human rights in a desperate search for justice.  It has been a progressive erosion and gradual repudiation of autocracy or the rule of dictators and tyrants in favour of democracy under the rule of law. History completely demonstrates that men and women in all societies are able to enjoy their inalienable rights as human beings when laws rather than the arbitrary activities of their fellow men direct their affairs.1 The traditional spheres in which the rights of human beings function are the public domain and the private domain. Within these two spheres, the influence of British imperialist ideas on our entitlement to human rights is to identify five conceptual levels at which the human rights of Ghanaians operate. These levels are in ascending order of operation: (a) the customary family unit; (b) particular customary communities2; (c) municipal statute law3; (d) the Constitution4; and (e) the international level.5

There are within the general concept of humanity, classes or groups to which distinct rights are accorded by virtue of the fact that their weak positions demand such concessions. These are young persons and children6 as well as the sick and the disabled7 who are collectively included in the concept of the human race, descriptively referred to as "mankind." The term "mankind" or "humanity" biologically embraces the male and female of the specie; hence, the true scope or dynamics of the human rights notion is concerned with the exercise of these rights by men and women in the entire world.  A logical analysis of all forms of human rights discloses political rights exercisable in the public domain and civil rights which operate in the private sector of human activity. Thus the entire notion of human rights consists of political and civil rights.  Having regard to the types of activity which human beings by nature normally engage in, one can also monitor the exercise of human rights in terms of the economic, social and cultural activities and practices of men and women. This way of considering human rights enables us to divide all rights into political and civil rights and their [p.85] exercise in the economic, social and cultural spheres.

As I have just pointed out, the exercise of all forms of human rights takes place in the civil or political plane.8 The civil involves the exercise of economic, social and cultural rights.9 The average male has always had more physical strength of the animal variety than his normal female counterpart. This physical strength manifests itself in violent effusions and incipient actions in all manner of ways. The actual use of this strength has enabled groups of men throughout the tortuous history of mankind to dominate their fellow men. Its psychological effect as well as the threat of its use has cowed down the female of the specie in almost all countries of the world, and inhibited their entitlement of human rights on a basis of equality with men. This is apparently the reason for differing human rights norms in male and female societies.

Even at this moment in human history, with mankind's march to higher civilised standards and living conditions, the rights of women who form half of the human race are in a perilous state. With mankind's traumatic experience of two devastating world wars in modern times comes the intellectual realisation that violence or the threat of violence is merely a bad and a very crude emotional demonstration of an unacceptable tendency in human nature; and that a healthy beneficent and praiseworthy manifestation of our common humanity is an intelligent consideration of problems and the fastening on of solutions by the weighing and selection of options based on man's intellectual capabilities rather than his emotional reactions.  It is the ability to so function on the intellectual plane that stamps mankind or humanity as a very sublime and the most superior of all living creatures.

A discussion of the scope of human rights, having regard to man's vaunted superiority in the natural world, inevitably encompasses a consideration of the purview of these rights as a global notion and as a municipal concept. The intellectual exercise involved in the discussion necessarily requires the focusing of attention on the subject matter of human rights.  Human rights as a notion, when thus viewed, are seen as primarily and exclusively concerned with only one single subject matter and that is: the "human being." On further consideration it is apparent that the idea of human rights can be definitively described as the activities which human beings, both male and female, consider themselves to be entitled to indulge in without any interference whatsoever from any authority or their fellow human beings. Clearly, therefore, these activities or the entitlement to be involved in them are claimed by men and women on the basic justification that they the claimants are human beings. There is no other rational foundation supporting human rights claims. On further reflection, it would [p.86] seem that the human activities of all persons, having regard to the norms operating in their societies, can be subjected to a test as to whether as humans in the respective societies, they are entitled to engage or be involved in such activities or not. If they are by the traditions and understanding operating in the society entitled to be involved in such activities, then they have passed the test and their activities fall within the scope of human rights. And under such circumstances, there must be an obligation reposing in other persons or authority to respect and protest the exercise of these rights. lf however they fail the test, then their activities fall outside the scope of human rights and the claims will be aberrant.

As already indicated, human beings, the subject of human rights, are biologically and physically divided into male and female and within that dual categorisation there are classified groupings dictated by the natural phenomenon associated with human life and thus peculiarly characteristic of the human person. Looking at this human person, progressively from a period before birth, to birth, childhood, adulthood, old age and finally death,10 groupings necessarily emerge. Besides adult persons of sound mind11 and body, one can identify human groups within the matrix of the human family, the subjects of human rights, as generally infants and children, young persons, the aged, the sick and the disabled.

With this analysis the concept and problems that arise within the scope of human rights can be meaningfully dealt with under four topics by considering first, the topic of the rights of man; the word "man" is used comprehensively to embrace men and women. Secondly, women's rights because of the peculiar status of inferiority which men had consigned to the rights of women. Thirdly, the rights of children and of the sick and disabled will be discussed. Finally, the substantive nature of these rights which are inextricably wound with the first topic will be emphasized in the fourth topic as they have been traditionally recognised in the modern world as consisting of economic, social and cultural rights.

The Rights of Man (including woman)

The notion of the rights of man and the dimension of the practices which have come to be regarded as the scope and focus of these rights have been largely embedded in customary understandings which human societies have considered to be the bedrock and the fundamentals of their culture. The history of the development of these rights is also the history of the development of the notion of the rule of law.  It parallels the concept of government as it had been coloured and sustained in political organisations throughout the tortuous history of the human race.

[p.87]

The philosophical foundation for the early beginnings of the concept of the rights of man is the ancient and medieval theory of the divine right of kings. Token resistance to the claims of these autocratic pretenders culminated in King Charles of England losing his head in the seventeenth century. In the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, and in some societies even in the twentieth century, this obsolete and unenlightened tradition of "the divine right of Kings" to rule over their subjects still prevails. It was sponsored by both the clergy and secular  leaders of thought in many jurisdictions. Its modern advocate is the brilliant orator, Edmund Burke, who, echoing a dying cultural past, argued passionately in favour of despotism by submitting naively that the doctrine of the "rights of man" would lead to "the utter subversion not only of all Government in all modes but all stable securities to national freedom and all the rules and principles of morality itself."12

Even from this narrow perspective, it is apparent that the human rights concept is being projected on the public sphere. It is in effect an effort at mapping out areas of public life where the ruler or the ruling elite or government has the right to control and regulate the life and affairs of the populace. In contrast to this, the areas of personal choice or action and interpersonal relations and relationships where the authorities should not and would not be able to control and regulate people's affairs inevitably become the private sphere. This classification into private and public spheres is significant because, with regard to the so-called private sphere, a further conceptional dichotomy exists in identifying areas of human activities in the said private sphere where government control or regulation is traditionally conceded. The areas where such interference is usually frowned upon and invariably repudiated is under modern conditions described as the domain of the individual's right to privacy.13

The repudiation means that far from the government interfering with the exercise of rights in the private domain, it rather assumes an obligation, for instance, to protect the individual in the exercise of his or her right to privacy in the private domain without molestation from any other authority or person. This right to privacy includes, for example, the right to choose with whom to speak or associate, the right to choose with whom to have commercial or economic dealings, the right to choose with whom to have intimate relations including sexual relationship and its concomitant right of an inherent discretion to choose whether or not to conceive with its supplementary right of a woman to decide whether or not to terminate her pregnancy.

These rights of such private nature have not necessarily been traditionally protected nor are they even now universally recognised as rights demanding [p.88] protection in all countries. Laws forbidding inter-racial marriages were once widely prevalent in America and were common place in apartheid South Africa. In the common law world, reproductive freedom is almost always very severely controlled by criminal legislation designed to inhibit women in their exercise of their right to decide not to carry a pregnancy to term. Very few rights better deserve to be properly described as private relative to this right, as Mr Justice Harry Blackman so ably reminded us as recently as 1986 when in the US Supreme Court he poignantly proclaimed:

"Few decisions are more personal and intimate, more properly private or more basic to individual dignity and autonomy than a woman's decision ... Whether to end her pregnancy."14

The contours of this right to privacy need to be theoretically formulated and legally articulated to demystify its dimensions and to introduce clarity into its areas of operation. This is because it is a constant source of confrontation between individuals and executive authority.  It must be observed that the right to privacy is not the same as an unqualified freedom from governmental interference with activities within the family or in the so-called private sphere.  It does depend historically and traditionally on the nature of the relevant activity, having regard to the cultural norms operating at a particular point of time in the particular society as well as constitutional and international obligations which the society has legally accepted.

In this regard, it is noteworthy that all societies and all countries do interfere under custom or usage or positive law with internal and what obviously appears to be private matters, including what are unmistakably private family and personal matters. They do so through regulating marriage and divorce and many customary and legal practices ranging from rules prohibiting incest and other forms of sexual relationships to those providing the mode of inheritance of privately owned movable and immovable properties.15

Violations of the right to privacy and the state's inertia or apparent lethargy and consistent reluctance to protect this and some other rights have been notably prevalent throughout history and they persist even in this era of improved scientific achievements and high technology in the prevailing and enlightened conditions of modern civilization. In the private domain in many countries including Ghana, women's property rights along with their right to choose whom to marry as well as their right to divorce are greatly curtailed and thus severely compromised.16 A classical traumatic case in point is [p.89] revealed in the genital mutilation of girls and women in many jurisdictions despite the fact that it is common knowledge that such practices at times kill, maim and blight the physical and psychological health of millions of women and girls every year in many parts of Africa and Asia.17

The varying practices of clitoridectomy are clearly crude and unacceptable violations of the human rights of girls and women. It consists in some cases in the cutting off of the clitoris apparently as a calculated design to deprive women of sexual pleasure and thus inhibit probably their suspected desire for sexual experience outside marriage. In some cases, it involves cutting off the labia and tightly sewing the vaginal orifice in order to make sexual intercourse a painful experience or actually impossible for the female involved until a larger opening is subsequently cut before marriage. Among African nations that have begun to take measures against sexual mutilations are Egypt, Kenya and Sudan where in 1979 the Khartoum seminar organised by the World Health Organisation was held and recommendations were made to eradicate these outrageous practices.18 Many tribes in northern Ghana, notably the Sissala and the Grunshie practice female circumcision and apart from a not too serious tinkering with the practice by the imperialist government, the indigenous governments since independence have shown no policy interest whatsoever in the practices and their eradication.

Women's Rights

There is a gradual recognition by the enlightened world that women in all countries are entitled to the "exercise and enjoyment of human rights and fundamental freedoms on a basis of equality with men."19 It is noteworthy that since 1948, the civilized world, acting through the United Nations, have expressed recognition by declaring without reservation that the inherent dignity and inalienable right of all the male and female members of the human family is the foundation of freedom, justice and peace in the world.20 They also reaffirmed and declared their faith in fundamental human rights and in the dignity and worth of the human person as well as in the equal [p.90] rights of men and women.21

This recognition and affirmation of faith in human rights led to the formulation of a declaration that everyone is entitled to all the rights and freedoms "without distinction of any kind, such as race, colour, sex, language, religion, political or other opinion, national or social origin, property, birth or other status."22 This declaration by the United Nations, sponsoring as it does an inherent concept of the rights of women on a level of equality with men, has not been matched by practices in many countries. Since the declaration on 10 December 1948, blatant violations of women's rights have prevailed and continue unto this day throughout the world, particularly in the developing countries. As a consequence, the world body has consistently since 1948 made praiseworthy efforts to correct or ameliorate this anomalous imbalance by subsequent declarations and conventions.23

In almost all societies in the world, both primitive and civilized, a lower status has been given to women from a period before the dawn of recorded history to modern times. Until the holocaust of the first world war and the Nazi atrocities of the second world war engendered the formation of the League of Nations and the United Nations respectively, no articulated recognition was ever given to women's rights on the basis of equality with men's. Typical of the crude and untidy thinking of the arrogant male before the enlightenment of modern times, is the effusions of respectable philosophers like John Locke in the seventeenth century and JJ Rousseau in the eighteenth century. These were men whose ideas inspired the French Revolution and offered the philosophical and ethical justification and impetus to the American rebellion.

Yet in their writings on the rights of man, they articulated and apparently approved the now obsolete notion and cultural understanding of a past era, which defined men as individuals innately possessed of certain "natural rights" while women were considered not as individuals as such but as members of a man's household and thus with their children under the control of men. Theoretically the rights so accorded to men in their formulation and conception of the rights of man has been in their view: [p.91] "men's inalienable right to life, liberty and property."24 In this formulation "men" excludes women who by the tradition of the past were under the power and dominion of men.

Indeed, this concept of the power of the male over the female of the species, is under English common law, psychologically an attenuated equation of the so-called "divine right of the king" to rule over the populace.25 After the traumatic events of the two world wars, the United Nations rejected the unequal treatment meted to women which is inherent in the inferior status traditionally accorded them. The world body unambiguously conceded equality in resounding language and commended fair treatment in the very first article of its Declaration of Human Rights where it is clearly stated:

"All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights. They are endowed with reason and conscience and should act towards one another in a spirit of brotherhood."

(Author's emphasis.)

Specific Rights and Freedoms

The rights which have been daubed "human", whether classified as fundamental or not, are the foundation of the international community's concept of democracy.26  They are considered to be "the foundation of freedom, justice and peace in the world"27 and are indeed universally recognised to be derivable from the inherent dignity and equality of all persons constituting the human family.28 That the framers of our Constitution were greatly influenced by these international perspectives is reflected in the Ghana constitutional mandate that functionaries, including the judiciary, should be guided by international human rights instruments which recognise and apply particular categories of human rights in the development processes. 29

[p.92]

The Ghana Constitution also urged these State functionaries to adhere specifically to international law, the principles enshrined in the United Nations Charter (this of course includes human rights provisions) and the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) Treaty.30 Persons in Ghana, and in fact all other persons affected by the exercise of State or other power, have as much right to have these powers exercised in accordance with law as the wielders of these powers also have the right to exercise such powers over them in accordance with the enabling legal provisions. Thus, those governing as well as the governed acquire rights which are human rights because the donees of these rights are all in essence human beings even when they operate under the veil of constitutional bodies.

Most of the rights under discussion arise under the general laws of the land. Many of the said rights, whether under the general laws or not, are nonetheless considered so essential that they are classified as fundamental rights and freedoms and are specifically enshrined in the Constitution, 1992, of Ghana to secure the dignity and freedom of Ghanaians and as cornerstones of ordered liberty and bastions of democracy.31 The said Constitution, 1992 guarantees these fundamental rights and freedoms to all persons in Ghana, whatever their race, place of origin, political opinion, colour, religion, creed or gender.32 The Constitution specifically provides that these rights be respected and upheld by the Executive, the Legislature, the Judiciary and all other organs of government and its agencies and in appropriate cases, by all natural and legal persons.33

The Constitution, 1992 is unique and unprecedented in the annals of Ghana's constitutional history.  It is the one and only Constitution of Ghana, which can be said to have been definitely adopted, enacted and actually accepted by the people of Ghana for the people of Ghana. This was done in a true and real exercise in a referendum of their proclaimed natural and inalienable right to establish a framework of government with the avowed aim of securing for themselves and their descendants, the blessings of liberty, equality of opportunity and prosperity.34

The framework of government that was devised to fulfil these noble objectives has five essential pillars as superstructures supporting the whole edifice.35 The mix of the first pillar consists of: "Freedom and Justice, Probity and Accountability." The second pillar is anchored on "the principle that all powers of Government spring from the sovereign will of the people."

[p.93]

Its abiding third pillar, which stamps it as a democratic system, is its fidelity to "the principles of universal adult suffrage." The bastion of its strength, its pervasive fourth pillar, is the adherence to the "rule of law." And finally, its fifth pillar is the paramountcy of its dynamic commitment to "the protection and preservation of fundamental human rights and freedoms, unity and stability for the nation."36

The estates of the realm, charged with the responsibilities of functioning within this framework to fulfil the commitments in the Constitution in order to promote the welfare of the people and satisfy their legitimate aspirations are the executive,37 the legislature,38 the judiciary39 and apparently the media.40 It is noteworthy that these establishments are all institutions manned by human beings who are citizens of Ghana obliged by the Constitution, their source of power, to exercise their authority for the welfare and in the interests of their fellow human beings.41 These estates, as agents of the Republic, have only one paramount duty and no others. They must minister to the welfare of the people and promote their interests, well alive to their true and single function which is "the protection of individuals and their property."42  Hayfron-Benjamin J (as he then was) must have had this in mind when be observed:

". . . the power given to any sovereign or government has no other end than the preservation of life, liberty and pursuit of happiness, and therefore it 'can never have a right to destroy, enslave, or designedly impoverish the subjects." '43

The final responsibility for enforcing the human rights provisions of the Constitution is exclusively reposed in the courts.44 The judiciary fulfils this role in the name of the Republic and does so as an independent body subject only to the Constitution and outside the control or direction of any person or authority.45 For this purpose final judicial power is vested in the judiciary in such a manner that neither the President nor the legislature, nor indeed any [p.94] other person or authority, can lawfully defy or legally challenge the final exercise of judicial power by the judiciary, acting through the Supreme Court.46

The implication of the Supreme Court's exercise of final judicial power can have far-reaching consequences for the integrity of the human rights provisions in the fair and proper administration of justice. To err is human; and where a decision of the Supreme Court is demonstrably erroneous, it would seem to be morally wrong for the finality provision to perpetuate it. Surely its consequence for the human rights of the citizen involved can be traumatic and disastrous. Fortunately for our constitutional system, the Supreme Court is not bound by its own previous decisions. The position is that while treating its previous decisions as normally binding, it has constitutional authority in prescribed or exceptional cases to review its decisions and depart from them if it is right to do so.47

A further implication of this concept of final judicial power as is thus enshrined in the Constitution is the idea that any judicial power exercised by any person or authority or organ of State is subject to the supervisory48 or appellate49 jurisdiction of the judiciary. As I have already indicated, under the Constitution, 1992 and previous ones,50 all organs of State including the judiciary are mandated to exercise their powers for the welfare and in the interest of the people in the manner and within the limits laid down in the Constitution.51 Under this scheme of things the Constitution is the fundamental law. In this respect, it is interesting to note that we share with our American friends, this culture of a higher law, the product of Western European experience, which as Professor Capelletti points out is the result of ". . . centuries of European thought and colonial experiences, which had made western man in general willing to admit the theoretical primacy of certain kinds of law . . ."52

The hard core premise of this "higher law" notion, is that it embodies higher principles than other laws and thus enjoys a primacy that is unique. Its imperative logic which would seem to have impressed the fathers of our Constitutions, is that it is so supreme that any other law in a constitutional [p.95] democracy which is found to be inconsistent with it should be considered null and void.53 The practical legal effect of this transcendent primacy is that any act done under any law or any act or omission which is inconsistent with the human rights provisions or any other provision of the Constitution is unlawful. Whether it is to the detriment of a complainant or not is quite irrelevant to the Constitutional right of that complainant to mount an action in the Supreme Court for a declaration that the said act or omission is unlawful.54

It is common knowledge that the Parliament of the Fourth Republic has not as yet enacted laws. The administration of the State is in the circumstance based on the existing laws.55 These comprise the written and unwritten laws of Ghana as they existed immediately before the coming into force of the Constitution on 7 January 1993. Included in these existing laws are any Act, Decree, Law, or statutory instrument, issued or made before 7 January 1993, but which is to come into force on or after that date.56  The most dramatic effect of the Constitution on the existing laws is that they are all to be construed with such modifications, adaptations, qualifications and exceptions as are necessary to bring into conformity with the provisions of the Constitution or otherwise to give effect or enable effect to be given to any changes effected by the Constitution.57 The purport of this modification clause is clear. As from 7 January 1993, the Constitution and existing laws not inconsistent with it, are the only true measure of legality in Ghana.58  This means that all the laws that are derogatory of the human rights provisions of the constitution are subject to modification to conform to these rights. If however they are not merely derogatory, but inconsistent or conflicting and  cannot be saved by adaptation and qualification then they cease to be part of the laws of Ghana.

The responsibility for ensuring that the existing laws are modified and made consistent with the Constitution devolves on the courts, when in cases brought before them the construction of any provision of the existing law necessitates the requisite modification.  In this connection, in order to ensure a coherent legal order devoid of conflicts and contradictions, the first President under the Fourth Republic is given temporary power at any time within twelve months after assuming office, to harmonise the existing laws [p.96] by constitutional instrument to attain conformity with the Constitution without waiting for cases to go to the courts or for Parliament to pass the necessary legislation.59

An outstanding and unique feature of the Constitution, 1992 by which it practically parted ways with virtually all previous Constitutions of Ghana is the explicitly enabling and comprehensively all-embracing nature of the jurisdiction of the High Court, the only superior court that traditionally exercises original jurisdiction. The way jurisdiction had been reposed in it in past Constitutions and in legislation had created doubts as to the ambit of its jurisdiction in certain matters. Henceforth, and subject only to the Constitution, the High Court is vested since 7 January 1993 with jurisdiction in all matters.60 The jurisdiction so given to the High Court and the further enforcement jurisdiction in relation to the fundamental human rights guaranteed by the Constitution,61 mean that neither the President nor Parliament or any other State authority can lawfully restrict the High Court in the exercise of its judicial function in adjudication. This is significant for the survival of human rights.

Under the previous Constitutions, the judges had vacillated on the ambit of statutory provisions purporting to restrict the perceived constitutional jurisdiction of the High Court. One group of judges repudiated the claim that statutory provisions were capable of restricting the jurisdiction of the High Court.62 A second school of thought conceded constriction of the jurisdiction of the High Court and drew inspiration from the fact that an apparent reading of the legislative measures in the Chieftaincy Act63 and the Courts Act64 would seem to support their contention.65

The apparent conflict or doubt has now been constitutionally settled by the Constitution, 1992 in favour of unrestricted jurisdiction in the High Court, and so all the laws passed by previous legislatures including the erstwhile Provisional National Defence Council which seek to restrict the [p.97] jurisdiction of the High Court to protect and enforce fundamental human rights are all redundant and void, and unless protected by the Constitution they can be so declared by the superior courts.66 It would seem therefore that PNDCL4,67 PNDCL31,68 PNDCL9169 and quite recently PNDCL32570 and all other laws inconsistent with the human rights provisions of the Constitution have been rendered redundant and ineffective by the Constitution with effect from 7 January 1993.

The said human rights provisions, the right to enforce which is guaranteed to all persons in Ghana,71 have been specifically enumerated and their respective ambits meticulously set down in considerable detail in the Constitution.72 Interestingly, virtually all the provisions so set out in the said Constitution are covered by similar provisions contained in the United Nations Universal Declaration of Human Rights.73 The said rights so specifically promulgated in our Constitution appear to be contained in eighteen articles although they cover 25 specified rights. The order of their appearance and their subject matters are as follows:

1  Article 13 deals with the protection of the right to life and this is contained in article 3 of the UN Declaration.

2   Article 14 covers the right to personal liberty which also is in article  3 of the UN Declaration.

3  Article 15 is concerned with human dignity and immunity from torture and cruel and inhuman treatment.  Its counterpart is in articles 1 and 5 of the UN Declaration.

4  Article 16 guarantees protection from slavery and forced labour. It is the same as that of article 4 of the UN Declaration.

5  Article 17 dealing with equality and freedom from discrimination is in article 7 of the UN Declaration.

6  For article 18 dealing with the protection of privacy of home and other properties.  See article 17 of the UN Declaration.

7   Article 19 the fair trial provision is in article 10 of the UN Declaration.

8 Article 20 giving protection from deprivation of property is in [p.98] article 17(2) of the UN Declaration.

9  Article 21(1)(a) concerning freedom of speech and expression including freedom of the press and other media is in article 19 of the UN Declaration.

10 Article 21(1)(b) guaranteeing freedom of thought, conscience and belief including academic freedom finds place in article 18 of the UN Declaration.

11  Article 21(1)(c) conceding the freedom to practice any religion and to manifest such practice is also in article 18 of the UN Declaration.

12 Article 21(1)(d): dealing with the freedom of assembly including freedom to take part in processions and demonstrations are included in article 20 of the UN Declaration.

13 Article 21(1)(e) concerning freedom of association including freedom to form or join trade unions or other associations, national and international is found in article 20 of the UN Declaration.

14  Article 21(1)(f) concerns the right to information, subject to such qualification and laws as are necessary in a democratic society. This would seem to be inherent in article 30 of the UN Declaration.

15  Article 21(1)(g) deals with freedom of movement.  See article 13 of the UN Declaration.

16  Article 22 guaranteeing the right of spouses to the protection of their property rights seems to be covered by article 16 of the UN Declaration.

17  Article 23 providing for the right to a fair hearing before administrative bodies and officials is similar to article 8 of the UN Declaration.

18  Article 24 deals with the right to work and the rights of workers. See article 23 of the UN Declaration.

19  Article 25(1) provides for the right to equal educational opportunities and facilities and to free and compulsory basic educations. Compare article 26 of the UN Declaration.

20 Article 25(2) concerns the right to establish and maintain private school or schools in accordance with law.  See article 26 of the UN Declaration again.

21  Article 26 protects cultural rights and practices. See articles 22 and 27 of the UN Declaration.

22  Article 27 covers women's rights. See articles 2 and 29(2) of the UN Declaration.

23  Article 28 protects children's rights. See also articles 2 and 29(2) of the UN Declaration.

24  Article 29 concerns the rights of the disabled. See articles 2 and 29(2) of the UN Declaration.

25 Article 30 provides for the rights of the sick. See articles 2 and 29(2) of the UN Declaration.

[p.99]

These then are the specifics of the fundamental human rights enshrined in the Constitution, 1992. Although we are part of the United Nations, there are no specific legal sanctions ensuring compliance by our government with the United Nation's intention that these rights be protected. The United Nations strategy for ensuring compliance with the terms of the Declaration was to have a multilateral covenant of member states by which the rights declared are formulated. Thereafter state parties who wish to be bound by the terms of the covenant, deposit instruments of ratification or instruments of accession and the covenant becomes operative after the thirty-fifth instrument of ratification or accession has been deposited in accordance with the provision of the covenant. On 16 December 1966 two covenants were adopted and opened for signature, ratification and accession.74 On the same day some state parties to the covenant on civil and political rights agreed to an optional protocol setting up a Human Rights Committee (HRC), to receive and consider communications from individuals claiming to be victims of violations of the human rights set forth in the covenant, who have exhausted all available domestic remedies.75 It would seem that as Ghana did not subscribe to the covenant or the optional protocol, any further consideration of these international instruments is academic. Their consideration becomes only necessary because the Constitution enjoins respect for international law.76

The jurisprudence sustaining the common law foundation of the Anglo-Ghanaian constitutional order is bedevilled by the emergence of written constitutions. This has become noticeable after the new breed of our indigenous lawyers and judges trained in the Inns of Court had learnt of the Blackstonian concept of parliamentary sovereignty.77 This has given rise to dilemmas in judicial interpretation of the human rights of Ghanaians after military dictators had interfered with the constitutional government. Although the military rulers purport to seize power in the interest of the people,78 some lawyers and judges hold the view that by their seizure, the [p.100] military regime repudiates the human rights entitlement of the people.79 Others hold a contrary view.80

The nature of the problem underlying the dilemma of our courts raised in these opposing views, has been identified by a Canadian writer who points out that:

"The problem consists in devising means for the protection and enhancement of individual human rights in a manner consistent with the democratic basis of our institution."81

Lord Scarman of the English House of Lords noticed a similar problem in the early American effort at constitutional interpretation, when the US Constitution began to function in 1789. He identified and traced the cause to the drafting techniques of the period and reminded us that the draftsmen of the United States Constitution were:

". . . lawyers brought up in the Middle Temple and other Inns, making sure that for the protection of individuals ... the English common law with the powers of the monarch removed should become the charter for basic human rights."82

Our judges and lawyers and legal draftsmen are in the same category and our draftsmen in particular have been similarly motivated and disciplined.

Our superior courts are manned by learned judges who qualify to be appointed to their office on the basis of their experience for a considerable number of years as lawyers and who are of high moral character and proven integrity.83 On the other hand, the vast majority of Ghanaians have had poor education and most of them are even illiterate.  In the circumstances, judges dealing with a written Constitution have a duty when delivering their decisions touching the human rights of the people to publicly expound the law in a manner that will inform and educate the people and foster the [p.101] growth and respect of human rights norms. The role of the judiciary in this respect, in thus shaping public opinion with the intent of promoting in the people and fostering in them development of human rights norms, was articulated by Eugene Rostow in the context of constitutional practice in the United States, a country with which we share a culture of operating a written Constitution. He rightly observed that:

"The process of forming opinion ... is a continuous one with many participants . . . the President, the Press, political parties, scholars, pressure groups, and so on. The discussion of problems and declaration of broad principles by the court is a Vital element in the community experience through which ... policy is made. The Supreme Court is amongst other things an educational body and the justices are inevitably teachers in a vital national seminar."84

In shaping opinion the judges are bound as members of their respective societies to reflect by their decisions the broad human rights values which the society aspires to cultivate and protect.

In this respect, it is worthy of note that the United States Supreme Court has been singularly successful in its praiseworthy endeavour to carry out this task. It has done so with zeal and courage in many cases where their Constitution has not spelled out the parameters of the precise choice of values calling for exposition and protection. We find them for instance grappling bravely with the issue of the compulsory sterilisation of the mentally subnormal.85 They faced squarely the social and constitutional problems, not an easy one to solve, of deciding whether minimum wages laws could be enacted by their legislature.86 They were not cowed as our judges would probably have been, when called upon to decide, whether their President was subject to the criminal law process.87 They were undaunted by the qualms of conscience associated with the difficulty inherent in the controversial question as to whether their constitution prohibits laws, restricting access to abortion.88 They wrestled with the novel problems as to whether married couples could lawfully use contraceptive devices.89 They did not flinch from conscientiously deciding the highly charged emotive question [p.102] whether school children could be required by law to salute the national flag.90 The catalogue is endless of judicial activism in the United States in the protection of human rights. It has been very few and far between in the Ghana judicial scene, and some of the judges who have made the effort, have either perished or lived to rue the day.

With regard to the commitment of the legislature in fostering human rights, it is pertinent to remember that the scope of human rights is, in fact, a focus on the provisions which custom or positive law indicates as proper rights to be protected. By tradition, legislators have a primary responsibility to formulate and promote human rights by appropriate enactment. This is a task for which the moral character, proper upbringing and the integrity of the legislators and their commitment to universal ethical standards enter the equation. In this connection it is rewarding to ponder on the observation concerning South African law makers made by Donald Woods, a self-exiled South African journalist, as he surveyed apartheid legislation directed at emasculating human rights.  He said:

"The obscene laws which constitute apartheid are not crazed edicts issued by dictators or the whims of a megalomaniac monster or the one man decisions of a fanatical idealogue. They are the result of polite caucus discussions by hundreds of delegates in sober suits after full debate in party congresses. They are passed after three solemn readings in Parliament which opens each day's proceedings with a prayer to Jesus Christ. There is a special horror in that fact."91

It would seem quite clear that the legislature and judiciary are the principal estates that can keep a recalcitrant executive and ill-motivated persons from degrading, derogating or destroying the human rights of people. The obsessive reliance on these bodies may however be disastrous having regard to the salutary caveat delivered by the celebrated American judge and jurist Learned Hand, when he lamented:

"I often wonder whether we do not rest our hopes too much upon Constitutions, upon laws and upon courts. These are false hopes; believe me they are false hopes, liberty lies in the hearts of men and women; when it dies, there is no Constitution, no law, no court can save it; no Constitution, no law, no court can even do much to help it. While [p.103] it lies there, it needs no Constitution, no law, no court to save it."92

The truth of this statement by Learned Hand becomes poignant when we try to understand the forces at work in the world that produced the two world wars. Even at the highest intellectual level at which enlightened men function there are conflicts of ideas. In this country, in our own judiciary, we have experienced unresolved conflicts.93

There is this story that two of the greatest figures in American legal history, Justice Holmes and Judge Learned Hand, had lunch together and afterward, as Holmes began to drive off in his carriage, Learned Hand ran over to him and shouted: "Do justice, Sir, do justice." Justice Holmes stopped the carriage and reproached Judge Hand saying: "That is not my job. It is my job to apply the law."94 Such differences of opinion need not result in violent exchanges and bloodshed.

The history of mankind, however, does show that such differences of opinion have given rise to human rights abuses that are amazing. In some countries the abuses are blatant and cowardly, in others they are subtle and hypocritical. Due to time constraints it is not possible to examine these abuses.

If we are able to do so however, our efforts will cover the crude and amazing antics of many African countries; a few random examples of which may include Angola, Ethiopia, Lesotho, Ghana, Malawi, Liberia, Nigeria, Seychelles, South Africa, Togo, Zambia, Zimbabwe and many others. We will have to deal with the bizarre hypocritical posture adopted by many of the countries of the Americas like Argentina, Bolivia, Haiti, United States of America, Venezuela and others in defiance of the human rights of man. Our efforts must come to grips with the incredibly subtle and inscrutable savagery of the Asian countries like Afghanistan, Bangladesh, India, Japan, Kampuchea, the Koreas, Laos, Thailand, Vietnam and many others. We cannot ignore the pretensions of the European nations like Albania, the Federal Republic of Germany, France, the Democratic Republic of Germany, the Hellenic Republic of Greece, Italy, Poland, Switzerland, Spain, USSR, the United Kingdom and many others where fantastic and sophisticated barbarism against the human rights of people have been doctored scientifically and shamefully to a fine art; nor can we forget the unpredictable and extravagantly inhuman proclivities of the Middle East and the Arab nations, like Algeria, Iran, Libya, lsrael, Lebanon, Tunisia, Yemen and others. The calendar of human rights violations is incredible with many of the Caribbean nations included in the obviously shameful bills of [p.104] indictment.95

The mounting of such a gigantic exercise, as I have pointed out, will be time consuming and will not serve any useful purpose and so those of you whose interest have been whetted by any references to all these countries can satisfy your curiosity by studying the Amnesty International documentation and appraisal contained in that august body's report.96
