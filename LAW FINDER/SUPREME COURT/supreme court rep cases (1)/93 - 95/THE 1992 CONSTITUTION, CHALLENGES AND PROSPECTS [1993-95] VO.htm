THE 1992 CONSTITUTION, CHALLENGES AND PROSPECTS [1993-95] VOL. XIX 28—40*

BY HON MR JUSTICE N Y B ADADE, JUSTICE OF THE SUPREME COURT

 

The Constitution of 1992 has respectable antecedents in the Constitutions of 1969 and 1979. When the Government of the First Republic was overthrown in 1966, few gave the impression of a realisation that we had overthrown a constitutional order. To them we had done away with a dictatorship. That was enough. The government which succeeded it lasted roughly three and a half years, ie February 1966—August 1969. Immediately after the overthrow, both the government and people plunged into finding out "what went wrong" with the First Republic. How did it happen that the euphoria of the late 1950s (remember we had our independence in 1957) quickly turned sour; that the high hopes and expectations evaporated into smoke; and self-government became a nightmare. It was generally agreed that the root cause of the malaise was that power, especially executive power, got concentrated in the hands of one man, and that this power was obviously abused. This concentration of power was later to be given constitutional approval, when the Constitution, 1960 proclaimed Ghana as a "one party unitary State." The wisemen who, after the 1966 coup, were given the responsibility of helping us draft a new Constitution in such a way as will avoid a repetition of the problems of the pre-1966 era were quick to realise that there was only one solution—viz, that whatever we did, we must try to ensure that power was diffused, and that there must be effective checks on the exercise of executive authority. This was the philosophical underpinning of the Constitution, 1969.

The fear engendered in us by our earlier experience made us go to great lengths to provide for such machinery as will, we hoped, make it impossible for any one man to wield absolute power. The Constitution, 1969 carefully crafted and structured, became the model for the Constitution, 1979 as well as the Constitution, 1992 save that the Constitution, 1992 carried over some features of the immediately preceding military order of the previous ten years. Basically, however, we can say that the Constitution, 1992 is the third edition of the Constitution, 1969, the second edition being the Constitution, 1979. Unfortunately, both (Constitutions, 1969 and 1979) failed; or is it more accurate to say they were not given the chance to succeed? Each lasted no more than 27 months. It is however not the length of their lives that matters; it is the fact that both were accepted by all as democratic.

People hardly regarded the Constitution, 1960 as democratic. With the [p.29] ballot box rapidly losing credibility as a measure of democracy, if it has not already done so with us (we only need to look no further than our part of the world for support for this statement), it appears that we can tell a democratic order by the extent to which the exercise of executive power is held in cheek at all significant levels, especially with respect to the government's human rights performance. The readiness and the ability of the executive to live and operate within these checks would seem to be a fair measure of whether the government is democratic or authoritarian; and whether the state is one which qualifies as a democratic state fit to be accommodated in the comity of matured nations.

The Constitution, 1992 declares in its article 1(2) that:

"(2) This Constitution shall be the supreme law of Ghana and any other law found to be inconsistent with any provision of this Constitution shall, to the extent of the inconsistency, be void."

By now, many of you may know this clause by heart. But, you see, familiarity breeds contempt. Over-familiarity with the passage can be dangerous; it may induce you to forget, or at least gloss over its significance; yet these words are significant. Their import cannot be overemphasised. To me they imply that in this country everything that is done, every law that exists or is passed, every movement of any person, citizen or not, must have its lawfulness or legality, its regularity anchored in the Constitution, otherwise it is unlawful. If you have reason to believe that anything done by any person whatsoever—the President, Parliament, the courts, the tribunals, public or private corporations or any individual at all, cannot be justified by reference to the Constitution, that action must be unlawful. That is why and how the Constitution is the fundamental law of the land. Maybe I am overstating the case. It is deliberate, for you may argue that many, many actions are lawful by virtue of laws other than the Constitution. This is true, save that those statutes themselves can be valid only if so declared by the Constitution. If the Constitution says that a particular statute is unlawful, I wonder whether an act based on that statute can be lawful. Therefore at the end of the day, the determining agent is the Constitution; it is the fundamental law, the law against which all other laws are measured.

But, of course, it is one thing saying that you consider an act unlawful, looking on for it to continue, as a child gazes upon a floating balloon in the skies; and quite another thing taking some steps to get that action expressly declared unlawful and brought to a halt. In other words, it is not enough to know that an action is unconstitutional and unlawful. What is important is to do something about it.

As regards what should be done, the Constitution, itself gives us sufficient guidelines. By article 2:

"2. (1) A person who alleges that—

[p.30]

(a) an enactment or anything contained in or done under the authority of that or any other enactment; or

(b) any act or omission of any person;

is inconsistent with, or is in contravention of a provision of this Constitution, may bring an action in the Supreme Court for a declaration to that effect."

(The emphasis is mine.) A declaration under article 2 may have attached to it such consequential orders and directions as the Supreme Court may deem appropriate. And as you are aware, failure on the part of any person to carry out the terms of an order or a direction constitutes a high crime, punishable, inter alia, by imprisonment without the option of a fine. In the case of the President or the Vice-President such a disobedience of an order or direction of the Supreme Court is a ground for removal from office under article 2(4) of the Constitution, if only because he would have acted in wilful violation of the oath of allegiance and the presidential oath, as well as of the provisions of the Constitution.1 But for this, and the prerogative writs, the President and his vice are immune from court process with regard to anything done by them in the discharge of the functions of their high office.2 If an act complained of as inconsistent with the Constitution sounds in a breach of any of the fundamental freedoms and rights, the Supreme Court ceases to have original jurisdiction; the action has to commence in the High Court.3

The Constitution is written in a language which I will regard as ordinary. Somehow the impression has been created (I do not know by whom) that the Constitution is a difficult and mysterious document, such that only lawyers can unravel the mysteries therein contained. I am sure many of you have come across highly educated members of society who do not happen to be lawyers, and who have observed to you: "well, this is a matter for you lawyers", whenever any topic of a constitutional nature arises for discussion. This perception, right or wrong, exists and imposes on the profession a serious responsibility.

The public look to the Bar and the bench for guidance through the Constitution, as well as for the proper interpretation and application of its several provisions. To be able to do this effectively we must ourselves be well-acquainted with, and possess an in-depth knowledge of the document. It is not enough to have a smattering of just a few chapters. As lawyers, the chapters on the judiciary and on the fundamental human rights and freedoms offer themselves readily for close study. So also, for obvious reasons, are the transitional provisions. But you will be surprised how much is embedded in the other chapters, some of which we may be tempted to ignore. A profound knowledge of the Constitution is a prerequisite, if we are to make ourselves [p.31] more extensively serviceable to the Ghanaian public.

Do you remember reading the under-quoted passage recently in the Mirror of 14 March 1993 (at p 5)? It reads:

"Brothers, let me tell you that the Constitution that you have helped to institute in the country can only work if you the people see to it that, it operates according to the tenets it contains. Any slight deviation from it either by an individual, group of individuals, or the Government or any arm thereof should be promptly pointed out.

In case of persistence on the part of the 'offender' the Supreme Court should immediately be intimated. The people need this exercise and so do the judges of the Supreme Court who need to be initiated into the intellectual exercise of interpreting the Constitution."

You may come across similar sentiments from other sources. The ordinary person believes that his duty stops at calling attention to a suspected infringement of a provision of the Constitution, usually through press articles, or speeches on platforms, and that immediately he does this the buck passes to the Supreme Court to take up the matter and deal with it. You and I know that there is nothing farther from the truth, and that unless an action is instituted in the court, there is nothing the court can do. More importantly, the court cannot file cases unto itself. This the public must know and appreciate. Part of the public's disenchantment with the courts may stem from this misconception of the role of the court. There is the tendency to think that the courts are not helping.

The Bar Association can play a very useful role here. You may take up the responsibility of educating the public. I do not see that any body of persons is more suited to perform this role than this association. I know that a human rights sub-group of the association has been formed and has proclaimed its preparedness to take up human rights cases on behalf of members of the public. This is a laudable objective. But it strikes me that this is not enough. We should begin and aim at intensive education of the public. So far, what I have read as passing for education has been general statements in general terms that we have these rights and that they are guaranteed by the Constitution; fair enough. We are yet to get the public to appreciate what the rights mean in their daily lives, and how they can pursue them. Let me here give you a few examples to indicate how my mind is working.

You are driving along a street in Accra. You see a man handcuffed, naked to the waist, followed by three hefty police officers wielding batons, and a sizeable crowd, some shouting yile, yile (beat him, beat him). This man must obviously have done something, and is being marched to the nearest police station. But this man, in the circumstances I have described, is in no position to do any harm to anybody; he cannot run away, he cannot strike anyone. Indeed, he is helpless and harmless at that stage. And yet, [p.32] egged on by the shouts of yile, yile and to show that "they are they", so to speak, the police take turns kicking and slapping this man and even baton-charging him, believing that the public are enjoying this display of sadism. The scenario I have painted is a common occurrence, and one which many of you do witness from time to time in our towns and cities. The question I ask is: Does this man know that he has any rights which are being infringed?  Have we done enough by way of education to let him know this? Are we ourselves aware of any such rights? Quite apart from notions of assault, etc, is his dignity as a human being also being affronted? If so, are we waiting for this man to come to our chambers to consult us before we help, or do we think we ought, as a group interested in human rights violations, to consider at this conference, strategies to aid people in such circumstances?

Let me paint another picture. X is in police custody. His usual fare is a bout of beating at the end of the day before the police retire to bed. In the morning of the third day the cell is opened, and he is found dead. Are there any questions of the right to life here involved? And who pursues this and; how? Or we only think of murder and/or manslaughter?

We shift from hapless prisoners to the newspaper which has reason to believe that somebody is deliberately and maliciously starving it of newsprint; he wants to kill that newspaper. What rights are involved and who has them—the proprietors, the newsvendors (right to work, right to decent living); you as a subscriber/reader (right to information), etc or what?

And whilst on newspapers, take the reporter who is persistently rebuffed by the civil servant in his quest for information. Has he a right to information in a constitutional order, subject to pleas of public security, public safety order, etc?

But why talk only of others: let us talk about ourselves. Shortly before the legal recess, there was this lawyer who was telling the Supreme Court that a case of his (I won't tell you from where) had been adjourned 62 times, and hearing had not even then commenced. Is the right to a fair trial limited to criminal cases only, or it extends beyond that? And if so, is it concerned only with allowing you, your client and his witnesses adequate time to talk in court? If it extends beyond the limits I have mentioned, who is the culprit?

Or take this issue of demonstrations and simultaneous counter-demonstrations. Is the counter-demonstrator risking actions for infringements of other people's freedom to assemble and/or to demonstrate; if the proper facts exist, can be assembled and established by evidence?

Standing in your midst, discretion dictates that I do not bring legal practitioners into this exercise. The Ashantis say, if you are tempted to insult a chief by all means do so, but make sure you do not do it at his palace.

The point I wish to make in all the examples I have given above is that it is in the area of human rights that the public see the worth of the Constitution, and it is in that area that they need you most. There is the need [p.33] therefore, very early in our constitutional order, to take human rights education very seriously. In particular, we should come down to specifics and to details so that the public may know, almost by intuition, when a right is infringed, and what should follow. Those members of the public who are financially able to do so, may pursue their interests alright. But as you and I know, by far the vast majority of the populace are not able; and for their sakes, given your training and expertise, you have a duty to map out adequate and effective strategies to come to their aid. You have a lot of satisfaction to be derived in this endeavour, even if you may not be sufficiently rewarded in money terms. You may wish to include, in the strategies you may have in mind, consideration of contingent actions in human rights cases.

I hope I am not going to be misrepresented as fanning litigation, and advocating a proliferation of court actions. Far from it. But I will not be averse to being represented as saying that the new constitutional arrangement opens up a whole vast new area of legal practice, which requires to be carefully studied and handled to the benefit and satisfaction of the profession, and more importantly, of the people, particularly the illiterate, the defenceless and the disadvantaged, persons whom our profession enjoins us to serve, no matter what the risks.

The problem with human rights is that there is the tendency for some people to think—at least they behave as if they think—that these rights exist for their benefit only, and no one else's. They forget that they exist for all and are intended to be enjoyed by all, enforced by all against all. This is what I call the triangular rule. You want to look at the right to free speech for example. I have that right; you have that right; and he too has that right. Between us, we complete the triangle. To derive the maximum benefit from this right, therefore, we three must evolve some conventions, written or unwritten, for its exercise. For instance, in a certain situation, it may become necessary that if we are to exercise the right at the same place, and at the same time (eg, passengers travelling on a bus), we have to watch the pitch at which we speak, if the bus is not to be turned into a mad house, a bedlam. So we impose limitations on ourselves.

Or take that person who thinks he can overindulge the freedom and the right to move freely anywhere in Ghana.4 He may soon find himself in the mortuary, run over by a vehicle whose driver also has, indisputably, the right to move freely anywhere in Ghana. To stay alive therefore, he has to watch where he moves, when and how. In other words, a good and balanced education will dispose the people to know what to expect, and what others expect of them.

In this area of public education you may find yourselves complementing the efforts of the Commission for Civic Education. Remember that under [p.34] article 233 of the Constitution the commission performs functions of educational character similar to those I am advocating for you viz, inter alia:

"(a) to create and sustain within the society the awareness of the principles and objectives of this Constitution as the fundamental law of the people of Ghana;

(b)  to educate and encourage the public to defend this Constitution at all times, against all forms of abuse and violation;

(c) to formulate for the consideration of Government, from time to time, programmes at the national, regional and district levels aimed at realising the objectives of this Constitution;

(d) to formulate, implement and oversee programmes intended to inculcate in the citizens of Ghana awareness of their civic responsibilities and an appreciation of their rights and obligations as free people . . ."

If the Commission performs well, as I hope it will, you may have very little to do. In the nature of things, however, you will be the best judges as to whether it will be better to institute and prosecute programmes of your own, rather than abdicate your responsibilities in this area to the commission or any other body. It strikes me that, all things considered, you will be far more effective as instructors and facilitators, than anyone else.

Let me now touch on a matter of some concern to some of us. Sometime before the constitutional order commenced, there was a spate of constitutional suits in the courts. Decisions on them became the subject of heated debates in our journals and newspapers. I am not here concerned with whether I agreed or disagreed with any or all or none of the decisions and rulings, nor whether they were correct or otherwise. I mention them here because it appears that in a number of those cases, the preparation seemed less than adequate, given what was published. The problem did not lie with the subject matter of the controversies, as with the preparation of the cases. It should be observed that the level of preparation often dictates the court's reaction to a particular case, and is bound to influence its outcome. I refrain from mentioning any case in particular. Such a reference may too easily be misconstrued. Besides, some of the cases may, unknown to me, be on their way up. In the circumstances, I can only make general statements. Standing before many elderly and learned practitioners, I feel very uncomfortable making statements about practice. A lot of what I am about to say I have picked from some of these senior practitioners, and need not be repeated back to them. It is therefore not intended for them. I realise, however, that we have within our ranks a fair number of young ones who may not have had the benefit of this kind of experience, and for their sake I feel justified in making the observations which follow.

It is good to know, at least for purposes of our examinations at the teaching institutions, all that there is to know about civil and criminal [p.35] procedure, and the laws of evidence. I suspect that there may be many lawyers around who may know the Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323) almost by heart. This knowledge is useful. But it is also useful to know that the good lawyer does, or should do, a lot of writing; this is essential to good case preparation, at least in the early years of practice. But I have known very senior lawyers, even QC's, who will not appear in court without a lot of writing.

In those places where solicitors brief barristers to appear for lay clients in the court, a typical solicitor's brief (ie, file) will contain:

(a) statement(s) taken from the lay client,

(b) statement(s) taken from other persons who may be useful as witnesses;

(c) any other statements;

(d) documents, including maps, diagrams, etc concerning the case; and

(e) some suggestions from the solicitor as to what he considers to be the likely cause(s) of action open to his client. But the decision on this is for the barrister.

If the solicitor is acting for a defendant in a civil or criminal case, you may visualise for yourselves what the brief is likely to contain. Upon receipt of the solicitor's brief, the barrister's chambers prepare what is termed "Advice on Evidence." If you happen to be a junior in the chambers, and the case is reasonably simple, you are likely to have a first go at this. Whoever prepares this advice is required to:

(a) read through the brief carefully;

(b) indicate the cause(s) of action likely to succeed, given the material on the brief;

(c) what further statements or other material the solicitor must be asked to procure from the client;

(d) whether it is necessary to ask the solicitor to come in person or to produce any person, to be interviewed by the barrister, and when;

(e) whether it is a good case to pursue, and the chances of success or failure; everything considered, case law and statute law included;

(f)   identify the issues;

(g) advice on the evidence required to prove each issue, including the documents and other written material that may be needed; and

(h) the witnesses who should be called to give the said evidence, including those who would be competent to tender any of the documentary materials.

The effort of the junior was naturally sent to the senior to be discussed, and an appropriate response sent to the solicitor. If I seem to have spent some time on this aspect of practice, it is because I have reason to believe that some of us may not be aware that there is, or should be such a stage in the course of litigation, and that this stage is the most important in the preparation of our cases for court.

[p.36]

Just imagine going through this exercise with respect to a case which has just come to you, and putting down on paper your responses to all the queries listed above. By the time you would have finished, even without discussing it with anyone, you would know whether you are on top of the case, or you are below it; whether it is worthwhile to file a writ at all, or to advise another course.

How many of us make this effort before we file our writs? And yet this is an exercise which is relevant to all types of cases; ordinary litigation, declaratory actions, constitutional cases, chieftaincy matters, etc.

Since the introduction of written briefs in the Supreme Court, counsel are forced to put their thoughts on paper any way, whether the jurisdiction being invoked be original, supervisory, appellate or referral. The same cannot be said of practice in the lower courts, where virtually every case commences. It is at these levels that sufficient preparation is called for, especially in terms of evidence, and yet it seems that it is at these levels that not much writing is done.

Of course preparing a case well does not necessarily mean that you will win it; but it helps. And sitting where we happen to be, it is all too easy to know where this "lot of writing" has gone into a case, and where it has not.

A short while ago I made reference to the several jurisdictions of the Supreme Court. The pronouncements of the Supreme Court are final, but final only in the sense that it is the final appellate court, the last court to which anyone can take any grievance. There is no adjudicating authority over and above, or beyond it. But our decisions are not final in the sense that they are ipso facto correct, or that after the Supreme Court has spoken, nobody else should. Some of the decisions may be atrocious, not deliberately so, but because we are human and liable to err. The Constitution allows us to depart from those which we may later discover to be faulty. To aid in this exercise of discovery, one would expect that the Bar will subject our decisions to constant scrutiny and assessment, and not wait for new cases to arise before seeking to persuade us to depart from a previous wrong decision. In the nature of things, judges have no platform to perform this task. And as for writing into magazines and newspapers, even under pen-names, this may not be advisable for serving judges in a small society like ours. The persons to discharge this function therefore must be you. So far this is not being done. It may be that in the past the decisions had been of pure civil and criminal matters, and therefore not sufficiently appetising. In the present constitutional order, I will expect a more vigorous and articulate Bar, especially with regard to decisions affecting the constitution, its enforcement and more particularly, human rights. We do not so much want to be told where we are right. If nothing is said, we run away with the impression that all is well. What I wish to see is a professional and informed assessment of what we write; not a partisan and, if I may say so, narrow-minded diatribes, born of pique for losing a case. I am not here thinking of lengthy academic [p.37] treatises on the issues at stake; we can leave these to academia. I have in mind short articles professionally written from a practitioner's standpoint.

When the legal practitioner takes hold of a judgment or a ruling, he wants to find therein the issues to be decided and the judge's decision on each of those issues. In the process the judge may have to advance reasoned arguments in support of his conclusions. But reasoned arguments need not be equated with a stringing together of quotable quotes, cliches, or of all the rules and canons of interpretation known to the writer of a text. Many a time, these canons, if put together in the same ruling, tend to get in each other's way, and end up confusing the reader as to the logical relevance of any of them to the arguments in hand.

These days judgments are becoming longer and longer, a practice to which many of us, I included, readily plead guilty. The length of a judgment is a function of the facts and the issues of the particular case. But no matter how long a judgment may be, there can be no excuse for befogging the issues and making the decision unclear. It may be that some of our judgments suffer from these defects. Unfortunately, the Bar is yet to place itself in such a position as to be able to assist us to correct them.

Not too long ago the Bar was kind enough to give some of us complimentary copies of a magazine. It was beautifully laid out, and contained very useful and interesting reading material. That was a very good beginning, for which the Bar has my congratulations. I formed the impression that we were going to see more of it. So far, I haven't. I am still hoping that it will re-appear. But may I, in all humility, take advantage of my presence here to offer a few suggestions. With the demise, so I learn, of the Ghana Law Reports Digest, you may consider publishing your magazine in shorter intervals, say, for a start, every three months. The publication should not aim at rivalling the Review of Ghana Law, or any such magazine. It should rather target the busy practitioner so that while it may have a few pages of association tit-bits—notices, activities, new publications, if any, etc—the bulk of the magazine should be devoted to intelligent summaries of court decisions and some notes on recent legislations. The publication must be sufficiently indexed, to enhance its usefulness. Mind you, the busy practitioner has not much time to read through lengthy judgments from us, even if he has copies. I cannot tell how current the law reports are now going to be; but even if they happen to be, there is always room for synoptic publications of the kind being advocated here, provided the standard is high enough. The Bar may consider stepping into the vacuum created by the demise of the Ghana Law Reports Digest. These days, almost every venture has a money motive. The publication I am recommending to you, if properly handled, cannot be a commercial failure. If the Bar, as a body, cannot take it on, maybe a few enterprising young men may seriously consider this, as a sideline.

If I have not succeeded in properly directing attention to challenges [p.38] attendant upon the Constitution, it is because, in my view, there cannot be many which were not with us before the Constitution. However, with everybody, including the Judiciary, proclaiming the sudden rediscovery of their courage and independence, the future cannot but be bright for all. Whether this hope will materialise, time will tell.

JUDICIAL SAYINGS

Comportment and Decorum in Court

"However emotionally overheated one may become, members of the legal fraternity have a duty to conform to the time honoured practice of comporting themselves always with decorum and dignity. This must be reflected in the language they employ which should be of measured elegance and sobriety" per Francois JSC in Botchway v Odonkor, Supreme Court, 9 February 1993, unreported.

Importance of Freedom of Expression and Information

"The democratic tradition that divergent views and dissenting opinions be given free expression may be summed up in the words Tallentyre used to describe the attitude of Voltaire on the burning of Helvetius' De l'caprit in 1759: 'I disapprove of what you say, but I will defend to the death your right to say it.' History abounds with examples where those in authority were so sure they were right that they regarded dissent as subversive. The reformation was preceded by the burning of heretics, and followed by the persecution of papists. The temptation to ride roughshod over the opinions of others must be resisted; for it is only by the free flow of ideas and discussion that error is exposed, truth vindicated and liberty preserved" per Amua-Sekyi JSC in New Patriotic Party v Ghana Broadcasting Corporation, Supreme Court, 30 November 1993, unreported.

"Turning to a practical and commonsense point of view, the free exchange of views is necessary to give the electorate an opportunity to assess the performance of the government in power as against the potential of an opposition in the wilderness. It keeps a government on its toes and gives the neutral, apolitic citizen an opportunity to make up his mind either to consign the disenchanted noises he hears around, to mere rabid rantings that proceed from electoral defeat or give it the evocative distinction of demonstrating the quality that unfortunately missed the boat through bad electoral judgment, and therefore deserving of a second chance at the next ballot. In a truly democratic environment this testing ground is a sine qua non to the survival of a free, pluralist society" per Francois JSC in Kuenyehia v Archer, Supreme Court, 25 May 1993, unreported.

[p.39]

Interpretation of Constitution

"Any attempt to construe the various provisions of the Constitution relevant to the present enquiry must per force start with an awareness that a constitutional instrument is a document sui generis to be interpreted according to principles suitable to its peculiar character and not necessarily according to the ordinary rules and presumptions of statutory interpretation. Though basic rules of statutory construction may provide the first steps, they should strictly be kept at the first rung as servants and never elevated in flight as masters" per Francois JSC in Kuenyehia v Archer, Supreme Court, 25 May 1993, unreported.

"It appears that the overwhelming imperatives are the spirit and objectives of the Constitution itself, keeping an eye always on the aspirations of the future and not overlooking the receding footsteps of the past.

It allows for a liberal and generous interpretation rather than a narrow legalistic one. It gives room for a broader attempt to achieve enlightened objectives and tears apart the stifling straight jacket of legalistic constraints that grammar, punctuation and the like may impose. Moreover it ensures that what has been termed "the austerity of tabulated legalism", is avoided, as also the dry interpretation of a section with its attendant difficulties . . ." per Francois JSC in Kuenyehia v Archer, Supreme Court, 25 May 1993, unreported.

Judicial Authority of Supreme Court

"The judicial authority of which this court is the beneficiary or endowed with is essentially a jurisdiction to deal with real or substantial disputes which affect the legal rights or obligations of parties who appear before us, and whose interests are adverse to each other. These competing interests will necessarily call for specific reliefs through conclusive and certain judicial decree or decrees. In these circumstances the matter could be said to be justiciable and not otherwise. The principle of justiciability precludes us from giving advisory opinions based on hypothetical facts which are not part of an existing controversy" per Kpegah JSC in Bilson v Attorney-General, Supreme Court, 12 December 1994, unreported.

Judicial Notice

"If we should provide any meaningful service to the people of this society wherein our courts operate, it is imperative that we in the courts muster sufficient courage to take cognisance of the happenings in our society and ensure that our judgments duly reflect facts of common notoriety. Facts so notorious that everyone in our society can be deemed to be aware of, can be said to be matters in respect of which judicial notice can be taken" per Brobbey J (as he then was) in Barake v Barake, High Court, Accra, 25 June [p.40] 1990, unreported.

Religion and Marital Status

"The legal position is however that religion per se is incapable of totally transforming the status of parties married under the Marriage Ordinance, Cap 127. Once the couple are held to have married under the Ordinance, they remain bound by all the incidents attached to that marriage and status created by that marriage until they take steps, according to the law, to rid themselves of the bond of marriage. Vicissitudes in a person's religious life are incapable of obliterating marital status created under a statute. Therefore, the fact that the petitioner variously altered her religion according to the circumstances and surroundings in which she found herself did not obliterate the marital status created by the marriage under Cap 127 to give the first respondent freedom to take on another wife" per Brobbey J (as he then was) in Barake v Barake, High Court, Accra, 25 June 1990, unreported.
