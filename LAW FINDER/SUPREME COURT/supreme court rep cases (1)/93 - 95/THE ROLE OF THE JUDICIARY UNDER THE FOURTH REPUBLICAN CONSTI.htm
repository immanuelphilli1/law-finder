THE ROLE OF THE JUDICIARY UNDER THE FOURTH REPUBLICAN CONSTITUTION [1993-95] VOL. XIX 18—27 *

BY MR ENOCH D KOM, FGA, SAG

 

I would like to make two preliminary remarks. In my 34 years of practice at the Bar, I have seen eight Chief Justices since independence; four were sacked, one left to become President, two retired, and the eighth is at post. Of the two who retired, one asked for and got an extension of time to stay on after attaining the retiring age of 65 years. Tomorrow will be the eleventh anniversary of the murder of the three High Court judges, namely Fred Poku Sarkodee, K A Agyepong and Mrs Cecilia Koranteng-Addo. In my funeral oration in 1982 as the then President of the Ghana Bar Association, I said: "With the murder of the three High Court judges, the judiciary in Ghana will not be the same again." I would like to dedicate this lecture to their memory. It is against this background of dismissals and murder that we discuss the judiciary and its role under the Fourth Republican Constitution.

The French political scientist, Montesquieu, after studying the writings of John Locke, propounded in 1748 the doctrine of separation of powers, ie legislative power, executive power and judicial power. He wrote: "Political liberty is to be found only . . . when there is no abuse of power." Montesquieu however noted that "constant experience shows us that every man invested with power is apt to abuse it, and to carry his authority as far as it will go." He suggested that to prevent this abuse, "power should be a check to power."1 He continued:

"When the legislative and executive powers are united in the same person, or in the same body. . . , there can be no liberty; . . . Again, there is no liberty if the judiciary power be not separated from the legislative and executive."2

The term "checks and balances" was first used by Madison in 1788 in his book, The Federalist, to mean the control the organs of state must exercise on one another to prevent arbitrary rule. The draftsmen of the United States Constitution embraced the doctrine and as President Woodrow Wilson puts it:

"The sure way to avoid the abuse of power by government agencies is to divide political power and vest it in the hands of diverse persons so that there is effective check on power by power." 3

[p.19]

This is not so in the United Kingdom where cabinet ministers, ie members of the executive, must be members of Parliament, ie the legislature. Indeed, they are answerable to Parliament for the way they run their ministries. The Lord Chancellor, who is the Speaker of the House of Lords, acts in a judicial capacity as he is the highest judge and sits as the president of the highest appellate tribunal, ie the House of Lords. Some of the highest judges are members of the second chamber, ie the House of Lords. Hence, by the Westminster Constitution, the judiciary intersects with the legislature and the executive with the legislature.

The Constitutional Commissions of 1968 and 1978 discussed the doctrine of separation of powers, whilst the 1968 Constitutional Commission proposed the Westminster system. The 1978 Constitutional Commission proposed the presidential system of government. Despite the fact that the Constitutions, 1979 and 1992 do not observe strict separation of powers, there is one thing which all the three Constitutions recognise, that is that "the judiciary has a vital and indispensable role in the constitutional process . . . and that the courts are essential for the operation of democratic and constitutional government in Ghana."4

The Fourth Republican Constitution, 1992 does not observe the strict theory of separation of powers for article 78(1) provides:

"78. (1) Ministers of State shall be appointed by the President with the prior approval of Parliament from among members of Parliament or persons qualified to be elected as members of Parliament, except that the majority of Ministers of State shall be appointed from among members of Parliament."

Despite the fact that the Fourth Republican Constitution does not observe the strict doctrine of separation of powers because the executive and legislature intersect, it observes, like its predecessor, the principle that judicial power must be completely separated from the legislative and the executive. Hence article 125 (3) provides that:

"(3) The judicial power of Ghana shall be vested in the Judiciary, accordingly, neither the President nor Parliament nor any organ or agency of the President or Parliament shall have or be given final judicial power."

In view of the fact that final judicial power is solely vested in the judiciary, adjudicating bodies like: (i) the Stool Lands Boundaries Settlement Commission, established under the Stool Lands Boundaries Settlement Decree, 1973 (NRCD 172); (ii) the traditional councils and the regional houses of chiefs, established under the Chieftaincy Act, 1971 (Act 370); (iii) the adjudicating committee of the Land Title Registry, established under the [p.20] Land Title Registration Law, 1985 (PNDCL 152); (iv) the regional tribunals: see article 142 of the Constitution, 1992; (v) the commissions or committees of inquiry: see article 280 of the Constitution, 1992; and (vi) the national tax tribunal, established under the National Tax Tribunal Law, 1988 (PNDCL 203), are all subject to the supervisory jurisdiction of the High Court. Article 141 states: "The High Court shall have supervisory jurisdiction over all lower courts and any lower adjudicating authority . . ." Appeals against a judgment or an order of these adjudicating bodies may end up finally in the Supreme Court. One can safely say that even though special and/or exclusive jurisdiction is conferred upon some of these adjudicating bodies, they are part and parcel of the judiciary and not independent of it.

One can further say that the stand taken by the Ghana Bar Association against the public tribunal system when it was introduced has been vindicated and their criticisms have been taken care of in the Constitution of the Fourth Republic.5 To seal the exclusive judicial power conferred on the judiciary, article 107 provides that: "Parliament shall have no power to pass any law—(a) to alter the decision or judgment of any court as between the parties subject to that decision or judgment." The Supreme Court in Mekkaoui v Minister of Internal Affairs6 declared the denaturalisation of 107 foreigners by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (AFRC) illegal and wrongful. The Provisional National Defence Council (PNDC) administration by section 60 of the Provisional National Defence Council Law, (Establishment) Proclamation (Supplementary and Consequential Provisions) Law, 1982 (PNDCL 42) declared the judgment null and void by giving retrospective effect to the Ghana Nationality (Amendment) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 42). By the Special Criminal Division Instrument, 1963 (EI 161) Parliament declared null and void the decision of the Supreme Court in State v Otchere.7 This sort of thing cannot happen now. In addition to the judicial power being vested solely in the Judiciary, article 127 confers independence on the judiciary. The article provides:

"127. (1) In the exercise of the judicial power of Ghana, the Judiciary, in both its judicial and administrative functions, including financial administration, is subject only to this Constitution and shall not be subject to the control or direction of any person or authority.

(2)  Neither the President nor Parliament nor any person acting under the authority of the President or Parliament nor any other person whatsoever shall interfere with Judges or judicial officers or other persons exercising judicial power, in the exercise of their judicial functions; and all organs and agencies of the State shall accord to the courts such assistance as the courts may reasonably require to protect [p.21] the independence, dignity and effectiveness of the courts, subject to this Constitution."

A justice of a superior court or any person exercising judicial power is not "liable to any action or suit for any act or omission by him in the exercise of the judicial power"8; and he shall not "be removed from office except for stated misbehaviour or incompetence or on ground of inability to perform the functions of his office arising from infirmity of body or mind."9 This gives judges security of tenure of office. A judge cannot be dismissed because the government of the day does not like a judgment he has given. To recapitulate:

(i) the judiciary is given exclusive final judicial power;

(ii) the judiciary is independent in its judicial and administrative functions, from the executive and the legislature;

(iii) the judiciary enjoys legal immunity from suits;

(iv) the judges enjoy security of tenure of office; and

(v) judgments or decisions of the courts cannot be tampered with by the President or Parliament.

Hence, the Constitution observes the second leg of Montesquieu's doctrine of separation of powers, ie the judiciary must be separate from the legislature and the executive. He said: ''... there is no liberty if judiciary power be not separated from the legislative and executive."10 In view of the fact that the judiciary has been completely removed from the control of the executive and the legislature, it has been vested with the following jurisdictions as a check on abuse of power and/or arbitrary rule:

(i) Article 2(1):

"(1) A Person who alleges that:—

(a) an enactment or anything contained in or done under the authority of that or any other enactment; or

(b) any act or omission of any person;

is inconsistent with, or is in contravention of a provision of this Constitution, may bring an action in the Supreme Court for a declaration to that effect."

(ii) Article 33:

“(1) Where a person alleges that a provision of this Constitution on the fundamental human rights and freedoms has been, or is being or is likely to be contravened in relation to him, then, without prejudice to any other action that is lawfully available, that person may apply to the High Court for redress.

[p.22]

(2) The High Court may, under clause (1) of this article, issue such directions or orders or writs including writs or orders in the nature of habeas corpus, certiorari, mandamus, prohibition, and quo warranto as it may consider appropriate for the . . . provisions on the fundamental human rights and freedoms to the protection of which the person concerned is entitled."

(iii) Article 64(l):

"(1) The validity of the election of the President may be challenged only by a citizen of Ghana who may present a petition for the purpose to the Supreme Court within twenty-one days after the declaration of the result of the election in respect of which the petition is presented."

(iv) Article 121 (3): enjoins the Speaker or the National Security Council to "refer the matter to the Supreme Court for determination whether the production or the disclosure of the contents, of the document would be injurious to the public interest or, as the case may be, prejudicial to the security of the State."

(v)  By article 130 (1) the Supreme Court is vested with exclusive original jurisdiction to determine:

       "(a) all matters relating to the enforcement or interpretation of this Constitution; and

      (b) all matters arising as to whether an enactment was made in excess of the powers conferred on Parliament . . . or under this Constitution."

See Republic v Director-General of Prisons; Ex parte Nti.11

(vi)  By article 3, all citizens of Ghana have the right and duty to defend the Constitution. Thus citizens have the right to resist any person or group of persons who by any violent or unlawful means, seek to suspend, overthrow or abrogate the Constitution. The Supreme Court by article 3(7), shall, on an application by such a person, award him adequate compensation for any suffering or loss incurred as a result of the punishment unlawfully meted out to him.

(The emphasis is mine.) In addition to the above, the courts are to determine disputes between the individual and the State and vice-versa.

In the past, certain decisions of the courts have not made the legal profession happy, for the members of the courts have at times behaved like what Lord Denning called "timorous souls." One of such decisions is the case of [p.23] In re Akoto.12 Okyeame Baffour Osei Akoto and seven others were arrested in November 1959 and detained without trial under the Preventive Detention Act, 1958 (No 17 of 1958) for acting in a manner prejudicial to the security of the State. Their application for habeas corpus to the High Court was dismissed and they appealed to the Supreme Court, the then final appellate court in Ghana. The court composed by Sir Arku Korsah CJ, van Lare and Akiwumi JJSC had to consider, inter alia, the effect of article 13(1) of the Constitution, 1960 which required the President upon assumption of office to declare his adherence to certain fundamental principles ie:

"That the powers of Government spring from the will of the people and should be exercised in accordance therewith. That freedom and justice should be honoured and maintained. That the union of Africa should be striven for by every lawful means and when attained, should be faithfully preserved. That the independence of Ghana should not be surrendered or diminished on any grounds other than the furtherance of African unity. That no person should suffer discrimination on grounds of sex, race, tribe, religion or political belief. That chieftaincy in Ghana should be guaranteed and preserved. That every citizen of Ghana should receive his fair share of the produce yielded by the development of the country. That subject to such restrictions as may be necessary for preserving public order, morality or health, no person should be deprived of freedom of religion, of speech, of the right to move and assemble without hindrance or of the right of access to courts of law."13

The court held that the declaration which was similar to the coronation oath of the Queen of England, did not constitute a bill of rights and as such did not create legal obligations enforceable in a court of law. The Supreme Court accordingly dismissed the appeal. In In re Dumoga14 a similar application was dismissed. With this clearance given to the executive by the courts, the way was open for the indiscriminate arrest and detention of Ghanaians without trial. It may be pointed out in passing that the Preventive Detention Act, 1958 was signed by Sir Arku Korsah CJ when he acted as Governor-General during the absence on leave of Lord Listowell, the Governor-General.

Sir Arku Korsah CJ, van Lare and Akufo-Addo JJSC in 1963 sat on the case of State v Otchere15 in which the accused persons were charged with conspiracy to commit treason and misprision when an attempt was made to assassinate the President. This case is popularly known as "the Kulungugu [p.24] case.” In a well-reasoned judgment of 73 pages which took a whole day to read the court acquitted and discharged the accused persons, but President Kwame Nkrumah was not amused. By the Special Criminal Division Instrument (EI 161 of 1963) the judgment was declared null and void and a new trial ordered. Sir Arku Korsah CJ, van Lare and Akufo-Addo JJSC were dismissed. If the two justices of the Supreme court who took part in the judgment of In re Akoto16 and State v Otchere17 had been sacked in 1961 for standing their ground, the support of the legal profession would have been otherwise than what it was in 1963.

In the Second Republic, the Court of Appeal handed down the decision in Sallah v Attorney-General.18 That was a case in which Kwaku Sallah, a manager of GNTC, challenged his dismissal under the transitional provisions of the Constitution, 1969 as illegal and wrongful. This case is known as "the Apollo 568" because of the number of people similarly dismissed. The Prime Minister of the day, like President Kwame Nkrumah in the case of State v Otchere, was equally not amused; but unlike President Kwame Nkrumah, all he could do was to go on radio and television to condemn the decision. That broadcast was later known as "No court, No court."

In the Third Republic, a case came to court which unfortunately was not reported in our law reports. To me everything about that case was important and revealing as later events show, eg the suit number, the date of judgment, the names of the parties, the witnesses for the plaintiff, the judge who heard the case and last but not the least, the judgment itself. The suit number is 1210/80 which means the suit commenced in 1980. The title of the suit is Tsikata v Odjidja.19  Some of the witnesses for the plaintiff were Adu Kofi Djin, a legal practitioner and later a PNDC Secretary for Internal Affairs, as the third plaintiff witness and Francis Amarkai Amarteifio, another legal practitioner and later a PNDC Secretary for Greater Accra Region. The trial judge was Hon Mr Justice Roger Korsah, son of the late Sir Arku Korsah CJ.

The judgment was delivered on 4 August 1981, that is 20 years after the decision in In re Akoto.20

The facts of the case were that the plaintiff alleged that he had been harassed by the defendants, members of the Military Intelligence Directorate of the Ghana Armed Forces in that they relentlessly pursued him in all his movements 24 hours a day. The plaintiff, in consequence of these acts on the part of the defendants, claimed by his writ the following reliefs:

"(a)  An order of perpetual injunction restraining the defendants from unlawful interference with the plaintiff's constitutional rights to [p.23] life, liberty and privacy.

(b) An order of perpetual injunction restraining the defendants from their continuing harassment and inhuman treatment of the plaintiff.

(c)  Damages for conspiracy against the plaintiff's fundamental human and constitutional rights to life, liberty and privacy.

(d) Damages for unlawful interference with the plaintiff's fundamental human and constitutional rights to life, liberty and privacy.

(e)  Damages for defamation.

(f)  Any further or other order as to the honourable court may seem meet."21

The trial judge prefaced his judgment by saying:

"Early in the history of man, he learnt that by sacrificing part of the absolute freedom he enjoyed to live in a group, he was enabled the better to defend himself against his foes. As society became more complex, legislation was enacted and Constitutions proclaimed defining the executive powers of the state and declaring the rights, liberties and freedoms of the individual. But every once in a while, a situation arises which demands of the courts that they demarcate the parameters between executive power and the rights, liberties and freedoms of the individual in the state. Such, are the issues posed in this case.22”

(The emphasis is mine.) Roger Korsah went on to make the following prophetic pronouncement:

"Power to be respected must be disciplined and controlled. When it is not, it becomes tyrannical and falls into odium and hatred. The security agencies of the country must act within the parameters defined by the laws of the land. It is to them that we look for the protection of our rights and liberties. If we are attacked in our homes we expect them to aid us in expelling the aggressors. It cannot be lawful for them to invade our homes or to interfere with our enjoyment of any of the fundamental human rights assured us under the Constitution without just cause."23

(The emphasis is mine.) He dismissed the claim for perpetual injunction because:

"If the plaintiff were ever minded to endanger the security of the State, the defendants would not be able to act without flouting an order of the Court. It seems to me that perpetual injunction is, therefore, not the [p.26] appropriate remedy to grant against the defendants, but some relief must be granted to the plaintiff."24

The trial judge, therefore, awarded ¢500 damages against each of the nineteen defendants and costs of ¢3,000 to the plaintiff against all the defendants.

Finally, Mr Justice Roger Korsah had this advice to give. He said:

"It is hoped that the heads of our security agencies, being honourable, intelligent and responsible citizens, having respect for the Constitution and the law of the land, will curb the excessive zeal of their men and make sure that they work within the framework of the law. To quote Mr Justice Frankfurter of the United States: 'The method by which the state chiefly exerts an influence upon the conduct of its citizens, it was wisely said by Archbishop William Temple, is the moral qualities which it exhibits in its own conduct."’25

It is sad to mention that Mr. Justice Roger Korsah left the judiciary of Ghana after the murder in 1982 of the three High Court judges, never to return. One must however salute him for his courage and boldness.

Honourable Mr Justice Apaloo, former Chief Justice and a Fellow of the Academy, on the occasion of the swearing in of new Supreme Court judges on 24 October 1980 said:

"To a large extent, the success of our present constitutional experiment will depend on the qualities that you bring to your work. You will need courage and a delicate balance of sensitivity and detachment: . . . to discern where justice lies, and above all, courage to pronounce your opinion—according to your oaths without fear or favour."26

I would humbly add my voice in reminding the Judiciary of the words of that great English poet, Rudyard Kipling (1865-1936):

"Teach us to look in all our ends

On Thee for judge, and not our friends;

That we, with Thee, may walk uncowed

By fear or favour of the crowd.27"

(The emphasis is mine.)

If they do, God bless Ghana; if they do not we are back to square one. In that event chapters five and six of the Constitution which deal with fundamental human rights and freedoms and the directive principles of state policy are not worth the paper on which they are written.
