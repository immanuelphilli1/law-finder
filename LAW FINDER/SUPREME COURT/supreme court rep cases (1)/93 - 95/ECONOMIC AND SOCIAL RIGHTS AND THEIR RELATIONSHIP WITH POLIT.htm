ECONOMIC AND SOCIAL RIGHTS AND THEIR RELATIONSHIP WITH POLITICAL AND CIVIL RIGHTS [1993-95] VOL XIX 182—200*

ALA ADJETEY PETER

 

*

In 1989 the General Assembly of the United Nations requested the Secretary-General of the United Nations to seek the views of governments, United Nations agencies and bodies as well as human rights and non-governmental organisations (NGOs) on the desirability of convening a world conference on the promotion and protection of human rights. In 1990 the General Assembly decided finally to convene such a conference and preparations were set in motion for the conference. It was finally decided that the conference should be held in Vienna, Austria between 14 and 25 June 1993. This was to mark the 25th Anniversary of the International Conference on Human Rights which was held in Tehran, Iran. Today, therefore, is the last day for the 1993 United Nations sponsored World Conference on Human Rights. Significantly, the Organisation of African Unity (OAU) decided that a week devoted to the consideration of human rights issues should be observed by all member states.

In this country, the non-governmental organisations operating in the field of human rights pooled their resources and energies and decided to celebrate the human rights week declared by the OAU from 21-28 June 1993 in a fitting manner by organising a series of lectures and symposia under the auspices of the OAU. Three addresses have already been delivered on various aspects of human rights since the programme started on 21 June 1993. Today’s address is therefore the fourth in the series and is on the subject, “Economic and Social Rights and their Relationship with Political and Civil Rights.”

SOME THEORETICAL CONSIDERATIONS

Any discussions of human rights inevitably raises the question of what human rights really are. All thinking on human rights has proceeded from theories of natural rights which themselves have emerged from philosophical thinking on natural law. The ideas of Thomas Aquinas contained a lot of material on medieval thought on natural law and natural rights which were conceived as a set of immutable rights conferred on individuals by reason of their being creatures of God.1 Subsequent philosophers and thinkers like [p.183] Grotius, who was also the father of modern public international law, and Pufendorf developed the medieval theories of natural law as exemplified in the writings of Thomas Aquinas into a system of theories which, while not deriving their authority from religion or from a supreme being, were based upon what were referred to as principles of the law of nature by which natural law was conceived as a system of principles existing outside municipal or domestic law and superior to it. It was considered by such theories that all domestic law has to be measured against the principles of natural law and to conform to them in order to have any pretensions to legitimacy.2

The ideas of Grotius and Pufendorf were developed considerably by the English philosopher John Locke to whom is attributed much modern thinking on natural law and natural rights3 which constituted the philosophical underpinnings of the French Revolution as well as the American Revolution, especially as are contained in the American Declaration of Independence whose principal author, Thomas Jefferson, was influenced in no small way by these theories.

All of this tell us precious little about what human rights are. What rights can one have or does one or should one have by virtue only of being human? This really is at the core of the idea of human rights conceived as rights which inhere to human beings by reason only of their humanity. The proponents of legal positivism such as Jeremy Bentham and of analytical jurisprudence such as John Austin would deny the existence of anything called natural rights or human rights to the extent that such rights are not conferred by positive law enacted by a process of the exercise of the will of the sovereign authority in any country.4 Indeed, Jeremy Bentham is said to have described natural rights as "merely imaginary rights" and to have castigated them as nothing but "rhetorical nonsense."5

The trouble about ideas of human rights in so far as they are predicated on the existence of a priori postulates or a set of principles or rights existing independently of municipal law or laws enacted by human law-making bodies and serving as a yardstick by which the legitimacy of such laws can be measured is that they depend to a large extent on the setting up of a set of norms of human conduct and of the conduct of governments. Norm-setting is described quite rightly as depending to a large extent on the predilections of the norm-setter. Natural rights or human rights were therefore considered as no better than shifting sands which move to give way as soon as one attempts to make a stand thereon.

In spite of all these theoretical and philosophical difficulties, it is now considered that there are a set of rights which find as their central pivot the [p.184] individual human being in a state of freedom in which he would take responsibility for his actions as a rational agent. The ideas of John Rawls typify modern thinking on human rights as based upon each human being possessing “an inviolability founded on justice,"6 an inviolability so fundamental and existing at the very core of humanity that nothing in society as a whole can override it. Human rights are thus their own justification. Every individual is considered to have and to be entitled to a system of basic liberties, so howsoever that such basic liberties should be consistent and compatible with the exercise of similar liberties by other individual human beings.

In all this thinking on human rights, the term "human rights" itself did not really come to the fore. The term that was often used was "natural rights", and, of course, the term "the rights of man" was also used by people like Thomas Paine, J. J. Rousseau and other theorists. Human rights as a term used in modern thought about the freedoms of human beings made its debut relatively recently. It does not find mention in the Covenant of the League of Nations and it is said to have been used in an international document for the first time in the proposals adopted by the United Kingdom, United States of America, the Soviet Union and China at Dumbarton Oaks in Washington DC. The word was subsequently used in the Charter of the United Nations which spoke for the first time in any international instrument of "fundamental human rights."

INTERNATIONAL HUMAN RIGHTS INSTRUMENTS

In what can be described as his charge to the World Conference on Human Rights then about to end in Vienna, Austria, the Secretary-General of the United Nations, Boutrous Boutrous-Ghali said as follows:

“We look to this conference to reaffirm the need for the full implementation of economic, social and cultural rights together with civil and political rights, and to reaffirm the link between development and the enjoyment of all human rights.”7

The Secretary-General here speaks of economic, social and cultural rights on the one hand and civil and political rights on the other. What are these rights? The answer to this question is given by the United Nations Universal Declaration on Human Rights which was adopted by the General Assembly of the United Nations on 10 December 1948 and intended as a “common standard of achievement for all peoples.” The Universal Declaration of Human Rights contains basic civil and political rights as well as fundamental economic, social and cultural rights which, in the thinking of the framers of the Declaration, were to be enjoyed by all human beings [p.185] wherever they will be. This surely must be the reason for the use of the word “universal.”

The United Nations Declaration on Human Rights contains 30 articles which made provisions for the following:

(1) the right to life, liberty and security of person;

(2) equality before the law;

(3) freedom of movement and residence;

(4) freedom from torture or cruel inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment;

(5) the right to seek in other countries asylum from persecution;

(6) freedom of thought, conscience and religion;

(7) the right to vote and participate in government;

(8) the right to education;

(9) the right to work and to form and join trade unions;

(10) the right to an adequate standard of living;

(11) the right to health protection; and

(12) the right to participate fully in cultural life.

Because the United Nations Universal Declaration on Human Rights is, as its name implies, a declaration and not a covenant, it is generally considered as not binding although its provisions have been so widely incorporated in municipal law and to have been so widely accepted as to have raised its juridical nature almost to the level of an international treaty or covenant. However, in order that there might be no dispute on a purely legalistic basis on the enforcement of the provisions of the Universal Declaration, a number of covenants or international treaties executed on a multilateral basis have emerged from the Declaration. Among these covenants are two which are considered as being of the foremost importance. These are the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights together with its first optional protocol and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights. These provide international legally binding provisions governing the rights and freedoms of human beings and the relations of governments to human beings in their societies.

These international covenants were both adopted in 1966 and came into force in 1976.There are other covenants or conventions like the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide, the International Convention on the Elimination of all Forms of Racial Discrimination, the Convention on the Elimination of all Forms of Discrimination against Women, the Convention against Torture and other Cruel and Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment and the Convention on the Rights of the Child which came into force in 1951, 1969, 1981, 1987 and 1990 respectively.

There are of course, as is well-known, a number of other multilateral conventions of a regional nature such as the European Convention on Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms, the American Convention on Human [p.186] Rights and the African Charter on Human and Peoples Rights which came into force in 1953, 1978 and 1986, respectively.

An examination of the United Nations Declaration of Human Rights and of the two main covenants which have been given birth to by it shows that the rights which are declared by the United Nations Universal Declaration of Human Rights are of two main categories, namely civil and political rights on the one hand and economic, social and cultural rights on the other. These are not considered to be exhaustive of all human rights and there is now considered to be a third category of human rights, namely such rights as the right to development, the right to peace as well as rights in relation to solidarity and also in relation to the environment. There are difficulties with this third category of rights because many of them are not really individual human rights but rather group or people’s rights. They are collective rather than individual rights. The rights which can strictly speaking be considered as individual human rights are those subsumed in the provisions of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights on the one hand and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights on the other.

Economic, social and cultural rights include the right to work, the right to an adequate standard of living, the right to social security, the right to education, the right to the highest attainable health care standards, the right to form and join trade unions and the right to participate in the cultural life of the community. The other rights—such as the right to life, freedom from torture or cruel or inhuman or degrading treatment, freedom from slavery, freedom of movement, freedom from arbitrary expulsion, equality before the law, the right to privacy, freedom of thought and religion, the right to peaceful assembly, freedom of association including freedom to form and join trade unions, the right to take apart in the conduct of public affairs including the right to vote at elections, the right to liberty and freedom of expression — are conceived as political and civil rights.

HUMAN RIGHTS IN THE GHANAIAN SETTING

The Constitutions of this country, except for the independence Constitution of 1957 and, to some extent, the republican Constitution of 1960, have always contained provisions on human rights; that is to say, political and civil rights as well as economic, social and cultural rights. The Constitution, 1960 attempted to incorporate some human rights provisions in the declaration which the President was to make upon taking the oath and assuming the functions of office as President but, as it well known, in Re Akoto,8 the Supreme Court of Ghana held that these declarations were not enforceable or justiciable but were mere declarations of goals to which the government and the President would aspire; and that the Constitution, [p.187] therefore, in the hands of the judiciary, left this country without adequate protection in the field of political and civil rights. It is now considered by practically all respectable legal opinions that the judiciary woefully failed the people of this country in their judgment in the Re Akoto case and missed a wonderful opportunity to strike a blow for justice and human rights. But of this, more anon.

It is against this background that the Constitutions of 1969, 1979 and 1992 made far-reaching provisions in the field of human rights. These provisions contain the usual categories of human rights, including political and civil rights and economic, social and cultural rights. It is not intended to examine the provisions of the Constitutions of this country which preceded the current  Constitution, 1992. My remarks will be confined to the provisions of the Constitution, 1992 of the Republic of Ghana.

The provisions dealing directly with human rights are contained in chapter 5 of the Constitution, 1992 headed "Fundamental Human Rights and Freedoms" and they contain the usual categories of human rights and fundamental freedoms. Article 13 deals with the right to life; article 14 with the right to personal liberty; article 15 with the right to human  dignity which includes freedom from torture or other cruel, inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment and freedom from any other condition that detracts or is likely to detract from a person's dignity and worth as a human being; article 16 with freedom from slavery or forced labour; article 17 with equality before the law and freedom from discrimination on the grounds of gender, race, colour, ethnic origin, religion, creed or social or economic status; article 18 with the right to privacy of home and other property; article 19 with the right to a fair trial; article 20 with the right to property and freedom from deprivation of property except in certain circumstances and upon the prompt payment of fair and adequate compensation; article 21 with freedom of speech and expression, including freedom of the press and other media, freedom of thought, conscience and belief including academic freedom, freedom of religion, freedom to assemble including freedom to take part in processions and demonstrations, freedom of association including freedom to form or join trade unions or other associations, the right to information and freedom of movement including the right to move freely in Ghana, the right to leave and to enter Ghana and immunity from expulsion from Ghana, the right and freedom to form and join political parties and to participate in political activities and the right of a person whose freedom has been restricted by the order of a court to have his case reviewed by that court; article 22 with property rights of spouses; article 23 with the right to a fair and reasonable conduct of one's case by administrative bodies and officials; article 24 with economic rights; article 25 with social and educational rights; article 26 with cultural rights and practices including a prohibition of all customary practices which "dehumanise or cause injuries to the physical and mental well-being of a person"; article 27 with women’s rights; article 28 [p.188] with children's rights; article 29 with the rights of disabled persons and article 30 with the rights of the sick. Articles 31 and 32 contain provisions regulating the exercise of emergency powers and severely restrict the curtailment of the rights and freedoms guaranteed by the Constitution during an emergency.

Article 33 gives wide powers of adjudication in matters relating to the violation or potential violation of the fundamental human rights and freedoms guaranteed by the Constitution so that anyone who complains that a provision of the Constitution had been or is being or is likely to be contravened in relation to him may, without prejudice to any other remedies that are lawfully open to him, apply to the High Court for redress; and the High Court is given wide powers to proceed by giving such directions or orders as it may consider appropriate for the purposes of enforcing or securing the enforcement of any of the provisions of the Constitution on the fundamental human rights and freedoms of persons in Ghana including the issue of writs in the nature of habeas corpus, certiorari, mandamus, prohibition and quo warranto.

The jurisdiction thus conferred on the High Court in respect of the enforcement of the fundamental human rights provisions of the Constitution and the prevention of violations of such rights and freedoms is so far-reaching that it is submitted that this jurisdiction may be exercised without necessarily conforming to the usual practice whereby an application for redress is categorised or brought under a particular rubric such as an application for habeas corpus, etc and subject to any rules that may be made to this end by the Rules of Court Committee under article 33(4) of the Constitution, it is submitted that a simple application by way of a motion to the High Court for redress will be sufficient and it would not be necessary to express one’s application or formulate it in any particular manner or form in accordance with the old rules.

 All the rights enumerated above as provided for in article 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20, 21 and 23 may be classified as political or civil rights whereas those contained in article 22, 24, 25, 26, 27, 28 and 29 may be classified loosely as social, economic and cultural rights. The latter category of rights and freedoms contains some rights and freedoms which cannot, strictly speaking, be classified as economic or cultural; and some of these such as the property rights of spouses, women's rights, children's rights, the rights of the disabled and the rights of the sick may in fact be political and civil rights.

Chapter 5 of the Constitution is not the only part of the Constitution dealing with fundamental human rights and freedoms. Chapter 18 which deals with the Commission on Human Rights and Administrative Justice is intended to put in place mechanisms for ensuring the speedy implementation of the human rights provisions of the Constitution as well as the expeditious redress of grievances arising out of violations and abuses of the fundamental [p.189] human rights and freedoms of persons in Ghana.

It is also important, when considering matters of fundamental human rights and freedoms, to take into account the Directive Principle of State Policy contained in chapter 6 of the Constitution and which all citizens, parliamentarians, the President, the Judiciary, the Council of State, the cabinet, political parties and all other persons and bodies are enjoined to be guided by in applying or interpreting the Constitution or any other law, and in undertaking and implementing any policy decisions so as to lead to the creation of a “just and free society.” The Directive Principles of State Policy contain the philosophical and ideological underpinnings of the Constitution as a whole and it is submitted that the provisions dealing with the Directive Principles of State Policy complement the provisions on fundamental human rights and freedoms as do the provisions on the Commission on Human Rights and Administrative Justice.

Civil and political rights are often regarded as negative rights in so far as they contain and impose restrictions and restraints upon activities by persons, organisations and governments which impinge on the enjoyment of life by persons in society. Economic, Social and Cultural rights are considered more in the nature of positive rights in so far as they contain and confer in positive terms, the right to education, employment or other economic or social needs. Solidarity or collective rights such as the right to development and the right of peoples to cultural, economic, political and social needs are equally considered as positive rather than negative rights.

THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN TWO SETS OF HUMAN RIGHTS

What is the relationship between civil and political rights on the one hand and economic, social, cultural and solidarity or collective rights on the other hand? It is often asserted that political and civil rights mean nothing to a homeless and hungry person. It is said that to such a person the only things that matter are social and economic rights. Such an argument is, without any doubt, not only facile but naive and proceeds upon the assumption that all that is necessary to a human being is a place of shelter, clothes to wear and food to eat. If that were so, human beings would not be very different from the lesser beings of creation. The Zoological Society of London maintains excellent facilities in the London Zoo for animals of various species, except human beings, where these animals are provided with more than adequate housing and sufficient food; yet none of us, and I am sure none of those who consider economic and social rights as pre-eminent, would consider going to spend a single day as an inmate of the London Zoo, enjoying equal facilities with the animals that are kept there. Man, the Bible says, shall not live by bread alone.

The point must be made that economic and social rights are complementary to political and civil rights. The enjoyment of political and civil [p.190] rights is a necessary precondition for peace and the removal of tension from society; and the prevalence of peace and the absence of tension are necessary prerequisites of social and economic development. The enjoyment of human rights and fundamental freedoms in the sense of political and civil rights is the goal of all economic and social activity; and economic and social well-being underpins the enjoyment of political and civil rights. Of the two categories of rights, economic and social on the one hand and the political and civil on the other, none can be said to be pre-eminent. Both sets of rights are important for the full realisation of the individual’s potential as a human being in society.

As was said by the Secretary-General of the United Nations, Boutrous Boutrous-Ghali in his message for Human Rights Day on 10 December 1992:

“Full human dignity means not only freedom from torture, but also freedom from starvation. It means freedom to vote as it means the right to education. It means freedom of belief as it means the right to health. It means the right to enjoy all rights without discrimination. And true development requires a solid basis of democracy and popular participation.”

It is often said by leaders in third world countries, most of whom preside over totalitarian or autocratic regimes, that people in developing countries have no need of human rights and that what they need is development in the sense of the acquisition of the material wherewithal for human survival. Development in the modern sense, however, includes participation in decision-making, equal opportunity, equal access to resources and adherence to international human rights standards. It is therefore necessary to assert that political and civil rights are as important for the under-developed and developing countries as they are for the so-called developed countries.

Some leaders of third world countries have also been heard to complain that the call for civil and political rights is an attempt to impose other beliefs and cultures upon the countries over which they rule. Such leaders are, however, quick to assert the fundamental equality of all human beings and to deny any inferiority in the peoples living in third world countries to those living in western industralised countries. They do not pause to consider what the necessary consequence or corollary of the fundamental equality of human beings is in relation to the enjoyment of those rights which are classified as human. If all human beings are equal, then they are entitled to the enjoyment of the same rights wherever they may be and whatever the level of their development or the nature of their cultural practices. To deny the entitlement of all human beings, wherever they may be and whatever their level of development, to fundamental, political and civil rights is to deny the fundamental equality of all human beings.

[p.191]

The argument about the universality of political and civil rights as well as of economic and social rights is unanswerable. The absence of civil and political rights creates a situation which enables autocratic and authoritarian regimes to blossom and it is therefore almost invariably such governments that deny the indispensable necessity for civil and political rights. They realise that if civil and political rights prevail, their regimes of autocracy and authoritarianism would not last long. They therefore hide behind a facade of human development and economic prosperity to prevent the exercise of political and civil rights so as to enable them carry on, for the rest of their lives, a regime or regimes of suppression of the peoples over whom they rule.

All talk of human rights and fundamental freedoms would be meaningless unless there is in place to enforce such rights and prevent their violation or abuse, an independent, incorruptible, impartial and courageous judiciary. To the extent that human rights are not enforceable or are not enforced, they are like "natural rights" which were castigated by Jeremy Bentham, as has been noted earlier, as "rhetorical nonsense" and "nonsense upon stilts."9 The famous Mr. Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes defined law in the following terms: "The prophecies of what the courts will do in fact and nothing more pretentious is what I mean by the law.”10 If the courts will not enforce the rights alleged to be guaranteed by the Constitution then such rights will cease very rapidly to be classified as legal rights in the sense in which such rights are ordinarily understood as enforceable claims and entitlements to which effect is given by a body established in society for the purpose.

THE JUDICIARY IN GHANA AND HUMAN RIGHTS ENFORCEMENT

As was noted earlier, except for a few notable cases, the judiciary in this country has woefully failed the people in the matter of the enforcement of their fundamental human rights. In the famous case of Re Akoto referred to ealier,11 the court was called upon to enforce a provision of the Republican Constitution of 1960. That provision, namely article 13(1), enjoined the President upon the assumption of office to make a solemn declaration of adherence to the principles which were described as "fundamental". These principles include the necessity for the maintenance of freedom and justice, and a restriction on the deprivation of freedom of religion or speech and of the right to move and assemble without hindrance as well as the right of access to the courts of law.

It is important to note that article 13(2) of the Constitution, 1960 en- [p.192] trenched the provision so that it could be altered only by the people of Ghana at a referendum or a special exercise to ascertain their wishes. One would have thought that such a fundamental principle is so important that it would make sense and be of any use only if it was enforceable by a legal process. Yet the distinguished members of the Supreme Court of Ghana found it possible to say that those who contended that presidential declarations were justiciable and enforceable were basing their contention on a misconception of the “intent, purpose and effect” of the article which, they held, contained nothing more than was contained and conceived in the Coronation Oath taken by the British Monarch, an argument which had been dangled before their eyes by Mr Geoffrey Bing, counsel for the Government of Ghana and which they were quick to grab.

The reasoning of the court in Re Akoto is now generally considered to be unsound but it has been followed in a number of other decisions of our courts in which a similar stand has been taken by our judges. There is a tendency on the part of our judges to shrink from the determination or resolution of social, political, sociological or other such issues on the ground that they are not strictly legal issues, forgetting that the primary concern of the law is with the behaviour of human beings in society and the regulation of such behaviour.

ENFORCEMENT OF HUMAN RIGHTS: THE INDIAN EXPERIENCE

Our Constitutions of 1969, 1979 and 1992 have been informed, to a large extent, by the Constitution of India. The Indian Constitution contains provisions on fundamental rights and on directive principles of state policy in language which is very similar to the language used in our said Constitution. The experience of India in the enforcement of the relevant provisions of their Constitution dealing with fundamental human rights has demonstrated that the Indian Supreme Court has not shrunk from grappling with social, economic and sociological issues and problems, and they have demonstrated an independence which is justified by the constitutional provisions under which they operate.12

In a celebrated case known as the UP Judges Case,13 a man published a pamphlet and was reprimanded by the Legislative Assembly of Uttar Pradesh, the largest state in the Indian Federation, for having committed contempt of the assembly. Thereafter, he wrote a letter which was also construed as constituting another contempt and for this he was sentenced to seven days imprisonment. Subsequently, an advocate of the High Court of [p.193] Uttar Pradesh moved the Court for a writ of habeas corpus and obtained bail for the gentleman five days after he had been sentenced to jail. The Speaker of the Uttar Pradesh Assembly, the chief minister and the superintendent of the jail where this gentleman was incarcerated were made parties to the application for habeas corpus. Two judges of the High Court granted the writ of habeas corpus.

The assembly refused to make a return on the writ but instead passed a resolution to the effect that the two judges, the advocate who applied for the habeas corpus and the applicant himself were guilty of contempt of the assembly and that they should therefore be taken into custody and brought before the bar of the House. In pursuance of this resolution, warrants were issued against the judges, the advocate and the applicant.

When news of this reached the two judges, they immediately asked the High Court of Uttar Pradesh for a stay of the Warrants and the full High Court of 28 judges, at a joint sitting, made the necessary order staying execution of the warrants, and another bench of 23 judges of the same court made another order staying execution of the warrants as regarded the advocate and the applicant. The situation thus created was one of a head-on collision between two giants, the Legislature and the judiciary. The Speaker of the Uttar Pradesh Assembly, realising the situation in which the action of the assembly had landed them, referred the matter to the privileges committee of the assembly and, on that ground, withdrew the warrants for the apprehension of the judges, the advocate and the applicant.

The privileges committee decided to call the two judges, the advocate and the applicant before the committee to give an explanation for their conduct and they thereupon issued notices to that effect. Thereupon a bench of 23 judges issued an interim order prohibiting the speaker and the privileges committee from implementing the resolution and stayed execution of the notices issued by the privileges committee. Thus a stalemate was reached.

In order to resolve this stalemate, the President of the Indian Federation made a reference under the Indian Constitution to the Supreme Court of India for its advisory opinion relating to the matters raised by the impasse that had arisen between the High Court and in the Legislative Assembly of Uttar Pradesh, and by this reference an opportunity and a way-out was created for the Legislative Assembly of Uttar Pradesh to withdraw its notices which it did. The reference by the President of India was argued in due course and the court sitting in a bench of seven judges decided by a majority of six to one that:

(1) The legislators had absolute freedom of speech in the house and were not subject to the restriction contained in the fundamental rights chapter of the Indian Constitution.

(2) A law made by the legislature was subject to Fundamental rights and would be void if it was inconsistent with it.

[p.194]

(3) If an application was made to the High Court or the Supreme Court for a writ of habeas corpus, no legislature would be competent to raise a preliminary objection as to jurisdiction.

(4) The right of the legislative assembly to make rules for regulating its own procedure is subject to the provisions with regard to fundamental rights in the Constitution.

(5) In the enforcement of fundamental human rights guaranteed to the citizen, the legal profession plays a vital role and the privileges of the assembly to punish a citizen or his advocate by general warrant for contempt for such enforcement is inconsistent with the Constitution.

(6) The privileges of the assembly were not subject to fundamental rights but where there is a conflict between a privilege and a fundamental right the conflict has to be resolved by a harmonious construction.

The majority of the judges therefore decided that the two judges of the Uttar Pradesh High Court were competent to issue the orders they did and that neither the advocate nor the applicant were guilty of contempt and the Legislative Assembly was not competent to direct production of the two judges, the advocate and the applicant before it for their explanation for contempt of the assembly. They further held that the full bench of the Uttar Pradesh High Court which had passed the interim orders staying execution of the notices and warrants issued by the Uttar Pradesh Legislative Assembly was competent to do so. What the committee of privileges of the Uttar Pradesh Assembly did next is very intriguing indeed. It recorded that the opinion of the majority of the Indian Supreme Court in the matter was wrong but that “in view of the importance of the harmonious functioning of the two important organs of the state, ie the legislature and the judiciary", the matter would be recommended to be closed by an expression of displeasure!

In this case the High Court of Uttar Pradesh and the Supreme Court of India displayed a robustness of independence and integrity which it would be difficult to imagine happening in our own country. The Indian courts have, by a formulation of a constitutional doctrine known as the doctrine of the basic structure of the Constitution, prevented the legislature in India from amending the Constitution of India so as to change "the basic structure"; of the Constitution. It would seem from judgments of the courts that this “basic structure” includes:

(1) the supremacy of the Constitution;

(2) the Republican and democratic forms of government, ie free elections, multi-party system, no monarchical or dictatorial government;

(3) equality;

(4) secular and non-theocratic character of the State;

[p.195]

(5) a federal policy; and

(6) independence of the judiciary and judicial review.

A celebrated case will illustrate this principle. This case is known to Indian constitutional lawyers as the Fundamental Rights Case.14 The background to this case is that India had a feudal land tenure called Zamindari which practically created a status of serfdom for those who work on the land and prevented them from owning any land. One of the main platforms of the Indian National Congress was to reform this land tenure so as to redistribute land to the landless peasants.

In 1951 and 1965 the Supreme Court of India had held in the cases of Shankari Prasad v Union of India15 and Sajjan Singh v Rejasthan16 respectively, that the power to amend the Indian Constitution included the power to take away or abridge fundamental human rights and particularly the right to property. However, in 1967 in the Golaknath Case,17 a bench of eleven judges by a majority of six to five, overruled the earlier cases and held that no constitutional amendment could take away or abridge fundamental rights conferred by the Constitution.

This judgment was a watershed in Indian constitutional history and it created quite a sensation and a stir among politicians who argued that the decision threatened various social welfare and social reform legislation. To get round this difficulty, the Supreme Court, on the ground of acquiescence and completed transactions, did not strike down earlier constitutional amendments which violated the decision they had arrived at. The Supreme Court, however, held that future amendments would be invalid if they took away or abridged fundamental rights.

The response of the politicians to this sensational decision, which practically drove a coach and four through the social legislative programme which they had planned, was swift. They passed the 24th and 25th Amendments to the Indian Constitution. The 24th Amendment sought to give power to Parliament to amend the Constitution in exercise of its “constituent power” and sought to put such amendment outside the purview of the “law” within the meaning of article 13 of the Constitution and thereby attempted to overthrow the reasoning of the Supreme Court in the Golaknath case.18

The 25th Amendment sought to permit property to be acquired for a public purpose on payment of an amount which may not necessarily be regarded as just compensation. It also included a clause so all-embracing as to make those who underestimated the ingenuity and genius of lawyers to [p.196] think that the jurisdiction of the courts had been totally ousted. That clause read as follows:

“Notwithstanding anything contained in article 13, no law giving effect to the policy of the state towards securing the principles specified in clause (b) or clause (c) of article 39 shall be deemed to be void on the ground that it is inconsistent with or takes away or abridge any of the rights conferred by article 14, article 19 or article 31 and no law containing a declaration that it is for giving effect to such policy shall be called in question in any court on the gound that it does not give effect to such policy.”

The two amendments were immediately challenged in court. A bench of thirteen judges of the Supreme Court, by a majority of seven to six, held that the constituent power of amendment granted to the legislature was not unlimited and carried implied and inherent limitations. They held further that “the basic structure” of the Indian Constitution could not be destroyed by a process of amendment; and although the court overruled the Golaknath decision, they held that the portion of the clause in the 25th Amendment seeking to oust the jurisdiction of the court to entertain actions challenging the validity of laws designed to give effect to the policy of the state towards securing certain principles on the ground that it does not give effect to such policy, was invalid and consequently struck it down.

It is worthy of note that in two subsequent cases, namely Indira Nehru Gandhi v Rai Narian19 and the Minerva Mills Case,20 the Supreme Court of India followed the decision in the Fundamental Rights Case21 and struck down two constitutional amendments passed by the Indian legislature.

Are our judges so sufficiently activist, innovative and courageous as to effectively tackle sections 34, 35 and 37 of the first schedule (Transitional Provisions) of the Constitution, 1992 which contain the infamous indemnity clauses and some of the most outrageous provisions making total nonsense of the idea of human rights contained in the bulk of the Constitution by following the trail blazed so ably and nobly by their Indian colleagues? I wonder or perhaps I should say, time alone will tell.

The Indian Supreme Court has also displayed judicial innovation, inventiveness and activism in the field of public interest litigation. Article 21 of the Indian Constitution provides that:

“No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law.”

In earlier cases, it was held that it was sufficient to deprive a person of [p.197] his liberty if the procedure adopted had been prescribed and had been adhered to. In later judgments, the concept of “procedural due process" was developed and it was held that article 21 contained a guarantee that the procedure adopted in each case should be fair, reasonable and just.

In a case known as Hussainara Khatoon v Bihar,22 news reports indicated that in the State of Bihar there was a large number of prisoners awaiting trial who were referred to as"under trials." This included women and some of these were detained for periods longer than the periods prescribed by way of punishment for their alleged offences. Being poor and weak and underprivileged, they were forgotten by both the courts and the state. A public-spirited woman lawyer moved the Supreme Court for relief on hearing of this news. The court immediately stepped in and granted relief. The court held that monetary bail insisted upon by the courts from poor indigents was violative of fair procedure under the Constitution and so also was the denial to these persons of a speedy trial. The court adopted the strategy of keeping the case in view before it and of giving affirmative directions concerning the applicants over a period of time so as to ensure that real redress was afforded them.

In another epoch-making case of D S Nakara v The Union of India,23 the government had made rules enlarging and increasing substantially the pension benefits of employees who retired after 1 April 1979; but employees who retired before that date were denied these benefits. When this differential treatment was challenged in court, a bench of five judges unanimously held that the same benefits should be given to the pre-April 1979 pensioners as had been given to the pensioners who retired after 1 April 1979 and that the denial to the pre-April 1979 pensioners of the benefits accorded the pensioners who retired after 1 April 1979 was discriminatory and arbitrary and violated the equality clause of the Indian Constitution. The equality clause of the Indian Constitution is contained in article 14 while the corresponding clause in our Constitution, 1992 is contained in article 17. The state resisted the action on the ground that the court had no power to direct affirmative action against the government which would mean an additional financial burden of several hundred million rupees and said that if the court desired, it could strike down or negative the pension benefits for all pensioners. The court, however, rejected this contention and ordered the state to pay to the pre-April 1979 pensioners the same benefits as the pensioners who retired after 1 April 1979. The court did not shrink from the political implications of their decision, nor were they overawed by such implications.

One more instance of the judicial activism and robust independence of the Indian judiciary and its preparedness to use the Constitution to come to [p.198] the aid of the oppressed so as to achieve social justice is exemplified in the case known as Bonded Labourers Case: Bandhua Mukti Morcha v The Union of India,24 a 1984 case in which a petition was presented to the Indian Supreme Court by an organisation dedicated to the welfare and release of bonded labourers in India. These labourers were alleged to be living in bondage and under insufferable human conditions. The stone quarries in which they worked belonged to the State of Haryana but the State farmed out the work to contractors who employed the bonded labourers. These labourers were not paid proper wages and were confined to the stone quarries in a state of semi-serfdom and were not allowed to leave and, what was worse for them, they were too poor to be able to mount an effective protest.

The court intervened on their behalf, appointed two lawyers to report to the court on the enforcement of a series of directions that it gave and a commissioner to make a sociological report on the condition of the labourers. The court overruled technical objections as to locus standi and access and said that the court’s powers under the Constitution were to give appropriate directions and were not confined to the old anglo-saxon writs of certiorari, mandamus and prohibition. Thus did the Indian Supreme Court strike another vital blow for freedom. The court concluded by observing as follows:

“When the court entertains public interest litigation, it does not do so in a cavilling spirit or in a confrontational mood or with a view to tilting at executive authority, or seeking to usurp it, but its attempt is only to ensure observance of social and economic rescue programmes, legislative as well as executive, freedom for the benefit of the have-nots and the handicapped and to protect them against violation of their basic human rights which is also the constitutional obligation of the executive. The court is merely assisting in the realisation of the constitutional objectives.”

It is submitted that the Supreme Court of this country has sufficient authority under Directive Principles of State Policy in chapter 6 as well as under article 2 of the Constitution, 1992 to provide for the disadvantaged in our community the same or similar reliefs as the Indian Supreme Court has been providing for the poorer classes in India if only they will be willing to use the powers they undoubtedly possess and if they fully realise the enormity of the powers that they possess under the Constitution. Much would depend upon how they conceive of their duties but I have no doubt that the makers of our Constitution have clothed them with sufficient authority. Let them now act.

[p.199]

CONCLUSION

We may end this lecture by making a few concluding remarks. The 1990s may well be classified as the decade of human rights. This decade has ushered in such a commitment to and a concern for human rights by the international community that no longer are they willing and prepared to look on hopelessly while governments especially in third world countries oppress their citizens under various pretexts while they at the same time extend financial and other aid to the governments concerned, which aid is often misapplied and misappropriated. This is a decade of aid conditionalities which is a welcome departure from several decades of indifference justified by an invocation of the retrograde principle of non-interference in the internal affairs of sovereign states.

 

This new change in aid-giving policy has resulted in no small way in the introduction of multi-party democracy in a number of countries and it is hoped that this change will gather sufficient momentum to lead to the dethronement of all oppressive regimes in the world. Autocratic regimes complain against what they describe as the imposition of alien standards and cultures on them under the guise of conditionalities. The response is that the international community and the western world do not owe the third world countries a living and they are entitled, therefore, as a condition for the extension of the largesse to insist that governments who will ask for and receive aid should ensure that fundamental human rights are observed in their countries. This is only fair because aid must ultimately be for the benefit of the masses of the people and it would have no significance for these masses if they were left to languish under the iron heels of oppressors and dictators. Such regimes have a clear choice: either to accept the conditionalities along with the largesse or to reject them and be denied any aid.

Fundamental human rights have a hollow ring if they are not effectively enforced by an independent, impartial, incorruptible and courageous judiciary. So far, in this country, we have not seen very encouraging signs of this emerging but we still entertain the hope that this would emerge in the not too distant future for the benefit of all Ghanaians and those within her territorial waters.

In the final analysis, whether we enjoy fundamental human rights or not will depend upon ourselves and no one else. We must not look to others to fight our battles for us. As Lord Byron said in one of the cantos in his immortal "Child Harold's Pilgrimage" in relation to the struggle to free Greece from Turkish domination25:

[p.200]

“Hereditary bondsmen! Know ye not. Who would be free themselves must strike the blow?”

Ghanaians are not hereditary bondsmen. We are a proud people with a history and tradition of courage. Let us be faithful to that history and be worthy of that tradition for that is the only way we can maintain our integrity as a nation and be secure against oppression and tyranny.
