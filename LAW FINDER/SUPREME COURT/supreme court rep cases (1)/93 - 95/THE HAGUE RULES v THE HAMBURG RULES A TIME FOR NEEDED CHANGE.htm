THE HAGUE RULES v THE HAMBURG RULES: A TIME FOR NEEDED CHANGE IN GHANAIAN LAW ON THE CARRIAGE OF GOODS BY SEA [1993-95] VOL. XIX 201—224

KOFI MBIAH*

 

INTRODUCTION

Over 90 percent of world trade is seaborne, that is by maritime transport. This means of transport is facilitated among others by two major ingredients, viz (i) an infrastructural network; and (ii) a regulatory framework. The thesis of this paper will deal with one aspect of the international regulatory framework for the movement of goods by sea. The first part will examine and bring out some of the weakness of the international rules for carriage of goods by sea commonly referred to as the Hague Rules1 while the second part will examine the Hamburg Rules2 which is of a relatively recent origin and seeks to redress some of the weakness of the Hague Rules.

In an attempt to evaluate the Hague Rules it is worth recalling the genesis of the rules. For many years, the English courts recognised the carrier's right to contract out of his liability as part of the doctrine of freedom of contract. Invariably, this led to a situation where in the words of one scholar it appeared that there was no other obligation on the shipowner than to receive freight.3 The shipowner thus carried goods free from any risk on his part. As might be expected, commercial organisations and other shippers having suffered as a result of the "freedom of contract", began to question the right of shipowners to contract out of their common law liability. So finally in September 1921, at a meeting held at the Hague, a code of rules known now as the Hague Rules were formulated and recommended for international adoption by the Maritime Law Committee of the International Law Association. Subsequently, in 1924, twenty-four maritime nations met in Brussels and signed the "International Convention for the Unification of Certain Rules of Law Relating to Bills of Lading" which is now commonly referred to as the Hague Rules. For the purpose of this paper, it is important to recall that the Hague Rules have been adopted in this country and incorporated in the Bills of Lading Act, 1961 (Act 42).

The Hague Rules have thus been in existence for well-nigh a century, during which period those who were instrumental in fashioning the rules, ie the developed world, have dominated shipping. In the words of Mike Shah:

"They laid down the law, they divided the oceans, they organised and [p.202] shared trade and most important of all, they did so largely as their own needs dictated. An exercise of power that only later generations in the present century would question as to its political and commercial morality."4

Shah's observations aptly sum up the domination and influence of the developed countries in the carriage of goods by sea. By 1988, the world merchant fleet of ships over 300grt consisted of 33,735 vessels with a total of 372.6 million grt (610.4 million dwt). The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) countries had a 32 percent share; open registries (most of them belonging to the developed countries) had 35 per cent and the developing countries had 20 per cent.5 Naturally the rules were skewed in the interest of the developed maritime countries who provided shipping space. The rules thus made light the responsibilities and liabilities of shipowners as against the interests of developing maritime nations who primarily were users of the service. Despite the fact that by 1988 developing countries had increased their percentage of total world tonnage to about 20 per cent, the developed countries still dominate the means of carriage.

The very recent times have seen feverish attempts by developing maritime nations to increase their merchant marine fleet, but these attempts should blind no one as to the need to protect the interest of developing maritime nations who will for ages to come still be users of shipping services predominantly controlled by the developed nations. Thus the Hague Rules hitherto introduced to curtail the unbridled freedom of contract which favoured shipowners, had by the turn of the century become woefully inadequate in protecting the interest of shippers. There was therefore the need to review the situation which had held sway for almost a century.

The United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD), which was dominated by the developing countries, was seen by the developing countries as the best forum to call attention to the inadequacies of the Hague Rules. After a series of deliberations the UNCTAD finally, in 1978 in Hamburg, came out with a set of rules known as the United Nations Convention on the Carriage of Goods by Sea commonly referred to as the Hamburg Rules. The rules seek to deal with some of the shortcomings of the Hague Rules and has been described by Tetley as an "extraordinary document." He explains: "extraordinary because the Rules show the influence of shipowners and shippers, the revendications of P & I and cargo underwriters, the complaints of carrier and shipper nations and the aspirations of wealthy and emerging nations."6 Thus the Hamburg Rules represent a hotch-potch of interests; and while none actually predominates, it seeks to achieve an [p.203] intricate and delicate balance between the varied interests. There is also no gainsaying the fact that the rules by way of modification of language used in earlier texts (notably the Harter Act, Hague Rules and Hague/Visby Rules) is now clearer and has done away with the great imbalance that skewed disproportionately in favour of the carrier.

II. THE HAGUE RULES

Salient Features of the Hague Rules

Definitions

Article 1 of the Hague Rules deals with the definition of words and phrases used in the text of the rules. These include "ship", "goods", "carrier", "carriage of goods", etc. While these definitions may seem clear in themselves, they have often times posed problems in their practical application and would be the subject of comment when the rules are examined. Article 2 of the Hague Rules deals with the various forms of risks covered by the rules. In brief, the rules cover risks relating to the loading, handling, storage, carriage, custody, care and discharge of the goods. Article 3 is one of the most important sections of the rules which has undoubtedly led to so much litigation. Under article 3, r l, the carrier is made duty bound before and at the beginning of the voyage to exercise due diligence in providing three things, viz:

(a) to make the ship seaworthy;

(b) to properly man, equip and supply the ship; and

(c) to make the holds, refrigerating and cool chambers, and all other parts of the ship in which goods are carried fit and safe for their reception, carriage and preservation.

One problem that has continually bogged legal minds as well as maritime merchants relates to what a seaworthy ship is and the time at which the ship is supposed to be seaworthy?

In the case of Re Unus Shipping Co7 an action was brought by the respondents against the appellants as owners of the vessel for damages for the loss of her cargo of 33,000 bushels of corn shipped at Halifax for Quebec. The vessel foundered shortly after setting sail due to high wind and wave action. It was the contention of the appellants that the loss was by causes beyond the control of the appellants by perils of the sea, or accidents of navigation from all of which the appellants, having exercised due diligence, were exempted by  the Hague Rules. It was the opinion of the Chief Justice that the vessel was unseaworthy when she began the voyage by reason of the inadequacy of the precautions taken to prevent the corn from shifting, that the situation resulted from actual fault and neglect by the shipowners who did not exercise due diligence to make her seaworthy. The [p.204] learned Chief Justice concluded that there was clear inference from the evidence that the shifting of the cargo was the direct cause of the foundering of the ship without which she would not have been lost; and that it resulted from negligent storage which made her unseaworthy from the beginning of the voyage.  Judgment was therefore entered for the cargo owners.

Seaworthiness cannot however be precisely defined. Professor Tetley has defined seaworthiness as "the state of a vessel in such a condition, with such equipment and manned by such master and crew that normally the cargo will be loaded, carried, cared for and discharged properly and safely on the contemplated voyage." He continues:

"Seaworthiness means many things—a tight hull and hatches, and proper systems of pumps valves and boilers and engines, generators and refrigeration equipment in good order. A seaworthy vessel must be equipped with up to date charts, mariners notices and navigating equipment; the crew must be properly trained and instructed in the ships operation and idiosyncracies. Equipment must be properly labelled, diagrams must be available and posted."8

There is no doubt that defining seaworthiness is cumbersome and added to this is the fact that the seaworthiness of a ship is closely related to the exercise of due diligence to make the ship seaworthy. What amounts to due diligence on the part of an owner or his servants has been a subject of contentious litigations. The case of Maxine Footwear Co Ltd v Canadian Government Merchant Marine Ltd9 serves to clarify the point. The facts were that after the ship had been loaded and was about to set sail, it was found that some pipes were blocked by ice at the point at which they met the ship's side. The master instructed one of the officers to have the pipes thawed out. This work was done by means of an acetylene torch. There was a cork insulation around the pipes. The heat of the acetylene torch acting on the cork started a fire which led to the scuttling of the ship and consequently the loss of her cargo. It was the contention of the cargo owners that the shipowner did not exercise due diligence to make the ship seaworthy.

In the course of their judgments, their lordships pointed out that the condition (that is, of the exercise of due diligence to make a vessel seaworthy) is not fulfilled merely because the shipowner is personally diligent. The condition requires that diligence shall in fact have been exercised by the shipowner or by those he employs for the purpose. The failure to exercise due diligence by the fourth officer was therefore failure to exercise due diligence by the carrier within the meaning of article 3, r 1. Their lordships went further to state that article 3, r 1 is an overriding obligation. If it is not fulfilled and the non-fulfilment causes the damage, the immunities of article [p.205] 4 cannot be relied on. In explaining the stage or period of unseaworthiness, their lordships opined that the phrase "before and at the beginning of the voyage" means the period from at least the beginning of the loading until the vessel starts on her voyage. It was therefore the view of their lordships that the obligation to exercise due diligence to  make the ship seaworthy continued over the whole period from the beginning of the loading until the ship sank. It further reasoned that there was a failure to exercise due diligence during that period. As a result, the ship became unseaworthy and this unseaworthiness caused the damage and loss of the cargo and judgment was thus entered for the cargo owners.

Further, in the case of Muncaster Castle,10 several cases of tinned food were shipped on the respondents' vessel at Sydney for carriage to London under bills of lading subject to the Australian Sea Carriage of Goods Act, 1924. When the goods were discharged, most of  the cases were found damaged by seawater which had entered the hold through defective inspection of the covers to storm valves. Evidence was led to show that the shipowner employed a reputable firm of ship repairers to carry out the job of fixing and inspecting the valves. It was the contention of the shipowners that by employing competent repairers, the burden of due diligence to make the vessel seaworthy was thereby discharged. One would have thought that it was sufficient for owners to employ reputable and competent repairers for the job. The House of Lords however held that the respondents had not discharged the burden of proving that they had exercised due diligence to make the vessel seaworthy.

The fact that the Maxine Footwear case and the Muncaster Castle case held for cargo interest is not what is significant here. While the Maxine Footwear case went as far as to the Privy Council, the Muncaster Castle case went as far as to the House of Lords. The arguments advanced by both parties and the fact that after the decision in the Muncaster Castle case, shipowners sought to have an amendment of the rules in Brussels in 1967 and 1968 is even more significant. It only serves to demonstrate the extent to which there is varied opinion as to the concepts of seaworthiness and due diligence. The decision in the Muncaster Castle case has been widely criticised but again this is due to the fact that the language used in the rules is not apt or precise enough.

Notice Period

While still on article 3, it may be relevant to deal with the notice regime which is also provided for under the same article. Rule 6 of article 3 provides:

"Unless notice of  loss or damage and the general nature of such loss or damage be given in writing to the carrier or his agent at the port [p.206] of discharge before or at the time of removal of the goods into the custody of the person entitled to delivery thereof under the contract of carriage or, if the loss of damage be not apparent, within three days, such removal shall be prima facie evidence of the delivery by the carrier of the goods as described in the bill of lading."

Rule 6 goes further to add:

"In any event the carrier and the ship shall be discharged from all liability in respect of loss or damage unless suit is brought within one year after delivery of the goods or the date when the goods should have been delivered."

It is worth mentioning that the notice period is fraught with problems. Sometimes the loss or damage is not apparent until after a week or more when a close examination of the goods is actually done. Three days is thus inadequate to enable a proper report on non-apparent loss or damage to be made. Further to this, the period within which one can take suit is limited to one year.

In the case of Compania Colombiana De Seguros v Pacific Steam Navigation Co,11 a cargo of electric cables loaded in the defendants' vessel was insured by the plaintiffs for a voyage from Liverpool to Buenaventura, Colombia and was delivered in a damaged condition on 12 December 1954. The plaintiffs indemnified the cargo owners who assigned to them their rights to sue the defendants. The plaintiffs brought an action in the Supreme Court of New York on 2 November 1955 against the defendant but it was dismissed for lack of jurisdiction. The plaintiffs then brought their action against the defendants on 7 January 1960 in the English courts. It was the decision of the court that the claim was statute-barred under article 3, r 6 for the words "unless suit is brought within one year" meant "unless the suit before the court is brought within one year." The fact that the New York proceedings were brought within one year was immaterial.

It is obvious that in most sea trades, it is sometimes a cumbersome process knowing who to proceed against as parties in an action, especially in cases where there is transshipment. By the time all is set to bring the action, one year might have elapsed. A case may begin in our courts within the limitation period of one year but if our courts decline jurisdiction after one year then the shipper would be faced with a similar situation as in the above case. It may be said that the most important effect of the rules is that the carrier can no longer contract out of what were previously regarded as minimum responsibilities and obligations. Viscount Sumner states this aptly in Gosse Millerd Ltd v Canadian Government Merchant Marine Ltd: 12

[p.207]

"The intention of this legislation in dealing with the liability of a shipowner as a carrier of goods by sea undoubtedly was to replace a conventional contract, in which it was constantly attempted, often with much success, to relieve the carrier from every kind of liability, by a legislative bargain..."

Laudable as this may seem, the rules still tilt very much in favour of the carrier. The rules for example, except "deck cargo", "live animals" from the definition of "goods."

Scope of Application

Under article 1 (c) goods are defined to include "wares, merchandise, and articles of every kind whatsoever" except live animals and cargo which by the contract of carriage is stated as being carried on deck and is so carried. Where parties agree on cargo being carried on deck, the Hague Rules do not apply and the carriage is then governed by the common law which permits the parties to agree in the bill of lading on certain exceptions. This gives shipowners a greater degree of protection since they can include all sorts of exculpatory clauses in the bill of lading and the limitations imposed on them by the Hague Rules will not apply.13  These days when a substantial percentage of container traffic is carried on deck, the shipper is highly disadvantaged as against the carrier by virtue of the fact that with the definition of "goods" under the rules, the carrier may choose to exempt himself from all liability for containers and their contents which are noted as being carried on deck and are actually so carried.

The Hague Rules make yet another important exclusion of liability in favour of the carrier as provided for in article 7. It states:

"Nothing herein contained shall prevent a carrier or a shipper from entering into any agreement, stipulation, condition, reservation or exemption as to the responsibility and liability of the carrier or the ship for the loss or damage to, or in connection with the custody and care and handling of goods prior to the loading on and subsequent to, the discharge from the ship on which the goods are carried by sea."

The import of this is that the carrier can exclude liability even for his own negligence where he holds goods otherwise than as a carrier, eg as warehouseman, before loading or after discharge. There is a dearth of Ghanaian case law regarding the carriage of goods by sea, particularly the interpretation of the Hague Rules as they apply under Ghanaian law. This view is borne out by an observation made by Archer JA (as he then was). He said:

[p.208]

"Finally, we wish to express our sympathy with learned trial judge in the court below in the predicament in which he found himself in his attempt to evaluate the provisions of the Hague Rules. The Hague Rules have been adopted in this country and have been incorporated in the Bills of Lading Act, 1961 (Act 42). Actions involving the application of the Hague Rules are very rare and cannot be found in our available law reports. Indeed, a trial judge who is confronted with an isolated case with no local previous decisions to guide him is bound to find himself in a maze."14

In the case of John Holt Shipping Services v Edward Nasser & Co Ltd15 where the learned judge made the above statement, the respondents ordered for a quantity of goods which arrived safely and were unloaded and stored in a public warehouse at the Takoradi Harbour. Their bills of lading delayed in arriving and when they eventually did, the goods could not be found. The Court of Appeal held the view that although at common law the shipowner's liability does not cease on the landing of the cargo, the appellants were exempted from liability by reason of the express provisions in the exemptions clause contained in the bill of lading. It was the contention of the court that the Hague Rules do in fact acknowledge the right of shipowners to contract out in matters not connected with the duties and obligations imposed by the rules. But as made out earlier, this affords a shipowner who at the same time is a custodian of cargo before loading or discharge, the opportunity to exempt himself from liability.

The view held by the Court of Appeal would be right if one holds the opinion that the Hague Rules apply only upon loading and discharge. In order to form an analytical view regarding the two schools of thought as to whether the rules apply only on loading and discharge, it is necessary to read article 7 together with article 3, r 8 which stipulates:

"Any clause, covenant or agreement in a contract of carriage relieving the carrier or the ship from liability for loss or damage to, or in connection with goods arising from negligence, fault or failure in the duties and obligations provided in this article or lessening such liability otherwise than as provided in this convention shall be null and void and of no effect.''

The Court of Appeal in the John Holt case went on to express that since by virtue of the definition of "carriage of goods" under article 1(e), "carriage of goods" covers the period from the time when the goods are loaded on to the time when they are discharged from the ship, article 7 was added ex abundanti cautela.

[p.209]

If the Hague Rules apply before loading and after discharge, then by virtue of article 3, r 8, any clause that exonerates the owner from liability during this period is contrary to the rules and therefore void. However, if they apply only from loading to discharge or tackle to tackle, then the decision of the court is in order. That the rules apply even before the cargo is loaded on the ship is expressed by Devlin J's opinion in the celebrated case of Pyrene Co Ltd v Scindia Steam Navigation Co Ltd.16 Here, whilst a fire tender was being loaded on board a vessel but before it had crossed the ship's rail, it was dropped and damaged through the negligence of the stevedores employed by the shipowners. No bill of lading was issued but the shipowners claimed that they could limit their liability under the Hague Rules. It is significant to note here that the cargo had not crossed the ship's rail and yet Devlin J was prepared to hold that the rules were applicable. One is tempted to ask: was it because it was close to the ship's rail? What could have been his reasoned judgment if it was farther away?" A priori, the conclusion can still be drawn that the rules are applicable before loading and consequently after discharge, ie going by the decision in the Pyrene case.

 

This then brings us back to articles 7 and 3, r 8. Indeed, if the Hague Rules apply before the goods are loaded, then it is submitted that the two articles do not complement each other but are rather contradictory; and if not, then article 7 is superfluous when considered alongside article 1(e) and would thus have been added ex abundanti cautela. The truth of it is that the rules thereby leave lawyers and judges alike, confused. In the John Holt case, reference was not made to the Pyrene case.

Nautical Fault Defence

The Hague Rules also provide for an almost unending catalogue of excepted perils which once established as the cause of loss, relieves the carrier from liability. Amongst these is the infamous article 4. Though case law abounds here, its direction has never been precise and litigation in this area of the law can only be described as prodigious. Under article 6, r 2 (a), the carrier and his servants are relieved from the consequences of error in the navigation or in the management of the ship. A bold attempt was made by Greer LJ in Gosse Millerd Ltd v Canadian Government Merchant Marine Ltd17 to set down precisely what is meant by "error in navigation and management of the ship." He puts it thus:

"If the cause of the damage is solely, or even primarily, a neglect to take reasonable care of the cargo, the ship is liable, but if the cause of the damage is a neglect to take reasonable care of the ship, or some part of it, as distinct from the cargo, the ship is relieved from liability; but if the negligence is not negligent towards the ship, but [p.210] only negligent failure to use the apparatus of the ship for the protection of the cargo, the ship is not so relieved."

It is submitted that it is easily discernible that there is so fine a line between fault in relation to the ship and fault in relation to the cargo, such that a clear distinction between the two would be difficult to draw.

In the case of Gosse Millerd Ltd,18 a cargo of tinplates was shipped in a vessel under a bill of lading incorporating the Hague Rules . During the voyage, she sustained damage and had to put into a port of distress; and whilst the repairs were being executed, the hatches were left open so that the workmen could go in and out of the hold more easily. The hatches were not protected when rain was falling and the tinplates were found damaged on arrival. It was the decision of the House of Lords that the shipowners were liable. Their lordships dismissed the plea by the owners that they could exempt themselves form liability since this was a case of negligence in the management of the ship. It was the view of the court that the term "negligence in the management of the ship" did not include negligence in the management of the hatches. This again is the fine line earlier referred to. How separate is the ship from its hatches?

One is inclined to believe that the adoption of this exception had its roots in the shipowner's apprehension about the skill of their crew during the embryonic stages of steam navigation, which incidentally was around the same time that the rules were drafted—1921. It is well-nigh a century now and despite the tremendous metamorphosis in the shipbuilding industry, coupled with the aid of computers as well as the acquisition of dexterity by sailors, this exception continues to exempt shipowners from liability. Even worse is the fact that, the provisions have even been applied to errors in routine acts of seamanship such as berthing and ballasting where cargo owners might justly expect the ship perform competently without risk to cargo.

It is not intended to deal with all that litany of exemptions contained in the Hague Rules. It is however worth drawing attention to another exception under article 4 (2) (c) which has often been relied upon by carriers to exonerate themselves from liability. The article exonerates the carrier from all losses occasioned by "perils, dangers and accidents of the sea or other navigable waters." What amounts to a "peril of the sea" has been subjected to varied interpretations and to this day, the application of the law to practical cases have been shrouded in obscurity and uncertainty. Generally speaking, the phrase "perils of the sea" is said to cover damages relating from dangers peculiar to maritime transport such as impact of waves, violent storms, fog etc, something which strictly speaking could have been controlled if the vessel was travelling on land. This exception is thus due to the peculiar [p.211] hazards of the sea. In the case of The Xantho19 Lord Herschell, speaking in relation to an insurance policy but observing that his remarks applied with equal force to bills of lading, reasoned:

"I think it is clear that the term 'peril of the sea, ' does not cover every accident or casualty which may happen to the subject-matter of the insurance on the sea. It must be a peril 'of' the sea. Again, it is well settled that it is not every loss or damage of which the sea is the immediate cause that is covered by these words."

He goes on:

"They do not protect, for example, against the natural and inevitable action of the winds and waves, which results in what may be described as wear and tear. There must be some casualty, something which could not be foreseen as one of the necessary incidents of the adventure."

Clear as this observation may seem, it has not enjoyed a general and unquestionable application. What is a "peril of the sea" has been explained in other cases differently. In the case of Keystone Transport v Dominion Steel & Coal Corporation20 the Supreme Court of Canada held thus:

"...to constitute a peril of the sea, the accident need not be of an extraordinary nature or arise from irresistible force, it is sufficient that it be the cause of damage to the goods at sea by the violent action of the wind and waves when such damage cannot be attributed to someone's negligence."

While in the earlier cited case an extraordinary circumstance which is unforeseen is stressed, in the latter, the point is made that the circumstance need not be extraordinary. The law on this is thus prone to varied interpretations. While some jurisdictions include non-violent wind and waves as perils of the sea, notwithstanding the presence of negligence by the crew, others make mention of violent wind and waves in the absence of negligence. In the United States, the judges have inclined to the view that the peril must be one that would ordinarily not have been expected in the area of the voyage at that time of the year. The present situation leaves the cargo owner who in the first place bargains from a position of weakness in no better situation with the confused state of the law.

[p.212]

Limitation of Liability

Yet another area of the Hague Rules that has brought about much difficulty in sea commerce is the provisions of article 4, r 5 which provides as follows:

"Neither the carrier nor the ship shall in any event be or become liable for any loss or damage to or in connection with goods in an amount exceeding £100 per package or unit or the equivalent of that sum in other currency unless the nature and value of such goods have been declared by the shipper before shipment and inserted in the bill of lading."

Under section 5 of the Bills of Lading Act, 1961 (Act 42) of Ghana, "two hundred Ghanaian pounds" is substituted for £100. The bone of contention as far as this provision is concerned had been with the expression "per package or unit." Two problems have often arisen with regard to this. One is the developments in packaging modes. Today, packaging is done by means of containers, pallets and other sophisticated modes of cargo movement, while the rules were fashioned at a time when packaging modes were bales, crates, drums, etc. The other is the deterioration in value of the limits set, due to rising inflation.

What is "package" for the purpose of limitation of liability is well expounded in the case of the Aleksandr Serafimovich.21 Here the plaintiffs shipped 150 cartons of sewing machine heads valued at $43.05 each from Japan for delivery at Vancouver, Canada. The cartons were strapped to three pallets each containing 50 cartons. The bills of lading included  a heading "packages" containing the words and figures "3 pallets (150 cartons)." The court in dealing with whether a pallet was a package within the meaning of article 4, r 5 reasoned that this depended on the facts and circumstances of each case and in particular on the intention of the parties as indicated by what was stated in the shipping documents; things said by the parties and the course of dealings between them. The court went further to rationalize that in determining whether a pallet was a package or not, one ought to look at the description of the goods on the bill of lading, the numbering of the cartons and their visibility from outside the pallets. It came to a conclusion that having regard to the above, the governing factor in the minds of the parties was 150 sewing machine heads each packed in a separate protective carton, rather than the pallet in which 50 of them were stacked. The defendants were thus not entitled to limit their liability in that respect. The packages in this context were 150 packages as expressed on the pallets.

The need for a more simplified and concise rule as to what could conveniently be referred to as a package is borne out by the case of Royden [p.213] Machinery Co Ltd v Owner of The "Anders Maersk."22 This serves to illustrate how the court can pay heed to the contractual terms, taking it to be the intention of the parties and can limit the liability of the shipowner under the rules however unfair it may seem. In that case, two large boilers belonging to the Royden Machinery Co were shipped on board the vessel Anders Maersk from Baltimore, USA to Shanghai, China. While the vessel was on its voyage, it encountered heavy weather resulting in the loss at sea of one of the boilers while the other fell on the deck and suffered heavy damage. In a claim by the Royden Machinery Co for the loss it had suffered, the shipowners sought to limit their liability to a thousand dollars having regard to the $500 per package limitation under the United States Carriage of Goods by Sea Act fashioned along the lines of the Hague Rules. The court upheld their contention. It expressed the opinion that it was essential to have regard to the contractual relations between the parties and unless reference was made to the contract between the parties there would always be a likelihood that there would be an element of uncertainty. Here, for damage to a boiler and loss of another, the shipowner was allowed to limit his liability to $1,000, ie $500 per package. Each boiler was regarded as a package for the purposes of limitation.

Quite a good number of shippers have suffered under the limitation of liability rule provided in the Hague Rules. A strict adherence to the bill of lading as an evidence of the contract between the parties often times leads to manifest injustices since as mentioned earlier, consignees always enter into these contracts from a position of weakness and quite often do not even see the terms of the contract. In Ghana, by virtue of Act 42, a shipowner's  limitation of liability with regards to a package is "two hundred Ghanaian pounds."23 Two hundred Ghanaian pounds would be equivalent to four hundred cedis (¢400) (basing it on a conversion of pounds to cedis at the time of adoption of the cedi). There is no doubt that this provision is outdated. Using today's forex bureau rates makes it no better.

Jurisdiction, Forum and Choice of Law

Yet another weakness of the Hague Rules as well as the Visby Amendment is its non-inclusion of any provision on jurisdiction, forum and choice of law. This has led to the inclusion by carriers of shipowner-oriented jurisdiction clauses in bills of lading which has prevented shippers, especially from the developing countries, from bringing suit or seeking arbitration at destination countries where in fact almost all cargo claims arise. Shippers are unable to choose a forum because shipowners have inserted clauses regarding forums in their bills of lading. A shipowner almost invariably chooses a forum convenient to him regardless of the destination of the cargo.

[p.214]

Most of the forums chosen by shipowners adopt a choice of law that is convenient for the shipowner. In the same vein the applicable law may even be uncertain in cases where the Hague Rules have not been incorporated into national law.

When all these happen, our local courts, situated in the place where the cargo arrives damaged, have no jurisdiction and the poor shipper is left to bear the burden arising out of the misdeed of the shipowner. Furthermore, the development of maritime law in the developing countries suffers. As Archer JA (as he then was) observed in the John Holt case24 "actions involving the application of the Hague Rules are very rare and cannot be found in our available law reports." It is submitted that this is the situation even today, because shipowners have reserved jurisdiction onto themselves. It had to take a judge of Hayfron-Benjamin J's (as he then was) calibre and boldness to set aside the jurisdiction clause in the case of Fan Milk Ltd v State Shipping Corporation.25 Here, under a bill of lading which incorporated the Hague Rules, the defendant-carriers contracted to ship 400 cartons of butter oil belonging to the plaintiffs for delivery at Tema. The cargo was delivered in a damaged condition and the plaintiffs sought to claim from the defendants. It was the contention of the defendants that since the jurisdiction clause in the bill of lading reserved jurisdiction to the English courts, the clause must be respected and given effect to. Hayfron-Benjamin J (as he then was) held that since the consignees were a company registered in Ghana and carrying on business in Ghana; the shipowner was a Ghanaian corporation; the vessel that carried the goods was a Ghanaian vessel, registered in Ghana and flying the Ghana flag; the goods were to be delivered in Ghana, for the use in Ghana and Ghanaian and English law on the matter did not differ in any material particular, proceedings would not be stayed for the case to be transferred to an English court. This decision was given by the learned judge in the face of the jurisdiction clause which the parties had themselves agreed upon as contained in the bill of lading. It is quite clear from the facts of this case that the plaintiffs would have suffered manifest unjustice if proceedings were stayed and the case transferred to the English courts.

Quite apart from the great inconvenience and expense in litigating in the English courts, the claim would have been statute-barred by the time it went before the English courts and the plaintiffs would have been left with no remedy against the carriers. Because of the existence of some "timorous souls" on the Ghanaian bench today, necessary caution needs to be taken to safeguard the interest of consignees, for it is trite learning that many a judge will uphold the jurisdiction clause despite its blatant unfairness in such circumstances.

[p.215]

Deviation

The legal questions regarding deviation is yet another area of the Hague Rules that remains unclear. Under article 4, r 4 of the rules:

"Any deviation in saving or attempting to save life or property at sea or any reasonable deviation shall not be deemed to be an infringement or breach of this convention or the contract of carriage, and the carrier shall not be liable for any loss or damage resulting therefrom."

This provision has posed a number of problems because what amounts to reasonable  deviation has always been a subject of contentious litigation. Despite the fact that the interpretation of reasonableness is one which from time immemorial judges have had the duty to pronounce upon, it has not been possible to formulate a criterion of universal applicability and precision, for as Lord Macmillan put it in the case of Stag Line Ltd v  Foscolo Mango & Co Ltd:26  "...the contingencies and emergencies which arise in maritime transport are as infinite in their variety as the vagaries of the sea itself." He was quick to add that: "An undefined standard of reasonableness may sometimes be difficult to apply..." Thus the choice of words in the provision has rendered the provision open to varied interpretations often to the detriment of cargo interests. Sometimes deviations to collect additional cargo leading to delay and consequently loss to the cargo owner has been covered under reasonable deviation.

There was however unreasonable deviation in the case of Nabb Brothers v The Owners & Parties Interested in MV Ocean Prima.27 The plaintiffs contracted with the defendants under a bill of lading in which the port of loading was stated as Bangkok. The bill of lading stated that the vessel was to sail from Bangkok to Owendo, thence to Tema. The vessel however sailed from Bangkok to Singapore, called at South Africa, Dakar, Abidjan, Owendo before finally arriving at Tema. The deviation from the agreed route resulted in delay and the consignee suffered huge losses as a result of the delay. It was held by the learned judge that the plaintiffs could claim for damages arising out of the delay. As pointed out earlier, the Hague Rules cover situations of deviation. It however does not cover situations of delay. Whether delay arising out of deviation is implicity covered under the rules is debatable. In the MV Ocean Prima case, the learned judge once again based his judgment on general principles of law and public policy considerations. Though he made reference to Act 42 he made no reference to the Act in terms of the legal basis upon which the terms of the contract was established. The issue of delay with regards to the application of the [p.216] Hague Rules was not discussed and it is doubtful whether the non-inclusion of delay was pleaded as a defence to the action.

Lord Atkin in the Foscolo Mango28 case laid down what is supposed to be the test for reasonableness, but again it is neither definite nor precise. He was of the view that to be reasonable, a deviation need not only be made with the joint interests of ship and cargo or to avoid an imminent peril. According to him:

"A deviation may, and often will, be caused by fortuitous circumstances never contemplated by the original parties to the contract, and may be reasonable though it is made solely in the interests of the ship, or solely in the interests of the cargo, or, indeed, in the direct interest of neither..."

It is submitted that the Hague Rules in codifying the common law, left us in no better position and varied interpretations would always be put on the provisions regarding deviation. Lord Atkin's test gives a very wide room for manipulation and it is no wonder that judges have often gone to town on this issue.

General Application

Another prevalent problem regarding the Hague Rules is the question of interpretation and the generality of its applicability in various countries in which it is set out to apply. Thus Lord Macmillan was to observe in Stag Line Ltd v Foscolo Mango & Co Ltd29 as follows:

"As these rules must come under the consideration of foreign courts it is desirable in the interests of uniformity that their interpretation should not be rigidly controlled by domestic precedents of antecedent date, but rather that the language of the rules should be construed on broad principles of general acceptation."

And in The Morviken,30 Lord Diplock had this to say on the interpretation of the rules:

"[The rules] should be given a purposive rather than a narrow literalistic construction, particularly whenever the adoption of a literalistic construction would enable the stated purpose of the international convention, viz the unification of the domestic laws of the contracting states relating to bills of lading, to be evaded by the use of colorable devices that, not being expressly referred to in the [p.217] Rules are not specifically prohibited.31

The intentions expressed in the opinions of the two judges are indeed profound and augur well for commercial practice in sea trade. However, the practice has not been so. All over the world, there are a lot of uncertainties arising from vague and ambiguous wording in certain areas of the Hague Rules which lead to conflicting interpretations and which complicate such matters as to the allocation of responsibility for loss or damage to cargo. In the end, quite commonly, the rules have been exploited to evade liability rather than to anchor responsibility. The shipper has been at the losing end of this trial.

All said and done, age seems to deal a negative blow to the Hague Rules. While like aged wine, it should have characteristically provided  the rules with strength, it has rather projected its weaknesses over and above its strengths. The weakness in the rules lies not only in the fact that it has been overtaken by the rapid technological advancement in the fields of communications, nautical education and computers but the entire politico-economic base in the maritime field has yielded to metamorphosing forces so considerable since 1924 that their present-day applicability is even questionable. And now pro bono publico it needs revision.

III. THE HAMBURG RULES

In view of the concerns expressed above regarding the inadequacies of the Hague Rules, it will be useful to take a look at the United Nations Convention on the Carriage of Goods by Sea (1978) commonly referred to as the Hamburg Rules32 which aims at achieving what could best be described as an intricate and delicate balance in the responsibilities, liabilities, rights and immunities between cargo and carrier interests.

Salient Features of the Hamburg Rules

It would be apt to mention at the outset that the Hamburg Rules were tailored to deal specifically with the shortcomings of the Hague and Hague/Visby Rules, realised in the practical application of the commercial law on the carriage of goods by sea.

Scope and Applicability of the Rules

There is no doubt that the scope of application has been widened under the Hamburg Rules. Under article 2, dealing with scope of application, five [p.218] different situations are catered for under which the rules apply. The rules apply between two different states if:

(a) the port of loading as provided for in the contract of carriage by sea is located in a contracting state; or

(b) the port of discharge as provided for in the contract of carriage by sea is located in a contracting state; or

(c)  one of the optional ports of discharge provided for in the contract of carriage by sea is the actual port of discharge and such port is located in a contracting state; or

(d)  the bill of lading or other document evidencing the contract of carriage by sea is issued in a contracting state; or

(e)  the bill of lading or other document evidencing the contract of carriage by sea provides that the provisions of this Convention or the legislation of any state giving effect to them are to govern the contract.

It is thus clear that the scope of application of the Hamburg Rules has gone beyond the mere issuance of the bill of lading in Ghana and now covers port of loading, port of discharge, etc. The rules can thus be invoked if goods are landed in Ghana, ie as soon as Ghana becomes a contracting state, irrespective of whether it is stated in the bill of lading that the Hamburg Rules shall apply. For a contracting state therefore, claims for damage to cargo can now be brought in the state where the cargo is discharged. This indeed is a useful innovation.

Period of Responsibility

The scope of application of the Hamburg Rules has been widened and so has the period of responsibility. The period of responsibility is no longer from "tackle to tackle." Under the Hamburg  Rules, the responsibility of the carrier covers the period during which the carrier is in charge of the goods at the port of loading, during the carriage and at the port of discharge.33 The carrier is deemed to be in charge of the goods:

"(a) From the time he has taken over the goods from:

(i)  the shipper, or a person acting on his behalf; or

(ii) an authority or other third party to whom, pursuant to law or regulations applicable at the port of loading, the goods must be handed over for shipment;

(b) until the time he has delivered the goods:

(i)  by handing over the goods to the consignee; or

(ii)  in cases where the consignee does not receive the goods from the carrier, by placing them at the disposal of the consignee in accordance with the contract or with or without the usage of the particular trade, applicable at the [p.219] port of discharge; or

(iii)  by handing over the goods to an authority or other third party to whom, pursuant to law or regulations applicable at the port of discharge, the goods must be handed over."

These provisions give statutory blessing to a wider interpretation of Devlin J's decision in the Pyrene case.34 The effect of this is that, though a bill of lading will not have been issued, the rules can still be applicable once the carrier has taken charge of the goods. That is to say where the issuance of a bill of lading or other transport document is contemplated. The Hamburg Rules would thus seem to deal conveniently with the difficulties that confronted our learned judges in the John Holt case.35 If it is established that the carriers were still in charge of the goods while they were kept in the public warehouse awaiting the arrival of bills of lading, then ipso facto they were liable by no stretch of imagination.

Excepted Perils

The Hamburg Rules also seek to reduce the unending catalogue of carriers exceptions contained in the Hague Rules with a view to achieving an equitable balance between carrier and cargo interest. It needs to be mentioned that while the litany of defences open to the carrier has been greatly whittled down, the defence of fire has been retained with a slight tilt in favour of the carrier. Under article 5(4) (i) the burden of proof in the case of fire has changed from one of actual fault or privity of the carrier36 which has been tested before the courts, to the new concept of presumed fault or neglect. One however needs to mention that the new provision by its wording requires the party without the evidence to make the proof, but again, as a trade-off it is welcome. The nautical faults defence which as stated earlier was a great worry to cargo owners has been eliminated. So that now, neither the carrier nor his servants or agents could plead negligence and be exonerated.

Deck Cargo

Again, as mentioned earlier, the Hague Rules did not include in its definition of goods "deck cargo" and "live animals." Under the Hamburg Rules the carrier is entitled to carry cargo on deck only if such carriage is in accordance with an agreement with the shipper or with usage of the particular trade or is required by statutory rules and regulations. Under section (2) of article 9, where there is an agreement to carry on deck, the carrier is enjoined to insert this in the bill of lading or the necessary [p.220] document covering the contact of carriage. There is no doubt that the modern concept of  "containerization" influenced the drafters of the Hamburg Rules and this has been taken care of in article 9. However, the rule is not wholly an advantage to the cargo owner because whereas under the Hague Rules, deck carriage, if not stated in the bill of lading was a fundamental breach, under the Hamburg Rules this does not amount to a fundamental breach. The breach only makes the carrier lose his per package limitation and the claimant who has to establish this has a heavy burden of proof. He needs to show that the deck carriage was done, and that it was done "with intent to cause such loss, damage or delay or recklessly and with knowledge that such loss, damage or delay would probably result."

As a plus for cargo interests, it could be said that at least it is now certain that the unit limitation applies to each  package inside the container when carried on deck.37 "Live animals" are now included in "goods" but the carrier is protected against "special risk inherent in that kind of carriage."38

Jurisdiction, Forum and Applicable Law

The Hamburg Rules also make meaningful improvements on the Hague Rules by dealing with the problematic issues of jurisdiction, forum and applicable law. As mentioned earlier, developing countries have been at the receiving end in this regard. Now the Hamburg Rules state that the plaintiff at his option may institute an action in a court which according to the law of the state where the court is situated is competent, and within the jurisdiction of which is situated one of the following places:

(a) The principal place of business or residence of the defendant.

(b) The place where the contract was made.

(c) The port of loading or discharge.

(d) Any additional place designated for that purpose in the contract of carriage.39

The above provision is a watershed as far as innovations in the development of maritime law is concerned. It would undoubtedly give maritime law a boost in developing countries such as ours. Once the question of forum is solved, that relating to jurisdiction and the applicable law becomes clear. Article 2(1) gives us the position as to what law is applicable and now local courts based in the place where the damage or loss is discovered can apply the lex fori after assuming jurisdiction. With the Hamburg Rules therefore, the Ghanaian courts would very often assume jurisdiction in damage or loss to cargo claims. The law in this area will develop and cargo interests will generally have a better deal.

[p.221]

Notice Periods, Delay and Deviation

Under the Hamburg Rules, an action is statute-barred if judicial action has not been taken within two years. The Hague Rules limit the claimant to one year. Whilst this affords cargo interests a greater leverage in bringing actions, at the same time, it brings the law in conformity with similar requirements under other transport conventions, as for example the Multimodal Transport Convention.40 Further, under article 19(2) of the Hamburg Rules, where the loss or damage is not apparent, the consignee now has fifteen consecutive days within which to give notice to the carrier instead of the short notice period of three days under the Hague Rules.

Delay in delivery is not specifically spelt out in the Hague Rules. The Hamburg Rules account for this. Under article 5(1) the carrier is liable for delay in delivery unless he proves that he took all reasonable measures to avoid the occurrence. Again, there is here a shift in the burden of proof from privity to one of presumed fault or neglect. The exception here is where the delay was occasioned by fire. In that respect, the burden of proof shifts unto the claimant.41 Again, this is clearer than the vague position under the Hague Rules where one ought only with imaginative conjuration to stretch article 3, r 2 to cover cases of delay.

Deviation has always been a bone of contentious litigation. The Hamburg Rules whilst it attempts to deal with the situation, does not stricto sensu leave us any better. Article 5 (6) makes the carrier liable for loss, damage or delay in delivery where these are occasioned by measures to save property at sea. One can only presume that there is a greater obligation upon the carrier since in cases of saving property at sea, he needs to take reasonable measures.

Carrier and Actual Carrier

Another remarkable innovation brought in by the Hamburg Rules is the inclusion of articles 1(2), 10 and 15 (1)(c). These articles make it clear that the contracting carrier is responsible for the acts of the actual carrier. The claimant does not now need to unravel the complicated contractual relationship between the charterers and vessel owner or the contracting carrier and actual carrier before taking suit. This is indeed a welcome innovation for cargo interests.

[p.222]

Documents other than Bills of Lading

Yet another important departure from the Hague Rules is that the convention is not limited to covering bills of lading.42 This is due to the fact that having regard to the high level of modernisation there is an increasing use in certain trades of way-bills, other types of non-negotiable documents including documents processed by means of Electronic Data Interchange (EDI).

Limitation of Liability

As stated earlier in this paper, the limitation of the carrier's liability pegged at "two hundred Ghanaian pounds" under the Bills of Lading Act, 1961 (Act 42) has long become meaningless. The new monetary limits have taken into account the common denominator of the Special Drawing Rights (SDR). Under the Hamburg Rules, the limit is now fixed for loss or damage to cargo at 835 SDR's per package or fixed for loss or damage to cargo at 835 SDR's per package or shipping unit or 2.5 SDR's per kilo whichever is higher.43 The position is clearer when one notes that if the package or unit weighs less than 334 kilograms, the limit will be 835 SDR's per unit. If the package weighs more than 334 kilograms, then the limit will be 2.50 SDR times the gross weight (in kilograms) of the goods lost or damaged.44

Under article 6(2)(a), the situation is obviated where under the Hague Rules, numerous packages loaded into one container would be counted only as one package for the purposes of limitation as happened in the Royden Machinery Co case. Even of utmost importance in this innovative approach to calculating limitation of liability is the fact that the value of the amount as converted in national currency will be the same in all contracting states. Akin to this is also the fact that the new levels reflects the levels contained in other transport conventions such as the Multimodal Transport Convention.45 In cases where a consignee brings an action for delay in delivery of goods, the carrier's liability is limited to two and half times the freight payable under the contract of carriage. This is definitely in favour of carriers and while it remains repugnant to cargo interests, it needs to be seen as part of the package deal without which the convention would not have seen the light of day.

[p.223]

Uniformity and Interpretation

The views held by various judges regarding the interpretation of the Hague Rules have already been discussed. Their view that the Hague Rules have an international character has now got statutory blessing in article 3 of the Hamburg Rules and contracting parties by virtue of this provision are to have regard to it. Upon accepting the Hamburg Rules, the Ghanaian courts would be part of this nomenclature.

It is also worthy of note that in seeking uniformity, article 31 instructs contracting parties to denounce the Hague Rules since at present there are a fairly large group of Hague Rules states, Ghana included by virtue of Act 42, a somewhat smaller group of Hague-Visby states and a not insignificant group of states which are both Hague and Hague-Visby Rules states at the same time. To have the rules operating pari passu would lead to enormous conflicts; hence the need to denounce the other rules upon acceptance of the Hamburg Rules. The Hamburg Rules strived to achieve uniformity and diffuse the fears of maritime interests which saw the shift in the burden of proof from privity of knowledge to presume fault or neglect as one close to strict liability. Thus the conference after a philological discussion included a common understanding in the final Act as an aid to interpretation of the convention.

IV CONCLUSIONS

There is a broad spectrum within which the Hamburg Rules ought to be viewed. The rules need to be viewed against the background of the weakness of the Hague Rules earlier referred to. This spectrum is neatly and curiously summed up in the words of Shah when he declares:

"...the indiscriminate use of invalid clauses in bills of lading, the abuse of the jurisdiction clause, the low monetary limit of liability and the wide exceptions permitted the carrier, which cohered into a clear realisation that an inexcusably excessive percentage of prima facie legitimate cargo claims was either not pursued for lack of precision as to the scope of the rules or was prematurely compromised by virtue of the blurred margins of absolution permitted carriers."46

This therefore sums up the need for the Hamburg Rules.

To conclude, it needs to be mentioned that the Hague-Visby Rules which was supposed to be an improvement upon the Hague Rules, itself excludes certain cargo damages from its scope, eg damage to deck cargo and live animals and damage arising before loading or after discharge. The Hamburg [p.224] Rules carter for these deficiencies. Further, the liability regime has now been extended to deal explicity with delay in delivery and the cargo owner may treat the goods as lost and thus claim for total loss where the delay exceeds 60 days. Problems regarding the insertion of the so-called "Himalaya clause" have now been dealt with in the interests of shippers but fairly balanced with the interests of owners by virtue of a tactical shift in the burden of proof. The thorny issues regarding allocation of liabilities and responsibilities during transshipment have all been dealt with  by making the contracting carrier responsible for the entire voyage. The new provisions on jurisdiction as stated earlier, are indeed a watershed in the development of maritime law in developing countries. The new provisions are designed to ensure that the cargo owner may bring his action in one of the countries with which the particular carriage is connected. In effect, the choice of forum which was the exclusive preserve of the carrier leading to situations where carriers inserted forum clauses based on their domicile, has been invalidated.

As a document that strives to achieve a balance, intricate and delicate as mentioned earlier, there is the need to always add that the interests of shipowners were also catered for. Thus the quid pro quo for the increase in liability of the carrier was the retention of the system of limitation of liability contained in the Visby Rules, including a provision restricting unlimited liability to cases of wilful misconduct by the carrier himself. It needs to be mentioned that the burden of proof for cargo interests is now greater.

The whole framework of the convention, the simplified language of the text, the shift in the burden of proof in liability situations from privity of knowledge to presumed fault or neglect, together with the "common understanding" is indeed a compromise between carrier and cargo interests. It is now left to see how our courts will apply these provisions having regard to its international character and the need for uniformity in order to achieve its stated objectives.
