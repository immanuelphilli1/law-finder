MISCELLANY-AT-LAW—ENROLMENT OF NEW LAWYERS  [1993-95] VOL. XIX RGL 1—17

 

A short but colourful ceremony was held at the premises of the Ghana School of Law on Friday, 1 October 1993, at which 78 new lawyers were enrolled. As has been customary, His Lordship the Chief Justice delivered a speech in which he, inter alia, stressed the need for the new lawyers to always behave with honour and dignity and to have "high moral character." The Review presents the edited speech of the Chief Justice.

Speech by the Chief Justice

Members of the General Legal Council, My Lords, Distinguished Guests, Ladies and Gentlemen:

In 1958, when the Law School was established, there were over 120 admissions. At the end of the five-year course, only nine passed and were enrolled in this hall in 1963. You will observe that if only nine passed then the rest, like sowing seeds, either fell by the wayside and were choked by thorns or fell on rocky ground. The results stemmed from the fact that at its inception, the conditionalities for admission were made as liberal as possible to enable as many students who wished to become lawyers to be trained. The first batch was a mixture of young and old persons.

Today, the qualifications for admission are stricter and stringent—a university degree is a sine qua non. The period of training remains five years and we have just enrolled 78 new lawyers—nearly ten times the number enrolled in 1963. From 1963 to 1993 the Law School has trained successfully 1,178 lawyers in addition to 516 post-call candidates who trained abroad, making a grand total of 1,694 for the past 30 years—an average of 57 lawyers per year. This is not a bad performance and our gratitude must go to the founding fathers of the Law School who, with foresight and imagination, have made this achievement possible. This morning we have welcomed into our fraternity 78 new lawyers.

I wish to stress that by choosing to become lawyers, they have taken the right decision. Unlike novices of an Order in a monastery, they have not been requested to take any vows of poverty or chastity. The General Legal [p.2] Council has only demanded from them an undertaking to comport themselves as lawyers in strict compliance with the professional code ethics—a subject which they have been taught in the lecture room. It is now up to the new lawyers to give practicality to the code of ethics in their practice of the law. Their life-styles and private lives are not the concern of the General Legal Council. But they should remember that in the civilized world there is an ideal called "good morality." Our own Parliament has recognised this quality and in section 46(3) of the new Courts Act, 1993 (Act 459) has emphasised the need for panelists who will serve the tribunals to be persons of "high moral character and proven ability." I will not attempt to define what is meant by "high moral character" because my definition may differ from that of a philosopher or a rationalist. Perhaps it will not be out of place to suggest that “high moral character." means good character as opposed to bad character.

My advice to the new lawyers is that as they have joined an honourable profession, one would expect them to behave with honour and dignity. One would expect them to have good character. Indeed, it is one of the criteria for judging or assessing the suitability of a person to be enrolled as a lawyer. Passing the examination is one thing, whether the person's character justifies his enrolment is another. The Legal Profession Act, 1960 (Act 32) as amended clearly lays down that the person to be enrolled must be of good character. Please do not therefore take it for granted that because the council has not alerted security agencies to investigate your character, you are immune from any future disciplinary action. If you behave contrary to the precepts of the profession, the disciplinary committee of the General Legal Council will deal with your alleged misbehaviour and may suspend you from practice or disbar you. You are at the threshold of your new career and every well-wisher in this hall will pray that you will live up to expectation.

You have been enrolled in a very important historic period. A new Constitution is in force and a new Courts Act has been enacted. As lawyers, one would expect you to have the provisions of the Constitution and the Courts Act, 1993 at your finger tips. You were lectured on constitutional law while at the university in your undergraduate days. At that time, the new Constitution had not been enacted and your lecturers could not have dealt with it. It is now up to you to lecture yourselves on the new Constitution and to be fully prepared to give sound and reliable advice whenever your future clients call on you for consultation. The need for legal services will continue to grow in all sectors of Ghana's economy. Moreover the Constitution has highlighted the indispensability of the provision of legal aid for indigents. The legal aid programme can only succeed if lawyers participate in the programme. One area with which you will constantly be dealing is violations of the Constitution, especially breaches of fundamental human rights. The Constitution is only eleven months old and the intricacies of its provisions are now being unravelled through court actions. It is a healthy sign for the [p.3] future democratic process we are all yearning for.

You cannot practice as from Monday, 4 October, as you will be expected to commence your pupilage in the chambers of an experienced lawyer. I would advise you to take your pupilage, however short, very seriously, because at the end of your pupilage, your principal would be the best person to stand as a referee when you apply for a job elsewhere.

This year marks the 30th anniversary of the first enrolment of lawyers locally trained. In the early sixties, I was requested by the late Sir Arku Korsah, then chairman of the General Legal Council, and the late Sir Leslie McCarthy to prepare a memorandum on the manpower needed to provide legal services for the general public. I used my imagination and suggested that the country would require about 1,000 lawyers during the next ten years to provide legal services to the government, local government, commerce and industry and the general public. It appears that my guess at the time was an overestimate because at the end of the ten years, ie in 1973, only one-third of my estimate had been trained for various reasons.

The new judicial set-up under the Courts Act makes it highly desirable for lawyers to practice in the countryside. Each political district must have a community tribunal with jurisdiction in both civil and criminal cases in addition to the functions of a family tribunal and a juvenile court. Lawyers must appear in these community tribunals in the 110 districts where I am sure many young lawyers can earn their living without hanging around in the regional capitals waiting for clients and briefs from senior lawyers. There will be plenty of work in these community tribunals and the opportunity to establish a successful practice should not be missed. Conditions in the rural areas may not be ideal but as pioneers in legal practice in these new community tribunals, you should be prepared to make sacrifices and forgo the vain luxuries of the urban centres.

Before 1958 every student who wanted to qualify as a lawyer was compelled to go abroad, especially the United Kingdom, to train. We should all imagine the total cost that would have been involved if the 69 students locally qualified today had been trained abroad. We should all be grateful to past governments and the present government for providing the necessary subvention and facilities for the training of lawyers locally. The Ghana School of Law is akin to what the Akosombo dam is to the people of Ghana.

 

I wish to congratulate all of you on behalf of the General Legal Council, the Board of Legal Education, the lecturers and staff of the school for bringing honour to yourselves, your relatives and friends some of whom are gathered here to witness the ceremony of enrolment.

May the almighty God guard and guide you to uphold the honour and dignity of the legal profession in Ghana and throughout the world of jurisprudence.

[p.4]

GHANA BAR ASSOCIATION ANNUAL CONFERENCE, 1994

The Ghana Bar Association held its annual conference for 1994 at Koforidua from 25-29 September. The conference, inter alia, discussed a number of key issues affecting the legal profession, the nation and the international community. The conference was also addressed by a number of legal luminaries and important speeches were made. The Review is privileged to reproduce here for the benefit of readers the speech by the President of the Bar as well as some important conference resolutions.

Speech by the President of the Ghana Bar Association

My Lord Spiritual, Mr Chief Justice of Ghana, Honourable Acting Attorney-General, Honourable Regional Minister, Honourable Ministers of State, Justices of the Superior Courts, Members of the Diplomatic Corps, Nana President and Members of the Eastern Regional House of Chiefs, Traditional Rulers, Judges and Magistrates, Fellow Members of the Bar, Distinguished Guests, Ladies and Gentlemen:

It is with great pleasure and pride that I welcome all of you, on behalf of the General Council of the Ghana Bar Association and on my own behalf, to our 1993-94 annual conference. We are indeed touched and appreciative of the fact that all of you could make the time and sacrifice despite your individual commitments and the constraints on your time. We are grateful to all of you and happy in the knowledge that you support our association.

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, please permit me however to extend a special welcome to His Lordship the Chief Justice, the Honourable Ag Attorney-General and our brother colleagues of the Togo Bar.

This will be the last time that His lordship the Honourable Philip Edward Nii Kwei Archer, the incumbent Chief Justice, addresses an annual conference of the association in that capacity as under the provisions of the Fourth Republican Constitution he has to retire in February 1994. My Lord, permit me to use your own words that you would in that case be "a retiree, a returnee and a retiree." It is true that our association has not always seen eye-to-eye with His Lordship the Chief Justice. We have differed, like human beings, on a number of issues, priorities and approaches. The now celebrated case of Kuenyehia v Archer, Supreme Court, 25 May 1993, unreported testifies to the extent to which we have carried our disagreements. But what is, however, true is that in accordance with the best tradition of our profession both the Chief Justice and the association have not allowed our disagreements to blind or colour our judgment. Both of us have sought mutual co-operation. The doors of His Lordship the Chief Justice have remained open to the association, particularly its officers, and without any insistence on protocol. He has heard quite a number of complaints, suggestions and recommendations from the association, even if we have not been able to carry him with us on each occasion. He has also been very [p.5] supportive of the programmes, particularly some new initiatives of the association and has insisted that the association should set itself and maintain high standards in order to enhance the dignity, image and income of its members. Hearty welcome!

The Honourable Ag Attorney-General, Dr Obed Yao Asamoah, is in farmiliar surroundings. Admittedly, this is the first time over a decade that he has joined us at our annual conference. But he is no stranger to the association. Having taught some of us, engaged in strenuous private practice, previously served as the president of the Volta Region branch of the association and a member of our General Council, the association welcomes him and hopes that his intimate knowledge of the association and the legal profession would be an asset for him to understand and rectify the numerous problems, difficulties, deprivations and shortcomings of the profession and that that intimate knowledge would not become a tool for its destruction. I may presently address some other concerns of the profession to the Honourable Ag Attorney-General. For the moment, let me recall the remarks of a judge who welcomed a former Provisional National Defence Council (PNDC) Secretary back to court with the words that "if all fail you, the Bar will not." This underscores the fact that we all have a common heritage in the law and have to protect and defend it. You are welcome!

We are particularly happy that our colleagues from the Togo Bar Association are able to join us today. It is a refreshing knowledge that co-operation between members of the legal profession in different countries in the West African sub-region, and indeed throughout Africa, can start from the grassroots and not necessarily from the top. Recently, both the print and electronic media carried the news of the courtesy call by Mr Yaovi Agboyibor, a lawyer and the leader of the parliamentary opposition in Togo, on President Rawlings in which he was described as the Secretary-General of the Association of West Africa Bar Associations. Regrettably, the Ghana Bar Association does not know about the existence of that association, nor about the visit of Mr Agboyibor. Neither did Mr Agboyibor find the time or think it desirable to call on and hold discussions with our association. It is said in Ewe that Ne klo yi nugbe la, abobo gboe wodze na meaning that “when the tortoise travels, he lodges with the snail." Obviously, both of them are shell animals. It must, however, be said that this is not the first such contact between our two National Bar Associations. In 1989 the Togo Bar sent a delegation to Ghana to express its sympathies and support to our association during those very difficult and traumatic times. It also invited and received a Ghanaian team at its opening of the legal year in the same year. It was the hope of those of us from the sub-region who were present at the time that an association embracing all the Bar Associations in the sub-region will come about before long. However, the political developments and instability in our various countries, not, to talk of mutual distrust and suspicion by our political leaders, did not allow that objective to be realised.

 

[p.6]

This explains our immense joy that our colleagues are with us this morning. Bon arrivé!

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, today is a historic occasion for both Koforidua, indeed the whole of the Eastern Region, and the association itself. This is not because this region has now produced its first National Vice-President for our association. It is historic because it is the very first time that Koforidua is hosting an annual conference of the association. Koforidua, an ancient and historic town with a very warm heart for visitors and which is reputed to be full of "flowers", is grappling with the challenges of modern development. It also seeks to blend its culture and tradition with modern administration in its unique roles as the capital of the New Juaben Traditional Area and the capital of the post-independent Eastern Region of Ghana. That it faces new challenges cannot be denied. So also does our country Ghana, with its chequered history. One of such challenges is how to nurture and sustain the survival of the Fourth Republic and constitutional and democratic rule in our country. The theme for this conference. "Democracy and the Fourth Republic: Review and Prospects", is therefore appropriate as the second year of the first constitutional and civilian administration of  the Forth Republic draws to a close and the nation begins to review its achievements and failures so far, while it projects its future prospects.  To that theme, I now address myself.

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, on 6 March 1957 our dear nation attained its independence from Britain after over a century of colonialism and subjugation by various colonial powers. It adopted for itself the motto "Freedom and Justice", no doubt being very much mindful of its history and the goals it had set for itself. The First Republic saw its independent Constitution, which had provided for a multi-party democracy, going through a number of changes to a one party state at the time that the First Republic was forcefully overthrown by the military junta of the National Liberation Council (NLC). 1969 saw the birth of the Second Republic; and the Second Republican Constitution reflected the concerns and lessons learnt from the failures, successes and excesses of the First Republic. But the Second Republic was not destined to be the last Republic as it was overthrown barely two years later by the National Redemption Council (NRC) in what has become known as the "Amenity coup" because its leaders thought, just as the present drama in the Gambia, that the Armed Forces had been denied its just portion of the national cake. The attempt by the Supreme Military Council (SMC), its successor, to foist an untested political system of governance which it dubbed "Union Government" provoked strong resistance of the nation. It was soon overthrown by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (AFRC), made up of junior officers and uncommissioned officers of the Ghana Armed Forces. Their imagination was fired by the claim that there was deprivation and the ordinary man could not make ends meet while the military junta lived in unimaginable opulence and luxury and would leave [p.7] the national stage without rendering any account of its stewardship.

The experiences of the NRC and SMC military juntas, as well as our previous ones, were reflected in the Third Republican Constitution. However, it lasted only a little over two years. It was supplanted by the Provisional National Defence Council (PNDC) which in decade and more launched a "revolution" and set up new political and legal bodies and systems in the belief that the old institutions were not responsive enough to the aspirations of our nation and our people. However, the Fourth Republican Constitution had had to find refuge in those very bodies and systems as it drew much inspiration from the Constitutions, 1969 and 1979. Our Constitution, 1992 decreed a multi-party, civilian and constitutional government and that our nation shall be governed by law. It brought into being the Fourth Republic which was inaugurated on 7 January 1993—now one year, eight months and nineteen days old. That is the road that we have traversed.

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, I have sought to have a cursory look at our history, even if you know it already, in order to address the issues as to where we have come from, where are we going, what are the institutions that are necessary for the very survival of our country, wat are the aspirations of the ordinary Ghanaian, what are the conditions and factors that can lead to the failure and demise of the Fourth Republic—May God forbid—and what role is expected of all of us.

My natural starting point therefore is the legal profession—both the bench and the Bar. The years of PNDC had been a very harrowing and nightmarish experience for the legal profession, originating with the abduction and murder of the three High Court judges. Judges, who normally judge others before pronouncing judgment, were dismissed without a hearing. Never mind that even God asked Adam: "Why have you eaten of the forbidden fruit?" The period also saw the resignation and exodus of some of the gems on the bench, morale was low and people wondered about its independence. Mention must also be made of the setting up of a totally separate adjudicating system called the "Public Tribunals" whose waters never crossed that of His Lordship the Chief Justice. But as events then and even now demonstrate, such as the trial and conviction of its chairmen, claims by its disgraced chairmen that they were subjected to dictation by other persons and bodies and a scientific analysis of its total output and performance compared with its available logistics and facilities, and the 40 per cent salary inducement allowance exclusively for its officials, that was and could not be an answer to the shortcomings and failures of the system. The judiciary, like any human institution, has its weaknesses and strengths, its failures and successes. The judiciary has so given a ray of hope that it intends to jealously guard and play its constitutional role. The constitutional role and importance of the judiciary in the survival of the Fourth Republic cannot be overemphasized. It is the judiciary that is [p.8] expected to be the independent arbiter in the resolution of disputes between individuals and also between the individual and the State. It must keep even the scales of justice. The judiciary has, to my mind, done creditably considering its many constraints. It has stood head and shoulders above some of the institutions set up under the Constitution.

But, Mr Chief Justice, there is so much that the judiciary can do if it is not to be found wanting.  It ranges from the trivial to matters of substance. The judiciary needs to become a modern institution in terms of technology. It is a crying shame that until a couple of months ago, the judiciary did not own one single computer but had to rely on its World War II manual typewriters. It has to confront such issues as the delay in the administration of justice, improvement of its delivery system, inefficient registries and disinterested personnel; the attitude, behaviour and compartment of some judges and magistrates; transparent honesty, integrity and independence. The judiciary needs to constantly throw the searchlight on itself. I submit that that is the only way it can properly address its shortcomings and the concerns of everybody, create an image for itself and put its integrity beyond question and doubt. It is only then that it can well and truly play its crucial constitutional role.

We of the Bar are the first to admit that all has not been well with us. We yearn for those golden years of decorum excellence and courtesy. There is room for improvement in our performance. We have, therefore, begun an inward looking exercise in order to address some of these shortcomings. The lawyer of today is equally industrious, well-meaning and motivated by the common good of his client and society, just as the lawyer of yesteryears.

The association at its 1992 mid-year conference declared its acceptance and avowed commitment to the Constitution, 1992 and its enforcement. The association has now sought to translate this into reality by the strengthening and expansion of its outreach programme. Already, its legal literacy committee has been incorporated as the Ghana Legal Literacy and Resource Foundation, a company limited by guarantee, under the distinguished chairmanship of Mr Kingsley-Nyinah, a retired Justice of the Court of Appeal and former Electoral Commissioner, drawing on the rich and varied experience of persons both within and outside the legal profession, and tasked with opening the frontiers of legal knowledge to the ordinary persons about basic laws that affect them through its programmes. The association's nine-man legal team, under the untiring chairmanship of Mr Peter Ala Adjetey, continues to study in depth the bills that are laid before Parliament and make recommendations to Parliament in the firm belief that prevention is better than cure. In a further attempt to extend and expand legal knowledge the association has now put a scheme in place under which it will publish on monthly basis the written judgments of the Supreme Court and the Court of Appeal. One does not therefore have to wait indefinitely to know what were the reasons of the Supreme Court in any particular constitutional case. We [p.9] have been very fortunate to have the active support of His Lordship the Chief Justice who has given his blessing to the project. We are also the fortunate beneficiaries of the United States Agency for International Development (USAID) which has provided us with a grant for its implementation.

The association has also initiated action to have one of our courts modernised and provided with the latest technology to speed up the trial of cases. It is envisaged that this model court would be computerised and would have a sound recording system, amongst others. His Lordship the Chief Justice who has enthusiastically welcomed this development, has designated Court 4, High Court, Accra for this purpose. The USAID is our benefactor for this project also, although we are beginning to realise that the project involves more funds than we had originally budgeted for. Our benefactors may well have to help us with the extra mile. For now, we can only express our thanks and appreciation to His Lordship the Chief Justice and the USAID for their support for the protects designed to advance the rule of law and good governance.

The talk of good governance naturally leads me to the executive, the first arm of government. It has been a very slow, difficult, and sometimes traumatic experience for the executive, made up barely of former PNDC appointees, to undergo the metamorphosis from an absolute military cum civilian dictatorship to one of a constitutional and multi-party democracy. In some instances, it could be compared with a difficult labour period while at times it looks like a reluctant child who has no other choice but to comply with the firm wishes of his no-nonsense and vigilant mother. Nevertheless, some progress has been made and the difference between our pre-Fourth Republican constitutional era and now can be likened to the difference between chalk and cheese.

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, there have been times when the very omissions of the executive portray an attempt to undermine the Constitution. It is known for example that it is almost three months since the Kumasi Metropolitan Assembly rejected the candidature of Nana Akwasi Agyeman as its chief executive. But, as at today, there has been no further nomination and the rejected candidate continues in that office. In those circumstances it is easy for others to interpret it as an attempt to subvert the Constitution.

Such omissions have also been exhibited when it comes to the promulgation of statutes enjoined by the Constitution.

Article 22 which deals with property rights of spouses provides that:

(1) A spouse shall not be deprived of a reasonable provision out of the estate of a spouse whether or not the spouse died having made a will.

(2) Parliament shall, as soon as practicable after the coming into force of this Constitution, enact legislation regulating the property rights of spouses."

[p.10]

(The emphasis is mine.) There has been no such legislation as I speak. Surely, it cannot be denied that "as soon as practicable after the coming into force of this Constitution", cannot by any stretch of imagination be taken to mean two years and more after the coming into force of the Constitution. If we are to go by the pronouncements and decisions of our Supreme Court over the years this cannot mean more than six months after coming into effect of the Constitution. Similarly, "the salaries and allowances payable, and the facilities and the privileges" for named public officials in article 71(1), including the Speaker, the Chief Justice and members of Parliament are yet to be determined almost after two years of coming into force of the Constitution. There have also been instances where adequate resources have not been provided for constitutional bodies like the judiciary. One only has to compare the financial appropriation made to the judiciary as against that of the National Commission on Culture to appreciate the point. In some instances there appears to be a blissful ignorance of the provisions of the Constitution. This explains why motorists and the travelling public are routinely stopped by the police both during the day and night, and more particularly as you approach or drive through Accra, and one's vehicle and luggage are searched by the police who do not even know what they are looking for. It has become part of the political and security culture of the land and the police are not yet aware of article 21 (g) which decrees "freedom of movement which means the right to move freely in Ghana."

If, however, there is one serious area where the actions of the executive have been called into question, it is in respect of its approach, development and maintenance of the spirit of the Constitution and constitutional conventions as against the law standing as a solitary statute. It is an  elementary knowledge that it is the spirit of a Constitution that underlines any Constitution. It embodies, amongst others, the aspirations and hopes of the people. It is that which unites a nation and its people. It leads eventually to the development of conventions that aim at ensuring the successful working of the Constitution and becomes a mechanism for the dynamic growth of the Constitution as a living organism. It is a concept that is to be encouraged in any given democracy.

The Constitution, 1992, based on our experiences, has a spirit. In my view, that spirit includes the acknowledgment that we are all Ghanaians, that we seek to build a just and equitable society, that we have chosen for ourselves a constitutional democracy that recognizes that each of us has a part to play in its sustenance, that power must be exercised in a fair, unbiased manner and without any discrimination whatsoever.

The spirit of the Constitution comes into sharp focus when the executive is to exercise some of the powers granted to it by the Constitution. Admittedly, the executive has been granted enormous power and authority. It has no limitation, on some of the appointments it can make. However, let us pause and ask whether any harm is done the executive if it confers or [p.11] consults other interested groups or parties before making appointments to some very sensitive national offices. To my naive mind, no such harm is done. On the contrary this approach would lead to the acceptance of such national institutions as indeed national in their Constitution and structure.

A lot of disquiet has been expressed in many quarters about the appointment of members of the National Electoral Commission and the National Commission for Civic Education. To some, it is a question of "jobs for the boys" and putting in place structures that will ensure the continued hold on power by the present executive. It may well be true or false. But the effect of such suspicion and mistrust is that it undermines the authority and integrity of that body and its acceptability. It seems to me that we can avoid some of these unfortunate incidents that undermine the survival of the Fourth Republic.

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, permit me to share with you a personal experience from the Republic of Malawi, where I was privileged to be a member of the Joint International Observers Group at its last election. When President Kamuzu Banda accepted the results of the referendum that voted for multi-party democracy, that country invited each of the registered political parties to nominate a person, not necessarily a member of the party, to serve on its Electoral Commission with the Banda Government nominating a High Court judge as the chairperson. This approach has its dangers. But its strength, as was demonstrated by the results of the elections, showed that each party had expected to win the elections so the rules and regulations were made with no particular favourite or condemned person in mind. The rules of the game were mutually drawn and known by all. In the result, there were no serious complaints from any of the losing parties and all concerned had no difficulty in declaring it "free and fair." We therefore urge the government to translate the oneness of Ghana into reality in its policies, programmes and pronouncements. This will naturally lead to drawing the line between the government of the day and the ruling party. In that way ministers of State will not have to don party garments at a public function to commission a water project for a community made up of all and sundry. That way celebration of national holidays and events would become national in character and reality.

I submit that no single Ghanaian is more patriotic than the other because he is in government or opposition. Equally, one is not a repository of knowledge because he is in government or opposition. Similarly, one is not a paragon of virtues because one is either in government or opposition. What these truths should impress on all and sundry, particularly those in the executive, is to treat and regard every individual Ghanaian as such, promote and develop reconciliation and national consensus, and to let the spirit of the Constitution be prevalent in everything that the government and its subjects do. That is the way for the survival of our democratic experiment.

Now, to the First Parliament of the Fourth Republic. In a sense, it is a [p.12] new beginning after eleven years of non-parliamentary culture and the absence of the veterans leaving the field to the new comers—the ninos. The absence of a parliamentary opposition has also not helped matters. Of course, it has to be a tough learning process. But with a term of four years, it cannot continue indefinitely to be the student. It has to begin to show the much desired result. It often times looks as if the long years of no Parliament has affected the decorum, oratory, sharpness, brilliance, productivity and evergreen presence and integrity of our present Parliament. The different but complementary roles of the executive and Parliament seem to some as blurred. In their view, Parliament is a mere rubber stamp of the executive and separation of powers cannot be immediately seen.

This state of affairs is not helped by recent media publications which tend to suggest that some members of Parliament believe that they are not independent of the executive and cannot make any meaningful contribution in that house without stirring the displeasure of some powerful persons and individuals. It can be said without any hesitation that on balance Parliament is the weakest arm of government which is yet to get its act together. It is imperative that both the executive and Parliament act quickly to preserve the integrity of Parliament, otherwise it spells the doom of our parliamentary democracy.

In the Weekly Spectator (No 1374) of Saturday, 24 September 1994, barely two days ago—the Honourable Daniel Francis Annan (I am reminded of the fact that one drops the title "Justice" when one leaves the bench because one is no more administering justice), the Speaker of Parliament, delivered himself in answer to a question as to the salary, conditions of service and facilities of members of Parliament as follows:

"It is a matter which affects everybody, including the Speaker. People work best when they know their terms and conditions of service. We've been at work for nearly two years and we still don't know from the Speaker right down, our terms and conditions of service. It applies to the President, Ministers and everybody. I think we've taken too much time in resolving this matter.  It is not conducive to efficiency. I think that whatever the problems are, we should face them, and come out as quickly as possible with service conditions. It is not right."

This state of affairs undermines the ability and integrity of Parliament. It is a recipe for lack of commitment, enthusiasm, temptations and leads to the breakdown of the system. It needs to be addressed with urgent despatch.

Parliament, presently made of the three parties in alliance, also ought to reassert itself and reassure us of its role.

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, I cannot end my address without a look at the press and some of the new constitutional bodies like the Council of State, the National Electoral Commission and the National Commission [p.13] for Civic Education.

A Council of State first made its appearance in our national politics in the Second Republican Constitution, 1969. It was designed to be an advisory body of elders and to hold in cheek the excesses of the President. This still remains by and large its role. In the Third Republic it even had members in opposition to the then government of the day in its fold. It is felt that this is not the case today. The role that the Council of State continues to play has been called into question and as to whether it is in fact playing the independent, non-partisan and senior citizen role expected of it. Events such as the chairman of the Council of State welcoming back to the country His Excellency the President, instead of the Vice-President who was conspicuously absent both at the departure and arrival of the President on his very recent eastern and southern African tour, raise the concerns of the ordinary citizen. Further, do members of the Council of State not compromise their positions when they regularly and continually are seen as messengers of His Excellency the President in delivering messages on his behalf or appearing at the congress of the ruling party without attending those of the opposition? These are not matters that can be easily dismissed with a wave of the hand. The council needs to address such concerns and also let us know whether their tenure is on full-time or part-time basis and whether they are beneficiaries of any special benefits. This is the only way to win and sustain the respect of all Ghanaians. Let it be patently evident that they do counsel His Excellency, the President.

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, the present National Electoral Commission was born in controversy. The appointment of its members met with opposition from a number of individuals, bodies and political parties. It was thought and felt that having regard to the past performance of some of its members, the commission was incapable of being independent. It is a credibility crisis. It is agreed by all that if the present experiment is to succeed then there is the need for fair, free, clean, transparent, honest electoral body, process and elections. This is the task that faces the commission. It is comforting to know that the commission itself appreciates the suspicion of the ordinary person and the enormity of its task. Its on-going consultations with all political parties and donor countries about electoral reform is in the right direction, commendable and needs the encouragement of all. It is the best safety valve. However, the commission would have to firmly and decisively grapple with such important issues as a clean and reliable voters' register, a well-thought out voter identification system, an electoral process that ensures fair and accurate election results while eliminating impersonation, intimidation, fraud and, rigging. We would also like to warmly congratulate all the political parties for being able to work together in this search and to call upon them to extend their co-operation to other spheres of national endeavour. We reserve special words of thanks and appreciation to the donor community and urge them on. We [p.14] would like to believe that electoral assistance to the National Electoral Commission does not mean that that commission is not independent or that this country has sold its sovereignty. If this claim is true, then by parity of reasoning, we have sold our sovereignty to the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund.

Ultimately, however, it lies on the commission itself to appreciate its constitutional and crucial role. It needs to give flesh to its independence and constitutional position. The commission needs to be reminded that each person or body charged with social responsibility leaves its marks on history. So did Pontius Pilate. And the National Electoral Commission would also have to leave its mark. Let it not be that it behaved like Pontius Pilate.

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, it can be said that the press, including the state-owned, has begun to chart a new path for itself. It has made some modest gains but it still has to confront such issues like mental emancipation, adequate resource and facilities, the warm co-operation of all and the eradication of some of the negative attitudes and practices of a small minority within its ranks. It deserves the support of all of us as the fourth estate of the realm.

The Commission for Human Rights and Administrative Justice as well as the National Media Commission are comparatively new institutions that are still mapping out their various courses. In the coming years, they must demonstrate their independence and understanding of their respective roles. It is our hope that they would become bulwarks of constitutional democracy in our country. The National Commission for Civic Education does not seem to have made any or much impact. The commission ought to bear in mind its constitutional importance and expected independence at all times.

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, there have been disquieting statements about some of these newly established constitutional bodies being packed with the so-called "revolutionary cadres." We hope that this is not true. We submit that the politicisation of these bodies will defeat their purpose.

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, our Constitution has very elaborate provisions on directive principles of state policy, economic rights, cultural rights and practices, children's rights, among others. The early initiation of programmes by the executive that will bring about the full realisation of these rights is imperative. As has already been demonstrated, our earlier attempts at constitutional rule failed because of a number of factors. It is therefore incumbent on all institutions of State, particularly the executive, and all of us to ensure that we do not give rise or occasion for the resurgence of these conditions. The economic rights of the ordinary man is under siege. The six-days-a-week government sponsored and endorsed lotteries and games of chance, the various district lotteries and the numerous raffles being organised ad nausem show the desperation of the ordinary person in the pursuit of economic self-sufficiency, Poverty, misery and deprivation can [p.15] also spell doom for a constitutional order.

Distinguished ladies and gentlemen, the role of the lawyer in a constitutional order cannot be over-emphasised. A heavy responsibility lies on the profession and more particularly the Honourable Attorney-General, the constitutional legal adviser to government. The government needs to be constantly guided; a lot of legislation would have to be enacted to achieve the objects of the Constitution and to promote social and economic reforms while law reform is intensified. It is only then that law becomes a dynamic tool for social engineering.

Finally, it is only when power is exercised for the total good and well-being of the citizenry that we can say, with our chests drawn out, in the words of article 1 (1) of the Constitution, 1992 that:

"The Sovereignty of Ghana resides in the people of Ghana in whose name and for whose welfare the powers of government are to be exercised in the manner and within the limits laid down in this Constitution."

It is only then we can make our nation "great and strong" and give flesh and meaning to our national motto of "Freedom and Justice."

Long live the Ghana Bar Association.

Long live the Republic of Ghana.

Thank you.

Conference Resolutions

(1) On matters affecting the legal profession it was, inter alia, resolved as follows:

"(i) That the General Council of the Bar should appoint immediately a committee to study and bring up proposals for all legal practitioners to be involved in the 'pro bono' scheme (ie, legal aid). It shall be obligatory for every legal practitioner to devote a certain number of working hours each year in participating in the scheme either in court or in chambers as a service to the community.

(ii) That having regard to the difficulties encountered by claimants (ie, persons injured in motor accidents) in locating the owners of the vehicles involved in the said accidents, the insurance houses should insist that every applicant for insurance provides full details of his residential address in addition to any other information that may be required by the insurance houses.

(iii) The conference, having reviewed the situation about the lack of disciplinary authority in the General Council of the Bar to discipline its members; appreciating that other professional [p.15] bodies in Ghana do have disciplinary authority to discipline their members, calls upon the General Legal Council to review the Legal Profession Act, 1960 (Act 32) so as to empower the General Council of the Bar to take responsibility for the enrolment and discipline of the members of the legal profession.

(iv) The GBA calls upon all members not to accept indentures, letters of administration and other documents prepared by non-lawyers: and further calls upon the regional executives to monitor the activities of such members whose actions bring the profession into disrepute."

(2) The resolutions on matters affecting the nation, inter alia, noted:

"(i) The Ghana Bar Association, cognisant of the fact that the Council of State created under the Constitution is meant to be the highest advisory body with a constitutional responsibility to counsel the President in the discharge of his duties devoid of partisan politics; noting that some members of the Council had identified themselves openly with the National Democratic Congress (NDC) by appearing on public platforms wearing party regalia and/or speaking for and on behalf of the President, acts and conduct which clearly derogate from their constitutional role, calls upon the members of the Council of State to insulate themselves from the ruling party so as to give confidence to the public of their non-partisanship.

(ii) The GBA notes with grave concern the disastrous and uninspiring results recorded in the Senior Secondary School Examinations: Realising that the said poor performance arose as a result of the lack of proper inputs and planning in the educational process, calls upon the government to review the whole educational programme as a matter of urgency so as not to jeopardise the educational opportunities of future generations.

(iii) The GBA calls upon the Inspector-General of Police to respect the provisions of the Constitution, which guarantee the freedom of movement of every person in Ghana, by instructing his officers to remove with immediate effect all barriers mounted on the highways and roads in the country which are being used as a means of extortion and/or harassment of motorists and passengers.

(iv) The GBA congratulates the private press on their courage in informing the public on important national issues but cautions them to avoid sensationalism, abusive and offensive language and misrepresentation of facts and to resort to investigative journalism.

[p.17]

(v) The GBA believing that there is the need for public office holders to exhibit high moral standard and proven integrity; noting with dismay recent allegations concerning some high officials of State which allegations have not been denied by the officials involved thereby bringing into disrepute the offices they hold; and also casting a slur on their ability to perform their duties effectively, calls upon the said office holders in the interest of probity and accountability to resign forthwith failing which we further call upon the President of the Republic to initiate steps to remove them without delay."

(3) Finally, the resolutions on matters affecting the international community, inter alia, also noted:

"(i) The GBA, believing that there is a need for political stability and tranquility in the world to enable the people of the world to go about their economic activities unhindered; noting the various violent conflicts in countries such as Angola, Somalia, Liberia, Rwanda, Bosnia-Hezergovenia calls upon all the leaders of political thought in the various countries to seek peace so as to enable the citizenry to live their normal lives unhindered.

(ii) The GBA mindful of the need for the citizens of every country to exercise their right of choosing those who are to govern them and noticing that there are still countries on the continent of Africa where democratic institutions are non-existent, calls upon the leaders in those countries such as Nigeria, Gambia, Sierra Leone and Zaire to institute as soon as practicable the process of returning the countries to constitutional rule."
