STATUTORY REGULATION OF EXCLUSION CLAUSES IN CONSUMER CONTRACTS IN GHANA

 

Contracts of Sale

The Sale of Goods Act, 1962 (Act 137) creates a number of duties which are statutorily imposed on a seller under every contract of sale and in specific cases prohibits or restricts the use of exclusion clauses to avoid those implied terms. On the issue of the quality of goods section 13 of Act 137 imposes on every seller of goods the duty to ensure that the goods are free from any defects unknown to the buyer; and further to ensure that the goods are reasonably fit for the purpose for which they are required.42 To prevent sellers from avoiding the liability imposed by section 13 of the Act, the Act limits the power of the seller to exclude these implied terms  by any exclusion or exemption clause, even though the limitation is not absolute.43 A trader or dealer in goods of a particular description cannot effectively exclude liability for the poor quality of goods supplied unless "he proves that before the contract was made the exclusion clause was brought to the notice of the buyer and its effect made clear to him."44  This provision appears to undermine the protection afforded to the consumer with respect to the quality of the goods since it allows the seller to avoid liability as long as he points out the exclusion clause to the customer.

It must also be noted that the limitation on the use of exclusion clauses to exclude liability for poor quality of goods does not apply to purely private sales, where the seller does not sell the particular type of goods in the ordinary course of his business. However, with the liberal interpretation traditionally given to the phrase "in the ordinary course of business", it is only in very limited situations that a seller would be able to unilaterally exclude his liability for the poor quality of goods supplied.Even though there is no Ghanaian case which has directly interpreted this phrase, a number of English cases which have construed an identical provision in the English Sale of Goods Act have given it a very wide scope.45

It has been held that as long as the seller sells goods of the kind in question, the duty will apply even if he manufactures or sells the commodity in a particular form or for a particular purpose for the very first time. In  Spencer Trading Company Ltd v Devon,46 a manufacturer of glues, gum [p.115] size, dextrine and other adhesives in liquid and powder form was asked to supply to the buyers glue suitable for putting on fly paper. The sellers, who manufactured the substance for the very first time for this purpose, delivered glue which turned out to be unsuitable for the intended purpose. It was held nevertheless that the duty arose since the product was of the description which the sellers sold in the ordinary course of their business, notwithstanding that they manufactured it for the very first time for this particular purpose. In Ashington Piggeries Ltd v Christopher Hill Ltd47 a manufacturers of animal feed compounds were asked to mix and supply a compound to be used as mink food according to specifications agreed between them and the buyers. When the sellers delivered a compound which was unfit for the intended purpose, they were held to be in beach even though they had never supplied this particular compound before.

This liberal approach, if adopted by the Ghanaian courts, would effectively expand the scope of the seller's liability and ensure that wider protection is given to the purchaser of goods who relies on the seller's expertise.It must be noted that since section 13 of Act 137 does not require that the purchaser must have exhibited any form of reliance on the trader's expertise, the protection in section 13(1)(b) could be invoked even if the purchaser, by reason of his occupation, skill or expertise, is in fact more knowledgeable of the nature or efficacy of the goods supplied than the seller.

The core question, however, is precisely what must be done by the seller in order to ensure that the exclusion clause is "brought to the notice of the buyer and its effect made clear to him?" Did the drafters intend that the general rules as to the giving of "reasonable notice" apply, or is the seller required to specifically or expressly inform the buyer of the existence of the clause (ie, read it out to him) and explain to the buyer in a layman's language how the clause affects his rights under the contract? The latter interpretation is more likely and if applied would require in our circumstances that the exemption clause be explained to an illiterate consumer or buyer in a language of his or her understanding. In the case of literate purchasers, the clause, presumably, must not only be clearly printed on the contractual document but must be pointed out to the purchaser and its effect made clear to him.

Perhaps some guidance on the issue could be drawn from the views adopted in the High Court decision in Ashitey v Ghana Industrial Holding Corporation.48 The plaintiff, an illiterate person, took his boat to the defendant's workshop for repairs, subject to the usual terms and conditions. The defendants removed the propeller and blade and kept it in their store and gave the propeller shaft to the plaintiff to have it repaired elsewhere.

[p.116]

The plaintiff returned with the repaired propeller shaft only to be informed that the propeller blade was nowhere to be found and therefore the shaft could not be fitted on the plaintiffs boat. It took the plaintiff 44 days to obtain another blade to replace the lost one. The plaintiff thereafter brought an action claiming damages for loss of use of the vessel for the 44 days, due to the negligence of the defendants. The defendants relied on an exclusion clause written in English on their notice board to avoid liability for the loss of the plaintiff's propeller blade. The court found that the exemption clause was not effective because there was no evidence that the notice, which was written in English, was explained to the plaintiff, an illiterate person, or that he fully understood the nature and consequences of the notice.

It is worth pointing out that the position taken in this case goes further than that adopted in some of the earlier English cases where the fact of a customer's illiteracy was considered immaterial and the requirement of "reasonable notice" was hardly ever defined to require that the consumer must have actually understood the nature and consequences of the exclusion clause.49 It must be pointed out that in a largely illiterate society such as ours, and in view of the extensive protection granted under the Illiterates Protection Ordinance (Cap 262) (1951 Rev) as amended by the Illiterates Protection Ordinance (Amendment) Act, 1963 (Act 217), the restrictive position adopted by the English courts cannot be followed here. The broad effect of section 13 of Act 137 applied in this context is thus to clearly discourage the rampant use of contractual clauses to exclude liability for poor quality goods by insisting on explicit explanations of such clauses to customers.

Other statutory bans on the use of exclusion clauses can be found in section 8(3) of Act 137, which appears to be founded on the controversial doctrine of fundamental breach. The relevant provisions stipulate that any exclusion clause which purports to exclude a seller's liability for failure to deliver specific goods agreed upon, or goods which do not substantially correspond to the description or sample by which they were sold in null and void.50

Act 137 also contains a curious but noteworthy provision which on the face of its seems to allow contracting parties the opportunity to vary their rights, duties and liabilities as defined in the Act. Section 76 of the Act states:

"76. Subject to the provisions of this Act, the right, duties and [p.117] liabilities of the parties to a contract of sale, as laid down in this Act may, as between the parties themselves, be varied by express agreement, or by the course of dealing between the parties, or by trade usage, or by a custom (whether a rule of customary law or not) which the parties may be taken to have agreed to be applicable to the contract."

Are parties to a contract of sale hereby entitled to exclude, by express agreement, the various terms implied by the Act for the benefit of the buyer? The courts have had the opportunity to comment on the import of this provision in the case of Donkor v Motor Parts Trading Company,51 where the plaintiff, as buyer, entered into a hire-purchase agreement with the defendant-seller, for the sale of a seven-ton Bedford truck at the price of £G2,400. At a time when an amount of £G2,170 had been paid by the plaintiff to the defendants, the Bedford truck was seized by the defendants on account of plaintiff's failure to complete payment of the full hire-purchase price as agreed. The plaintiff, on hearing of the seizure of the truck, paid a further £G200 to the defendants but the vehicle could not be started, it having broken down while in the possession of the defendants. The plaintiff brought an action for the recovery of the sum of £G2,370 from the defendants for the seizure of the truck by them, contrary to the provisions of Act 137, damages for wrongful seizure, or in the alternative, the return of the vehicle in a roadworthy condition and damages for loss of use. It was argued on behalf of the defendants, relying on section 76 of the Act, that under the agreement they had the unrestricted right to recover the vehicle from the plaintiff if the whole of the hire-purchase price of the vehicle was not paid.

It was held that even though section 76 of Act 137 gives the parties to a hire-purchase agreement the right to make variations by express agreement, this right to make variations is subject to the provisions of the Act. Therefore if any such variations are inconsistent with any express provisions of the Act, in this case section 69, they are null and void.52

In the case of Donkor v Owona Farkye Brothers Transport,53 where the parties to a hire-purchase agreement had opted to contract out of Part VIII [p.118] of Act 137 [as was allowed by section 75(2)of the Act], it was held that the rights and liabilities of the parties to the agreement should be governed by the terms of their agreement, so far as they were consistent with other provisions of Act 137 out of which they could not completely contract. In this case the issue was whether the owner was entitled to seize the motor vehicle, the subject matter of the agreement, even though more than 50 per cent of the hire-purchase price had already been paid. There was an express provision in the agreement which allowed the owner to recover possession of the motor vehicle upon default by the hirer in the payment of the hire-purchase price as agreed. The defendants (owners) sought to rely on the provision in section 76 of the Act to argue that since the parties had opted to contract out of Part VIII of the Act, their rights and liabilities had to be ascertained exclusively from their written agreement without reference to the restrictions contained generally in the Act. This argument was rejected by the court when it held that the parties to a contract for the sale of goods do not have the right to contract out of the provisions of Act 137 so as to permit their obligations to be regulated exclusively by the terms of their own agreement. Even where the parties had opted to contract out of Part VIII of Act 137, the rights and liabilities of the parties thereto should be governed by the terms of their agreement as far as are consistent with other provisions of Act 137, out of which they cannot completely contract.

The cumulative effect of these statutory restrictions as interpreted by the Ghanaian courts is clearly to discourage the use of widely drawn and unconscionable exclusion clauses in consumer contracts to deprive purchasers of the protection accorded them under the law. The Ghanaian courts have been quite vigilant in restricting the use of such clauses, particularly in consumer credit transactions where the inequality in bargaining power is more distinct and there is greater opportunity for the exploitation of the weaker party.

Hire-purchase Contracts

Legislative control measures have also been applied quite extensively to the making of hire-purchase agreements. The Hire Purchase Decree, 1974 (NRCD 292), prescribes detailed rules aimed at ensuring the existence and quality of the hirer's consent to the terms and conditions of the agreement. The Decree imposes a number of rigid, formalistic rules to govern the formation of the hire-purchase contract and prescribes mandatory terms which must be incorporated into the contract if it is to be valid, placing the entire burden of compliance on the owner of the goods.54

Quite significantly, NRCD 292 identifies a number of contractual [p.119] provisions commonly included in hire-purchase contracts which are deemed to be unconscionable and oppressive and prohibits their inclusion in any hire-purchase agreement.55 These provisions of the Decree leave the owner of the goods with very little opportunity to rely on his own protective clauses, thus ensuring maximum protection for the hirer who is often in a much weaker bargaining position.

A number of provisions expressly limiting the use of exclusion clauses can also be found in NRCD 292, which implies terms into every hire-purchase agreement with regard to the quality of the goods, fitness and correspondence with the description or sample by which they were sold.56 Section 14 of the Decree seeks to restrict the use of exclusion clauses to avoid these statutorily implied terms. Any exclusion clause in the agreement purporting to exclude liability for breach of the implied terms as to merchantable quality shall not be effective unless it is proved that before the agreement was made the exclusion clause was brought to the notice of the hirer or buyer and its effect made clear to him. Where the exclusion clause is in relation to any defect, it must be proved that the owner brought that defect to the actual notice of the hirer or buyer. This formidable array of protective measures introduced in the Decree empowers hirers to enforce their rights under the hire-purchase agreement in court, a process which is facilitated by the fact that such agreements were required by statute to be reduced into writing, unlike sale transactions.57

Who is Covered by the Exclusion Clause?

The basic principle that a contract only benefits those who are parties to it necessarily prevents a third party (an employee or servant of the supplier of the goods or service) from relying on any exclusion clause contained in the contract. The principle as it applies to the enforceability of exclusion clauses was discussed in the 1954 case of Adler v Dickson.58 The plaintiff, a first-class passenger, was on a Mediterranean cruise on the steamship Himalaya. While walking up one of the gangways on her way to the ship, she was thrown some sixteen feet to the wharf, sustaining serious injuries. The contract between the plaintiff and the shipping company was embodied in the ticket and contained this clause:

"Passengers are carried at the passengers' entire risk. The company will not be responsible for and shall be exempt from all liability in [p.120] respect of any injury to the person of any passenger."59

The plaintiff's action was brought not against the company but against the master and the boatswain of the ship. The Court of Appeal upheld her claim on the basis that the master and the boatswain were negligent in setting up the gangway and were not entitled to rely on the exemption clause contained in the plaintiff's ticket. The conditions set out there were conditions of the contract between the plaintiff and the company, and the company's servants were not parties to the contract. It must be noted however that if the exemption clause specifically excluded the liability of the company's agents, servants and employees, the customer's remedy would have been effectively wiped out.

In Ghana, the common law principle on privity has been substantially modified by section 5(1) of the Contracts Act, 1960 (Act 25) which allows a person to rely on or enforce a contract to which he is not a party once it is established that the contract purports expressly or impliedly to confer a benefit on him.60 The Act, however, is careful to make an exception to this rule in the case of exclusion clauses. In section 5(2) (b) it states that the provision in section 5(1) does not apply to: "A provision in a contract purporting to exclude or restrict any liability of a person who is not a party thereto." In other words, a person who is not a party to a contract cannot rely on any exclusion clause contained in it, whether or not the clause purports to exclude such a third party from liability. This provision amounts to an absolute ban on the exclusion of liability of persons who are strangers to the contract (including the contracting party's servants, agents or employees) for negligence. The Ghanaian statutory position prevents suppliers from evading the Adler v Dickson decision by the express mention of servants or employees in exclusion clauses. With the protection provided in section 5(2)(b) of the Contracts Act, a consumer, in the event of breach, would still be entitled to maintain an action against the servants or employees for negligence even if there is a clause expressly purporting to exclude liability of the supplier's employees and servants.

PROVIDING GREATER PROTECTION FROM UNFAIR EXCLUSION CLAUSESTHE WAY FORWARD

Even though there exists some statutory regulation of exclusion clauses in consumer contracts, it appears to be fragmentary and piecemeal. In spite of the restrictions in the Sale of Goods Act, most sale transactions are concluded with hardly any reference to statutory protective provisions.

[p.121]

Sellers rarely explain or point out exclusion clauses to buyers, let alone explain their import before the conclusion of the contract. The average purchaser of goods, who is unlikely to resort to court action for the refund of money paid for a single household appliance which is defective, is compelled to rely on the goodwill of the seller if he is to get any remedy at all. Shops are replete with notices stating that "money paid is not refundable", "goods once purchased cannot be returned", "the company accepts no liability for defects appearing in goods" or words to such effect. Such clauses or notices convey the erroneous impression that the purchaser's statutory rights are automatically excluded, leaving little scope for the operation of available statutory protection in everyday consumer transactions. In the absence of adequately enforced standards or regulations on the use of exclusion clauses, there is little incentive for suppliers of goods to ensure that the expectations of consumers are met, especially where there is very little likelihood of consumer claims being pursued in the courts.

It is quite apparent that some further legislative intervention is required to achieve a higher level of protection in actual practice for consumers. Drawing from the experience of other legal jurisdictions, it has been observed that two broad approaches have typically been employed in the regulation of exclusion clauses in consumer contracts, with emphasis being placed on one or the other.

The first is the effective legislative control of the terms of various categories of consumer contracts through the prescription of the core provisions and the restriction of the use of exclusion clauses for certain kinds of conduct. The second approach is the active approach involving the creation of an office or commission charged with investigating market practices and scrutinising the terms of recurrent standard form, consumer contracts with the aim of controlling unfair market practices and the use of unreasonable and unfair contract terms.

The legislative control strategy, to be effective, must empower the courts to strike out contractual provisions which are patently obnoxious, oppressive or unconscionable.61 Such regulatory legislation should provide specific guidelines as to what constitutes "acceptable practices or clauses" and clearly spell out the kinds of conduct or activities which are prohibited.

The English Unfair Contract Terms Act of 1977 for example imposes an outright ban on clauses which purport to exclude liability for personal injury or death resulting from a supplier's negligence.62 Under the [p.122] American Uniform Commercial Code, limits are also placed on the supplier's freedom to limit or exclude liability for consequential damages resulting from his breach of the contract, particularly where such damage consist of injury to the person, in which case it is treated as prima facie unconscionable and thus not effective. Article 2-719 (3) of the Uniform Commercial Code provides:

"Consequential damages may be limited or excluded unless the limitation or exclusion is unconscionable. Limitation of consequential damages for injury to the person in the case of consumer goods is prima facie unconscionable but limitation of damages where the loss is commercial is not."63

The Draft Trade Practices Bill proposed in Ghana in 1993 advocates extensive legislative control of contract clauses and business practices which are "deceiving", "misleading" or "unconscionable."64 Such conduct or clauses in consumer contracts are expressly prohibited, and broad guidelines are given for determining what constitutes unconscionable conduct. The draft bill also rightly proposes the outright prohibition of the use of exclusion clauses to avoid liability for death or personal injury caused by defective goods or the negligence of the seller or the exclusion of statutorily implied terms with regard to title, quiet possession and conformity of goods with description and quality. It is further proposed in the draft bill that other kinds of exclusion clauses, including those which seek to exclude or restrict liability for loss arising from breach of the duty to exercise reasonable skill and care be subjected to the test of reasonableness. An attempt is made by the draft bill to clearly define the factors to be considered by the courts in determining the reasonableness or otherwise of such exclusion clauses.

The active approach which clearly complements the legislative strategy usually involves the appointment of a Consumer Ombudsman or Commissioner to receive complaints, make investigations and refer such matters to the regular courts for resolution. In countries such as Sweden special courts known as "market courts" have been set up for this purpose with excellent [p.123] results.65 It has been suggested that a modified form of it could be employed in Ghana.66 Such an agency would provide a forum outside the regular courts to receive, investigate and assist in the provision of remedies for complaints of consumers or their representative bodies. It has been found that the submission of complaints to an administrative agency for investigation and the provision of services free of charge is more appealing to consumer-buyers as a preferable alternative to the more time consuming and expensive court procedures.67

The Draft Trade Practices Bill recommends the implementation of this active strategy in Ghana for the first time by proposing the establishment of a Trade Practices Commission headed by a Trade Practices Commissioner charged with the regulation of competition in trade and the control of restrictive or unfair trade practices and contract terms. Under this proposal the commissioner, who would be directed by the Minister of Trade on matters of broad policies only, would be empowered to investigate market practices and contract terms in areas where improper procedures are most common, keep consumer protection laws under review, publish information such as guidance notes for business on how to comply with the Bill, educate consumers on their rights and obligations and conduct consumer research.

To ensure that the strategy sets attainable and realistic objectives, it could be useful for such a commission to work in cooperation with business or industry representatives in the drafting of model standard form contracts. The commissioner could also be charged with the prior approval of the terms of standard form consumer contracts used by specific industries and, where appropriate, recommend that offending clauses be declared void or prohibited.

It is suggested here that any regulatory legislation to be enacted should go further to prohibit the exclusion of liability for losses caused by seriously reprehensible conduct such as fraud, gross negligence, deliberate conduct or recklessness. Considering the level of illiteracy, consumer rights awareness and the prohibitive costs of litigation, it is clear that the adoption of a strategy combining the two approaches identified, backed by intensive education of the consuming public of their rights, is most likely to achieve optimum results.

THE REMEDIES OF THE PURCHASER OF GOODS

Another issue affecting consumer rights which warrants examination [p.124] is that of the effectiveness of remedies available to consumers under contracts of sale. Under Act 137 the buyer's options where the seller is in breach are either to adopt the goods or to reject them and refuse to pay the price or recover it if already paid.68 It has been observed however that in practice the foremost concern of the average consumer who has purchased a defective product is to have the goods replaced or repaired so as to conform to his legitimate expectations.69 Thus the buyer's primary expectation of receiving goods which conform with the contract is not entirely met by the options presented him under the Sale of Goods Act, which, notably, makes no provision for replacement, re-tender or cure, either as a right of the buyer or the seller.

Right to Reject

Under Act 137, the buyer's right of rejection is circumscribed by certain conditions, viz, where the right is exercised as a result of a breach of a condition of the contract, the breach must be of a serious nature; and secondly, the buyer's right of rejection is lost if his previous conduct amounts to an acceptance of the goods.70

The first limitation on the right of rejection is the result of an innovation introduced by the drafters of Act 137 as a way of avoiding the rigid common law approach which effectively allows a buyer to reject goods supplied for the slightest deviation from the contractual terms, even where the buyer has not sustained any loss.71 The position under the English Sale of Goods Act is illustrated in the oft-cited case of Arcos Ltd v E A Ronaasen & Son,72 where the appellants agreed to sell to the respondents a quantity of red wood and white wood staves, each to be of a thickness of half an inch. The price was to include cost, freight and insurance (CIF) from Archangel to the River Thames. When the staves arrived, the buyers purported to reject the goods on the ground that they did not correspond with the descriptions given in that they were more than half an inch in thickness. The matter was submitted to arbitration and the arbitrator found that even though the staves exceeded the specified thickness, they were "commercially within and merchantable under the contract specification" [p.125] and awarded that the buyers were not entitled to reject them.

The House of Lords held that the fact that the goods were merchantable under the contract was not the proper test to be applied in determining whether the goods satisfied the contract description or not and was of the view that the finding as to measurement showed that the goods were not those contracted to be sold and therefore the buyers were entitled to reject them. This position was defended by Lord Atkin in the following words:

"It was contended that in all commercial contracts the question was whether there was a 'substantial' compliance with the contract:  there always must be some margin: and it is for the tribunal of fact to determine whether the margin is exceeded or not. I cannot agree. If the written contract specifies conditions of weight, measurement and the like, those conditions must be complied with. A ton does not mean about a ton or a yard about a yard. Still less when you descend to minute measurements does ½ inch mean about ½ inch. If the seller wants a margin he must and in my experience does stipulate for it. Of course by recognized trade usage particular figures may be given a different meaning, as in baker's dozen; or there may be even incorporated a definite margin more or less: but there is no evidence or finding of such a usage in the present case."72a

Under English law therefore a buyer is entitled to exercise the right of rejection for the slightest departure from the contractual terms, whether such deviation has seriously affected his position under the contract or or not.73

The drawback of this rigid approach was said to be that it allowed buyers to play fast and loose and reject goods at the slightest pretext of non compliance with contractual terms. The drafters of the Ghanaian Sale of Goods Act declined to follow this position, and instead sought to achieve some kind of balance so as to encourage the performance of sale contracts by both parties, while yet safeguarding the buyer's right of rejection for genuine breaches. The provision in section 49(1)(b) was employed to achieve this balance. It is interesting to note that the underlying philosophy of the approach of the Ghanaian Sale of Goods Act is strikingly close to that espoused by the English courts in the modern classification of contractual terms into conditions, warranties and "innominate terms" and in the determination of when an innocent party's right of rescission can be exercised.74

This novelty introduced by Act 137, in theory, requires the courts to make (in as objective a manner as possible) an assessment of the extent to which the buyer's position and expectations have been prejudiced by the [p.126] seller's less than perfect performance, taking into consideration the nature of the goods in question, acceptable market practices or trade usages, the purpose for which the goods are intended, the terms of the contract itself and the actual impact of the breach on the buyer's position.

It is however doubtful that this innovative approach in determining the consequences of breach would be of much significance in actual practice. Even though to date there has not been any case in which the Ghanaian courts have been called upon to make such an assessment, it is quite obvious that an objective determination of whether a breach is serious or trivial to a particular buyer would not be easy to make.75 Where there is a dispute it is more likely than not that the court would feel compelled to uphold the claim of the buyer who insists that a particular breach has affected him adversely. It must also be noted that the purchaser's alternative if he is denied the right of rejection would be a claim for damages only.76 This however is not likely to be very useful to him in actual practice, for if a purchaser cannot reject goods because the breach is trivial, it is unlikely that the award of damages would be substantial since the determination that the breach is trivial signifies that it has not occasioned any significant prejudice to the buyer's interests or expectations under the contract.

Perhaps in order to provide the purchaser with a more useful remedy, provision should be made for replacement or cure as pertains under the American Uniform Commercial Code.77 The relevant provision which in the opinion of the present writer accords more with consumer expectations states as follows:

(i) Where any tender or delivery by the seller is rejected because non-conforming and the time for performance has not yet expired, the seller may reasonably notify the buyer of his intention to cure and may then within the contract time make a conforming delivery.

(ii) Where the buyer rejects a non-conforming tender which the seller had reasonable grounds to believe would be acceptable with or without money allowance the seller may, if he reasonably notifies the buyer, have a further reasonable time to substitute a conforming tender.

The formation of the UCC not only favours the seller by providing him with an opportunity to re-tender, but is also beneficial to the purchaser whose interests would in most cases be better served by such re-tender than an action in damages. A provision guaranteeing both buyer and seller the right to replacement or cure would clearly be more beneficial to both parties [p.127] and would reduce the incidence of wrongly motivated rejections and thus avoid economic waste.78

Loss of Right to Reject

(i) Acceptance by conduct

The application of the universal principle that goods cannot be rejected once they have been accepted seems to raise more difficult questions for the courts. Section 52 of Act 137 stipulates that a buyer is deemed to have accepted goods when he intimates his acceptance to the seller, or does not within a reasonable time after delivery of the goods inform the seller that he rejects them; or when he wrongfully refuses or neglects to place the goods at the disposal of the seller after notifying the seller of his rejection. There would also be deemed to be acceptance where the buyer deals with the goods in such a manner as to indicate his approval of the goods without expressly communicating such acceptance to the seller.79

The underlying policy of section 52, which applies the general principle that a party cannot approbate and reprobate, is obviously aimed at ensuring the swift conclusion of contracts of sale. Further, the stipulation of a reasonable time for rejection is presumably to ensure that goods rejected can be returned in substantially the same condition in which they originally were, which in most cases requires that the buyer act timeously. Several important questions could arise in the determination of whether or not a buyer must be deemed to have lost his right of rejection, including the following: should an agreement that a cure be attempted, or an attempt by the buyer to effect a cure on the goods delivered cause the buyer to lose his right of rejection? Should the buyer be deemed to have lost his right of rejection when he has not had a reasonable opportunity to examine the goods delivered? In determining whether the buyer has rejected the goods within a reasonable time should consideration be given to the history of defects which have appeared in the goods? In other words should time begin to run from when the defects appeared in the goods or when they were discovered?

Even though Act 137 does not expressly state that a buyer cannot be held to have accepted goods unless and until he has had a reasonable opportunity to inspect them,80 it would only be reasonable to consider the stipulations in section 52 as subject to the buyer being offered the opportunity to inspect the goods to ensure that they are in fact in conformity with the contract terms. It is generally conceded that merely taking delivery of the goods does not of itself amount to acceptance, and in all [p.128] cases the buyer must have had the opportunity of inspecting the goods before his right of rejection could be said to have been lost.81

With regard to the buyer's conduct after taking delivery of the goods, what kinds of conduct amount to acceptance? It is well established that where the buyer's conduct amounts to an assumption of ownership rights over the goods, he cannot subsequently purport to reject them. Where the buyer, with full knowledge of the defects in the goods or any other breach, consumes or resells them, his right of rejection is clearly thereby forfeited.82 The determination of the scope of what amounts to the exercise of ownership rights in this context is however a question of degree.

In Robin Hood Mills Ltd v Farah,83 the defendants, a Canadian company, in accordance with a cost, insurance and freight (CIF) contract, sold and shipped 2,000 bags of Robin Hood flour to the plaintiff. They arranged for insurance cover upon terms current in the flour trade and in addition had a special cover clause which stated as follows: "warranted free from claim damage caused by weevil, insects, worms or any other inherent vice of property." A clean bill of lading, invoices, the insurance certificate and a bank draft were, in due course, delivered to the plaintiff. This ship arrived at Takoradi and the plaintiff, while in the process of removing the bags of flour from the habour to his warehouse, noticed that 339 bags of flour were caked and also found upon internal examination of 10 bags that they were infested with weevils. He immediately applied to the Lloyd's agent for a survey which was carried out and confirmed that the flour was weevil infested. The Lloyd's agent however did not think that the flour was all that bad and suggested sifting, but the plaintiff rejected this suggestion, being of the view that the flour was not fit for human consumption.

At the suggestion of the agent the plaintiff asked the Port Health Officer to examine the goods to decide whether the flour was fit for human consumption or not. Upon examination of the flour, the Port Health Officer expressed the opinion that the flour was not fit for human consumption. The plaintiff thereupon surrendered the 2,000 bags of flour to the health authorities for destruction and wrote to the defendants advising them that the 2,000 bags of flour were unfit for human consumption and therefore had to be destroyed. He later instituted an action in the High Court against them claiming the cost, insurance and freight value of the flour as damages for breach of contract for the sale of the 2,000 bags of flour to him and, in the alternative, money paid for a consideration which had totally failed. The trial judge gave judgment for the plaintiff and the defendants appealed to the Supreme Court.

[p.129]

The Supreme Court held that the buyer had no right of rejection since the sellers had performed all their obligations under the cost, insurance and freight (CIF) contract. However, even if the buyer had any right of rejection, such right had been forfeited by the plaintiff's act of surrendering the flour to the Port Health Authority for destruction. In the words of Ollennu JSC:

"Such surrender and destruction are powers or rights exercisable only by an owner, the person in whom is vested title, possession and control of the goods. It follows logically that the surrender of the flour to the municipal authorities by the plaintiff and the destruction thereof upon his request, must be acts inconsistent with the ownership of the flour by the defendants. . . It follows that even if the learned judge had been right in holding that the plaintiff rejected the flour, he still should have found against the plaintiff since by his said acts, he is deemed in law to have accepted the flour and is not entitled to refund of the purchase price. . ."84

The view adopted by the Supreme Court on the legal effect of the plaintiff's surrender of the defective goods for destruction is in the opinion of this writer somewhat difficult to justify. It would appear from the facts that the plaintiff-purchaser took all possible steps to indicate his rejection of the flour which was apparently in a deplorable state. The act of surrendering the bags of flour for destruction seemed to have been the ultimate and inevitable thing to do upon having made his intention to reject quite clear. In the circumstances of this case it is difficult to see how the act of surrendering defective goods for destruction as required by the regulations of the port, upon seeking expert advice, could be deemed to amount to an unequivocal acceptance of the goods in question.

(ii) Agreements for the Repair of Defective Goods

In cases where the goods delivered are defective in some respect, it may be agreed between the parties that the seller attempt to effect a cure on the goods or that the buyer undertake to repair the goods. Where for example a buyer, upon taking delivery of a secondhand motor vehicle and discovering latent defects, asks the seller to repair the car, would such  request be construed as conduct indicating an acceptance of the goods?

Under the common law, such conduct has in some cases been held to amount to an affirmation of the contract, which deprives an innocent party of his right to rescind a contract for innocent misrepresentation.85 The Ghanaian courts seem however to take the position that even though express provision is not made in the Act for cure, parties should be free to make [p.130] such informal arrangements between themselves.86 A principle which stipulates that an agreement for cure or repair be treated as a final acceptance of the goods, would discourage its use since purchasers would not be guaranteed their right of rejection if such attempts at cure or repair should fail.87

(iii) Too late to Reject

In a number of cases the courts have had to determine whether a buyer has lost his right of rejection by reason of lapse of a reasonable time. In Rockson v Armah,88 the appellant sold a secondhand Mercedes Benz car to the respondent. The respondent made cash payments for part of the purchase price and remitted two post-dated cheques for the balance of the price. Upon delivery the car was found to have been involved in an accident and had suffered considerable damage. The appellant accepted liability and agreed to repair the car. Consequently, the appellant approached Messrs RT Briscoe to effect the repairs but found their estimates too high. The car was eventually repaired by a "wayside" fitter in a day and the respondent took delivery. However, after the respondent had used the car for almost two months, he repudiated the contract on the ground that he had discovered some latent defects in the car. The trial judge held that the respondent was justified in repudiating the contract. On appeal against this decision, the main issue for the consideration of the court revolved around the right of a purchaser to repudiate a sale upon the discovery of latent defects.

It was held, allowing the appeal, that even if the purchaser had the right to reject the vehicle, that right was lost by unreasonable delay. What amounted to reasonable time was a question of fact and might vary with the circumstances of a case, but in the court's view the retention of a secondhand car for almost two months was unreasonable and it was not thereafter open to the respondent to avoid the transaction.

In Armah v Baffour,89 the plaintiff sold his "home-used" or secondhand Bedford engine to the defendant under a written agreement. The defendant agreed to make part-payment immediately and to pay the rest of the amount in weekly instalments. The engine was tested to the defendant's satisfaction before he took delivery of it. Subsequently, the defendant defaulted on the payment terms. The plaintiff sued for the payment of the balance of [p.131] ¢180,000 together with interest and damages. The defendant resisted the claim and pleaded that the plaintiff had fraudulently represented to him that the engine was brand new and faultless. He had however discovered a fault on the engine and had to repair it at a cost of ¢20,000. He tendered two receipts for the repairs on the engine. The defendant therefore sought to repudiate the transaction and counterclaimed for damages. The court found, however, that after the purchase the defendant had installed the secondhand engine on the vehicle and carried out the repairs on the vehicle after using it for commercial purposes for five months rather than exercising his right to reject it. It was held that on the facts the defendant's conduct was more consistent with a willingness and readiness to accept the Bedford engine by keeping the engine for a period upwards of five months after he had discovered the latent defects and the transaction was therefore binding on him.

In both cases the relevant time lapse was determined from when the purchaser found that the goods were in fact defective. The question could however arise whether time should be made to run from the time of delivery of the goods, the time the defects appeared in the goods or when the defects were actually discovered by the buyer. What if the defects in the product appeared six months after its purchase, or what if the purchaser began to use the product one year after it was bought and it was only then that he noticed the defect?

It would be interesting to consider the views of an English court in a recent case which happens to be the first decision directly on this point since the enactment of the English Sale of Goods Act of 1893. In Bernstein v Pamson Motors (Golders Green) Ltd90 the plaintiff purchased a new Nissan Laurel car on 7 December. As a result of illness he had not driven the car much. He had only driven 100 miles to accustom himself to the controls and was on his first substantial trip the following 2 January when the car broke down, it having done only about 140 miles. It was found that a manufacturing defect had caused sealant to enter the lubrication system and cut off the oil supply to the camshaft, which broke down completely. The plaintiff sought to reject the car on the ground that it was not of merchantable quality.

The court held that the car was not of merchantable quality but found that the buyer was too late to reject the car because a reasonable time had elapsed under section 35 of the English Sale of Goods Act.91 The court took [p.132] the view that the purpose of the relevant section of the English Act being to ensure that rejection was barred reasonably soon after the completion of the transaction, the nature of the defect and the speed with which it might have been discovered were not relevant to the determination of a reasonable time. The decision which ignored seemingly relevant issues in the determination of reasonable time has been rightly criticised.92

It is obvious  that defects in some manufactured products may not become apparent until after a period of use and sometimes a purchaser would not, for a variety of reasons, notice the defects immediately after purchase. It is suggested that where the defects can only be detected after a period of use the consumer must be able to reject the goods supplied if he acts timeously, subject of course to the goods being in substantially the same condition as they were when delivered.  The essential question, as the decided cases show, is whether the circumstances taken as a whole, particularly the buyer's conduct after discovering the defects in the goods, are more indicative of a willingness to accept the goods or to reject them.

(iv) Ensuring that the goods are of good quality

The drafters of the Ghanaian Sale of Goods Act seem to have gone to great lengths to ensure that the legitimate expectation of every purchaser to receive goods of assured quality is guaranteed. The right of the buyer to receive goods of assured quality is guaranteed by the innovative provision in Act 137, s 13 which states:

13.(2) Subject to the provisions of this Act and any other enactment there is no implied warranty or condition as to the quality or fitness for any particular purpose of goods supplied under a contract of sale except as follows

(a) There is an implied condition that the goods are free from defects which are not declared or known the buyer before or at the time when the contract is made. . .

(b) Where the goods are of a description which are supplied by the seller in the course of his business and the buyer expressly or by implication makes known the purpose for which the goods are required there is an implied condition that the goods are reasonably fit for the purpose."

Act 137 scrupulously avoids the use of the term "merchantable quality" with its definitional problems and inherent uncertainty of scope and instead refers specifically to "defects" in the goods and their "fitness for the [p.133] intended purpose." A buyer who is dissatisfied with the quality of goods must be able to point to some defect in the physical condition of the product or in its functioning, or show that the goods supplied are not reasonably fit for the purpose for which they were intended.

The provision in section 13 has been described as one of the most innovative legislative achievements in Ghanaian sales law.93 The provision begins with a statement of the caveat emptor principle, creates an extensive exception to it by introducing a broad implied term, and stipulates a number of limited situations in which the implied term will not apply. The general principle in section 13, which is based on the caveat emptor principle, is so overshadowed by the exception that the result is something close to a complete negation of the caveat emptor rule where the quality of goods is concerned. In the words of one commentator "we started with caveat emptor, and have ended with caveat venditor in a novel and extreme form."94

In practice, section 13 places a very high responsibility on the seller of goods with respect to the quality of  the goods, whether as manufacturer, wholesaler or retailer. It is significant to note that in the case of latent manufacturing defects a retailer of goods would be in breach of the implied condition even if he had no means of knowing of the defect. According to section 13, liability for latent defects in goods sold is only avoided where the goods are not of the kind sold in the ordinary course of the seller's business, and the seller was not aware and could not reasonably have been aware of their existence. Where the seller of the goods is a trader dealing in goods of that description, the liability is almost strict. In the case of latent defects which are not easily discoverable upon examination, the trader would in all cases be liable, whether or not he was aware of their existence.

(v) Trends in the Case Law.

In spite of the strict duty imposed by Act 137, s 13 on sellers with regard to the quality of goods, the case law hardly exhibits this trend. Particularly where the goods sold are secondhand goods, the Ghanaian courts, for the most part, appear to have ignored the provision in section 13 and instead applied the decisions of English cases which are clearly at variance with our statutory provisions.

The case of Rockson v Armah95 for example, seems to suggest that the principle of caveat emptor is still alive and well when it comes to the secondhand motor vehicle trade irrespective of the express provision in section 13 of the Act. In that case the Mercedes Benz car purchased by the respondent from the appellant, upon delivery, showed signs of recent [p.134] involvement in an accident. There were serious defects in the clutch and starter systems and widespread damage to the mudguard and one of the headlamps. The respondent, who had test driven the car and had appeared satisfied with it before the conclusion of the contract, subsequently purported to reject the car, after the appellant had made an unsuccessful attempt at repairing the defects.

Quite apart from the issue of whether the purported rejection had been made within a reasonable time, the courts had to address the fundamental question of whether the buyer could at all exercise any right of rejection by reason of the latent defects in the car. In so doing the Court of Appeal adopted the view of Lord Denning MR on the right to repudiate a sale of a secondhand car as expressed in the case of Bartlett v Sidney Marcus Ltd.96 The English position, based on a direct interpretation of section 14(2) of the English Sale of Goods Act of 1893 which employs the term "merchantable quality", is to the effect that defects in a secondhand car do not necessarily render it unmerchantable. Under English law, a secondhand car is merchantable if it is in usable condition, even though not perfect, and the presence of latent defects in such a car does not impose any liability on the seller, in the absence of an express warranty. The wholesale application of this dictum raised some concern, considering that the relevant provision under the Ghanaian Sale of Goods Act does not make any distinction in the case of secondhand goods and does not employ the "merchantable quality standard" at all. As has been pointed out the position adopted by the court in this case cannot be supported by the provisions in the Act because according to section 13 the only way the appellant in Rockson v Armah, not being a secondhand car dealer himself, could have avoided liability, was if he could show that he did not in fact know of the defects and could not reasonably have known of their existence.97

In a recent decision on the same issue, but involving a sale by a dealer, the Court of Appeal in the case of Yirenkyi v Tormekpey98 followed the Rockson v Armah approach in determining the issue of when a buyer is entitled to reject a secondhand car on grounds of latent defects without making any attempt to interpret and apply the provisions of section 13 of Act 137. The plaintiff bought a secondhand Toyota truck for ¢157,000 from the defendant, a motor vehicle dealer. He then spent ¢56,860 to make substantial repairs to rehabilitate the truck. Subsequently, the plaintiff brought an action against the defendant to recover both sums and for damages for loss of use. He pleaded that after taking delivery of the truck he found that contrary to the warranty given by the defendant to him, the [p.135] truck was not roadworthy, and that the defendant then authorised him to have the truck repaired and promised to refund the cost of repairs. The plaintiff further claimed that as a result of the defendant's failure to transfer ownership of the truck to him, he had not been able to use it and had thereby suffered substantial loss. The defendant admitted receipt of the purchase price, but denied having given the plaintiff any warranty and claimed that before the plaintiff bought the truck he brought his own mechanic to examine it. The trial judge allowed the plaintiff's motion for summary judgment on the grounds that the plaintiff was entitled to repudiate the contract, return the vehicle and claim the refund because the defendant was in breach of the implied condition under Act 137, s13(1)(b) that the vehicle was reasonably fit for the purpose it was required.

On appeal, it was held that the trial judge was not entitled to conclude without evidence that there was a breach of warranty that the vehicle (a secondhand one) was reasonably fit for the purpose for which it was required, especially as the allegation that the truck was not roadworthy, deposed to by the plaintiff, was denied by the defendant. The judge should have admonished himself that in buying a secondhand car, defects might appear sooner or later, and that in the absence of an express warranty, the buyer had no redress. And even if be bought it from a dealer the most he could require was that it should be reasonably fir for the purpose of being driven along the road.

The statement of the law in this decision which appears to be a wholesale application of the decision in Rockson v Armah is clearly at variance with the express provision of section 13 of Act 137 which allows the seller, being a dealer in the goods in question, to escape liability for defects in goods sold only if the buyer had been given the opportunity to examine the goods and the defects in question could have been discovered upon such examination or if there was a valid and enforceable exclusion clause.99

The attitude of the courts however raises some crucial questions about the practical implications of the provision in section 13 for the smooth running of trade, particularly trade in secondhand goods such as cars. It is quite obvious that in the secondhand vehicle trade, business would be almost impossible to conduct if purchasers, after examining the vehicle and concluding a sale, could in all cases be entitled to subsequently reject it upon discovering some latent defects. The attitude of the courts in dealing with this issue may perhaps be an indication that the absence of any distinction in the case of secondhand goods constitutes on obstacle in the implementation of the provision which is aimed at achieving maximum protection for consumers.

REGULATING ADVERTISING AND OTHER PROMOTIONAL ACTIVITIES

The increased availability of goods and services has been accompanied by a sharp growth in the advertising industry in Ghana. Suppliers are employing an increasingly wide range of novel business devices and techniques for the expansion of their markets and the promotion of sales. More aggressive forms of advertising are now being employed, involving direct appeals to consumers and the giving of firm assurances on the performance and quality of goods. Considering its distinct influence on consumer behaviour and the limitless opportunity for exploitation, dishonesty and deception, it is important that there be an effective regime for the regulation of advertising and other promotional techniques. A look at the existing law governing this fast expanding industry however reveals that effective regulation and oversight is lacking.

Under the common law, commendatory expressions of statements made in advertisements in describing a product and its attributes are generally designated as "mere puffs" which do not attract any liability under common law or equity if untrue.100 Such descriptions, in any case, were usually couched in such vague and indeterminate terms that strict verification was virtually impossible. Thus, the common law gave considerable latitude to suppliers of goods and services in making of flourishing statements and giving of broad assurances for the purpose of attracting purchasers.101 In a number of isolated cases however, offers of precise amounts payable to consumer-purchasers as guarantee for the performance of advertised products have been held to create legal liability where all the elements of a contract exist.102 Statements made by suppliers in advertisements through the print or electronic media, brochures, catalogues, etc on the availability and quality of products are also generally regarded by the law as "invitations to treat", and thus do not directly give rise to any liability for the supplier.

The state of the common law on liability for statements made in advertising campaigns clearly does not provide the consuming public with sufficient protection against deceptive and unacceptable conduct such as misleading advertising, bait advertising, the use of false trade descriptions, etc. In Ghana, even though there are few isolated statutory provisions which have some bearing on the conduct of suppliers of goods in advertising, it can hardly be said that there exists any comprehensible legal regime for the regulation of advertising. Examples of such legislative [p.137] provisions deal with matters such as public health,103 trade descriptions,104 weights and measures,105 indecency and obscenity.106

The Criminal Code makes it an offence to publish charlatan advertisements which are calculated to deceive members of the public.107 The Merchandise Marks Act, 1964 (Act 253), which primarily deals with the use of fraudulent marks on merchandise specifically prohibits the use or application of any false trade description to goods. The interpretation section defines "trade description" to mean any description, statement, or other indication, direct or indirect as to the number, quantity, measure, gauge, or weight of any goods; standard of quality of the goods; fitness for purpose, strength, performance or behaviour of the goods, the place or country in which the goods were produced; or the material of which the goods are composed, among others.108 The Act makes it an offence for any person to apply a false trade description to goods or to sell or expose for sale such goods, and the offence is deemed to have been committed where the description appears in a sign, advertisement, invoice, list, business letter or paper.109

Existing laws which purport to regulate advertising are for the most part limited to the prohibition of advertisements for particular purposes which are considered inimical to the interest of society, but for the most part do not touch directly on the various forms of dishonest and deceptive conduct in advertising. The Food and Drugs Law, 1992 (PNDCL 305B) purports to address this issue in the advertisement and sale of drugs and food items. The law prohibits the advertisement of any drug, cosmetic, device or chemical substance in a manner that is false, misleading or deceptive as regards its character, quality, safety, etc and further prohibits the advertisement of drugs as prevention or cures for specific diseases and disorders listed in schedule 2 to the Law.110

The Draft Trade Practices Bill attempts to fill this gap by advocating the prohibition of unfair trade practices such as bait advertising, misleading advertising, false undertakings to provide compensation, rewards or gifts and the employment of other forms of promotional devices and gimmicks which are dishonest. The adoption of such legislative control measures would go a long way to protect consumers from trade malpractices, [p.138] particularly if the conduct of suppliers in advertising and related activities is further regulated by an authorised board or agency.

To date, most of the efforts made at regulating misleading conduct in advertising in Ghana are in the form of self-regulation by the advertising industry. The Code of Outdoor Advertising Practice drawn up by the Advertising Association of Ghana represents one of such attempts at self-regulation. The code of conduct, among others, prohibits members of the Advertising Association of Ghana from engaging in the advertising malpractices which have been identified.

To achieve an appreciable level of protection for consumers or purchasers of goods, the efforts of the advertising industry in the form of self-regulating codes of conduct must be supplemented by legislation aimed at imposing a high standard of honesty on suppliers and the effective monitoring of the advertising industry.

CONCLUSION

It is quite clear that there must be a strengthening of legislative control of the use of exclusion clauses in consumer contracts. Considering the level of adherence to existing statutory prescriptions for the use of exclusion clauses, it would be necessary to explore the possibility of adopting a more active approach by creating an office which would be charged with investigating business malpractices and recommending legislative intervention in the case of unfair or unconscionable terms in standard form contracts in common use. In striving to achieve greater protection for Ghanaian consumers, regard must be had to the fact that the costs of litigation are usually so disproportionally high for the purchaser (as compared to suppliers of goods) that there is little motivation to seek redress for defects in goods purchased. The setting up of a commission or agency which would receive and investigate such complaints at little or no cost to consumers and oversee and regulate trade practices would go a long way to enhance consumer welfare. Such a commission would be mandated to establish alternative dispute resolution procedures to obtain redress for consumers in a relatively cheap and informal manner.

It is conceded here that legislation alone would not be an automatic solution to consumer problems. The huge expense involved in the running of the institutions or agencies set up for the effective enforcement of the laws could lead to delays in the full implementation of policies aimed at enhancing consumer protection. As a supplementary measure, trade associations and industry representatives must be encouraged to develop voluntary codes of practice and other self-regulating procedures to govern the conduct of business so as to enhance consumer welfare. Such codes of conduct, to be effective, would have to be carefully drawn up and provide precise guidelines which must be constantly updated to accord with [p.139] changing business practices and consumer expectations. It is suggested that the commission or agency to be set up could provide guidelines for the various industries for the performance of this task.

It is recommended that the scope of redress available to purchasers in the event of the seller's breach must be expanded to cover the following options: the right to a replacement of the goods in appropriate cases; a reduction in the price if the purchaser decides to keep the goods; and a right to compensation for damage sustained by the purchaser which arises out of the contract.

The attempt made by the drafters of the Sale of Goods Act to ensure that purchasers receive goods of satisfactory quality, though excellent on paper, appears in practice to be unworkable in some respects. This has created a situation where the courts, particularly where the goods involved are secondhand goods, have consistently declined to apply the stringent standards laid down in section 13 of Act 137. It is suggested that section 13 of the Act be amended to limit the duty of the seller in the case of secondhand goods, to ensure that the goods are reasonably fit for the intended purpose only.

The fast expanding advertising industry in Ghana should be more rigorously regulated. Effective consumer protection must begin from the point when the decision to purchase is made, and since advertising significantly influences the making of such decision, it is absolutely essential that adequate controls are imposed on the activities of sellers int he promotion of their goods through advertising.

Finally, it must be emphasised that the impact of consumer legislation in any society depends to a great extent on the awareness of consumers and their willingness to enforce their "rights." Consumer education should therefore be a significant part of any attempt to enhance consumer welfare and to this end there must be developed policies which will help to strengthen consumer associations and reinforce their role in educating the general public as to consumer rights and suppliers' obligations in the distribution of goods and services.
