THE SCOPE OF THE RIGHT OF TESTATION AND INCIDENTS OF THE GA SIX-CLOTH MARRIAGE [1993-95] VOL XIX RGL 140—158.

NII-APONSAH DA

 

*

Certainly the common place experience of Ghanaian legislators, lawyers and jurists alike is that an overwhelming percentage of their law consists of rehashed erstwhile legislation of the English Parliament. Well-known instances of such legislation include the Marriage Ordinance1 and the Wills Act, 1971.2  Certainly, Ghanaians are not unique in having to pattern their laws on those of their former colonial masters, for they join truck with all the ex-colonies of England including Canada, United States of America, the Commonwealth of Australia, the Republics of India, Nigeria, Sierra Leone, Uganda and all the others.

But perhaps one Ghanaian indigenous institution which was far ahead of English legislation and which England could have profitably studied and patterned its family provision legislation upon is the Ga six-cloth marriage and the extent to which it affects the right of testation of a man married according to its rites. Had the British, while they were here, studied the niceties of that customary law marriage to the requisite depth, they would have had something to copy, at least for once, from the subjects they regarded as primitive natives whom they hoped to advance towards Christianity and they would  not have had to look so far away for ideas for the enactment of their Inheritance (Family Provision) Act, 19383; for the six-cloth marriage is one institution which is already posited upon what the English had to resort to legislation to provide for, perhaps for the first time, for their people. What then are the assumptions and expectations of the six-cloth marriage which, regrettably, have not featured as prominently in the literature of jurists as they should? This article will explore and demonstrate these.

Social Implications.

There are, of course, many reasons why some Gas elect or even prefer to marry or get married by this form of marriage rather than by any of the other forms known to the customary law. Some of these reasons include the social prestige or esteem which such marriage imports and which it confers not only on the couple who contract it, but also on the issue of that marriage, male or female. One could imagine the extent or measure of that prestige at a time when that form of marriage ruled the age as the most grandiose or the form one could derive the greatest pride from, when marriage under the [p.141] ordinance had not been introduced to compromise part of the prestige that used to attend it.

Frankly, in an earlier article we were inadvertent in our comment on the heydays of the six-cloth marriage when we described it in rather slighting terms as follows:

"The true effect of this kind of marriage among the Gas is no more than the social prestige it confers on the wife and offspring of that marriage: a social prestige which is merely for self-glorification or exaltation in personal standing."4

This instant article will now endeavour to expose the other side of the coin and show that with the admitted social prestige go other more solid rights and incidents thereof.

Hereditary Office

With hindsight, it is now possible to avow that the six-cloth marriage has other more solid results or effects for both the marriage bond itself and the offspring therefrom than the ephemeral one of social prestige. The most important of these more solid effects for the issue of the marriage is the assurance it gives that out of those issue shall immediately or mediately (as the case may be) come forth the constitutional, political or hereditary successors to the founder of the particular lineage.5

Except for very compelling reasons, it is a breach of customary norm to bypass issue of the six-cloth marriage or to prefer others to them. Sometimes consideration for the six-cloth status of an issue is taken to such lengths that even where the issue happens to be a female, precedence is given to her in selections of candidates to occupy a constitutional office which a female is not expressly disqualified from holding. And even where she is personally disqualified, she is competent to supply a male issue of hers to take her place. This is one area where the usual male hegemony in Ga society may give way to female primacy. There are a few such instances in Ga history.6 In such a case the elders or principal members of a family first consult with her for a   [p.142] suitable candidate and if none is available from amongst her own issue, they concede to her the right to nominate a non-six-cloth member of her lineage or family to occupy the office concerned. In this case, her status is not dissimilar to that of the Akan queenmother in the selection of candidates for Akan stools. Like the Akan queenmother, however, her nomination too could be overruled if it was frivolous or manifestly unsuitable according to customary norms.

Permanent Indefeasible Dependency and Security

The third solid implication for the six-cloth marriage (which has had a chequered history7 in the higher courts of Ghana) is the permanent or indefeasible dependency of the issue of such marriage. According to customary norms the said issue are entitled as of right to eat of the resources and enjoy the landed estate of their father durante vita and to pass over their patrimony during their turn to their own issue and the latter's issue. This is what has been aptly called permanent dependency. Where there is only one indivisible interest which it is desirable or imperative that only one of the issue should hold or enjoy, primacy is and will be accorded to one of the six-cloth issue and it is not open to the issue of any other form of customary marriage to contest or query the tenancy of such estate or accommodation or the choice of the incumbent. It is for this reason that one of the children of six-cloth status is usually invited and appointed to occupy a room once used by the founder of a family and then he becomes the pater familias or head of that family eventually.

A further implication is that by and in virtue of the aforementioned permanent dependency attaching to the six-cloth status, it is contrary to Ga custom for other members of a family to attempt to deal with or alienate a family asset, interest or estate without consulting the six-cloth issue; for if it were otherwise, their six-cloth status would be of no consequence and could easily be defeated or stultified. It is for this reason that one finds the Ghana Law Reports replete with legal battles commenced by or on behalf of six-cloth issue of persons who have died intestate.8 They are battles justified by [p.143] Ga custom. Not being abreast with this incident of the six-cloth marriage in the past, the courts and jurists often slighted this right or gave it a derogatory interpretation, denied its substantiality simpliciter,9 or approbated and reprobated thereon.10 Yet it is amongst the chief reasons for contracting the six-cloth marriage and why it is so highly placed, valued and greatly respected amongst the Gas in general and the Ga-Mashi peoples in particular.

It assures unrivalled security to both the wife and the issue of such marriage. By reason of this, the issue are not subject to the inadequately examined condition that they could only enjoy accommodation in their father's self-acquired house subject to good behaviour; for their permanent dependency is indefeasible—save where a special ritual of family severance called tako mlifoo11 involving the slaughter of a sheep with concomitant formalities has been performed by their family. We will presently demonstrate that even the father is estopped  from attempting to defeat this right to a permanent dependency ex post mortem by will. It is not open to another person in office as a successor or in loco parentis to defeat the right of permanent dependency of six-cloth issue or of their mother by means of that custom of family severance.

So far as the six-cloth wife is concerned, she enjoys in common with her issue  both social prestige and security. She was even treated with a measure of veneration, the effect being that any person who ventured in the past to commit adultery with her was done to death by decapitation, drowning or was disciplined by banishment. Later, under more benign influence, the death penalty was commuted to a heavy fine that often resulted in condemning the guilty to servitude either permanently or temporarily.

The incident of security enjoyed by the six-cloth wife was even carried to such lengths that it appeared to fetter her in a number of ways. She could not easily divorce her husband. In point of fact, it was rare for such a notion to be entertained in connection with such couple. Whatever happened, save for conduct that imperilled the life of the other partner, divorce was not entertained; every effort was made to patch up the couple or do away with or contain the cause of their misunderstanding. As aforesaid, an adulterer might be banished, enslaved, drowned or decapitated just to appease an irate husband married to such a wife. Even where such a wife was found in adultery she was made either to slaughter a sheep to appease her husband or was decapitated along with the adulterer, if she was once married to a traditional office holder, for disregard of her assumed sanctity.

[p.144]

It would seem strange to the more modern permissive society that traditional society should have placed such a great premium on moral virtue; but having regard to the absence then of a standing police institution it seemed proper to set such lofty standards, if peace and harmony were to be more easily preserved. It was also necessary for social stability to set up such paragons of virtue for the youth to admire and emulate.

Child Custody and Disposition

The six-cloth wife was affianced on purpose to reproduce for the man12 and therefore was fettered in the extent of the right that she could exercise in respect of the custody of her own issue who were above three years of age. At all material times, her children were deemed to belong in a proprietary and all other senses to her husband, and he alone or his family had the power of disposition over these issue. Ceteris paribus, females amongst them could only be properly given in marriage by the husband or a person standing in his place in loco parentis. The issue were subject to customary and practical duties in the husband's family. As a corollary to the latter, they were entitled to enjoy any right legally belonging to their father's family or to their father himself.13 In the past, her issue could be customarily "arrested" and forcefully installed into a traditional office vacated by a member of her husband's family. Before the advent of emancipation of slaves, it was her husband or his family which had the right to condemn her issue or any of them into slavery for such intolerable acts of misconduct as operated to prejudice the family's prestige or survival, or where it was necessary to provide a pawn for a family pledge or other obligation.

Another fetter that attached to her status was the strict limitation on her freedom of choice of another man as a husband both during her husband's lifetime and after his death. If she happened to elope with or transfer her affections to another man during the husband's lifetime and during coverture, the husband had the right to trace her and recapture her back to his own [p.145] home. Even if the adulterer had had children with the six-cloth wife, it used to lie within the competence of the six-cloth husband to take custody of such issue, abolish their putative or natural father's names and to give them patronymic names of his own.14 Recent decisions of the courts seem rightly to frown15 on the exercise of such right by the husband for its harshness, but this cannot obscure the fact that such used to be the true custom in vogue, which even now survives in the practices of many Ghanaian communities up to the present.16 For the same reason, all the issue of the six-cloth wife are deemed to be legitimate, however born, including the issue of illicit unions.

We have seen the fetter on her right of choice during coverture. More so, death was not allowed to dissolve automatically the marital bond that had linked her to her husband personally because it was also deemed to link her to the husband's paternal family. The consequence of this is that she was still liable to be given in levirate marriage (Kunayere gblahamo) to a member of the  husband's family either on his father's or mother's side, depending on the availability of a suitable choice amongst the husbands cognates. Here the strictly patrilineal principle gives way to the operation of a cognatic principle whereby the selection of the successor husband could be made from either the paternal or the maternal family of the deceased husband. With growing individualism, this practice is now more honoured in its breach than in its observance. Nevertheless it is still persistently observed as a formality. However, few now take advantage thereof to marry in fact.

The use of the phrase "liable to be given in levirate marriage" might create the misleading impression that a six-cloth wife is forced into a match with her late husband's kinsman against her will. That is far from being the case, for both society and the widow recognise the levirate as an insurance against the insecurity and prolonged loneliness of widowhood and so most widows per se like it rather than resent it.

Irrebutable Presumption of Paternal Family Membership

From what has been set out so far, it is evident that it was inevitable for Ga custom to presume an issue of a six-cloth marriage, so tightly integrated with and entrenched in the father's family and its fortunes to be an undisplaceable member of the man's family with indefeasible interests such as the court recognised for once in Ankrah v Odamtten17 where the court pointedly observed 18:

[p.146]

"The house in question was the self-acquired property of George Aryee Odamtten, deceased, who died intestate...

The defendants herein are some of the customary children of the deceased. It appeared from the evidence that they are, by the custom of their locality in the Ga division members of their father's family and as such have indefeasible interests in the estate of the deceased with the head of the family. . ."

(Author's emphasis.)

In Mills v Addy19 the court opined that:

"The contraction of a marriage under Marriage Ordinance (or in Accra of a customary Ga six-cloth marriage) by a male life tenant followed by procreation of children within such a marriage in no way affects this disposition of the property, for such children are not members of the family to which their father belonged and although they have rights in their father's self-acquired property they can thus have no rights in the family property.''

To the extent that the above impliedly defined a Ga father's family as his maternal one, it is with respect misconceived in true Ga Mashi norm and so the conclusion drawn therefrom as to children's interests in a father's family property cannot be accurate. Ankrah v Odamtten is therefore to be preferred to Mills v Addy for it was the former that drew the correct inference from the presumption of family membership by the children of a father. Mills v Addy was inconsistent with this presumption.

In the earlier case of Lutterodt v Solomon20 the nexus between marriage, presumably a six-cloth match, and membership of a father's family was projected and underscored by Deane CJ, recounting the history of the case before him as follows21:

"On 30th December 1930, the Tribunal delivered judgment declaring that as lawful issue of a lawful marriage according to native customary law plaintiffs were members of the family of the deceased. . ."

The reference in the passage to the "deceased" was a reference to the plaintiffs' late father and this underscores the family to which the issue were recognised to be affiliated. Such membership of a father's family is the very basis for the rights of support, accommodation, dependency, custody, etc and the duties attaching thereto which had been the concern of the courts, and not in spite of it, as has otherwise been held without customary warrant.

In this connection, it is important to examine a few misconceptions which [p.147] find expression in Okoe v Ankrah22 from the passage which reads23 :

"Some attempts have been made in Accra to formulate something more definite, to say that in such remote possibility of the maternal family completely failing, children constitute the family. . ."

Ollennu J (as he then was) found the just-mentioned position to be erroneous and, for reasons other than those upon which Ollennu J founded himself, it is correct to say that the "something more definite" formulated by the votaries was misconceived. But while Ollennu J rightly faults those votaries for advocating that children alone constitute the family "in such remote possibility of the maternal family failing completely",  we part ways with him in his opinion that a Ga person's family for succession purposes was in anyway his or her maternal family.

Here it must be pointed out that both the critic and the criticised, with great respect, went astray in the respective stand points they adopted. Both proceeded on the false premises of a Ga family system founded upon matrilineal principles; but note that these are only partially supported by Ga Custom and usage. Their oversight is due to a failure to recognise that the paternal principle is the mainspring or fulcrum of Ga social organisation, as climaxed by the agnatic-orientated rules for appointment of candidates to substantive family rights and offices. Their error also involves a want of recognition of the male hegemony on which Ga society hinges. It is a failure to realise that while ab initio Ga society proceeds upon paternal principles, it is only subsequently, and not antecedently, that it evolves into a constitution founded upon those mixed principles called the cognatic principle—drawing upon both agnatic and uterine relations to constitute a heritable class which jurists have peculiarly called "family." But even at the mixed patrilineal and matrilineal stage of evolution of the original agnatic family, it often still happens to be organised or constituted, not as a matriarchy (abusua), but rather as a patriarchy governed by paternal right. This is shown by the fact that the head of family is often elected or approbated by virtue of such paternal right. Male hegemony is also evident in the concession or admission customarily made by uterine kinsmen within a family that it is the agnatic members who have the upper hand or the controlling (or bigger) voice in the affairs of that family. An observer able to read between the lines also sees paternal right in several areas of Ga social activity and usages. Instances covering a wide spectrum of Ga society wherein an unmistakable paternal right is evinced in some of these are, the patronymic names which  most Gas bear, the Ga father's or his family's power and right to give away, in normal cases, a daughter in marriage, the criteria for eligibility to [p.148] traditional or sensitive positions and dignities, the Gas' traditional obligation to observe their fathers' taboos and perform their paternal rites including such puberty rites as dipo, otsentse, bladzoo, akorkor-suehsueh, etc; as well as their nuptial rites. Those are performed according to the Ga subject's paternity.24

Paternal principles are also evident in the husband's power over his wife's issue of three years of age and above, a usage which prima facie settles custody in the husband's favour. In traditional worship, we see it in the right to pour libation, hold sacred/sensitive positions and officiate generally. In kpodziemo custom, we see it in the share-out of monetary gifts into a ratio of two-thirds for the husband and one-third for the wife. On funerary occasions, we see it in the share of expenses to the deceased's paternal and maternal kinsmen, respectively; the paternal family's power over the corpse and its right to decide where to lay it in state for the paying of final respects. We see it in the determinants of kingship affiliation; in inheritance matters, we see it in the precedence of full brothers or sisters over uterine ones; and it is also evident in the old practice of plamatsemo whereby a group of young men, usually agnatically related, used to stow away from their ancestral homes and set up settlements to which their paramours eloped or were enticed for what in Aggrey v Aggrey25 Archer J (As he then was) called "conjugal solace, succour, comfort and consortium and sexual satisfaction which all virile husbands expect from their wives."

Ga custom abhors polyandry but tolerates polygamy, thus showing pristine male right as against a female right to take on more than one spouse, granting that this right is not unique to patrilineal societies. Nevertheless even in matrilineal societies, the role of the male is preferred in certain circumstances.

Our statement that "unlike in Akan society in Ga Society premium is placed on paternity and it is the key to the determination of most rights; social, political and proprietary"26 might have sounded rather categorical to the non-initiate; but there can be no two minds that a close study or insight into the above wide-ranging areas of the Ga social milieu could yield no [p.149] other inference than that very one conclusion to the objective scholar possessed of the requisite originality and freedom of thought and initiation who, in the language of Lord Wright, is "the master and not the slave of his authorities."27

We are therefore entitled to sum up thus: Ga society starts off primarily on agnatic/paternal principles and not upon those maternal/uterine principles on which Akan society is organised. However, when subsequently (and only subsequently) it comes face to face with the realisation, compelled by natural evolution, that human society cannot perpetually continue segregated into watertight compartments of agnates all alone and abusua (matrilineal relations) all alone, it compromises for a constitution which spells out cognatic or mixed principles. Thus, where a family starts its origin with Tettey, Tetteh and Mensah being three full brothers as the only members, on their defunct survived by their sons and daughters, the latter with their own descendants, male and female, will generally be accorded recognition by Ga custom as members of the weku (family) of the founding fathers, although stricto sensu the issue of the daughters are first and foremost members of their own father's paternal families. This recognition is empirically verifiable and it would be futile to attempt to contest or deny this unpreventable phenomenon of natural evolution.

After all, the family as an organic unit, which all the time undergoes changes by deaths, births and severances, is not supposed to be static at all. A typical example of a family's evolution is provided by the Ankrah family of Otublohum (Ga Mashi) founded by three brothers: Mantse Ankrah and his brothers Ayi and Okanta whose descendants now form that family.28 Since this stage and circumstances of a family are an irresistible function of natural evolution and not the making of the native courts, it does not seem to be a well-taken criticism for the court in Okoe v Ankrah29 to state that:

"A careful examination of each of these cases reveals however, that the tribunal and the native courts were making desperate attempts to change the custom of succession in Accra from matrilineal to a half way house between matrilineal and patrilineal families."30

It goes without saying that the court in the above passage assumes that the starting point of Ga society is the matrilineal beat. An overview of the sign-posts of Ga social and customary practices as already shown evidently exposes the fundamental  fallacy of that pernicious assumption for which the courts must, it is respectfully submitted, now sing the requiescat in pacem.

[p.150]

Otherwise, it would be an upside down reading of all that true Ga culture projects, just as the West African Court of Appeal unfortunately did in Captan v Ankrah. Having said this, the caveat must also be filed that it is feasible for certain self-made and outstanding women to found families, and there are real life instances, where families have been founded by women and where uterine principles prevail in certain Ga houses. But that is the exception rather than the rule of Ga Mashi in particular and in Ga society in general.

The above false assumption apart, the criticism against the native courts continued in the foregoing passage from Okoe v Ankrah seems all the more misplaced and meaningless for those bodies were being true to their judicial oaths by doing no more than faithfully interpreting the evidence of de facto situations existing in the particular families that actually appeared before them, as their oath of office enjoined and expected of them; but they were not trying, as the learned judge would have wished them to do, to lay down categorical imperatives, applicable as immutable law to each and every family irrespective of its unique history or posture at any point in time. Such honest construction or deductions founded upon the evidence, as judicially properly expected of such tribunals, cannot therefore be properly faulted as "desperate attempts" by them, for after all, as we have seen, it was society itself which "enacted" what these judicial bodies interpreted. Thus the supposed deviation cannot rightly be blamed on them if any blame was properly attributable at all to them, which is denied.

The last misconception of relevance in this connection is the opinion which the learned judge himself expressed in that case in a supposed effort to confute others whom he rightly believed, though for untenable reasons, to have misdirected themselves. He said31:

"In my opinion the principle applicable to succession in Accra upon failure of the maternal family is exactly the same as that which applies in any other matrilineal system, eg. among the Akans32 that is that in such eventuality children come within the group of persons, who constitute the family for purposes of succes- [p.151] sion, but they alone do not succeed."

(Author's emphasis.)

Apart from the unsoundness of the matrilineal premise upon which the above opinion is founded, we must also point out that the assumption that "children come in" only when a maternal family has failed shows a fundamental failure to recognise that in Ga culture, children are  procreated chiefly with intent to ensure the very succession of the parents and hence the joy and congratulations that are gleefully expressed at the performance of the kpodziemo custom with the fulfilment of that expectation. The issue, who are thus brought into being to fulfil that very objective, cannot therefore be meaningfully said to "come within the group who constitute the family for purposes of succession" only when a relevant maternal family has failed.

It is difficult to appreciate how an issue procreated just to fulfil the purpose of natural succession can be said to come within a group entitled only after an extinction of a maternal or even a paternal family.

The better statement of the position would be to aver that the issue are actually by natural right within the heritable class and are so within, right from the date of their birth; but customarily so from the day of their kpodziemo (outdooring) when they are ritually inducted as full-fledged members not particularly of their maternal family,  but more so of their paternal family: whose name is given to them in the same way that a club gives its members their prized membership cards by which they afterwards become universally known. The principle involved is not dissimilar to the status brought into being when a university awards degrees to its graduants. The award distinguishes them as graduates of the particular institution. It is for the same rationale that practitioners' letterheads and complimentary cards distinguish them as being of certain Inns of Courts.33

Rights of Issue of the Marriage

Both the issue of six-cloth marriages and those of marriages other than six-cloth matches enjoy an equality of right of support. This ex facie admits of no qualification whatsoever. But a deeper study of the areas of discrimination entertained by Ga custom in favour of six-cloth children seems to suggest that that custom treats the interests of issue of a six-cloth marriage as of greater moment, graver, more profound in degree, more pervasive and permanent than it treats the interests of issue of those marriages not being six-cloth ones. That prima facie would seem to be the foundation for giving positions of importance in civic, social and constitutional life to such issue in preference to the non-six-cloth issue of the same father. As such positions often carry with them avenues to personal wealth, power, greater prestige and right of enjoyment of any estate attached [p.152] to those positions, it would seem to be a mere verbal contradiction of social reality to continue to hold that the principle of equality of right as once espoused  by us34 was consistently followed in practice. It seems admittedly to be more honoured by breaches thereof than by its observance. For how else could one honestly construe the evidence that, where there is only one indivisible interest to be enjoyed, such as a single room, it is given as a matter of course to a six-cloth issue, to the prejudice of or in postponement of the issue of an informal marriage? Why is it that positions that involve substantive family rights are virtually restricted to tenure by six-cloth issue who thereby automatically postpone the issue of an informal marriage?  The reader is left to make his own deductions. But it seems a consideration of the following incidents also tends to give more glaring weight to the impression that six-cloth rights are more entrenched.

Meanwhile, it must be stated that we do not share the narrow view of those jurists and the courts which have held that the rights of support are the only rights to which six-cloth issue are entitled.35 They do enjoy more, as has been demonstrated above.

Rights of Testation

It has been the fashion of some courts and jurists36 inspired by the egalitarian and libertarian conceptions of the common law, to recognise or assume the existence of an unfettered right of testation in any individual who, durante vita, acquired some property by his own individual effort. Little consideration is given by that assumption to the extent to which some native wisdom, in its determination to give permanent, solid and inalienable security to a wife and children, has so compromised and conditioned other facets of a couple's life as to yield one irresistible conclusion and no other, to wit:  that since recognition is given to a right of permanent dependency inherent in the six-cloth issue, it is irreconcilable with a concept espousing the plenary right of the father of such children to make a will bequeathing or devising all his property away from the very children whose right to a permanent dependency is rightly deemed by Ga customary law to be assured. Undoubtedly, if in one breath Ga customary law holds out the rights of six-cloth children to be entrenched, the same  law cannot reasonably or consistently hold those very entrenched rights to be alienable without any restriction.

[p.153]

Though no court37 has been able as yet to perceive the constraint necessarily implied and imposed by other facets of the six-cloth marriage on a father's testamentary capacity, it seems that in the future, armed with the benefit of the facts unfolded here, it would not be difficult for any court to realise that the other implications hitherto expounded could yield no other conclusion than that the testamentary capacity of a Ga man, who married under the six-cloth custom, becomes automatically limited by these conditions, once he has an issue surviving this six-cloth wife.

That such a limitation by custom on testamentary competence is a reality of life rather than a mere ideal is supported by Professor David Hughes Parry when he observed in his book as follows38:

"We have tended perhaps in modern times to regard succession under a will as normal or natural and intestate succession as exceptional or accidental. Indeed, at one time to die intestate other than in cases of sudden death was regarded with horror, because it was practically tantamount to dying unconfessed. . .

In early communities, however, a will was something very different from what it became later in law. It took the form of a conveyance or transfer which was neither secret, ambulatory nor revocable. It was made in contemplation of impending death and took effect at once. However, it was an abnormal occurrence involving the diverting of property from the family or group to whom by custom it would normally have passed and its distribution according to the wishes of the testator. This power of diverting was usually limited in its operation to a part only of the disposer's property. It was seldom allowed to interfere with the right of the widow to a definite share or [p.154] of the children to fix proportions of the devolving inheritance.39 Traces of the predominating claims of members of the family still persist in the laws of many modern states in the form of legitim. . ."

(Author's emphasis.)

It is submitted that "it is in the interest of the Republic" that the courts should accord recognition now to such a limitation on the right to make a will where a six-cloth marriage is concerned; otherwise the resultant mischief would be the defeat of all the conditions and restrictions that Ga customary law has designedly and painstakingly laid down to ensure permanent and inalienable security to the six-cloth wife and her issue. The Intestate Succession Law, 1985 (PNDCL 111) has the effect of only restoring partially the latter's rights. That such is the intent and purport of that customary law is further evinced by the last topic which we will now consider hereunder.

Inheritance

It has been shown elsewhere that so far as the Ga Mashi are concerned, the pristine as well as the current customary arrangement for succession purposes when a man, whether survived by children or not, dies intestate is for his immediate or mediate serho (me kyiriba), the next junior uterine brother, ordinarily to succeed him.40 He may or may not also inherit the late senior brother's wife by way of levirate marriage. The late brother's children, however come under his care. He is expected to be their father in much the same way as their natural father had been to his issue.41 This social reality prima facie cuts across the doctrine of nephew inheritance incautiously bandied about by some courts in some Ga Mashi cases, as happened in [p.155] Vanderpuye v Botchway42; Captan v Ankrah43 and Ankrah v Ankrah.44

The main thrust, purport and inspiration of Ga inheritance are largely incompatible with nephew inheritance for the foregoing and following reasons. When the new positional father dies, he is likewise succeeded by his uterine serho, male or female, seriatim until the patriarchal generation45 is exhausted.46 But that is not what is of immediate interest in this connection. What we here wish to draw attention to is the fact that the children, in particular the six-cloth children, become immediately thereafter reversioners, remainder men and women, residuary legatees (using the term as obtains in matters of common law wills).  In this connection, children are deemed to include nephews and nieces, paternal or uterine. This depends upon the bias prevailing in each particular family. It is impossible, and rash, to attempt to lay down any stereotyped rule here as to whether the nephew or niece must be a paternal or uterine one, for the circumstances and usages tend to differ in this respect from family to family. Hence the need for caution.

This class of children eventually step into the position left vacant by the "fathers", meaning their natural father and his siblings. Such was the pristine custom and it is wrong therefore to postulate, as was done in Omane v Poku 47 [p.156] and other cases, that children become successors to a father only by reason of failure of a father's maternal family. In the Ga experience, whether or not a paternal or maternal family survives, children do succeed as of right when the father and his uterine siblings have passed away. They even become entitled at once to succeed if, besides being of six-cloth status, they have been made to observe the father-burial custom. Their right of succession in this respect is independent of the continued existence vel non of a family, paternal or maternal. It just naturally happens to be their natural turn and not a matter of concession at all. For this reason, it must be underscored that the whole gamut of chapter 27 of Ollennu's book48 dealing with the failure of the maternal family is quite irrelevant in both its philosophy, doctrine and concept, so far as Ga Mashi is concerned.

Tsefuu Custom and Succession

In Ga custom, one of the solemn obligations of adult children is to "bury their father": tsefuu. This means that they are bound, as a matter of custom, to provide the most important things to assure the most decent burial for their father on his passing away from this life. The most important things for this purpose are, of course, the shroud (or nowadays his suit) and the coffin in which he would lie entombed. These may be commuted to cash. As is to be expected, the six-cloth children have a special duty in this matter. If the father was lucky enough to have had daughters with responsible husbands, such husbands, by Ga, unlike Fanti custom, are expected (even obliged) to contribute to the expenses of the burial. They are therefore customarily notified to come to fulfil their obligation to "bury their father-in-law", an obligation they cannot escape even if the deceased was the mother-in-law: for the same obligation operates. Curiously, Fanti custom relieves the son-in-law or daughter-in-law of this duty.

A Ga husband too is bound to "bury" his deceased wife, ie provide her coffin and shroud. In both Ga and Fanti custom and practice, the husband could be sued, ridiculed and mocked if he failed to do this last honour to his wife. He is however relieved of this duty if he is old, improvident and possessed of responsible adult issue. In the latter situation, his own family [p.157] would meet with such adult issue and discuss the father's physical and financial predicament or plight and invite contributions to save their father's face before his wife's or perhaps their mother's family. They usually gladly oblige by responding gleefully and with pride in their breasts for being able to rise to the occasion. Where their father has predeceased their mother, the duty to bury their mother also devolves in Ga custom on the adult "children" on the mother's death.

When, however, it is the father who predeceases the wife or whose burial  is in point, the children gleefully respond to the paternal family's invitation to "bury their father" for a special reason. Ga custom is: moni fuor kpititse le, lenonn eklo-or etokotai, meaning: "he who buries a leper is as of right the very one who is entitled to his sandals." The invitation to the children to bury their father, therefore, carries the implication that they are those whom the paternal family have recognised and will support as eligible to inherit the father's properties, real or personal. Evidently, this is the same philosophy that inspires the Akan custom allocating the wife's burial to her abusua, as Danquah highlighted. It is therefore an obtuse statement of Ga custom to aver categorically that a "family" or even the serho (kyiriba) succeeds automatically when the children have dutifully observed the father's burial custom. In this event, the children must succeed, not mediately, but at once. The oversight of the jurists in this respect is remarkable and very pernicious.

If such eligible children are too young to look after their own affairs, their father's brother, cousin or adult nephew could be appointed to be their social father, but one must hasten to underscore the fact that such appointment does not entitle the appointee to the beneficial enjoyment of the property of the deceased, as has been misunderstood all along the line. The correct position is that he is expected to husband the property of the deceased in such a way as to enable him to care for the children and widow committed to his charge; but, where the children are immediately qualified without any interregnum, they are entitled to enter upon the beneficial enjoyment of their patrimony as soon as possible after the funeral obsequies are over. But where they are not sui juris and it becomes as such necessary for a social or positional father like the Ewe tefenola to be appointed over them, they have to wait but are entitled afterwards on attaining their maturation to respectfully approach such social father of theirs to formally hand over their patrimony to them after paying the expenses made for their father's burial. This is especially the case where the issue of the deceased are of the six-cloth status; but children not of the six-cloth who have been burdened with the father-burial custom also become in Ga custom equally entitled to demand a delivery to them of their patrimony, or to stem social family conflict they might wait until the generation is over and then assert their inherent right, otherwise it is possible for an adult-biased court to cite at them a countervailing maxim of Ga customary law: yitso taashi ni nakutso abu fai, ie "it is improper for the knee to wear a hat when the head sits down"; and on this footing discountenance [p.158] their righteous claim.

It is submitted that the foregoing conditions or implications of the six-cloth marriage demonstrate, import and postulate, that so far as a Ga six-cloth father is concerned, his right of testation is restricted to making provision or dispositions amongst his wife and children on the one hand, and on the other, his collaterals who would  upon intestacy have been otherwise his customary successors. For, if a customary law prohibits easy family severance and divorce to its issue and spouse, and fetters and constrains them in the diverse ways above mentioned to continue with a man's family, it forcefully stands to reason, and it is self-evident, that that customary law must make abiding and inalienable provision for them for truly, "native law consists in the performance of the reasonable in the particular circumstance." That, on an adequate view, is what is spelt out by the concept of (a) children's paternal family affiliation; (b) the entrenchment of their right to a permanent dependency; (c) the paternal family's right to their custody and disposition; (d) the severe restrictions imposed on the six-cloth wife's freedom of choice of even a new husband after the original husband's death; (e) the prestige; (f) reverence attributed to her status whether under coverture or not; and (g) the duty to bury their father. All these do combine to show that the man, husband or father, who so freely chooses to fetter or position such wife and issue of his, is estopped by conduct from acting in a way that could defeat the expectations and implications of the veriest customary law above expounded. Equally so is his family, who contracted the six-cloth marriage for him, estopped from contradicting its implications.

It is submitted therefore that the rider that a Ga father's right to make a will, including samansiw, is impliedly restricted has been amply demonstrated quod erat demonstrandum. A Ga father thus cannot lawfully disinherit his six-cloth issue by will or by samansiw, nor even by means of gift inter vivos,49 until and unless the twar kar or tako mlifoo (family custom) has been ceremonially performed.
