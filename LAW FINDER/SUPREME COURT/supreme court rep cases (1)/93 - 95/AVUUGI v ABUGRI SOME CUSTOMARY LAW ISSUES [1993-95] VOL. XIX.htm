AVUUGI v ABUGRI: SOME CUSTOMARY LAW ISSUES [1993-95] VOL. XIX  252—262.

 MENSA-BONSU H J A N

 

*

INTRODUCTION

Customary law has always been an area of the law with which the courts seem to be ill at ease. Whilst some judges and lawyers are intent on modernising the law, others are content to apply the customary rules as they are.1 However, for both the conservative and modernising schools of thought there appears to be one common ground, namely the point from which one may proceed to modernise or preserve customary law. The content of a rule must first be known before a modernising influence can be brought to bear upon it. Hence, neither school of thought would find defensible any attempt at modernisation by a judge without first ascertaining the rule.

Another issue that arises is what may be described as the "Akanising" approach whereby rules of custom are held to exist in non-Akan communities merely because Sarbah wrote about their existence among the Fantis. This attitude which has been criticised over a long period by scholars2 gives cause for concern not only because it shows disrespect for the customs of the non-Akans but also because it creates rules which have never been known by the people who are supposed to practice them.3

It is against this background that the decision in Avuugi v Abugri4 by Essilfie-Bondzie J (as he then was) is being examined. This case attempts to look at the issues raised within the modern Ghanaian decision-making context. Although it was decided under the old Courts Act, 1971,5 it is still relevant today because the provisions on ascertainment of customary law have been re-enacted under the new Courts Act, 1993. 6

[p.253]

The facts of Avuugi v Abugri are not complex by any standards. The plaintiff and the defendant, both Kusasi men, hailed from the same village — Teshie in the Upper East Region. One day in August 1986 the plaintiff's wife, Faustina, went from Teshie village to the Zebilla market. When returning home at night later that day, she met the defendant on the way. The defendant was also on his way to the village so they (obviously) kept each other company. The defendant started making advances at Faustina. She offered some resistance but her resistance was overcome by the defendant. Consequently, the defendant had sexual intercourse with her. There was no firm finding as to whether her resistance was overcome by force or verbal persuasion. On her return home, she reported the matter to her husband — the plaintiff. He took the matter up and as a result, the defendant and his elders were informed that in accordance with custom they had to purify the woman before the plaintiff could take her back as his wife.

The defendant refused to perform the custom. The plaintiff, who was thus compelled by custom to either divorce the woman or purify her and take her back, opted to perform the purification ceremony. His reason for performing the custom of purification himself was that he had three children by the woman and therefore did not wish to see the marriage dissolved. The purification ceremony involved the provision of two sheep, one cock, a new cloth and beads for the wife and some pito. These were provided at a total cost of ¢20,000. It was to claim the cost of the items bought for purification, as well as general damages for seduction, that the action against the defendant was instituted.

The trial judge awarded ¢10,000 for each of the two heads of damages and ¢500 costs. The instant appeal was brought because the defendant-appellant contended, inter alia, that the award of damages for seduction had no basis in Kusasi customary law and also that what had been awarded was inordinately high.

The appellate court, presided over by Essilfie-Bondzie J (as he then was), dismissed the appeal on all the grounds. Of interest in this case note, however, is the basis for dismissing the challenge to the existence of the Kusasi customary law rule and also the basis for the award of the damages. The defendant had contended first, that the sanction under Kusasi customary law was the performance of purification rites by the adulterer and not general damages as awarded by the trial court. Secondly, that in principle, damages in the area of seduction are not meant to be punitive but a palliative for injured feelings. His lordship, in dismissing the grounds of appeal, began by stating at 519 that "It is now established law that damages for seduction is known to our customary law." He then cited as the authority, Dapah v Madzigbe7 a case decided by Francois J (as he then was) in which damages were awarded to the plaintiff for the seduction of his wife. He again cited

 [p.254]

Asante v Wiredu a case decided by Apatu-Plange Ag J who noted that8:

“ . . . the main basis for payment of damages in customary law for seduction is for the dishonour and disgrace brought upon the family of the girl seduced. That being the case it stands to reason that in the interest of uniformity and on the ground of principle, the same rule ought to apply, so that customary law should also hold a man who has seduced another person’s wife, liable to pay damages to the husband for the injury done to his feelings, honour and pride.”

Armed with these two cases, his lordship held that the ground of appeal challenged the existence of Kusasi customary law should fail. In the first place, there was an indication that the two cases cited above had anything to do with Kusasi customary law. Therefore, to use the expression “our customary law” without indicating whose customary law was being referred to, is misleading. Although the assimilation of a rule of customary law by the common law of Ghana has been made a possibility,9 no steps have as yet been taken in this direction by the National House of Chiefs—the body entrusted with that function. Customary law thus remains rules of law which are by custom applicable to particular communities in Ghana. It should therefore make a difference when the parties are from an ethnic group different from those covered by precedent. Secondly, the conclusion drawn by Apatu-Plange Ag J in Asante v Wiredu was not unassailable. Even though his lordship obviously agreed with that conclusion, it would have been helpful if the basis of the agreement had been indicated.

Thus, the cases relied upon were not particularly helpful either in establishing the basis for the action under Kusasi customary law or the award of damages. There was therefore not enough reason to hold that “ . . . the parties in the instant case are bound by the customary law as stated above.”10

Choice of Law

Under the Courts Act, 1971,11 under which the case was decided, a court faced with a problem in a tort issue may, in deciding the applicable law, be guided by any of the following:

(1) There may be a reference to the intention of the parties express or implied as to the system of law that should govern the transaction.

(2) The personal law of the parties ought to be applied where they [p.255] are subject to the same law.

(3) Where the parties are not subject to the same personal law, a mixture of the rules of their different systems as would achieve a result conformable to natural justice, equity and good conscience ought to apply.

(4) Where rules of personal law of the parties provides no assistance, the court shall apply principles of common law or customary law so as to achieve a just result.

(5) In any issue arising from common law or customary law, the court may fashion appropriate remedies from any system of law which would be efficacious in conformity with requirements of justice, equity and good conscience.

The court may thus resort to the personal law of the parties or fashion out a remedy. Presumably, where the court decides to do the latter, there should be some indication that the court is embarking upon such an enterprise. The question is whether the court may fashion its own remedy whilst giving the impression that it is actually applying the personal law of the parties. In the interest of the proper development of our law, it should be clear at any point in time what is the nature of the particular activity being engaged in by the court.

In Avuugi v Abugri, both parties were Kusasi men hailing from the same village. The parties had at all material times indicated that they were operating under Kusasi customary law. Both the trial court and appellate court made no secret of the fact that they were attempting to apply Kusasi customary law. In such a situation, therefore, it would seem that it was the content of the Kusasi customary law that was the real point at issue. The treatment of that issue was therefore less than adequate, seeing that that was the crux of the case.

Ascertainment

The holding in Avuugi v Abugri brings into sharp focus issues relating to the choice of law and ascertainment of the content of customary law rules. Having come to the conclusion that Kusasi customary law was the applicable law, the question then was what the rights of the parties were. This then led to the issue of the ascertainment of the content of a rule of customary raw. The court neatly side-stepped it by relying on judicial precedents in coming to its conclusions although none of the precedents addressed Kusasi customary law per se. The issue here then is, when may a court ascertain the content of a rule as prescribed by the Courts Act?

Section 50(1) of Act 372 (or section 55(1) of Act 459) states:

“(1) Any question as to the existence or content of a rule of customary law is a question of law for the Court and not a question of fact."

[p.256]

This has been held by Edward Wiredu J (as he then was) in Abangana v Akologo12 to mean that although a court may seek expert opinion, it remains the final arbiter and may decline to uphold a custom which is considered unreasonable. Indeed, this seems to be reminiscent of the attitude of Sowah J (as he then was) in Fiaklu v Adjiani13 when he says:

"Whatever be the content of a custom, if it becomes an issue in litigation and the courts are invited to pronounce thereon, any declaration made by the courts supersedes the custom however ancient and becomes law obligatory upon those who come within its confines."

The two learned judges however hardly go far enough to say that the content need not be known if other rules serve the purpose of doing justice between the parties.

In fairness, although those cases were not expressly relied on by Essilfie-Bondzie J (as he then was), the attitude adopted in Avuugi v Abugri appears to misrepresent those expressed sentiments. It is submitted that those cases do not suggest that there is no need to ascertain the content of customary rules, but that those rules, when ascertained, are not sacrosanct. Therefore if a judge finds a rule of custom unreasonable, he could refine it notwithstanding the fact that the practice would continue to be different. This attitude, though criticised for the possibility of creating a "cleavage" between judicial customary law and the customary law in practice,14 does not proceed from the point that the content need not be known.

Edward Wiredu J (as he then was) further makes a point15 which though liable to a misinterpretation, is also consistent with the view that there is a duty to ascertain the content of a rule before the "civilising" influence of the judiciary may be brought to bear upon it. He was considering the issue of whether it was necessary to organise an inquiry every time there was an issue of customary law. The judge considered the provisions of section 50(2) of [p.257] Act 372 which are as follows16:

"(2) If the Court entertains any doubt as to the existence or content of a rule of customary law relevant in any proceedings after considering such submissions thereon as may be made by or on behalf of the parties and consulting such reported cases, textbooks and other sources as may be appropriate, the Court may adjourn the proceedings to enable an inquiry to take place . . ."

(The writer's emphasis.)

From these provisions, the learned judge concludes that there is no obligation to hold an inquiry. He says: "It is clear from the language of section 50 ... that it is only in the circumstances in which a court finds itself under situations covered by subsection (2) . . . that any consideration of resorting to an expert opinion becomes necessary . . ."17 Certainly the conditional tense used there indicated that in the absence of that condition, there would be no obligation. The learned judge rightly pointed out the prescribed condition under the provisions — the issue of the existence of doubt in the court's mind. However, this was done in such a manner as to be liable to the misinterpretation that there is no obligation to hold an inquiry even if by objective standards there is a need to do so. For, stating that the obligation arises only upon a stated condition is not the same as stating that there is no obligation whatsoever.

Whilst it is true that the rule prescribes a certain condition which appears to be a subjective standard, it would seem that the circumstances under which that condition would be regarded as having been met are themselves deserving of some consideration. This is because it is not inconceivable that a judge who ought to consider himself in doubt would nevertheless declare himself not in doubt. The question then is whether the issue of "doubt" is to be determined by a subjective or objective standard.18

In addition, the provisions state that the court must consider submissions by the parties and consult textbooks and other sources as "may be appropriate." This means that if the court considers sources which are inappropriate, the question of "doubt" is not closed as this may be the subject of an appeal. It would seem therefore that the issue of "doubt" ought to be an objective one [p.258] as well. If, for instance, the court, having received contradictory evidence on either side, declares itself not to be in doubt in circumstances in which the basis for rejecting one version of the evidence as opposed to the other is not clear, surely, the issue could not be closed merely because that particular court did not regard itself as being in doubt. Another possible scenario is where the court has cosidered sources which are inappropriate. It is submitted that in both situations it would be proper to apply an objective standard and to hold that the court remained in doubt because the "sources" considered were inappropriate ones.

Once that conclusion is arrived at, it would be difficult to maintain the position that the holding of an inquiry is an optional affair dependent on a particular judge’s discretion. It would seem therefore that although there is no obligation to hold an inquiry if the court entertains no doubt the same cannot be said of other situations.

In Abangana v Akologo, however, the issue was not one of doubt as to the content, but rather as to whether the rule of customary law ought to be applied in the form it stood. It can therefore not be portrayed as an example of how judges who consider themselves to be in doubt, ought to proceed. There was ample evidence before the court as to what the Frafra customary law rule. To require a judge who has ample evidence of what the rule is to hold an inquiry would be an exercise in superfluity. This is a different problem from a consideration of whether there is need to modernise. Edward Wiredu J (as he then was) was therefore right in declaring himself not bound to hold an inquiry.

The confusion of the two issues is also made manifest in Edward Wiredu J’s criticism of Taylor J (as he then was). With the greatest respect, however, the criticism of the mode adopted by Taylor J (as he then was) in Billa v Salifu19 was unfair, though the same may not be said for his refusal to modernise the rule. It is one thing to invite expert opinion on an issue in which the court is in doubt, and quite another thing altogether to apply the outcome of the inquiry. It is unclear whether Edward Wiredu J (as he then was) was criticising the resort to expert opinion or the application of the rules so declared. Clearly, Taylor J (as he then was) was in doubt and did the best under the circumstances. His refusal to “modernise” should therefore be seen as a separate issue from whether or not his decision to hold the inquiry was justified. It was only after he had held the inquiry that, on the facts as reported, he found himself on the same footing as Edward Wiredu J (as he then was) in Abangana v Akologo. Our judges must be encouraged to set the [p.259] records straight on the content of customary rules even if they later refuse to apply them on grounds of "modernisation." It would also make it easier to attack the basis of the claims to modernisation because one would know the take-off point.

Following from the above discussion, it is respectfully submitted that the court in Avuugi v Abugri ought to have been in doubt when the parties joined issue on whether or not there was such a rule under Kusasi customary law. Essilfie-Bondzie J (as he then was) when he proceeded to give the decision, held the parties in the instant case bound by the customary law as stated in Asante v Wiredu. However, in that decision, Apatu-Plange Ag J said:

“ . . . the parties to the suit are all Akans residing at Abetifi Kwahu and for that matter the learned trial magistrate was right in determining the claim by their personal law…”20

Personal law is defined as "the system of customary law to which [a person] is subject   . . ."21 If the part of that decision quoted in the instant case, and relied upon as the authority, was preceded by a reference to the personal law of the parties, it appears to be somewhat awkward when the decision is applied to parties whose personal law is in all probability different. It has been proved by cases such as Billa v Salifu22 that it is not every ethnic group whose customary law permits the recovery of damages for seduction as Akan law does.23 To hold therefore that a man who brings an action under customary law is entitled to damages when the customary law under which he is suing knows no such remedy smacks of "Akanising"—a practice which many scholars find offensive.24

In any case, it would appear that the formulation of the Akan rule to permit a husband to sue for damages for seduction is of recent vintage. Whilst dealing with seduction under Akan customary law, Apatu-Plange Ag J purported to modify the customary law rule when he noted:

"It seems to me therefore that the main basis for payment of damages in customary law for seduction is for the dishonour and disgrace brought upon the family of the girl seduced. That being the case it stands to reason that in the interest of uniformity and on the ground of principle, the same rule ought to apply, so that [p.260] customary law should also hold a man who has seduced another person's wife, liable to pay damages to the husband for the injury done to his feelings, honour and pride."25

(The writer's emphasis.) The learned judge does not go on to explain what kind of "uniformity" or "principle" he means and therefore it is impossible to join issue with him on these matters.

Seduction: A Product of Modern Life-Styles?

Avuugi v Abugri also raises the interesting issue of whether the action for seduction is outdated. Here, there is some ambivalence that is worthy of note. Edward Wiredu J (as he then was) in Abangana v Akologo criticises the opinion of Taylor J (as he then was) in Billa v Salifu for applying an outdated rule. The issue of whether a man whose customary law did not permit him to receive damages for seduction was entitled to an award was in the opinion of Edward Wiredu J (as he then was) not satisfactorily resolved. He was of the opinion that a man “ . . . whose legal right to the privacy of his wife had been invaded," was entitled to damages although the system under which he was suing viewed the act as a crime or at least quasi-criminal. (The writer's emphasis.) His lordship in the next breath criticised Osei-Hwere J (as he then was) for holding in Badu v Boakye26 that a certain Akan custom was not unreasonable. The basis of his criticism was that it “ . . . has the effect of throwing women back into the slave trade days." With due respect, no cause of action smacks more of the "slave trade days" than the action for seduction. If the court is minded to introduce modern ideas into the customary law, then one useful area would be to undermine the customary law notion of women as a species of the husband's property. In a modern era where many women are breadwinners and many are well-educated, it seems odd that women should continue to be seen as pliable objects who are made to bend one way or the other by voracious males. If modern life-styles are different from traditional areas, so ought to be the rules based upon them.

Thus in the mid-1970s when this statement was uttered, it was already anachronistic. It may have served the ends of traditional society to credit women with wisdom and strength of mind for some purposes such as chieftaincy matters, and with a puerile and pliant mind for others. In this present society however, there is no basis for keeping up that fiction that in matters carnal, married women are always led astray by unscrupulous males [p.261] who must then be made to pay for their misbehaviour. How would a court react if evidence showed that far from being the "seducer" the man had been "seduced?" If we are going to don the garb of crusaders, some care must be exercised in the choice of targets. To persist in upholding actions for seduction by husbands has nothing "modern" about it.

The Award of Damages

In the award of damages, the judge in the case under consideration created further problems. Instead of resting the award on the basis that seems to have some judicial support, the learned judge chose to go one better. Dapah v Madzigbe, applying Micah v Plange,27 had expressly stated that damages were to be awarded not according to the depth of the defendant's pocket but according to customary law rules. Having no customary law rules to rely upon, however, did not deter his lordship. Instead, he chose the rather dubious ground of the plaintiff's status as an educated man in that village. He said ". . . the plaintiff being one of the few educated people in the village, adultery committed by his wife with another citizen of the same village is bound to affect his honour and pride . . ." Of all the dangerous suggestions, one would like to believe that his lordship was not suggesting that the more educated men there are in one's village the smaller is the worth of one's honour and pride; or conversely that uneducated men have no honour and pride. Even if his lordship was not minded to reduce the damages, the basis upon which he refused to tamper with the decision of the trial court is with respect, completely untenable.

In the colonial and early post-independence days when there were very few educated people, some amount of education however low, was a status symbol. This is not exactly true of this country now. Again, in a country where feverish efforts are being made to integrate illiterates into the political system, it does seem a little unfortunate that fresh areas of privilege are being created for the educated. If indeed damages for seduction is known to Kusasi customary law, there must be a basis for calculating what is due. This basis would definitely exclude the educational status of the plaintiff. It is a fact that education in some parts of the country is of such recent origin that any rule which purports to be a customary law rule would be suspect if it gave recognition to a person's educational status. Western education has ceased to be the sole measure of a person's worth in this country and must not now be re-introduced under a different guise by the courts.

CONCLUSION

The decision in Avuugi v Abugri therefore throws up some problematic issues of customary law in this country. Not only does it represent the old line of cases in which authorities on Akan customary law were applied [p.262] without discussion, but also the practice of grafting new ideas onto old and often outdated notions underlying particular rules of custom. Lastly, the award of damages based on a person's status as the only educated man in the whole village has a strong flavour of outdated ideas and almost certainly no grounding in customary law, modern or ancient.

If customary law is to be modernised, then it is not enough to modernise the principle if the underlying rationale dates from the "slave trade days." Modernisation must start from the root and not be grafted onto a dying branch. "Akanisation" should not also be disguised as "modernisation." This creates bad feeling on the part of non-Akans for there is no indication that the customary rules of Akans on particular issues are necessarily the most efficient ways of dealing with those issues. If others see the problem differently, respect must be accorded their view. In any case, the formulation of some rules by the courts in a long line of cases as representing Akan law is being challenged.28 We must therefore proceed with caution.

Lastly, a cause of action based often upon the acts of two consenting adults needs to be looked at with some care in the light of changing social circumstances. To borrow the words of Edward Wiredu J (as he then was), rules of customary law which do not take into "consideration ... the impact the present social, political and economic changes in our society [has] had on the customs [ought not to] be accepted without question."29
