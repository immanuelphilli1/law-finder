A CRITIQUE OF SOME ASPECTS OF THE INSURANCE LAW, 1989 (PNDCL 227) [1993-95] VOL XIX 263—269

BUABENG A. G

 

.*

The Insurance Law, 19891 regulates the insurance industry in Ghana. The Law establishes the National Insurance Commission and sets out, very comprehensively, a code of conduct for those engaged in the insurance business. However, in its attempt to regulate the insurance industry in Ghana PNDCL 227 has made some far-reaching changes in some aspects of the common law insurance in Ghana. It is not the intention of the learned writer to touch, in this brief article, on the regulation of the industry in Ghana. The learned writer will only attempt to comment on some aspects of the common law of insurance in Ghana which have been changed by PNDCL 227.

It cannot be gainsaid that common law principles of insurance as applied in Ghana have, at times, produced results considered difficult for insured who are prevented from recovering losses on account of conduct falling short of applicable principles. Some academics have in fact questioned the justification for the continued application of English common law principles to domestic claims given the differing socio-economic circumstances; and have made calls for reforms of those aspects perceived as inimical to the interests of the insuring public. However, the question which must be posed here is whether the reforms effected in the common law of insurance by PNDCL 227 achieve this objective.

As has been pointed out earlier, the learned writer will in this note only address problems raised by the reform of the common law of insurance in Ghana by PNDCL 227. In this connection section 52 (1) of PNDCL 227 is quite apposite. It states as follows:

"52. (1) Notwithstanding anything to the contrary contained in any contract of insurance where an insurance chief agent or agent completes an insurance proposal form or such similar document on behalf of any person, he shall be deemed to be the agent of the insurer and not the agent of the person on whose behalf he completes the proposal form."

Section 52 (1) no doubt drives a coach and a horse through the well-known English case of Newsholme Brothers v Road Transport and General Insurance Co Ltd2 which, inter alia, decided that an agent of an insurance [p.264] company who helps a proposer to fill in a proposal form was merely an agent of the proposer; that the knowledge of the true facts by the agent could not be imputed to the insurance company; and consequently that the company was entitled to repudiate liability on the ground of untrue statements in the proposal form. This case, in spite of the high level of illiteracy in Ghana, was with the greatest respect followed by the Court of Appeal in Guardian Assurance Co Ltd v Khayat Trading Store.3 There were two conflicting English authorities on this particular point: Bawden v London, Edinburgh and Glasgow Assurance Co4 and the Newsholme Brothers case. The Court of Appeal could have tapped a new stream of authority by following the Bawden case. However, the court, in its wisdom, did follow the Newsholme Brothers case and denied the insured the opportunity to claim under the policy. The position of the law, to put it mildly, was most unsatisfactory and not in the best interest of the assured.

In spite of the mordant criticism made against the decision by some academics, the position of the law remained unchanged until the promulgation of PNDCL 227. And with the promulgation of that Law, the Khayat case must now be cast into that special limbo reserved for wrongly decided cases. There is also no doubt that the change in the law will be most welcome and will be no small crumb of comfort to the uninitiated whose proposal form or similar document is completed by an agent of the insurance company.

The problem highlighted above also confronted the Nigerian courts in the case of Northern Assurance v ldugboe5 where an illiterate, capable only of appending his signature, claimed disclosure of certain facts to an agent of the insurer who advised the insured's clerk to omit those facts in the proposal. The insurers repudiated liability on the cover note issued by the agent alleging non-disclosure and misrepresentation of material facts by the insured. The trial judge, finding the alleged facts disclosed to the agent, relied on the case of Bawden v London, Edinburgh & Glasgow, Assurance Co to hold the insurer bound by the knowledge of its agent. The Supreme Court, distinguishing the Bawden case from, and relying on Newsholme Brothers v Road Transport and General Insurance Co as though the facts fell on all fours with the case, held on appeal that in completing the proposal, the agent acted as the agent of the insured only, and that knowledge of the truth could not be imputed to the insurer. This was so though, unlike in the [p.265] Newsholme Brothers case, the agent had authority to issue cover notes on behalf of the insurer. Paradoxically, the court held that the insured's illiteracy was not a ground for departing from the Newsholme Brothers principle. As already indicated, the change of the common law position will be hailed by those who have to complete proposal forms. Any misrepresentation or non-disclosure of material facts by the insurance agent will not be deemed to be that of the assured.

Sections 57(1) and (2) and 58 of PNDCL 227 also introduce some far-reaching changes into the common law of insurance in Ghana. Section 57 states as follows:

"57. (1) A party to a contract of insurance shall not be under an obligation to disclose any fact about which no question is asked by the insurer or his agent.

(2) Notwithstanding subsection (1) of this section, where a party to a contract of insurance with intent to avoid the rejection of the risk by the insurer or the payment of higher premiums, conceals from, or fails to disclose to the other party to the contract any fact which he knows or believes or has reasons to believe to be material to the contract, the contract may be rescinded by that other party."

(The writer's emphasis.) Section 57(1) makes a facile assumption that the duty to disclose is only confined to the assured. It should be observed ad nauseam that nothing can be further from the truth. With the greatest respect, the assumption is totally devoid of merit and was not carefully thought out before it was legislated. Even a critical analysis of the foundation judgment of Lord Mansfield in Carter v Boehm6 and again the judgment of Farwell LJ in Re Bradley and Essex and Suffolk Accident Indemnity Society7 demonstrate that the duty of disclosure applies to both parties to the contract. In other words, a duty of disclosure is on the insurer as much as the insured. It is therefore totally unacceptable to assume, as it is assumed in section 57 (1) and (2) of the Law, that the duty rests only with the assured. This Law was made on 19 September 1989 and the date of the Gazette notification was 29 December 1989. It is pertinent to observe that if the makers of the Law had been abreast with the latest developments in the law of insurance, they would have come across the case of Banque Keyser Ullman S A v Skandia Insurance Co.8 This case, succinctly, demonstrates that the duty of disclosure is on both parties to the contract and not only on [p.266] the insured.

A second criticism of section 57(1) is that it stipulates that a party shall not be under any obligation to disclose any fact about which no question is asked by the insurer or his agent. The point which the present writer wishes to observe is whether the questions are to be asked orally or in writing or put on a proposal form? This is not even made clear by the legislation. What form then are the questions to take? Assuming, without admitting, that the questions are to be asked on a proposal form, it must be appreciated that whilst proposal forms are used in all consumer contracts and certain commercial risks, they are rarely used in marine and aviation insurances.9 The learned writer has had the opportunity to negotiate insurance contracts for the erstwhile State Shipping Corporation (BSL) and Ghana Airways Corporation and on no occasion were proposal forms used in placing the risks. They are rarely used if at all.

Section 57(1) of PNDCL 227 was evidently not carefully thought out and will in practice create more problems for the insurance industry and the courts than it attempts to solve. The subsection will only succeed in muddying the already untidy waters.

Section 57(2) attempts to mitigate some of the rigours of section 57(1); but in so doing, that section introduces more complications into the law and will continue to plague the courts in Ghana with the delicate task of determining the intention of the assured. Surely the intention of the assured will be subjective for, in an attempt by the insurer to avoid the risk, the assured will come up with the defence that he did not intend to avoid the rejection of the risk or the payment of a higher premium, because the burden of determining what he knows or believes or has reasons to believe to be material to the contract is put on the assured. Nice piece of legislation! If one wants to take out a hull policy in marine insurance, would it not be too much to expect the insured in this very technical area to know any fact which he the assured believes to be material? Perhaps what may be material to the assured may not be material to the insurer.

The question to be asked is whether the view of the industry was sought at all in drafting section 57(2). It is regrettable that this piece of legislation is already on the statute books for it will have a devastating effect on the insurance industry. At the end of the day, it will become, to the detriment of the assured, a fertile source of litigation in Ghana; for insurance companies with all the ammunition they have, will always seek the intervention of the courts to determine whether the assured should have disclosed this or that material fact. Thus, a piece of legislation possibly designed to assist the assured may turn out at the end of the day to become a weapon of oppression for the assured.

[p.267]

Section 58 of PNDCL 227 stipulates as follows:

"58. For the purpose of any contract of insurance, a fact shall be deemed to be material if in the circumstances it would be considered material by a reasonable person."

It is absolutely clear from the wording of the section that it applies to every type of insurance. The question one may ask is, who is a reasonable person for the purpose of this section? Is a reasonable person the man on the Clapham omnibus or the man on the Nima trotro? How on earth can a reasonable person, possibly a man on the Nima trotro, determine whether a particular fact is material and should therefore be disclosed? The section, in the learned writer's opinion, has great ramifications for the insurance industry. A fact has always been considered material for the purpose of both non-disclosure and misrepresentation if it is one which would influence the judgment of a prudent insurer in deciding whether or not to accept the risk or what premium to charge. Assuming, for the purpose of argument, that the learned writer wants to take out a hull policy on a ship; how can a reasonable person determine that a particular fact is material and should therefore be disclosed? It is not a matter which should be left to the prudent insurer to determine. Will a reasonable person go to court to give evidence that a particular fact is material and should therefore be disclosed? Will the courts not rather rely on expert evidence from people with immense breadth of experience in the insurance industry for the determination of the materiality of a particular matter? The learned writer will be watching with absorbing interest the sort of interpretation the courts in Ghana will give to this section but it is regretted that the section introduces more complications into the law.

The prudent insurer test is formulated in section 18(2) of the Marine insurance Act of 1906. Even though the Act does not apply to Ghana as a statute of general application, it was the codification of the common law applicable to all insurance contracts as determined in the English Court of Appear in the case of Lambert v Co-operative Insurance Society.10 The prudent insurer test was also adopted by the English Court of Appeal in the much criticised case of CTI v Oceanus Mutual Underwriting Association (Bermuda).11 And here it is to be added that there was a stream of authorities favouring the prudent insurer test at the time PNDCL 227 was promulgated which makes it difficult to fathom why a reasonable person test was adopted as the test for materiality against the weight of all the authorities, including the decision of the English Court of Appeal in CTI International Inc v Oceanus. Was the reasonable person test adopted just to depart from the well-tested common law position? As has been pointed out earlier, this, at [p.268] best introduces complications into the law and much will depend on the judicial interpretation the courts in Ghana will put on the section.

Finally, some brief comments on section 59 of PNDCL 227. Section 59 has somewhat altered the common law of misrepresentation, but the import of the section is far wider than that. On a careful reading of the section, it will be appreciated that it has whittled down the so-called "basis of the contract clause" which is inserted at the foot of proposals warranting the accuracy of answers supplied to be the basis of the contract between the parties and signed by the proposed. The effect is to convert representations in proposals into warranties of existing or future facts entitling the insurer to avoid the policy on account of inaccuracy without reference to the materiality of information obtained or the honesty or care with which it was given. No doubt, this provision will be most welcome by the insureds.

The section also in a way does whittle down the effect of warranties and conditions. Perhaps, the most common reason why insureds in Ghana are denied indemnity upon loss is on account of breach of policy terms described as warranties or conditions, even though such warranties or conditions may not be material to the risk. The wide import of the section notwithstanding anything contained in any contract of insurance presupposes that even if it is material to the risk, the insurer cannot repudiate or avoid the policy on that ground. (The writer's emphasis.) What is difficult to appreciate is why the Law does not explicitly dwell on conditions and warranties in insurance law in general. The approach adopted by PNDCL 227 in dealing with these two areas which have long plagued most common law jurisdictions is not wholly welcome by the learned writer, in whose view, an attempt should have been made to reform extensively the law on warranties and conditions in insurance contracts.

As stated earlier, PNDCL 227 was enacted to regulate the insurance industry in Ghana. In an attempt to regulate the industry some reforms have been made to the common law of insurance in Ghana under section 52 of the Law and this is most welcome. For reasons already addressed, section 59 is also most welcome but it is regrettable that sections 57(1) and (2) and 58 of the Law have introduced more complications into the law of insurance in Ghana. The courts will be faced with the delicate task of interpreting the two sections. Perhaps, it is still too early to speculate on the success of these sections as much will depend on judicial interpretation of the provisions and the attitude towards insurance litigation likely to surround them. However, there is a certain inevitability in the conclusion that the provisions raise more questions than they answer. Some of the provisions reveal a lack of understanding of the basic principles and in areas such as warranties, condi- [p.269] tions and the “basis of the contract clause”, the reforms should have been more far-reaching.
