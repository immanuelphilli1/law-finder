BIGAMY AND ISLAMIC MARRIAGES IN THE LAW OF GHANA: THE LEGISLATOR'S DILEMMA OR STUDIED SILENCE [1993-95] VOL. XIX 69—83

KEN Y YEBOA

 

 

*

INTRODUCTION

In Attorney-General of Ceylon v Reid, Upjohn LJ, delivering the advice of the judicial committee of the Privy Council, noted thus1:

"Ceylon is a country of many races, many creeds and has a number of Marriage Ordinances and Acts...Whatever may be the situation in a purely Christian country (as to which their Lordships express no opinion) they cannot agree that in a country such as Ceylon a Christian monogamous marriage prohibits for all time during the subsistence of that marriage a change of faith and of personal law on the part of a husband resident and domiciled there...In their Lordships' view in such countries there must be an inherent right  in the inhabitants domiciled there to change their religion and personal law and so to contract a valid polygamous marriage if recognised by the laws of the country notwithstanding an earlier marriage. If such inherent right is to be abrogated it must be done by statute. Admittedly there is none."

In 1933 the accused, while a Christian domiciled and resident in Ceylon, married a Christian woman by Christian rites under Ceylon's Marriage Registration Ordinance. The marriage was monogamous. She later left him and obtained a maintenance order against him. Subsequently the accused and another woman were converted to the Muslim faith and were duly married by the registrar of Muslim marriages under the provisions of the Muslim Marriage and Divorce Act, notwithstanding that the earlier monogamous marriage was subsisting.

The accused's conviction for bigamy by the Acting District Judge was quashed by the Supreme Court of Ceylon from whose judgment the Attorney-General appealed to the Privy Council. The Council held that Reid had not committed bigamy because, having become a Muslim, he had acquired capacity to marry several wives and, therefore, his Muslim marriage, contracted during the subsistence of the monogamous marriage, was valid.

[p.70]

[p.70]

This decision provokes a simple but difficult question for the law of Ghana, particlarly as to and because of the general tone of its current law on bigamy2: Can a man, domiciled and resident in Ghana, be justifiably held to have committed bigamy in circumstances similar to those in Reid's case?

The Ceylonese provision under which Reid was charged reads as follows:

"Whoever, having a husband or wife living, marries in any case in which such marriage is void by reason of its taking place during the life of such husband or wife, shall be punished with imprisonment of either description for a term which may extend to seven years, and shall also be liable to fine: "3

On the face of it, this provision is so broad in its sweep and scope that any second marriage by a man to a second woman during the currency of the first marriage, no matter what the nature of that marriage is, would subject the man to a successful charge of bigamy. It is submitted that unfortunately this effect is the basic premise postulated and established by Ghana's Criminal Code. It provides:

"263.(1) A person commits bigamy who, knowing that a marriage subsists between him or her and any person, goes through the ceremony of marriage, whether in Ghana or elsewhere, with some other person."4

Standing by itself alone, this provision is uncompromising and socially undesirable. One would be very hard put to it to find a comparable provision in another ex-British colonial African country. In most of such countries one discerns certain moderating and limiting policies to this sweeping principle. One such policy is that in order to find the accused guilty of bigamy, his second marriage must be demonstrated to be void,5 assuming, of course, that the first one was valid. Such a policy seems to be a fundamental prerequisite to liability; it is dictated and compelled by each country's ethnic configuration, particularly as to the co-existence of pagans, Muslims and Christians. It would be observed that, generally, standing one polygamous marriage on top of another does not amount to bigamy in those countries where the legal order permits polygamous marriages. The singular and most surprising exception to this observation exists in Kenya where it is bigamous to marry another woman by Muslim rites whilst already and still married by native law and custom. In the ipsissima verba of the statute:

[p.71]

"Whoever contracts a Mohammadan marriage being at the time married in accordance with native law and custom to any person other than the person with whom such marriage is contracted and without having first obtained a divorce in accordance with native law and custom shall be liable to imprisonment for a period not exceeding five years, and in such a case Mohammadan law shall have no application."6

Outside this aberrant exception, "the essence of the offence of bigamy is the accused person's involvement in two marriage ceremonies one at least of which is monogamous."7

MARITAL PERMUTATION AMOUNTING TO BIGAMY

The purest and easily the most indisputable form of bigamy is committed where a man, H, who is already monogamously married to a woman, W1, goes through another monogamous ceremony of marriage with a second woman, W2, during the currency of the first marriage.8 This remains the only form of bigamy known to English law.9 Conviction for bigamy in such cases are firmly rooted in the social policy stand in England that marriage is "a voluntary union for life of one man and one woman to the exclusion of all others."10 Owing to the introduction of monogamous marriages, initially from the influence of Christianity and later by legislation, the law in Ghana incorporates the English policy even though its people are by custom polygamists. As against monogamy, polygamy has a different social policy focus: it tolerates several wives by one man at any one point in time. It can consequently co-exist with monogamy only if each system of marriage is exclusively confined within its own policy sphere. And it is precisely at this point that Ghana law diverges from the law in England in a significant respect. This in Ghana H commits also bigamy if, while still married monogamously to W1, he contracts another marriage by native law or under [p.72] customary law, that is a polygamous marriage, with W2.11 The reverse combination also constitutes bigamy.12  It has been held in England, however, that a polygamous marriage is not such a sufficient first or second marriage as would support a charge of bigamy.13 To a large extent, Ghanaian law rejects this posture and seems to have resolutely taken the stand that once the second marriage is void on the basis of the validity of the first one the accused has committed bigamy.14

It is important to note that from the above analysis, Twumasi's statement that bigamy means "the celebration of a second marriage by a person who is already married to another person monogamously" is only partially accurate. With the exception of a conversion marriage,15 a man may commit bigamy if he superimposes a monogamous marriage on a customary one.16

Law of Bigamy not Developed

The law of bigamy in Ghana has remained undeveloped. This has not been due to the absence of statutory provisions touching the offence but to a web of complex factors. First, the statutory provisions are not without doubt as to the kind of polygamous marriage which in combination with a monogamous one would amount to bigamy. The provision refer merely to marriages contracted under customary law and therefore leave doubts as to the role or involvement of Islamic or Muslim marriages in the bigamy equation.

Secondly, there is an almost complete absence of prosecutions for the offence and, therefore, of decided cases explaining the nuances and ramifications of the offence.17 It is not being suggested that people should have been committing bigamy. The fact of the matter, however, is that the offence is [p.73] regularly committed within the society and the statutory provisions have remained more honoured in the breach than in the observance. Many divorce cases in the courts are redolent with suggestions of bigamy, yet our procedure does not encourage the trial judge to call for investigation and possible prosecution; that is not the issue before him. Furthermore, in tune with existing social attitudes, such problems are better settled between the parties with the participation of their parents, families, church leaders and influential persons in the society.

Thirdly, the indigenous Ghanaian society is polygamous; monogamy was unknown, though a man may marry and cling to only one wife throughout his married life. It seems clear, then, that in days before the intervention and eventual introduction of monogamy, bigamy was inconceivable as a crime. Perhaps it is fair to say that bigamy is, in Ghana, an alien implant that has survived rejection but has become somewhat atrophied.

Fourthly, prior to the introduction of statutory provisions on bigamy in Ghana, the offence was regulated by the common law. Its prescriptions were founded on the fundamental factor of a monogamous marriage. Therefore the intervention of a monogamous ceremony of marriage was absolutely essential to a conviction of bigamy. This combination of a monogamous and a polygamous marriage to produce a hybrid called bigamy seemed very strange indeed to local perception and few are those who would readily agree that the taking of several wives constituted an offence; the monogamous ceremony would thus be looked upon as a mere form and not one prohibiting the husband from taking other women as wives.18

Distinction between "Customary" and "Polygamous" Marriages

So far, in this discussion, the terms "polygamous marriages" and "customary marriages" have been used as if they are interchangeable. This is not necessarily the case; for, while a customary marriage is a polygamous marriage, not all polygamous marriages are, on current usage, customary marriages. On the side of polygamous marriages there are for example, Hindu marriages of certain Hindu sects, Chinese marriages and Jewish marriages.19 Each such marriage may be characterised as a customary marriage when considered in relation to the society or ethnic community whose law or custom prescribed the procedures by which it may be contracted and the incidents attached thereto. A Hindu marriage would not be customary marriage among Chinese people, nor would a Jewish marriage be a customary marriage among Akans. A customary marriage is therefore, [p.74] fundamentally, a marriage which is indigenous to a particular people or ethnic community or tribe. Such a marriage may therefore be polygamous or monogamous. In Qureshi v Qureshi,20  for example, Sir Jocelyn Simon asserted that in England "monogamy is the rule."  He could as well have said monogamy is the "custom." The term "polygyny", then, refers to a characteristic, that is a man or woman's capacity to take several wives or husbands concurrently or one after the other and during the subsistence of the earlier marriage or marriages. In the technical language of sociology, the marriage of one man to several women is referred to as "polygyny." In general usage, however, the practice is referred to as "polygamy". On the African continent, the term "customary marriage" has been used to refer to indigenous and native marriages of African tribes and communities south of the Sahara.21  Hardly is it employed to include Muslim marriages.

MUSLIM MARRIAGES

From the above analysis, Muslim marriages are customary marriages in certain communities and among certain peoples, particularly those of Africa north of the Sahara and the Arabian peninsula; they are not necessarily customary in most sub-Saharan communities, even where they have almost entirely displaced the indigenous marriage systems. In many of these communities, Muslim marriages were introduced either forcibly by rulers on an unwilling people or by Muslim immigrants and superimposed on the existing indigenous institution or gradually and willingly adopted by native members as a necessary and compulsory aspect of their new religion.22 The point bears emphasis that marriage, whether monogamous or polygamous is at once a legal, religious and social institution. For this reason, it has an insidious capacity to permeate and establish itself in any community as an institution of the people even if it is practised by a minority of them. But this permeation may be merely superficial and the seeming identification of the new practice with the indigenous system may derive simply from the fact of the new system's similarities vis-a-vis the native system. In this respect, the [p.75] polygamous character of a Muslim marriage easily presents it as unalien and not entirely opposed to the native African's conception of marriage, that is, polygamy. Essentially for these reasons, one has to exercise caution in determining whether or not the Muslim institution of marriage is "customary" in any particular African community.

As a social and religious fact, Muslim marriages exist in Ghana also; and they are frequently contracted by members and sections of the community. In terms of the criminal law, however, our statutes have always specified customary marriages or marriages contracted under customary law as the marriages which in combination with monogamous ones may produce bigamy.23 Therefore the very crucial question arises as to whether the term "customary marriage" includes a Muslim marriage; or, to generalise the question, whether customary law in Ghana includes Muslim law.

Arguments In Favour

Several arguments may be urged in favour of the view that customary law includes Muslim law and therefore the term customary marriage includes Muslim marriage. First, when native courts were established by Ordinance in this country, they were enjoined to apply "the native customary law prevailing within" their respective areas of jurisdiction.24 No separate or specific mention was made of Islamic or Muslim law. And this was done in spite of the legislator's knowledge that at least in the northern parts of Ghana significant sections of some of the communities were Muslims governed by Muslim law in many respects of their daily lives. The situation was similar in scattered areas in the southern parts, particularly Kumasi, Saltpond, Swedru, Elimina, Cape Coast and Accra.25 It may be argued then that the legislator intended the term "native customary law" to include Muslim law; indeed, he expected that Muslim law would, under the rubric of native law, be applied as the personal law of the parties, particularly where both were Muslims. A clear provision which was manifestly intended to take care of this expectation was an injunction to the courts to apply in all causes and matters before them the provisions of any law binding between the parties.26  This statutory fiat provided further the escape hatch for the application of Muslim law under the general heading of native customary law. Permission was also given to persons to act extra-judicially as arbitrators "in any dispute [p.76] of a petty nature in respect of which the parties ... consent to his so acting under native customary law . .. "27 This provision patently empowered Zongo headmen and Imams to settle disputes according to Muslim law and it may be argued that the legislator intended the reference to native customary law to include Muslim law.

In 1945 the senior judicial adviser, in refusing a request from the Islamic community for the establishment of Islamic courts and the application and enforcement therein of Islamic law, wrote that headmen and Imams could act as arbitrators.28 Obviously they would apply Muslim law but this was expected to be done under the general prescription of native customary law.

Secondly, in Ghana itself, there is, in communities permeated by Islamic practices, a rather hazy application of the distinction between what is native and what is Muslim or Islamic. This exists particularly in the area of marriage and succession. Muslims contract their marriages initially according to native rites and then add the Muslim Sadaki as if it were a native custom requirement. In such circumstances it is easy to conclude that a native marriage includes a Muslim marriage. Professor Anderson notes that in Yendi, among the Dagombas and Konkombas, when it comes to divorce, "no distinction is made by the court as to whether the litigants are pagans or muslims; no mallam is consulted, and the sadaki is apparently treated as any other payment."29 Indeed, it is this mixture of ceremonies which compels the suggestion by Ollennu, Apaloo and Daniels that a Muslim marriage which is invalid for non-registration should be treated as a valid native customary marriage.30 The reverse of this suggestion is, however, a non sequitur and invalid: it does not follow that an invalid native marriage may qualify as a valid Muslim marriage because, as demonstrated above, only a Muslim marriage may involve a native and a Muslim marriage rolled up in a Muslim marriage. Thus, while a Muslim marriage may include a native marriage, a native marriage does not and cannot include a Muslim marriage. Hence a provision, particularly in a criminal statute regarding customary marriage does not, and should not, include a Muslim marriage.

Thirdly, however, and in further support of Muslim law's inclusion in customary law, Professor Anderson observes about Ghana:

"For the rest, however, Islamic law is applied, wherever it is applied at all, as 'native law and custom' or as a factor which is regarded by [p.77] certain native courts as modifying the normal native law and custom in so far as muslim litigants are concerned."31

And in Hausa v Haruna, Lassey J (as he then was) expressed the same view when he said:                     

"Save for the provisions relating to the registration of marriages and divorces contained in the Marriage of Mohammedans Ordinance, Mohammedan or Moslem law in Ghana is treated as native law and custom and administered as such.”32

These observations may well be so. But both Anderson and Lassey were speaking solely with reference to the civil and not the criminal law; their views cannot be taken to apply equally to the criminal law.

Arguments Against

Some points have already been made above in this regard. But there are several other arguments against the view that customary law includes Muslim law, and that the term customary marriage includes a Muslim marriage.

First of all, Islam is not native or indigenous to any community in southern Ghana. Even the northern communities, though consisting of a larger proportion of Muslims than in the south are a mixture of pagans and Muslims, the Muslims being in the minority. They consist of either foreign Muslims or indigenes who have embraced the Muslim religion and practised it alongside their traditional and native ways of life.33  Studies have shown that outside religious marriages and their attendant rules of intestate succession, indigenous Ghanaian Muslims are generally governed by their native customs.34   Professor Anderson observes that "Islam is in fact incomparably weaker in the Gold Coast (Ghana) than in Nigeria."35 He adds that the prevalent impression in the south (of Ghana) "that the Northern Territories (Northern, Upper East and Upper West Regions) are largely Muslim is chiefly derived from the fact that most northerners seem to profess that religion when they come south, whereas in their native setting the overwhelming majority is frankly pagan and the Islam of the minority largely skin-deep."36 He makes a final and very telling observation: ". . . only to a very limited extent can this system [Islam] be said to have become identified [p.78] anywhere in the Gold Coast (Ghana) with native law and custom ...”37

Admittedly, Professor Anderson's research was conducted in 1954 and the results recorded by him may not be the same today. The Muslim population, for example, has been on a steady increase and now stands at about 11 per cent of the total Ghana population. Even so, there is rather a growing greater awareness among Muslims of the separateness of their marriage vis-a-vis native customary marriage.

Secondly, as the numbers of Zongo establishments and Muslims grew in this country, the Hausas and Yorubas especially made incessant demands for their own courts in which Muslim law, as opposed to local native customary law, would be applied. They complained:

"How can we entertain the thought of stating our cases before a tribunal which are [sic] consider it just to thrust out the children of a man in order to allow a sister's children bearing the name of another man, and perhaps reckless and extravagant to enjoy the property... how can such a tribunal enter into our feelings when we dispute our ancestral dignity and right?"38

The administrator replied:

"I see no reason for granting this application. There are courts of arbitration no doubt in the Hausa community, but for strangers in a strange land to have a recognised tribunal from whose judgments there must be the right of appeal, I am adverse.39  They have voluntarily come into the territory of another people and in my view should, while they remain there, be subject to the laws of that territory even if their own law differs materially from that in vogue in the country they have come to, in the same way that an Englishman would be amenable to Soviet law, however much he disliked it, should he go to Russia."40

These developments show that both Muslims themselves and the administrator did not consider the Muslim law as included in the customary law.

Officially, the administrator ignored the Muslim law and maintained a studied silence as to its role in the corpus of the country's legal system. This official policy is not limited to colonial times alone; it was again recently manifested in the provisions of the Intestate Succession Law, 1985 (PNDCL 111). The only reference to Muslim law in the pages of this Law is the simple [p.79] repeal by section 19(b) of section 10 of the Marriage of Mohammedans Ordinance without any indication as to what effect this repeal is supposed to have on the Muslim law of succession which had been applicable under the said section 10. Since the object of the Law is to provide a single uniform pattern of intestate succession for all persons domiciled in Ghana at death, it is clear that the aim should have been to abolish or modify all the then existing patterns of inheritance. Yet, in the result, only the Muslim pattern was sought to be abolished. Like the Criminal Code, PNDCL 111 provides for the application of customary law alone to certain portions of the intestate estate and completely, ostrich-like, shuts its eyes to the application of Muslim law.

The policy of ignoring Muslim law is to be seen also in the severe limitations that are imposed on the recognition of Muslim marriages. A Muslim marriage must be registered within one week of its celebration41 or within a reasonable time after one week or probably not registrable thereafter.42 An unregistered muslim marriage remains invalid until it is registered.43 In Ex parte Ali, Cecilia Koranteng-Addow J emphasised this when she opined that: "The essence is not the fact of the ceremony but the registration; that gives the marriage validity."44 An unregistered Muslim divorce has no effect as a divorce and so a registered marriage remains a valid marriage until the divorce from it is registered.45 An unregistered marriage is bereft of any claim to the application of the Muslim law of succession to the intestate properties of the deceased party.46 All these disabilities were not similarly imposed by the colonial administrator in predominantly Muslim areas or countries. For example, in Kenya, the Mohammadan Marriage and Divorce Registration Ordinance of 1906 (passed only one year before the similar Ordinance in Ghana) enjoined the compulsory registration of all Muslim marriages as in Ghana but provides that: "Nothing in this Ordinance contained shall be construed to (i) render invalid, merely by reason of its not having been registered, any Mohammadan marriage or divorce which would otherwise be valid; (ii) render valid, by reason of its having been registered, any Mohammadan marriage or divorce which would otherwise be invalid." The same provision is contained in section 17 of the Marriage and Divorce of Mohammadans Ordinance, 1906 [p.80] of Uganda. Indeed, all over ex-British colonial Africa, provisions have been made specifically and distinctly separating indigenous or pagan or native customary marriages from Muslim ones. It cannot therefore be convincingly argued in the teeth of this evidence that the term "customary marriage" or "marriage under customary law" includes "Muslim marriage".

Thirdly, it seems the most compelling early argument against treating Muslim marriages as included in customary marriages is the passage and promulgation of our Marriage of Mohammedans Ordinance, Cap 129. This statute was passed in 1907 for the southern parts of Ghana, introduced into the northern regions in 1931 and amended in 1935. It is still in force. Only section 10 of it which deals with intestate  succession has been repealed by section 19(b) of the Intestate Succession Law, 1985 (PNDCL 11I). Thus, the statutory law controlling Muslim marriages separately and alone has been left substantially intact. The recent Customary Marriage and Divorce (Registration) Law, 1985 (PNDCL 112) deals exclusively with customary marriages. Its enactment provides further evidence of and accentuates the exclusiveness of customary marriages as against Muslim ones. There can be no clearer evidence that the legislatures of this country, at least since 1907, have not regarded customary marriages as including Muslim ones. This legislative choice is unmistakable. That it has not been explicitly articulated anywhere is irrelevant.

Fourthly, if it were indeed intended by the colonial administrators and, later, even today, by our post-independence legislators to draw Muslim or Islamic marriages into the ambit of customary ones in provisions on bigamy they would have done this unambiguously. For in Kenya, for example, the same British colonial administrator, in creating the offence of bigamy, expressly mentioned both marriages individually and separately and unambiguously. Section 51 of the Kenya African Christian Marriage and Divorce Ordinance of 1931 provided:

“Whoever contracts a marriage under the provisions of this Ordinance being at the time married in accordance with Mohammadan law to any person other than the person with whom such marriage is contracted, shall be liable to imprisonment for a period not exceeding five years.”

Nothing ever stopped our legislators from being this explicit if they wished to. Criminal law is often dramatic and authoritarian in character; and it is difficult to imagine that such an important basis for bigamy was left to conjecture in Ghana and to be implied from a provision on customary marriage.

Professor Daniels’ comment on the effect of section 44 of our Marriage Ordinance provides further support for the legislator’s exclusion of Muslim marriages from the definition of bigamy. He says:  “For reasons which it is difficult to discern, the section impliedly permits the contracting of a second [p.81] marriage in accordance with Muslim law."47 It is contended that the reasons are not difficult to discern for section 44 mentions only native customary marriages and leaves out Muslim ones; and in terms of statutory interpretation, expressio unius est exclusio alterius.

Furthermore, in Coleman v Shang48 the Ghana Court of Appeal, in stating the permutations of prohibited marriages, was at great pains to carefully exclude or at least make no reference to Islamic marriages.

CONCLUSION

As we have seen, the sections of our Criminal Code creating which permutations of marriages amount to bigamy, mention expressly only monogamous marriages and marriages "contracted under customary law" in section 265(1) or marriages "under customary law" section 265 (2).49 There exists complete and absolute silence on Muslim marriages. The Code provides, however, that its provisions should not be strictly construed "either as against the State or as against a person accused of any offence, but shall be construed amply and beneficially for giving effect to the purposes thereof."50 Pursuant to this directive, it may be argued that social policy considerations compel a reading of section 265 to include Muslim marriages. Such reading may be supported on the ground that both customary and Muslim marriages are potentially polygamous in character and, as a matter of policy, the bigamy provisions are intended to prohibit a mixture of monogamous and polygamous marriages by one person at any one point in time; in other words, Ghana's policy, as broadly appears from its provisions on bigamy, is that one person should not be simultaneously involved in a mixture of monogamous and polygamous marriages. In terms of this policy, the incongruous result created by the Privy Council's decision whereby Reid remained apparently married monogamously to one woman and simultaneously polygamously married to a second would be avoided. The present criminal law of Ghana would seem, however, to permit the Reid incongruity, strengthened very much by the express silence on the role of Muslim law and Muslim marriages. In a criminal statute, any presumption that a customary marriage includes a Muslim marriage must be expressly created and not left [p.82] to conjecture and to be implied.51 In R v Princewell,52 a Nigerian case, which turned on facts similar to those in the Reid case, the accused was convicted of bigamy precisely because in northern Nigeria, native customary law includes, by legislation, Muslim law.

In the Reid case, it would be remembered, the accused had had himself converted from a Christian to a Muslim, and thereafter married another woman by Muslim rites during the currency of his Christian marriage. The suggestion that such a marriage is valid on the basis of section 44 of our Marriage Ordinance may be justified also by the principle in  Hashmi v Hashmi.53 In that case, a Pakistani Muslim who had a subsisting Muslim marriage with a woman in Pakistan, went through a monogamous marriage with an English woman in England. An English court held that this second marriage was void as a monogamous marriage but valid as a polygamous one. That court validated the marriage on the basis of the legal fact that the man was domiciled in a country which permitted polygamy and under whose law he had capacity to take several wives. A Muslim possesses capacity to marry several women; and it is contended that a Ghanaian Christian who has converted to Islam and become a Muslim acquires such capacity. And there is nothing in Ghana's present general law which prohibits him from a valid Muslim marriage. If therefore his Muslim marriage is valid, then he cannot and must not, on principle, be convicted of bigamy.

It has been suggested in Kwakye v Tuba that a Muslim marriage which is invalid for non-registration should qualify as a valid customary marriage.54 Of course this must be subject to the qualification that the principle should apply only to persons domiciled in Ghana and identified with a particular Ghanaian tribe. Our courts have not yet had an opportunity to consider the desirability of such a principle. It is suggested that it should be accepted because of the social facts: (i) that most of such marriages are a combination of native and Muslim marriages; and (ii) that even if the courts do not recognise the marriage as a valid one the parties would resort to their Muslim elders and headmen to settle problems such as those of succession consequent upon such marriages as if they were valid. The decision in Hashmi v Hashmi ought, then, to commend itself to our sensibilities.

Another reason for supporting the suggestion in Kwakye v Tuba is the desire to avoid bastardizing the issue of the marriage. This, it is submitted, [p.83] was the paramount, though unexpressed, concern of the learned judge in Hashmi v Hashmi. We are a society with a plural system of marriages and we should aim at validating marriages as in Hashmi. When we do this, the Muslim marriage in issue in this discussion would not be valid or void, and the accused would not be guilty of bigamy—precisely as it was held in the Reid case. The monogamous wife may be given a remedy: she may present a petition for the dissolution of her marriage, coupled with an action for damages for the husband's breach of a fundamental condition that she would remain his only wife.

A reverse Reid situation exists where a Muslim, already married to a woman, W1, by muslim rites, becomes converted to the christian faith and thereafter marries a second woman, W2, by monogamous rites during the currency of the Muslim marriage. It is submitted that neither on the basis of our Marriage Ordinance nor under the Criminal Code is this second marriage void. The husband may be convicted of deceit of a public officer in knowingly making misrepresentations which enabled him to obtain a marriage licence, but no bigamy seems to have been committed.

All told, an adoption of the clearer Kenyan or nothern Nigerian provision referred to earlier would remove many of the doubts and uncertainties discussed in this essay. Indeed, a serious and very careful review of the relevant law is called for.
