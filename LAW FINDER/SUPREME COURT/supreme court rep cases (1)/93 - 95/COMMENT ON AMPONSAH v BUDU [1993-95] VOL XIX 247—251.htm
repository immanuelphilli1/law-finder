COMMENT ON AMPONSAH v BUDU [1993-95] VOL XIX 247—251

NII-APONSAH D A

 

*

The Abiriw-Kyerepong case of Amponsah v Budu1 is one more case of customary law complexion which, with respect, emphasises the paramount need for the inclusion of compulsory sociological studies in the curriculum of law schools in Ghana. For far too often, cases emanating from one Ghanaian ethnic group or another appear to have been misjudged on one sociological point or another. Ga Mashi decisions are replete with misdirections on sociological points.2 Other such cases in which blunders are patently discernible on ground of lack of proper insight into the sociological point in issue are Ghamson v Wobill 3 and Ekem v Nerba.4

In Ghamson v Wobills,5 despite the fact that the deceased ancestress in respect of whose property the issue of capacity to inherit or to sell was raised happened to have hailed, according to the accepted evidence, from Gomoa Ajumako, the trial judge applied Efutu customary law because he was of the opinion that the lex situs, Efutu customary law, was the relevant law which he called the lex loci rei sitae. On appeal to the West African Court of Appeal, the court per M'Carthy J stated thus6:

"We are of opinion that, although Fanti law would not ordinarily be binding between Wobill, an Efutu, and Kwesi Kra, a Fanti, it is in this case because Wobill's claim depends on the issue as to succession, and he claims through the Danquahs who are in this respect subject to Fanti customary law" [ie their mother's personal law].

It is submitted that the discussion of the lex situs would not have arisen at all if the trial court had concentrated on the sole succession question which is a socio-legal one and the exclusive and relevant determinant of right to inherit and consequently to dispose of the property notwithstanding its situation or locus.

In Ekem v Nerba7 there was a similar misdirection by which it was unnecessarily imported into the disposal of the case the applicability or otherwise of Fanti customary law of succession to property left in Cape Coast [p.248] by an outright lbo citizen who never abandoned his indigenous Nigerian domicile, though he had married two Fanti women and had had issue with both. Although in the long run the court did apply the correct personal law of the intestate to extend relief to some of his issue including the plaintiff, the court surprisingly denied relief to other surviving issue of the intestate on the bare technicality that they had disclaimed any interest in their late father's property. But the question is whether their disclaimer should have been the criterion for deciding whether by the intestate's personal law all his issue, however born, were equally entitled or not to shares in their late father's estate in the same manner and by the same yardstick or norm upon which the plaintiff's share had been declared; that is to say whether by lbo customary law children are entitled to their deceased father's estate either per stirpes or per matres. Unfortunately, the court's decision did not reflect any advertence upon the question of method of distribution whether it be either per stirpes or per matres.

In the pure Ga Mashi cases of Captan v Ankrah8 and Amarteifio v Ankrah9, concepts inspired by pure Akan matrilineal underpinnings were imported indiscriminately without the least consideration that the court was on a different terrain (polyetechinic Ga Mashi) where the warning of the West African Court of Appeal in Owoo v Owoo10 must always be kept uppermost in the minds of all adjudicators and jurists who happen to be seised with consideration of Ga Mashi cases in particular and other Ga causes or matters in general. There, the same learned M'Carthy J formulated the epoch-making and indelible warning as follows11:

It may be assumed that the learned Judge was aware that whereas Fanti customary law is a variety of Akan customary law, Ga customary law is not, and that although it has been much modified by Akan or Fanti influence, the Courts, when considering questions of the customary law, though often guided by authorities as to those other bodies of customary law, are not bound by them."

It is submitted that Korsah J in Captan v Ankrah grossly, with respect misdirected himself by disregarding that indispensable warning and holding as follows12:

"According to native law, as I understand it, children of three brothers cannot form one family; even children of the same father by two wives cannot in native law be memebrs of one family for the [p.249] purpose of inheritance or succession to property; because every child can only be a member of his mother's family."

It is respectfully submitted that the foregoing observation is a very dangerous overgeneralisation which overlooks or disdains the different sociological arrangements of ethnic groups other than the Fanti to whom Korsah J was affiliated and to whose customary law he exhibited such unalloyed chauvinistic allegiance. But surely, the learned judge must have been aware that Fanti customary law was not the only pebble on the beaches of Ghana, then the Gold Coast.

Recently, two similar smugglings of the conceptual underpinnings of pure Akan matrilineal law have been betrayed by Tagoe v Idun13 and the more modern case of Amponsah v Budu14 from the healthy Akwapim mountains of Abiriw-Kyerepong.

In Amponsah v Budu the facts simply put are that A and B were mere uterine brothers in the predominantly patrilineal Kyerepong community of Abiriw. A had died intestate leaving some property and was survived at the time of suit by only his daughters' children whose mothers had property inherited from their father A, according to the Kyerepong (Guan) time-honoured usage. They (the granddaughters) were the plaintiffs against a child of B, the mere uterine brother, who was the defendant.

It should have posed no difficulty to both the Court of Appeal15 and the Supreme Court when they considered the case on appeal to appreciate that if any set of persons, issue or descendants were socio-legal strangers to A's family, they were B and his issue, because of their disqualifying maternal tie. Notwithstanding this salient fact and the fact that there is a more natural, if not more practical, connection or proximity between A and his descendants (whether male or female), the two courts, surprisingly with respect, approved of the succession bid made against A's descendants, ie grand-children of A, by one whose father, B, by the patrilineal determinant was a non-starter in A's family strictly conceived as his patrilineage.

The question is, since B was himself disqualified by the patrilineal social organisation from holding himself out as a member of A's patrilineage, how could the courts have reasonably given the nod to B's scion's own bid, when he was even still farther away from A? It is submitted that in the absence of direct mate hiers of A, natural justice dictated that A's property ought not to be alienated from any descendant of his who could trace a direct pedigree to A. It is further submitted that by giving the property to B's son the courts incautiously contradicted or negated even the patrilineal principle which they had earlier waxed eloquent about.

[p.250]

In the premises, it is submitted, with the greatest respect, that Edward Wiredu JSC's dissenting judgment was more practical and more in accordance with natural justice, equity and good conscience than the judgment of the majority. Edward Wiredu JSC noted16:

"He [Ollennu] admits, however, that the Guan of Akuapim do not practise strict patrilineal inheritance which the Court of Appeal applied in this case. It is therefore clear from the above analytical examination of the practice that the Court of Appeal erred and that the only caveat that can fetter the hand of a sole surviving daughter of a deceased parent of Guan origin from disposing of property she had succeeded to as in the instant case is the interest of her own children (whether male or female)... With the daughters now deceased, the plaintiffs (as grandchildren of the deceased . . . ) hold the legal as well as the beneficial interest in the disputed properties. . . "

It is regrettable that though that decision was in line with other decisions like Ankrah v Aryeh; Ankrah v Ankrah17; Quarcoopome v Quarcoopome18; Davies v Randall19 and Omane  v Poku20, the two courts had reason to differ from the more practical and just decision of Edward Wiredu JSC.

However, it might be pointed out that devolution of property to the next-of-kin or the nearest relative has divine approval in Numbers, chapter 27, verses 1—11 where after Moses had consulted the Almighty Lord about the deprivation of the daughters of Zelophehad of any ancestral property the Lord God is reported to have directed as follows in the Good News Bible:

". . . (7) What the daughters of Zelophehad request is right; give them property amongst their father's relatives. Let his inheritance pass on to them. (8) Tell the people of Israel that whenever a man dies without leaving a son, his daughter is to inherit his property. (9) If he has no daughter, his brothers are to inherit it. (10) If he has no brothers, his father's brothers are to inherit it. (11) If his father has no brothers or uncles, then his nearest relative is to inherit it as his own property. The people of Israel are to observe this as a legal requirement, just as I the Lord have commanded you."

This appears to run in line with the law of most patrilineal communities like the Kyerepongs of Abiriw; and, in our humble opinion, an ancestor's nearest relatives are his own descendants whether issue of male or female.

[p.251]

The patrilineal inheritance in vogue here in no way justifies the judicial volte-face or swing in favour of the maternal kinsmen. A's progeny, whether male or female—routed, was more proximate to him as his next-of-kin in this peculiar circumstance.21 It appears that the way out of such judicial confusion, with great deference, would be to give greater and more emphatic recognition to the dichotomy between two classes of family property, as Edward Wiredu JSC suggested in the case under review; that is to say (a) ancestral property from a more remote forebear like that of a grandfather or great-grandfather, who had died intestate leaving property behind; and (b) "family property" supposedly resulting from the intestacy of our immediate parent (father or mother).

With the class of family property in (a), such as in the estate involved in the Amponsah v Budu case, all persons who could establish a pedigree or lineage, whether matrilineally or patrilineally, should qualify under natural law under rules 6 and 7 of section 54 of the Courts Act, 199322 in the beneficial enjoyment on a cognatic basis, as in the actual practice amongst the Ga Mashi and Ga Adangme peoples — a practice which often eludes the courts. But with the class involved in (b), the Intestate Succession Law, 198523 or the time-honoured custom of succession in the family concerned (which might vary from family to family) must apply.

Equally, when the intestate property descends from a member, both the male and female issue, or their issue as well must be admitted to the beneficial enjoyment, since all the descendants of an ancestress, both male and female, could legitimately and acceptably point to the forebear involved as his or her bona fide ancestress. The male descendants could do so no less than the female descendants of the remote mother in the patrilineal system in particular. It is submitted that section 54, rr 2, 3 and 5 of Act 459 support this humble opinion and ought to be resorted to, consistently, whenever devolution of property is concerned to do justice to all.
