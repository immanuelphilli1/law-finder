J U D G M E N T

 

 

PROF. T.M. OCRAN, J.S.C:-

 

This is an appeal from the unanimous decision of Judicial Committee of the National House of Chief, Kumasi, delivered on 8th July 2004.

 

THE FACTS AND ISSUES

The facts of the case, as laid out in the decision of the National House and the Statements of Case submitted by both sides to the controversy, may be summed up as follows.

As a result of a war between Juaben in Ashanti, and the rest of Ashanti, in or about 1875, the Juabenhene and some of his people migrated to the present Eastern Region, settled in the Koforidua area and created New Juaben. The land acquired by Nana Asafo Agyei, the Omanhene who led his people to New Juaben, included Suhyen, where   some of his subjects had settled.

 

In or around 1915, a successor Omanhene of New Juaben, Nana Kwaku Boateng I, while on a tour of Suhyen, appointed an Odikro for the town, purportedly because of its strategic importance as the boundary between the Stool lands of New Juaben and Akyem. This first Odikro, Nana Kwaku Amofa Diatuo, belonged to the Asona Clan. He was succeeded by his maternal brother, Nana Ankam, who, with the permission of the New Juaben Omanhene, blackened the Stool of his predecessor. Thus a black Stool for the ruling family of Suhyen came into being.

 

The position of Odikro continued to be held by the Asona family of Nana Diatuo, until after the reign of Nana Obiri Nyebi, when members of other clans including the Ekuona, Aduana and Agona, began to sit on the Stool.  Thus Nana Frimpong Manso I, an Ekuona, occupied the Stool in 1954. The Respondents insist that anytime someone outside the Asona Clan sat on the Stool, he did so with the prior consent or approval of the Asona ruling family. During the reign of Frimpong Manso I, the Omanhene of New Juaben Nana Akrasi elevated the Suhyen Odikro Stool into an Mponua or Divisional Stool. The occupant of the new elevated Stool then created the queen mother Stool, whose first occupant was also an Ekuona. Nana Frimpong Manso I was followed by Frimpong Manso II, also an Ekuona. The dispute that has found its way to this Court arose after the latter was destooled and the Elders embarked on a search for a successor.

 

The Plaintiffs/Respondents claimed that they remained the rightful owners of the Stool, which had been created by their Asona ancestors with an Odikro status and later elevated to a divisional status. This elevation, in their view, made no difference to their ownership of the Stool. The Defendants/Appellants, on the other hand, contended that with the elevation of the Suhyen Odikro Stool to an Mponua status in 1954 a new Stool had come into being, created for the Ekuona clan as a gift from New Juaben Omanhene Nana Akrasi to the Ekuona matrilineal family of Nana Frimpong Manso I. They contended that even if the Suhyen Stool was a continuation of the original Stool created around 1915 by Nana Kwaku Boateng I, the occupancy of the Stool by the Ekuona clan for over 50years since the reign of Nana Frimpong Manso I meant that the Asona clan had acquiesced in the new status of the Ekuona clan and were estopped by conduct in laying claim to the Stool. The Plaintiffs/ Respondents lost the case in the Judicial Committee of the New Juaben Traditional Council but won an appeal to the Judicial Committee of the Eastern Region House of Chief by a unanimous decision. The Defendant/Appellants then appealed further to the Judicial Committee of the National House of Chiefs, which dismissed their appeal with costs, on 8th July 2004. The Defendants/Appellants of the Ekuona clan remain unhappy with the positions taken by the Eastern Region and National House of Chiefs, and have come before us on further appeal.

 

The grounds of appeal, as spelt out in the Notice of Appeal dated 17th September 2004, may be summarized as follows:-

a)     The Judgment of the Judicial Committee of the National Chiefs is against the weight of evidence adduced.

 

b)     The Judicial Committee erred in law when it held that  Plaintiffs/Respondents were not conclusively estopped by the Evidence Decree (NRCD 323) SS. 24 and 26 (and decided cases thereon) from challenging the exclusive right of the Defendants/Appellants to the male and female Stools of Suhyen.

 

c)      The Judicial Committee erred in law by failing to consider decided cases on the creation of gifts as it relates to the purported gift of the Suhyen Mponua Stool to the Ekuona matrilineal family by the New Juaben Omanhene.

 

The first ground of appeal, namely, that the judgment of the National House of Chiefs is against the weight of evidence, follows the unhelpful practice of throwing in an omnibus ground of appeal as a backup, even when there is very little difference in the evidence or the facts as submitted by parties on both sides to a suit.

 

My Lords, if we look closely at the Statement of Case of the Defendants/Appellants filed on 6th May 2005, and the Statement of the Plaintiffs/Respondents filed on 16th June, 2005 there is convergence of evidence on the core facts upon which the case before us had been framed. Those are the core facts we have outlined in our summary of the facts of the case at the beginning of this judgment. The differences between them point rather to differences in the legal interpretation or legal consequences of the events that took place with regards to the elevation of the Suhyen Stool in 1954 and the undisputed ascension to the Stool by members of the Ekuona clan at some point in the history of the Suhyen Stool. Moreover, with the possible exception of the Judicial Committee of the very first traditional adjudicatory authority-- the New Juaben Traditional Council-- the Judicial Committees of the Eastern Region House of Chiefs and the National House of Chiefs had no difficulty in distilling and agreeing upon the core facts of this case when the matter came before them on appeal. We therefore do not put much weight on the weight of evidence leg of the Grounds of Appeal. 

 

We will therefore turn to what we regard as the real issues in controversy, i.e. the legal consequences of the events surrounding the creation and subsequent occupancy of the Suhyen Stool as we have just alluded to. The issues in law may be stated as follows: -

(i)            Did the elevation of the status of the Suhyen Stool to Divisional Mponua Stool during the reign of Nana Frimpong Manso I turn the Suhyen Stool into a new creation, or was this merely a continuation of the Stool originally occupied by Nana Amofa Diatuo of the Asona clan in or around 1915 on his appointment as Odikro by the New Juaben Omanhene Nana Kwaku Boateng I?  

 

(ii)          Was there indeed a gift in law from New Juaben Omanhene Nana Akrasi to the Ekuona clan in respect of the newly elevated Mponua Stool,  thus vesting in them the ownership of the Suhyen Stool?

 

(iii)         Did the conduct of the Asona clan in accepting the occupancy of the Stool by members of the Ekuona clan, including the creation of a queen mother from that clan, amount to acquiescence in the claimed right of the Ekuona clan to the Mponua Stool?

 

ANALYSIS

Did the elevation of the Suhyen Stool to a Divisional or Mponua status create a new Stool in New Juaben? Our direct answer is “No”. A Stool, once created, never dies, except perhaps by conquest or annexation of the people represented by the Stool.

 In the Suhyen case, there was only one Stool, which grew in stature during the reign of Nana Frimpong Manso II. It should be pointed out that throughout the history of Suhyen, there has been only one black Stool, the one created by the Asona occupants of the Stool. The black Stool is the symbol of family ownership or title to a chiefly position among the traditional leadership structure, such that the family owing the black Stool might appropriately be said to be the owners of the Stool. Dr. Max Assimeng, eminent Ghanaian sociologist, in his book entitled The Social Structure of Ghana (GHANA Publishing Corporation, 1981) has noted at p.94 that “ an important element in the social life of the town is the blackened Stool…. In the family, the Stool is the bond that unites the people together…what is especially important is the position of the chief’s Stool in rallying the other segments of the tribe or clans together”.

 

There is no evidence on record that during the long reign of members of the Ekuona clan at Suhyen, a black Stool other than that created by the Asona clan after Diatuo’s rule surfaced in the town, possibly to symbolize the elevated Mponua status of the Suhyen Stool. This goes to underline the point that mere occupation of a Stool does not amount to ownership of the Stool, for such occupation might simply be in the nature of a caretakership.

 

As regards the Appellants Ground of Appeal 2(b) stated in terms of estoppel and conclusive presumptions under the Evidence Decree, 1975 (as amended), there appears to be some confusion in the pleadings and in the language of the Grounds of Appeal as to whether the contention is based primarily in estoppel or in conclusive presumption.

 

Mr Justice J. Ofori-Boateng, former Justice of the Supreme Court of Ghana, in his book, The Ghana Law of  Evidence [1993 Waterville Publishing House, Accra], draws an instructive distinction between estoppel and conclusive presumptions, which we hereby adopt. His Lordship argues that though all the examples of conclusive presumptions given in S.25-29 of the Evidence Decree seem to be instances of estoppel, and estoppel and conclusive presumptions functionally often bring about the same legal effect, an estoppel is not a synonym for a conclusive presumption.

 

He cites Nokes’ Introduction to Evidence (4th Ed. P.124) to explain the distinction between the two.“…[E]stoppel” writes Nokes, “has two characteristics of evidence to distinguish it from a presumption, which is a rule  of substantive law”.

 

Ofori-Boateng then goes on to explain that the first difference is procedural Estoppel usually must be pleaded before evidence to establish it will be allowed, but a presumption is not pleaded. The second difference becomes particularly clear in cases of estoppel by conduct. The party pleading this type of estoppel has to adduce evidence to establish the estoppel. Under such circumstance, the existence or non-existence of the estoppel would be impossible to tell until the end of the trial, after the other side would have supplied evidence in rebuttal. But the essence of conclusive presumption is to stop the other party in the first place from adducing evidence to the contrary. Ofori-Boateng notes further that in such cases, the operation of an estoppel bears very little or no resemblance to that of conclusive presumption.

 

If we view Grounds of Appeal 2(b) as sounding in conclusive presumptions rather than in estoppel, then it is important to point out that the condition stipulated in the first part of section 24(1) of the Evidence Decree must be satisfied before the legal effect of conclusive presumption, i.e. the disallowance of contrary evidence, may be applied. In other words, the basic facts that arguably gave rise to a conclusive presumption must first be found or otherwise established. Of the instances of conclusive presumptions stated in SS.25-29 of the Evidence Decree, S.26 is perhaps the portion arguably relevant to this case. However, on the facts, what are the statements, acts or omissions of the Plaintiffs/Respondents that in the words of S.26. “intentionally and deliberately caused or permitted another person to believe a thing to be true or to act upon such belief…”?

 

We do not see any such statements, acts or omissions by the Plaintiff/Respondent that would have given rise to a conclusive presumption that the Mponua Stool of Suhyen was now the property of the Ekuona Clan in the area. On the contrary, the record shows that the Asona clan insisted that every time a non-member of the Asona clan sat on the Stool, it was done with their permission. See p.23-26 of the Records narrating the circumstances under which Opanin Kwaku Frimpong of the Ekuona clan became the Odikro of Suhyen in 1954, particularly the initial move by the Asona clan to torpedo his selection, and then their ultimate agreement to have him occupy the Stool with an acknowledgement of the Asona title to the Stool. In our view, the predicate condition for applying the legal effect of a conclusive presumption under the Evidence Decree of 1975 (as amended) has not been fulfilled.

 

In this connection, the citation of the Supreme Court decision in Nartey v. Mechanical Lloyd Assembly Plant (1987/88) GLR p.314 is not particularly helpful to the Defendants/Appellants. Nartey dealt with a controversy regarding the proper authority to convey  Frafraha lands situated in  La rural area. Could lands there be conveyed by the  La Mantse alone, or by the  Frafraha village Mantse alone, or by both  of them working  together? In the course of the judgment, the Supreme Court considered the applicability of S.26(1) of the Evidence Decree and decided that the La Stool’s inaction to and acquiescence in the Frafraha Mantse’s alienation of  Frafraha Lands raised a conclusive presumption by conduct under the said  provision of the Evidence Decree against the  La Stool and disabled its supposed successor in the  title or any grantee of the La Stool from taking under the grant in question. Nartey was a case in which the Supreme Court concluded that the predicate condition in  S.24(1) had been satisfied, that the matter fell under S.26, and therefore went ahead to apply the legal effect of conclusive presumptions. This, in our view, is not the situation in the case presently under consideration.

 

On the other hand, to the extent that the Defendants/Appellants’ contention is couched in terms of acquiescence or estoppel rather that the conclusive presumption rule in the Evidence Decree 1975, this contention also fails; first, because it was never established that the Asona clan displayed lack of vigilance in their claimed ownership of the Suhyen Stool; and secondly, because if we deem the period of reign by the Ekuona clan as a caretakership, mere caretakership cannot rise to the level of an estoppel in pais or equitable estoppel, i.e. a situation in which a person may be precluded by his  act or conduct, or silence when it is his duty to speak, from asserting a right  which he otherwise would have had.  We are not persuaded that the conduct of the Asona clan in witnessing the occupancy of the Suhyen Stool by the Ekuona clan, or in allowing the creation of a queen mother Stool occupied by the Ekuona clan, amounts to acquiescence.

 

We now turn to Ground 2(c) of the Grounds of Appeal which deals with the question whether or not the Omanhene of New Juaben made an effective gift of the Suhyen Stool to the Ekuona clan when it was elevated to the status of an  Mponua or Divisional Stool. The Judicial Committee described the claim of a gift to the Ekuona clan as “not worthy of belief”.

 

Strictly speaking, if you start from the position that we have taken, namely, that the elevation of the Suhyen Stool to Mponua status did not mean the creation of a new Stool but rather the continuation of the old Stool created for Nana Amofa Diatuo; and further, that there was no acquiescence or estoppel working against the Asona clan, the backbone of a contention based on gift is broken.  For what other Stool was available out there under these circumstances for the Omanhene to come over and deliver as a gift to a third person or entity? And did the honourable Omanhene, without cause, rhyme or reason, simply decide to come over and, as it were, expropriate the Asona Clan’s title to the one and only one Stool in Suhyen and just hand it over as a gift to another clan?

 

For the moment, let us sidestep these questions and confront the Appellants’ contention of a gift of the Stool on its own terms. The appellants’ position here is that even if one could not point to the offering of a customary gift or “Aseda” to the Omanhene of New Juaben upon his purported act of vesting the Mponua Stool in the Ekuona clan, such an omission is not fatal, for there are ways of perfecting a gift in customary law other than through the offering of aseda. For example, the user and enjoyment of gift would be one such mode of acceptance.

 

Indeed, the legal argument of the Appellants on this point is supported by the judgment of Chief Justice Apaloo in Anaman v. Eyeduwa (Court of Appeal, Accra, November 1977) [1978 1GLR 114] cited by the appellants in their Grounds of appeal 2(c) as well as in their pleadings.

 

In this judgment, the late Chief Justice referred to Mensah Sarbah’s classic formulation of the requirements of a gift at customary law. As far back as the first edition of his Fanti Customary Laws (p.69-70), Sarbah had made allowance for alternatives to aseda as modes of acceptance of a gift. His position was later  cited with approval by the West African Court of Appeal in Krakuwah v. Nanyenna (1938) [4WACA 165]

 

 Sarbah’s formulation, as interpreted and explained by subsequent judicial decisions including Anaman v. Eyeduwa consists of the following requirements: (1) there must be a clear intention on the part of the donor to make a gift; (ii) publicity must be given to the making of the gift; and  (iii) the donee must accept the gift by himself or herself giving thanks or conventional aseda, or by simply using and enjoying the gift, or in the words of Apaloo in Anaman v. Eyeduwa, by doing an act “which fulfills the objective which  the giving of aseda is meant to fulfill, namely, the expression of gratitude and the symbolic acceptance of the gift”. In the case of Anaman, the non-conventional act which was taken as the fulfillment of the aseda requirement consisted of the payment of the donor’s debts by the donee in respect of the farm that was the object of the gift.

 

We fully accept the validity of the alternatives or substitutes for Aseda in the requirements of a valid customary law gift. The Defendants/Appellants in the case before us did not need to establish what Apaloo calls “aseda in conventional form”. However, they still needed to establish the first leg of the requirements, namely, a clear intention on the part of the Omanhene of New Juaben to make a gift of the Mponua Stool to the Ekuona clan. As Professor Bentsi-Enchill, Justice of the Supreme Court of Ghana in the Second Republic, has remarked in his Ghana Land Law (1964) at p.365, “the three modes of acceptance listed by Sarbah are intrinsically neutral… They are therefore inconclusive in the absence of manifestations of donative intent and grateful acceptance”.

 

We do not see the existence of such a donative intention in the Record before us, and we therefore conclude that no customary gift of the said Stool was made to the Ekuona clan.

SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION

I now summarize my holdings in this case as follows:-

  1. The elevation of the Suhyen Stool to a Divisional or Mponua status did not create a new Suhyen Stool in New Juaben. There remained only one Stool, which simply grew in stature during the reign of Nana Frimpong Manso I.

 

  1. No conclusive presumptions have been established in terms of the Evidence Decree (1975) which would have prevented the Plaintiffs/Respondent from giving evidence on their historic title to the Suhyen Stool. The predicate conditions in S.24(1) of the Evidence Decree (1975) for the  application of the legal consequences of conclusive presumptions have not been fulfilled.

 

  1. No acts of acquiescence, estoppel in pais or equitable estoppel, have been established against the Plaintiffs/Respondents as regards their attitude towards the subsequent ascendancy of the Ekuona clan to the Suhyen Stool, either in its original or elevated form. The Appellants have failed to demonstrate lack of vigilance on the part of the royal family of the Asona clan; and mere catertakership of a Stool does not establish an estoppel in pais.

 

  1. No gift was made of the Mponua Stool of Suhyen to the Ekuona family because the clear intention of the purported Donor-the New Juaben Omanhene-to make such a gift was not established in the Record.

 

In the result, I uphold the decision of the Judicial Committee of the National House of Chiefs and thereby dismiss the appeal in its entirely.
