THE HISTORY OF THE GHANA LEGAL SYSTEM: THE EVOLUTION OF A UNIFIED NATIONAL SYSTEM OF COURTS* [1991–92] VOL. XVIII RGL 1—35

YEBOA K Y

 

*

INTRODUCTION

The outgoing three decades have seen rapid and vigorous nation-building activity on the African continent. With the possible exception of Ethiopia, Egypt, Liberia and the Union of South Africa, over 36 countries have by now extricated themselves from the throes of political and legal domination of foreign powers and raised their own national flag as independent countries. Most of them had had very long contracts and continuous association with many and varied kinds of European powers. Ancient writers ascribe the first contacts of foreigners with West Africa to Egyptians and Phoenicians.1 French authors claim discovery of the Gold Coast in 1382 “in which year the merchants of Dieppe, with those of Rouen, sent three ships to the West African coast, one of which, La Vierge, they say, went as far as Odena in the Gold Coast, which was named La Mina because of the large quantity of gold there obtained”2 The Portuguese arrived in  the  Gold Coast in 14713 and were followed in quick  succession by the English, the French, Dutch, Swedes, Danes and Brandenburgers and Germans.4 In common with many West African countries, therefore, Ghana had had, before her independence in March 1957, about four and a half centuries of economic association, at first sporadic, but of steadily growing dimensions in the last one and  a half centuries with Western European powers. With England, she had three centuries. But only half of that time saw the beginnings and consolidation of a partnership exclusive of other European competitors. Within that century and a half, trade, at first in gold and slaves, then later (as from 1847) in other commodities, such as ivory, rubber, and in 1894, cocoa, etc. grew and expanded. British courts were established, English law was introduced, at first within the forts and then later in the whole countryside, side by side with native courts and native law.

JUDICIAL INSTITUTIONS

By and large, most Ghanaian peoples had grouped themselves into states, large or small, before the advent of European travellers. The eminent Ghanaian jurist, John Mensah Sarbah, in his book Fanti Customary Laws (2nd ed, 1904) referred to the accounts of several travellers or adventurers to the Gold Coast and remarked that

“[A] study of these ancient authors abundantly proves that when, in 1481 [he means 1471] Portuguese navigators and other European trading adventurers first appeared on the Gold Coast, they found an organized society having kings, rulers, institutions, and a system of customary laws, most of which remain to this day.”

Though the size, strength and internal cohesion of the political organisation differed from one state to the other, the hierarchy of the courts or of the adjudicatory machinery followed a rather common pattern. Most disputes between members of a family would, as now, be resolved by a council of elders presided over by the head of the family; or where such dispute involved two branches of a lineage, then at a council presided over by the lineage head or an arbitrator from a nearby friendly and reputable family assented to by the two branches. The overriding primary motive would be reconciliation and the preservation of the social fibre. This procedure persists even today, and under present legislation, would seem to be properly relegated to the sphere of arbitrations.

Although the decision or award of elders through such arbitration may be enforced by the regular courts of the country and may, in appropriate circumstances, constitute estoppel per rem judicatam, such family councils have not been and are not courts either under the traditional customary system or under any enactment or decision of the courts. Traditionally, therefore, the lowest public tribunals were the village or town tribunals.

TRADITIONAL NATIVE COURTS

All the rulers of the polities comprised in the Gold Coast presided over courts having jurisdiction over all causes and matters and persons within the confines of their particular political and geographical areas: hence in each state there were basically three rungs in the ladder of courts. They included (a) the town or village courts, which had original jurisdiction in petty civil and criminal offences originating within the geographical limits of the town either between citizens or strangers; (b) courts of divisional chiefs whose political domain comprised a number of towns or cities. Hence, apart from original jurisdiction in case of some significance, a divisional chief’s court exercised appellate powers in cases decided in and appealed from the town courts; and (c) the paramount chief’s court.

Few matters are often left to the highest court of any realm to deal with at first instance. But it should be remembered that in centralised states such as Ashanti, for example, the Omanhene occupies a dual position: (1) He is the occupier of the stool of the capital city of the whole state or federation; to that extent, he exercises original jurisdiction much in the same way and over the same types of cases as an odikro or ohene or divisional chief. A further extension of his jurisdiction at first instance over matters occurring anywhere in the state found its genesis in a procedural initiative on the part of any of the parties to the suit. The swearing of the Omanhene’s great oath, or the great oath of the state, immediately brought a suit into the Omanhene’s Supreme Court. (2) As a Supreme Court of the land, it acted also as the final appellate court in all matters dealt with in the courts of the state and brought to it on appeal. Such, in a broad outline, was the common traditional pattern of courts in the states of Ghana before European intervention and for a long time thereafter.

2. The Rise of British Court in Ghana

The history of British Administration in India, South Africa and West Africa, generally shows a lot of foot-dragging, indecision and reluctance by the central government in assuming positive political responsibility for the protection of countries considered to be mere trading posts of private companies.5 One finds in these countries, therefore, long decades of dubious attempts by merchants at political administration coupled with a certain measure of law enforcement where these combined to produce an atmosphere safe for and conducive to the decimating and spoliatory trade engaged in by the merchants. As time went on, however, the lucrative nature of the trade and the growing resentment of local people at the sharp deals of merchants combined to urge upon the British Government the desirability of the central government's assuming broader and more lasting political control in the trading posts. When in 1859 and 1872 the Dutch transferred all their forts to the British and left Ghana,6 the stage was set for active British Administration in Ghana. Before then, however, there had been feeble attempts at judicial administration. To these we will now turn.

Petty Debt Courts

Sir Charles McCarthy arrived in the Gold Coast in 1822 as Governor. In that capacity he established petty debt courts and appointed magistrates from the ranks of the local merchants. The jurisdiction of these courts was limited to the British forts (at this time very few indeed) and therefore to be exercised, as far as the local inhabitants or natives were concerned, only upon those who worked as assistants in the forts. The exigencies of British economic interests and the appeal of the white man's law to the natives soon led the magistrates into extending their jurisdiction into areas immediately around the forts and into the immediate countryside. Governor George Maclean's report of November 1836 acknowledged the glaring excess of jurisdiction but defended the status quo and recommended its regularisation7 for the promotion and maintenance of peace and trade.

The British Settlements Act8

Following upon Dr Madden's Report of 1841 the British Settlements Act was passed empowering the Queen to establish all such laws, institutions and Ordinances, and to constitute such courts and offices, and to make such provisions and regulations for the proceedings in such courts, and for the administration of justice as may be necessary for the peace, order, and good government of Her Majesty's subjects and others within the said present or future settlements respectively, or any of them; any law, statute, or usage to the contrary in anywise notwithstanding. Thereafter a judicial assessor's court was established with Governor George Maclean as judicial assessor. He sat with and acted as adviser to an august court of Amanhene. Implicit in this development is a certain degree of recognition of native courts. This court, though masquerading as the highest native court of the Fanti states, was a creation of the British. Technically, it was neither a British nor a native court. But its existence provided a useful and fruitful liaison between the two systems. It functioned without any pretensions to a usurpation of the work of native courts. The reasons for this are not far to seek.

Extent of British Influence—Bond of 1844

British political administration had not yet penetrated into the inner recesses of Ghana so as to allow an abolition of native courts. In a confidential letter of 31 August 1857. Governor Pine wrote to Labouchere as follows: “if the country were directly subject to the Crown, and British magistrates were scattered over it, the sooner the native authority (were) destroyed, the better.”9 The eastern states, mostly beyond the Volta River, were    mainly in the hands of the Danes and the Germans. During the period under consideration, therefore, British influence extended mainly and very thinly from Dixcove to Accra and only slightly-beyond. In fact, as late as 1873 the forts and the areas immediately around them seemed to be the only areas over which British jurisdiction was effectively exercised.10

The First Supreme Court: 1853

In April 1853 the first British Supreme Court to operate within Her Majesty’s forts and settlements on the Gold Coast and to be presided over by a barrister-at-law as Chief Justice was established.11 The court sat mainly at Cape Coast. Its jurisdiction was stated to be that of the Common Pleas, Queen’s Bench and Exchequer at Westminster 12 and of the Admiralty over treasons, piracies, murders, conspiracies, and such other offences, of what nature or kind whatsoever committed, upon the sea, or any haven, river, creek, or place where the Admiral or Admiralty have authority, power or jurisdiction, according to the common course of the laws of the realm of England, and not otherwise.13

Law Applicable

With the exception of admiralty offences, the Supreme Court was unrestricted in the particular substantive or procedural law which might govern an issue or even a crime before it.14 In the resolution of such conflictual problems as might arise, traditional conflict rules as applied to private international law would not come amiss: in civil suits, such as when an European sued another European or a native sued an European, the parties to the transaction out of which the action arose might control the application of English law or native customary law, in criminal cases the place where the crime was committed, or the parties to the crime, including even the court before which the parties were either lucky or unlucky to have been brought, were the determining factors. As has been argued earlier, the terms of the Bond of 1844 and the directives to the judicial officers established a clear presumption in favour of customary law unless it was “barbarous”, or an “abomination” and therefore “contrary to law” or where it was incompatible with the lay of England.15

On 4 April 1856 the Crown issued an Order-in-Council under the Settlements on Coast of    Africa and Falklands Act, 1843 extending British jurisdiction outside the forts in all cases where Her Majesty might exercise jurisdiction “without the co-operation of any native chief or authority. . .”16 “Bankruptcy and insolvency” as well as certain articles with regard to the administration of estates”17 were stipulated as the courts’ new areas of jurisdiction. Considerable doubt prevailed, however, as to what such articles were. As Sarbah remarked, “What that jurisdiction (i.e. in bankruptcy, insolvency and administration of estates) was it is difficult to say, but as the chief Justice was also Judicial Assessor there would not have been any conflict.”18 Section 3 of the Order-in-Council emphasised, as before, that equitable regard be paid to local customs by all judicial officers. No clear conflictual rules emerge from such a confusing situation. While it may be said that a clear presumption was established in favour of customary law, the limitations and qualifications surrounding its application were such as to allow a judicial officer, if he chose to, to apply English law by stigmatising a particular customary law rule as barbarous, abominable, contrary to natural justice, equity and good conscience, and the like.

Advent of the Modern Pattern of Courts

A Select Committee of 1865 recommended the consolidation of the four British West African colonies under one supreme government in Sierra Leone. In accordance with this recommendation the British Government abolished the Gold Coast Supreme Court and replaced it with a chief magistrate's Court presided over by a chief magistrate as the highest judicial officer of the British courts.19 With the successful completion of the Sagrenti War of 1875 and the removal of the Ashanti threat, however, the way was cleared for a more meaningful and fruitful involvement in the administration of the country. The newly won victory must be consolidated. In 1876, therefore, the Supreme Court Ordinance of that year was passed.20 The title of this Ordinance declared that it was “An Ordinance for the Constitution of a Supreme Court, and for other purposes relating to the administration of Justice.” It established a Supreme Court “for the Gold Coast Colony and for the territories thereto near or adjacent, wherein Her Majesty may at any time before or after” 4 April 1877 “have acquired powers and jurisdiction.”21

Court of Appeal and Divisional Court

The Supreme Court thus established consisted of (a) a Court of Appeal or the Full Court 22 which could be duly constituted by any two of the judges of the Supreme Court, one of whom must be either the Chief  Justice or “the person for the time being discharging the function of the Chief Justice”23; and (b) the Divisional Court, which might be constituted by any one of the judges of the Supreme Court, authorised under section 6, for exercising the original jurisdiction, civil or criminal, of the Supreme Court.24

Judicial Divisions

Under sections 23 and 24 the colony was divided into the Eastern, Central and Western Provinces; and a Divisional Court sat in the capital of each province, with the proviso, however, that where necessary “several Divisional Courts may be held concurrently in the same Province.”25 Under this provision, a second Divisional Court for the Eastern Region sat at Keta.

Magistrates Court

It will be remembered that on the amalgamation of the Gold Coast administration with that of Sierra Leone in 1866 the courts of the judicial assessor and magistrates continued to operate. The Supreme Court Ordinance of 1876 cleared this dead-wood away.26 and transferred the powers of these courts to the new Supreme Court. New magistrate courts were established27 deriving their birth entirely from the Supreme Court Ordinance of 1876. Their only link with the passing era would seem to be the retention of the old geographical limits. Their personnel was mainly district commissioners, for, by then, the ranks of qualified lawyers were still very small indeed.28 District commissioners, authorised to exercise judicial powers, were therefore constituted ex officio commissioners of the Supreme Court and invested with authority to exercise the powers of a judge of the Supreme Court.29

Concurrent Jurisdiction

Magistrates and divisional courts had concurrent jurisdiction except that cases decided in the magistrate courts of a particular province came on appeal to the provincial Divisional Court. A provision for reference enabled the magistrates courts to refer cases of significant gravity to the Divisional Courts in their provinces.

Native Courts

With the abolition of the post of judicial assessor and the provision for appeals from the decisions of his court to the Governor's council, the only links which native courts had had with the English courts were thus severed. But the native courts continued to enjoy their age-old independence and the inherent jurisdiction that went with it.

Thus at the beginning of 1877 the pattern of courts would seem to be a rigid dichotomy between the British and customary courts. Indeed, in spite of the useful liaison provided by the Judicial Assessor's Court, there was, until 1877, no fusion of courts. The Chief Justice of the pre-1866 era did not preside over a fused court. As judicial assessor he acted merely as adviser to native chiefs although in time he tended to act more and more like the president of the court.30 From 1877, however, our chiefs played an advisory role in the British courts whenever the British judges needed their advice on questions touching on customary law.31 The lingering desire to see native courts fall into desuetude had become revived.

The Applicable Law

A more elaborate system of law than had hitherto been the case was introduced in 1876. Section 14 of the Ordinance provides:

“The common law, the doctrines of equity, and the statutes of general application which were in force in England at the date when the colony obtained a local legislature, that is to say, on the 24th day of July 1874, shall be in force within the jurisdiction of the Court.”

Jurisdiction was also conferred in probate, divorce and matrimonial causes and proceedings32 But the whole body of English law thus introduced was applicable “so far only as the limits of the local jurisdiction and local circumstances permit, and subject to any existing or future Ordinances of the Colonial legislature. . .”33

Two points call for comment here: (1) the limitation of local jurisdiction and circumstances import a territorial limitation; and (2) customary law was not to be ignored since it formed part of the local circumstances. This second limitation received fuller clarification in section 9 which provided that nothing in the Ordinance:

". . . shall deprive the Supreme Court of the right to observe and enforce the observance, or shall deprive any person of the benefit, of any law or custom existing in the said colony and territories, subject to its jurisdiction, such law or custom not being repugnant to natural justice, equity and good conscience.”34

The significance of these provisions in the development of customary law and ultimately, of the common law of Ghana is very far-reaching indeed. Recording of decisions and adherence to stare decisis led to a greater and growing degree of certainly and uniformity in decisions touching principles of customary law. For the ascertainment of such principles, English tests as to when a custom could qualify as or matured into law were applied, leading sometimes to the denial of legal force to certain customs which customary law itself would have enforced as law.35

DEVELOPMENTS UP TO 1935

Native Courts: The Struggle for Survival

It is not clear what reasons of policy or convenience dictated the total silence of the Supreme Court Ordinance on native courts in 1876. However, for some time prior to the passing of the Ordinance official preference had been running in favour of their complete abolition.36 Success in the war of 1873 and the more extensive political and judicial control which followed seemed to strengthen in the British mind the case for abolition37 The Order-in-Council of 6 August 1876 directed that Her Majesty’s powers be exercised in the Colony as if they were acquired by cession or by conquest.38 It would appear then that when in 1876 the Supreme Court Ordinance was being passed to establish judicial institutions for the country, a clear but unexpressed official policy decided against any recognition of native courts. But could the native courts be ignored?

Britain's hold on native authorities was, in 1874, precarious in the extreme. As late as 21 August 1874 Lord Carnavon, writing to Governor Strahan, assessed the position as follows:

“Her Majesty's Territorial dominion is of narrow local range. It extends merely to the forts, or at most to so much of the lands immediately adjacent as may be required for defensive, sanitary or other purposes essential to the maintenance of the British position on the coast. All beyond that is foreign territory. That country is foreign soil, divided amongst native chiefs and rulers, standing in no relation of allegiance to Her Majesty, independent of one another, and each presumably sovereign within the local limits of his own domain.

But within the territory of each ruler, the English sovereign has, by cession or sufferance, acquired a varying degree of authority, and over the whole an undefined and somewhat anomalous jurisdiction.”39

Evidently, such jurisdiction as the Supreme Court possessed, would require for its successful administration a large measure of co-operation from the natives and their rulers. Furthermore, although British justice was becoming popular, there were many and varied kinds of disputes which a Ghanaian would prefer to submit to the decision of local rulers instead of to the white man. In effect, the native courts could not be ignored, or abolished; and if they could not be abolished without serious dislocation and disturbance, their existence had to be officially recognised, and their relationship, legal, administrative or otherwise, worked out for the better administration of the country.

Native Jurisdiction Ordinance: 1878, 1883

In 1878, therefore, the Native Jurisdiction Ordinance was passed "to facilitate and regulate the exercise in the protected territories of certain powers and jurisdiction by Native Authorities.”40 The Ordinance was allowed to lapse but re-enacted in 1883. It did not recognise the existing native courts. Instead it empowered the:

“Governor, with the advice of the Executive Council, by proclamation to be issued by him for that purpose, to declare from time to time as he may think desirable, that any head chiefs division, or part thereof, shall be brought from a time to be named therein within the operation of the Ordinance.”

“Head Chief” was defined to mean “a chief who is not subordinate in his ordinary jurisdiction to any other chief, and includes the chiefs known as Ohen, Ohene, Manche and Amega.”41 By 1910 at least 39 such chiefs were brought under the Ordinance and their royal councils constituted into local legislative councils and judicial tribunals.42 In their legislative capacity the chiefs' councils promulgate-bye-laws “for promoting the peace, good order and welfare” of their respective areas, stipulating sanctions for breach thereof. As tribunals, they heard and determined cases based on the violations of the bye-laws.43 Beyond the bye-laws, the said native courts enjoyed an extensive civil jurisdiction which included the hearing and determination of all “personal suits in which the debt, damage or demand does not exceed” £25 or such other sum as might be fixed under the rules governing the exercise of jurisdiction,44 and, generally, suits relating to the ownership and possession of land held under customary law as well as “suits and matters relating to the succession to the goods of any deceased person “where the whole value of the goods . . . does not exceed . . . fifty pounds.”45

Two significant observations about this designation of subject matter jurisdiction must be noted here: (1) The native courts had no jurisdiction in suits relating to succession to the land of deceased natives. Suits relating to ownership and possession are land suits, and a land suit is not a succession suit unless the claim to ownership and/or possession is based upon the right to succeed an owner or possessor whose title is not in dispute or in issue.46 Thus, a native court's decision touching succession to land could be attacked for excess of jurisdiction. The native court itself was obligated to refer such matters to the “Commissioner” (which means the Secretary for Native Affairs, or other officer appointed by the Governor for the consideration of all or any matters relating to or in dispute respectively in the native tribunals).47 Appeals also lay to the commissioner who might divert them to a superior native tribunal; and (2) Personal suits and suits relating to succession to goods of deceased natives were not expressly limited to suits in which the law involved is customary law. Hence, native courts could apply English law, especially where the nature of the transaction on which the suit was based contemplated the application of English law; or in the case of wills under the Wills Act for the disposition of the personally acquired goods of a native, or in the case of suits relating to the succession to the goods of a deceased native married under the Marriage Ordinance. The mere fact then that the law involved was English law would not be sufficient to bring the case within the reference provisions of section 21. Although the presumption would be that only customary law would apply in the native courts, nothing prevented any such court from a pretension to sophistication and the application of English law. Whether or not these possibilities were present to the minds of the legislators may be debatable. But the provisions relating to jurisdiction over persons reveal skepticism about the quality of customary law itself as a just system of law and doubt as to the judicial competence of those who administered it.48

JURISDICTION OVER PERSONS

The Government

Until 1947 (when the Crown Proceedings Act was passed) English jurisprudence denied its courts any jurisdiction over the Crown except upon the Attorney-General's fiat.49 Native courts had no such jurisdiction under any circumstances, provided the Crown was a party and/or the suit was brought “for the protection of the revenue.”50 Very nice distinctions could arise under such provisions. In England, the denial of jurisdiction where the Crown was a party to a suit often led to suits against the wrongdoer personally. A servant is always primarily liable for his torts, for example, and inability to reach and enforce a judgment against his master does not extinguish the servant’s liability. If a servant were personally sued before the native courts for a debt, damage or demand arising from his own acts, though performed in the course of his employment, he would be unable to avoid the court’s jurisdiction because the Crown would not be a party to the suit. Secondly, any suit to recover money from the crown even if revenue is not involved would seem to be outside the native courts' jurisdiction because the Crown would be a party.

Natives

Native courts had jurisdiction over all natives but not Europeans. In these first days “native” was defined to include “mulattos, and all persons resident in the country, other than those commonly known as Europeans.”51 Jurisdiction might be exercised over a non-native if he “consents in writing to his case being tried by the native tribunal,”52 The class of persons defined as natives was narrowed in 1910 to include only members of a native community of the Colony, Ashanti and the Northern Territories.53 Thus, in Brown v. Miller,54 where a native of Jamaica had resided in Accra for 62 years, the Full Court held that:

". . . mere residence alone, (on the Gold Coast even if prolonged, or even if he should purchase Accra land, does not make a stranger coming to the Colony a Native so as to bring him within the jurisdiction of the Native Tribunals as established under the Native Jurisdiction Ordinance, 1883, as amended.”55

The system of native judicial administration thus established was clearly a half-way measure, reluctantly introduced to operate only in areas where British Administration was still merely incipient and therefore requiring a larger measure of local participation or co-operation. As Sarbah notes, “The settled policy of the government has been, not to extend the provisions of the Native Jurisdiction Ordinance to any place or district where a District Commissioner is stationed.”56 Secondly, the native courts were largely kept within a rigid system of their own with appeals to the Commissioner for Native Affairs as an absolute prerequisite to any appeal to a British court.57 While a British court could directly refer to native courts cases properly cognisable by them, references from native courts, must first pass through the Commissioner for Native Affairs. In land cases, i.e. for ownership or possession, the commissioner could refuse an appeal to a Divisional Court only if the Queen's Advocate agreed with him. In the event of a disagreement between these two functionaries, the matter was referred to the Governor, or, at the Governor's discretion, to the Chief Justice; the decision of the Governor or the Chief Justice was final. In the areas (generally along the coastal belt) where the administration's native courts were not established, the traditional native tribunals continued to function, but apparently shorn of certain powers in the official hope that they might by gradual disuse, become unimportant and fall into desuetude. This trend of events reflects, to some extent, the cautious recognition which in 1872 Chalmers thought the native courts should receive, if at all. Said he:

“The influence which our courts have been capable of using in the abatement of crime, and of the darker forms of cruel native customs, depends much on this, that these courts have been regarded as a refuge in all cases of hardship and oppression. To relegate a complaint for first hearing, even to a native court acting under our authority, should only be done with caution and, I think, not at all to any native court which has not been so approved by us.”58

The continuing lack of a definite policy on the part of the administration in respect of native courts raised questions even in judicial circles as to the status of these courts and their relationship with British courts. Did the Supreme Court Ordinance, 1876 abolish native courts?

In 1878, Marshall J held in Quacoe Koom v. Owea that the Supreme Court was not intended to supersede the authority of the “kings and chiefs.”59 Nine years later the Full Court said of the 1883 Ordinance that “It also treats native tribunals as existing, but requiring regulation.”60 To its own question whether “the Supreme Court Ordinance, 1876, swept away the previously existing judicial powers of native kings and chiefs?”61 the court answered:

“. . . we are clear that the Supreme Court Ordinance, 1876, has in no way impaired the judicial powers of native kings and chiefs, and so far as we know, it has not been suggested that any Ordinance has taken them away.”

But, since the bedrock of English constitutional law is the legislative supremacy of Parliament (“Parliamentum omnia potest”),62 the foregoing decision of the British courts in Ghana would have had to be entirely different if the Supreme Court Ordinance, 1876, had, in expressis verbis, abolished all native courts. It was even argued that jurisdiction, being an inherent ingredient of sovereignty, could not be taken away from the chiefs nor conferred upon them (nemo dat quod non habet); the Queen never had the jurisdiction of the native chiefs.

The Orders-in-Council of 1901

The Orders in Council of 190163 which “annexed" certain areas of the colony to the British Crown added more fuel to the existing legal and political uncertainty surrounding the fate of native courts. The Full Court to which the question was submitted in 1903 in Bainyi v. Dantsi and Bainyi v. Appiah63a ended up hopelessly deadlocked. The presiding Chief Justice, Sir W B Griffith, who had cast his vote for the continuing existence of the native courts, maintained his stand four years later in Mutchi v. Annan.64 The battle for survival was won by the native courts in the amendments which were made in 1910 to the 1883 Ordinance.65

The New Native Courts

Broadly speaking, the pattern of native courts which were finally recognised in the Gold Coast Colony followed the common traditional patterns analysed earlier and which existed before European intervention66; Owing to the absence of constitutional difficulties in Ashanti, Northern Territories and Togoland under British sphere, the British Administration did not feel itself compelled to follow traditional hierarchies of native courts. In these areas, therefore, the pattern of native courts became in essence a creation of statute and ranged from grade A at the top to grade D at the bottom.67  For Southern Togoland, paramountcies and sub-divisions had the same jurisdictional status. There were courts of appeal at Kpandu, Ho, Avatime, Borada, in the Akpini State Council, Asogli State Council, Avatime State Council and the Buem State Council respectively. Southern Togoland may be said therefore to have only two tiers of courts—native tribunals of first instance and native appeal courts not designated by grades. The Northern Territories had only grades A to C and thus only Ashanti had four grades. State councils, provincial councils and confederacy councils were pressed into service, wherever possible, to act as courts of original jurisdiction in constitutional cases touching native political institutions68 or as courts of appeal.69

The Blackall Committee and Thereafter

Following the Blackall Committee Report of Native Tribunals in 1943 there was reorganisation so that at the close of 1949, all of the Gold Coast was covered by a four-grade native court system except the Northern Territories where the three-grade system prevailed. By 1950 a significant shift away from strict adherence to the traditional hierarchy of courts could be seen to be well underway. But the chief interest in these new arrangements lay in the jurisdictional structure that had emerged.70 Before sketching out the jurisdictional structure, it may be illuminating to bring up to date the picture on the side of the colonial courts.

The Colonial Courts: Developments up to 1950

In the introduction to his work on The Development of Laws and Constitutions of Ghana, Dr Elias writes:

“The former British West African Colony of the Gold Coast consisted of the settled colony of the coastal areas, the conquered colony of Ashanti in the middle belt, the Protectorate of the Northern Territories, and the Trust Territory of British Togoland. It thus contained within its own boundaries the three main types of British overseas dependencies.”71

These differences in the mode of acquisition dictated differences in administrative arrangements and therefore differences in the judicial system and often times even the nomenclature of courts with ultimate appeal to the West African Court of Appeal.

The Colony

A Land Court was established in 1944.72 It dealt in the main with “all causes and matters relating to the ownership, possession or occupation of land . . .":

(a) where there is no native court competent to try the cause or matter;

(b) where the cause or matter has been transferred to the Land Court from a native court in accordance with the provisions of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, 1944;73

(c) where immediately prior to the appointed day, any such cause or matter is pending—

(i)  before a Provincial Council, or a Judicial Committee of a Provincial Council or a Joint Judicial Committee under the Native Administration (Colony) Ordinance; or

(ii) before any State Council or Native Tribunal under the Native Administration (Colony) Ordinance, where there is no native court competent to hear and determine the cause or matter; or

(iii) in the Court of a Provincial Commissioner; or

(iv) in the Supreme Court.

This jurisdiction was further extended in 194574 to cover land suits between (1) a chief of Ashanti and a chief of the Colony; and (2) a chief of Ashanti and a chief of the Northern Territories whether or not such suit was pending prior to 31 December 1945. Furthermore, the Land Court was obligated to try with assessors land cases which involved customary law.75 Failure to do this rendered the proceedings null and void.76 An important guiding rule was that an agreement to convey land by a deed of conveyance amounted to an agreement between the parties that the obligations under the contract should be regulated by English law.77

Ashanti, The Northern Territories and Togoland.

Between 1807 and 1900 Ashanti fought seven gruelling wars with the British. This long drawn resistance against British power from the South, and Ashanti's own strategic position delayed until 1901, effective organisation of administration in Ashanti and the Northern Territories. After the Yaa Asantewaa War and the promulgation of the Orders-in-Council of 1901 annexing Ashanti and establishing a protectorate over the Northern Territories, Ordinances were passed immediately to secure political and judicial administration. Togoland, on the other hand, came under the British after the First World War as a British mandated territory.78 Administratively, Togoland was divided into a northern79 and a southern section.80 For convenience, the northern section was administered as part of the Northern Territories, and the southern section as part of the Eastern Province of the Gold Coast.81 Therefore, with minor administrative modifications, the British courts' pattern existing in the Colony and the Northern Territories became the model for Togoland. With a Divisional Court at Keta, however, only a district commissioner's court was established at Ho for the southern section of Togoland with power to exercise the same kind of jurisdiction as was exercised by the district commissioner of the Keta-Ada District of the Colony when sitting at Keta. Occasionally, the court's personnel included the provincial commissioner himself or his deputy who, because of their higher political status, became thereby invested with power to try offences ranging up to “man-slaughter by negligence,”82 where in the opinion of the said court, the offence was one which could adequately be dealt with by a sentence of imprisonment, with or without hard labour, for a term not exceeding two years.83 The chief commissioners’ courts, presided over by the Chief Commissioners of Ashanti and the Northern Territories respectively, were established in the two areas as their highest British tribunals.84 Subsequently, circuit judges were appointed to these courts,85 but the courts continued to be styled the chief commissioner's court. With the appointment of circuit judges, the jurisdiction of the chief commissioner’s court was extended to include divorce and matrimonial causes and therefore co-extensive with that of the Divisional Courts.86

Appeals lay in civil cases “where the sum or matter involved amounts to one hundred pounds”87 to the Full Court of the Gold Coast Colony only with leave of the Governor “in writing.” In this way, links were maintained with the courts of the Colony. Apart from powers to stop and transfer to themselves cases before native courts and direct such cases to be heard by themselves,88 the chief commissioner's courts acted as courts of appeal in decisions from native courts within their respective areas.

Commissioner's Courts

These courts, in certain cases called police magistrate's courts, ranked below the chief commissioners’ courts and exercised the same jurisdiction as magistrates courts exercised in the Colony.89

Appeals in Criminal Cases

One significant departure from practice in the Colony has to be noted here. It is common practice that the exercise of the right of appeal against a conviction and/or sentence lies in the discretion of an accused person. This procedure prevailed in the Colony. In Ashanti, the Northern Territories and the northern section of Togoland, however, appeals from the decisions of a commissioner in criminal cases or matters were prohibited. Instead, every commissioner was required to furnish to the chief commissioner at the end of every month.

“a complete list of all criminal cases decided by or brought before the commissioner during that month, setting out the name of the defendant, the offence with which he was charged, and, if convicted, the date of conviction, and the sentence or order in full.”90

Such a list operated as an appeal91 and the chief commissioner, without hearing any argument, could amend any judgment “which shall have been given contrary to law”, or “set aside and order an entry to be made in the minutes of the commissioner's court that, in his judgment, the person convicted ought not to have been convicted,” or make any of the consequential orders which an appellate court could make on any decision it came to on appeal.92 Generally, a conviction followed by a sentence of death was reported to the Governor who then acted as a court of appeal and as fount of mercy.93

The Grand Unification of British Courts, 1935

Under the Courts Ordinance of 1935 the judicial pattern existing in the Colony was extended to the rest of the country,94 and divisional as well as magistrates courts to operate as lower courts of the Supreme Court of the whole of the Gold Coast were immediately established in Ashanti and the Northern Territories.

The chief commissioner's courts passed muster,95 though the requirement that they be presided over by circuit judges was dropped and substituted with one for “a fit and proper person” to be appointed by order under the Governor's hand.96 In Ashanti, the chief commissioner's court retained jurisdiction only over suits and matters relating to the ownership, possession or occupation of lands arising between two or more headchiefs of different divisions or between a head chief and his subject.97 In addition, the court acted as a court of appeal from decisions of a magistrate's court, the Asantehene's court or any other native court established under the Ashanti Native Courts Ordinance, 1935.

In the Northern Territories, the chief commissioners court had the same jurisdiction as that of Ashanti in respect of lands. In addition it tried land disputes between a head chief of Ashanti and a head chief of the Colony. Appeals against decisions and orders made by the chief commissioner's court under its original and appellate jurisdiction lay directly to the West African Court of Appeal which had been established earlier in 1928.

Native Courts: Developments After 1950

Dissatisfaction with the existing plethora of native courts led to the appointment of the Korsah Commission in December 1950. Its recommendations clearly indicated that a single judicial system with a national character was gradually emerging; but the recommendations were stored away until 1958 when the country had become independent.

By the Local Courts Act of 1958, the four grades of native courts (three grades in the Northern Territories) were transformed into one native court.98 As before, they were again placed at the bottom of the British hierarchy of courts.99 They administered mainly customary law100 and were presided over by either one or three local court magistrates instead of traditional rulers; but provision was made for the appointment of an assessor “in any land or other cause in which it appears to the local court magistrate that an exceptionally difficult question of customary law is involved.”101 Traditional rulers were no longer the necessary and automatic members of the new local courts. This is perhaps the most revolutionary and significant break with the past; and it prepared the way for the conversion of native courts into the lowest rung in the emerging national ladder of courts. The incorporation of the provisions establishing local courts into the Courts Act, 1960 (CA 9) with appeals to the British type magistrate's courts in civil cases other than land, and to the High Court in land cases strengthened the trend towards the fusion of British or national and native courts.

The local court's jurisdiction now extended for the first time to all persons except that no local court had jurisdiction in any cause or matter in which the government of Ghana or any public officer acting in his official capacity was a party, or in which the revenues of the Government of Ghana or the actions of any public officer while acting in his official capacity were in issue.102

The law applicable, as stipulated under sections 10 and 15, was mainly customary law; but under section 10(V1) the court could apply any law in “personal suits where the debt, damage or demand does not exceed fifty pounds.” Further evidence that the court was not limited to the application of customary law alone is to be found in section 15(b) which permitted the court to apply “the provisions of any law binding between the parties.” In addition, where a law other than customary law seems clearly applicable, the court could still assume jurisdiction “if the parties agree thereto.” Quite apart, then, from the fact that sections 10 and 15 were not inconsistent with each other, the local courts did not become more customary than the native courts which they replaced.103 The application by native courts of a law other than customary law was itself no novel provision in 1958. It was perfectly possible under the very first enactment regulating native courts.104 Subsequent legislation prior to 1958 also gave native courts jurisdiction over “personal suits in which the debt, damage or demand does not exceed one hundred pounds.”105 Legislative history clearly shows that the native courts in Ghana were never at any time intended to be, and the native courts did not personally consider themselves to be, mere customary courts applying only customary law. After the military coup d'etat in February 1966, the National Liberation Council repealed the Courts Act, 1960 (CA 9) and promulgated in its place a new Courts Decree, 1966.106

Abolition and Disappearance of Local Courts.  Emergence of District Courts Grade II.

The most revolutionary development under this Decree was the disappearance of local courts. In their place district or magistrates’ courts were established. The division of these magistrate's or district courts into grades I and II creates the unfortunate picture of the continuation of the local courts in a new garb. This may well be the discerning lawyer's analysis hinged on lingering memory. And such observation appears to receive telling emphasis from the fact that while lay magistrates may preside over the district courts grade II they cannot try cases pending before district courts grade I: such a trial will result in a nullity.107 The Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372) has not changed the existing structure of courts.

Personnel of the Courts

As has been noted earlier, a lay magistrate cannot try a case in a district court grade I. This does not prevent such a magistrate from presiding over a grade I court. But while sitting in such grade I court the lay magistrate can apparently only postpone or adjourn proceedings for trial by a grade I magistrate or a circuit judge or a judge of the superior court. Although lay magistrates can therefore still be found on the bench, various legislative enactments indicate a growing trend towards their gradual phasing-out and complete elimination from the ranks of the judiciary.108 This development follows the recommendations of the Korsah Commission of 1950 and constitutes the clearest break with tradition in modern Ghana's judicial administration.

The Judicial Committee of the Privy Council

The Privy Council, properly known as the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council, was set up in 1833.109 It hears cases as the highest court of appeals for the dominions and territories of the British Crown which have not become republics. It is presided over by the same law lords as decide cases in the House of Lords but its decisions are not binding on the House of Lords nor are decisions of the House of Lords binding on it. It is in essence and in constitutional theory an advisory judicial body to the English monarch and thus invariably declares all its judicial opinions as advice to the Monarch. Although the monarch is not obligated to follow the committee's decision, there has not been any case in which an English monarch has acted contrary to the committee’s opinion. The committee heard appeals from the Gold Coast and later Ghana from 1874 to 1960 when its functions were taken over by the Supreme Court established under CA 9.

The West African Court of Appeal

The West African Court of Appeal dates a little further back than the Privy Council's connections with Ghana. It was first established in 1866 with its seat in Sierra Leone; but it petered out by 1873.110 It was set up again in 1928, replacing the Full Court established under the Supreme Court Ordinance, 1876. Ghana withdrew from membership of the court after independence and the court's functions then devolved upon the Supreme Court established under CA 9.

Under the Constitution, 1960, as well as NLCD 84, the Supreme Court and the High Court were separate and distinct courts and not, as before, a Supreme Court consisting of a Court of Appeal and a High Court. Under the Constitution, 1969, however, three superior courts were established. These were the Supreme Court, the Court of Appeal and the High Court. After the 1972 coup d’etat the Supreme Court was abolished on the argument that the small volume of work undertaken by it between July 1969 and January 1972 did not justify its existence. Indeed, since its functions were designed to be mainly structured around conflicts involving the Constitution, it was felt also that its raison d'etre was seriously eroded by the suspension of the Constitution, 1969.

Circuit Courts

Circuit courts which had been tried in Ashanti and the Northern Territories under the title of chief commissioners’ courts, were introduced in 1960 and have now become a permanent rung in the ladder of courts. It should be noted, however, that the chief commissioners’ courts were courts of concurrent jurisdiction with the former Divisional Courts, which became the High Courts and therefore had a status higher than that of present day circuit courts.

The Constitution, 1979 preserves the pattern of courts established since 1960. In particular, the Supreme Court abolished in 1972 was re-established. These developments have substantially survived the 31 December 1981 military coup d’etat and revolution. Section 1 of the Provisional National Defence Council (Establishment) Proclamation, 1981 preserved all courts in existence immediately before 31 December 1981 with the same powers, duties and functions under the existing law subject to the Proclamation and laws issued thereunder. Two laws subsequently enacted under the Proclamation dealt with the regular courts. These are the Courts (Amendment) Law, 1983 (PNDCL 47)111 and the Courts (Amendment) Law, 1987 (PNDCL 191). These enactments did not effect any changes whatsoever in the existing basic structure of the regular courts; instead, they merely made relevant adjustments (taking the dramatic and drastic fall in money values into consideration) in the amounts which set out the limits of the jurisdiction of the district and circuit courts.112 In order to remove the necessity for frequent central government legislative amendment to the Courts Act in this regard, power to make relevant adjustments in the currency values delimiting the jurisdiction of the courts was now vested in the Chief Justice.113 Furthermore, district courts grade I114 and circuit courts115 were for the first time given jurisdiction to grant probate or letters of administration. This new policy relieves very substantially the work-load and pressure on the High Courts in respect of such applications. In addition it affords an opportunity for these applications to be presented and processed in the courts closer than before to the last known place of abode of the deceased. An opportunity was taken also to re-arrange and refine the language of the various sections of the principal enactment, Act 372, delineating the allocation of appellate competences between the various courts.116 In many cases an appeal lies “as of right” either from the High Court to the Court of Appeal or from the Court of Appeal to the Supreme Court.117 Generally, however, the High Court and the Court of Appeal may refuse leave for further appeal from their decisions where such decision confirmed the decision of the court from which the appeal was presented. But such a refusal is not final because the aggrieved party may apply to the next appellate court for the leave.118

Public Tribunals

The establishment of public tribunals was decided upon and announced by the PNDC in the very first enactment which it promulgated on its assumption of control of the country’s administration.119 Pursuant to that decision, a Public Tribunal Board and a four-rung ladder of tribunals was duly created by the Public Tribunals Law, 1984 (PNDCL 78).120 The four tribunals are, in descending order, the National Public Tribunal, the Regional Public Tribunal, the District Public Tribunal and the Community Public Tribunal. Each has original jurisdiction over specific offences and each exercises only criminal jurisdiction121 but, even so, only over some and not all offence. They are not courts of co-ordinate jurisdiction with courts in the regular system. The tribunals constitute, therefore, not a parallel but a separate system of adjudicatory organs alongside the regular courts.

Under the regular courts system, the judicial power of the state is vested in the judiciary headed by the Chief Justice.122 Public tribunals are controlled, firstly, by the Public Tribunals Board123 and ultimately by the PNDC itself.124 They are not subject to any control whatsoever by the Chief Justice and they are expressly and completely insulated against the supervisory jurisdiction of the superior courts of judicature in the regular system. Indeed, section 24 of PNDCL 78 unambiguously provides that:

“24.(1) No court or other tribunal shall have jurisdiction to entertain any action or proceedings whatsoever for the purpose of questioning any decision, finding, ruling, order or proceeding of a Public Tribunal set up under this Law; and for the removal of doubt, it shall not be lawful for any court to entertain any application for an order or writ in the nature of habeas corpus, certiorari, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto, injunction or declaration in respect of any decision, order, finding, ruling or proceeding of any such Public Tribunal.”125

The Public Tribunals Board is charged generally with the administration of the public tribunals.126 It is composed of not less than five and not more than fifteen members appointed by the PNDC “at least one of whom shall be a lawyer of not less than five years’ standing as a lawyer.”127 This is the only organ of the public tribunal system on which, by law, it is required that “at least one” member shall be a lawyer. Nor is it mandatory that this one lawyer shall be the chairman of the board.128 In effect, the whole of the public tribunals system can be legitimately operated on the scanty and almost insignificant presence of a single lawyer among its personnel. Considering that the trend since 1958 in respect of adjudicatory personnel in this country has been to gradually phase out lay adjudicators, this obvious revulsion against trained and professional legal personnel is retrograde. The point bears emphasising even though this writer is aware that in actual practice the panels of the public tribunals have a few lawyers among them.

Instead of the Attorney-General and his staff, the public tribunals system has a special public prosecutor and assistant special public prosecutors appointed by the PNDC.129 These officials prosecute offenders mainly before the National and Regional Public Tribunals; nothing stops their appearance before the district and community public tribunals. Section 37 of PNDCL 78 provides that “The District and Community Public Tribunals shall have People's Public Prosecutors...” appointed by the special public prosecutor and removed from office by him on grounds of “proven misconduct”, “inefficiency or inadequacy in the performance of his functions” or “counter-revolutionary activity.” It would seem then that a people's public prosecutor is, impliedly at least, debarred from appearing before the Regional or National Public Tribunal.

Under the public tribunals system, the PNDC is very much involved in the administration of justice. It may refer to a tribunal for trial “any offence under any enactment.”130 It may refer to the National Public Tribunal for trial any case, even if hearing has already commenced before a Regional, district or community public tribunal, where the case is of public importance and interest.131 Such transfer of a case by the Council is “final and shall not be subject to any appeal.”132 In the case of a person convicted of an offence in his absence and who commenced to serve his sentence only after the expiry of 90 days from the date on which the sentence was pronounced, it is the PNDC which decides whether or not his confiscated assets shall be returned to him.133

Other differences between the public tribunals system and the regular courts may be noted. A public tribunal can compel the attendance and appearance of an accused person before it by way of an announcement publicly “on national radio, newspaper publication or national television or other notice in any medium of mass communication,”134 and, if the accused fails to appear, try him in his absence.135 A regular court cannot proceed to trial by any of these methods, if an accused, duly served with notice to attend, fails to appear, the court can only issue a bench warrant, keep the case in view and wait for the police to arrest and bring the accused to court on the warrant.136 Generally, a regular court does not admit relevant evidence if it is hearsay or prejudicial. A public tribunal is enjoined to receive “all relevant evidence in proof or disproof of a charge against the accused . . .” The only restriction on the use of such “relevant" evidence is that "the accused shall not be convicted on hearsay evidence alone.”137 A tribunal possesses power to convict an accused if it “is satisfied that all things considered, the offence was committed by the accused.”138 This bland fiat permits the trial tribunal to act on a kind of hunch and a certain consideration of social justice rather than rigidly subject the evidence to the ingredients of the specific offence charged. Indeed, a tribunal is permitted to act, in the course of a trial, in accordance with what “appears to it to be just having regard to the circumstances.”139

Section 13 of PNDCL 78 sets out very elaborately in 22 subsections the mode of trial before a public tribunal; but subsection 17 of this very section provides that “Non-compliance with the rules governing the mode of trial shall not render a trial invalid unless a substantial miscarriage of justice has been occasioned."140 Provision is made for a petition of appeal or a review to the Regional or the National Public Tribunal as the case may be. Even though, as at present, the public tribunals exercise no civil jurisdiction, and do not try all criminal offences, the system is conceived as a complete and independent judicial system by and in itself”141 with no links whatsoever to the regular courts.142 The tribunals are expected to avoid technicalities in their administration of justice and one of the many ways devised for realising this goal is the obvious substantial reliance on lay adjudicators who would decide cases on considerations of social justice rather than on strict adherence to the principles of law.

PROSPECTS

Supreme Court

It has been observed that the Supreme Court Ordinance, 1876 laid the foundations of the modern system of courts. The foregoing analysis has shown, however, that since 1876, much change and adaptation has been effected, sometimes very revolutionarily, to meet constitutional exigencies and to achieve efficiency. A single national system of courts now emerges from these changes. But the changes in some directions at least invoke some disturbing observations.

The term “Supreme Court” has often been used to refer to a composite cluster of courts that has not consisted of the same number of courts on each occasion, eg the Court of Appeal was established for the first time in 1960 and made part of the Supreme Court. This quite clearly creates confusion in the use of the term the “Supreme Court.” It is suggested that the term be reserved solely for the highest court of the country - in other words, the  Supreme Court should be the Supreme Court and no other court.143

High Court's Geographical Jurisdiction

The High Court's geographical jurisdiction has been subject also to varying dimensions since 1876. Under the 1876 Ordinance, each Divisional Court exercised jurisdiction only within a particular province or territory geographically delineated. This limitation to a distinct geographical area was abolished in 1971 with the result that the High Court was considered to be a single court throughout the whole country with branches in the regional capitals. Recent legislation has created a return to the distinct geographical jurisdiction of each High Court.144 It is not clear what policy considerations underlie these chameleon-like changes. Obviously, the 1971 conceptual picture of the High Court as “the High Court”, a single court with branches in other parts of the country appears to be a more desirable alternative in structuring. It prevents technical objections to venue, prevents loss of filing fees if the papers are initially filed in the wrong geographical jurisdiction. If the objection is that the single court concept might lead to too many cases in places like Accra, such difficulties can be solved through appropriate transfers of cases.145

District Courts

These have been the magistrates’ courts under the Supreme Court Ordinance, 1876. Native courts (later changed into local courts) have now been fused with them into a single court at the bottom of the ladder of courts. But the present district court is two courts rolled up in one - and distinguished into grades I and II. This is unfortunate in two respects. Firstly, it is reminiscent of a continuing cleavage between native courts and British courts. Secondly, it creates the impression of an inferior and superior court between the grade I and grade II courts. This impression is presently strengthened by the fact that lay magistrates can try cases only in grade II courts. It is suggested that steps be taken immediately to legislate for a single gradeless district court.

Article 125(2) of the Constitution, 1992 of the Fourth Republic provides nostalgically, however, that “Citizens may exercise popular participation in the administration of justice through the institutions of . . . customary tribunals . . .” This seems a recipe for the revival of native tribunals - institutions that had been drained out of the judicial system for more than 30 years now. If they should be established, the question would arise as to the level of the courts’ hierarchy at which they could be perched—either at the same level as or below the district courts grade II? Indeed, a more fundamental question would be whether such cluster and proliferation at the bottom is desirable. Article 126(1)(b) which enumerates the courts forming the corpus of the judiciary drops the term “customary tribunals.” With respect to the inferior courts, it provides that they shall consist of “such lower courts or tribunals as Parliament may by law establish.” Perhaps, articles 125(2) and 126(1), read together, permit the establishment of customary tribunals.

Personnel

One of the interesting developments underway in the personnel of the judiciary was the gradual phasing-out of lay magistrates who preside over some of the district courts grade II. The Korsah Commission of 1950 recommended the abolition of lay magistrates. That the lay magistrates continue to function is clearly due to difficulties in persuading trained lawyers to go on the bench in the required numbers. This clearly indicated policy of abolishing adjudication by lay persons in the regular courts is laudable and obviously progressive. The public tribunals system as it currently operates, is a substantial negation of this policy. The principle of lay judges has now been accepted for the Regional Tribunal and any lower tribunals which may be established.146 In the opinion of this writer, lay participation in the superior courts should have been limited to service as jurors and assessors.

Tribunals

Provision has been made in the Constitution, 1992 of the Fourth Republic for the integration of the public tribunals into the regular national court system.147 The National Public Tribunal remains but will assume the status of a High Court.148 The term “public” will be dropped and the tribunal will have the new title “Regional Tribunal.149 Similarly, lower tribunals, if established, will also drop the term “public.”

What status such lower tribunals would or should have has not been stated. Presumably, they would rank as district courts grades I and II but, even so, their personnel may not need to possess the same qualifications as those of the district courts. The tribunals, regional as well as inferior, would, unlike as now, be subject to the control of the Chief Justice and the supervisory jurisdiction of the superior courts.150

Presumably, also, the special public prosecutor, the assistant special public prosecutor and the people’s special prosecutors would remain. The Constitution, 1992 of the Fourth Republic says nothing about them. Like the Attorney-General and his staff, the public prosecutors would not constitute part of the personnel of the courts. It is not clear whether, in the Fourth Republic, they would then become part of the Attorney-General’s Department and be controlled by the Attorney-General. Whether or not such control is exercised is, however, an administrative matter that does not concern the structure of the courts or the judiciary.

The creation of the public tribunals has drawn mixed reactions and much criticism. The Ghana Bar Association is on record as having advised its members to refrain from appearing before them. Opposition to the tribunals lies in their short-shrift procedures, trials in absentia, stiff (purportedly deterrent) sentences, undue control and possible direction from the PNDC as to the outcome of trials and a feeling among the public that a man, once accused, is guilty even before he is heard. Opposition revolved also around the separate existence of the public tribunals, totally independent of the regular courts and wholly insulated from the supervisory jurisdiction of the superior courts in the national system. The Committee of Expects (Constitution) on proposal for a Draft Constitution for Ghana empanelled under PNDCL 252 deprecated the separate existence of the tribunals and recommended their abolition or integration into the regular national courts system.151 The latter alternative has now been accepted by the Consultative Assembly and indorsed by the Ghanaian electorate in the constitutional referendum of 28 April 1992. In the Fourth Republic, therefore, the tribunals will, up to the level of the High Court, remain a system within a system. In the view of this writer, this symbiotic existence is undesirable. If the numbers of the regular courts are felt to be inadequate, they can be added to. If work in the regular courts is lethargic because of their procedures, the procedures can be reviewed. In this regard, the procedures now available to the tribunals can be placed at the disposal of numerically increased regular courts. If the reasons for the introduction of more lay adjudicators is to inject into decisions considerations of social justice rather than strict adherence to rules of substantive law and procedure, the requirements of the qualifications for the personnel of the regular courts can be correspondingly amended. Courts enforce law, and no regular court will refuse to follow new rules of substantive law and procedure if duly enjoined to do so.

Another interesting observation is that, in the Fourth Republic, the tribunals will function merely as special criminal divisions of the regular courts of their respective levels. Why then can they not simply bear the names of the regular courts at their levels? The word “tribunal” means “a court of justice.” So, why not maintain consistency in nomenclature?

CONCLUSION

An increasing population and rapidly expanding central and local government regulation of life and business clearly require more courts and arbitral or dispute settlement institutions. As we have seen, five levels of courts have grown up in response to this need: these are (1) the district courts; (2) the circuit courts; (3) the High Court, (4) the Court of Appeal; and (5) the Supreme Court. A three-tier appeals system exists within this five-rung ladder of courts, that is from the inferior courts to the High Court, from the High Court to the Court of Appeal and, finally from the Court of Appeal to the Supreme Court. It seems clearly a case of unwarranted proliferation and therefore undesirable to further load up the bottom of all this gradation with district, community and customary tribunals as provided for in articles 125(2) and 126(1)(b) of the Constitution, 1992 of the Fourth Republic. Fortunately, it is not mandatory that Parliament establish these lower tribunals; and it is suggested that they should not be established. The need to bring justice to the doorsteps of the people can be achieved by increasing the numbers of the regular district courts (indeed all levels of courts), revamping their jurisdiction and reviewing their procedure for a faster disposal of cases. This process, particularly an increase in the numbers of existing courts, is already going on and merely needs to be supported and kept under constant review.
