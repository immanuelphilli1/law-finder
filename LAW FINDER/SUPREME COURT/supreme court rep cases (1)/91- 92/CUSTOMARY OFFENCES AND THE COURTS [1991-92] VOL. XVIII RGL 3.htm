CUSTOMARY OFFENCES AND THE COURTS [1991-92] VOL. XVIII RGL 36—67

Acquah G. K

 

*

INTRODUCTION

Ghana, like most nations, is made up of a number of ethnic communities, each with its own deep-rooted customary practices and offences handed down from generation to generation. Within a particular tribe, there are as many varied customs as there are communities. Some of the customary practices and offences are related to the history of the founding fathers of the community, others to particular incidents in the lifetime of the people, others to marriage and puberty rites of the women, and others to the day to day life in the community.

There are certain communities where it is forbidden to work in the farms on certain days; where certain animals are not to be reared; where the eating of yams, burying the dead, and playing music are forbidden at certain periods of the year. In certain parts of the Volta Region, for example, it is a punishable customary offence to invoke a fetish on another person without the prior consent of the chief of the community; again where a person is accused of using juju to kill someone, and the person accused refuses to undergo a trial by ordeal to prove his innocence, he is subjected to heavy customary sanctions.1 It is also a serious customary offence in Kadjebi area for anyone to collect stone particles in front and around the palace of the chief.2 Among the Krobos, it is an unpardonable customary offence for a female who reaches the age of puberty to become pregnant if she had not gone through a customary rite called "dipo." Such a female is not only to be socially ostracised but she is liable to be banished from home and disowned by her parents.3

In almost every community, it is a serious customary offence for anyone to commit adultery. And Taylor J (as he then was) in Billa v Salifu4 pointed out four categories of adultery in Dagomba custom, viz first, taking someone's wife away and carnally knowing her; secondly, carnally knowing someone's pregnant wife; thirdly, carnally knowing someone's wife whose newly born child is dead; and finally, carnally knowing a woman who since her delivery had not slept with her husband. The rationale underlying some of the taboos and offences is easily discernible while others defy any intelligible rationalisation.

A personal experience of the present writer while in private practice provides a good example. It happened that the paramount chief of a traditional area lost his mother. He asked a carpenter to make a fitting coffin for her burial. The carpenter built a beautiful coffin and submitted his bill. A year after the burial of the woman, when the carpenter realised that the chief was not making any effort to settle the bill, he called on him at his palace and respectfully demanded his money. The chief who was at that time with his linguist and few friends, immediately retired with his linguist for consultation. They returned and the linguist informed the carpenter that his demand had reminded the chief of the death of his mother, and thus caused grief to him. He went on to inform the carpenter that such a conduct was a serious customary offence, and therefore he was liable to provide six bottles of schnapps, a carton of minerals, a sheep, tubers of yam and ¢5,000 cash. Notwithstanding all pleadings from the carpenter that he was not aware of any such customary offence and that his intention was not to cause such grief, he was ordered to go and bring the items before his bill would be considered. The carpenter immediately ran to my chambers for legal advice since he felt that the alleged custom, if it existed was oppressive. The letter written to demand the settlement of the bill quickly cautioned the chief to settle same without demanding the fines for the alleged customary offence. Indeed the settlement of the bill prevented me from issuing a writ to determine how far such a customary offence could aid the chief in refusing to settle the carpenter's bill. It would have been interesting for the issue to be determined as to whether a court could have ordered the chief to settle the bill and at the same time ordered the carpenter to provide the items imposed on him as fines for the breach of that alleged custom.

Now the object of this paper is not to embark upon a critical examination of these customs and their offences with a view to determining their validity. Our task is to examine the jurisdiction and attitude of the courts when they, ie the courts, are called upon to punish or indorse the punishment meted out to those alleged to be in breach of these customary practices. In other words, to what extent are the courts prepared to assume jurisdiction, when called upon to do so, to enforce or not to enforce compliance with customary practices? It will in this regard be instructive, to look briefly at the historical background of the subject.

2. HISTORICAL BACKGROUND

Before the introduction of British rule in this country, head chiefs or kings held the supreme power over their various indigenous states. Chalmers, Chief Magistrate and Judicial assessor, in his memorandum on Native Courts dated 6 June 1872 stated:

"Every village has its headman, who exercises a sort of patriarchal rule over its few inhabitants; he, again, as well as his villagers, is subject to some chief, who has control over three, four, five or more villages; and this chief is again the subject of the chief or king of a large district."5

The chiefs were responsible for the physical, spiritual and moral well-being of their subjects; and they were further duty bound to offer their subjects protection from physical and spiritual invaders. In consultation with their fetish priest and other spiritual advisers, the chiefs took steps to ward off any impending plague, epidemic or bad omen. Thus whenever there was any outbreak of draught leading to famine or epidemic, the oracles and fetish priests would be consulted for the requisite customary rites to be performed. If the solution to the crisis laid in passing laws forbidding certain practices or imposing particular types of behaviour, the chief and his elders would order accordingly. And any infractions of such laws and directives were regarded as punishable.

Rattray, in his book, Ashanti Law and Constitution, devotes a number of chapters to some of the customary offences in the Akan speaking areas. He enumerates offences like murder, suicide, invocation of a curse on a chief, witchcraft,  violation by word or deed of any recognised tribal taboo, refusal to obey any law or command issued and qualified by a conditional curse, ie by an "ntam" or oath, sexual offences like incest and various forms of adultery. The learned author points out that all those charged with committing these offences were tried before a tribunal of the head chief and his body of councillors while other cases not involving violations of customary practices and taboos were settled more or less privately and unofficially by and among the parties themselves. J E Casely Hayford in his book, Gold Coast Native Institutions, describes the nature of this native judicial system at that time as follows:

"The king is the Chief Magistrate of the community, and,. . .there are minor courts exercising concurrent, but not coordinate, jurisdiction with the King's Court. You have first the Courts of Headmen, then Chiefs' Courts, and, finally, the King's Court, which is both a court of first instance and a court of appeal. In suitable cases, the King's Court can require a matter before a minor court to be brought up before it for adjudication."6

The punishment for committing such offences depended on the severity of the offence. The offender was sometimes subjected to public ridicule, sometimes he had his head cut off, or he was sometimes mutilated; flogged, imprisoned, sold into slavery or fined.

Since the head chiefs were themselves the legislators and custodians of the customs and taboos, they had no difficulty in trying and punishing offenders brought before them. Thus, we find, for example, that in Aguah v Effee7 the defendant was accused of having charged the plaintiff with practising witchcraft and with having thereby caused the death of a child. The plaintiff partially succeeded in proving his case and the defendant was fined and ordered to pay costs. The defendant was further ordered that neither he nor his family should trouble the plaintiff in future under a penalty of four ounces of gold. In Beddomassoo v Bossoo,8 the defendant was accused of going to the house where one of the plaintiff's captains stopped, and called down a fetish to kill the plaintiff. The court found this to be a serious customary offence and accordingly found him guilty and was fined four acks with costs.

Now, around the 1840s the British merchants were imperceptibly establishing British authority over the indigenous people especially on the coast. The British Settlements Act, 1843 and the Foreign Jurisdiction Act, 1843 enabled the administration of British territories and those on the Gold Coast to be placed on regular footing. On 6 March 1844, the Bond of 1844 by which the coastal chiefs acknowledged the power and jurisdiction which had been de facto exercised in the territories adjacent to the British forts and settlements was signed. It was declared in the Bond that "[T]he first objects of law are the protection of individuals and property" and that "Human sacrifices, and other barbarous customs, such as panyarring, are abominations and contrary to law." Paragraph (3) of the Bond reads:

"Murders, robberies, and other crimes and offences, will be tried and inquired of before the Queen's judicial officers and the chiefs of the districts, moulding the customs of the country to the general principles of British law."

The chiefs thus rid themselves of jurisdiction to try and punish capital and other serious offences and reserved for themselves jurisdiction in customary and other lesser offences. They further laid the foundation for the requirement that the native customs to be administered by the courts should not be repugnant to natural justice, equity and good conscience nor incompatible either directly or indirectly or by necessary implication with any Ordinance in force.

Then came the Proclamation of 1874 followed by the Supreme Court Ordinance of 1876 which established with effect from 31 March 1876 the Supreme Court of the Colony of Lagos as the superior court of judicature for "the Colony of and for the territories thereto near or adjacent wherein Her Majesty may at any time before or after commencement of this Ordinance have acquired power and jurisdiction." Two years thereafter the Native Jurisdiction Ordinance9 was enacted. It was replaced in 1883 by another Ordinance of the same title.10 The 1883 Ordinance gave chiefs of specified status power to create bye-laws, breaches of which they had jurisdiction to try as criminal offences. Ordinance No 7 of 1910 amending the 1883 Ordinance, made the Native Jurisdiction Ordinance applicable to chiefs throughout the country.

These enactments, especially the Supreme Court Ordinance of 1876, did not sweep away the previously existing judicial powers of native kings and chiefs. As the Full Court authoritatively stated in Oppon v Ackinie11:

"Now that we have considered it from every possible point of view, we are clear that the Supreme Court Ordinance, 1876, has in no way impaired the judicial powers of native kings and chiefs, and, so far as we know, it has not been suggested that any other Ordinance has taken them away."

Later on, a series of Native Court Ordinances were passed to make provision for the administration of justice in native courts. The native courts were composed of the kings, head chiefs, chiefs and other persons. They had civil and criminal jurisdiction over natives in terms of the Ordinance. The native courts in the Colony and the Southern Section of Togoland were given jurisdiction to try the following offences which the Ordinance described as "Native Customary Offences":

(1)  Putting any person into fetish.

(2)  Sexual connection with a chief's wife or with any woman in an open place.

(3)  Recklessly, unlawfully or frivolously swearing an oath.

(4)  Knowingly using disrespectful or insulting language or offering any insult whether by word or conduct to a chief.

(5)  Withdrawal of allegiance owned to a chief by a subordinate chief.

(6)  Withdrawal by a chief of the allegiance owed by his stool to another stool.

(7) Possessing any poisonous, noxious or offensive thing with intent to use such thing to endanger or destroy human life or to hurt, aggrieve or annoy any person.

The native courts in Ashanti and the Northern Territories had jurisdiction in criminal causes in respect of any offence which in the opinion of the native court can be adequately punished by a fine of £50 or six months' imprisonment in the case of grade B; £25 or three months' imprisonment in the case of grade C, and £5 or one month's imprisonment in the case of grade D courts or both such punishments, or in the case of male persons under sixteen years of age, by whipping not exceeding twelve strokes whether with or without such term of imprisonment as aforesaid.

Thus the native courts in Ashanti and the Northern Territories appeared to have power to try all customary offences within their jurisdiction, but the proviso to section 9(a) of the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, Cap 99 and section 8(a) of the Native Courts (Northern Territories) Ordinance, Cap 104 provided that in regard to criminal offences by virtue of native law and custom, such court should take cognisance only of such offences as might from time to time be prescribed by order of the Governor. In respect of native courts in the Colony and the Southern Section of Togoland they were enjoined to take cognisance only of those offences set out in the Schedule to the Ordinances - which offences I have tabulated above. Thus we find, for example in R v Essel12 that the accused was convicted on a charge of "being found in possession of fetish and other noxious medicines with the intent thereby to poison, endanger and injure the people of Cape Coast Castle." Then there is the case of Native Tribunal of Elmina v Mensah,13 in which the respondent, Alfred Mensah, was acquitted on appeal from the native tribunal for the offence of "having violated the great oath of Elmina (Amanswiw) during the month of October, for disrespecting the call of the Oman duly sworn on you by Kow Ackon and others." The native tribunals continued to exercise their jurisdiction in the specified customary offences up to the middle of the 20th century.

Following the political disturbances in 1948 the Watson Commission was appointed to inquire into the situation. The commission recommended that there should be constitutional reforms. The Coussey Committee which undertook the task recommended the development of local administration and further pointed out some defects in the native courts' system. The Sir Arku Korsah Commission which was subsequently tasked with the examination of the native courts' system, recommended the setting up of local courts. And the Local Courts Act, 195814 was accordingly passed to implement Korsah's recommendation. The 1958 Local Court Act abolished the native tribunals and in their place created a number of local courts presided over not by chiefs any longer, but by lay magistrates appointed by the Minister of Interior. Later the Minister of Local Government took over the responsibility and appointment of lay magistrates.

The local courts were given jurisdiction to try only three specific customary offences which were:

(1)  Putting any person into fetish.

(2)  Recklessly, unlawfully or frivolously swearing an oath.

(3) Possessing any poisonous, noxious or offensive thing with intent to use such thing to endanger or destroy human life or to hurt, aggrieve or annoy any person.

They had no jurisdiction to try any other customary offence. And in respect of the above offences the maximum punishment the local courts could impose was a fine of £25 and/or a sentence of three months' imprisonment.

When the country attained Republican status, the local courts survived under the Courts Act, 1960 (CA 9) although the local Courts Act, 1958 was repealed. Sections 146 and 147 of CA 9 defined the criminal jurisdiction of the local courts, and section 147 empowered them to continue trying the same customary offences which they were trying under the repealed Local Courts Act, 1958. The Courts Decree, 1966 (NLCD 84) which superseded the Courts Act, 1960 abolished the local courts and provided that all cases pending before the local courts were to be continued and concluded by a district court having jurisdiction over the area in which such local courts were situated.

Now the customary offences which were spelt out in previous enactments and tried by the native and later local courts were conspicuously missing in NLCD 84. Furthermore, none of the courts set up in NLCD 84 were entrusted with jurisdiction to try any specified customary offence. NLCD 84 therefore formally brought to an end the prosecution and punishment of customary offences except that of disrespect to a chief, in our courts. Since then such offences have not resurfaced in any subsequent enactment, neither has any court been set up exclusively to handle customary offences as a whole.

3. NATURE OF CHIEFLY JURISDICTION AFTER 1958

The chiefs and their elders who are the repository of our customs did not also have any statutory jurisdiction to try and punish any one found to have committed any of the customary offences, nor to award customary reliefs to victims of such offences. In their early customary tribunals existing before the advent of British rule, and which tribunals were later statutorised into native tribunals, the chiefs exercised both criminal and civil jurisdictions in such matters. The chiefs had their own prisons into which those condemned to terms of imprisonment were incarcerated. And the Prisons Ordinance, 1883 legalised such traditional prisons. But Sir Arku  Korsah in his report in the 1950s into the native courts system found no justification for retaining the native court system. His lordship stated at 24 of his report:

"We consider the name Native Courts should be discarded for the main reason that the term is misleading when applied to the new courts. The expression may have been an appropriate description of the customary tribunals both before and after 1883, but ...The Native Courts have already got past this state and the term is quite inappropriate for the new Courts."

Hence the Local Courts Act, 1958 which stripped the chiefs of their native courts. Just before the enactment of this Local Courts Act, 1958, the Houses of Chiefs Act, 195814 had been passed on 26 July 1958. Now with the passing of the Local Courts Act, 1958 our traditional rulers lost completely all their ancient powers and jurisdiction to adjudicate on disputes other than disputes in matters of constitutional nature. For the Houses of Chiefs Act, 1958 retained the jurisdiction of the state councils and the houses of chiefs in matters of constitutional nature, and went on to create appeal commissioners appointed by the Judicial Commission to hear appeals in matters of constitutional nature affecting chiefs.

The Houses of Chiefs Act, 1958 was superseded by the Chieftaincy Act, 1961 (Act 81) which consolidated with amendments, enactments relating to chieftaincy. Act 81 took pains to ensure that the tribunals created under it, did not at any time try and punish anyone for any breach of a customary practice. Thus it gave the tribunals no jurisdiction to punish anyone for an alleged obstruction of their proceedings. Secondly, it provided in section 44 thereof that a traditional council shall not have authority to punish any person by imposing a fine or awarding a term of imprisonment except the power to impose customary constitutional sanctions and customary awards of a civil nature in respect of any cause or matter affecting chieftaincy.

The Chieftaincy Act, 1971 (Act 370) which repealed Act 81 retained the restrictions on the various chieftaincy tribunals from punishing those found to be in contempt and obstructing their proceedings and from punishing anyone in the form of imposing a fine or prison term.15 Act 370, the previous Act 81 and the Houses of Chiefs Act, 1958, restricted the judicial functions of the chiefs to determining causes or matters affecting chieftaincy, and created the National House of Chiefs in addition to the regional houses of chiefs and the traditional councils.

Section 53(1) of the Provisional National Defence Council (Establishment) Proclamation (Supplementary and Consequential Provisions) Law, 1982 (PNDCL 42) as amended made no changes in the functions, composition and powers of the National House of Chiefs, regional houses of chiefs and traditional councils established under the Constitution, 1979. Similarly, the Constitution, 1992 has not widened the jurisdiction of chiefs in their adjudication of chieftaincy disputes further than the existing one.

It is therefore clear that the statutory jurisdiction of our traditional rulers have since 1958 been confined to determining causes or matters affecting chieftaincy, and that since 1966 no institution of the State nor any individual is vested with jurisdiction to adjudicate and punish breaches of customary practices. However, both the Constitution, 1969 and the Constitution, 1979 gave power to Parliament to establish such inferior and traditional courts in addition to existing courts.16 But no such traditional courts were established. If such courts had been established, they might naturally have been the appropriate courts to take over the trial and punishment of such offences designated in the Native Courts Ordinances as "native customary offences." The Constitution, 1992 does not talk of establishing traditional courts, but it gives Parliament power in article 126(b) to establish such lower courts and tribunals which are deemed necessary for the effective administration of justice. And this provision is wide enough to enable Parliament to set up a court with jurisdiction to handle customary offences.

However, we must point out that the various Chieftaincy Acts did not deprive our traditional rulers of their customary authority to arbitrate in disputes. Thus section 5 of Act 81 for example provided:

"5. The power of any Chief to act extra-judicially as an arbitrator under customary law in any dispute in respect of which the parties thereto consent to his so acting is hereby preserved."16a

We do not have the above provision in either the Houses of Chiefs Act, 1958 nor the present Act 370. But the power mentioned in section 5 of Act 81 is not exclusively confined to the institution of chieftaincy alone but extends to every individual who agrees to adjudicate on a dispute between parties who have voluntarily consented to that effect. Thus its absence in Act 370 will not disable  any chief from so acting as an arbitrator when requested to do so by the litigants. Indeed the power of a chief to act as an arbitrator when so requested was regarded as part of the inherent customary functions of a chief during the passing of Act 370. For Mr Victor Owusu, the then Attorney-General, in moving the second reading of the Chieftaincy Bill in the National Assembly in 1971 said:

"I would like to demonstrate this distinction between customary functions and statutory functions with a few examples. Pouring libation on the black stool on important festivals, serving as customary arbitrator or conciliator in inter family disputes, receiving customary homage and tribute from subjects and performing all religious duties incidental to chieftaincy are functions which lie exclusively within the province of customary law. But exercising appellate jurisdiction over decisions of a traditional council of say the Manya Krobo Traditional Area is certainly not customary law. No paramount chief has appellate jurisdiction over another paramount stool. The only basis for the exercise of such appellate jurisdiction is the Constitution and statute."17

But this power to act as a customary arbitrator or conciliator, which can be exercised not only by chiefs but by any individual, has its own limitation and cannot therefore give jurisdiction for the punishment of those alleged to be in breach of customary practices. For from the language of section 5 of Act 81 and a long line of respectable authorities, no one can be forced to submit to the jurisdiction of any arbitrator. Therefore for any fine or award made at such an arbitration to be enforceable, it must be proved that the parties voluntarily submitted to be bound by the decision of the arbitrator. Because as Anin JA (as he then was) said in Nyaasemhwe v Afibiyesan18:

"For one thing, the party summoned may have attended the proposed arbitrators' call only out of respect for their dignified social position and with the limited intention of merely explaining himself or of giving his version of the dispute."

There is certainly an air of oddity surrounding a suggestion that a chief sitting as a customary arbitrator can legitimately try and punish a customary offender. Which offender would voluntarily offer himself to be punished? How fair would the chief and his elders be in view of the fact that they are the custodians and guardians of the alleged custom? The obvious likelihood of the chief and his elders being the prosecutors and judges at such an arbitration are so glaring as to vitiate the award of the arbitration. For an arbitration proceeding must observe and adhere, as much as possible to the basic tenets of the rules of natural justice.19

The difficulties involved in resorting to customary arbitration as a forum to punish customary offenders is illustrated by the case of Debrah v The Republic20 (supra). The facts of this case are that Yaw Debrah, a mason was seen in the early hours of the morning of 7 July 1991 collecting stone particles on the street in front of the paramount chief's palace. The stone particles he was collecting were chippings swept off following the tarring of the road by a contractor. When Yaw Debrah was confronted, he explained that he was going to use them in manufacturing grinding stones. He was reported to the linguist of the paramount chief and "arrested" in the customary manner and brought before the paramount chief and his elders on the ground that his conduct in collecting the stone particles from the street in front of the paramount chief's palace was against custom. And notwithstanding his explanation that he was not aware of any such custom and that he was going to use the stone particles in manufacturing grinding stones for a living, the paramount chief and his elders found his conduct to be in breach of the custom and fined him four bottles of schnapps, one live sheep and a pot of palm wine.

The quantity of stones he was alleged to have collected was not produced. In fact, he was alleged to have abandoned the stones he was collecting. Yaw Debrah found the rationale underlying the fines difficult to appreciate and so he refused to pay them. All attempts by the elders to get him pay the fines after the proceeding proved futile. And so he was reported to the police. The police charged him with the offence of disrespect to a chief contrary to section 53(a) of Act 370, and arraigned him before the Magistrate Court Grade 1, Kadjebi. The chief alleged to have been insulted was the paramount chief who sat with his elders, tried and imposed the fines on him. At the trial the police did not know which conduct of the accused constituted disrespectful conduct. The police investigator was asked in cross-examination:

"Q  Describe the conduct that has become the subject matter  of complaint.

A  I was not present at Nana Ogyeabour's court at the time the accused put up his conduct."

The chief to whom the disrespectful conduct was exhibited never testified, but his linguist who posed as the complainant listed the various conducts constituting the disrespect as follows21:

"(1) refusing to talk through the okyeame but speaking to the chief direct;

(2) leaving the palace without asking for leave from the okyeame as tradition demands; and

(3) by picking particles of stones from the chief's palace without seeking permission."

Thus the disrespectful conduct was made up of various customary misconducts, assuming the allegations of the linguist were true. The trial magistrate in overruling a submission of no case made on behalf of Yaw Debrah said:

"The prosecution's contention is not that the accused had no right to protest or show disagreement but that the manner he expressed it was against custom and tradition and constituted an offence under section 53(a) of the Chieftaincy Act, 1971 (Act 370)."

 

One may wonder whether the offence of disrespect to a chief created in section 53(a) of Act 370 is meant to protect a chief or custom.  Be that as it may, the chief's linguist made it clear that the proceedings at which Yaw Debrah was fined were an arbitration and that they did not report the accused to the police because of his refusal to pay the fines, but for his conduct, at the said proceedings. But it was quite glaring from the record of proceedings that the appellant, apart from collecting stone particles from the street in front of the palace of the paramount chief, did not put up any of the alleged disrespectful conducts during the alleged arbitration. However he did indicate after the fines were imposed that he did not see his way clear in paying the fines, and consequently refused to pay the fines. But he was indeed reported to the police because the chief and his elders were peeved at his refusal to pay the fines, and they hoped that the police intervention would bring fear into him to pay the fines. Yaw Deborah was eventually acquitted by the High Court, Hohoe following his appeal against the ruling of the trial magistrate.

But assuming that the proceedings were customary arbitration, does its enforcement consist in reporting the appellant to the police? Certainly not. Who was the complainant with whom he voluntarily submitted to the jurisdiction of the chief and his elders? And is this not a case where the chief and his elders were being prosecutors and judges at the same time? This case clearly demonstrates the legal impediments in the way of seeking the court's assistance in punishing people alleged to be in breach of customary offences.

It again demonstrates the short comings involved in resorting to customary arbitration as a forum to punish customary offenders.

4. PRESENT LEGAL POSITION UNDER THE CRIMINAL CODE, 1960 (ACT 29)

As pointed out earlier on, the position after 1966 is that the customary offences which were within the jurisdiction of the native courts and later the local courts, were not retained in our statute books to be tried and punished by an individual nor the present courts. The only customary offence which is still found in a statute is that of disrespect of a chief, found in section 53(a)of Act 370. But then we all know that day in and day out, at the self-created tribunals of our traditional rulers and elders, the citizens are punished for various customary offences as it happened in Yaw Debrah's case. The punishment imposed on the offenders are sometimes so harsh and out of proportion to the offence. The punishment takes the form of fines of a certain number of schnapps, sheep, foodstuffs and money. And sometimes the offender is banished from the locality.

Now what legal norms and propositions can enable the courts to sustain and enforce these punishments imposed at the self-created tribunals of our traditional rulers? Can it be argued that because our traditional rulers are the repository and guardians of our customs, they have an inherent jurisdiction to try and punish citizens alleged to be in breach of such customs?

It is conceded that our country is made up of various ethnic communities traditionally brought up along their own peculiar customs and practices. The impact of colonial administration and way of life had not succeeded in abolishing our traditional roots. Thus the institution of chieftaincy for example had been guaranteed in every Constitution of this nation. Because, as the Coussey Committee on Constitutional Reform observed in their report of 194922:

"The whole institution of chieftaincy is so closely bound up with the life of our communities that its disappearance would spell disaster. Chiefs and what they symbolize in the society are so vital that the subject of their future must be approached with the greatest caution. No African of the Gold Coast is without some admiration for the best aspects of chieftaincy and all would loathe to do violence to it any more than to the social values embodied in the institution itself. Criticisms there have been, but none coming from responsible people whom we have known or met is directed towards the complete effacement of chiefs."

We cannot therefore pretend not to appreciate that our chiefs, like the central government, must in the exercise of their traditional functions, have some authority to maintain their dignity, and demand punishment of those found to be in violation of the various customs and practices which they superintend. Hence the claim to inherent authority of chiefs to try and punish violations of customary practices. And this is the reason why those who are punished at the various tribunals of our chiefs, in reverence to the chief, obediently comply with the terms of the punishment, although, as we have shown above, there is no statutory justification for such punishments. The courts are therefore impotent to accept and enforce such punishments.

Some thinkers have even gone to the extent of equating the position of a chief and his subjects to that of a parent and his children, and accordingly argued that just as a parent has authority to punish his children so has a chief authority to punish his subjects.

But this reasoning is patently faulty. For whereas a chief is installed by his subjects who have a right to destool him, a parent is not installed nor created by his children. Furthermore, whereas the right of a parent to punish his children has statutory recognition and justification under section 31(i) of Act 29, the right of a chief to punish his subjects has no such statutory recognition. Nowhere under section 31 of Act 29 is force justified if meted out by a chief or on the orders of a chief to a subject who has violated any customary practice.

But in the light of one's increasing awareness of his fundamental human rights, any attempt to clothe any individual with inherent authority to punish a citizen for what that individual considers to be a breach of some customary practice will certainly be punctuated by the universally respected Latin maxim: nullum crimen sine lege, nulla poena sine lege - there must be no crime or punishment except in accordance with fixed, predetermined law. And this maxim has been regarded by most philosophers as a self-evident principle of justice.

The principle surfaces in various formulations of the fundamental principles of human rights, and is further enshrined in the criminal legislation and national constitutions of all civilised institutions and nations including Ghana. The principle is implied in the proposition that no citizen shall be deprived of his life, liberty or property without due process of law—a proposition which appears in the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments of the Constitution of the United States of America. The expression "due process of law" requires fairness in the government's dealing with persons. The "due process of law" idea dates from the English Magna Carter of 1215 which provided, inter alia, that the King could not imprison or harm a person "except by the lawful judgment of his peers or by the law of the land." The courts, especially those in the United States, have developed various safeguards called procedural due process, to ensure that persons accused of wrongdoing are treated fairly. The procedural due process includes the following requirements:

(1)  The law must be administered fairly.

(2)  A person must be informed of the charges against him and must be given a fair hearing.

(3)  The person bringing the charges must not be allowed to judge the case.

(4) Criminal laws must be clearly worded so that they give adequate warning of the action prohibited.

The American courts have again used the due process clauses of the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments to limit the contents of laws, even though there was no procedural unfairness. For example, they have declared unconstitutional some laws restraining personal freedoms and business on the ground that the laws violate the due process of law.

Thus in Winters v New York,23 the appellant, a book dealer, was convicted of violating a statute authoritatively construed by the state court to forbid publications that so massed their collection of pictures and stories of bloodshed and of lust "as to become vehicles for inciting violent and depraved crimes against the person." On appeal to the Federal Supreme Court, the conviction was reversed on the grounds that the statutes violated the due process of law clause of the Federal Constitution. The Federal Supreme Court explained that the statute was so vague as to make it difficult for the ordinary man to determine which publications came within its purview and thus was likely to lead to the punishment of a book dealer who would genuinely and innocently believe that he was publishing books outside the scope of the statute. The Supreme Court said at 515-520:

"The standards of certainty in statutes punishing for offences is higher than in those depending primarily upon civil sanction for enforcement. The crime must be defined with appropriate definiteness . . . There must be ascertainable standards of guilt. Men of common intelligence cannot be required to guess at the meaning of the enactment. The vagueness may be from uncertainty in regard to persons within the scope of the act or in regard to the applicable tests to ascertain guilt . . . It does not seem to us that an honest distributor of publications could know when he might be held to have ignored such a prohibition (as that in the statute here at issue)".

The Supreme Court then proceeded to make the following important pronouncement at 520: "Where a statute is so vague as to make criminal an innocent act, a conviction under it cannot be sustained." This practice of using the due process idea to assess the contents of enactments is called substantive due process as opposed to the procedural due process. But both methods are aimed at ensuring that not only the procedure by which a person is deprived of his life, liberty and property are clear but also the enactment by which he is so deprived are just, well-laid and unambiguous.

The self-evident principle of justice epitomised in the above Latin maxim is also implied in article 11(2) of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights adopted on 10 December 1948 by the General Assembly of the United Nations. Dicey, writing on this principle in relation to the unwritten Constitution of England said24:

 "Englishmen are ruled by the law, and by the law alone. A man may with us be punished for a breach of law, but he can be punished for nothing else."

In Nigeria, the 1958 Constitutional Conference mindful of the above principle, took a decision to abolish customary criminal law in the entire federation. Thus in the 1959 Bill of Rights, a section of which became article 22(10) of the Nigerian Constitution, 1963 it was provided:

"No person shall be convicted of a criminal offence unless that offence is defined and the penalty therefor is prescribed in a written law."

The only exception being the unwritten law relating to contempt of court. Thus in Aoko v Fagbemi25 wherein the applicant was convicted and sentenced by the Ijebu-Ijesha Grade D customary court for "committing adultery by living with another man without a judicial separation"—an offence unknown in the Nigerian criminal statutes, Fatayi-Williams J (as he then was) sitting at the High Court of the then Western Region of Nigeria quashed the conviction on grounds that it was contrary to the provision of section 22(10) of the Constitution.

And in Ghana, sections 8 and 10 of our Act 29 provide that apart from acts constituting contempt of court, no person shall be liable for punishment by the common law for any act. The expression "common law" as defined in section 17(1) of the Interpretation Act, 1960 (C A 4) embraces all uncodified offences whether customary or otherwise. Indeed the various past Constitutions of this country had been more explicit in making provisions identical in wording to that of article 22(10) of the Nigerian Constitution, 1963. Thus articles 20(11) of the Constitution, 1969 and 26(10) of the Constitution, 1979 provided:

"No person shall be convicted of a criminal offence unless that offence is defined and the penalty therefor is prescribed in a written law."

The same wording is adopted in article 19(11) of the Constitution, 1992 of the Fourth Republic.

Now in the face of section 8 of our Act 29 can it be said that an individual can be punished for any uncodified customary offence? I am aware that our various Constitutions and indeed, the Constitution, 1992 guarantee the institution of chieftaincy, but that guarantee does not limit the rights of the individual enshrined in that very Constitution. For the avoidance of doubt, article 12(2) of the Constitution, 1992, for example provides:

"12.(2) Every person in Ghana, whatever his race, place of origin, political opinion, colour, religion, creed or gender shall be entitled to the fundamental human rights and freedoms of the individual contained in this Chapter but subject to respect for the rights and freedoms of others and for the public interest."

And article 12(1) provides that these rights shall be enforceable by the courts.

As pointed out earlier, section 8 of Act 29 is one of the principles of the fundamental human rights largely rekindled by the writings of the English philosopher John Locke whose ideas of limited government and natural rights became part of the English Bill of Rights, 1689, the French Declaration of the Rights of Man, 1789 and the United States Bill of Rights 1791. And in Ghana it is gratifying to note that similar to the provisions in the Constitution, 1992 the PNDC in its Directive Principles of State Policy set out in section 1(b) of the Provisional National Defence Council (Establishment) Proclamation (Supplementary and Consequential Provisions) Law, 1982 (PNDCL 42) committed itself and all organs of government including the judiciary to:

"(b) respect for fundamental human rights and for the dignity  of the human person are to be cultivated among all sections of the society and established as part of the basis of social justice;"

The net effect of these two provisions is to render the courts in their criminal jurisdiction impotent in sustaining and enforcing fines and punishment imposed by our chiefs and elders on persons alleged to have committed uncodified customary offences.

5. CODIFICATION

It follows therefore that for the courts to have jurisdiction to deal and punish breaches of customary practices, such punishable customary practices should be defined and the punishment therefor prescribed in a written law. This is all what the law requires. The rationale for this requirement is stated by Glanville Williams as follows26:

"The citizen must be able to ascertain before hand how he stands with regard to the criminal law; otherwise to punish him for breach of that law is purposeless cruelty. Punishment in all its forms is a loss of rights or advantages consequent on a breach of law. When it loses this quality it degenerates into an arbitrary act of violence that can produce nothing but bad social effects. Opinions about what people ought morally to do are almost as numerous as human beings, but opinions about what people are obliged legally to do should be capable of being ascertained by legal research."

The courts of this country have no jurisdiction to create new crimes nor punish a person for any offence other than those specified in an enactment - contempt of court excepted. Indeed unlike the English courts, our courts do not have the power declared by Lord Mansfield in Jones v Randall27 to be vested in the English courts and is exercised when:

" whatsoever is contrary, bonos mores est decorum, the principles of our law prohibit, and the Kings Court, as the general censor and guardian of the public manners, is bound to restrain and punish."

The power of the Ghanaian courts to impose punishment for an offence is derived from the enactment which creates and defines that offence and which further prescribes the punishment for it. Thus unless a particular customary offence has been codified, like that of disrespect of a chief, no court can recognise and punish or endorse the punishment imposed by any other person for such an offence.

I certainly realise that it will not be easy to present one legislation on various customary offences and their punishment. For what is a customary offence in one community may not be an offence in another community. And this difficulty is evidenced by the fact that even in the era of the native courts, no uniform native customary offences could be legislated for all the country. In the Colony and Southern Section of Togoland, seven such offences were specified in the Schedules to the Ordinances. For the native courts in Ashanti and the Northern Territories, the Governor had to specify in an order, the native customary offences which the courts were empowered to handle. The local courts which replaced the native courts were empowered to handle only three of the previous customary offences - which have already been set out above. A single enactment setting out uniform customary offences and their punishments for the entire nation, therefore, is a difficult task.

Thus the solution lies first, in the making of bye-laws by the various district assemblies on what constitutes customary offences in their respective districts. Indeed inquiries made from a few district councils whether there are bye-laws on the various bans imposed in the locality during the celebration of some major local festivals had negative answers. Previous local government laws including the present Local Government Law, 1988 (PNDCL 207) as amended, empower each district to make bye-laws in respect of the numerous functions assigned to the district. And the councils can, in pursuance of these objectives, enact the relevant bye-laws in respect of any customary offence which obstructs their goals.

The second mode of achieving codification is that a traditional area may take advantage of the provisions of sections 41-47 of Act 370 to make proposals through its regional house of chiefs to the National House of Chiefs for onward transmission to the Head of State to make a legislative instrument giving effect to the trial and punishment of specified customary offences in the area. The procedure as set out in the relevant sections are simple: Each traditional council is empowered to consider the customary rule of practice in force in its area and if that rule of practice is uncertain or it is considered desirable to be modified, the council must make proposals to its regional house of chiefs which shall study the proposals and forward same to the National House of Chiefs with its own views on them if necessary. The National House of Chiefs on receipt of the proposals would in turn study the proposals and forward same, with its own recommendations if necessary, to the Head of State. The latter, after consultation with the Chief justice, may make a legislative instrument giving effect to the proposals. Searches for legislative instruments made in pursuance of these provisions of Act 370 have yielded no fruitful results. Only two legislative instruments, LI 32 and LI 59, dealing with Dagomba State and the Akwapim State respectively - both of which dealt with succession to the respective paramount stools were discovered.

These are the avenues open to any traditional area which is of the considered view that particular conducts are offensive to the cultural well-being of the area, and that the said conducts ought to be tried and punished.

The third solution which can be explored is to impress upon the incoming Fourth Republican Parliament to set up traditional courts with jurisdiction to handle such offences.

Today society is fast expanding and changing in line with rapid scientific and enlightened ideas. Science has made it possible to explain what was inexplicable in the olden days. Barrenness in women, infant mortality, drought, and sudden death arising from heart failures and so on, are now known not to be the work of any juju nor witches. And customary offences based on such obsolete ideas cannot be permitted to exist in our modern days. To tolerate a community which still brook such primitive ideas and resort to punishing innocent subjects accused of such practices, is to condone primitive brutality. As Taylor JSC said in Hammond v Odoi28 "Old customs under the pressure of changing circumstances give way to new ones." We cannot therefore still live in the era in which what constituted a customary offence and the punishment for it depended more on the whims and caprices of the elders and the fetish priests. Such a condition is bound to result in injustice and oppression to sections of the community.

The codification of punishable customary offences would serve real purpose. First, the authorities who would codify the said offences would satisfy themselves that the said offences are in line with the general law of the land. Secondly, the codification itself would satisfy the requirements of section 8 of Act 29 and thereby afford every citizen the opportunity to know beforehand the nature and punishment for such an offence. For once our various communities are no longer inhabited solely by the indigenous people but also by others who are compelled by national assignments and other vocations, there is the need for some certainty in what is and what is not an offence in the area.

6. CIVIL ACTION IN RESPECT OF CUSTOMARY OFFENCES

We have so far dealt with the subject as if it is only through the criminal jurisdiction of the courts that breaches of customary practices can be remedied. But just as a stealing case can be the subject matter of a civil action for conversion, so can a person who is affected or feels aggrieved by any breach of customary practice institute an action for compensation, declaration or any appropriate relief. And of course in a civil suit there is no need for the codification of the customary practice in question. What is important in such a civil suit is for the plaintiff to succeed in proving his case or the defendant in establishing his defence. Let us take the example of a community which believes that it is a taboo for a man to grow beard and that any man who violates this taboo would have to provide drinks and sheep to pacify the gods. Mr A, a bearded man, had just been transferred to the community. He is approached by the elders of the community to shave his beard but he refuses. An elder of the community who stays with this man in the house believing that his refusal to shave is a bad omen to his household, issues a writ at the court claiming an order to compel Mr A to shave his beard,  ¢100,000 damages for breach of a customary practice, an injunction restraining him from further growing beard and so on. Are the courts bound to award the elder his reliefs if he succeeds in proving his claim? To answer this question let us look at one or two relevant decided cases.

In Abaye v Akatia29 the plaintiff's food crops in his backyard garden were destroyed by the defendants during the funeral rites of the Tufuhene of the area. It was an accepted custom in the area that whenever a Tufuhene died the people while performing the customary funeral rites could go on a rampage slaughtering goats, sheep and destroying food crops grown in the backyard of houses. The trial magistrate found that the custom was an established one in the area and dismissed the plaintiff's claim. On appeal to the High Court, the plaintiff argued that the alleged customary practice was illegal and obnoxious and therefore unenforceable. The High Court dismissed same, holding that any required change in the custom was to be effected by those who practised it and not by the court. His lordship said30a:

" it is not given to the court to conduct a research into the time-honoured practices in order to pass a judgment on its validity or otherwise. The court is incompetent to do this, I think, for this appears to be one of the functions of the appropriate traditional council in a given case."

The court obviously had in mind the provisions of sections 41-47 of Act 370 which empower traditional councils to make representations for a change or modification of a particular rule of customary law in their locality. However, the High Court realised that the said custom was not in consonance with modern trend of affairs and indeed the economics of the day. He said30b:

"It may well now be arguable that considering the modern trend of affairs and the fact that since the days of 'Operation Feed Yourself' in the mid-seventies, the Ghanaian has been made increasingly aware that he must try as much as possible to produce food to feed himself and his family on every available piece of land free for cultivation, be it in the backyard, or the outskirts of a town, or a fallow land, a custom which permits destruction of food-crops like yam, cocoyam, maize, plantain, sugar-cane and vegetables may well be considered to be out of tune with modern thinking and indeed the economics of the times. It may even appear to be in conflict with the general common law of the land and contrary to public policy."

Notwithstanding the above weighty observations, the High Court confirmed the magistrate's judgment and thereby sanctioned such a custom because in its view the courts are incompetent to refuse to disapprove such practices. But, are the courts really incompetent to disapprove customary practices which are contrary to public policy and at variance with the law of the land?

Before attaining our Republican status, these customary practices and rules were to be applied by the courts if they were not "repugnant to natural justice, equity and good conscience."30c The existence or otherwise of an alleged customary rule or practice at that time was a question of fact and its enforceability by the courts had to pass the repugnancy test. Thus in Wood v Thompson31 Earnshaw J said:

"Any native law or custom depending on slavery ... must at once be regarded with suspicion; and if ... repugnant to natural justice, equity and good conscience ...  then this Court is not compelled, and indeed ought not, to enforce it."

But since 1960 such customary rules of practice are part of the laws of the country and no longer a question of fact. And the test of equity, natural justice and good conscience which they had to pass, was no longer enacted in any subsequent statutes. But then as part of the law of the land, these customary rules and practices must, like any other law, be in conformity with the general law of the land. For as Parker J said in Johnson v Clark32:

"... a custom to be valid must be such that, in the opinion of a trained lawyer, it is consistent, or, at any rate, not inconsistent, with those general principles which, quite apart from particular rules or maxims, lie at the root of our legal system."

Similarly Taylor J (as he then was) in Ibrahim v Amalibini33  made the same point as follows:

"The yardsticks of equity, natural justice and good conscience used in the colonial and pre-Republican days for measuring their validity were not re-enacted after their repeal. Obviously our Republican legislature must have considered these colonial yardsticks as an affront to the integrity of our customary laws and not in keeping with the dignity of a modern sovereign state. As a result after 1 July 1960, the customary laws of the various tribal communities became part of the law of the country and the only test for their validity is whether they fulfil those conditions which under the general law of Ghana must be satisfied by all customary laws."

And what are some of the conditions which are to be satisfied? Certainly the customary rule must not be inconsistent with the general law. Thus where the general law forbids an intentional killing of a human being on grounds not justified by any of the matters set out in section 31 of Act 29, and a rule of customary practice in a particular community sanctions an intentional killing of any woman who has grown hairs on her chin, it is doubtful whether notwithstanding the fact that rules of customary practices are part of the law of the land, such a customary rule would be accepted by the courts as part of the customary law of Ghana.

Again the customary rule of practice must not be contrary to the national aspirations and public policy. Thus in Akrofi v Akrofi34 Sowah J (as he then was) did not hesitate to castigate any customary rule of practice which discriminates against a person solely on the basis of sex, in the following words:

"I am of the view that if there be such a custom and I do not so find, whereby a person is discriminated against solely upon the ground of sex that custom has outlived its usefulness and is at present not in conformity with public policy. Our customs if they are to survive the test of time must change with the times."

Again in Republic v Accra New Town District Magistrate Court; Ex parte Papafio,35 an action based on Grunshie custom, Archer J. (as he then was) said:

"The question is whether according to Grunshie custom, an adulterer is entitled to institute proceedings to claim the child of the adulterous union . . . Such an action by an adulterer is no doubt contrary to public morality and decency but I would venture to suggest that if the categories of public policy are not yet closed, such an action is clearly contrary to public policy that unashamed adulterers should be permitted by the courts to demonstrate without any sense of compunction their acts of immorality although the spouses have reconciled and have decided to forgive each other."

And in Attah v Esson36 the Court of Appeal disaffirmed a customary  rule of practice whereby a customary landlord is permitted to enter onto agricultural land he had granted under customary tenancy to gather the fruits of economic trees planted by the tenant. The court held that this customary rule became out-dated and ceased to be law as soon as conditions in society changed so as to make it unreasonable for persons to conduct themselves by it. The court, per Amissah JA said36a:

"No proposition would be more out of accord with the hopes and aspirations of Ghanaians today than that a landlord who has spent no effort whatsoever towards that end should enter and collect at will the fruits of the labour of his tenant. Who amongst us would today be prepared to take land to cultivate on that basis? We cannot imagine an arrangement more ruinous of agricultural enterprise, subversive of expansion and consequently prejudicial to national development than that."

It is therefore clear that although various rules of customary practices have since 1960 been questions of law for the courts, the courts have since then ensured that such customary rules are in conformity with the general law of the land, and that they have refused to accept such rules whenever the rules are found to be contrary to public policy. Indeed Edward Wiredu J (as he then was) in Abangana v Akologo37 refusing to enforce the customary rule whereby a wife can be compelled to cohabit and have sexual intercourse with her husband against her will said:

"The principle of law relevant here as I understand it is that equity does nothing in vain. If therefore the trial court found as a fact that there was no love between the plaintiff and his wife ... then its order asking the woman to go back to the plaintiff was, with respect, wrong and cannot be sustained on appeal."

Now on the basis of the attitude of the courts since 1960, it is my respectful view that when the High Court in Abaye v Akatia (supra) found that the custom under consideration was, in the court's own words, "out of tune with modern thinking and indeed the economics of the times" and further "in conflict with the general common law of the land and contrary to public policy" it ought not to have given its blessing to the custom. A custom contrary to the general law of the land and public policy cannot be part of the customary law of the land. But in fairness to the High Court, its attention was not drawn to the Court of Appeal's decision in Attah v Esson (supra) in which the custom involved had also been found to militate against the agricultural policy of the nation.

We certainly concede that presently it is not the concern of the courts to change a particular custom. As Archer JA (as he then was) put it in Tanor v Akosua Koko38: "Whether the custom should be abolished or not, is not a matter for the courts." But the courts being primarily concerned with doing justice according to law, must ensure that any customary rule of practice which comes before them to be enforced is in accord with the public policy which the general law of the land must protect.

Indeed the power of the courts, under the PNDC regime, to refuse to enforce any rule of law, whether customary or otherwise, which is inconsistent with the national aspirations or public policy as set out in section 1 of PNDCL 42, is put beyond doubt by the provisions of section 61 of the Law. The section reads:

"61. Notwithstanding the provisions of any enactment all laws in existence before the coming into force of the Proclamation, especially where they are derived from foreign sources, shall only be in operation to the extent that they are compatible with national aspirations."

On the strength of this provision, the Court of Appeal in a recent case of Akorninga v Akawagre39 did not hesitate to declare as invalid a Frafra customary practice which provided that on the death of a husband his widow was perpetually restrained from marrying any other person except the successor or a member of the deceased's family. The court held, as per the headnote, that such a Frafra customary practice:

"... was so unjust and blatantly discriminatory against Frafra women, it could not under modern conditions be considered part of the customary law of the country and could not have survived the various constitutional provisions which had now ended up with PNDCL 42."

The position therefore is that the only impediments in the way of instituting civil actions to claim damages for breach of a customary rule or practice or to enforce compliance with same is that the said rule or practice must not be against public policy nor the general law of the land.

Thus in the problem posed earlier on, there is no doubt that the landlord's claim against Mr A is one touching on the fundamental rights of Mr A in deciding to grow or not to grow a beard. And since it is one of the national aspirations of the government to protect such fundamental liberties of the individuals as set out in section 1 of PNDCL 42, the landlord's action appears to be in conflict with this national aspiration. Accordingly, notwithstanding the fact that Mr A's beard is a breach of the customary practice, the landlord may not be entitled to his reliefs.

7. CONCLUSION

We began by alluding to the fact that there are numerous ethnic communities with their varied tribal taboos and offences in this country. Some of these taboos have laudable objectives, others have been demonstrated by scientific researches to be based on ignorance and therefore not worthy of commendation. For example, the Krobo custom of "dipo" is aimed at ensuring that girls lead virtuous lives before they reach the age of puberty. And in the face of the present campaign against promiscuous sexual practices to stop the spread of the AIDS epidemic, no custom can be more in keeping with such a national objective than the "dipo" custom. However one wonders whether the harsh punishment for its breach is realistic and in accord with the fundamental right of the girls concerned.

On the other hand, the custom of female circumcision practised in a number of communities is severely criticised by our health personnel to be injurious to the health of the females. To what extent then, should the court be prepared to enforce compliance with this custom if a female refuses to be circumcised in such a community? As already pointed out, it is not the duty of the courts to change customs. It is for the elders and traditional rulers who insist on compliance with these customs to be alive to the truth that society is not static, and that with the passage of time various practices and offences must be moulded in line with the changing circumstances of the day.

Indeed it is difficult to believe that in the present day world, an accusation that someone is using juju to demobilise a hospital mortuary so that the dead remains of a citizen deposited there cannot have a decent burial, is regarded as such a serious accusation by the chiefs and people of a community that the accused ought in accordance with the custom of that community, to undergo a trial by ordeal to establish his innocence. The punishment for refusing to undergo such a trial is very harsh. And in a particular instance, when the accused went through the trial by ordeal at a fetish shrine and was exonerated, the community refused to accept the result. The accused finding the reaction of the community to his acquittal very hostile took action at the High Court against the chiefs of the community for damages for defamation and malicious prosecution. The particulars of the malicious prosecution being that he was maliciously prosecuted before the fetish shrine by the chiefs, and that since he had been acquitted at the said trial, he was entitled to damages for his prosecution. The High Court was therefore called upon to examine, inter alia, whether in the face of our modern notions of fair trial, a trial by ordeal was one which deserved the recognition of the courts so as to enable the plaintiff to be entitled to his damages for malicious prosecution.40

The Constitution, 1992 of the Fourth Republic which came into force on 7 January 1993, has far-reaching provisions which are bound to punctuate the enforcement of most customary practices and offences. Article 1(2) of the Constitution unambiguously lays it down that the Constitution is the supreme law of the land and any other law (which of course includes customary laws) found to be inconsistent with any provision of it shall, to the extent of the inconsistency, be void. Commenting on an identical provision in the Constitution, 1969 in Sam v Comptroller of Customs and Excise41 Taylor J (as he then was) said:

"[T]his country has never in its history experienced so profound and imperial a constitutional provision as transcendent and supreme as article 1(2) of the Constitution, reminiscent as it is of medieval, monarchical absolutism ...  It seems to me that this constitutional provision at once cast into a crucible . . . all the laws of the land and subjected them to the test of constitutional propriety. All those that failed the test became void and the task of an interpreter and therefore of a Ghanaian judge became to some extent simplified: It is to search in the crucible for the relevant remnants of the inconsistent provisions so that he can refuse to give effect to them. Thus the concept of legislative supremacy, which necessarily colours judicial interpretation in England, can hardly apply here. Our legislature is a subordinate not sovereign institution and it bends to the supremacy of our Constitution as our courts must bow as well to our Constitution when construing any enactment of our legislature."

The two most important chapters of the Constitution likely to affect customary practices and offences are chapters 5 and 6. Chapter 5 deals with fundamental human rights and freedoms, while chapter 6 deals with the Directive Principles of State Policy. Article 12 enjoins all persons in Ghana together with the executive, legislature and judiciary to respect and uphold the provisions of the fundamental human rights and freedoms. These provisions guarantee the right to life, liberty and property, equality before the law, immunity from torture and inhuman punishment, presumption of innocence, a fair and open trial, the right to counsel, no ex post facto laws, and of course, freedom of speech, freedom of religion and the right of assembly. The provisions accordingly prohibit the conviction of any person of a criminal offence which had not been defined and the punishment thereof prescribed in a written law. It further guarantees the inviolability of the dignity of all persons. And mindful, perhaps, of the fact that some customary practices are injurious to the individual and ought not to be regarded as part of the law of the land, the framers of the Constitution blast such injurious customary practices in article 26(2) in the following words: "All customary practices which dehumanise or are injurious to the physical and mental well-being of a person are prohibited." And in line with this fundamental right, article 39(2) dealing with the cultural objectives of the Directive Principles of State Policy, directs all organs of the Republic including the judiciary to ensure that only appropriate customary and cultural values are adopted and developed as an integral part of the growing needs of the society and further to abolish those customary and cultural values which are injurious to the health and well-being of the individual.

The message of the Constitution, 1992 on customary practices and offences is therefore very clear. For if after 1960 the court did not have power to pass judgment on the validity of such practices, as contended in Abaye v Akatia (supra), there is no doubt that under the Fourth Republican Constitution, 1992, the courts have been given ample authority not only to declare a customary practice as invalid, but also to order its abolition. Society is dynamic and such provisions are necessary to enable the rejection of archaic and obsolete customs with a view to keeping pace with the ever changing patterns of development.

It is however gratifying to note that our chiefs were among the category of persons who had the greatest number of representatives at the Consultative Assembly which drafted the Constitution, 1992. Their knowledge of the provisions of chapters 5 and 6 is thus presumed. And it behoves our chiefs to ensure that the provisions of the said chapters are respected and applied to all citizens not only by the central government but also by them in their dealings with their subjects. The chief and his elders forming the traditional government in our various localities are, like the central government, bound by the provisions of the Constitution. This Constitution has received the widest publicity in the rural areas than any previous Constitution of this country. It has been simplified for the understanding of the ordinary man. It has also been translated into various local languages for the benefit of those who cannot read English. The public's awareness of their rights under the Constitution cannot thus be underrated. Any attempt therefore to subject individuals to obnoxious and unhealthy customary practices, are likely to be resisted in the courts.

The fundamental human rights guaranteed in the Constitution, 1992 are neither novel nor too difficult to be respected. They are rights which had had their beginnings from the days of the great Biblical prophets. When Moses brought the Ten Commandments down from Mount Sinai, he published to his tribes not only commandments against wrongdoing but a proclamation of fundamental rights. Apart from the commandments relating to the deity, the following related to man: Thou shalt not kill; Thou shalt not steal; Thou shalt not bear false witness; Thou shalt not covert thy neighbour's house nor his wife, nor his manservant, nor his maidservant nor his cattle, nor any of his things. In other words everyone is entitled to be free from arbitrary and unlawful interference.

But one of the areas where the fundamental rights of citizens of this nation are much abused is in the arbitrary sanctions and punishments imposed for breaches of alleged customary offences. This may be due to the fact that some of our chiefs believe that they still possess the ancient powers of their predecessors to adjudicate and impose punishments on their subjects. But we have demonstrated in this paper that apart from the power to adjudicate in chieftaincy disputes, they were rid of all such powers as far back as 1958. The Constitution, 1992 is an emphatic road-block to the exercise of any such arbitrary power. Its provisions on the individual rights and freedoms create moral, social and legal obligations on our traditional rulers to respect them in their dealings with their subjects, and further to take steps to ensure that all meaningful customary offences are brought in line with the requirements of the Constitution if such offences are to be punishable by the courts.

To this end we are of the considered view that a way out of getting the courts' assistance in punishing customary offences and in enforcing compliance with the various customary practices, is for the National House of Chiefs to undertake and execute the tasks assigned to it under article 272(b) and (c) of the Constitution, 1992. The tasks are to:

"(b) undertake the progressive study, interpretation and codification of customary law with a view to evolving, in appropriate cases, a unified system of rules of customary law, and compiling the customary laws and lines of succession applicable to each stool or skin;

(c) undertake an evaluation of traditional customs and usages with a view to eliminating those customs and usages that are outmoded and socially harmful."
