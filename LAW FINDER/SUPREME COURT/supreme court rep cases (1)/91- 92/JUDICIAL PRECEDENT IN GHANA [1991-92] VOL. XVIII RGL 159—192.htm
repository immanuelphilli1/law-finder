JUDICIAL PRECEDENT IN GHANA [1991-92] VOL. XVIII RGL 159—192

 Taylor J. N. K.

 

*

INTRODUCTION

To appraise and understand the doctrine of judicial precedent in Ghana one needs full access to the judicial decisions of the Ghana superior courts. The body with the statutory duty of providing such access is the Council for Law Reporting and before its establishment on 17 August 1971,1 that responsibility was undertaken by the General Legal Council.

The General Legal Council was established on 9 August 1958 by the Legal Profession Act, 19582 and was given general responsibility for the legal profession and the organisation of legal education in Ghana. Presumably, it was under the umbrella of these dual statutory obligations thus imposed that the General Legal Council undertook not unreasonably to issue and publish a series of law reports to be called the Ghana Law Reports, to commence with judgments given on and after 1 January 1959.3

The Ghana Law Reports for 1959 and the five other Ghana Law Reports from 1960 to 1964 were published by the General Legal Council and the series from 1965 to date are the handiwork of the Council for Law Reporting. The statute establishing the Council for Law Reporting was passed as a result of the untiring efforts of Mr E Kofi Tetteh, then Editor of the Ghana Law Reports, who in a report which he submitted to the General Legal Council spelled out the problems facing law reporting in Ghana and indicated possible solutions. He subsequently published it as an article.4 In that article, he succeeded in demonstrating in an overwhelmingly convincing manner the need for the establishment of a Council for Law Reporting in Ghana. In further effort, he succeeded in pressing the then Attorney-General, Mr Victor Owusu, to prevail on the government to sponsor and promote the idea. His tenacity bore fruit in the eventual promulgation of the Council for Law Reporting Act, 1971.5

The statute establishing the Council was repealed, but the Council was continued in existence by the repealing enactment.6 In the circumstance, the 20th Anniversary of the founding of the Council for Law Reporting falls on 17 August 1991 and its anniversary year runs from 17 August 1991 to 16 August 1992.

The functions7 of the Council spelled out in the repealed Act and repeated in the new enactment without any alteration in form or substance are stated thus:

“The Council shall be responsible for the preparation and publication of the report known as ‘the Ghana Law Reports’ containing the judgments, rulings and opinions of the superior courts in the country and may also effect such other publications as in the opinion of the Council could conveniently be effected together with the preparation and publication of the said reports.”

Law reporting in this country was started a long time ago. The early reports were privately prepared by lawyers. They commenced with the admirable pioneering work of John Mensah Sarbah, the first indigenous barrister who in his Fanti Customary Laws first published in 1897 reported a number of cases spanning about 50 years from 19 April 1844,8 barely two months after the Bond of 1844 to 26 November 1894.9  A second series by Sarbah in his Fanti Law Reports published in London in 1904 covered cases from 20 January 1845 to 6 January 1903.

Hayes Redwar a former expatriate puisne judge in this country in his Comments published in 1909 reported a number of cases from 1889 to 1909. In his Gold Coast Native Institutions published in 1903, Casely Hayford, an indigenous barrister, reported a few cases from 1887 to 1901. Mr. Justice Earnshaw, also an expatriate puisne judge of the country from 1909 to 1910, published in 1910 the judgments he delivered as a puisne judge covering his period of office.

The most ambitious and certainly prolific of the unofficial reports were the Reports of Cases in two volumes in 1915 by Peter Awoonor Renner, a barrister-at-law of Lincolns Inn and President of the Gold Coast Bar. Mr. Renner’s reports covered the period from 186810 to 1914.11 King-Farlow’s Gold Coast Judgments covering the period 1915-1917 was published in 1917.

From 1919 onwards official reports of some judgments of the Divisional Courts and the Full Court started to trickle into the system. These covered the period from 1911 to 1937 but the last of the reports covered selected judgments of the Divisional Courts for the period April 1931 to 1937. Apparently these reports include a complete set of all judgments of the Privy Council on appeal from this country published in 1929 and covering entirely the period 1874 to June 1928.

When the West African Court of Appeal was set up, fourteen series of reports which included appeals from this country were published and were cited as "WACA" reports, and they covered the period from 1930 to March 1955. From April 1955 to March 1960 two reports, the first, 15 WACA, covering Nigerian cases from April to December 1959 and the second, 16 WACA, covering selected judgments from Sierra Leone and the Gambia for the period April 1955 to March 1960 were also published. These two reports contain no cases from this country. Mr Justice Ollennu in his Customary Land Law included selected decisions of our superior courts covering the period 1943 to 1961 and the UK Journal of African Law has selected decisions from 1946 onwards.

Between March 1955 and the end of 1958, after the establishment of the General Legal Council, a series of three unofficial reports in the tradition of the early private reporters, but with some financial assistance from our government12 appeared as West African Law Reports (WALR). The three volumes covered cases decided in the Supreme Court of the Gold Coast; the Supreme Court of Ghana; the Court of Appeal of Ghana; High Court of Ghana; Supreme Court of the Colony of the Gambia; the High Court of the Gambia Protectorate; the West African Court of Appeal and mostly appropriate cases, including many Ghanaian cases, decided in the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council.

Some time between 1967-1968, Dr Woodman of the Faculty of Law in the University of Ghana was commissioned by the General Legal Council and caused to be published under the authority of the council three volumes of reports of Ghana Land Cases, containing cases decided in the Divisional Court from 1938 to 1955. The reports include certain cases decided in the courts of the Chief Commissioner of Ashanti and the Northern Territories.

Pending the publication of the Ghana Law Reports, the Judicial Service produced in the late 1950s, after independence, half-yearly cyclostyled decisions of the superior courts cited as Written Judgments Delivered. They covered two types of cases: criminal and civil and in each case the relevant half-yearly periods are January to June and July to December. These continued till the later years of the 1960s when the official Ghana Law Reports sponsored by the General Legal Council made their appearance. But when, however, perennial printing delays started afflicting the Ghana Law Reports, another series of cyclostyled reports known as Current Cases (CC) appeared from February 1965 onwards but their production ceased at the end of 1970 when the 1971 Ghana Law Reports took the field.

The Current Cases were not intended to be cited as authority in the courts. The aim was to notify its readers of the existence of the judgments digested in order to narrow the time between the delivery of the relevant judgments and their becoming available to the profession as a result of appearance in the Ghana Law Reports.13 The delay in producing the Ghana Law Reports however seems to have impelled the Council once again to introduce another series since September 1981 which is cited as Ghana Law Reports Digest (GLRD). These digests contain the headnotes of the edited judgments and rulings as approved by the judges who delivered them.

Quite recently in 1990 another privately sponsored reports cited as West African Supreme Courts Reports (WASC), covering cases decided in the final appellate courts of Nigeria and Ghana14 have appeared on the scene. They are supposed to be weekly law reports confined apparently to cases in the Supreme Courts of these countries.

I have deliberately drawn attention to these reports as a preface and an introduction to my discussion of the doctrine of judicial precedent in Ghana. I have done this because any meaningful exposition of the application of the doctrine is predicated on the reporting of cases and the availability of such reports. It is not just the reporting of a single case on a matter or an issue but rather the reporting of a series of cases on the matter or the issue, with such cases illustrating a relevant rule of law running through the cases and applicable logically without straining their import to the matter or the issue. And the rule of law so identified and illuminated must be in accordance with and not subversive of well-recognised fundamental principles which inform the common law and sustain the legal system.

This is essential to a true understanding of the doctrine of judicial precedent. As Sir Carleton Allen so succinctly put it:

". . . precedents are employed in order to establish principles. Throughout the whole application of the law, the principles are primary and the precedents are secondary, and if we lose sight of this fact, the precedents become a bad master instead of a good servant."15

All the law reports I have adverted to herein are repositories of the precedents which are applied in the superior courts of Ghana, for the sake of achieving the one and only purpose for which the courts exist, and for which the judges were appointed, namely to do justice within the law without fear or favour, affection or ill-will.16

The Concept of Judicial Precedent

The superior courts in Ghana today and those that preceded them, since Courts17 operating under the common law system of justice were first established in some parts of this country, have applied judicial precedent profusely. This means the utilisation of some form of the rule of stare decisis in their judicial decisions. Stare decisis, a Latin phrase, is a constricted rendering of the Latin maxim: stare decisis et non quieta movere which means literally: “to adhere to what has already been decided and not to unsettle established things."

The concept of stare decisis is therefore merely a rule or maxim which many persons on occasions find convenient and indeed feel constrained by the logic of a particular situation to resort to in resolving problems. Clearly, although the terms judicial precedent and stare decisis are often interchanged in the nomenclature of legal writers, it would seem that the terms are inherently not identical. Implicit in my approach is a dichotomy of the rule of stare decisis and the doctrine of judicial precedent. Stare decisis I perceive as a general philosophical concept embodying a normal human tendency which can be said to be a rule or tool of universal application in the reasoning process of mankind. Judicial precedent on the other hand is in my view, a jurisprudent concept and a particular and peculiar doctrine applied in specific jurisdictions for the decision making process of their judicial system.

In the adjudicatory environment of the English courts this normal human tendency of finding a rational foundation for one's decision by praying in aid the analogy of previous thinking was not uncommon. Such reasoning in previous cases however, crystallising as precedent with legal conditions under which a court of law is bound to follow them as a doctrine is not a universal phenomenon. Many continental judicial systems renounce it. The American jurist Wigmore is critical of an inflexible application of the stare decisis rule. He states18:

"Stare decisis as an absolute dogma has seemed to me an unreal fetish. The French civil code expressly repudiates it and though French and other continental judges do follow precedent to some extent, they do so presumably only to the extent that justice requires it for safety's sake."

But as Sir Carlton Allen so rightly points out19:

"It has never been claimed, even in the most rigidly codified systems, that the judge should shut his mind to the reasoning of others in like circumstances. No intelligent system would so crudely paralyse the indispensable instruments of analogy and parity of reasoning.

The concept of judicial precedent as we inherited it from the English common law,20 is a doctrine by which under certain circumstances previous judicial decisions are considered binding on the courts.

Historical Foundation

The changing phase of the rule of stare decisis into a judicial doctrine which the courts are obliged to respect, developed very slowly in English law. Traces of the doctrine baldly and rather widely stated can be gleaned from Bracton's work21:

"If any new and unwanted circumstances hereto unprecedented in the realm shall arise then if anything analogous has happened before, let the case be adjudged in like manner (Si tamen similia evenerint per similia indicentur) since it is good opportunity for proceeding a similibus ad similia."

In medieval times a trend discernible in the Year Books which was to influence the future of the doctrine, was developed in an apparently diametrically opposed fashion. Sir Carlton Allen noted it22:

". . . judges continued to be in two minds—at one time they would say, like Hillary J. that they could not, even if they would, change the established law; at another they seemed disposed to hold that no amount of precedent could justify what they considered to be bad law; and certainly no medieval judge would have conceded that, merely because a doctrine had been laid down in previous cases, it was necessarily law which he was bound to follow."

It is doubtful whether this vacillation did disappear entirely from the English system, seeing the prodigious number of cases which have been overruled over the years by British appellate courts.

By 1673 John Vaughan who was the Chief Justice of the Common Pleas from 1668 to 1674 ventured to describe the nature of the doctrine prevalent at this time as he understood it thus23:

"An extra-judicial opinion given in or out of Court is no more than the prolatium of him who gives it. An opinion given in Court, if not necessary to the judgment given of record, but that it might have been as well given if no such, or a contrary opinion had been broached, is no judicial decision, nor more than a gratis dictum. But an opinion, though erroneous, concluding to the judgment, is a judicial opinion, because delivered under the sanction of the judge's oath, upon deliberation, which assured it is, or was when delivered, the opinion of the deliverer. Yet if a court gave [sic] judgment judicially, another Court is not bound to give the like judgment unless it thinks [sic] that judgment first given was according to law."

Clearly the obiter dictum of the modern law is admirably highlighted in this passage. This is the period of Lord Mansfield and by this time the doctrine was beginning to put on some of its modern garb, and in Jones v Randall24 he attempted to state its rationale in language which reflects the true doctrine even today, but which it seems has not been properly appreciated by many. He said:

"The law of England would be a strange science if indeed it were decided upon precedents only. Precedents serve to illustrate principles and to give them a fixed certainty. But the law of England, which is exclusive of positive law, enacted by statute, depends upon principles, and these principles run through all the cases according as the particular circumstances of each have been found to fall within the one or the other of them."

And in Fisher v Prince25 he said that "[t]he reason and spirit of cases make law, not the letter of particular precedents", a view which succinctly states the modern rule relating to the authoritative quality of ratio decidendi in the doctrine of judicial precedent. This aspect of the doctrine was adverted to, inter alia, by many judges notably Lord Hardwicke in Gorton v Hancock26 and Parker CB in Omychund v Parker.27

Around the closing years of the 18th century the doctrine as was to prevail in England for a long period had been quite developed and had crystallised. And in 1833, Parke J one of the greatest common law lawyers of his generation reaffirmed and formulated it in resounding language which is considered as the locus classicus. In Mirehouse v Rennell28 he stated:

"Our common law system consists in the applying to new combination of circumstances those rules of law which we derive from legal principles and judicial precedents, and for the sake of attaining uniformity, consistency and certainty, we must apply those rules, where they are not plainly unreasonable and inconvenient to all cases which arise; and we are not at liberty to reject them, and to abandon all analogy to them, in those to which they have not yet been judicially applied, because we think that the rules are not as convenient and reasonable as we ourselves could have devised. It appears to me to be of great importance to keep this principle of decision steadily in view, not merely for the determination of the particular case, but for the interests of law as a science."

Most of the statements I have adverted to so far which tend to delineate the historical evolution of the doctrine of judicial precedent in English law were made by judges presiding at intermediate appellate courts. The House of Lords, the final appellate tribunal in the hierarchy of the English court system, spelled out the doctrine and the ambit of its application in a landmark decision in 1859. In that case, Beamish v Beamish,29 Lord Campbell, the Lord Chancellor, in addressing the House of Lords propounded the essence of the doctrine and its application to itself and to the rest of the British Empire. He said:

“. . . the rule of law which your Lordships lay down as the ground for your judgment, sitting judicially as the last and Supreme Court of Appeal for the Empire must be taken for law till altered by an Act of Parliament . . . The law laid down as your ratio decidendi being clearly binding on all inferior tribunals and on all the rest of the Queen's subjects, if it were not considered as equally binding upon your Lordships, this House would be arrogating to itself the right of altering the law and legislating by its own separate authority."

In 1859 the date of Beamish v Beamish, the Supreme Court Ordinances of 1853 and 1856 had already been passed and the judicial assessors were endeavouring to dispense justice in the English fashion30 and they continued to do so31 until the Supreme Court Ordinance 1876 came into force. Although the basis of their jurisdiction is not very clear, it must be remembered that Beamish v Beamish was decided 15 years after the Bond of 6 March 1844. By the terms of the Bond some of the chiefs of the country accepted for themselves and their subjects, the jurisdiction of Queen of Great Britain and Ireland within their various territories. They in fact specifically conceded to the British Crown the power of moulding the customs of the country to the general principles of British law.32 It will be recalled that the doctrine of judicial precedent as propounded by Lord Campbell LC was a fundamental general principle of British law and he specifically indicated that the doctrine was applicable to the British Empire.

The ambit of the concept of the principle of judicial precedent which we thus inherited from the English common law and its public policy considerations were articulated by another Lord Chancellor in a House of Lords decision in 1898 about 20 years after our Supreme Court Ordinance came into force. In that case, Lord Halsbury in his speech to the house said33:

". . . I am prepared to say... that a decision of this House once given upon a point of law is conclusive upon this House afterwards, and that it is impossible to raise that question again as if it were res integra and could be reargued, and so the house be asked to reverse it’s own decision. That is a principle which has been, I believe, without any real decision to the contrary, established now for some centuries,34 and I am therefore of (the) opinion that in this case it is not competent for us to rehear and for counsel to reargue a question which has been recently decided . . .

My Lords ‘interest rei publicae' that there should be ‘finis litium' at some time, and there could be no ‘finis litium' if it were possible to suggest in each case that it might be reargued . . .”

Earlier in his speech Lord Halsbury had considered the situation where a decision of the House of Lords had been given in ignorance of a statute relevant to the ratio of the decision. He proffered the answer that this would be a mistake of fact and that the decision founded on such a mistake would not be binding. It would seem however that until it is reversed, such a decision will bind the parties to the suit and those claiming through them in view of the principle of res judicata. However, under the principle derivable from English law35 and now made famous in Ghana in the celebrated case of Mosi v Bagyina36 such a decision if it is void, because it is not warranted by any law or rule of procedure can be declared a nullity by any court of competent jurisdiction and will consequently be set aside, even if it is the decision of the court of last resort.

The Privy Council, whose jurisdiction our appellate Supreme Court actually inherited, when the Constitution, 1960 came into force, had consistently held that in operating the doctrine of judicial precedent, it had power to depart from its previous decisions in circumscribed circumstances. In Ridsdale v Clifton37 barely one year after our Supreme Court Ordinance was promulgated, Lord Cairns, the Lord Chancellor, pointed out the consideration which informs the Board's attitude to stare decisis. This was reiterated in many cases. The best formulation of the Board's rationale is discernible in its 1929 opinion in the case which assisted the Ghana judges to formulate principles for judicial review in the Supreme Court.38  The Board in that case stressed39:

“To suggest that . . . this Board is constrained, blindfold, to adhere to a decision based on a material error in fact, appears to be repugnant to good sense, and to attribute to the Board, as a Court of final resort, an impotence which would be deplorable.”

At the time of the Supreme Court Ordinance, 1876, the principle we inherited is what I have so far endeavoured to canvass in historical perspective herein.

Theoretical Foundation and the Practice in the Courts Before 1st July 1990

It is noticeable that pronouncements of the courts have prima facie not been very helpful in explaining why in one instance the doctrine is applied and in another it is not. The reason, however, why pronouncements by superior courts on the binding force of prior decisions seem to create so much confusion is the failure to understand the nature of the decision which is said to be binding and the rationale offered by the judicial system justifying its adoption of the binding criterion as a practice mechanism for administering justice.

Before the Constitution, 1960 attempted to spell out the constitutional basis for the operation of the doctrine in Ghana, the Full Court had emphasised that it had in many cases, in appropriate circumstances, overruled decisions of previous Full Courts and in other cases had approved and respected such decisions. For instance, in Rottmann v Dennis40 it held that it respected the binding force of the earlier Full Court case of Halliday v Alapatra.41 However, in Swanzy v Bailey42 the Full Court, differently constituted, had already decided to overrule Halliday v Alipatira (supra). Rayner CJ with whom Nicoll J agreed, did not mince words. He said: “I think that the decision of the Court in Halliday v Alipatira (a Full Court decision) was wrong and should be overruled.”

In 1911, in the same year when Griffiths CJ in Concession Enquiry (Seccondee) 101643 warned the Full Court that it “should not presume to upset a solemn considered judgment of its own”, Gough Ag CJ and Earnshaw J undauntedly at the Full Court proclaimed in a case before them44 “. . . it is sufficient for the decision in this case to say that this court does not agree with the judgment in the case Anyidoho v Markham (a Full Court decision).That case was wrongly decided and will not be followed in this case. It is quite competent for this Court to take that course, as several cases in the records of previous Full Courts show."

These apparently conflicting stances which the courts seem to have taken are in truth no contradictions at all. The conditions precedent for its operation and the inherent nature of this common law doctrine of judicial precedent need to be articulated in order to understand these seemingly vacillating positions. In his study of this English doctrine, Sir John Salmond45 draws a sharp significant distinction between declaratory and creative precedents. A declaratory precedent under his classification is one that a court had applied in fact situations of past cases, which are similar to the case in hand. A creative precedent on the other hand is one which having regard to the logic of the situation and other legal and non-legal but pertinent considerations in the instant situation, is created and applied for the first time to the fact situation in the instant case. A declaratory precedent is therefore applied because it is a precedent controlling the instant case. A creative precedent on this analysis is strictly speaking only a potential precedent for future cases if it satisfies conditions applicable to precedents, as will appear hereafter.

Now, the literal meaning of the rule stare decisis et non quieta movere as I have already pointed out is "to adhere to decided cases and not to unsettle established things." Two significant conditions precedent inextricably inter-related are indicated here: The first condition precedent encompasses: "a series of decided cases" and the second condition precedent is "the presence of established positions or principles." If these two conditions precedent are not simultaneously satisfied, judicial precedent cannot be applied, for under English law as was indicated in relation to statutory power in Stroud v Bradbury,46 "If a condition precedent for the exercise of . . . power has not been satisfied the exercise of the power will not be lawful."

With regard to the first condition precedent, Jessel MR one of the greatest equity judges at the closing years of the 19th Century appreciated it when in 1875 he pointed out the conditions under which he would operate stare decisis with regard to statutory interpretation in the case before him47:

"If one authority were produced to me, and my own opinion were the other way, I would not follow that authority; but if the authorities are numerous, I admit that I must be bound. On that point the case of In re Newman's Settled Estates (Law Report 9 Ch. 681) is an authority."

As regards the second condition precedent, Lord Mansfield in 1783 stated the principle in language that I cannot hope to better when he said48: "The law does not consist of particular cases but of general principles which are illustrated and explained in these cases."

This view of what precedent does connote is essential to a proper understanding of the English doctrine and therefore of the doctrine we inherited. The nature of the two conditions precedent which I have adverted to herein and which ought indeed to exist before the rule of stare decisis can be operational, does mean that the invocation of the doctrine in a particular fact situation may very well be considered to be illegitimate and therefore wrong in the absence of the two conditions precedent.

Precedents whether declaratory or creative must accord with the general principles which lie at the root of the common law and are the bricks with which over centuries it has been structured. A precedent no matter how long it had even stood if it blatantly violates these fundamental and paramount principles is not likely to survive the ravages of time. If the courts fail to deflate its claim to legal validity, the legislature may mute its pretensions. And if the legislature condones this illegitimate creature, the people in their righteous anger will assuredly consign it to the limbo to which it belongs. Peacock v Bell and Kendall49 for instance, because it was decided on right principles has survived for over 300 years and our courts keep citing it with approval and its ratio has been followed.50 The decision of the Kings Bench in Sir Thomas Darnel's case51 that the King could imprison on suspicion without cause shown,52 violated fundamental principles of the common law and the understanding reached since Magna Carta in 1215 and so that decision perished long ago. The similar claim by the executive in England to incarcerate on suspicion in the English war time cases53 have also not survived the ravages of time. The test which Justice G K Acquah in his brilliant paper on "Customary Offences and the Courts"54 referred to as applicable to the validity of customary practices on the authority of Parker J's dictum in Johnson v Clark55 is equally applicable to the binding force of judicial precedent. To adopt the language of Parker J, judicial precedent must be ". . . in the opinion of a trained lawyer . . . consistent, or, at any rate, not inconsistent with those general principles which . . . lie at the root of our legal system."

An important matter by no means academic, which throws considerable light on the doctrine is the consideration of the source of the power enabling the doctrine to be accepted in the courts as a rule tool in the administration of justice. In Fosuhene v Pomaa (supra) relying on an English authority,56 point was made in the Supreme Court57:

"Under the common law system of justice every court of record exercising judicial function has an inherent power to regulate and provide its own practice and procedure, provided the practice or procedure is not inconsistent with statutory provisions or any practice or procedure which has crystallised by constant and consistent usage and become thereby precedent."

This position supported in the courts of the USA which operate a common law court system similar to ours, is reflected in many pronouncements of many of the American state courts. The California court in Albermont Ltd v Cunningham stated the principle that the power inherent in courts of record to make rules of procedure which did not conflict with constitutional or legislative provisions had long been recognised.58

When the Supreme Court Ordinance came into force on 31 March 1876, it reposed in the Supreme Court thus established,59 "all the jurisdiction, powers and authorities which are vested in or capable of being exercised by Her Majesty's High Court of Justice in England with the exception of its Admiralty jurisdiction." At that point in time the Majesty's High Court of Justice in England had adopted and pressed into service the doctrine of judicial precedent not only as part of the common law but indeed as the cornerstone of its machinery for developing and laying down the principles of the common law – a law regime which by the Supreme Court Ordinance we also inherited as from 24 July 1874. Apparently Her Majesty's courts in England had already absorbed the doctrine by virtue of their inherent powers to forge rule tools not inconsistent with statutory provisions, to enable them to regulate the manner in which they would carry out their traditional and statutory functions of dispensing justice.

The legal fortunes of the doctrine of judicial precedent in Ghana are accordingly tied down to the principles of the common law applied in Ghana with effect from an ill-defined period before 1876 to the years after independence but before the establishment of the First Republic. Within that period our principle coincided with the British practice.

Operation of the Doctrine Before and After the First Republican Constitution.

As I have already indicated, immediately before 1 July 1960 when the Republican Constitution came into force, the courts of Ghana operated the traditional English principle which Parke J enunciated so convincingly in 1833 and which the Privy Council with its adjectival qualification would seem to have reiterated in the Transferred Civil Servants case (supra) of 1929. The question that is necessarily posed by this view is what effect, if any, the Constitution, 1960, particularly article 42 (4) thereof have on the doctrine in the Ghana courts after that date?

On a consideration of the various decisions, pronouncements, and significant obiter dicta of both the English and Ghana courts, I am firmly of the view that the provisions in article 42 (4) of the Constitution, 1960 did no more than to encapsulate in a neat and admirable form the rather complex rule of stare decisis which the English judges had evolved over the centuries as a cornerstone of the common law structure.60 That it introduced no new principle with regard to the stare decisis rule is underlined by the fact that in the memorandum to the Constitution, 1960 there was no comment or reference whatsoever to article 42 (4) to indicate that the old principle was being abandoned for a novel principle. The only reference was article 42 (2) which was the constitutional mandate giving to "the Supreme Court the sole jurisdiction over questions as to the validity of Acts of Parliament."

It would seem therefore that at independence on 6 March 1957 and on the establishment of the Republic on 1 July 1960 and thereafter until 24 September 1969, when the Second Republic was established, the doctrine of judicial precedent operating in Ghana was virtually identical with the English practice as it existed on 6 March 1957, under the common law. Under the law61 of Ghana, the courts "In deciding upon the existence or content of a rule of the common law . . . may have regard to any exposition of that rule by a court exercising jurisdiction in any country." Clearly because we have inherited the English common law since 24 July 1874 and operated it even on 6 March 1957, it would appear that at independence and no doubt thereafter we were committed to the whole British tradition of judicial precedent. In the circumstances, the practice developed since 1876 of citing and referring to English decisions as was adumbrated by Michelin J in 192962 was given a statutory lease of life in the Interpretation Act, 1960 (CA 4).

We dropped the colonial yoke in 1957 and having assumed a Republican status in July 1960, one would think a new legal order responsive to our customary laws and parting ways with the inherited British tradition would set in. One school of thought took the view that at the time of the Republican Constitution our superior courts were bound "to the whole British legal tradition."63 This view was repudiated by an opposing school of thought. The matter has created some unresolved and needless doubts. The problem needs to be rationally dealt with by considering the constitutional position and the actual practice of our superior courts in the years before and after the First Republican Constitution.

Ghana as an independent state with an independent judiciary applying settled judicial principles in its adjudicatory procedures came into being on 6 March 1957. Judicial precedents in the superior courts of Ghana therefore cover the period of 6 March 1957 up to date. The country did not however break off with the traditional practice and procedure for resolving issues which statutory courts had consistently applied from about 6 March 1844 to 6 March 1957.64 The pre-independence judiciary of the period from a date after 6 March 1844 to 6 March 1957 would seem to have applied the notion of judicial precedent prevailing in England at the time, with some form of vacillation which they would seem to have observed, not unreasonably, as characteristic of the English doctrine.

The doctrine, however, as I have already indicated, is conceptually a jurisdictional mechanism and is thus operated within the particular legal system of the relevant independent judiciary to advance the interest of justice. Thus in a show of judicial independence in the application of the doctrine, Michelin J in 1929 protested in the Full Court:

"The Court is not bound by (English) judicial decisions yet if the section of the local ordinance contains provisions which are similar to those of the corresponding English statute, the decisions of the Superior Courts in England are entitled to respect here and have in such circumstances for many years past been as a rule followed by the courts in criminal cases.”65

With regard to the judgments of the Full Court of the 1876 Supreme Court, Griffiths CJ in 1911 echoing the 1898 views of Lord Halsbury in the London Street Tramways Co case66 said: "the judgments of the Full Court . . . as such binds us in any case where the facts are identical" and he continued67:

"In my opinion the Full Court should not presume to upset a solemn considered judgment of its own; any such practice would not only render the law more uncertain than it is, but it would tend to destroy the responsibility of the individual members of the Full Court, who might think that their judgment, if erroneous, might be set right at another sitting."

Before 1 July 1960, the traditional English doctrine was clearly what was applied in this country. After that date, the Constitution, 1960 specifically offered a constitutional foundation for the operation of the doctrine in the Supreme Court and the High Court which were established under the said Constitution. In article 42 (4) of the Constitution it was provided:

"(4) The Supreme Court shall in principle be bound to follow its own decisions on questions of law, and the High Court shall be bound to follow previous decisions of the Supreme Court on such questions, but neither court shall be bound to follow the previous decisions of any court on questions of law."

Mr F A R Bennion then at the Attorney-General's Office, who had a hand in the drafting of the Constitution, 1960 explained that "in principle" it was intended to permit the Supreme Court in exceptional cases to depart from its previous decisions.68

Mr Justice Ollennu expressed extra-judicially in a speech he made in September 1962 his firm views regarding the operative force of article 42(4) in regard to the stare decisis rule. He said69:

"The Supreme Court as established under the Constitution stepped into the shoes of, and assumed the jurisdiction of both the Court of Appeal—the West African Court of Appeal and the Privy Council in so far as it affected Ghana and no more. That being the case, it is respectfully submitted that the authoritative decisions which under article 42 section 4 of the Constitution both the Supreme Court and the High Court are bound to follow comprise and include decisions of the Privy Council in Ghanaian cases given prior to 1960, those made by the Privy Council under and by virtue of section 16 of the Constitution (Consequential Provisions) Act, 1960 (CA 8) and it also includes decisions of the Court of Appeal, of the West African Court of Appeal as well as decisions of the Full Court."

The above opinion of Justice Ollennu has been criticised by Dr Asante on the grounds that the wording of article 42 (4) is against the Ollennu position.70 This criticism seems to find support in the views of Bennion and Justice Azu Crabbe71 and in a judgment of Apaloo JA (as he then was) in a case he dealt with at the Court of Appeal on 11 December 1972.72 In my opinion, the controversy regarding the application or non-application of the doctrine of judicial precedent to pre-Republican decisions of the country's superior courts is best resolved by considering the law applicable in Ghana after 1 July 1960.

Under article 40 of the First Republican Constitution the common law continued to operate in Ghana and in section 1(1) of the Ghana (Consequential Provisions) Act, 1960 (8 & 9 Eliz, c 41) it was provided as follows:

"On and after the date Ghana becomes a Republic all existing law, that is to say all law which whether being a rule of law or a provision of an Act of Parliament or of any other enactment or instrument whatsoever is in force on that day or has been passed or made before the date and comes into force thereafter shall until provision to the contrary is made by an authority having power to alter that law ... have the same operation in relation to or in connection with Ghana as it would have had if Ghana had not become a Republic."

After studying this provision, Dr Ekow Daniels came to a conclusion which I find quite satisfactory that73:

"rules of law embodied in decisions of the pre-Republican courts were to be recognised by the Supreme Court as if they formed part of the laws in force before Ghana became a Republic; and such rules of law are precedents."

As against this stand the Azu Crabbe, Bennion and Asante views were buttressed in a judicial decision delivered by Apaloo JA (as he then was) in the Court of Appeal in Donkor v Agyepong74:

"We are . . . squarely faced with the question whether a pre-1960 decision of the then Court of Appeal binds any court after the Republican Constitution of 1960. We think not. Since 1960, there has been a completely new constitutional set-up with a fresh hierarchy of courts."

On this question of not following decisions of WACA and of other earlier superior final appellate courts, academic expostulations are nothing compared with the actual practice of the courts. The cases illustrating the true position from 1 July 1960 to the 1990s are so numerous that only a few samples will suffice.

At the time in 1962 when the point was made by Mr Justice Ollennu that judicial precedents are accorded by law to the Privy Council and the West African Court of Appeal decisions in Ghana cases, the Supreme Court that emerged after the Constitution, 1960 had on 29 April 1961 in Nyame v Yeboah75 unanimously refused to sanction an arbitration contrary to principles laid down in a Privy Council case.76 And in Mahamadu Lagos v Commissioner of police77 it unanimously followed the principles so well known to the Ghana Bar, which was propounded in R v Abisa Grunshie78 by the West African Court of Appeal. With regard to the 1960 High Court, Adumua-Bossman J (as he then was) on 9 January 1961 in a characteristically brilliant decision at the Accra High Court in Okyere v Boye Adjei79 followed the reasoning of the West African Court of Appeal in Quarm v Yankah II.80

As regards even the post-1969 and the pre-1979 Ghana courts, a litany of cases is unnecessary: Three will suffice. On 21 December 1970 in the Court of Appeal81 Azu Crabbe JA (as he then was) with whom Sowah JA (as he then was) agreed, applied the dictum of Verity CJ in the West African Court of Appeal case of Emegwara v Nwaimo82 and followed the decision of the West African Court of Appeal in Oloto v Williams83 in a decision which was subsequently approved even by the 1979 Supreme Court in Hammond v Odoi84 ; and in Adjeioda v CFAO.85 Abban J (as he then was) followed and applied the law as expounded by the West African Court of Appeal in Phares v Abdallah86 and Kpeshie II v Kojo II.87 The instances both in the Court of Appeal and in the High Court can be multiplied. Indeed, less than three months after Apaloo JA (as he then was) had, in effect, denied the status of judicial precedents to pre-1960 decisions of the Court of Appeal in Robertson v Nii Akramah88 he even cited with approval the pre-1960 High Court cases of Ashiemoa v Bani89 and Donkor v Danso.90  As for the post-1979 superior courts, the Supreme Court as recently as 17 July 199091 considered the decision of Anane v Efriyea,92 a West African Court of Appeal decision which had been already applied in Abraham alias Salloum v Salloum93 by the Court of Appeal on 14 December 1989 and the High Court applied the West African Court of Appeal case of Azumah v Kehodo94 in Amoah v The Republic.95

The reason practice and rhetoric seem to be at cross purposes is because the authority of a precedent, even if it is a declaratory precedent, is founded on its ratio decidendi being in consonance with the general and fundamental principles which sustain the legal system. A creative precedent to survive and qualify to play its potential role must also satisfy the same criteria. Indeed, if the decision declaratory or creative satisfied the criteria, then even if it emanates from a court lower in the hierarchy, it will be recognised as proper judicial precedent and where appropriate may be cited, considered, followed and applied by all courts including even the highest.

A study of the cases decided in the superior courts from a period long before 1959 to 1991 as are contained in the reports of the Council for Law Reporting and other reports reveals no noticeable change in the methodology of the Ghanaian courts' operation of their rule of stare decisis, during the entire period.96

In the Constitution, 1969 the "in principle" formula of the Constitution, 1960 was altered and a new constitutional fiat in article 104 (2) was substituted in these terms:

"(2) The Supreme Court may, while treating its own previous decisions as normally binding, depart from a previous decision when it appears right so to do; and all other Courts shall be bound to follow the decisions of the Supreme Court on questions of law."

(Author's emphasis.)

This new provision was in fact repeated in the Constitution, 1979 and has been restated in substance in the Constitution, 1992 where "appears right so to do" has been replaced by "appears to it right to do so." It is clear therefore that as far as the constitutional mandate regarding stare decisis is concerned, the position from 24 September 1969 to 1992 is the same; but is it different from the post-1960 and pre-1969 positions?

It seems the 1960 position reflected the English practice as at that date. This was the apparently rigid principle enunciated in rather peremptory language by the two Lord Chancellors in Beamish v Beamish97 and the London Street Tramways Co case98 respectively, but which was competently explained by Parke J in Mirehouse v Rennell.99 The apparent conflicting pronouncements of the English courts are explicable on the simple ground that in fact the doctrine is not and has not been all that rigid. Indeed, when Parke J formulated it in 1833 he specifically indicated that the legal rules which qualify as judicial precedents are only applicable" where they are not plainly unreasonable and inconvenient.

In this connection and in support of Parke J, it will be recalled that in the London Street Tramways100 case Lord Halsbury suggested that a decision given by the House of Lords in ignorance of a statute is an erroneous decision in fact and one that would not bind the house. By plain unreasonable, I am inclined to think that over the years this has been accepted to apply to cases which to most persons, especially those who have as professional men studied and mastered the substance of the law, are obviously erroneous as running counter to fundamental principles which lie at the root of the judicial stem.101 As Lord Hardwicke put it102: “Neither Law nor equity consists merely of casual precedents, but the general rules and principles by the reason of which the several cases coming before the courts of justice are to be governed."

The second ground adverted to by Parke J for denouncing rules of law as precedents is where their application is "plainly ... inconvenient." This must mean no more than that applying them will create injustice.103 Lord Wright MR quoted with approval the following statement of principle by Lord Loreburn104:

"Great importance is to be attached to old authorities, on the strength of which many transactions may have been adjusted and rights determined. But where they are plainly wrong, and especially where the subsequent course of judicial decisions has disclosed weakness in the reasoning on which they were based, and practical injustice in the consequences that must flow from them, I consider it is the duty of this House to overrule them . . .”

It seems to me also that where the purported application of a precedent gives rise to unequal treatment, then the judicial decision must necessarily be unjust and resort to such precedent must in the circumstances create great inconvenience. As Karl Llewelyn so ably observes105:

"The force of precedent in the law is heightened by an additional factor; that curious almost universal sense of justice which urges that all men are properly to be treated alike in like circumstances."

And as Romer LJ put it106; "A sense of injustice is a grievous thing.” The pre-1960 application of judicial precedent did reflect the English position and the post-1960 position made no difference. However, article 104 (2) of the Constitution, 1969 which is now contained in both the Constitution, 1969107 and the Constitution, 1992108  introduced, as already pointed out, a new stare decisis rule, specific and unequivocal. The effect of the new rule is that the Supreme Court "may depart from a previous decision where it appears right so to do." The use of the word right, an antonym of wrong, introduced a moral yardstick into the whole equation. The new rule throws us back to the old debate under new circumstances as to whether as from 24 September 1969, when the Second Republican Constitution came into force, we opted to abandon the whole British legal tradition regarding the use of precedents in our judicial decision making process.

It is significant that by 1969, the British legal system had already accepted this new stare decisis regime. The British House of Lords following the common law tradition109 had announced on 26 July 1966 a new stare decisis regime. Lord Gardiner, the Lord Chancellor proclaiming the new order said110:

“Their Lordships regard the use of precedent as an indispensable foundation upon which to decide what is the law and its application to individual cases. It provides at least some degree of certainty upon which individuals can rely in the conduct of their affairs, as well as a basis for orderly development of legal rules.

Their Lordships nevertheless recognise that too rigid adherence to precedent may lead to injustice in a particular case and also unduly restrict the proper development of the law. They propose, therefore, to modify their present practice and, while treating former decisions of this House as normally binding, to depart from a previous decision when it appears right to do so."

On 26 November 1987 after examining this English practice statement and comparing it with article 104 (2) of the Constitution, 1969 it was observed at the Supreme Court as follows: "A close reading . . . reveals . . . quite plainly that the draftsman of article 104 (2) . . . merely followed substantially the ipsissima verba of the Practice Direction of the House of Lords."111

It is apparent that as from 24 September 1969 up-to-date the stare decisis rule applied in Ghana is identical with that which operates in England for the same period. In the circumstances it is worthwhile to make a comparative study of how a legal criterion virtually identical in both jurisdictions has been operated by their respective judiciaries. The exercise ought to help us to map out the direction towards which we ought to constitutionally develop our law.

A study of the English case of Liversidge v Anderson112 is most rewarding. It is a Second World War case decided in accordance with the previous First World War case of R v Halliday.113 In the said Liversidge case, the majority of the House of Lords took the apparently unreasonable and extravagant view in effect that the words "if the secretary of state has reasonable cause to believe . . ." which appear at the opening sentence of the famous regulation 18B of the Defence (General) Regulations, 1939 are capable of being interpreted and were in fact construed to mean "if the secretary of state honestly thinks that he has reasonable cause to believe . . ." In a forceful dissenting opinion, Lord Atkin repudiated in unambiguous language this clear abuse of language bordering on judicial assault on commonsense. He pointed out that it undermined fundamental principles of the common law.

Barely nine years after this infamous decision, the Privy Council had occasion to consider a somewhat similar phrase in Nakkuda Ali v Jayaratne.114  The Board robustly but courteously renounced as precedent, the construction in the Liversidge case which the House of Lords had adopted. Lord Radcliff observed115:

"After all, words such as these are commonly found when a legislature or law-making authority confers power on a minister or official. However read, they must be intended to serve in some sense as a condition limiting the exercise of an otherwise arbitrary power. But if the question whether the condition has been satisfied is to be conclusively decided by the man who wields the power the value of the intended restraint is in effect nothing."

The Liversidge case was decided long before Lord Gardiner's practice statement and it must be remembered that decisions of the House of Lords do not bind the Privy Council and the Privy Council had always claimed the right to depart from its previous decisions. The effect of the new English practice statement of the rule was demonstrated in the Rossminster case in 1980.116 In that case, in interpreting a statute which had used a phrase reminiscent of the Liversidge variety, the House of Lords gave consideration to the ratio decidendi of the majority decision in the Liversidge case and in overruling its ratio, Lord Diplock without any compunction whatsoever stated very bluntly117:

"For my part I think the time has come to acknowledge openly that the majority of this House in Liversidge v Anderson were expediently and, at that time, perhaps, excusably, wrong and the dissenting speech of Lord Atkin was right."

Lord Scarman in a more picturesque language was more scathing when he said118

"The ghost of Liversidge v Anderson casts no shadow upon this statute. And I would think it need no longer haunt the law. It was laid to rest by Lord Radcliffe in Nakkuda Ali v Jayaratne . . . and no one in this case has sought to revive it. It is now beyond recall."

The majority decision in Liversidge v Anderson ran counter to a stream of cases which have for years been accepted as precedents for a fundamental principle of the English common law. That principle is that any person imprisoning or causing the imprisonment of a citizen has the onus of justifying his action in a court of law. The Rossminster case in overruling Liversidge v Anderson, repaired the deep crack which the Halliday and Liversidge types of cases had caused by breaching this fundamental principle. The principle was reinstated in its pristine purity in Ex parte Khawaja in 1984 by Lord Scarman in this pithy language119:

"The classic dissent of Lord Atkin in Liversidge v Anderson . . . is now accepted as correct not only on the point of construction . . . but in its declaration of English legal principle. Lord Atkin put it thus . . . ‘that in English law every imprisonment is prima facie unlawful and that it is for the person directing imprisonment to justify his act."’

The principle declared here is needless to say a fundamental principle of our own law, for we inherited it from the common law. Let us now make a comparative study of how the superior courts in Ghana have carried out their functions under the two stare decisis regimes mandated by the Constitution, 1960 and the Constitution, 1969 having regard to the common law principle contained in this English position. The first relevant case which our post-1960 and pre-1969 Supreme Court was called upon to decide was Re Akoto.120 That case postulated a Liversidge v Anderson situation in a habeas corpus application where the High Court had refused the application. On appeal, the Supreme Court affirmed the refusal. The Supreme Court had access to the impeccable reasoning of Lord Radcliffe in the Nakkuda Ali case renouncing the Liversidge ratio and although with us the Privy Council decisions are hierarchically more authoritative than those of the House of Lords, the Supreme Court nevertheless apparently rejected the Nakkuda Ali case and followed the Liversidge ratio. The Supreme Court did this in spite of the fact that the fundamental principles that sustain the common law which are contained in many common law cases had been ex abundanti cautela specifically indicated in the Constitution, 1960121 and one of these principles adverted to by Lord Atkin in the Liversidge case and referred to by Lord Scarman in the Khawaja case was blatantly ignored in Re Akoto.

The judges of our post-independent final appellate Supreme Court thus missed an opportunity to assert their independence guaranteed apparently under the Constitution122 and to demonstrate their awareness that established legal principles are not thrown overboard by forms of judicial interpretation placed on statutory provisions where those provisions do not in terms unambiguously decree their rejection. The rule is that where the language of an enactment is not explicit in this regard it is not a proper exercise of the judicial function to utilise any legal mechanism or any form of stare decisis to emasculate the law and unsettle established legal principles."'

The decision in Re Akoto was clearly unsatisfactory and it was rather left to the legislature to repair the damage and in 1964 the legislature passed an Act124 which in effect statutorily overruled not only Re Akoto, but it would seem all other cases with similar ratios.125 That Act is still on the statute books although it has consistently been undermined by virtually every military regime that has bedevilled this country.126 Our courts were no doubt aware of the fact that the application of the Liversidge ratio in Re Akoto was wrong, for in 1967 Akufo-Addo CJ presiding over the full bench stated clearly that the dissenting view of Lord Atkin in Liversidge v Anderson (supra) had come to be accepted as a correct statement of the strict common law doctrine.”127

The judicial mind however is highly conservative128 for almost fifteen years after the ratio in Re Akoto had been thus deliberately repudiated by the First Republican Legislature and the dissenting view of Lord Atkin having been virtually approved by the full bench of the Court of Appeal, Coussey J (as he then was) at the High Court, Ho felt bound by the discarded and discredited ratio when he stated129:

"I am bound by the decision of the Ghana Supreme Court in Re Akoto which in 1961 followed Liversidge v Anderson . . . I wish I could accede to the request to depart from the said decision . . . But being a court just lower to the Court of Appeal I will go along with them."

Two other cases will illustrate how we have operated the post-1969 constitutional mandate which was obviously intended to provide both certainty and flexibility in the use of stare decisis in our courts. It was calculated to pre-empt the difficulties which the British had experienced prior to 1966 and which had led the House of Lords to state through the Lord Chancellor, Lord Gardiner, a new stare decisis regime for Great Britain. The cases of Hansen v Ankrah130 and Kwakye v Attorney-General131 present features of the stare decisis rule which are of extreme importance to the future development of case law in Ghana.

The Hansen case involves a judicial precedent said to be contained in the ratio decidendi of the Full Court in Pappoe v Kweku, a 1924 case132 to the effect that in customary law a head of family had absolute control over family property including money and that he cannot be called upon to account in a court of law to any member of the family. The supposed precedent which post-dated the Supreme Court Ordinance is alleged to be founded on a statement of the customary law by the celebrated Ghanaian jurist John Mensah Sarbah. A close and meticulous examination of every sentence in Sarbah's Fanti Customary Laws, the alleged source of the law, reveals no such statement. The precedent was nevertheless applied and a head of family and non-heads of family who had paid family moneys into their bank accounts were held not to be accountable in the courts of the Republic. Apaloo CJ who presided at the Supreme Court hearing on 21 March 1985 was visibly scandalised, but in his inimitable way he gave vent in his dissent in these memorable words133:

"This court ought clearly to decline to follow the Pappoe v Kweku ... line of cases. The customary rule laid down by them is productive of injustice and provides a patent shield for the breach of fiduciary duties. After all the law must adapt itself to changing social conditions and those precedents are inapplicable to modern conditions. There is some question whether doing this will amount to judicial legislation. Refusing to follow an obviously unjust precedent cannot rightly be construed as judicial legislation. We have constitutional authority134 to refuse to be bound by a precedent which injures the innocent, benefits the guilty and puts a premium on blatant breach of fiduciary duty. To do otherwise would be an exhibition of judicial inertia wholly indefensible in our day and age."

Less than three months after this unfortunate decision the legislature stepped in to stem the tide of this destructive precedent which clearly "injures the innocent, benefits the guilty and puts a premium on blatant breach of fiduciary duty." They passed an appropriate law on 14 June 1985,135 to stop the rot.

The Kwakye case involves the interpretation of section 15 (2) of the transitional provisions of the Constitution, 1979. The section reads:

"(2) For the avoidance of doubt it is hereby declared that no executive, legislative or judicial action taken or purported to have been taken by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council or by any person in the name of that Council shall be questioned in any proceedings whatsoever and accordingly it shall not be lawful for any court or other tribunal to make any order or grant any remedy or relief in respect of any such act."

(Author's emphasis.)

It would appear from the wording that what is proscribed in this ouster clause is "executive, legislative or judicial action, taken or purported to have been taken." The action must prima facie be either executive, or legislative or judicial, before the question whether it was taken or purported to have been taken arises. On this analysis a criminal or unlawful act is outside the purview of this ouster clause. And when faced with the provision, almost all the judges in the lower superior courts had no difficulty in holding in case after case that unlawful and criminal acts are completely outside the purview of the transitional provisions. There are some instances136: In Darkwa v The Republic, Charles Crabbe JSC, Edward Wiredu JA (as he then was) and Edusei JA held in effect that the transitional provisions did not cover and protect illegal acts. In Armah v Ghana Water and Sewerage Corporation and again in Republic v Director of Prisons; Exparte Shackleford, Cecilia Koranteng-Addow J came to the same conclusion. That conclusion was also followed by Sarkodee J in Djaba v Attorney-General and by Agyepong J in Republic v Director of Prisons; Ex parte Djaba. Amua-Sekyi J (as he then was) came to a similar conclusion in Republic v Director of prisons; Ex parte Doh. In the Shackleford case, virtually on all fours with the Kwakye case, it was alleged that the applicant was tried by the AFRC. But the only document purporting to be a warrant for the detention of the applicant in the habeas corpus application before the court was a photostat copy of an alleged warrant with an undecipherable signature of an unidentifiable person. The applicant claimed he had never been tried. Cecilia Koranteng-Addow J after a most brilliant and meticulous examination of the authorities, with a penetrating appraisal of the facts poignantly queried137:

"Suppose that the AFRC, after interrogating the applicant, was satisfied he had committed no offence, and yet some unauthorised person picked one of these forms, filled it, and sent it to the prison officers, would this be an act which should be protected or saved by the transitional provisions? Certainly not."

The manner in which the Supreme Court dealt with the transitional provisions is reminiscent of the vacillation of the early English courts. In the Kwakye case on 22 March 1981 it had held quite unambiguously that the provisions did not oust its jurisdiction.138 Barely nine months later, it held, however, in the same case in a majority decision that the provisions oust its jurisdiction; such a volte face is normally extraordinary but in this case its extravagant feature is that the highest court in the land acquiesced in a proposition of law, unprecedented in the legal history of the country, whereby a man alleged to have been convicted of a criminal offence, has his sentence and conviction affirmed with neither the court nor the man being made aware of the nature of the criminal offence the man had committed.

The Kwakye decision was not in accordance with any earlier principle or previous decision ever laid down throughout the history of our law. It declared no precedent, it rather sought to create a new precedent limiting the jurisdiction of all courts in Ghana. It did this in one aspect of the case by putting forward the proposition that by the legislative use of one word, purported", a nullity will cease to be null and void; but as Bimpong-Buta in his paper139 pointed out quoting Lord Reid140:

"Statutory provisions which seek to limit the ordinary jurisdiction of the court have a long history. No case has been cited in which any other form of words limiting the jurisdiction of the court has been held to protect a nullity."

Furthermore, the Supreme Court in the Kwakye case acquiesced in a proposition, clearly fallacious, that a group of persons under oath of secrecy to remain anonymous can conduct a secret trial, without indicating the authority under which they act, and by virtue of such secret trial they can lawfully convict a Ghanaian citizen. On such conviction the superior court further agreed that such a Ghanaian citizen can lawfully be imprisoned and presumably executed if so sentenced. This surely undermines a fundamental and paramount principle which is the very foundation of our criminal justice system. Such a precedent reminiscent of the Liversidge case is aberrant and like that case can hardly survive the ravages of time. Such an interpretation of the transitional provisions would seem to be frowned upon by the Ghanaian public including those who have no pretensions to knowledge of our law. Glance through Ghanaian newspapers from 28 April 1992 to the date of this lecture and in particular to the Daily Graphic of 16 April 1992 where the Chairman of the PNDC, Flt-Lt J J Rawlings gave as his opinion that the transitional provisions do not protect criminal and unlawful acts.141 If you had listened to the lecture by the Deputy Attorney-General, the Honourable Martin Amidu,142 you could realise that like the Chairman of the PNDC, he was also of the view that the transitional provisions do not protect criminal and illegal acts.

A friend of mine has suggested that these very important views should be documented and like the American Federalist Papers, they may in future help as contemporaneous papers in interpreting the Constitution, 1992. The authorities were probably embarrassed by the Kwakye case for it is common knowledge that the convict is at large and free in Ghana without serving any of the 25 years sentence imposed.

There is a respectable school of thought in the country that decisions of the final appellate court should be always respected and followed by the lower superior courts and that those courts should not criticise the final appellate court.143 The number of cases in which lower courts have criticised higher courts are very many indeed.144 In some cases the criticism has been good for the law, it has enabled the court to take a second look at earlier decisions and rationally consider whether in the light of the criticism, it can in the interest of justice continue to hold on to its previous opinion.145

Stare Decisis: Problems And Prospects

A significant problem surrounds the extraordinary, and it would appear an unconstitutional power which, with the acquiescence of the Supreme Court, the Chief Justice has virtually acquired to control the facts of cases in the highest court in the land. In a landmark decision of extreme constitutional importance, the Supreme Court, in a majority decision, held that even though the appointing authority has designated persons as Supreme Court judges to sit in the Supreme Court, nevertheless the Chief Justice has authority in effect to refuse to allow them to sit and permanently assign them to the lower courts.146  This problem will still be with us after the Constitution, 1992 comes into force.

Furthermore, a power given to the Chief Justice to invite Court of Appeal justices to sit in the Supreme Court has been construed as a power to ignore at his pleasure all Supreme Court judges,147 and in his discretion to constitute in effect a Supreme Court of only Court of Appeal judges. The Chief Justice can then in theory assign a High Court case to a Supreme Court judge; and an appeal from the decision of the Supreme Court judge sitting at the High Court can be assigned to a Court of Appeal made up of two Supreme Court judges and one Court of Appeal judge; and an appeal from the decision of this Court of Appeal can go to a Supreme Court of three Court of Appeal judges and two Supreme Court judges. Assuming the decision of the High Court was affirmed unanimously by the Court of Appeal, if the decision of the Court of Appeal is set aside by a majority of three Court of Appeal judges as against the two Supreme Court judges148 we have the extraordinary situation in which the view of the law taken by the Supreme Court judges and one Court of Appeal judge is set aside by three Court of Appeal judges. This is clearly unsatisfactory. Hopefully with the demise of PNDCL 42 when the Constitution comes into force this problem will disappear.

A problem likely to survive even after the Constitution, 1979 has spent itself is the exclusive power which the Chief Justices have arrogated to themselves to fix dates for the hearing of Supreme Court cases. In spite of the fact that the Supreme Court has been specifically mandated to fix dates for the hearing of Supreme Court cases by the rules of court,149 nevertheless this statutory duty has been nonchalantly abandoned by the Supreme Court and, in an apparent usurpation of its statutory function to fix these dates, the incumbent Chief Justices have been exercising it. This is obviously unlawful and it is clearly unsatisfactory, because if a Chief Justice chooses not to fix a case, the rights of the litigants will be compromised since the power he wields is allegedly uncontrollable150 as there is no superintending authority to monitor its exercise.

Perhaps the most intractable problem and one that will plague the Supreme Court in future and erode the evolutionary process of decisions of the Supreme Court becoming binding precedents is the practice which has relentlessly been developed of the Supreme Court sitting in panels. The final judicial power in this Republic is vested in the Supreme Court. This is the position under both the Constitution, 1979 and the present Constitution, 1992. Now with the final judicial power in the State given to one Supreme Court, it cannot exercise this power in committees or panels selected by an unauthorised body. A famous US Supreme Court judge, Justice Brenan, stated the position with regard to courts like our Supreme Court when he described the decision making process in the US Supreme Court saying that their "Constitution vests the judicial power in only one Supreme Court. This does not permit Supreme Court action by committees/panels or sections."151  We have under the Constitution, 1992 a Supreme Court membership of at least ten. By sitting in panels, as has hitherto been the practice, a situation can be created where the decision of one panel may conflict with the decision of another. A more disastrous recipe for chaos and confusion in forging judicial precedents is difficult to imagine. Indeed, the disaster has already descended upon us.

In Dei v Darke XII152 a decision given in the morning of 8 July 1991 in the Supreme Court, the court decided (per Adade, Amua-Sekyi JJSC and Ofori-Boateng JA, with Osei-Hwere and Edward Wiredu JJSC dissenting) that the Supreme Court has power under the rules of court to extend the time for filing statements of cases in the Supreme Court. On the same day with Ofori-Boateng JA dropping out and Wuaku JSC joining the second panel, the court decided in In re Oppong (Decd); Mensah v Bediako153 (per Wuaku, Osei-Hwere and Edward Wiredu JJSC with Adade and Amua-Sekyi JJSC dissenting) that it has no jurisdiction to exercise such power. Subsequently, in Joseph v Farisco154 the Supreme Court had an opportunity to remove the confusion but its practice of sitting in panels exarcebated the problem and it ended up by deciding to leave this matter so vital to the proper administration of justice unresolved. The Court unanimously decided:

"We do not think it will be necessary to resolve or reconcile the conflicting views of this court within the purview of this ruling and will therefore leave it for a better opportunity."

The Supreme Court's paramount duty for which it is created is to do justice: rules of court are tools to advance this overriding purpose. As Collins MR put it155:

"The relation of rules of practice to the work of justice is intended to be that of handmaid rather than mistress and the court ought not to be so far bound and tied by rules which are afterall only intended as general rules of procedure, as to be compelled to do what will cause injustice in the particular case."

The whole judicial power of the State is by the Constitution exclusively vested in the judiciary with the Supreme Court in complete control of this power. The judicial power of the State so vested is total and can be regulated but it cannot be restricted by rules of court which are merely the products of the exercise of powers by bodies inferior to the legislature and the Constitution. Even the legislature under the Constitution cannot restrict the exercise of judicial power by the Supreme Court. Indeed under article 117 (2) of the Constitution, 1979 (repeated in article 131 (2) of the Constitution, 1992), the Supreme Court has constitutional power to entertain an application for special leave to appeal and grant leave accordingly. Moreover the Supreme Court itself in exercise of its inherent jurisdiction as a court of record committed to do justice can suo motu admit an application for leave to appeal in any case criminal or civil.

The decision of the Supreme Court in In re Oppong (supra) runs counter to very many cases some of which were adverted to by Adade JSC in the Dei v Darke case. As I have been at pains to point out in this paper, any decision, even of the highest court in the land, which violates general principles at the root of the legal system is no precedent to be applied in future cases. The Rossminster156 case vindicates this proposition. The In re Oppong case violates the rule of the common law that nothing is out of the jurisdiction of a superior court but that which specially appears to be.157

The solution of this problem in order to restore credibility to decisions of the Supreme Court lies in supplying every member of the Supreme Court with all the papers in every case filed in the court. When the cases are ready for hearing the entire membership will hold a conference and decide by discussion and votes the decision that the entire court considers appropriate.158 Thereafter a court reflecting the views so decided upon, can be empanelled to hand down the relevant considered decision of the court. In this way certainty and consistency will be achieved.

At the moment there are a number of Supreme Court decisions which can be demonstrated beyond all reasonable doubts to be plainly wrong and are as Parke J said in the Mirehouse case159 and as Lord Halsbury conceded in the London Street Tramways Co case160 no precedents to be followed. As a random selection, the majority decision of the Supreme Court in Republic v Appiah161 clearly misconceived the meaning of obtaining in law and the effect of section 124 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30) and it completely ignored section 51 of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372). The majority decision also of the Supreme Court in Ababio v Mensah is cleared a nullity as it is a chieftaincy case which completely ignored the relevant and decisive provisions of the Chieftaincy Act, 1971 (Act 370). The majority decision in Kwakye is another case. It sanctioned secret trial procedure, a procedure unknown and subversive of the criminal law regime in Ghana. And it put forward the proposition, plainly wrong, that a person can be convicted of a criminal offence although no act or omission of his is involved; this is contrary to the fundamental principle of criminal law made famous in the latin aphorism actus non facit reum nisi mens sit rea.

In my well considered opinion, in order to avoid perpetuating questionable and unsatisfactory principles which knowledgeable citizens and the legislature as well as responsible professional legal opinion consider inimical to the people's welfare, it may be necessary to device a watchdog committee to supervise these aberrations in the legal system.162  The committee can be made up of the Chief Justice, the most senior member of the Supreme Court, the most senior members of the Court of Appeal and the High Court, the Chairman of the Law Reform Commission, the Editor of the Ghana Law Reports, the Attorney-General, members of Parliament representing the political parties who have knowledge of the law, a number of senior members of the Ghana Bar, and some eminent members of the public with legal knowledge, a member of the Faculty of Law, University of Ghana and any other persons likely to understand the issues involved.

The committee is to ensure that the law is responsive to the Constitution and the aspirations of the people. It is to make periodic reports through the Law Reform Commission to the President and the legislature. The Law Reform Commission is to make its own recommendations on the report thus submitted to Parliament as to how the situation can be constitutionally rectified. I am not advocating a super Supreme Court to review Supreme Court decisions. I am rather thinking of a committee that will seek to eliminate injustice in the society where it is abundantly clear that a Supreme Court decision has without doubt created injustice. For instance, in the case of an injustice in a criminal case resulting in imprisonment or a death sentence, a recommendation from the committee may enable the President to exercise his powers of mercy by pardoning the victim without interfering with the Supreme Court decision. In civil cases, the legislature may work out and forge by the legislative process, a strategy by which victims of injustice certified by the committee can be compensated from state funds, a strategy that can in appropriate cases be extended to victims of the criminal justice system.

The Supreme Court has been given exclusive jurisdiction to do justice. Its confessed impotence to do so in certain circumstances is disturbing. One of the ablest judges who has served in our courts, Hayfron-Benjamin J (as he then was), deprecated this stance when in inimitable language he cautioned163:

"In the infant democracy of Ghana it is exceptionally important that the courts should not put fetters on their ability to protect the fundamental human rights so admirably enshrined in the Constitution of the Second Republic, by adopting highly technical and artificial limitations on their powers, limitations which are being rapidly discarded even in monarchial regimes. Democracy in the last analysis, receives its sustenance from remedial laws and procedures; it is the availability of effective and reasonably quick remedies for doing justice that gives meaning to democracy... To restrict these powers by adopting self-imposed limitations would considerably impair the power of the courts to perform and fulfil their duties of securing the enforcement of fundamental freedoms and supervising other adjudicating authorities."
