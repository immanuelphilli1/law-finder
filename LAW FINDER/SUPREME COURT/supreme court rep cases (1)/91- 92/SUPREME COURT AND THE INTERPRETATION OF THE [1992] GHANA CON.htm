SUPREME COURT AND THE INTERPRETATION OF THE [1992] GHANA CONSTITUTION VOL. XVII RGL 95—119

BIMPONG-BUTA, S.Y

 

.*

INTRODUCTION

The decisions of the Supreme Court - in the exercise of its original and final appellate jurisdiction under the previous Constitutions of Ghana and the Constitution, 19921 - have had and would in future have far-reaching socio-economic and political consequences. In the past, the court at the apex of the Ghana Judiciary, sometimes described as the full bench of the Court of Appeal, and now the Supreme Court, has, by a process of the interpretation of the Constitution, determined the rights and obligations not only between individuals but also between the individual and the State in matters such as: (a) the right of the government to dismiss persons holding some specified public offices in the exercise of its powers under section 9(1) of the transitional provisions to the Constitution, 19692; (b) the disqualification of a person duly elected in the 1969 general elections as not qualified to be a Member of Parliament because he had been "adjudged or otherwise declared” by a committee of inquiry to have acquired assets unlawfully while holding public office3; and (c) the appointment and removal of the Chief Justice under the Constitution, 1979.4 In relation to the present Supreme Court, the Constitution, 1992 of the Republic of Ghana5 has vested the court with original and final appellate jurisdiction to determine issues of interpretation which are binding not only on the Supreme Court itself but on all other courts in Ghana.6 Thus in relation to the exercise of its original jurisdiction, the Supreme Court may: (a) in an action declare that, "an enactment or anything contained in or done under the authority of” that enactment or any other enactment or "any act or omission of any person" is inconsistent with or contravenes a provision of the Constitution7; (b) declare the election of the President invalid on hearing a petition for that purpose8; (c) where there is doubt, may determine as to whether a document or its contents required to be produced before Parliament by a public officer should not be so produced because the document "would be injurious to the public interest or ... prejudicial to the security of the state;9 and (d) it has supervisory jurisdiction over all courts and adjudicating bodies.10

As regards the exercise of appellate jurisdiction, the Supreme Court determines the final appeal from the decision of the Court of Appeal in matters relating to the protection and enforcement by the courts of the fundamental human rights and freedoms conferred on every person by the Constitution, 199211; it hears appeals from the decision of the High Court on matters relating to the conviction for the offences of high treason or treason12; and an appeal from the Court of Appeal in civil and criminal matters13 and from the judicial committee of the National House of Chiefs in a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy.14 Finally, the Supreme Court has the power to review its previous decision.15

In exercising these functions or jurisdictions, certain fundamental questions arise for consideration by the Supreme Court: (i) should the court adopt the creative and purposive approach or the purely literalist or strict constructionist approach in construing the provisions of the Constitution - especially the provisions relating to the enforcement of the fundamental human rights and freedoms and the Directive Principles of State Policy; (ii) should the Supreme Court construe the Constitution not only in accordance with its letter but its spirit as well?; (iii) are the ordinary rules of interpretation appropriate in construing the fundamental law of the land?; and (iv) what is the true legal effect of the transitional provisions – especially sections 34 and 35 thereof relating to the indemnity given to the acts or omissions of a member of the PNDC, PNDC Secretary or appointee? We propose to examine in turn these questions and other related issues.

PROPER APPROACH TO THE INTERPRETATION OF THE CONSTITUTION

The case for the creative approach, as opposed to the purely literalist approach, is epitomised by the well-known dictum of Denning LJ (as he then was) in his dissenting opinion in the English Court of Appeal case of Magor and St Mellons RDC v Newport Corporation,16 namely:

"We do not sit here to pull the language of Parliament and of Ministers to pieces and make nonsense of it. This is an easy thing to do, and it is a thing to which lawyers are too often prone. We sit here to find out the intention of Parliament and of Ministers and carry it out, and we do this better by filling in the gaps and making sense of the enactment than by opening it up to destructive analysis."

This view in support of the creative approach was sharply rejected when the case came on appeal before the House of Lords where Lord Simonds said17:

"it is sufficient to say that the general proposition that it is the duty of the court to find out the intention of Parliament - and not only of Parliament but of Ministers also - cannot by any means be supported. The duty of the court is to interpret the words that the legislature has used; those words may be ambiguous, but, even if they are, the power and duty of the court to travel outside them on a voyage of discovery are strictly limited...

The second part of the passage that I have cited from the judgment of the learned Lord Justice is no doubt the logical sequel of the first. The court, having discovered the intention of Parliament and of Ministers too, must proceed to fill in the gaps. What the legislature has not written, the court must write. This proposition,... appears to me to be a naked usurpation of the legislative function under the thin disguise of interpretation."

On the available authorities, one can say that the Ghana Court of Appeal as the then highest appellate court in 1974 and 1975 in the cases of Asibey III v Ayisi18 and Armah v Naawu19 respectively and the present Supreme Court in the 1990 decision in Ribeiro v Ribeiro20  have in effect taken a rather ambivalent position on the vexed issue of adopting a creative or the strict literalist approach to interpretation of statutory provisions.

Thus in Asibey III v Ayisi (supra) and later in Armah v Naawu the Court of Appeal gave a diametrically opposed interpretation to the same statutory provision, ie the Courts (Amendment) Decree, 1972 (NRCD 101), s 3(2)(d) which stated: 

“3.(2) A full bench of the Court of Appeal shall have jurisdiction—

(d) to hear and determine any appeal which had been duly filed with the Supreme Court before the commencement of this Decree."

In both cases the court of Appeal was called upon to decide whether it had jurisdiction under section 3(2)(d) of NRCD 101 to determine an application “for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court" before its abolition by NRCD 101. It was conceded in both cases that no appeal had been physically filed with the Supreme Court before the commencement of NRCD 101. In adopting a purely literalist approach in Asibey III v Ayisi, the Court of Appeal unanimously dismissed the application for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court as incompetent. The court per Azu Crabbe CJ said21:

“It is plain that at the commencement of this Decree [NRCD. 101] there was no appeal in this case pending before the Supreme Court, and therefore, the full bench of the Court of Appeal is not clothed with jurisdiction to entertain any such appeal coming before it by leave. The Decree did not provide for cases falling under article 105 clause (1)(c) of the Constitution or section 3(1) (d) of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372), and unprovided for they must remain. The court cannot take upon itself to supply omissions in an enactment, for this would be a naked usurpation of the legislative function under the thin disguise of interpretation.”

However, in 1975 the Court of Appeal differently constituted in Armah v Naawu adopted a creative approach to the interpretation of the same section 3(2) (d) of NRCD 101. The majority per Amissah JA conceded that the application for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court, could not be heard by the full bench of the Court of Appeal under section 3(2) (d) of NRCD 101. But the majority in Naawu went on to criticise the unanimous decision in Asibey III v Ayisi as having been given per incuriam for its failure to consider whether the application for leave to appeal could not be converted into an application for leave to apply for a review by the full bench under section 3(2) (a) of NRCD 101. The majority granted the application on that basis and thus refused to follow the decision in Asibey III v Ayisi.

The majority in Naawu per Amissah JA based its decision on three main grounds : First, even though from the standpoint of its conception and procedure, a review was different from an appeal, in terms of its objects and effect, a review was the same as an appeal; second, since a person who had filed no application for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court against a decision of the Court of Appeal was entitled, after the abolition of the Supreme Court by NRCD 101, to apply for a review by the full bench of the Court of Appeal, there was no just reason why another person in the same position should be damnified or prejudiced from also applying for leave for a review by the full bench merely because he had exercised his right to apply for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court; and thirdly, the decision in Asibey III would have been otherwise if that court had considered section 3(2) of  NRCD 101 as a whole with particular reference to section 3(2) (a) instead of concentrating on section 3(2)(d) exclusively in resolving the issue before it. We must point out that the majority’s concern to do justice in the Naawu case and to arrive at an interpretation which fulfils the supposed intention of the legislature in enacting section 3(2) of NRCD 101 was the bedrock on which the decision was founded. In the words of Amissah JA,22 “there is the clearest intention expressed by the Decree that the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court should in some form continue in the full bench of the Court of Appeal.”

Not surprisingly, Archer JA (as he then was) who was on the panel that had earlier decided Asibey III dissented from the majority decision in Naawu. He held that an application for leave to appeal was not the same as an appeal duly filed with the Supreme Court. The argument founded on injustice was dismissed. He said23:

“It has been argued that injustice and hardship will be caused. I agree but when the legislature through an enactment has clearly conferred jurisdiction (with express limitation) on a court of law, I do not think it is open to that court to enhance and expand its jurisdiction in order to avoid injustice and hardship. In the present case, NRCD 101 is completely silent on application for leave to appeal to the former Supreme Court. Is it open to us for reasons for injustice and hardship advocated by an applicant, that the court should drive a coach and horses into NRCD 101 and confer upon itself jurisdiction to hear the application? With all due deference to my learned brothers, I am inclined to think that the course they have chosen in granting the application smacks of judicial subversion.  It seems to me that when certain matters have not been taken care of in an enactment, the defect or omission should be brought to the notice of the body with the appropriate legislative power.”24

It appears that the charge of judicial subversion levelled against the majority in Armah v Naawu is indefensible in that the decision avoided by a process of interpretation an unjust situation without doing any violence to the specific provisions of NRCD 101.

As earlier indicated the ambivalent position taken by the Court of Appeal (as the final appellate court) in the cases of Asibey III on the one hand and in Naawu on the other, is very much reflected in the decisions of the present Supreme Court. Thus the majority in the 1989 decision in Ribeiro v Ribeiro25 adopted the creative approach at the expense of the literalist approach. In this case, the High Court had ordered (after grant of divorce) the transfer to the wife of one of the houses owned by the husband as part of the award of financial provision under section 20(1) of the Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971 (Act 367) which provided:

"20.(1) The court may order either party to the marriage to pay to the other party such sum of money or convey to the other party such movable or immovable property as settlement of property rights or in lieu thereof or as part of financial provision as the court thinks just and equitable."

The decision of the trial court was affirmed by the Court of Appeal26; and also affirmed on a further appeal by the Supreme Court by majority of three to two.27 On an application by the husband for a review of the decision, the Supreme Court in dismissing the appeal by a three to two majority decision held per Adade JSC that28:

"Although section 20(1) speaks of the conveyance of such movable or immovable property . . . as part of financial provision, I believe in appropriate circumstances, a transfer of ‘movable and immovable' will not be illegal... It seems the intention in section 20(1) of Act 367 was not to limit the court to one or the other only. It is a case where or should properly be read as "and/or."29

The creative approach adopted by the Supreme Court may be contrasted with its earlier 1985 decision in Republic v Circuit Judge, Tamale; Ex parte Volta30 where on a purely literalist approach, it was held that an application for an extension of time to appeal against a final judgment of the Court of Appeal, filed seventeen days outside the permissible six months' deadline, could not be entertained under rule 8(6) of the Supreme Court Rules, 1970 (CI 130, for want of jurisdiction, regardless of the consequences.

It is interesting to note that the decision by the Supreme Court in Ex parte Volta that the courts had no power to extend statutory time limits regardless of the consequences has also been reached by the Nigerian Supreme Court in Ifezue v Mbadugha.31 The court had to construe section 258(1) of the 1979 Nigerian Constitution which provided "Every court... shall deliver its decision in writing not later than 3 months after the conclusion of evidence and final addresses." The Nigerian Supreme Court by a six to one majority decision construed section 258(1) as being mandatory and not merely directory, ie the court must deliver its judgment three months after conclusion of evidence and final addresses—regardless of the results arising from declaring a judgment void for non-compliance with the statutory period for delivery of judgments. The court pointed out that once the meaning of a statute was clear, the courts must give effect to it and not defeat the plain meaning by introducing its own words into the statute amounting "to a process of judicial legislation."

However, in a powerful dissenting opinion, Bello JSC construed section 258(1) of the Nigerian Constitution as merely directory and not mandatory otherwise a delay for even one day after the three months limitation period, would invalidate any judgment given resulting in unsatisfactory consequences such as a case being tried de novo. Such a situation could increase the length of trial at great cost instead of expediting the proceedings and would frustrate and not promote the object and purpose of the constitutional provision. In the words of Bello JSC, "It will be a shackle to the administration of justice and hinder its speed." In the view of his lordship a creative stance in the interpretation of section 258(1) would best serve its object and purpose.32

PROFFERED APPROACH TO THE INTERPRETATION OF THE CONSTITUTION

It is suggested that in construing the Constitution, 1992 the Supreme Court should abandon the present unsatisfactory ambivalent approach to interpretation—in one breadth adopting a creative approach, in another the literalist approach. It should rather adopt the creative approach, that would promote the apparent legislative purpose as gleaned either from the wording in the preamble or the enacting parts of the Constitution - what Lord Denning MR refers to as the purposive approach.33 It was this approach which informed the 1981 decision of the House of Lords in Fothergill v Monarch Airlines.34 The question in this case turned on the construction to be placed on the word "damage" in article 26(2) of the 1955 Warsaw Convention on International Carriage by Air.35 In adopting the purposive approach to the construction of the word "damage" as including partial loss of the content of a baggage, the court per Lord Wilberforce said36:

"I start by considering the purpose of article 26 . . . Consideration of the purpose of an enactment is always a legitimate part of the process of interpretation..."

It would appear that it was the same purposive approach which informed the decisions of the Court of Appeal in Armah v Naawu and the Supreme Court review decision in Ribeiro v Ribeiro. As earlier noted, the literal approach maintains that judges just stick to the plain and literal meaning of the statutory words despite the unjust, capricious, absurd and unreasonable results emanating from such an interpretation. Such an approach places the judges in a dilemma because they have the duty to do justice and not injustice to all parties appearing before them. It is therefore suggested that it is permissible for the judges, without doing violence to the words of the Constitution, to fill in apparent loopholes and adopt a creative interpretation that would advance the apparent constitutional policy, objectives and purposes.

We must, however, stress that the literalist approach or interpretation that advances the objects and purposes of the Constitution, ie the legislative will is preferable to an interpretation founded on technicalities which might in fact defeat the objects and purposes of the Constitution. Thus in Awoonor-Williams v Gbedemah37 the Supreme Court by a four to one majority rejected the defence contention that the words "adjudged or otherwise declared" in article 71(2)(b)(ii) of the Constitution, 1969—were terms of art with a recognised legal meaning and that a judgement or declaration could only properly be made by a court of law and not by a committee of inquiry. In the words of the majority per Apaloo JA:

"Indeed the ordinary dictionary meaning of the words show that the court has no monopoly over the use of these words . . . the words ‘adjudge or declare' have no technical connotation and in the context of Article 71(2)(b) means that the Commission of Inquiry ‘found or pronounced' that a person acquired assets unlawfully . . ."

Again in Sallah v Attorney-General38 the attempt by the defence to construe the word "established" in section 9(1) of the transitional provisions to the Constitution, 1969 in a meaning other than its ordinary meaning, ie a technical meaning as "deriving legal validity from" was rightly, in my respectful view, rejected by the Supreme Court. The court by a three to two majority held that section 9(1) must be construed in its ordinary, popular dictionary meaning as "to set up or create." Therefore it was held that any person who held or acted in any office created or set up by the NLC Proclamation, a Decree of the NLC or under an authority of the NLC was caught by section 9(1). And since the appointment of Mr Sallah did not fall under any of the above heads, the termination of his appointment by the Government was unlawful. In rejecting the technical meaning or interpretation founded on Kelson’s jurisprudential theory of law, Archer JA (as he then was) said (in words which may be endorsed by the present Supreme Court)39.:

"The Constitution of Ghana is a conglomeration of English words. The lives of citizens depend on those words. Unless these words are specially defined in the Constitution, then the ordinary meaning of these words as properly and generally understood by literate persons should prevail. No system of jurisprudence, however popular, be it analytical positivism, the Pure Theory, or the Historical, can assist the courts in this country in their interpretation of the Constitution."

And whilst stressing the literal meaning of the provisions of the Constitution as advancing the objects and purposes, the judges must, with respect, also stress the fact that a constitution is a political document. As the fundamental law it must not be narrowly construed by the courts. It must be given a wide, generous and purposive construction in the context of the people's aspirations and hopes and with special reference to the political, social and economic development of the country. It is therefore necessary to stress the commendable words of the Supreme Court per Sowah JSC (as he then was) in Tuffuor v Attorney General40:

"A written Constitution such as ours is not an ordinary Act of Parliament. It embodies the will of a people. It also mirrors their history. Account, therefore, needs to be taken of it as a landmark in a people's search for progress. It contains within it their aspirations and their hopes for a better and fuller life.

The Constitution has its letter of the law. Equally, the Constitution has its spirit . . . Its language . . . must be considered as if it were a living organism capable of growth and development . . . A broad and liberal spirit is required for its interpretation. It does not admit of a narrow interpretation. A doctrinaire approach to interpretation would not do. We must take account of its principles and bring that consideration to bear, in bringing it into conformity with the needs of the time."

It would be recalled that the Supreme Court in Tuffuor held that the incumbent Chief Justice, Hon Mr Justice Apaloo, was a member of the class of justices referred to in article 127(8) of the Constitution, 1979 and that on the coming into force of the Constitution, Justice Apaloo, became the Chief Justice of Ghana by virtue of article 127(8) and (9) (incidentally the same provision is to be found in section 4 of the 1992 Transitional Provisions). It is interesting to note that barely a month after the Ghana Supreme Court had delivered its opinion in Tuffuor that the narrow rules of interpretation were inappropriate in construing a national Constitution, similar views were expressed by the Supreme Court of Nigeria in Rabiu v Kano State41 where the case turned on the interpretation to be placed on the word "decision" in section 277 of the 1979 Nigerian Constitution. The Nigerian Supreme Court per Udoma JSC said42:

"The function of the Constitution is to establish a framework and principles of government, broad and general in terms, intended to apply to the varying conditions which the development of our several communities must involve, our being a plural, dynamic society, and therefore, mere technical rules of interpretation of statutes are to some extent inadmissible in a way so as to defeat the principles of government enshrined in the Constitution. And where the question is whether the Constitution has used an expression in the wider or in the narrower sense this court should whenever possible, and in response to the demands of justice, lean to the broader interpretation, unless there is something in the text or in the rest of the Constitution to indicate that the narrower interpretation will best carry out the objects and purposes of the Constitution.”

A similar view was expressed in Attorney-General of the Gambia v Jobe43 where the Privy Council, on appeal from the judgment of the Gambian Court of Appeal, held that a Constitution, particularly that protecting and entrenching fundamental rights and freedoms, was to be given a “generous and purposive construction” and that the word “property” in section 18(1) of the Gambian Constitution was to be read in a wide sense to include choses in action such as a debt owed by a banker to a customer; and that the expression “right over or interest in” would cover a claim for repayment of a debt or any part of it on demand. It was further held that if section 10 of the Act had the effect of conferring an executive discretion on the Inspector-General of Police to prevent a customer from drawing on his bank account, then the section would be ultra vires section 18(1) of the Constitution.

It is suggested that these are some of the principles of interpretation which may be applied by the Supreme Court in construing or interpreting the provisions of the Constitution—particularly those relating to the Fundamental Human Rights and Freedoms.

INTERPRETATION OF FUNDAMENTAL HUMAN RIGHTS AND FREEDOMS

It would be recalled that under article 13(1) of the Constitution, 1960 it was mandatory for the President of the First Republic, immediately after assumption of office, to make a number of solemn declarations before the people, including the declaration:

 

“That no person should suffer discrimination on grounds of sex, race, tribe, religion or political belief. That subject to such restrictions as may be necessary for preserving public order, morality or health, no person should be deprived of freedom of religion or speech, of the right to move and assemble without hindrance or of the right of access to courts of law.

That no person should be deprived of his property save where the public interest so requires and the law so provides.”

It is common knowledge that article 13(1) (which carried in its sidenotes, the words "Declaration of fundamental principles") was the subject-matter of interpretation by the Supreme Court in the 1961 decision in Re Akoto.44 If the Supreme Court in this case very much appreciated the political nature of the declaration of fundamental principles as specified in article 13(1), it also made it very clear that it was not prepared to give a wider and generous interpretation to the wording of article 13(1). Unlike the 1980 decision in Tuffuor v Attorney-General (supra) where the Supreme Court warned of the danger of relying on a narrow technical rules of interpretation in construing a national Constitution,45 the Supreme Court in Re Akoto applied the ordinary rules of interpretation when it held that whereas in other parts of the 1960 Constitution where a duty was imposed - the word "shall" was employed, throughout the declaration of fundamental principles in article 13(1), the word "should" was used. The court thus held that the Preventive Detention Act, 1958 did not contravene article 13(1); that the declaration by the President on assumption of office was similar to the Coronation Oath of the Queen of England and that such a declaration did not constitute a bill of rights enforceable in a court of law. In the words of the court per Korsah CJ46:

"In our view the declaration merely represents the goal which every President must pledge himself to attempt to achieve . . .

The declarations however impose on every President a moral obligation, and provide a political yardstick by which the conduct of the Head of State can be measured by the electorate. The people's remedy for any departure from the principles of the declaration, is through the use of the ballot box, and not through the courts."

It is very clear, in the light of this pronouncement, that the Supreme Court overly stressed the political underpinnings of the fundamental declarations of the Constitution, 1960. Even if it were right in so doing, that was no justification for holding that article 13(1) merely created a moral right and not a legally enforceable right. If the declarations in article 13(1) were merely moral, bereft of any legal right enforceable in the court, why did the framers of the Constitution describe them as fundamental principles? If the peoples' remedy for any contravention of the fundamental principles was in the words of the court, "through the use of the ballot box" what (one may ask) was the purpose of the provision that "no person should be deprived of freedom of religion or speech, of the right to move and assemble without hindrance or by the right of access to courts of law?" Surely these words were not meant to be mere political rhetoric: "The fundamental rule is that a Constitution is a meaningful document; its voice carries higher and further than ordinary legislation."47 It is suggested that having taken due account of the political character or dimensions of the fundamental principles of the Constitution, the Supreme Court in Re Akoto should have used it as a basis for giving a generous and broad interpretation to the declarations in article 13(1), ie that it sought to give to the individual a legal right enforceable by the courts.48

It is for this reason that the framers of the Constitutions, 1969, 1979 and 1992 must be highly commended for providing in plain words that the enjoyment of the fundamental human rights and freedoms of every person in Ghana is not a mere moral obligation on the President as the Supreme Court regrettably held in the Re Akoto case: they are all enforceable by the courts.49

Such an action may be brought in the High Court and on an appeal therefrom to the Court of Appeal and a further right of appeal to the Supreme Court. Article 12(1) of the Constitution, 1992 goes further: the fundamental human rights and freedoms "shall be respected and held by the Executive, Legislature and Judiciary and all other organs of government and its agencies." In fact under article 69(1) of the Constitution, 1992 the President is liable to be removed from office if he wilfully violates any provision of the Constitution, inclusive of course of the provisions relating to fundamental human rights and freedoms. These rights include50: the right to life and personal liberty; the right of equality before the law; the right to own property and to payment of prompt and adequate compensation upon compulsory acquisition of property by the State and the right to fair trial; the right to freedom of speech and expression; the right to practice any religion and the right and freedom of all Ghanaian citizens to form or join political parties.

It is suggested that in interpreting the provisions relating to the fundamental human rights and freedoms, the Supreme Court in particular and the Judiciary as a whole must be guided not only by the Directive Principles of State Policy as required of them under article 34(1) of the Constitution; they must also be guided by and their decisions must reflect what the Constitution refers to as the spirit of the Constitution. The Constitution does make reference to the phrase "the spirit of the Constitution" in articles 17(4)(d) and 21(4)(e) but it does not define what is meant by the "Spirit of the Constitution." It is suggested that the Supreme Court may, in the exercise of its power of interpretation, define it to include not only the aspirations of our people but also their declaration and affirmation of their commitment to freedom and justice, probity and accountability, the rule of law, the protection and preservation of fundamental human rights and freedoms within the meaning of the preamble to the Constitution. We may therefore in this respect underline the words of the Supreme Court in Tuffour v Attorney-General per Sowah JSC51:

"The Constitution has its letter of the law. Equally, the Constitution has its spirit. It is the fountain-head for the authority which each of the three arms of government possesses and exercises . . . It is a source of power . . . A broad and liberal spirit is required for its interpretation . . . And . . . a construction should be avoided which leads to absurdity and when a particular interpretation leads to two, shall we say ‘inconsistent’ results, the spirit of the Constitution would demand that the more reasonable of the two should be adhered to. We must have recourse to the Constitution as a whole."

And it is the spirit of the Constitution and the principles of interpretation expressed in the above dictum which may be applied by the Supreme Court in construing the provisions of the 1992 Constitution—particularly its transitional provisions.

THE TRANSITIONAL PROVISIONS

The transitional provisions deal with other matters of a purely transitional nature: the election of the first president and parliament52: the issue of succession of courts and the public tribunals and proceedings pending before them immediately before the coming into force of the Constitution53; the appointment of certain offices such as the Commissioner for Human Rights and Administrative Justice54 and the appointment of the electoral commission after the coming into force of the Constitution.55 "These purely transitional matters are vital for the smooth transition from a military government to a constitutional and democratic government. These provisions have not engendered any controversy. What is controversial and thus requires judicial interpretation by the Supreme Court are sections 34-37 of the transitional provisions - particularly section 34 which has as it side-note, the word "indemnity."

THE INDEMNITY CLAUSES

The said section 34(1)-(5) provides56:

"34.(1) No member of the Provisional National Defence Council, Provisional National Defence Council Secretary, or other appointees, of the Provisional National Defence Council shall be held liable either jointly or severally, for any act or omission during the administration of the Provisional National Defence Council.

(2) It is not lawful for any court or tribunal to entertain any action or take any decision or make any order or grant any remedy or relief in any proceedings instituted against the Government of Ghana or any person acting under the authority of the Government of Ghana whether before or after the coming into force of this Constitution or against any person or persons acting in concert or individually to assist or bring about the change in Government which took place on the twenty-fourth day of February 1966, on the thirteenth day of January, 1972, on fourth day of June, 1979 and on the thirty-first day of December, 1981 in respect of any act or omission relating to, or consequent upon— . . . the suspension or abrogation of the Constitution of 1960, 1969 and 1979.”

(3) For the avoidance of doubt, it is declared that no executive, legislative or judicial action taken or purported to have been taken by the Provisional National Defence Council or the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council or a member of the Provisional National Defence Council or the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council or by any person appointed by the Provisional National Defence Council or the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council in the name of either the Provisional National Defence Council or the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council shall be questioned in any proceedings whatsoever and, accordingly, it shall not be lawful for any court or other tribunal to make any order or grant any remedy or relief in respect of any such act.

(4) The provisions of subsection (3) of this section shall have effect notwithstanding that any such action as is referred to in that subsection was not taken in accordance with any procedure prescribed by law.

(5) It is not lawful for any court or tribunal to entertain an action instituted in respect of an act or omission against a person acting or omitting to act, on the instructions or authority of the Provisional National Defence Council or the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council or a member of the Provisional National Defence Council or the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council and alleged to be in contravention of any law, whether substantive or procedural, in existence before or during the administration of the Provisional National Defence Council or the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council."

The generally accepted dictionary meaning of the word "indemnity" is: “protection or insurance against penalties incurred by one’s action."57 The question which may have to be determined by the Supreme Court after the coming into force of the Constitution is: what is the extent or ambit of the indemnity accorded by section 34? Compared with the indemnity clauses in the 1969 and 1979 transitional provisions, article 34 of the 1992 Transitional Provisions covers three distinct areas of indemnity. First: section 34(2); second section 34(3), (4) and (5) and third, section 34(1). We shall examine these distinct areas of indemnity in turn.

The wording of the indemnity in section 34(2) is basically the same as section 13(3) of the 1969 provisions and section 15(1) of the 1979 provisions. This provision, ie section 34(2) in effect seeks to legalise the unlawful, violent and unconstitutional overthrow of the Constitutions, 1966, 1969 and 1979. It grants immunity from prosecution to the organisers of the successful coup d’états, of the 1966 NLC, 1972 NRC, 1975 SMC, 1979 AFRC and 1981 PNDC. It is suggested that, this immunity is necessary and justifiable and may be upheld by the Supreme Court on grounds of pragmatism, good sense, and the maintenance of peace and stability in the country.58

The next area of indemnity is provided in section 34(3), (4) and (5). Again the wording of this section is basically the same as section 15(2) and (3) of the 1969 provisions. There is, however, no equivalent provision in the 1969 provisions. The section seeks to prevent the court from questioning in any proceedings whatsoever the executive, legislative or judicial action taken or purported to have been taken either by the PNDC and AFRC or by any person appointed by these regimes even if the action "was not taken in accordance with any procedure prescribed by law." It seems the provision in section 34(3), (4) and (5) seeks to give indemnity to the subsequent action taken in furtherance of the illegal action indemnified by section 34(2). The provision in section 34(3), (4) and (5) is therefore also justifiable and necessary for the reasons advanced for the provision in section 34(2).

The provision in section 34(3), (4) and (5) (the same as 15(2) and (3) of the 1979 transitional provisions) had already been judicially construed by the Supreme Court in Kwakye v Attorney-General.59 In this case, the plaintiff, the former Inspector-General of Police, sued in the Supreme Court for declaration that he was never tried and convicted by the special court set up under the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Special Courts) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 3) and that his purported imprisonment of 25 years by the special court was an infringement of his fundamental human rights under the Constitution, 1979 and therefore void. The State raised a preliminary objection to the application founded on the indemnity clause or what lawyers refer to as the ouster clause in section 15(2).The preliminary objection was unanimously dismissed by the Supreme Court in its first ruling delivered on 22 March 1981: see Kwakye v Attorney-General.60 It was unanimously held that the defendant, ie the State - relying on the ouster clause in section 15(2) must provide the factual basis for the application of the section. In other words, the State must produce facts showing that the AFRC took or purported to have taken judicial action against the plaintiff.61 The significance and implication of the earlier ruling in the Kwakye case must not be lost on anyone! The Supreme Court would not initially shy away from satisfying itself that there was an executive, legislative or judicial action taken or purported to have been taken by the PNDC in terms of section 34(3) of the transitional provisions.

Be that as it may, the Supreme Court in its final judgment by a five to two majority decision dismissed the plaintiff’s claim and held62 that even though there was no proof of judicial action, ie an action satisfying the requirements of the law both procedurally and substantively, there was proof of a purported judicial action taken, ie an action which looked like or was intended to be or had the outward appearance of judicial action. The majority in Kwakye were very much influenced by the provision in section 15(3), the same as section 34(4) of the present indemnity clause, ie the purported judicial action would not be questioned even if not taken "in accordance with any procedure prescribed by law." In the words of the majority per Archer JSC (as he then was)63:

"The true meaning of section 15(2) [the same as 34(3) in 1992 transitional provisions] appears to be that whenever the court is satisfied that the AFRC took or purported to have taken an executive, legislative or judicial action, then that court shall not question the validity, the correctness, the fairness or the justice of that decision or action. Simply, the court should not interfere."

The majority so held even though there was no evidence placed before the Supreme Court showing that the body or group of persons, ie "the court", had, before convicting the accused, heard and considered evidence in the course of its purported trial of the accused. However, the minority, particularly Taylor JSC, took the view that the need for calling evidence was “not just a procedural requirement, it (was) a substantive requirement." He held that in the absence of evidence orally or by affidavit, there could be no trial or appearance of a trial or a purported trial. If it is conceded that the calling of evidence by the special court under section 3 of AFRCD 3 was not just a matter of procedure but a fundamental requirement in a trial or purported trial by the special court, then on the facts, the majority in Kwakye clearly gave it blessing to a proven case of a nullity. In the words of Sowah JSC64: "Even though I consider the trial, conviction and sentence of the plaintiff were a nullity because the trial itself did not match up to the criteria set by AFRC 3, s 5, nevertheless I hold the view that it was a purported trial . . .”

We should point out, however, that in the words of Lord Reid in the House of Lords decision in the Anisminic case65: "No case has been cited in which any other form of words limiting the jurisdiction of the court has been held to protect a nullity." It must, however, be conceded that the majority in Kwakye per Apaloo CJ sought to distinguish the Anisminic case when he said66:

"The court [in the Anisminic Case] held that determination meant plainly determination and not a "purported determination." If the statute had provided an ouster in relation to "purported" determination as well, that would have made a world of difference. In our constitutional provision, the constitutional shield extends not only to the judicial action of the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council but its ‘purported' judicial action as well.”

The Supreme Court's decision in Kwakye raises to the fore the vexed question: as to whether in the light of the peoples' declaration and affirmation of commitment to Justice, and Rule of Law as expressed in the preamble to the Constitution, 1992 the Supreme Court as the final appellate court can or should give its blessing to clear cases of nullity, injustice and illegalities and proven cases of criminality on grounds of technicalities of the law? In my view, the answer is a simple No!

The Supreme Court may have to declare when it considers the true effect of the third indemnity in section 34(1) of the 1992 transitional provisions which provides that:

"34.(1) No member of the Provisional National Defence Council, Provisional National Defence Council Secretary, or other appointees of the Provisional National Defence Council shall be held liable either jointly or severally, for any act or omission during the administration of the Provisional National Defence Council."

It must be stressed that this is a novel indemnity clause. It has no equivalent provision in the Constitutions, 1969 and 1979. What appears to be the true legal position? On the face of it, section 34(1) means that no member of the PNDC, PNDC Secretary or appointee is personally liable or to use the popular word, accountable "for any act or omission during the administration of the PNDC."

It seems to me that it is this novel indemnity clause, ie section 34(1) which has generated so much public debate and controversy. It appears the views publicly expressed for and against the transitional provisions are properly referable to section 34(1) and not the other provisions which as already indicated, are justifiable on grounds of pragmatism, peace and harmony so as to ensure smooth transition from the PNDC military government to a constitutional democratic government.

Some of the views publicly expressed have raised the issues of: (a) the policy rationale behind the inclusion or exclusion of the indemnity provision in the transitional provisions—particularly the unaccountability clause in section 34(1). On the one hand, Professor Ofosu-Amaah, Dean of the Faculty of Law, Legon, was quoted as having said in an interview with The Graphic67 that he had expected the transitional provisions in the Constitution, 1992 to deal "with who will control Ghana during this transitional period." He was also quoted as having said that: "because of the PNDC Government’s rhetoric and the things they did immediately after taking power, he assumed that they would not have entertained any question of their actions being indemnified in any way . . ."

He was also quoted as having said that he assumed that the PNDC would have rejected any indemnity offered by the Consultative Assembly itself. Another notable personality Dr Hilla Limman, the President under the Third Republic, was also quoted by the People's Daily Graphic as having said at a Press Conference68: that the 1992 transitional provisions contradict the provisions of the Constitution which guarantee fundamental human rights, freedoms, probity and accountability. On the other hand, in support of the inclusion of the transitional provisions, Professor Kwame Arhin also of the University of Ghana, Legon and who was the PNDC nominee at the Consultative Assembly, expressed the view in a letter to the People's Daily Graphic that69:

"It is touchingly human that the PNDC Government should seek to protect, not only themselves, but also their agents in the prosecution of the Revolution."

It must be pointed out that even though the Consultative Assembly decided to include the whole of the transitional provisions in the Constitution without a word of debate, the Chairman of the Assembly's Committee on State Policy, Mr Stanley-Pierre, was subsequently quoted by the People's Daily Graphic as having explained in an interview with the paper that70:

"The Consultative Assembly indemnified PNDC Members and appointees in the transitional provisions in the final draft of the Constitution because it believes that the Fourth Republic should be started on a clean slate without sowing the seeds of rancour, litigation and retribution."

Mr Stanley-Pierre was also quoted as having given the example of five writs of summons which had been issued against a PNDC appointee for acts allegedly committed whilst in office - within a week of his removal from office. In the words of Mr Stanley-Pierre, "If we didn't cover these people imagine the number of writs that will be issued. Ghanaians will fight among themselves when the Fourth Republic is ushered in."

In my view, whatever the policy rationale for and against the insertion of the indemnity clause—particularly, section 34(1), it is not with respect, the business of the Supreme Court to concern itself with the propriety or otherwise of the insertion of the indemnity clause in the transitional provisions. It is a political decision; and it is not the duty of the Supreme Court to question that decision by a process of interpretation. Neither should the judges base their interpretation of the law on what they conceive to be the general policy or what should be the general policy of the realm. In R v Barnet LBC; Ex parte Shah71 the English House of Lords reversed the Court of Appeal's interpretation of the 1962 UK Education Act and the 1979 Regulations made thereunder because the lower court's judges "were influenced by their own views of policy." In the words of the House of Lords per Lord Scarman 72:

"The way in which they used policy was, an impermissible approach to interpretation of statutory language. Judges may not interpret statutes in the light of their own views as to policy."

Similar opinion was expressed by his Lordship Taylor JSC in his opinion in support of the 1984 unanimous decision of our Supreme Court in Patu-Styles v Amoo-Lamptey73 where he adopted the following dictum of Lord Sterndale MR in Scranton's Trustee v Pearse74:

“I think it is an extraordinarily dangerous and mischievous doctrine to hold that a Court in considering whether it can give effect to an Act of Parliament or not, is to examine the Act and see whether it considers it is the kind of legislation which is consistent with the general policy of the realm. Such an attitude . . . is one which might lead to extraordinary results . . . all this Court can do is to say: Is that what is enacted by the statute? and if it is, it must give effect to it . . ."

The question then is what is the true legal effect of section 34(1) of the transitional provisions? It is clear that the unaccountability or indemnity extends to "any act or omission during the administration of the PNDC." Some newspaper columnists and politicians have expressed varying views as to the extent of the indemnity in the novel section 34(1). One columnist in a comment entitled "Provisional Temptations"75 wrote inter alia, perhaps jokingly: "So then now whatever anyone pilfered, pinched, grabbed devoured, swallowed is forgiven so long as it was official theft." On a more serious note, the Chairman of the PNDC was quoted by the People's Daily Graphic of having explained the extent of the indemnity whilst addressing a workers durbar at Koforidua, in the Eastern Region of Ghana on 15 April 1992.76 He was quoted as having said that: "any individual who for personal interest abused the office he held under the PNDC or who committed fraud or embezzlement would not be protected by the Transitional Provisions."

He was also quoted as having expressed the regret that:

"Some people are trying to mislead the public into believing that the transitional provisions give protection to any PNDC Member or appointee for any wrong or injustice or crime committed since 31st December 1981."

Chairman Rawlings was further quoted as having said that:

"What is being protected by the transitional provisions are PNDC Laws, policies and directives made in the national interest and those whose duty it is to implement them."

It is suggested that on a proper interpretation of section 34(1) the Supreme Court may hold that the words "any act or omission" are not to be construed as any act or omission, generally, whether lawful or unlawful. That on a reading of the Constitution as a whole, the words "any act or omission" must be construed in its larger extensive meaning of being limited to lawful acts or omissions taken in the course of official or public duty.77 Such an interpretation would be in accord with the spirit of the Constitution, especially article 57(5) and (6). Under this article civil or criminal proceedings may be instituted against the President within three years of his ceasing to be President in respect of anything done or omitted to be done by him in his personal capacity before or during his term of office.78 Furthermore, the proffered interpretation of section 34(i) as not providing indemnity for personal unlawful acts or omissions is also in accord with the recent developments in law of accountability. By the promulgation of the Head of Family (Accountability) Law, 1985 (PNDCL 114), the PNDC, in line with its avowed aim of ensuring accountability and probity in public life has abolished the previously settled customary law doctrine of non accountability of the head of family in respect of family property held in trust.79 Equally the Supreme Court in its decision 1991 unanimous decision in Owusu v Agyei,80 (the Kumawu Land Acquisition case) has exploded and disaffirmed previous settled customary law doctrine that an occupant of a stool was not accountable to his subjects.

The conclusion is clear: section 34(1) does not prevent a PNDC Member or appointee from being proceeded against for any personal civil and criminal liability in respect of anything done or omitted to be done by him in his personal capacity or for things done on a frolic of his own, ie not as authorised by the PNDC or any law.

AMENDMENT OF THE CONSTITUTION

I would like to conclude this lecture by considering a very important question which the Supreme Court might be called upon to determine. Are all the provisions of the Constitution amendable? In this respect, a leading politician was recently quoted by the People's Daily Graphic of May 6 1992 as having said that his club would be turned into a political party and that: "when it is voted into power, some clauses in the Constitution, including those on indemnity would be repealed."81 Such a statement on the question of the amendment by repeal of the indemnity clause shows a gross misreading of the amendment provisions of the Constitution. Under the Constitution, art 289, Parliament has the power to amend any provision of the Constitution but that power and the mode of exercising it, is severely limited by other provisions of the Constitution, ie articles 290-292 and section 37 of the transitional provisions. Thus all the provisions of the Constitution have been classified as entrenched or non-entrenched.82 The entrenched provisions include: chapter 5 - the Fundamental Human Rights and Freedoms; chapter 25 - amendment of the Constitution and article 299 which provides that the transitional provisions specified in the First Schedule to the Constitution shall have effect notwithstanding anything to the contrary to the Constitution.

A Bill providing for an amendment of an entrenched provision can be passed by Parliament after it has been submitted to a referendum and at least forty per cent of the persons, entitled to vote, voted at the referendum and at least seventy-five per cent of the persons who voted cast their votes in support of the passing of the Bill.83 However, a bill proposing an amendment of a non-entrenched provision, can be passed by Parliament after a three-month publication in the Gazette without a prior approval in a referendum.84 The other severe limitation to Parliament's power of amendment is section 37 of the transitional provisions which provides that:

"Notwithstanding anything in chapter 25 of this Constitution, Parliament shall have no power to amend this section or sections 34 and 35 of this Schedule."

It must be stated that under the Constitution, 1979 the indemnity clause could also not be amended by Parliament85 except that as already pointed out, the 1979 indemnity clause did not include the present unaccountability clause in the novel section 34(1).

The effect of the amendment provision in section 37 is clear: On its proper construction, the Supreme Court may hold that section 34 - the indemnity clause and section 35 dealing with the preservation of confiscation and penalties imposed by the AFRC and the PNDC - cannot be amended by Parliament in exercise of its power of amendment under the Constitution. It is therefore clearly a misnomer to describe sections 34 and 35 as – transitional provisions. They do not even fall into the category of an entrenched or non-entrenched provisions. They are best described as permanent provisions. The fundamental question which the Supreme Court may have to grapple with is whether it can or may refuse to give effect to section 37 of the transitional provisions on the grounds that it is against public policy or does it? If "sovereignty resides in the people of Ghana" as stated in the very first article of the Constitution, why can't the people in a referendum amend any provision of the Constitution which had been previously approved by the people in a referendum? That is a political decision which the Supreme Court may best leave alone!!

CONCLUSION

We have in this paper considered and examined what, in the overall interests of the people of Ghana, should be the proper attitude of the Supreme Court and the principles of interpretation which may guide them in their onerous task of interpreting the provisions of the Constitution, 1992—particularly the provisions relating to the fundamental human rights provisions and the indemnity clause, particularly section 34(1) of the transitional provisions. In performing that role - a very crucial role, if the Fourth Republic is to succeed - the Supreme Court must not interpret the Constitution on the basis of its conception of what the policy of the law should be. On the contrary, it must promote by a process of interpretation, the apparent and express policies of the Constitution as a political document capable of growth. In exercising the power of interpreting the provisions of the Constitution, the judges themselves must, with the utmost respect, be committed to upholding freedom, justice, probity and accountability. Judicial courage must not desert them. Furthermore, "it is necessary as far as practicable to have [in the Supreme Court] some unanimity in... decisions - particularly in the interpretation of the Constitution86 This not only ensures the efficacy of the judgment of the Supreme Court regarding the interpretation of the Constitution; it also ensures its development as a living political document. In this vein the extra-judicial pronouncement of Archer CJ on the occasion of the newly appointed judges of the Supreme Court must be underlined. He said87:

"The Judges had been appointed at a time when the nation was engaged in a serious exercise to work out a new Constitution. The success of the Constitution will depend on the integrity, skill and wisdom of the Justices of the Supreme Court in the interpretation and enforcement of that Constitution."
