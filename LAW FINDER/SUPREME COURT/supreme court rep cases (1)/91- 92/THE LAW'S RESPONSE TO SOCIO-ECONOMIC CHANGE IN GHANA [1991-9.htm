THE LAW'S RESPONSE TO SOCIO-ECONOMIC CHANGE IN GHANA [1991-92] VOL. XVIII RGL 244—268

Agbosu L. K.

 

*

INTRODUCTION

The functioning of society is regulated by social norms, rules and laws. The laws largely reflect social practice. Such practice is founded on producing the material means of sustenance.  Therefore the economic structure that emerges at any historically determined stage of society's development would produce laws in its own image.  The laws and the economic structure are thus interconnected, interrelated and interdependent. They affect, overlap, influence and dovetail into each other. But as legal expressions of property and other social relations, the law stands in a secondary relationship of socio-economic activity.

That is what it should be; for knowledge or cognitive activity is based on the reflection of concrete objective reality in the consciousness of man. It involves the conscious reproduction of the object under study, its properties, relations and connections in its ideal form in the consciousness of the observer. A fortiori all objects, processes and phenomena are primary in relation to consciousness.  That is to say that consciousness is secondary in relation to matter.

Ex hypothesi, the law must necessarily reflect the concrete objective reality of social practice, ie socio-economic activity.  If we are right in this view, then Roscoe Pound's conception of the law as a tool of social engineering could not be accurate if the underlying assumption of his thesis is that the law can, by itself, bring about social and economic progress.  That would be a travesty of the true position—a metaphysical abstraction of reality. The law as a reflection of social practice plays a promotional role.  It gives impetus to socio-economic activity by liberating and removing all the legal impediments to social and economic progress.  At that level of society's development where it ceases to mirror social practice and thus becomes a letter or a clog on further development, new laws are required to repeal those which have become outmoded. The new law then reflects the new economic trends and structures, thus giving a fillip to development.  In this sense, the law must follow social practice as does equity, the law.

It is thus our major preoccupation in this paper to attempt, by way of specific examples, to show that as consciousness is primary in relation to matter, all changes that have taken place in the customary and common laws of Ghana are reflections of the corresponding changes in economic activity and its concomitant social progress.  An attempt would accordingly be made to highlight the limitations of the law as a "tool of social engineering.  "When the law does not follow, but is at variance with the social and economic arrangements by which production and services are organised in society, it would be observed more in its breach than in its obedience. This is the fact which the lawmaker and all those concerned with the administration of law and justice must continually bear in mind.1

In order to fulfil the task set for ourselves, two specific areas of socio-economic activity in Ghana will be scrutinised. These are: (a) accountability under the customary law, and (b) indigenous mining.

ACCOUNTABILITY UNDER THE INDIGENOUS LAW

(i) The Customary Pledgee

One of the controversial themes in the customary law of Ghana is the alleged unaccountability of certain persons under the indigenous law. It is thus a well-known customary principle that the main benefit conferred on a pledgee under the indigenous pledge system is the right to exploit the land, the subject of the pledge, and enjoy its profits without accounting for it. In applying this established principle to specific cases in the adjudicative processes of the courts, some of the judges would seem to conceive of the law in metaphysical terms by ignoring the social norms and practices of which the principle is reflective. The principle is often treated in its application as if it is an a priori proposition existing eternally, that is as a category apart, existing independently of the socio-economic activity that should determine its form, essence and content.

We thus find in the West African Court of Appeal case of Dapaah v Poku,2 for instance, that the appellant "mortgaged" a cocoa farm to the respondent to secure a loan of £60.  The agreement provided that the appellant should recover the farm after thirteen years. After four years, the appellant sought to redeem the mortgage. During those four years the respondent had made profit of between £20 and £60 per annum from the farm.  The first issue argued in the appeal was whether or not the transaction was a native mortgage. Reversing the judgment of the trial court which had held that the mortgage should continue for the thirteen years, Blackall P said3:

"The essence of a native mortgage is that the mortgagee gets possession and enjoys the profits without accounting for them, while the mortgagor on his part may at any time redeem the mortgage, provided he pays back the loan and also all the expenses which the mortgagor has been put to."

(The emphasis is mine.)

This statement of the principle without qualification creates problems. It does not take into account the transformation taking place in the womb of the traditional communal system of production on account of the dominant influence of the capitalist ways of organising production established at the commercial and city centres of the country.  The fact has been overlooked that in pre-colonial times the exploitation of land within the family was predominantly at subsistence levels. Before the introduction of cash crops and permanent cultivation of the soil, land values were not high. Even at the early periods of European contacts and the establishment of capitalist institutions, the hinterland remained largely unaffected by those influences. Moreover, the effect of the Trans-Atlantic slave trade had not only disrupted economic and social life but it also depopulated the countryside in West Africa.4 This made the population to land area ratio very large. Under these conditions, land, the possession of which was granted in return for a loan, was usually a virgin land—a property of nature ready to be exploited through the expenditure of the pledgee's labour.

The benefit derived from the exploitation of the land by the pledgee who entered into possession was the fruit of his own labour.  While group efforts in the production and exploitation of resources is a feature of traditional societies, individual effort in the creation of wealth is recognised and protected as the creator's personal property.  What the pledgee produced through the cultivation of the land thus became his personal property of which he was accountable to none.  Moreover, inefficient methods of production due to technological backwardness made the production of large surpluses difficult and almost impossible.  These material conditions were responsible for the customary practice whereby the pledgor would deliver possession to another in return for a loan with a right in the pledgee to benefit from the exploitation of the land without accounting to the pledgor.  It was thus the material conditions dictated by the nature of the traditional social system of production that produced the corresponding rule concerning the non-liability of the pledgee to account.  To state the rule in modern times as an inflexible one applicable to pledge transactions is to ignore the quantitative and qualitative transformations taking place in the traditional socio-economic structure under the influence of the dominant capitalist ways of production at the urban and city areas of the country.

Nwabueze, for instance, describes the changing material conditions in the traditional economic system as follows:5

"Nowadays not only the ground but also cocoa, rubber or palm tree plantation may be pledged.  The pledging of the latter creates a situation which could hardly have been within the contemplation of the customary rule as to the non-liability of the pledgee to account for the profits from the land.  In applying the rule in such a situation it is necessary to ensure that the profits bear a just relation to what the pledgee could reasonably have charged as interest; otherwise the law might appear to be condoning  unconscionable dealings by a slavish adherence to the customary rule in situations outside its contemplation.  Where a cocoa farm or a concrete rent-bearing house is mortgaged, it often happens that the profits from the plantation or house exceed by far the amount of the loan together with any interest that might have been chargeable upon it. It would seem rather unconscionable that in such a situation the mortgagee should be allowed to keep all that profit and still demand the amount of the original debt in full.  Does justice not require that, if the pledgee is not to account for them, the profit should at least be used to liquidate the debt?"

These new developments and the problems associated with them as described by Nwabueze underline the conflict between the pristine customary law reflecting a lower stage in the traditional society's dialectical evolution and a more complex higher form of its development under the dominant influence of the capitalist system in modern times. The new ideas being generated today, for instance—the idea that the pledgee must be made accountable, at least in some cases, mirror new changes in the economic base of traditional society.  It is within the context of such conflicts that cases such as Dapaah v Poku,6 Addo Kofi v Agbo Kofi,7 Amoo v Adingun,8 Kuahen v Avose9 and Agada Okoiko v Ozo Esedalue10 can be understood.

In the Nigerian case of Kuahen v Avose,11 for example, the plaintiff sought to eject the defendants from the possession of certain palm trees at Gedu in the Badagry area of the colony.  The palm trees, the annual yield of which was estimated at £12, was pledged by the plaintiff's great-grandfather for an alleged sum of 1,360 bags of cowries (the equivalent of £340).  The money was required to meet an annual tribute obligation to the King of Apapa.  The highest annual payable tribute was £9.  But the payment of tribute had ceased altogether following the death of the recipient eight years before the action.  The plaintiff sought to eject the pledgee's successor from the farmland without having paid the loan of £340.

The case raised several problems. The redeemability of the pledged property under pristine customary law is dependent on payment of the loan. Can the plaintiff obtain possession despite this failure to pay the loan?  Under what Kludze calls "old style customary law pledge"12 by which undeveloped land was usually the subject - matter of the pledge, the question would have been answered in the negative without much problem. But the problem posed by the question is made more complicated by the fact that in this case  the pledgor had planted on the land economic trees, a product of his own labour.  The annual tribute was £9, while the annual yield from the palm trees was £12, an annual gain of £3.  The harvesting of the palm fruits went on for 30 years amounting to £360, exceeding the original loan by £20.  The payment of the tribute for which the loan was secured had ceased altogether. These factors introduced new elements not within the contemplation of the old style customary pledge out of which the customary law evolved.

Resolving these issues, Smalman Smith CJ said that whenever palm trees were given in pledge to secure a loan, the amount of produce realised from the trees should be taken into account and some portion of it set aside each year against the original debt until the loan was paid off.  When the loan was liquidated in this way, the pledgor and his successors could resume possession of the farms.  The Chief Justice regarded it as "unjust and inequitable and opposed to natural justice" that the descendants of the pledgee should continue farming the trees and hold on to them until the original loan should be paid. For, under the present transaction, the Chief Justice thought the pledgee might have got back the money advanced many times over.13

The decision in Amoo v Adingun14 presents similar problems.  The plaintiff secured a loan of £35 from the defendant and pledged his shop as security for the loan.  The pledgee took possession of the shop and let it out to a tailor. The pledgor sought to redeem the shop and claimed an account of the rents collected by the defendant since October 1952.  The trial judge acceded to the plaintiff's claim, holding that the latter was entitled to credit from the  defendant's exploitation of the shop and ordered an account against the defendant.

Here again, we have seen, as in the case of Kuahen v Avose15 that the pledgor had expended his labour and developed the land which was the subject-matter of the transaction.  Under an old style pledge, the pledgee developed the land creating wealth thereon as the fruits of his labour.  Under modern type pledges, on the other hand, the development of the land is the fruit of the pledgor's labour from which the pledgee makes profit.16

It becomes self-evident from the facts of the cases that the material conditions at the economic base of traditional societies have so changed that pristine customary law reflecting old style pledges cannot be applied to them. To do so would obstruct the evolution of the customary credit system with the pace of changes in economic circumstances.  Under the modern type pledge, the material conditions exist for the introduction of a system of accounting to which, not only the pledgee, but the pledgor also must be subject.

Today, when a pledgee takes possession of a cocoa, coffee or rubber plantation as security for a loan, he may not be entitled to charge an interest on the loan.  It is therefore arguable that the benefit to be derived from the transaction is the profit to be gained by harvesting the crops. But the yield from the farm could depend on the age of the trees at the time of the transaction, the vagaries of the weather, fluctuations in commodity prices, etc.  All these could affect receipts.  This implies that depending on these factors, the pledgee could make profit which is grossly out of proportion to the amount advanced or may even enter into possession at a loss where for example, after clearing, spraying and fertilising the soil at his expense, prices fall or the harvest is poor.

All these contingencies make the keeping of accounts, that is the recording of receipts and expenses during the period of the pledge, a matter of necessity.  By going through the accounts the interests of the parties could be better balanced.  For this reason, we think that Kludze is right in urging that a distinction must be drawn between the old style customary pledges and self-liquidating types under which economic trees are pledged in modern times.17

Failure to draw the kind of dichotomy reflecting changes in economic circumstances with which the learned author is concerned lies at the root of the difficulties besetting such a decision as Agada Okoiko v Ozo Esedalue,18 for example.  In that case, the plaintiff's grandfather pledged the land in dispute many years ago to the defendant's grandfather to secure a loan of three pieces of cloth at the time of the trial assessed at N30.  The plaintiff sought an order of the court to allow them to redeem the land from the defendants on payment of the N30 in discharge of the pledge debt, even though the defendants had planted rubber trees on the land.

Yet the Nigerian Supreme Court upheld the decision of the lower court which held that the defendants were not entitled to any compensation for any improvements they had made on the land. The Supreme Court reviewed Kuahen v Avose19 and Amoo v Adigun20 and on the basis of such reviews concluded that21:

"No longer, it would seem, can the pledgee in possession take all the benefits from his commercial exploitation of the land and still get back his original capital, much less can he claim the land with economic crops like cocoa or rubber or from his having carried out improvements on the pledged premises."

It is clear from the Nigerian Supreme Court's pronouncements that it confused the new rules which have developed in response to changed economic circumstances, for application to new style self-liquidating pledges, with the rules applicable to an old style pledge involving expenditure of labour by the pledgee.  The circumstances of this case are completely different from those of Kuahen v Avose, Amoo v Adigun and Dapaah v Poku.  In this case, the pledgee and his successors have clearly developed the land by planting economic trees on it for many years.  In addition to this, their ancestor had parted with three pieces of cloth now  assessed at N30.  This assessment did not include any interest on the loan.  For the courts to adopt such a techno-juristic approach to a case such as the instant one involving economic and social life is to say the least unfortunate. For the Nigerian Supreme Court to say that the pledgee's successors should not be compensated for their labour without going into the accounts is unjust and absurd.

That transaction was apparently concluded at a time when the material conditions for accountability did not exist. The transaction was, however, subsisting when economic circumstances have so changed that the need for accounting is now being felt.  It is in such cases that interstitial legislation in the adjudicative processes of the courts becomes important.  Because the Nigerian Supreme Court adopted a mechanical approach to the resolution of the issues, it was unable to recognise the relationship between practical economic activity at the traditional society's economic base and decisions like Amoo v Adigun, Dapaah v Poku and Kuahen v Avose,22 etc.

In a case like this, departure from pristine customary law and going into the accounts would have revealed the cost to the pledgee of the development on the land and receipts over the years.  On the basis of the information obtained from the accounts, the Supreme Court would have been in a better position to determine as between the parties, the one who was making undue profit at the expense of the other or taking undue advantage of the other. The application of the customary law rule as if it is not a purposive ordering of human affairs but a juristic mensuration can produce absurd results. Professor Ezejiofor, for instance, in a well-reasoned article in which he discussed these problems, seems to adopt the view that the customary law rule as to the pledgee's non-accountability need not be disturbed by the courts. Making a case for retaining the rule, the learned author writes23:

"The pledgee is not entitled to resist redemption but must accept back his money and yield up possession or else be ejected.  It does not lie in his mouth to argue that he has not obtained sufficient return on his capital. Indeed, he may not as a matter of fact, have got any return at all.  If therefore, where there is either no return or no sufficient return on the pledgee's capital, the pledgor is nevertheless compelled to repay the amount which represents a reasonable return, then, what equity has the pledgor to demand an account when he thinks that more than reasonable profits have been made from the land?  Certainly none.  Pledge of land under native law and custom is a delicate transaction which the courts should handle rather cautiously unless it is intended to destroy it completely."

These arguments are sound and convincing particularly in respect of old style pledges under which virgin land was usually the subject-matter of the pledge. Concerning the modern self-liquidating type pledge, however, they do not take into account the dynamics of constant motion and development in society in obedience to universal objective laws in correspondence to which social norms and jural postulates must change.

The introduction of a system of accounting in relation to pledge transactions in response to the need which has obviously been occasioned by new economic circumstances would not operate in the interest of the pledgor alone.  Accounting in this regard will operate both as a sword and a shield. When such issues are called into question, going into the accounts would provide a sound basis for determining where the loss, if any, must fall.  The question would then cease to be determined by strict adherence to the pristine customary law rule that the pledgee is not liable to account.  Instead, on the basis of the account, it can be determined whether or not the pledgor would be making an undue or unreasonable profit at the expense of the pledgee if he is allowed to regain possession without compensating the latter.

The same criterion would be used to determine whether or not the pledgee has also either made unreasonable profit or has taken such an undue advantage of the pledgor that it would be unconscionable to allow him to retain all profits made during the period when he was in possession. Failure to accept and introduce the principle of accountability to meet the needs of the changing society would, rather than preserve the customary institution of the pledge, lead it to its destruction altogether.

(ii) The Family Head

All students of the customary law are familiar with the rule that the head of family is not liable to render accounts to members of the family; a rule supposedly established in Abude v Onano24 and which is claimed to be based on the interpretation of Sarbah's statement of the law in 1894.25 The rule is perhaps the most criticised aspect of the customary law.  Few modern commentators on the subject have anything to commend it.26 But when the opportunity was presented in Hansen v Ankrah27 for the Supreme Court to jettison the discredited rule, it was frustrated by the "timorous souls" at the Supreme Court trembling before the ghosts of the great judges alleged to have laid down the precedent.28

One of the key issues for determination was whether or not the trial judge was right in his view that Abude v Onano29 was of questionable authority. This entailed the examination of the scope and the extent of the family head's immunity to account under the customary law.  Unhappily, the Supreme Court, by a majority of three to two, held, inter alia, that the customary rule that a head of family was not accountable in court to the suit of a member of the family, whether a principal member or otherwise, while he still held the status of a head was reasonable and justifiable because the head should have the capacity to handle the routine orders and disorders of the family.  The family head was, however, accountable to the family at a family meeting for his stewardship.  Consequently, a member of family who considered that an incumbent head of family was incompetent, irresponsible or frivolous in the handling of family matters or was dissipating family funds could ventilate his grievance against the head at a family meeting.  But when he was found to be guilty of serious charges such as disobedience to the advice of the elders, dissipation or misappropriation of family funds, then he might be removed.

In coming to that conclusion, the majority of the Supreme Court rejected the more progressive dissenting views canvassed by Apaloo CJ and Taylor JSC. In the opinion of the former, the Supreme Court ought to decline clearly to follow the Pappoe v Kweku30 and Abude v Onano line of cases31 because the customary rule laid down by them was productive of injustice and provided a potent shield for the breach of fiduciary duties.  His view is that the law must adapt itself to changing social conditions and those precedents are inapplicable to modern conditions. To do otherwise would be an exhibition of judicial inertia wholly indefensible in our day and age.  The latter supported him ably in these views.  According to Taylor's dissenting opinion, the operation of such a rule would be incompatible with national aspirations inherent in the need to infuse probity and accountability into the public and private lives of the populace as well as the necessity to discourage corrupt practices and the abuse of power, whether discretionary or mandatory, as would seem to be prescribed by sections 1(1)(c) and 61 of the Provisional National Defence Council (Establishment) Proclamation (Supplementary and Consequential Provisions) Law, 1982 (PNDCL 42).

Unlike the minority views, the majority opinions overlooked the fundamental question of the relationship between the dynamic customary practices of the people and the development of the law, ie their interconnection, interdependence and their overlapping effect on the form, essence and content of the law.  Some of our judges fail to take account of the fact that in modern times the concept of accounting in the formal sense imports highly organised and complex social system of production such as that towards which our society is making progress.  Keeping records of account is known to the socio-economic formations that preceded the capitalist system.  But it was during the industrial revolution in Europe that accounting as an economic and legal concept attained maturity reflecting the higher and more complex nature of capitalist production and services than those that preceded it.  A system of accounting is a necessary condition for businessmen whose existence depends on the maximization of profit.  The record of receipts and expenditure is essential for determining business prospects and attainable profit margins.  It also helps in projecting into the future decisions regarding future investment policy objectives.  There can be no doubt that the social system of production in the closing years of the twentieth century is capable of accommodating that kind of development. That is why social practice in this modern day and age requires accountability.

African traditional societies of Sarbah's day were, however, organised on the family or kinship principles of sharing on community membership basis. Communal ownership of the land, the basic means of production, was central to such an economic system. Under such an economic system, one cannot expect the evolution of a formal accounting system as an economic category. Moreover, the written word is a necessary condition for that level of sophistication in recording accounts. Yet in 1894 when Sarbah published his research findings, writing could hardly have been known in Fanti rural communities that were organised on the family principles of sharing under a dominant communally organised social system of production.

Therefore when Sarbah's work is relied upon today as the basis for the proposition that under the indigenous land the head of the family cannot be sued for an account, that could only be a statement reflecting social norms and practice in Sarbah's time. The application of such a rule in our society today would constitute a recipe for open conflict. The rationale for the Supreme Court's decision would seem to be the need to protect the dignity and the integrity of the family head as the "fountain of honour." Yet a suit against the family head for an account brings into question his integrity and honour. That is why the court, as the independent arbiter, would seem to be the best institution apt to restore that dignity when a clean account is rendered through its adjudicative process. The survival of the discreditable rule for nearly a century, and the attitude of the Supreme Court in Hansen v Ankrah32 show that the bench had failed in its exposition, interpretation and application of the customary law to respond positively to the tremendous metamorphosis that our society has undergone over the last hundred years under the influence of western education and its capitalist ordering of society. The legislature on the other hand would seem to have in the last twenty years or so responded positively to these developments. Consistent with the need to infuse accountability into public life as prescribed under the Provisional National Defence Council (Establishment) Proclamation (Supplementary and Consequential Provisions) Law, 1982,33 the legislature had to intervene so as to restore the balance between social practice and the law. The result is the enactment of the Head of Family (Accountability) Law, 1985.34 It should be pointed out that it is not the law which introduced formal accounting as an economic and legal concept. It is the result of social practice of producing the material means of sustenance. The law's response to this is to recognise that fact and to adapt itself to it.

Concerning customary pledges, the Mortgages (Amendment) Decree, 197935 has virtually caused the demise of the customary pledge and hence, the principles governing it.  It provides that every customary loan transaction in respect of which any farmland is given as security for a loan shall be made in accordance with the Mortgages Decree, 1972.36 There is no doubt that the majority of cases dealing with the customary pledge concern farmlands. AFRCD 37 now converts all such transactions which would otherwise have been customary pledges into mortgages.37 This explicit conversion of all such transactions into mortgages has thus virtually destroyed whatever might have been left of the indigenous credit institution in Ghana.

It should be pointed out, however, that the effect of the Decree on the customary pledge is the true reflection of the changing historically conditioned socio-economic setting in the countryside.  Thus even if statutes had not intervened, the customary pledge would through the process of socio-economic transformation have become extinct, and replaced by modern capitalist credit institutions. Through the operation of money-commodity relations, communal lands have been transformed into commodities.  Legal estates are recognised as existing in them independently. As titles in themselves - choses in action, they can be used as collateral for the acquisition of liquid capital in the modern money market.

Similarly, it should be pointed out that it is not the law which introduced formal accounting as an economic and legal concept.  It came about as a result of social practice of producing the material means of sustenance. The law's response to this phenomenon is to recognise that fact and adapt itself to it. When we turn to mineral exploitation within the traditional scheme and at the capitalist sector, we find these examples of conflicts between pristine customary law reflecting its economic base and the received common law mirroring capitalist economic structures with varying consequences for the law and development.  This should be our next subject of discussion.

THE INDIGENOUS MINING INDUSTRY

Until the Trans-Saharan trade was interrupted by the collapse of the ancient empires of Ghana, Mali and Songhai in the thirteenth, fourteenth and the sixteenth centuries respectively, gold, cola and slaves had been sent across the Sahara to the Middle and Far East from very early times.38 Moreover, the arrival in the Gold Coast of the Portuguese in about the middle of the fifteenth century marked the beginning and the revival of the export of gold and forest products from the Gold Coast, this time no longer across the Sahara, but across the Atlantic to Europe and the Americas. Yet it was not until the late nineteenth century that European firms began the development of the mining industry in the Gold Coast on modern lines.

For this reason, the supply of gold and other goods to support the trade was controlled by "native" intermediaries and some Europeans who later settled near the forts along the coast. For reasons of insecurity, inland penetration was kept to the barest minimum. The frequent internal wars, sometimes encouraged by the slave trade, prevented Europeans from taking the risk of investing in mineral exploitation. This meant that the production of gold and other precious minerals to support such a large volume of trade for so long a period was controlled by the indigenous people until the last decade of the nineteenth century.39

Indigenous mining technology had developed to appreciable levels.  Gold was mined and refined into fine qualities. Division of labour in the precious metal industry was underscored by the development of the jewellery industry in which blacksmiths featured prominently in the manufacture of gold ornaments.  Thus when the Portuguese arrived in 1471, they met the chief of Cape Coast wearing a golden necklace.40 The high level of skills developed in the mining industry was underscored by the large volume of supplies. In 1554 for example, Captain Thomas Winham, the first Englishman to visit the Gold Coast returned to England with 150 lbs of gold dust.41 This could be seen as the beginning of more than four centuries of trade in gold and other products between the United Kingdom and the Gold Coast. We thus find the editor of the African Times reporting in 1880 that the mines of the Gold Coast had been the source of supply of gold for many years and were among the most productive in the world.42 The assessment of their richness was based on the fact that "gold of the value of several millions of pounds sterling" exported from the ports of the Gold Coast derived solely from the surface washing of the metal or the crude crushing of gold-bearing quartz carried on by the "hands of the Natives."43 It should be pointed out, however, that indigenous skills in the field went far beyond the mere surface washing of the metal.  As the paper itself had reported, "lacking in scientific skills and mechanical appliances, the natives had been unable to work below 70 or 80 feet from that depth."

This implied that they worked up to at least 80 feet of depths.  Indeed the indigenous miners had, before the European firms took over from them and reduced them into labourers, identified the most suitable logs for props in the mines. Due to its strength and durability, the species "Kaku" or "fillacopsis kaku"  was identified and successfully used for that purpose. Thus when European miners arrived, they adopted similar technology. If such levels of skills and technology were attained before the direct involvement of foreign miners, what destroyed the indigenous mining industry? That is the subject to which we should turn our attention.

The Received English Law

The virtual demise of the local mining industry by the end of the nineteenth century was due not only to capitalist competition with which the indigenous miners were unable to cope, but largely as a result of the introduction of English law and statutes, the doctrinal, theoretical and philosophical underpinning of which were inconsistent with social practice founded on traditional socio-economic structures. Such laws became clogs and fetters on further development of the industry.

Since the transition from the medieval to modern times in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, status had gradually ceased to be the basis for organising economic and social life in England. In its place developed the law of contract. The theoretical, philosophical, and the ideological foundations of that law were the laissez-faire ideas anchored on capitalist competition. It was assumed under such ideology that parties to a transaction stood on an equal bargaining position; and, as it was the outward manifestation of the parties' intent and not their actual intent that led to the conclusion of a valid contract, either party must look out for himself. The maxim is caveat emptor. This meant that if a party bought an ornament made of brass in the mistaken belief that it was made of gold while it was not, unless the other party had innocently or fraudulently misrepresented its quality, the purchaser was bound by the transaction. That was the form, essence and content of the law reflecting the relatively primitive capitalist economic structures of eighteenth century England which was transplanted into our societies which were predominantly organised on kinship basis of co-operation and sharing of resources in common.

Under the "received" English law, concepts of individual ownership of land had already natured. It had developed to levels where the land was conceived of in terms of the physically identifiable piece of land and in which exist intangible estates, independently of the physical object itself. That is to say that lesser successive estates could be carved out of it and transferred as properties in themselves; ie choses in action. Thus the land was a marketable commodity subject to unrestricted absolute alienation.  Under the indigenous schema, however, most communities regarded lands over which they had control as a community asset and resource over which no one individual should lay absolute claim.

It was believed that the ancestors would not permit any dealing with the property that would have the effect of depriving the future generations yet unborn of the use thereof.  Compliance with such egalitarian and equitable principles underlying the distribution of community resources were enforced by the fear of the ancestor's wrath which might be visited on "offenders." But continued compliance depended on the fear of the ancestors.  However, the impact of European economic, social, religious and legal ideas seriously undermined ancestor worship and such fears of the ancestors.  Such ideas produced in the few educated local elite of lawyers and merchants who had embraced Anglo-American conceptions of tenure, as well as conveyancing forms, certain attitudes that were in conflict with the traditional norms.

Therefore, when European firms began the acquisition of mining and timber concessions in the last two decades of the nineteenth century, such local elite became the intermediaries between the concessionaires and those responsible for land administration in the traditional system. Trained in English property and conveyancing forms, their attitude was to employ Anglo-American terminology in the drawing up and preparation of deeds of conveyance. English law and conveyancing forms introduced the conception of individual ownership as a necessary step leading to the alienation and commercialisation of communal lands.  It was however soon to be realised that failure to dichotomise between traditional schemes of interest in land and English notions of tenure was a serious error.44 The use of English terms which the illiterate chiefs could not understand to describe interests affecting lands under the customary law began to cause confusion and led to serious disputes concerning the mining rights of the local people. The problems which arose were succinctly described in 1893 as follows45:

"The would be concessionaire, who is usually a native with a certain amount of education goes to the chief of the locality where mahogany or gold is to be found and, in exchange for a few pounds induces him to affix his mark to a formidable-looking document which conveys to the concessionaire complete rights over a stretch of country, varying in extent from a few hundred yards square . . . to 30-40 miles in dimensions."

The extent of improvident alienation of stool lands could be seen from the West African Lands Committee Report.46  In the course of its inquiries, it sought to ascertain the character and the extent of land alienation under the Concession Ordinance.  On the basis of the information obtained from instruments affecting lands supplied to it; the total area of lands alienated within the relevant period was found to be 36,000 square miles.  Yet the estimated area of the colony at that time was 24,335 square miles. That meant the purported alienated area exceeded the actual estimated area of the colony by 12,136 square miles.  That was certainly chaotic!  The extensive, vague and imprecise nature of the rights acquired through the use of English conventional conveyancing forms is illustrated by the case of Captain J A Duncan.  In a lease for a term of 100 years, he acquired exclusive rights to47:

". . . all the lands of the lessors and all the adjacent and intermediate villages and rivers . . . and all the adjacent streams and mountains together with the rights of the lessors in all rivers and water courses in the said lands with all metal ores and minerals, precious stones, rocks or other mineral substances or material within or over and under the said land with full power, to licence and authority to sink, make and use any shaft ... and other mining works now existing or hereinafter to be constructed . . ."

(The emphasis is mine.)

The effect on the mining rights of the indigenous people of the introduction of English contract law and the unquestioning reception of Anglo-American conceptions of tenure and the increasing use of their forms and techniques of conveyancing in land transactions in the traditional schema was amply demonstrated by a Tarkwa case.  In 1888, the chief, Kofi Chay, having granted a large tract of his stool land to Essamang Mining Co, allowed his subjects to continue digging gold on the land, apparently in genuine belief that his subjects still had the right to do so.  The company would most probably have succeeded at the regular courts in the ensuing action for trespass and damages in the sum of £3,000, but for the intervention of the Governor. The latter intervened on the grounds that the terms on which the company had obtained the grant was so one-sided and unreasonable that the legal action was entirely unjustifiable.48

Such occasional official interventions did nothing to reverse the trend towards the usurpation of the indigenous people's rights to work and mine precious metals. Lacking the capital and modern technical know-how, the local middle class were incapable of competing with their "foreign friends."  To avoid being reduced into the ranks of the growing army of the proletariat, they joined the race for concession acquisition and speculation.49 As David Kible has pointed out, enterprising groups of natives and individuals continued in the fields of speculation and of concession acquisition and mining activities to such an extent that their speculative activities became a "source of annoyance to the tidy mind of officialdom during the 1890s."50

Indeed, most of the transactions might be perceived by the indigenous people as unjust. However, there is a direct link between the caveat emptor maxim under which parties are supposed to look up for themselves and the doctrine of consideration. Under the common law, consideration need not be adequate. Hence, if from the outward manifestation of the parties' intent an agreement could be seen to have been reached, the parties are held to be  bound no matter how inadequate the consideration might seem to the  observer.  A classic operation of this rule was exemplified in the negotiation of the Adansi and Bekwai concessions which became known as the Ashanti Goldfields Corporation.  The consideration for the 100 square miles of territory was £12 and a bottle of rum.51 Commenting on the disastrous consequences for the indigenous people of the introduction of English law into the traditional system in which land rights were community based, Illegbune writes52:

"The disadvantage in this kind of language in contracts with the generally illiterate chiefs seems obvious.  It was beyond their comprehension and in most cases they signed without legal advice.  The chiefs' misfortune of illiteracy thus became a contractual fortune for the concessionaire.  A chief who granted a concession over a stretch of land subsequently discovered that he ran the risk of action for breach of contract if he attempted to exercise customary rights supposed by him to have been reserved for him."

It should be noted that in most of the transactions, the grantor-chiefs were referred to as owners.  Surely, under the customary schema, the chiefs were mere administrators and not owners with exclusive dispositive rights.  It is the stool, the symbolic representative of the social group or community, in which ownership is vested.  Therefore the operative effect of the nemo dat quod non habet rule would have been sufficient for declaring all such  transactions invalid.  It was also obvious that the chiefs acting alone had no legal capacity to dispose of interests in the lands.  The position was made worse by the fact that the local middle class and elite, particularly the lawyers who could have accorded protection to the chiefs and the community, could not do so because, as intermediaries and speculators in concessions, their  interests became linked with those of their foreign "friends." We thus find (apart from the infamous Ashanti Goldfields Corporation Concession), Prince Albert Owusu Ansa acquiring vast areas of land for speculative purposes. On 22 June 1894, for example, he purported to have obtained from chief Quacoe Attah 200 square miles of land for a term of 99 years for £100.53

The problem faced by the indigenous mining industry as a result of the unquestioning reception of Anglo-American notions of tenure and the increasing use of their forms and techniques of conveyancing in the traditional system led to the usurpation and privatisation of communal lands over which the indigenous people had for centuries, exercised their rights of mineral exploitation without any hindrance.  This created the conditions which became potentially dangerous to the peace.

Legislative Response

When the true social and economic implications of granting away all the known mining areas over which the local inhabitants were accustomed to mining became known, there were serious reactions from the communities. The peaceful atmosphere under which the mines could be profitably exploited was threatened.  The colonial administration whose responsibility it was to protect the economic interest of the capitalists introduced a legislative measure in 1900.54 In a memorandum to a proposed Concession Ordinance under which a concession court would be established through which the government would exercise supervisory control over land transactions between European firms and chiefs, the Colonial Secretary, Chamberlain, stated the objectives of the measure. It was an attempt to confirm the rights of the chiefs and elders to exercise their traditional land control functions properly and to ensure that in the course of transactions with strangers, advantage was not taken of their ignorance to defraud them. But he cautioned that the "native owner" should be "left free, as now, to make his own bargain if he wishes to sell to a European . . ." The policy foundations of the Concession Ordinance therefore was to:

"Watch the land operations of the natives and to insist upon the observance of certain regulations with respect to them as may appear to be called for in the interests of Natives on the one hand and capitalists on the other."55

The main duties of the concession court, among others, were to declare concessions valid if they satisfied certain prescribed conditions.  They should not be declared valid unless:

(i) The court was satisfied that the proper persons were parties thereof and they may reasonably be presumed that they understood the nature and terms thereof;

(ii)   it was not obtained by fraud or other improper means;

(iii)  it was made with adequate or valuable consideration;

(iv) the court was satisfied that the customary rights of natives were reasonably protected in respect of shifting cultivation, collection of firewood, hunting and snaring game, etc.56

It is interesting to note that indigenous mining rights were not included for protection by the concession court. And significantly, concessions acquired before 10 October 1895 were excluded from the ambit of the concession court.

Yet by that time most of the known rich mining areas had already been acquired.  Hence such famous acquisitions as the 100 square miles of Ashanti Goldfields Corporation acquired for £12 and a bottle of rum were protected. Sections 47 and 20 sought to place limitations on the size of concessions that could be acquired by a person or corporation. However, the provisions were so frequently breached that it might be concluded that they were rendered ineffective against the evils at which they were directed.  One often found in the Gazette notices of certificates of validity in respect of concessions in clear violation of section 20 restricting the area of concessions to five square miles, not to mention the paltry sums at which they were acquired.57

The position of the indigenous miners was aggravated by the introduction of the Gold Mining Products Protection Ordinance, apparently intended to protect concessionaires, rights to gold mining products.58 No person shall without the consent of the Governor in writing deal in or purchase gold mining products. The business of goldsmiths was not to be carried on unless a licence had been obtained.59 The administrative procedures required for such licences proved difficult for the illiterate community.  While the rights of the people to mine gold was usurped, the jewellery and ornaments industry which had developed as an offshoot of mining had been completely undermined.  Having been reduced to labourers, those who could not be employed by the concession owners went underground and began illegal mining which became known as "galamsay" until the present day.

Effect of "Received" Law on Local Mining

The consequences for the indigenous mining industry as stands out clearly from the discussion demonstrate the adverse effect of the introduction of laws that do not mirror social practice.  The development of the mining industry in Ghana on capitalist lines in the last two decades of the nineteenth century could be seen as the extension to the country of the commercial and industrial activities to which the industrial revolution had given impetus in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries in Great Britain.  Its success in the Gold Coast depended on the availability of capital, the technical know-how, cheap labour and the stronger bargaining position in which the concessionaires stood vis-a-vis the illiterate chiefs.  The level of success achieved did not depend on the introduction of the "received" law per se.  That had already evolved and developed from socio-economic practice in   England.  It had proved successful in promoting that kind of development.  Hence its introduction to promote the type of economic activity here.

However, such laws were in conflict with practice in the social system of production within the indigenous schema and thus became impediments to further development and therefore completely killed the industry and its supporting skills.  The unquestioning reception of Anglo-American conceptions of tenure and its supporting conveyancing forms and contract doctrine could not promote development in systems that were egalitarian with community-based rights of enjoying things in common.  The laissez-faire ideology was alien to them.

Not surprisingly, the independent governments of Ghana had done nothing to rectify the situation until the enactment of the Minerals and Mining Law of 198660 and the Small-Scale Gold Mining Law, 1989.61 The former confers power on the Minister for Lands and Minerals Resources to designate, by notice in the Gazette, areas for small-scale mineral operations.62 The latter also permits gold mining operations such as the type organised on "galamsay" basis provided a licence has been obtained from the Secretary for Lands.63

Until the enactment of these statutes, the law restricted the indigenous people's rights either to mine, sell or purchase mining products.64 It was therefore unsafe to sell their products in the authorised market.  Their business had to be conducted clandestinely, most of the time, with the connivance of security agents such as the police, security services of the mining firms themselves and other officials. Despite the dangers to which they were exposed, alien smugglers of the precious metals and security agents were the main beneficiaries of the gold won.

It is common knowledge that the "galamsay" miners sometimes pay agreed sums of monthly allowances to security personnel assigned the responsibility of checking "galamsay." At any time they fail to honour their "obligation", raid operations are carried against them and their winnings either seized or shared disproportionately in favour of their captors.65 Under such conditions, it does not matter whether or not it is the security agencies or the "galamsay" miners that retain the gold.  It finds its way outside the country. Indeed, there are acknowledged dealers in mining products in each mining area in the country.  They buy the minerals from the diggers and smuggle them out to be sold in Ivory Coast, Niger, Togo, Nigeria and elsewhere in the world.

This is a tragedy for the local miners and the country.  As the business is illegally carried on, winnings can only be sold in the black market.  More often than not, the buyers of the gold and other precious metals won by the "galamsay" miners are the well-established illegal dealers and smugglers. They are also the financiers of the former. Therefore in that clandestine, restricted market, the dealers are in the position to dictate the price as the miners stand in a weak bargaining position.  Under such conditions, they become subjects of unconscionable exploitation by their financiers despite the hazardous nature of the industry. The real tragedy of this phenomenon lies in the fact that the dealers who are often aliens or having alien connections, smuggle their haul to be sold in their countries of origin or elsewhere in the world.  There, like the "galamsay" miners, the gold is sold below the prevailing world market prices.  In the process, therefore, Ghanaian labour is cheaply employed for the exploitation and carrying away of a large proportion of the country's mineral resources to enrich other countries over the last hundred years or so.

The enactment of the Minerals and Mining Law, 198966; the Small-Scale Gold Mining Law, 198967; and the Mercury Law, 198968 could be seen as an attempt to reverse this trend.  Section 1 (i) of PNDCL 218 therefore provides:

"1.(1) Notwithstanding any law to the contrary, no person shall engage in or undertake any small-scale gold mining operation unless there is in existence in respect of such mining operation a licence granted by the Secretary for Lands and Natural Resources or by an officer authorised in that behalf by the Secretary."

It is clear from this provision that provided the Secretary exercises his power under section 77 of PNDCL 153 by designating areas for small-scale mining, all that is required to legalise a local miner's mining activities is to acquire a mining licence. It should be pointed out, however, that the material conditions which have frustrated the development of the indigenous mining industry over the years remain unchanged. It should be noted that by 1895, almost all the fertile mining areas from which the indigenous people were operating had been acquired by European firms and their local educated intermediaries. What remained had been subsequently acquired in the manner referred to earlier.  The present legislative measures could thus be seen as closing the stable door after the horse has escaped.  It is therefore not surprising that currently, the "galamsay" mining is largely carried on in already acquired areas despite the great risks involved in the exercise. What is more, in present day conditions, small-scale mining is an expensive business. Apart from the time-consuming and expensive procedures to be gone through before the acquisition of a licence, the prospective miner is expected to build platforms, sink new wells to the depths limited by the law and provide other tools and infrastructure.

These are material conditions which the law per se cannot improve. It requires practical policies and measures directed at removing the constraints. It should be pointed out that the present enactments both in their form and content are substantially the same as their predecessors. Indeed, there had never been any law expressly proscribing mining by the indigenous people. It was the conditions that had to be fulfilled, particularly formal acquisition of concessions, which implied the formation of companies and capitalist exploitation of the mines on modern industrial lines, that had made it impossible for the indigenous people to compete with the big-time miners.

However, one could see two significant differences under the present legislative measures. First, it has paved the way for legalising a socio-economic practice that was before their enactment illegal. Secondly, it enables those who manage to obtain the prescribed licences to sell their products in the open market. It also affords those who are unable to obtain it the opportunity to sell to those acquiring such licences, what has been mined under "galamsay" conditions. This is a very important development.

There are signs that the measures have helped to promote the sale of these minerals to the State and has to a large extent minimised smuggling of the products. The following figures obtained within two and a half years of the coming into force of the relevant enactments are instructive69:

The above figures for two and a half years of the coming into force of the statutes bring home vividly the colossal amount of resources that had hitherto been illegally carried away from the country for many decades as a result of failure to adopt practical measures to enact laws that reflect social practice within the socio-economic structures of the particular communities concerned.

The success of the present measures would depend largely, not so much on the law as such but on devising socio-economic, political and legal measures to remove the practical constraints hindering the operations of small-scale miners.  At present there are over three thousand applications for mining licences before the Minerals Commission.70 However, since the implementation of the measures, only 302 have so far been granted.  Out of this number of licensees, less than 40 per cent are operating as small-scale miners.  This is apparently due to the financial and other constraints indicated earlier. The problems are underscored, as pointed out already, by the fact that not many fertile lands for mining have been left after large acquisitions in the past.  That accounts for the Secretary's inability to designate so far, any area or areas as a small-scale mining land.71

The very limited success achieved should not overshadow the fact that "galamsay" continues in earnest. Until practical policies and measures going beyond mere legislative enactments are taken and promptly too, the picture would remain the way it has been recently presented by a commentator. He wrote:

"For many years `galamsay' has flourished in Tarkwa, Aboso and Prestea areas even in the face of police arrests and convictions of offenders.  In Akwatia and Kade areas in the Eastern Region, some `galamsay' miners continue to brave the odds.  Sometimes, `galamsay' miners have to climb and descend abandoned trenches and shafts in search of gold. Falling stones and loose heaps of soil sometimes claim lives. Some fall and sustain injuries. But the daring ones have not given up."72

The above statement sums up the problems that continue to persist about three years after the introduction of the measures. It should be borne in mind that the chaotic state in which the indigenous industry can be seen towards the closing years of the twentieth century could be attributed largely to the reception and application of laws that did not and still do not promote social practice in Ghanaian communities in which the industry is undertaken. Being largely in conflict with the traditional exploitative norms, the restrictions they imposed on the development of the time-honoured industry frustrated its orderly development into a modern one.

CONCLUSION

An individual's perception of the law's response to socio-economic change in a given society would ultimately depend on that individual's conception of the function of law in society. The question is a philosophical one. His perception would be influenced by the way he sees the system of views on the world as a whole and how he generally interprets processes occurring within it.  Here, the fundamental question of philosophy, ie the relationship between consciousness and matter becomes relevant.

If the individual recognises the primacy of matter over consciousness, he would be a materialist.  If on the other hand he takes the position that consciousness is primary in relation to matter, he would be counted among idealist philosophers. We are of the opinion that metaphysical and a period conceptions of the function of law in society often lead to the erroneous conclusion that the law per se as a superstructural phenomenon can be effectively employed as a tool for socio-economic development.

This is usually neither so in theory nor in practice.  Both law and economic structure do interact, overlap, affect and influence each other.  But the former which reflects the latter, must necessarily be secondary and subordinate to that which determines its form, essence and content. Therefore in order for the law to be effective and functional, it must respond to social and economic needs by following social practice. It must be tailored to follow and supplement socio-economic practice in playing a promotional role.

When the law follows social practice as does equity the law, it helps to remove the impediments or the fetters that hinder economic development. Where the former is made to play a primary role in directing economic activity such as, for example, enacting price control laws, seeking to prevent market forces to determine prices, it does not work. The secondary but promotional role played by the law in developing accountability under the customary law and the developments in the indigenous mining industry, as discussed above, provide good examples. We conclude by pointing out that the introduction of forex bureaux which had legalised irregular foreign currency transactions since 1990 could provide the basis for an interesting study in Ghana.
