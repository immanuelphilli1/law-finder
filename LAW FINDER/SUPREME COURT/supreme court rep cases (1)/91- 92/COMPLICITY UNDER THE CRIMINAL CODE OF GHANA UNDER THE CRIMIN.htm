COMPLICITY UNDER THE CRIMINAL CODE OF GHANA UNDER THE CRIMINAL CODE OF GHANA [1991-92] VOL. XVIII RGL 193—219

Mensa-Bonsu, H.J.A.N.

 

*

INTRODUCTION

Complicity describes the mental state of being united with others in the commission of a crime coupled with acts or omissions that further the commission of that crime. The persons so united, are known as "confederates" or "accomplices"1 because they, inter alia, share the state of mind of the principal offender and, consequently, the criminal liability.  Owing, however, to the fact that they are not the principal actors during the commission of the crime, their liability is subject to special rules at common law. These common law rules, with some modifications, have been codified under the Ghana Criminal Code, 1960 (Act 29).2 The law on complicity under Act 29 appears to be almost taken for granted as there is very little to be found on the subject in the law reports.  Even in cases where there are issues of complicity, these issues are rarely discussed as everybody takes it for granted that who an accomplice is in any given situation, is too obvious to require discussion.  Hence the virile debates on the subject in other jurisdictions have generated little or no interest in ours.3

The apparent lack of interest in the subject may have been caused by a combination of two related factors, namely either an improper appreciation of the scope of the law on complicity leading to a very low rate of prosecution of accomplices; or, a dearth of appeals from lower court decisions on the subject. Even in those cases in which issues of complicity are raised, the discussion, is often confined to a specific context; the usual context being one in which the merits of the appeal of a principal offender depended upon whether or not sufficient complicity of the witness was established to warrant the evidentiary requirement of corroboration. For these reasons, there has been very little discussion of the substance of the provisions of Act. 29 on the subject. It would seem, however, that there are questions from our own jurisdiction which are as interesting as the issues being discussed elsewhere.

The purpose of this article is to analyse the Ghanaian law on the subject with a view to increasing the appreciation of members of the profession of the material aspects of the offence. This would in turn increase the use of the provisions of Act 29 on complicity in combating criminal activity. The discussion of the subject involves principally two sections of Act 29, namely sections 20 and 21. Section 20 deals with the modes of participation in crime.  These are described by the single word "Abetment." The provision also deals with the manner in which an abettor may be tried and punished, as well as jurisdictional issues.  Section 21 deals with situations where a different crime from that which was abetted takes place, as well as other issues of joint enterprise. The discussion of these provisions is carried out by seeking assistance from other common law jurisdictions that have attempted to deal with problems relating to accessorial liability.

Abetment: Some Preliminary Matters

Section 20 of Act 29 reads as follows:

"20. (1) Every person who, directly or indirectly, instigates, commands, counsels, procures, solicits, or in any manner purposely aids, facilitates, encourages, or promotes, whether by his act or presence or otherwise, and every person who does any act for the purpose of aiding, facilitating, encouraging, or promoting the commission of a crime by any other person, whether known or unknown, certain or uncertain, is guilty of abetting that crime, and of abetting the other person in respect of that crime.

(2) Every person who abets a crime shall, if the crime is actually committed in pursuance or during the continuance of the abetment, be deemed guilty of that crime.

(3) Every person who abets a crime shall, if the crime is not actually committed, be punishable as follows, that is to say—

(a) where the crime abetted was punishable by death the abettor shall be liable to imprisonment for life; and

(b) in any other case the abettor shall be punishable in the same manner as if the crime had been actually committed in pursuance of the abetment."

Section 20, which defines the circumstances in which accessorial liability would arise, is essentially a re-enactment of the old Criminal Code of 1892 which was in operation in the Gold Coast Colony.4 Those provisions were in turn a consolidation of various provisions in the English criminal statutes.5 The long list of activities, therefore, stem from this effort at systemising the various provisions into one whole.

Under Act 29, there is also the elimination of the traditional distinctions between principals in the second degree and other accessories. These distinctions have caused countless problems in the English courts with the result that time and again, the courts have had to explain which words refer to which category of accomplice. In Ferguson v Weaving,6 Lord Goddard CJ explained that:

"It is well known that the words ‘aid and abet’ are apt to describe the action of a person who is present at the time of the commission of an offence and takes some part therein.  He is then described as an ‘aider and abettor’. The words ‘counsel and procure’ are appropriate to a person who, though not present at the commission of the offence, is an accessory before the fact."

Under section 20, there is no mention of the various kinds of accomplices, thereby avoiding the twists and turns in which we must indulge in order to distinguish between accessories before the fact and principals in the second degree. Both categories are described as "abettors" and treated alike.

The definition also does away with post-commission accessorial liability, ie accessory after the fact.7  Since the assistance that such a person renders occurs after the commission of the offence anyway, the person contributes nothing towards making the commission of the offence a reality. There is consequently a sense in which imposing liability for the offence is unfair. Thus, under Act 29, the act complained of must precede or be contemporaneous with the commission of the crime. This point was taken up and discussed in Commissioner of Police v Sarpey and Nyamekye.8  The case involved a policeman who had allowed a lorry with stolen goods to pass by him unchecked. His conviction for the abetment of stealing was quashed because the court was of the opinion that at the time the appellant became involved, the stealing had already taken place.  The act of the appellant had in no way enabled the stealing to occur.  The court therefore came to the conclusion that under Act 29, all acts which constitute abetment must be "contemporaneous in place, time and circumstance with the commission of the offence . . . [or] must precede [it]."9

The abolition of accessoryship after the fact as a specific form of offence has caused many professionals in the field to complain that it hampers the effective administration of the criminal justice system.10 Although this complaint may have some merit, it does appear to somewhat exaggerate the importance of provisions on accessories after the fact.  After all, there are various provisions which cover the various activities that may be engaged in to assist a fugitive from justice11; to hide stolen booty12; and to destroy incriminating evidence in homicide cases.13  It would seem therefore that although there is no provision dealing with accessories after the fact, every felony has its own particular kinds of activity that could be considered as accessorial offences.

The long list of activities all describe a range of acts that could precede the commission of a crime.  Owing to the fact that such acts of assistance encourage the commission of crime, these acts are also made criminal. The criminalisation of those acts is justifiable also on the grounds that the morally weak must be protected from the activities of those who would use them for their own ends.  Many are those who have committed crimes they would not otherwise have engaged in, but for others who dared them to show their mettle or live forever under the tag of "coward". Such persons, who are the more dangerous to society because their activities are the more insidious in nature, would otherwise escape censure.

A cursory look at the provision would also indicate that the meanings of some of those words used in the catalogue of words overlap. There is therefore the temptation to treat them as meaning one and the same thing. Such temptation must be resisted. The reasoning here is that if Parliament in its wisdom thought that all those words must be included in the list of activities, then there is a presumption that they are intended to mean different things. The wisdom of this reasoning has also been supported by Lord Widgery CJ, a judge of the English Court of Appeal. As he observed14:

"We [must] approach the section on the basis also that if four words are employed here, ‘aid, abet, counsel or procure', the probability is that there is a difference between each of those four words and the other three, because, if there were no such difference, then Parliament would be wasting time in using four words where two or three would do."

Having thus approved the wisdom of this attitude, the discussion must proceed upon the assumption that the Ghanaian Parliament also intended to punish the various kinds of activity that could conceivably come within the meaning of the provision.

Another issue that must be got out of the way before the discussion can proceed is that despite the use of one word "Abetment" - to describe the various activities, the burden of the prosecution has not been made any lighter than it would otherwise have been.  Parliament may have thought that providing one word for all the activities would obviate the necessity for the prosecution to split hairs on how to categorise a particular course of conduct. If this was the motive, they failed because the need to prove what kind of conduct each activity amounts to is still paramount, for it would be inconceivable for the prosecution to successfully prove complicity without specifying the precise activity engaged in by the person accused of abetment.

With these preliminary points addressed, it would be necessary to begin the discussion by examining each of the words used in Act 29, in order to determine what Parliament meant to include in this category of offence. An examination of the words reveals that some words indicate some physical activity on the part of the abettor during the commission of the offence, whilst with others, it is more a question of "remote control." Thus all the activities which would have rendered a person a principal in the second degree or an accessory before the fact in the old mode of categorisation, come under the omnibus term of "Abetment."

It is consequently appropriate to begin this undertaking with the word “abet” itself. This word has all the connotations of "assistance" without conveying any particular idea of what activity it covers. As Glanville Williams observes, it is "... a useful technical term for helping in the commission of crime . . . but it is poor for instructing juries, or for a code."15 If judges in England who are native speakers of the English language have problems explaining this word to other native speakers, then the nature of the problem in this country can be better appreciated. In essence, a Ghanaian judge who has to explain this word to a Ghanaian jury has two main problems: first, effectively explaining what kinds of assistance are covered by the term; and secondly, doing so in terms which would not indicate that a verdict is being suggested to the jury.

Giving meaning to the word is rendered even more complicated by the fact that there is no consensus even in the community of legal scholars as to what the word means.  Explaining the various words used in the English Offences Against the Person Act, 186116 in terms of "consensus" and "causation", the learned authors of an authoritative text on criminal law state that "abetment" implies consensus with the principal offender, but no causation.17 In other words, the liability of the secondary party lies in showing the criminal mental state and communication with the principal rather than in acts that produce the event. A Canadian judge also gave a somewhat different definition to the word. He held that "To abet is to be personally or constructively present at the commission of an offence and to assist in the criminal act.18 Thus he implies both consensus and causation, for the abettor in the second definition does more than simply share the criminal mental element that the offender has. Neither of these meanings seems adequate because they do not appear to cover the situation in which the abettor is actually the instigator or the mastermind behind the crime. What seems to be made clear from the authorities as well as these divergent views is that "abet" is a word that conveys only vague notions of complicity, but no clear images.  Indeed, it has so often been used in conjunction with "aid" that it has tended to take its colour from that word. This is obviously because "aid" is so much easier to explain or even to conceptualise. The vagueness of the word, then, must account for the long list of words giving form and substance to it. The catalogue of words reads as follows: "instigate"; "command"; "counsel"; "procure"; "solicit"; "aid"; "facilitate"; "encourage"; "promote".

Our discussion of the several activities that constitute abetment would begin with "instigate" only by reason of it being the first on the list.  At common law, the preference seems to be for its synonym, "incite." However, in Act 29, "incite" is not used at all in the substantive provision although it is used in the illustration provided to explain the application of the provision.19 It would be interesting to discover why it was that Parliament preferred "instigate" to "incite" and yet could not abandon the latter term completely. Whatever the reason, the inconsistency of expression is regrettable. For the present purposes, however, this leads to the conclusion that Parliament intended the words to be used interchangeably. For this reason, the authorities cited are all on "incite", but are equally applicable to "instigate."

To "instigate" means to goad into action, or "to bring about", as by incitement or urging . . . on to some drastic and unadvisable action"20 Incitement is thus a central idea in "instigate." What then does "incite" mean? To incite means to seek to influence another's mind in the direction of the commission of a crime.  Therefore an inciter21:

". . . is one who reaches and seeks to influence the mind of another to the commission of a crime. The machinations of criminal ingenuity being legion, the approach to the other's mind may take various forms such as suggestion, proposal, request, exhortation, gesture, argument, persuasion, inducement, goading or arousal of cupidity."

This is a very wide definition indeed. It appears to cover all forms of communication that can be indulged in, in order to influence the mind of another in the direction of crime. As the authorities point out, nothing need be done by the person who is the focus of the activity. Indeed, it has been held that an agreement to indemnify a person against any fine that may be incurred in the future on account of certain acts, may be an act of incitement.22 The reasoning of the court here may be due to the fact that a person with the assurance of such an indemnity may read into it an invitation to go ahead and commit the offence.  Thus there need be no evidence that the person who was the object of the incitement was influenced one way or the other. It is immaterial if the words had no effect at all.23 For all material purposes, it is the act of the one who incites, rather than the effect of the incitement on the victim, which is the cause of the censure. This offence is therefore intended to rope in all manner of activity that could be the genesis of criminal activity.

Next is the word "command." To "command" is more easily understood in terms of ordering an activity to be carried out. In that situation, the offender is seen more as a tool than as a willing participant. This is because implicit in the relationship of the commander and commanded, is the presumption of the superior status of the former as against the latter, as well as an obligation to obey. It could hardly be said of co-equals that one had "commanded" the other. Hence, it would seem that a person who commands bears more moral responsibility than the one who is commanded.24 In order for a person to be guilty of "commanding", it would seem that nothing less than a legal duty to obey would be enough to establish that relationship. This would therefore exclude the "Your-wish-is-my-command" kind of situation which is a situation of voluntary submission to the authority of another, rather than a legal obligation to submit. Since the subject matter is the commission of an offence, it would seem that there is no room for a "moral obligation to obey." A "command" must therefore proceed from one with lawful authority otherwise the act becomes one of "instigation."

"Counsel" immediately brings to mind the giving of advice.  This is the ordinary meaning of the word which is considered the one most reasonable to adopt.25 Any other construction would be artificial. Thus advice given by another which enables an offence to be committed would qualify as abetment even if it was the offender who sought out the advisor.  It is however not enough to establish criminal liability to merely prove that the advice was given. Unlike "instigation", an offence of a particular nature must have been committed in pursuance of that advice. As Parker LJ explains, "[T]he actual offence must have been committed . . . by the person counselled."26  To this end, there clearly must first be contact between the parties, and then such a connection between the counselling and the commission of the offence that it would be impossible to deny that the offence was committed as a result of the advice given.  This means that a person who gives advice to the "whole world", as in a television broadcast, would not be guilty of counselling, but of incitement.

In providing illustrations of the kinds of activities covered under the provision, the drafters did not consider it necessary to provide one for "counsel."  Relying on the authorities, therefore, it would seem that there are situations which our preferred construction does not provide for. For instance, if a different crime suggests itself to the offender as a result of the counsel given, it would seem somewhat ludicrous to deny criminal liability merely because the crime committed was different from that which was counselled. At the most, this should be the case only when the offence is so radically different that there could be no relationship at all between them. Thus a person who counsels rape by demonstrating how tactically easy it would be, ought not to escape blame if those tactics were adopted not to rape, but to rob.  There is a lot to be said for the law's attitude in this regard because a different attitude might result in otherwise innocent people being linked to crimes by force of circumstances. However, in situations where the person could not escape moral opprobrium, it seems somewhat irrational to adopt so technical an interpretation that the purpose of the law would be defeated.

To "procure" the commission of an offence is also a specie of abetment. What acts then would come under its umbrella? In general, one who "procures" the commission of an offence is, in a causal sense, the one who brings about the mental state which results in the commission of the offence. In the words of one commentator on the English Draft Code:  "What the procurer brings about or causes is a state of mind on the part of the perpetrator, leading him to perpetrate the crime.:27 The point was demonstrated in the English courts when the Attorney-General referred a case to the Court of Appeal for a ruling because a person prosecuted as an accessory to the offence of driving under the influence of alcohol, had been acquitted. The facts leading to that reference was that the accused person had introduced alcohol into the drink of a person he knew would shortly be driving. When the person was arrested and charged, it transpired that it was the act of the accused that had caused the commission of the offence. In consequence, the accused was also charged with abetment as having procured the commission of the offence, but was acquitted. Lord Widgery CJ, ruling on the issue, held that the act did indeed amount to procuring. He explained that: "[t]o procure means to produce by endeavour. You procure a thing by setting out to see that it happens and taking appropriate steps to produce that happening."28 Thus one who gives alcoholic drinks to another whom he knows would be driving, abets the offence of driving under the influence of alcohol.

It has been suggested that “procure” is “unhelpful and misleading”29 and that a better word would be “influence.” With the greatest respect, it would seem that “influence” does not go far enough in conveying the full meaning of "procure." The very essence of the word "procure" indicates that a crime must have been committed. It is thus a step further than "incite" for it is impossible to show that an offence was "procured" unless there is evidence that there was a personality in the background pulling the strings.  The possibility of the activity and the offence must go beyond the merely coincidental to evidence that the activity was "produce[d] by endeavour." Thus, "influence" does not convey the same sense of purpose that "procure" conveys and, in the opinion of the present writer, is therefore an inappropriate synonym. It seems that Lord Widgery's meaning is more in accord with the sense in which the word is used under our Act 29.

This does not mean that an act that may be lawfully engaged in would amount to "procuring", even though it could be foreseen that the probable result of that activity would be to tempt another to commit an offence. This point is made in Republic v Tema District Magistrate Grade I; Ex parte Akotiah.30  In that case, the applicants had been ordered to appear before a magistrate to show cause why they should not be made to execute a bond to keep the peace. The problems leading to this order by the magistrate had arisen because some persons had, in exercise of their lawful right, attempted to instal a chief at Ada. Their political opponents, who claimed to have already installed one, brought an application before the district magistrate to stop the installation of the new chief. They alleged that the acts of the respondents would induce a breach of the peace by their own supporters. The magistrate upheld this claim, and ordered those persons to appear before him and to show cause why the application should not be granted. It was in consequence of this order that the instant application for certiorari and prohibition was brought to quash the order of the magistrate and to prohibit a further hearing of the case.

In granting the application, the court held that one could not be guilty of procuring the commission of an offence by embarking upon a course of conduct which was lawful, even though it would induce a breach of the peace. The court applied the English case of Beatty v Gillbanks,31 a case which involved the Salvation Army and its opponents, the Skeleton Army, in almost similar circumstances as in the instant case.  From these authorities, the inevitable conclusion is that the act which "procures" the event should be one that is not pursued in exercise of one's legal rights.

"Solicit" is given several meanings in the dictionary.  Some of these are: "To appeal for something", to ask for the purpose of receiving"; "to ask for something earnestly"; "to endeavour to obtain by pleading."32  All these definitions show one person operating upon another by wearing down that other's moral objections, to obtain an advantage. In the case law, it has also been explained to cover the tempting of a person, or the luring of another into evil.33  Therefore any act done would constitute the offence of soliciting, if a person’s participation in a crime is assured by another's importuning.

The common element in all these activities captured in the list of words is the point in time at which they take place.  They all indicate circumstances which would make one liable as an accessory before the fact at common law because they would all have preceded the commission of the offence.  It is also accurate to state that those engaging in these activities would in most cases not be present during the commission of the main crime - their activities would just make the commission of the crime possible.

The next batch of words are of a different genre. These words are descriptive of certain activities. They also indicate that one way or the other, the activities would have been done not only with the purpose of assisting another to commit a crime, but would also have taken place during the commission of the main crime.  The acts described are thus in the nature of physical assistance during the commission of an offence, and would at common law make a person liable as a principal in the second degree.  Owing to the fact that these acts are ordinarily harmless unless done with that aim of rendering assistance to a person engaged in committing a crime, Act 29 uses "purposely” to describe their actions. This insistence on "purpose" is quite logical since a person being unaware that the commission of a crime is in progress, cannot be accused of rendering assistance that would further that effort.  The use of that word therefore rules out accidental presence, or acts done without the requisite mens rea. The distinction is made clearer by considering the following example. Two people are committing a burglary whilst a third uses his body to block the public view by standing in the doorway.  In almost similar circumstances, two people are committing a burglary when it begins to rain.  The rain causes a passer-by without an umbrella to take refuge under the eaves, in the company of two people the passer-by took to be workmen.  In so doing, the passer-by's body blocks the view of the public and so the burglary is successfully committed and the burglars escape.  In the second instance, it is quite clear that although the result of the act was the same as the first, the presence of the third person was entirely accidental.  The same cannot be said for the person in the first instance.  The difference is that it was the mens rea that defined the purpose and which in turn dictated the course of conduct appropriate for the occasion.

The discussion of the words which describe these activities begins with the word "aid".  This word has always been used in conjunction with the word "abet."  The difficulty here, then, is not only to separate them as they are found in the authorities, but to subsume "aid" under the general meaning of "abet."  Although the word "aid" is often used to denote the general idea of assistance, it would be useful to examine the precise meaning of the word at this juncture.

To "aid" is to "help" another to commit an offence. This was the conclusion that the Privy Council came to in Thambiah v R.34 This was an appeal by a person who had been convicted of "aiding" the offence of forgery of cheques. The appellant had opened a bank account under a false name and description. The account was later used to dispose of forged cheques.  In dismissing the appeal, the court held that the conviction had been proper because it was the act of the appellant that set the stage for the commission of the crime. One can see clearly here that the assistance rendered to the forgers made it possible for the offence to be committed.  The dismissal of the appeal meant that one could "aid" without being physically present. However, since the provision of the bank account was the necessary tool for the completion of the offence, there is a sense in which the accused could be deemed to have been present. From this, one can conclude that although a principal in the second degree is supposed to be present during the commission of the offence, "presence" can be either physical or constructive, depending upon the nature of the offence.

The decision in National Coal Board v Gamble35 lends credence to this view. In that case, a truck driver had been arrested for driving an overloaded vehicle. The load complained of was coal, which had been bought from the premises of the appellants. The official in charge of the weighbridge had noticed the overload and drawn the driver's attention to it. The driver had then said that he would take the risk, and the official who could have prevented him from leaving the premises, handed him the ticket which enabled him to do so. On the facts, it was clear that the servant of the appellants enabled the driver to commit the offence.  The appellants were therefore charged with aiding and abetting the offence.  The majority of the court was of the opinion that the appeal should fail because "[a] person who supplies the instrument for a crime or anything essential to its commission aids in the commission of it ... "36  It is thus clear that a person who opens a bank account to enable dishonest activities to be carried on, would definitely have supplied the instrument essential to the commission of a range of offences. Since the opening of the account had been done "purposely", the Code would also support this view that such a person had aided the commission of the offence.

To "facilitate" also means to render assistance which makes the commission of the offence easier for the perpetrator.  Thus, it has been held that a person who buys uncustomed goods facilitates the commission of fraud on the revenue-collection agencies.37 It also seems to be the case that a person who "facilitates" must do more than remain passive.  Therefore a private individual who sees a crime in progress and does not report it, cannot be said to have facilitated the commission although the fact of not reporting may have enabled the crime to be completed. This was the holding in Apetorgbor v The Republic38 in which case a woman who was a witness to a murder but did not report it was held not to be an accomplice.

However, the same could not be said for a law-enforcement official who winks at a situation and thereby enables a crime to be committed.  The kind of conduct in contemplation must have gone beyond mere indolence or negligence to a purposeful act of omission. For instance, a policeman in uniform who, by his presence at the scene of a crime, wards off intruders by giving the impression that it is a police-supervised operation, would definitely have facilitated the commission of the crime. It is equally true that a person whose official duty it is to detect or prevent the commission of crimes may "facilitate" the commission of a crime by doing nothing to prevent it when the facts come to his attention.

Encouraging or offering encouragement to one committing an offence is a form of abetment. The issue of what constitutes an "encouragement" has given the courts much to chew on just as the issues relating to the liability of curious spectators and bystanders. From the various discussions of these issues in the authorities, it is possible to distil some principles, namely that a person may encourage by his/her silence or non-interference, or intentionally by expressions, actions and other gestures intended to signify approval.39 However a merely curious spectator cannot be guilty of "encouraging" without the intention to do so even if his or her presence has the effect of doing so.40 It is thus no offence of abetment to stand by as a mere passive spectator of a crime, even of murder.41

The issue of when a person's presence during the commission of a crime constitutes "encouragement" appears tricky, judging by the fact that "encouragement" need not be in words.  Difficulties often arise where the court is required to assess conduct which is capable of being construed one way or the other. What is the position of one who accompanies a friend to negotiate for a criminal abortion although she utters no word of encouragement nor takes any part in the negotiation? Although in Obeng v The Republic42 the Court of Appeal by a majority decision of Sowah and Lassey JJA (as they then were) held that a woman who accompanied a friend twice to negotiate for an abortion was not an accomplice to the crime of illegal abortion, the law seems to suggest otherwise.  It is true there was no evidence of any activity beyond offering her companionship to her friend as the deal was being negotiated.  All the same, it is curious that the majority refused to accept the fact of her complicity when the evidence showed that she at all material times knew what her friend desired to do and raised no objection thereto. The dissenting judgment of Azu Crabbe JA (as he then was) puts more emphasis on the fact of her voluntary presence to hold that she was an accomplice.  Clearly, the majority was wrong on this point because the fact of her silence, coupled with her non-accidental presence on two occasions, rendered her act an "encouragement."

This case may be contrasted with the earlier one of State v Boateng43 in which a prosecution witness was held to be an accomplice because he had been tainted in the transaction which led to the prosecution.  The facts were that a policeman nicknamed "Tiger" was on patrol duty one morning when he heard: "Tiger is coming!" as he approached one house.  He saw the second accused person dash into a particular room. His suspicions aroused, he also went into that room.  There, he found the first and second accused persons, as well as two other persons who were subsequently made the second and fourth prosecution witnesses respectively.  All of them were looking very frightened.  This further deepened his suspicion and he therefore proceeded to search the room.  This search produced some police uniforms and other accoutrements as well as a photographic plate for a currency note.  Consequent upon these discoveries, all the occupants of the room were placed under arrest.  Further police investigations resulted in two of the persons being released and turned into prosecution witnesses.  During the trial, the judge failed to warn the assessors that those witnesses were accomplices whose evidence required corroboration.  The accused persons were subsequently convicted and they brought the instant appeal.  The appeal was allowed because the circumstances were such that the witnesses were particepts criminis.  Crabbe JSC was not in doubt that the parties were accomplices even though they were merely present in that room.  Their guilty looks suggested that they knew what was afoot and it was therefore unsafe to convict on their evidence. This it is submitted, is a correct interpretation of the law because an accomplice need not do anything in particular. It is enough if the person's presence is not a coincidence, and he or she shares the mens rea of the principal offender. The existence of this authority should therefore have inferenced the decision in the Obeng case.

There are other occasions on which mere presence could render one an accomplice. Where the presence acts as an encouragement, that could be evidence of complicity if the presence is non-accidental because44:

"The fact that a person was voluntarily and purposely present witnessing the commission of a crime, and offered no opposition to it, though he might reasonably be expected to prevent and had the power so to do, or at least to express his dissent, might under some circumstances, afford cogent evidence upon which a jury would be justified in finding that he wilfully encouraged and so aided and abetted."

Willful encouragement then seems to be the butt of this provision.  The person must know of the circumstances constituting an offence and must have intended to encourage the commission of the offence by being present.  Thus, in a typical scene of a street fight in Accra, it is not only those who shout yii le45 or clap their hands in encouragement to the fighters, who are abettors. Those who, supporting neither side, give vent to their excitement by uttering the now famous Ga expression46 to cheer on the fighters would be equally guilty. However, those persons who are drawn to the spot as a result of the commotion and remain to see the end of the business would be regarded merely as curious onlookers. The fact of their presence per se would not be considered an "encouragement." This is so although the authorities hold that the non-accidental presence of a person at an illegal prize-fight constitutes "encouragement." The two situations are distinguished by the fact that it is of the essence of a prize-fight that there be spectators, whilst the presence of spectators during an ordinary fight is merely incidental to the affray.47

A person who "promotes" the commission of an offence is also an abettor under Act 29. To "promote" goes beyond mere assistance to activities that actually ensure that the commission of the offence would be undertaken.  The person who engages in the traditional act of throwing a challenge to an opponent—by holding out his hand and asking the contestants to signify their acceptance of the challenge48 —would be the promoter of an unlawful fight. Such a person would not merely have encouraged the fight, but would have ensured that neither contestant would pull out of the contest without cost to that contestant’s reputation. The promoter's activity would thus have made the likelihood of the commission of the offence more certain.

Apart from these activities discussed above, any act that is done "for the purpose of aiding, facilitating, encouraging, or promoting the commission of a crime . . ."49 is an act of abetment.  This means that no act done with the requisite mens rea would be regarded as harmless.  The act itself may have been useless for the purpose for which it was undertaken, but this would not be a material fact if it was motivated by a desire to render assistance to one engaged in committing a crime.  For instance, a person who designs a system of alarm which is intended to give a warning upon the approach of the police, would still be caught by this part of the provision if it turns out to be completely useless for that purpose. A further point that raises an interesting issue is whether the act done could be a perfectly lawful one, though done for the purpose of assisting a criminal venture. For example, is the act of one who prepares a delicious meal for the purpose of ensuring that the offenders would be well-fed before undertaking the crime, liable under this provision?  There certainly is no reason why such a person should not be.

This provision also ensures that acts done with a view to affecting the conduct of the public at large would be punishable if there was evidence that that act was likely to assist the commission of crimes.  It is true that in most situations of complicity, the accomplice and the offender are acquainted and have some kind of link with each other. This is however the classic mode by which people get implicated in the crimes that others commit.  However as the world gets more complex and mass communication technologies improve, other problems are being created that defy the categories that were served by the traditional theories.  If for instance a person wrote a computer programme which could assist persons who are so inclined to successfully defraud the government, would there be any qualitative difference between that activity and the act of one who uses inter-personal communication methods to teach identical skills?  If so, why is this so?  If not, why should the latter's act be an act of abetment, whilst the former escapes such liability? This was the issue at stake in the English case of Attorney-General v Able50. The accused person in that case produced a booklet known as "A Guide to Self-Deliverance" which described various ways by which suicide might be successfully committed. The booklet was supplied to a strictly-controlled group within the membership of the Euthanasia Society of which the accused persons were also members. The Attorney General of England sought a declaration, inter alia, that the supply of the booklet to persons likely to include those contemplating suicide, constituted an offence or an attempt to aid, abet, counsel and procure suicide.  The English court was of the opinion that the act should attract criminal liability because such a supplier assumes a risk in circulating such information to various persons, some of whom might be contemplating suicide. Therefore the fact that the state of mind of the actual recipient is unknown, is immaterial to the liability of the one who gives currency to those ideas. The court's position was strengthened by the fact that some suicides, with whom copies of the booklet had been found, were outside the controlled group to which the booklet had been supplied. The reasoning is supported by Act 29 because an act of this nature is capable of rendering assistance to persons contemplating that crime, and it is also undertaken for that purpose. It is therefore submitted that in such situations, the emphasis should shift from a physical connection between the parties as actors, to that of the acts that produce the crime. This shift would cover these new situations which pose an even greater danger to the welfare of the society by reason of the sheer number of possible recruits to a criminal cause that the method of mass communication adopted, would offer. If a person cannot vouch for the state of mind of all the recipients of information that is dangerous in the hands of the uninitiated, that person must bear the risk.

Consequences of Abetment Of Abetment

Under Act 29, a person who abets a particular crime is deemed guilty of that crime.  It is likely that even for an offence such as manslaughter, the courts in Ghana would be minded to follow the reasoning in the English case of R v Creamer.51  The issue in that case was whether a person could be guilty as an accessory before the fact to an unintentional act such as manslaughter. The court came to the conclusion that in the appropriate situation, this would be possible.  The basis of the reasoning was that if a person counselled or procured the doing of a dangerous act that could result in death, then the person was liable if death did result. This is a defensible position to take since such a person should be presumed to intend the natural and probable consequences of his act. If the natural and probable consequences of an instigation to riot is death, there is no reason why the instigator should escape liability.

Punishment for the act of an abettor is the same as that for the principal actor, except where the punishment is a death sentence.  This is the case probably because they share the mens rea, and differ only in terms of the actus reus.  One fact that must be noted is that on occasion, it is the abettor who even sows the idea of the crime, or converts a remote possibility into one with better prospects; or makes the thought of the crime less repulsive; or offers such assistance that problems likely to impede the successful commission of the offence are eliminated. All these are acts that, in no small measure, give prospective offenders the confidence and support needed to undertake the venture.  If all these activities make the possibility of the commission of the offence more certain, then there is good reason for punishing the principal offender and the abettor to the same extent.

Act 29 stops short of the death penalty for the abettor even though the offence abetted is a capital one.  There is a lot to be said for this attitude since the hand that effects the deed carries a bigger burden of guilt, than the one who assisted it. However, since various kinds of activity constitute abetment, the rule can sometimes work unfairness, depending upon the activity in issue.  Where the abettor is the instigator of the offence, punishing that person less than the principal offender may be unfair when consideration is given to the fact that the abettor was the originator of the idea in the first place.  R v Udo52 illustrates the point being made. In that case, the accused persons had committed murder upon the exhortation of the chief of their village.  The chief, one of whose subjects had been murdered by people from a neighbouring village, instructed all his male subjects that they were to bring the head of any of those villagers for the funeral rites of their deceased kinsman.  The accused persons, believing it to be their duty to ensure a proper funeral for their murdered colleague, committed the murder.  In a situation such as this, is the chief any less guilty of murder than the people who acted upon those instructions?  Why then should those people be sentenced to death whilst the chief gets off with a lighter penalty?  Much as it is appreciated that there are degrees of guilt, a blanket rule for all abettors unduly favours those who use others as tools for their own misdeeds, at the expense of those who contribute in minor ways to the commission of the offence.

A case of abetment stands on its own, and therefore the trial of an abettor need not be with the person abetted or even with the co-abettor since each person is answerable for the part that he or she played in the commission of the offence. Owing to the operation of this rule, it is theoretically possible to be convicted of the abetment of an offence when the principal offender is acquitted in a subsequent prosecution. This is in spite of the general rule that one cannot be guilty of abetting an act which is not an offence.53  The above scenario could occur in cases where a material part of the offence, eg mens rea, is found to be non-existent.  In such a situation, the offence would not have been proved and therefore no one ought to be guilty as an accessory; but if the alleged abettors have already been convicted of the offence, the incongruous situation described would have occurred.54

For the same reason, an abettor may have the benefit of a defence to which none of the other participants may be entitled.  Thus an abettor who was provoked by the act of another may, unlike the principal offender, have the benefit of the defence of provocation.55 In other words, an abettor could be guilty of a lesser charge than the principal offender.  The question of whether the converse is also true has not been without its difficulties.  The issue has by and large been settled so that now under English law, the same rule makes it possible for an abettor to be guilty of a greater charge than the principal offender.  Earlier authorities had held that this could not be possible since it was the principal offence that determined the liability attached to the adjunct ones.  Thus in R v Richards56 it was held that one who procured the commission of an offence could not be guilty of a graver offence than the principal offender.  This case has been expressly overruled in R v Howe.57 Under Act 29, it is unlikely that the Richards position would be sustainable since provision is made for the punishment of offences that are not committed according to the intent of the abettor.  Consequently, where the offence abetted is more serious than what is committed, the abettor could be punished for this and could thus be guilty of a more serious offence than the principal.

Penalties

An abettor is punishable in the same manner as the perpetrator of the offence whether or not the offence abetted is successfully committed.58 If the particular offence abetted is not committed, then the abettor is punishable for the offence abetted and not what was eventually committed. However, where the offence is punishable by death, the abettor would be liable to the lesser penalty of life-imprisonment.59

Defences

The next set of issues relate to the effect that an abettor's repentance might have on his or her criminal liability as well as the other defences that are open to a person accused of abetment. The general rule is that an abettor who has a change of heart after the commission of the offence has begun, is not thereby released from criminal liability even if the change has been communicated to the offender.  As expressed by Ollennu JSC in Boahene v The State60:

"The defence of countermand may only avail with respect to an offence to be committed in [the] future, e.g. where a person having given instructions to, say, his servant, to go and beat up someone, and before the servant could put the directions into effect, recalls his directions."

This therefore implies that timing is of the essence because once the commission of the offence has begun, it is impossible to effectively dissociate one's self from the event.

It is also important to point out that it is not every act of abetment that relates to future conduct.  Some acts of abetment, such as incitement (instigation), are analogous to the offence of conspiracy and constitute a complete offence the moment they are undertaken.  In such situations, the statement of Ollennu JSC in respect of conspiracy61 is equally relevant because though a conspirator "may recant and withdraw from the preparation of the substantive offence ... he cannot undo the act of his previous agreement."62  Such a change of heart would have come too late to materially affect the outcome of the venture.

Apart from these situations in which the defence would not be available, there are other circumstances which would enable an abettor to escape criminal liability.  An example is where the accused person shows that there was such an effective countermand of instructions given that the principal was not left in any doubt that the erstwhile abettor had no further interest in the commission of the offence.  In the view of Lawrence J, to escape being held guilty as an accessory before the fact, the accused must establish that he countermanded or determined the agreement in such clear terms that the  subsequent conduct could not be laid at his door.63 Even in this situation, it is clear that the kind of act in question would be material in showing that there was an effective countermand.  Where the accused is alleged to have counselled the commission of an offence, it is likely that the principal would be difficult to dissuade once the criminal possibilities have been exposed to him or her.  Ought a person who gave currency to those ideas be able to set up such a defence when the offence is committed?  Since the human mind cannot be switched on and off like a tape, it may be impossible to effectively "countermand" some activities especially the psychological kind once they are undertaken.

Complicity and Common Purpose: Section 21

In dealing with issues of complicity, one usually comes up against problems that arise when a multiplicity of persons undertake a joint activity, or act together under a loosely-defined arrangement. These include problems regarding excessive zeal on the part of some of the actors, impulsive acts that others do under pressure, and last minute changes in plans which are not communicated to the other parties.  Some of these raise issues of varying degrees of difficulty.  For instance, is a person who abets a common assault guilty if the principal uses an offensive weapon during the commission of the offence?  What is the liability of a person who abets a riot if the other parties commit murder during the riot?  Whilst the former problem relates to the degree of gravity of offence, the latter is a completely different kind of offence, but springing out of the acts of abetment done.

Section 21 of Act 29 makes provision for when any of the situations identified above occurs. The provision reads64:

"21. (1) Where a person abets a particular crime, or abets a crime against or in respect of a particular person or thing, and the person abetted actually commits a different crime, or commits the crime against or in respect of a different person or thing, or in a manner different from that which was intended by the abettor, the following provisions shall have effect—

(a) if it appears that the crime actually committed was not a probable consequence of the endeavour to commit, nor was substantially the same as the crime which the abettor intended to abet, nor was within the scope of the abetment, the abettor shall be punishable for his abetment of the crime which he intended to abet . . ."

The provision thus acknowledges that an abettor's plan could go awry in one of several ways:

(1) The crime is committed but against a different person.

(2) The crime is committed in a different manner from that which was agreed upon.

(3) A completely different crime altogether is committed.

(4) A different crime is committed against the person who was the subject of the plot.

(5) A completely different crime is committed and against a completely different person.

(6) The principal exceeds the scope of what was planned.

(7) The original plan was not criminal, but it enabled one of the parties to exploit the plan for a criminal purpose.

When these things occur, a person may or may not be liable depending upon the relationship between what was planned and the act that resulted from the plan. The various circumstances and the effect on the liability are discussed below.

The Crime is Committed but against a Different Person

Where the crime is committed but against a different person, there would be no liability if it could be proved that the person against whom the offence was committed was completely outside the contemplation of the abettor.  If the offence was one of murder, it would be subject to the general rules of intent.65  Therefore a person who abetted a riot in circumstances that showed that the natural and probable consequences of the riot would be murder, could not escape liability merely by pointing to the fact that the offence under contemplation was unlawful rioting and not murder.66  The original act of rioting being unlawful, the consequences-predictable and unpredictable - would be blameable on the abettor.  In the same vein, if the wrong person were killed, there would still be liability if the evidence showed a plan to cause death.  The issue of whose death had been planned, would be of little relevance.67

The Crime is Committed or Attempted in a Different Manner

In this particular situation, it is the result of the "endeavour to commit" the crime that would be the focus of attention.  Thus if the crime that was committed happens to be a lesser offence than what was planned, the abettor would be guilty of the original offence. It would therefore be possible for the abettor to be liable for a graver offence than the principal. This is the situation represented by R v Richards.68  It is the commission of the offence that is material. Therefore the manner of commission would not be relevant unless it was so different from the one in the parties' contemplation that it cannot be attributed to the influence of the abettor.  If for instance a person supplies a gun for a murder, it would not be material if the gun was used to bash the deceased on the head instead of being fired. The important issue would be the supply of the murder weapon.  There would however be a material difference if the death was caused by poisoning and not by shooting.

A Different Crime is Committed

In a situation such as this, the abettor would be liable under the provisions of Act 29 which prescribe penalties for when an offence has been abetted, but not committed. Even this would depend on the act of the abettor. If it was an act of counselling, then the law would not permit liability because liability arises only when the offence is committed.  However, in most other cases, the act of the abettor would amount to incitement which in turn is punishable whether or not anyone acts pursuant to the incitement. This is not the case where the acts done could support a range of crimes, though done for a particular purpose. In that case, the crime committed would not be considered to be different from the one in the contemplation of the abettor.  The issue came up in Director of Public Prosecutions for Northern Ireland v Maxwell69 in which the appellant was convicted of the abetment of offences relating to the use of explosives.  The facts of the case were that the appellant had been a member of an illegal organisation in Northern Ireland which had been responsible for some murders and bombings.  He received orders one day from his superiors to lead another car to a pub in a certain area.  He complied with the instruction although he did not know exactly what was to be done there. Unknown to him, the vehicle contained explosives that were to be used to bomb the pub. When the attempted bombing failed, he was arrested and charged and convicted as an accomplice.  He appealed, contending that he had no way of knowing that there was a bomb in the car.  Dismissing the appeal, the court held that an abettor need not know the actual crime intended.  It is enough if the activity he engages in can only lead to the commission of a crime.  Lord Scarman expressed a view supported by the Code in the following words70:

"[A]n accessory who leaves it to his principal to choose is liable, provided always the choice is made from the range of offences from which the accessory contemplates the choice will be made."

Therefore any offence that the principal chooses to commit within the range of possible offences is not considered "different."

A Different Crime is Committed against the Object of the Abetment

This would manifest itself in two ways: either a completely different crime takes place, or one of the parties goes beyond what was planned to commit a different kind of offence.  In both situations, Act 29 would require evidence that what was done was not probable consequence of the endeavour. This, as the cases show, is not easy because the court is apt to be averse to drawing strict lines between what was intended and what occurred. Three cases illustrate the problem: the Canadian case of Puffer v R71; the English case of R v Smith (Wesley)72 and the Privy Council case of Chang Wing-Siu v R.73

In the Puffer case, the facts were as follows: three persons planned to rob the deceased who had invited one of them into his room for homosexual activity.  One of them put a pillow on the face of the deceased who was in consequence, suffocated to death. All three were convicted of the offence.  Two of them appealed against their convictions, contending that what happened was different from what they planned to do.  The court dismissed the appeals, holding that the parties, not having strictly defined what they were going to do, were all guilty of what their colleague had done during the execution of the plan.  Freedman CJ expressed his opinionin the following words74:

"In any event, the present case is not one in which expressly or tacitly the accused had agreed upon or defined just how far they would go or just what they would do.  The enterprise was just described as "to roll a fag".  I find it completely unrealistic to say that blows to the face or body, the pinioning of hands, the tying of feet could be regarded as within the plan, but the use of the pillow as the gag must be looked upon as falling outside the plan. Distinctions of this kind could lead to a finding that a blow above the belt was within the scheme to rob, while the blow below the belt was not.  The violence involved in the carrying out of the robbery ought not later to be measured or tested by the Marquis of Queensberry rules or anything of that nature!"

In the case of Wesley Smith, the appellant and his friends went to a pub to drink. An argument developed there which involved the friends of the appellant.  The appellant and one of his friends left the room, but when the friend declared his intention to "tear the place down", the appellant proceeded to help to gather bricks and to hurl them through the glass into the bar-room.  In the meantime, those within the pub continued the fight till one of them stabbed the barman with a knife which he carried. The appellant was subsequently convicted of manslaughter because he knew about the knife which his friend carried.  His appeal was dismissed because the judge was of the opinion that it was entirely within the contemplation of the appellant that in the disturbance they were creating, anybody who tried to stop them would be attacked.  That being the case, his knowledge that one of them had a knife made it obvious that the knife would be used in any such attack.  Therefore if the knife had been used with deadly results, the appellant could not escape liability. It is clear that the court was refusing to be drawn into a line-drawing contest in a situation in which clearly the parties had all agreed to create a disturbance.  This is a sensible attitude on the part of the court since a contrary attitude would lead the court into drawing all manner of artificial distinctions after the fact.

It is unlikely that the Ghanaian courts would be finicky about drawing lines and deciding which of the various occurrences was within the scope of the abetment.  Indeed, in situations of riot of unlawful assembly, Act 29 enjoins the court not to draw any lines at all because one who lets loose an untameable horse should bear the consequences.75  Thus every activity which takes place as a result of the riot could be laid at the door of the abettor of the event.

In Chan Wing-Siu, three persons attacked a prostitute and her husband in their flat.  Two of the attackers carried knives, but the third one did not carry any.  The husband was stabbed to death and the three were convicted of murder. The third appellant contended that since there had been no prior agreement that they would take a knife along or use it, he had been wrongly convicted of murder. The Privy Council held that in such circumstances, a foreseeability test would be applied.  Was it foreseeable that even though not desired, the event would occur?  If yes, then the criminal culpability is founded on the fact that the person took part in that enterprise with that foresight. 

This position seems to have been challenged by the later case of R v Wakely, Symonds, Holy76 in which a trial judge was reversed because he directed the jury using the foreseeability test.  The three accused persons in that case had gone to the house of an elderly man to rob the gas meter.  One of them, against the agreement of the others, had taken along an axehandle.  This weapon was eventually used to beat the old man to death.  In holding that the trial judge had misdirected the jury, Lord Lane declared that77: "The suggestion that a mere foresight of the real or definite possibility of violence being used is sufficient to constitute the mental element of murder is prima facie, academically speaking at least, not sufficient."  This is somewhat strange, given the circumstances. Even though the axehandle had been taken along against the agreement of the majority, they must have known that their colleague intended to use it if their attempt to rob was resisted. As things turned out, it was unclear which of them used the axehandle and therefore their initial opposition notwithstanding, the presence of the weapon must have been a source of comfort to them as they embarked on their criminal activity. Lord Lane's statement has been criticised by one author78 who maintains that Chan (supra) is still the leading authority on the subject because Lord Lane showed some confusion in reinterpreting his own earlier decision.

In insisting on the test of "probable consequence of the endeavour to comment,"79 Act 29 imposes a test akin to one of "objective foreseeability", rather than what was expressly or tacitly agreed upon between the parties.  If an occurrence was a probable consequence of an act, then it would be treated as having been foreseeable, although the import was not appreciated rather than what was expressly or tacitly agreed upon between the parties. If an occurrence was a probable consequence of an act, then it would be treated as having been foreseeable, although the import was not appreciated by the actors themselves. To borrow Freedman CJ.’s  words,80 "[T]he violence involved in the carrying out of the [crime] ought not later to be measured or tested by Marquis of Queensberry rules."  Thus it appears to support the reasoning in Chan rather than Wakely, Symonds and Holy.

A Different Crime is Committed against a Different Person

This appears to be the only situation in which the act of the alleged abettor would have nothing to do with the particular crime actually committed.  The caveat here is that the crime must be so unrelated to that which was abetted that it would be defensible to relieve the others of liability.

Lawful Means for Unlawful Ends.

Where the original act planned is not a crime though one of the participants does an unlawful act in pursuance of the planned objective, there would be no liability. This situation was discussed in R v Anorba81 in which the court held that there would be no liability provided that person played no further part in the subsequent  unlawful activity.  This position makes sense because for an act to constitute an abetment, it must be done for an unlawful purpose.  If this were not so, virtually every act could take on a criminal character once subsequent events enabled the activity to be put to a criminal use.

All these different circumstances which have been discussed indicate that although there are situations in which the court would come to the aid of one involved in a joint enterprise which goes awry, no foregone conclusions can be drawn.  Depending upon the activity and the possible scope of the adventure, a person may be liable for acts done in excess of the desired results.  The mere fact that Act 29 provides for situations in which a particular crime abetted would not have been committed indicates that one involves one’s self in criminal activity at one’s own risk.

CONCLUSION

In this paper, an attempt has been made to examine the law of complicity under the Criminal Code, 1960 (Act 29) of Ghana.  This has been done with the aid of decided cases from Ghana as well as other common law jurisdictions.  Although it has been noted that there are very few Ghanaian authorities on the issues discussed, the usefulness of the exercise lies in the certain knowledge that these questions would confront our courts with the passage of time.

The law on complicity in Ghana can therefore be summarised as follows: Any person who in any way assists or renders assistance in the commission of an offence incurs criminal liability.  This does not include mere presence at the scene of a crime, if the presence is merely accidental or does nothing to encourage the actors.  In like vein, any person who does any act for the purpose of assisting another in the commission of an offence whether the act does in fact do so or not - is guilty of an offence. This may include acts which though ordinary, may have been done on a particular occasion for the purpose of assisting in the commission of an offence.  It, however, excludes the mere fact of chancing upon the commission of an offence by others, coupled with a subsequent failure to report the incident.

As to issues of joint enterprise, it would appear that a person is liable for results that are the foreseeable consequences of criminal acts that are done.  This would cover crimes that are substantially the same as those abetted as well as those which though different, are foreseeable as being possible of commission in the course of the abetment. There is also criminal liability for the one who provides the facilities for the commission of a range of offences, when any of that range of offences is committed. The reasoning for this is sound as it would be impossible to pin any of those crimes on the abettor if that were not the case. 

The abolition of the distinction between accessories before the fact and principals in the second degree is also commendable. This not only ensures that moral support, material support, as well as physical assistance are treated the same, but also simplifies the task of the prosecution. This is because there is no difference of substance in moral culpability between the one who supplies a ladder for a burglary and the one who holds it during the commission of the crime.  Indeed, it is arguable that the one who incites another to crime is more evil than one who acts as a lookout and so enables the crime to be committed. Therefore, owing to the abolition of this distinction, everyone who is party to an unlawful enterprise is punishable to the same extent.

The provisions on complicity have been enacted to assist in the fight against crime and must therefore be used for that purpose. The practice of using abettors as prosecution witnesses must be discouraged in order to enable the law deal with those whose activities foster crime.  It is only by so doing that the prosecution net would be cast widely enough to trap all those who mastermind crimes, as well as those who lend their companionship to those engaged in committing crime.

Boateng v The State [1964] GLR 602, SC.  The appellant had been convicted of procuring an illegal abortion by giving his pregnant girlfriend some tablets.  When the girl subsequently miscarried, he was charged with the offence.  The tablets turned out to be vitamin tablets which he had given to her to stop her feeling tired at school.  The subsequent miscarriage had therefore not been artificially induced.  Consequently, the appeal was allowed since the evidence disclosed no wrong doing, and consequently no abetment.

54. R v Mensah [1959] GLR 314, CA. The appellant successfully challenged his conviction when the evidence showed that the principal offender lacked the requisite mens rea of fraud in a charge of forgery.  This issue however should have been whether the accused intended to defraud and not whether the principal offenders had the requisite mens rea for the offence.  His act, standing by itself, was an act of abetment and should have been treated as such.  This should be distinguished from Boateng (supra), note 53.  This is because in Boateng the intention was not to commit an offence, but to stop the girl from feeling sleepy in school, whilst in Mensah the intention was to procure the commission of an offence by playing on the ignorance of the principal offenders.
