DEVELOPMENT OF CUSTOMARY LAW [1991-92] VOL. XVIII RGL 68—94

PROFESSOR EKOW DANIELS W C

 

*

The purpose of this paper is to attempt to trace the growth of our customary law and its survival in spite of the many challenges it has encountered over the years. Since the success story of its survival cannot be told without noting the contribution of the system of law reporting, it would be churlish not to pay tribute to the efforts of the Council for Law Reporting since its establishment towards the preparation and publication of the decisions of the judges in the Ghana Law Reports.1

It can hardly be disputed that if our written sources of customary law had been limited to the documentary records standing in the name of early European writers who only had brief encounters with the people of the then Gold Coast, customary law would have been dismissed by lawyers as primitive because some aspects of the law were presented by the foreign writers in a manner which tended to make them appear ridiculous. One example is the statement which equated the payment of dowry with the purchase price of the bride.2  Tribute must therefore be paid to the pioneering effort of the venerated John Mensah Sarbah who saw the need to record the decisions of the courts and published the first law reports in Part II of his book Fanti Customary Laws under the title "Fanti Law Reports of Decided Cases" in 1897.3

In the preface to the book which he dedicated to George Emil Emizang, Esq of Elmina, Sarbah acknowledged his gratitude to him for his encouragement. He also expressed his thanks to Sir Joseph Turner Hutchinson, then Chief Justice of the Gold Coast, "who readily gave permission when I applied to him to take notes from the records of the Court at Cape Coast Castle." In the course of his selection of cases, Sarbah recounts how he "suddenly received an intimation from the Registrar that the Chief Justice had withdrawn his permission." Consequently he appealed to his readers to pardon him for any errors which might be detected in the reported cases.

One such report which crept in, in spite of the special care taken by Sarbah is the decision of Anno v Abbagee.4  It was an action to claim damages for trespass. The judgment which is attributed to Bailey CJ deserves to be quoted in full in order to appreciate the possible reason for the sudden withdrawal of permission granted to Sarbah:

"Before BAILEY CJ . . . 

JUDGMENT

There was a monkey who wanted to get some nuts that were hot and afire; he got a cat, and used her hands to pull the nuts out of the fire. The monkey got the nuts, and the cat burnt her fingers. Anthony and the three chiefs are the monkey, and you are the cat, and you have burnt your fingers to the extent of £1 each. Judgment: £3 and costs - damages."

We were to be thankful to God that that report did not in anyway detract from the value of Sarbah's collection of cases on customary law.

ORIGINS OF CUSTOMARY LAW

The origin of our customary law must be as old as the history of the ancient states of our fatherland which were founded as a result of ethnic migration, trade and ethnic warfare. By this process our country was peopled by inhabitants who settled here from various parts of the African continent. Historians suggest that the Mole-Dagbane states of Mamprusi, Dagomba, Gonja and the Mossi states of Yatenga and Wagadugu were among the earliest of the kingdoms to emerge in Northern Ghana. The area below Northern Ghana or the middle belt of Ghana saw the founding of such ethnic groups as the Banda, Adansi, Denkyira, Fante, Akyem and Asante collectively described as the Akan people who moved south rather than accept the religion of Islam5  It is estimated that nearly 50 per cent of the population of Ghana are members of this group. Oral traditions affirm that as the Akans continued their migration down south they came into contact with peoples living in the coastal districts who were linguistically and culturally different from them. The western coast consisted of the Guan and the eastern coast comprised the Ga, the Adangbe, the Shai, Krobo and the Ewe. In the light of the diverse ways by which our country was built, it is not surprising that the process of settlement spawned traces of customary law whose sources were as diverse as the different people who founded the country. Some 30 years ago, Lord Denning found a fitting description of the variety of laws which prevailed on the continent of Africa with these words:

"It is at the moment a jumble of pieces much like a jig-saw. One group of pieces is founded on the customs of the African peoples, which vary from territory to territory and from tribe to tribe. Another group of pieces is founded on the law of Islam, with all its many schools and sects. Yet another group is founded on the English common law. Another on the Roman-Dutch law. Another on Indian Statutes. And so forth."

THE COLONIAL CHALLENGES

There can be no doubt that British colonialism posed the first challenge to the existence of our customary laws. Colonial rule in this country began in earnest in 1821 when the corporation of the Company of Merchants trading in Africa was dissolved and all its possessions including the forts and castles on the then Gold Coast were vested in His Majesty, his heirs and successors forever.7

The judicial system at the time of the assumption of British rule was far from satisfactory. The only civil authority was vested in individuals who acted as justices of the peace and commissioner of requests and adjudicated over small claims. After 1821 when the Gold Coast was administered as a dependency of Sierra Leone, the administration of justice became more cumbersome as serious criminal suspects had to be transported there for trial. This arrangement was resented by both the citizens and the administrators. The individual justices were therefore compelled by sheer force of circumstances to exercise irregular jurisdiction extending beyond their legal limits.8 Eventually the Gold Coast's dependency upon Sierra Leone was terminated and she was raised as a separate and distinct colony under the British Settlements Act, 1843.9 The law had had a two-fold purpose. First, it enabled the British Crown to make laws and establish courts for the preservation of order and the administration of justice. Secondly, it made it lawful for the Crown to delegate its powers to any three or more persons within the colony. In the same year the office of Judicial Assessor was created and Captain George Maclean, an experienced administrator, was appointed to that office with power to review the decisions of the British magistrates on appeal cases which involved customary law. In recognition of the trust which the inhabitants of the Gold Coast had in these new judicial arrangements, the Fanti chiefs within the sphere of British influence assembled at Cape Coast on 6 March 1844 and executed a "Bond" whereby they acknowledged the power and jurisdiction of the British Queen. They also renounced human sacrifices and other barbarous practices. Finally, they agreed that murder, robbery and other crimes should be tried by the Queen's officers whilst moulding the customs of the country to the general principles of British law.

Some historians interpret the Bond as a unilateral declaration by which certain Gold Coast rulers voluntarily acknowledged British law and jurisdiction by renouncing part of their sovereignty. This view of the matter can only be accepted for what it is worth; for the juristic basis of the right of the British imperial government to administer law and justice in her dependency stemmed not from the Bond but from general principles of English law which were supplemented by statutes. Thus according to the English Master of the Rolls, it has been decided by the Privy Council that10:

". . . if there be a new and uninhabited country found out by English subjects as the law is the birthright of every subject, so wherever they go, they carry their law with them, and therefore such new found country is to be governed by the laws of England."

The textbook writers seem in some doubt as to the category in which the Gold Coast Colony should be placed; whether it was acquired by settlement (i.e. occupied) or by cession or otherwise.11

But one thing of which there can be no doubt is the fact that at the commencement of British rule the Gold Coast had an impressive indigenous population.

Lord Justice Scarman in a lecture delivered in 1974 defended the "success" of English law overseas on the controversial supposition that "Wherever it went, it found a legal vacuum ....." And in some lands as in Central Africa and great areas of North America, the existing systems of law were "too primitive to compete with the imported product."12 With the greatest respect to the learned justice, statements of this nature tend to distort the truth. No student of colonial history will disagree with the proposition that English law was decidedly exported to Africa to be used as an instrument for government notwithstanding the existence or the quality of the indigenous law prevailing in the area of settlement. And if there was any doubt about the jurisdiction of the colonial power, the British Foreign Jurisdiction Act, 1843 was enacted to remove any traces of doubt. The object of the Act was spelt out in the preamble as follows:

"Whereas by treaty, capitulation, grant, usage, sufferance, and other lawful means Her Majesty hath power and jurisdiction within diverse countries and places out of Her Majesty's dominions: and Whereas doubts have arisen how far the exercise of such power and jurisdiction is controlled by and dependent on the laws and customs of this realm, and it is expedient that such doubts should be removed . . ."

Section 1 of the Act went further to confirm that the jurisdiction held by the Crown should be enjoyed "in the same and ample manner as if Her Majesty had acquired that jurisdiction by cession, or conquest of territory." The combined effect of the British Settlements Act, 1843 and the British Foreign Jurisdiction Act, 1843 gave the colonial power ample power to exercise sweeping powers in the territory of its settlement. In consequence, the indigenous legal system based on our customary law could only exist at the pleasure of British legal concepts or principles.

FORMAL RECOGNITION OF CUSTOMARY LAW

With the right of British rule firmly established, the Gold Coast was on 24 July 1874 proclaimed a British Colony and given its own colonial executive and legislative council body and a Supreme Court. The new Court Ordinance passed by the colonial legislature provided not only for the application by the Supreme Court of English common law, the doctrines of equity and so much of the statutes of general application which were in force in England on 24 July 1874, but also it made elaborate provisions as to the terms and conditions under which "native law and custom" should be observed and enforced by the court.13

Section 19 of the Ordinance entrenched the right of a citizen to be governed by his own native law with these words:

"Nothing in this Ordinance shall deprive the Supreme Court of the right to observe and enforce the observance, or shall deprive any person of the benefit, of any law or custom existing in the said colony . . ."

It then went on to lay down various conditions by which customary law should be observed and enforced. The first condition was that the "law or custom must be proved to have existed in the said colony." This condition was subject to a curious interpretation in Welbeck v Brown14 by H Lesingham Bailey CJ who delivered a majority judgment of the Full Court. Referring to the evidence of witnesses as to the existence of customary law, he observed:

 " . . . they are speaking of a custom, if custom it can be called, which not only does not date from 'a time to which the memory of man runneth not to the contrary,' but actually dates from a time which is in the memory of men now living. How can this be called a custom? I know we are to give effect to native law and custom as it existed at the date of the passing of the Supreme Court Ordinance, viz. 1876. But the man who drafted that Ordinance was a lawyer, and I have no reason to suppose that when he spoke of 'customs' he meant anything more or less than the word imparts to legal ears."

His Lordship continued:

"but one thing does concern me, and that is, that the Courts should do all that is in their power to fix these fugitive will-o'-the-wisps called native customs, and transfer them to the records of the Court, rejecting all those which are alleged to be custom, but which do not bear the test to which I have subjected this one."

In other words, although the court was to give effect to native customs it was only to do so when the customs existed at the date of the passing of the Supreme Court Ordinance, and were customs which dated from a "time whereof the memory of man runneth not to the contrary", and proved to have had the essential character of immemorial customs according to the English law on the point.15

The second condition is this, even when the court had been satisfied of the existence of a particular rule of customary law it was not bound to apply it if in its opinion it was "repugnant to natural justice, equity and good conscience." These provisions left a wide field of uncertainty as to the exact law which might be applied in any particular case.16  Indeed the question by what criterion customary law was to be recognised, yielded different answers to different individual judges or colonial administrators. Some officials were emphatic that in order that it might be recognised, it had to conform with English colonial notions of justice and not African conceptions of these; for an Englishman because he is an Englishman and not someone quite different, cannot adopt some other person's conception of justice.17 Statements such as this, coming from the lips of a top colonial legal officer helped to lower the standing of customary law in the eyes of the colonial powers; but the value of customary law was not lost on all colonial officials. As far back as 1887, Chief Justice Macleod expressed his faith in customary law with these words: "I have found the native laws and customs always founded on very good and intelligible reasons, which are perfectly rational and consistent."18  Other officials were prepared to be patient and sympathetic towards the evaluation of customary law. Three examples may be recalled. Recounting his experience as a judge on the Gold Coast, Sir James Marshall had this to say19:

"What I wish to say is that the natives of the Gold Coast and the West Coast of Africa, have a system of laws and customs which it would be better to guide, modify, and amend, rather than to destroy by Ordinance and force."

A second example is contained in the advice of a writer on African law who wrote thus20:

"Let us be gentle with custom, more particularly when we are not fully acquainted with it. Custom is the outcome of generations of experience under conditions which we can hardly fully appreciate, and as a working principle we shall do well to assume that no custom is entirely foolish or groundless."

The last example comes from the pen of an administrative officer who served in Nigeria21

"The attitude of the English lawyer towards African law and custom is not that of adaptation but contempt for a worthless thing, which, should be abandoned and replaced by European law whole and undefiled. The problems of Africans today of how to adapt themselves to the sudden inrush of strange European ideas get no help from English lawyers, but perhaps we may hope that some day the lawyers, like the missionaries, will discover that there is something in African ideas of justice and right worthy to be built into the new structures that Africa demands."

The third condition is that the native law or custom must not be incompatible either directly or by necessary implication with any Ordinance or statute for the time being in force locally. It is difficult to understand the import of this provision in view of the fact that the regime of the operation of each law was meant to be generally separate. The best comment on this provision comes from Professor Allott who wrote thus:22

"On the whole, it is unreasonable to invalidate a rule of customary law because it is inconsistent with an introduced rule of English law. Unless the English law was introduced specifically so as to remedy some defect in the customary law, then the customary law should prevail (or at least be administered without modification)."

The fourth condition as set out in the Ordinance was that customary law was to apply in causes or matters where the parties were natives of the Colony, but this condition was to operate subject to the rider that no native would be allowed to claim the benefit of any local law or custom,

"if it shall appear either from express contract or from the nature of the transactions . . . that such party agreed that his obligations in connection with such transactions should be regulated exclusively by English law."23

Finally, the Ordinance provided that where no express rule was applicable to the matter in controversy, the court should be governed by the principles of justice, equity and good conscience.

Apart from the provisions of the Ordinance which specified the conditions for the recognition of customary law, the Supreme Court laid down its own rules of practice for the judicial recognition of customary law. One such rule is contained in the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954 which states24:

"31. In all cases in which the party pleading relies upon a native law or custom, the native law or custom relied upon shall be stated in the pleading with sufficient particulars to show the nature and effect of the native law or custom in question and the geographical area and the tribe or tribes to which it relates."

In other words the content or existence of customary law was regarded by the court as a question of fact in much the same way as English courts treat foreign law as facts: As Redwar Ag J put it25:

"Native law, when not incorporated by judicial decision in the law of this land . . . must stand therefore on the same footing as foreign law, and must be proved by the evidence of expert witnesses."

The highest authority on proof or recognition of customary law is the rule laid down by the Privy Council in Angu v Atta which is as follows26:

"As is the case with all customary law, it has to be proved in the first instance by calling witnesses acquainted with the native customs until the particular customs have, by frequent proof in the Courts, become so notorious that the Courts take judicial notice of them."

The rule that customary law was to be treated as foreign law on its own soil must have been drafted for the benefit of the expatriate colonial judges who were deemed to be ignorant of the rules of customary law, hence the insistence on frequent proof of customary law so that the court might take judicial notice of them. Although the rule was subjected to criticism on national grounds, there is no doubt that the doctrine of judicial ignorance of customary law contributed much to the process which led to the recording of customary law in the pages of the law reports. Therefore when the time came for the Courts Ordinance to be replaced by the Courts Act, 1960,27 it was easy for the legislature to provide that "Any question as to the existence or content of a rule of customary law is a question of law for the Court and not a question of fact."  Under the new law, the court was given a discretion to consult recognised textbooks, refer to reported cases or to order the holding of an inquiry if it entertained any doubt as to the content or existence of any rule of customary law.

REASONS FOR ITS SURVIVAL

In spite of these stringent conditions, customary law has survived. The main reason for its survival is that it was handed down to us by our forefathers not in the form of a Code, but in the form of living law. Its growth was therefore evolutionary rather than revolutionary. Even though it has been exposed to social, economic, political and external influences, its essential features remain with us. It can safely be described as the embodiment of our tradition. It may be difficult to give it a precise legal definition. The various Constitutions we have had have described customary law to mean the rules of law which by custom are applicable to particular communities. This is not an adequate definition.28 Nevertheless its features are easily identifiable.

The first essential feature of our customary law is that it is not static and therefore has been able to escape the fate of fossilisation which has befallen some Codes in the past. In the preface to his book on customary law, Sarbah expressed his awareness thus29:

". . . to [of] the danger of reducing Customary Law to a condition of fixity in a semi-developed state of society, the effect of which may hinder the gradually operating innate generation of law by a process of natural development, independent of accident and individual will, which best accords with the varying needs and spirit of a people so circumstanced as the inhabitants of the Gold Coast."

If you examine any aspect of our laws which have been the subject-matter of judicial determination you will not fail to be struck by its progressive growth over the years. Thus in the case of Golightly v Ashrifi30 the then West African Court of Appeal observed that:

"Reference to the works of Redwar and Casely Hayford shows that outright alienation of land, although originally unthought of, has for many years past come to be recognised by native usage."

Flexibility has been recognised as its chief characteristic. Sir Brandford Griffith, a former Chief Justice of the Gold Coast, singled out the progressive nature of customary law with these words31:

"No doubt there are some native customs which are more or less inflexible but native custom generally consists in the performance of the reasonable in the special circumstances of the case."

The second feature of customary law is its popularity. As Sarbah noted32 "customs may not be as wise as laws, but they are always more popular." Thirdly, in the formative years, customary law was largely unwritten, and hence it was able to adapt itself to all the circumstances of the moment of a locality.

Today, a great many rules of customary law, which have received the sanction of the courts are recorded. The judiciary and the legal profession are very well served by the existence of a series of law reports published since 1897 to date. Customary law has also been made more accessible by the publication of legal works by eminent members of the Bench and Bar. Foremost among them is John Mensah Sarbah who more than anyone else campaigned through his writings to give respectability to our customary law. The others are Casely Hayford, Redwar, J B Danquah and Justice N A Ollennu, to name just a few.

The fourth characteristic of customary law is found in the communal nature of its application. Maintenance of the social equilibrium between groups related by kinship has been its main objective. A logical consequence of the recognition of the kinship basis of the society gave rise to the development of customary law on communal lines. Marriage, for example, was seen not so much as a contract between the individuals directly concerned, but as one between the two groups of individuals whom they represent.33

 

Further, the corporate nature of our society constituted the juristic basis of the land tenure system. The leading pronouncement of ownership of land in the traditional system was made by Viscount Haldane when delivering the judgment of the Privy Council in a Nigerian case. He said34:

"The next fact which it is important to bear in mind in order to understand the native land law is that the notion of individual ownership is quite foreign to native ideas. Land belongs to the community, the village or the family, never to the individual."

One Nigerian chief described the customary concept of land ownership vividly as follows35 "I conceive that land belongs to a vast family of which many are dead, few are living and countless are yet unborn."

Finally, the distribution of property on the death of a member of the group favoured the family more than the individual. "Therefore" observed Justice Ollennu, "upon the death of a person control and authority over his person and property vested absolutely in his family."36

DEVELOPMENT OF THE LAW THROUGH THE FAMILY SYSTEM

Enough has been said to illustrate the general climate in which customary law developed in the midst of political, social, cultural, economic and religious challenges. I propose to consider the institution of the family as an example with which to illustrate its evolution.

Not many people will disagree with the proposition that the basis of every society from the most primitive to the most complicated is the family. As an institution, it is of great antiquity. In the very first chapter of the Bible, God's command after He created male and female beings was37  "Be fruitful, and  multiply, and replenish the earth and subdue it."

As the history of the evolution of the human family shows, there have existed in various times and places many kinds of family groups.38  The ancient type as found by Sir Henry Maine used to be sustained and enlarged not necessarily by birth, but rather by the absorption of strangers within its circles.39  That conception give way to the formation of a family group which was founded on consanguinity or kinship. The movement of the "progressive societies" gradually led to the dissolution of family dependency, and the growth of individual obligation in its place.40  The disparate evolution of the family is confirmed by other studies: Engels wrote that41:

". . . The word 'familia' did not originally signify the idea of our modern philistine, which is a compound of sentimentality and domestic discord. Among the Romans, in the beginning, it did not even refer to the married couple and their children, but to slaves alone."

In English law, the law relating to the family is said to be of recent origin. Lord Scarman wrote that until the mid-19th century, family life was not the law's concern, but the business of the church.42 Wickens VC described it as "a popular and not a technical expression, and may mean several different things."43

The institution of the family has had its critics as well as its favourites. Samuel Butler, the 19th century novelist, was of the view that the family is a survival of the principle which is more logically embodied in the compound animal. He argued that the family was a primitive or positively unnatural institution44:

"The fishes know it not and they get along quite nicely. The ants and the bees, who far outnumber man, sting their fathers to death as a matter of course."

Radical post-Freudians argued that the family was a source of psychological distress, schizophrenia especially.45 Feminists saw it as the perpetuation of patriarchy. The male chauvinist's view is contained in the following words of Strindberg, the Swedish play-

wright, novelist and poet46:

"The Family! Home of all social evils, a charitable institution for indolent wives, a prison workshop for the slaving breadwinner, and a hell for children."

Sir Edmund Leach, in a famous sentence in his Reith Lectures, summed it up when he said that "Far from being the basis of the good society, the family, with its narrow privacy and tawdry secrets, is the source of all discontents. Currently there are some Akan ballads in which individual misfortunes are blamed on the family into which a person is born.

In spite of its critics, the family has persisted as a popular and useful institution. One of the impressive Reith Lectures given by Dr Jonathan Sachs was largely devoted to a panegyric on the family's role and individual responsibility. He identified the family as one of our social institutions on which all others depend. He saw it as the birthplace of our social world and liberty. He pinpointed the family to be "where we care for dependants, the very young and the very old, those to whom we gave birth or who gave birth to us."47

One fact that has to be borne in mind is that the family as a useful institution persisted because, in spite of the fact that it has meant different things to different people, it has always had the ability to adapt to changing circumstances. One cannot help but agree with the observation of Meyer Fortes that48: "from an anthropological point of view no family system hitherto devised by mankind has been perfect, in the sense that it has been perfectly adapted for its social tasks or fulfilled all expectations of those who live by it."

In the Ghanaian context the word family is all important in our legal system. In the opening pages of his book on the Ashanti, Rattray appropriately observed that "it is in the social organisation of a people in which lie the germs of the legal system which they are later to develop."49  A child is born into a family of persons who may reckon their relationship with one another on the basis that they are either lineally descended from a common ancestress in the female line such as the Akan, or a common ancestor in the male line such as the Ewe. That relationship arises generally by birth and in exceptional cases by adoption but not because of marriage. The Akan have a word for the term "family." It is ebusua which is a matrilineally determined social group. In that family, the wife and children of a married man were supposed to belong not to his family but to the wife's mother's family. Among the patrilineal Ewe, a word used to describe the family is dzotinu,50 which is meant to denote a lineal blood relationship through a common male ancestor whereby children were said to belong to his family and a wife considered to be a member of her father's family. That word is literally translated as a burning piece of firewood to signify its expensive characteristic.

To be born into a family is to belong to a social organisation which is committed to the protection of its members from sickness and misfortune. In the event of death, it is obliged to give its member a decent burial. As the English saying goes, there is safety in numbers. The importance which the Ghanaian attaches to the membership of his family is summed up in at least two Akan proverbs. The first is ebusua ye dom, meaning "to belong to a family is to belong to a crowd." The second is ankonam ye mborbor, literally translated to mean: "to be alone is to be miserable."

In the eyes of customary law, the family is regarded like a corporation aggregate which consists of several members related genealogically. It has the distinctive characteristic that it never dies. The death of a member made no difference to the collective existence of the aggregate body.51

The corporate seal of the family is in the form of a consecrated stool in which the souls of the deceased members arc believed to be resident. The stool is the source of its authority. In law, the legal title of all the stool property is vested in the stool itself and never in the occupant of the stool for the time being.

THE HEAD OF FAMILY

The family system was distinguished by the status of the person who is elected or appointed as its representative. He is called the head of the family. He is the one who has custody or charge of the family stool. He has considerable and onerous duties to perform, varying in degree, of course, according to the size, wealth and importance of the family. His functions are neatly summed up by Graham Paul J in a Nigerian case which applies with full force in the Ghanaian context. His Lordship said52:

"The head of the family is in charge and control of the family property; he collects the revenue of family property; he has to make certain disbursements out of the family revenue for family purposes, upkeep of the family property, funeral, marriage and baptism ceremonial expenses of members of the family often involving the entertainment of strangers, litigations on behalf of the family, maintenance of indigent members of the family, education of children and so on."

He holds the family property in trust for himself and for the members of the family. In return he expects the members to pay their contributions in kind or cash for the attainment of the objectives of the family. Having accorded judicial notice to the complex and extensive powers vested in the head of family over its members' property, the courts in cases that came before them displayed a remarkable reluctance to entertain actions instituted by members calling on their head of family to account for the management of family property. The courts in effect ruled out that heads of family were not liable to render an account to their members. Delivering the unanimous judgment of the West African Court of Appeal, Korsah J (as he then was) said53:

"It is an accepted principle of Native Customary law that neither a chief nor the head of a family can be sued for account either of state or family funds."

He went further to elaborate that no individual member of even a section of the community was entitled to institute an action for account against him.

Admittedly, there is much to be said for the custom of allowing a head of family to discharge his numerous duties without undue interference from meddlesome and disgruntled members of the family because suits in a court of law are bound to have a deleterious effect on the smooth running of the family's affairs. Having said that, it has to be recognised that the conferment of immunity from suits against heads of family for misappropriation of family funds could, if not put under scrutiny, have led to an abuse of the very principle the rule was introduced to prevent. Public concern over the system of non-accountability of heads of family led to the intervention by the government.

Following the acceptance of the report of the Law Reform Commission, the government in 1985 enacted a law called The Head of Family (Accountability) Law, 1985.54  It is a very short piece of legislation consisting of only three sections. Section 1 provides that: "any head of family or any person who is in possession or control of, or has in his custody, any family property shall be accountable for such property to the family to which the property belongs." To that end, a duty is also imposed on a family head to cause and file an inventory of all such family property. The second section provides a remedy to the members who might feel aggrieved. But before any such suits may be entertained by the court, the challenging member is required to satisfy it (a) that he has a locus standi or a beneficial interest in the matter, and (b) that he has taken steps to settle the matter within the family and that all such attempts have failed. The third section empowers the courts to make an order to compel the head of a family to render an account in respect of family properties in his control or custody.

These reforms are welcome. In Ghana there is a gradual movement towards this objective that the modern family exists for its members and not the other way round. The acceptance of the curbs on the immunity of family heads has improved the public perception of the value of the family.

THE FAMILY AND MARRIAGE

The institution of marriage has met with a number of challenges both external and internal as a result of socio-economic pressures. In spite of these factors, the family continues to play a useful role in marriage.55 Whereas in most European countries, marriage is considered to be the basis of the family in accordance with the Christian decree that "a man shall leave his father and mother and unite with his wife, and the two will become one,"55a in Ghana it would be more accurate to describe marriage as an institution which has its roots in the family rather than the family being rooted in marriage.56  In the Akan system as explained by Fortes.57 "there is a perpetual, if normally latent conflict between the apparently artificial bonds of marriage on the one hand (be it customary marriage or church marriage or marriage by some legally binding Ordinance), and on the other, the natural bonds established by birth, between brother and sister, and parent and child with a special accent on the bonds with the mother as opposed to the father."

The passing of the Marriage Ordinance in 1884 however, posed a serious challenge to customary law marriage in the context of the family system. There is no doubt that the policy behind the Ordinance drafted by the Colonial Office was to spread English social ideals, with the hope of the eventual eradication of indigenous social values. Its chief objects were: a to provide a machinery for the celebration of a statutory monogamous marriage locally in accordance with the principles of English law;

(b) to encourage persons already subject to customary laws to avail  themselves of the provisions of the Marriage Ordinance;

(c) to offer them and their offspring proprietary benefits in the event of death intestate of one of the parties to such a marriage;

(d) to make it possible for persons already married under customary law to marry again under the Ordinance and thereby to convert that marriage into a statutory monogamous marriage;

(e)  to clothe the parties with a status superior to and unknown to customary law; and

(f)  to make it virtually impossible for parties who are married under the Ordinance and their offspring to relapse into polygamy.58

Indeed, one leading editorial in a local newspaper in 1885 expressed the view that the Ordinance was "a mere pretentious measure" adding that "it deals with native as well as Christian marriages and it also attempts to make people moral by legislation.59  It cannot be seriously denied that the main aim of the Ordinance was to confer on the parties married under it certain material benefits which were denied to them under customary law. One reason was that the customary law conception of marriage did not regard wives as forming part of the husband's economic unit so as to entitle them to a separate beneficial interest in the assets of the marriage. As Sarbah wrote: "Whatever a wife helps her husband to acquire is the sole property of the husband."60  This view of the law was given a judicial indorsement by Ollennu J (as he then was) who said this in Quartey v Martey"61 "The proceeds of this joint effort of a man and his wife and/or children, and any property which the man acquires with such proceeds, are by customary law the individual property of the man." The fact that customary law was clearly lagging behind social realities in this context was brought out in a series of court cases.61a But gradually the different legal incidents that flowed from a marriage under customary law on the one hand and a marriage under the Ordinance on the other, were marginalised partly by judicial decisions and partly by legislation.

One such legislation is the Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971. Before the passage of that law, a spouse married under customary law which was polygamous in nature was not entitled to apply to the High Court for the purpose of enforcing his rights of marriage or for the purpose of dissolving it. But now under the 1971 legislation such a person can have redress in the High Court, for section 41(2) provides as follows62:

"(2) On application by a party to a marriage other than a monogamous marriage, the court shall apply the provisions of this Act to that marriage, and in so doing, subject to the requirements of justice, equity and good conscience, the court may -

(a) have regard to the peculiar incidents of that marriage in determining appropriate relief, financial provision and child custody arrangements;

(b) grant any form of relief recognised by the personal law of the parties to the proceedings, either in addition to or in substitution for the matrimonial reliefs afforded by this Act."

On a true construction of this provision one cannot really say that by the stroke of section 41 parity of status has been conferred on spouses married under the Ordinance and at customary law. Prejudices against spouses married under customary law still subsist, but progress is being made in that area of the law. The number of petitions for the dissolution of customary law marriages is on the increase gradually. "The mere fact", writes Wanitzek,63 "that the Matrimonial Causes Act provides for divorce on the application of a husband or wife under the breakdown principle, for the settlement of matrimonial property, and for financial provision for a spouse, represents already a benefit for women because they do not have all these rights under the various customary laws."

The other legislation is the Customary Marriage and Divorce Registration Law, 198564 which has made some elaborate provisions for the compulsory registration of customary marriages and divorce before a state official. The application for registration may be made at any time after the marriage has been contracted. The essential requirements for the validity of the marriage in accordance with the particular customary law are, however, left to be resolved by the parties and their families. The omission to spell out the essential requirements of customary marriage does not detract from the value of that legislation, for over the years, the courts have, in a number of cases, laid down the minimum requirements comprised in all such marriage contracts. Thus, in the case of Yaotey v Quaye65 Ollennu J (as he then was) listed the agreement of the individual parties and that of their respective families together with consummation as adequate proof of customary marriage. The role of the family with respect to formation and maintenance of marriage at customary law has been preserved. It can safely be concluded that customary law marriages have survived in spite of the introduction of statutory monogamous marriage by the Marriage Ordinance, 1884.

THE FAMILY AND INHERITANCE

Ghana's society in its infancy developed its customary law through the same stages as were traversed by many of the earlier civilizations known to history. As Hailey then wrote, "The laws of the ancient Babylonians, Hebrews, Romans, and Saxons present many analogies to what is to be seen among the African peoples of the present era.66:

Perhaps the system of inheritance which came under increasing criticism was the matrilineal system. Barbot, a seventeenth century European traveller on the Gold Coast, found it strange. He then wrote67:

"The right of inheritance all over the Gold Coast except Accra, is very strangely settled; for the children born legitimate never inherit their parents effects. The brothers and sisters children are the lawful heirs."

Our inheritance law may be said to have been based on at least three main ideas or concepts which were identified some three centuries ago by Sir Henry Maine as follows68: (a) that men are regarded and treated, not as individuals, but always as members of a  particular group, e.g. a gens, house, clan, lineage or family; (b) that the society has for its units not individuals, but groups of men united by the reality or the fiction of blood-relationship; and (c) that the group which we call the family was considered to be a corporation which never died. Therefore, wrote Maine: "The decease of individual members makes no difference to the collective existence of the aggregate body, and does not in any way affect its legal incidents, its faculties or liabilities." This view of the law is echoed by Sarbah who then wrote69:

"There is no such thing as succession, in the proper English meaning, in a family owning ancestral property. The whole family, consisting of males and females, constitutes a sort of corporation; some of the members being co-partners, i.e. persons entitled to a portion of the property on partition (cutting Ekar), and others who are dependants, and are entitled to reside in the dwelling-house for life, such as sons and daughters, subject to good conduct and not disputing right of the family."

The right of the family to the property of an individual member on death intestate was recognised as a presumption of law by the courts. Thus Deane CJ said in Larkai v Amorkor70  "Now the presumption of law on the Gold Coast is that property held by an individual becomes family property on his death intestate, and that presumption can only be displaced by satisfactory evidence that during his lifetime he parted with the property by giving it to another."

This view of the law was restated by Coussey J who said in Vanderpuye v Botchway71 that: "the family is the unit for the purpose of ownership of property. All the members have a joint interest in the family property which is indivisible."

Thus in theory, among the matrilineal Akan the widow and her children were excluded, whilst in the case of an Ewe male the children enjoyed an interest in the estate to the exclusion of the widow.72 But the court saw to it that the family as successor inherited the property of a deceased intestate subject to some contingent interest of the widows and children of the deceased, such as the enjoyment of life interest subject to "good behaviour."

So the two aspects of our system of inheritance which needed to be reformed in order to ensure its survival in our legal system was, firstly, the rule that gave a lion's share of the estate of a deceased individual to his family, and secondly, the Akan rule that excluded the widow and children from enjoying an indefeasible interest in the estate of their deceased husband and father. But changes in the system of inheritance did not come easily and quickly.

The first attempt at reform was made by statute and was inserted into the Marriage Ordinance, 1884 which provided that where any person who is subject to native law or custom contracted a monogamous marriage under the Ordinance and died intestate, two-thirds of his property was to devolve on his widow and children and the remaining one-third was to be given to his family (by blood). Given the fact that only a small number of Ghanaians were prepared to contract their marriage under the Ordinance, this reforming measure benefited only a small section of the society.

This limited statutory measure was followed by a series of campaigns mounted by religious and youth leaders and chiefs for the reform of the law on inheritance. However, it had a limited effect on the populace.73  For example, some churches passed their own bye-laws on inheritance; but they were observed by their members only. Besides, the bye-laws did not have the force of law. In the absence of legislative initiative, the challenge for reform fell on the shoulders of the judiciary. In In re Kofi Antubam (Decd); Quaico v Fosu,74 Archer J (as he then was) seized the opportunity to focus public attention once more on the anomalies in the regime of our inheritance laws.

The case was instituted by the head of the family of the deceased intestate who asked the court to determine a number of questions relating to the extent, if any, to which the surviving widows and children had an interest in the estate of the deceased intestate.  The learned judge after reviewing the state of the law of matrilineal succession asserted his view with these words75:

"[T]he proposition that children are not considered members of the father's family is contrary to all biological principles, alien to well-known doctrines of all accredited religions and opposed to common sense. The logic of the customary rule is that because children are not considered members of the father's family, therefore they are completely excluded from any share of or right to his property. As I have already argued if the basis for this exclusion does not make sense then the exclusion itself cannot stand."

His Lordship therefore determined the question in favour of widows and children being given some interest in the estate of the deceased intestate. Even though the ruling had an impact on the thinking of the judiciary the reformative measures introduced in the Antubam case (supra) had a limited success. The customary rule of inheritance by which the family of the deceased "could come and immediately sweep away all, not leaving the widow or widower the least part thereof" as recounted by Bossman,76 prevailed notwithstanding the fact that social change had made it absurd. Thus in In re Ackom-Mensah (Decd); Ackom-Mensah v Abosompem77 the plight of widows and children of a deceased intestate at the hands of his family as persisted at the time of the case were recounted with dramatic effect by Hayfron-Benjamin J (as he then was) as follows:

"In the lifetime of their relative, they cannot vent their spleen on his wife. The opportunity comes when he dies. The poor widow and her children are subjected to a vulgar and humiliating abuse; they are made to pay unreasonable and unjustifiable funeral dues, to incur other liabilities in respect of the funeral which can find no foundation in customary law; and after the funeral are harassed and driven to desperation by unnecessary litigation."

Over the years it became clear that judicial decisions on customary law of inheritance however progressive could not effect a widespread reform of the law. Unless the reforms were cast in a legislative stone the inequities contained in the customary law of succession would die hard.

In 1985 the old customary law system was radically overhauled and by the promulgation of the Intestate Succession Law, 198578 which was enacted by the Provisional National Defence Council on the recommendation of the Law Reform Commission whose chairman then was Mr Justice P.E.N.K. Archer. It can be described as an important landmark and possibly a turning point in the development of the law relating to inheritance.  Professor Allott's prognosis is that it "will revolutionalise family relationships in Ghana, and contribute strongly to its evolution towards western form of social system." Accompanying the law is a memorandum which fully explains the rationale behind the abolition of the old law. By way of introduction it states that:

"This law is aimed at removing the anomalies in the present law relating to intestate succession and to provide a uniform intestate succession law that will be applicable throughout the country irrespective of the class of the intestate and the type of marriage contracted by him or her."

The memorandum also stressed the need for the recognition of the nuclear family by pointing out that"79:

"The growing importance of the nuclear family brings with it its own logic of moral justice. Simply put, this argues that a surviving spouse be compensated for his or her services to the deceased spouse; that a spouse is more likely to look after the children on the death of the other partner than anybody else, and that the expectation of the spouses are probably best satisfied by giving the property of one to the other on the former's death."

Dealing with the question of redistribution of the property of the deceased, the memorandum concluded as follows:

"The provisions of this Law are therefore aimed at giving a large portion of the estate of the deceased to his spouse and children than is normally the case at present. They also remove the discrimination between succession by widows and widowers which exist under both the Marriage Ordinance (Cap. 127) and the Marriage of Mohammedans Ordinance (Cap. 129); the same scheme of distribution will apply whether the deceased is a man or woman. Parents are more directly identified as beneficiaries than is the case ordinarily under the present law."

The main thrust of the new law is to give the matrimonial home and its contents to the widow and children absolutely in equal shares. Then the residue is to be shared amongst the widow, the children, the parents of the deceased and lastly, the family of the deceased so that the family which used to be at the top of the league table has now been relegated to the bottom. The Intestate Succession Law, 1985 was greeted with widespread commendation: It ought to be seen as a reform of our family system rather than a break with the past.

In In re Kofi Antubam (Decd); Quaico v Fosu, Archer J (as he then was) prophesied that80:

"If the customary law is to retain its place as the greatest adjunct to statutory law and the common law, it cannot remain stagnant whilst other aspects of the law are in constant motion."

Indeed, it has to be acknowledged that within the space of just over a decade we have moved a long way from the days when the legal system proved inadequate to address the rights of widows/widowers and their children. From 1979 the legal rights of surviving spouses and their children were elevated to the level of fundamental human rights which were guaranteed by the Constitution, 1979.81 Article 22 of the Constitution, 1992 has made similar provisions. It states:

"22. (1) A spouse shall not be deprived of a reasonable provision out of the estate of a spouse whether or not the spouse died having made a will.

(2) Parliament shall, as soon as practicable after the coming into force of this Constitution, enact legislation regulating the property rights of spouses.

(3) With a view to achieving the full realisation of the rights referred to in clause (2) of this article -

(a) a spouse shall have equal access to property jointly acquired during marriage;

(b) assets which are jointly acquired during marriage shall be distributed equitably between the spouses upon dissolution of  the marriage."

At the end of the day, and speaking for myself, we rejoice at the fact that our family system is still with us and that the more it faces challenges, the more it demonstrates its enduring features. Indeed, we can say with a firm degree of certainty that the process of democratisation of our family system will ensure its survival rather than its demise.
