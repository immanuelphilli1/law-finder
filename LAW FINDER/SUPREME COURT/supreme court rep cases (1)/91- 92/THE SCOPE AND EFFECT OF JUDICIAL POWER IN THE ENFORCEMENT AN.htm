THE SCOPE AND EFFECT OF JUDICIAL POWER IN THE ENFORCEMENT AND DEFENCE OF THE CONSTITUTION, 1992* [1991-92] VOL. XVIII RGL 120—158

Hon. Martin A B K Amidu

 

*

Following the traditions of the framers of the Constitutions 1969, and 1979, it is provided under article 125(3) of the Constitution, 1992 that:

“(3) The judicial power of Ghana shall be vested in the Judiciary, accordingly, neither the President nor Parliament nor any organ or agency of the President or Parliament shall have or be given final judicial power."

By the provisions of article 1(2), the Constitution, 1992 is the supreme law and any other law found to be inconsistent with any of its provisions shall to the extent of that inconsistency be void. Article 2 gives the Supreme Court original and exclusive jurisdiction to enforce the Constitution by way of a declaration where: (a) a person alleges that an enactment or anything contained in or done under the authority of that or any other enactment; or (b) any act or omission of any person is inconsistent with, or in contravention of a provision of the Constitution. In this connection the Supreme Court is given powers to make such directions as it may consider appropriate for giving effect or enabling effect to be given to the declaration so made. These provisions are in pari materia with the provisions of articles 1(2), and 2(1) and (2) of the Constitutions, 1969 and 1979 respectively. Unlike the Constitution, 1969 but like the Constitution, 1979 disobedience of any direction or order of the Supreme Court constitutes a new crime categorised as high crime.1 In article 130 of the Constitution, 1992 the original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court is substantially the same as under article 106 of the Constitution, 1969 and article 118 of the Constitution, 1979 respectively. It states:

"130.(1) Subject to the jurisdiction of the High Court in the enforcement of the Fundamental Human Rights and Freedoms as provided in article 33 of this Constitution, the Supreme Court shall have exclusive original jurisdiction in -

(a) all matters relating to the enforcement or interpretation of this Constitution; and

(b) all matters arising as to whether an enactment was made in excess of the powers conferred on Parliament or any other authority or person by law or under this Constitution.

(2) Where an issue that relates to a matter or question referred to in clause (1) of this article arises in any proceedings in a court other than the Supreme Court, that court shall stay proceedings and refer the question of law involved to the Supreme Court for determination; and the Court in which the question arose shall dispose of the case in accordance with the decision of the Supreme Court."

The independence of the judiciary from control and direction of any person or authority is likewise enshrined in article 127.2 The supervisory jurisdiction of the Supreme Court over all courts and any adjudicating authority is preserved under article 132.3 The High Court has also been given original jurisdiction by articles 33 and 140 to enforce the fundamental human rights provisions contained in articles 12 to 32 inclusive of the Constitution 1992.4 The transitional provisions scheduled to the Constitution like those of 1969 and 1979 have what may be called ouster clauses which enjoin the courts not to inquire into the matters referred to. In substance and in form therefore the provisions of the Constitution, 1992 dealing with the judicial power of the State are the same or substantially the same as the provisions of the Constitution, 1969 and that of 1979 dealing with the same subject matter. One major point of departure is that article 125(1) stipulates that justice emanates from the people. The preamble and the directive principles of state policy also enjoin Ghanaians under the Constitution, 1992 to strive for the protection and preservation not only of fundamental human rights and the rule of law but also of the unity and stability for our nation. Save for these more elaborate provisions which could affect the enforcement or interpretation of the Constitution and therefore the scope of judicial power, cases decided under the Constitution, 1969 and that of 1979 can be safe guides to the scope and effect of judicial power in the enforcement and defence of the Constitution, 1992. The concept of judicial power in our post-independence jurisprudence antedates these Constitutions. We will therefore have to go back to the Constitution, 1957 for an appreciation of the express attributes of judicial power as used under the Constitution, 1992.

We have attempted in the foregoing discussions to set forth in outline the provisions of the Constitution, 1992 which deal with judicial power and its form and essence. The task we propose to set ourselves in this lecture is to trace the development and nature of judicial power under the four Constitutions that preceded the present one. We would in the process attempt to contrast these developments with the nature of judicial power enshrined in the Constitution, 1992. An attempt at an analysis of the essential features of judicial power and its limitations would be undertaken. It would be demonstrated that constitutional adjudication involves recourse to political, economic and social policy objectives for choosing between competing claims. The basic rules of interpretation of statutes would still guide the courts, but the controlling effect of such social and economic policies must always be borne in mind in the constitutional adjudicative process in order to facilitate an organic growth, stability and consensus in democratic governance. It would be contended finally that whether judicial power becomes a weapon for the defence of the Constitution or not will depend on the perception of the judiciary of its role as engaging in balancing conflicting claims of rights for stability and good governance.

THE NATURE OF JUDICIAL POWER IN GHANA

(i) Constitution, 1957

The question of the scope and exercise of judicial power has engaged the attention of Ghanaian courts since independence. The question has always arisen in constitutional cases where an objection is taken to the assumption of or refusal of jurisdiction by a court in matters involving the enforcement or interpretation of a constitutional provision. This has been so whether or not the Constitution in question expressly confers or vests judicial power in the judiciary.5 Under the Constitution, 1957, for example, the Supreme Court was given exclusive original jurisdiction in all proceedings in which the validity of any law was called into question. Section 31 empowered Parliament to make laws for the peace, order and good government of Ghana; Parliament was also specifically prohibited from making laws which would make persons of any racial community liable to disabilities to which persons of other communities were not made liable. Subject to restrictions in the interest of preserving public order, morality or health, Parliament was forbidden to make laws which would deprive a person of his freedom of conscience or the right freely to profess, practice or propagate any religion.6 In Lardan v Attorney-General (No 2)7, deportation orders were made against the plaintiffs who brought proceedings at the courts to challenge their validity on the grounds that being Ghanaians they could not be deported. Parliament in response passed the Deportation (Othman Lardan and Amadu Baba) Act, 1957 with the purpose of determining the proceedings challenging the validity of the deportation orders in respect of the plaintiffs. The plaintiffs brought an action for a declaration that the Act was unconstitutional and ultra vires as it infringed the Ghana(Constitution) Order-in-Council, 1957 as being discriminatory within the meaning of section 31(2) and also being repugnant to the powers of making laws for peace, order and good government as contained in section 31(1).The court held that notwithstanding the form of the original proceedings it was clear that their primary purpose was that of impugning the deportation order made under the Aliens Act and were therefore caught by the Deportation (Othman Lardan and Amadu Baba) Act, 1957 and were accordingly determined. It held further that there was nothing in the said Act to indicate that the persons who were the subjects of the order were being discriminated against on racial grounds to bring it within the ambit of section 31(2). It also held that in as far as Parliament did not transgress section 31(2) and (3) or any other specifically prohibitive section of the Constitution, the court had no jurisdiction to call into question the validity of an Act of Parliament on the grounds that it was not conducive to peace, order and good government. His lordship, while asserting that the courts were vested with judicial power to pronounce on the constitutionality of an act in cases where a question arises as to whether a restriction imposed elsewhere in the Constitution had been infringed, admitted that judicial power under the Constitution, 1957 was of limited scope. Murphy J also took the same view of the scope and exercise of judicial power under the Constitution, 1957 in Ware v Ofori-Atta.8 In this case section 35 of the Ghana (Constitution) Order-in-Council, 1957 prescribed the procedure for passing Acts which affected the traditional functions or privileges of chiefs. The Minister of Local Government purporting to act under section 4(1) of the Statute Law (Amendment) (No 2) Act, 1957 made the Ejisu Stool Property Order, 1958 which provided that "Nana Ofori-Atta Ampabame Odikro, was authorised to take possession and prepare an inventory of the stool property pertinent to the Ejisu Stool."

The gyasehene of Ejisu, claiming that he was a chief and the customary custodian of the property of the Ejisu stool, instituted an action claiming among other things: (a) a declaration that the Statute Law (Amendment) (No 2) Act, 1957 was invalid, null and void because it contravened the Constitution, 1957; and (b) a declaration that the Ejisu Stool Property Order, 1958, made under the Act, was also null and void, invalid and of no effect. Specifically the declarations of nullity in respect of both the Act No 2 of 1957 and the Order of 1958 were being sought on the grounds of non-compliance with the procedural requirements of section 35 of the Constitution, 1957 which required Acts affecting the traditional functions or privileges of a chief to be referred to the regional house of chiefs of the region in which the chief exercises his functions as such. No regional houses of chiefs were in place when the Act was passed. The plaintiff thus invoked the original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court under section 31(5). In spite of the submission by counsel for the defendant that the Act affected the traditional functions of chiefs only incidentally, and that it was not sufficient for the plaintiff to show that the Act affected a class of persons, some of whom may be chiefs, his lordship looked at the object and purpose of the Act and came to the conclusion that it directly affected the traditional functions and privileges of chiefs, rather than the community at large. He accordingly held, among other things, that the procedure laid down in section 35 of the Constitution, not having been followed, the Act was invalid. Consequently, his lordship held that the order made thereunder was also invalid. On a submission by counsel for the defence that the Act was made for peace, order and good government under section 31(1) as its primary purpose, his lordship held that there was only one legislature in Ghana and all laws passed by it were presumed to be for peace, order and good government, in accordance with section 31(1) of the Constitution. His lordship was however of the view that a law so passed could not be excluded from the specific ambit of section 35 since if it were so, section 35 would not have applied to any law. Thus, his lordship accepted the position that the original jurisdiction and therefore the judicial power of the Supreme Court was circumscribed by section 31 of the Constitutional Order-in-Council. But it would be prepared to declare any act which transgressed the specific restriction imposed on the exercise of legislative power as ultra vires and invalid in appropriate cases. On this occasion, the Act having transgressed the specific requirements of section 35 of the Constitution the court exercised its power to declare it invalid.

From the cases of Lardan, and Ware one can safely come to the conclusion that the scope and exercise of judicial power was strictly circumscribed by the Constitution and was of very limited scope. Where judicial power could be invoked it was limited to determining whether an Act was in contravention of sections 31(2), (3) and 34 or other specific prohibitive sections such as section 35 of the Constitution. Nonetheless the two cases discussed above illustrate the scope of judicial power, demonstrating that the extent of constitutional limitations placed on the exercise of such power depends on the court’s own conception of the limits of those powers. Thus in Lardan v Attorney-General,9 Smith J took a narrower view of the effect of the Act notwithstanding the fact that the essence of the matter was the determination of the status of the plaintiffs. In Ware v Ofori-Atta on the other hand Murphy J gave an expansive interpretation to section 31(5) to enable him deal with the substance of the Statute Law (Amendment) (No 2) Act, 1957 and declared it invalid.

(ii) Constitution, 1960

The Constitution, 1960 would seem to have conferred on the Supreme Court restrictive judicial powers. Article 41(2) specifically and expressly conferred judicial power on the judiciary. The Constitution also gave the Supreme Court original jurisdiction in all matters where a question arises as to whether or not an enactment was made in excess of the powers conferred on Parliament by or under the Constitution, etc.10 By article 40 the laws of Ghana were defined to include the existing law immediately before the coming into force of the Constitution.

An opportunity for the interpretation of the scope and effect of article 42 of the Constitution, 1960 was presented in Re Akoto.11 In that case the appellants were arrested and placed in detention under an order made by the Governor-General under section 2 of the Preventive Detention Act, 1958.12 Their application to the High Court for habeas corpus was refused. They appealed. Counsel argued seven main points on their behalf, two of which shall be considered for our present purposes, namely:

(1) The Preventive Detention Act, 1958 by virtue of which the appellants were detained, was in excess of the powers conferred on Parliament by the article 13(1) of the Constitution or was contrary to the solemn declarations of fundamental principles made by the President on his assumption of office.

(2) The Preventive Detention Act, 1958 not having been passed upon a declaration of a state of emergency was in violation of the Constitution.

It was argued on behalf of the Republic, among other things, that Parliament by virtue of article 20 of the Constitution was sovereign and its legislative powers were limited only in respect of entrenched clauses. The Preventive Detention Act, 1958 not being an enactment which contravened the Constitution could not be declared invalid by the court. In other words the jurisdiction of the court in respect of judicial review could not be invoked by the appellants. It was held that article 13(1) of the Constitution imposed only a moral obligation upon the President. It did not constitute a bill of rights and did not create legal obligations enforceable in a court of law. The court was of the view that the word "should" used in the article clearly indicated the non-justiciable nature of its provisions. It was further held that the effect of article 20 of the Constitution was that Parliament was sovereign and its legislative powers were qualified only with respect to the entrenched articles. Consequently, it was also held [that the Preventive Detention Act, 1958 not being violative of article 20 of the Constitution was within the competence of Parliament to pass. It was therefore neither invalid nor void.

By this decision the Supreme Court by a unanimous judgment upheld the broader submission on the scope and effect of parliamentary sovereignty which limits judicial power within the context of a narrowly defined compass of the Constitution. The submission for a liberal interpretation which would increase the scope of judicial power under the Constitution was rejected. The ordinary meaning of the constitutional provision was so clear in their lordship's view as not to permit of any other interpretation. The scope of judicial power in the enforcement or interpretation of the Constitution was thus spelt out as follows:

"We are of opinion that the effect of Article 20 of the Constitution which provides for `The Sovereign Parliament', is that subject to the following qualifications, Parliament can make any law it considers necessary. The limitations are that (a) Parliament cannot alter any of the entrenched articles in the Constitution unless there has been a referendum in which the will of the people is expressed; (b) Parliament can however of its own volition, increase, but not diminish the entrenched article; (c) the articles which are not entrenched can only be altered by an Act which specifically amends the Constitution."13

One ought to mention that the invitation to the court by counsel for the appellants to interpret the Constitution so as to confer on itself the Madbury v Madison14 type of expansive powers of judicial review was resisted by the court. Even though the Supreme Court refused to comment on the nature and scope of the judicial power conferred upon it by article 41(2) the question of the meaning or essential features of judicial power was soon to pre-occupy the Court of Appeal established after the coup d'etat of February 1966 which suspended the Constitution, 1960. By some accident of fate the validity of the Corrupt Practices (Prevention) Act, 1964 (Act 230) was being called into question by some functionaries of the Nkrumah regime as being ultra vires article 41(2).

Akainyah v The Republic15 thus became the first case in which the question of what constitutes judicial power appeared as a major issue. The court had to determine whether or not an Act was inconsistent with the Constitution as being a usurpation of the judicial power vested in the courts. In that case, adverse findings were made against the appellants by a commission of inquiry appointed under the Corrupt Practices (Prevention) Act, 1964 (Act 230). The Attorney-General in exercise of his powers under section 4(1) of the Act, preferred various charges against the appellants in the High Court on the basis of the findings made against them by the commission. The charges included conspiracy to commit extortion and corruption. They were convicted and sentenced to various terms of imprisonment. On appeal against their convictions, the appellants contended that Act 230 was invalid and ultra vires the Constitution, 1960 because (i) article 41 of the Constitution conferred the judicial power of the State on the courts and that the effect of Act 230 was to confer judicial power or at least part of it on the commission which was not a court; (ii) that section 5(2) of the Act which provided that the findings of the commission shall be prima facie evidence of the facts found circumscribed the exercise of judicial power by the trial court in such a manner as to constitute an erosion of the court's judicial power; and (iii) section 8(2) of the Act by prescribing the minimum sentence to be imposed by the trial court, was an erosion of judicial power because it interfered with the trial judge's discretion to sentence. The case raised the issue of what is judicial power in its broadest signification as applicable to courts of law.

In dismissing the appeal, the Court unanimously held that Act 230 was valid and not ultra vires article 41 of the Constitution, 1960 because the essential feature of judicial power was not only the power to decide on claims of parties in accordance with established principles of law but also the power to enforce those decisions. All that a commission appointed under the Act was empowered to do was to inquire into the truth of allegations of corruption and thereafter submit a report in a manner prescribed by the Act and although the commission might make adverse findings against any person no penalty was suffered by any such person until the Attorney-General, in exercise of his powers under section 4(1) of the Act invoked the judicial power of the court and procured a conviction. Consequently, such a commission was not a court and it was wholly bereft of judicial power. It was further held that the contention founded on sections 5(2) and 8 of the Act was based on the supposition that the exercise of judicial functions was synonymous with the exercise of judicial power, which was not the case. A characteristic of judicial power was that it was conclusive. The effect of section 5(2) was to regulate the form in which the evidence of the prosecution was to be presented to the court. Section 5(2) was thus not a fetter on the court in the exercise of its powers either to convict or acquit. It was also held that the prescription of a maximum sentence by section 8 of the Act did not interfere with the judge's discretion, did not erode judicial power and by the same token, neither did the prescription of a minimum sentence by section 8(2).The court observed that unless a departure from procedure took away the power of independent decision and the power to enforce that decision from the courts, the novelty of a trial procedure was not an erosion of judicial power.16 Although the Act was passed by Parliament under the Constitution, 1960 and the National Liberation Council was using it in pursuing its "house cleaning" exercise, the court held that Act 230 was passed to meet a social need and its object was to provide a better and obviously more expeditious methods of investigating and dealing with corrupt practices which was one of the greatest evils with which the country was beset.

The judgment of Apaloo JA (as he then was) in Akainyah v The Republic17 which has been adopted in subsequent judicial pronouncements summarises the nature of judicial power when he said that ". . . an essential attribute of judicial power is not only the power to decide on claims of parties in accordance with established legal principles of law but also the power to enforce those decisions."18 But while it is said that one of the attributes of judicial power is the ability to enforce the decision of a court, it is not stated who determines the established principles of law to be applied in determining claims. It is however clear that only a court can exercise judicial power even though organs of the legislature or executive may exercise judicial functions.

The effect of the Court of Appeal's decision in Akainyah v The Republic was to place a restrictive interpretation on the scope of judicial power in order to meet the social needs and objects of an Act which had been passed to deal with one of the most serious evils that had beset the country. A broader interpretation of the judicial power to include established principles of trial procedure in Ghana would have led to declaring the Act ultra vires. What the case however demonstrates is that the concept of judicial power depends on established legal principles that may vary from one age to another. In any case even though judicial power of the State was vested in the judiciary under the Constitution, 1960 it was not stated to be the repository of final judicial power. From the foregoing analysis one can contend that judicial power in so far as the enforcement or interpretation and defence of the Constitution is concerned is the original jurisdiction entrusted to the Supreme Court to determine disputes between interested parties as to the constitutionality of acts of the legislature or the executive and to make an enforceable decision on the issues presented for adjudication.

THE CONSTITUTION, 1969

The words “final judicial power" were added to our constitutional vocabulary in the Constitution, 1969. As one writer has indicated, the scope and effect of judicial power as provided in the Constitution, 1969 was an overreaction to the decision in Re Akoto.19 Admittedly membership of the Constitutional Commission and of the Constituent Assembly, 1968 could on analysis of political inclinations be said to have been tailored to react against what was subjectively perceived as the tyranny of the Nkrumah regime, and the docility of the judges of that regime in failing to defend fundamental human rights. The Constitution, 1969 thus specifically provided that the judicial power of Ghana was vested in the judiciary and accordingly no organ or agency of the executive shall have final judicial power. Articles 1(2), 2 and 3 of the Constitution gave the Supreme Court power to interpret, enforce and defend the Constitution. The High court was given original jurisdiction under article 28 to enforce the fundamental human rights provisions of articles 12-27 inclusive.20 Article 106(1) further conferred original and exclusive jurisdiction on the Supreme Court to interpret or enforce, and to determine questions of whether an enactment was made in excess of the powers conferred upon Parliament or any other authority or person by law or under the Constitution. This was subject to article 28 which conferred original jurisdiction on the High Court in relation to questions of enforcement of the fundamental human rights provisions of the Constitution. Prima facie if Parliament or any other authority purport to confer judicial power on any adjudicating body, the final power to pronounce on the decisions or findings of that body in as far as they related to the Constitution was vested in the judiciary. The judiciary was thus made the supreme defender and guardian of the Constitution. Subsequent developments were to cast doubts on this scope of judicial power. The setting for this was the case of Gbedemah v Awoonor-Williams.21

The regime of the National Liberation Council which usurped political power from the First Republic had in apparent pursuit of its object of introducing "democracy" and engaging in a "house cleaning exercise" against the functionaries of the Nkrumah regime set up a series of commissions of inquiry to investigate certain matters. As part of the process of returning the country to "democracy” and constitutional rule without laying bare the findings of these commissions of inquiry to adjudication by the judiciary, the decisions, declarations and findings of these commissions of inquiry were made final and not subject to appeal or to the supervisory jurisdiction of the courts. The consequence of some of these findings were that they were to constitute prima facie evidence of the commission of certain offences and also of grounds of disqualification from holding public office in the "democratic" and constitutional regime that was to be ushered in by the National Liberation Council.22 The question concerning the finality of the findings of a commission vis-a-vis the final judicial power as enshrined in article 102(1) of the Constitution became an issue of serious constitutional adjudication. Gbedemah v Awoonor-Williams23 was the first such case in the early life of the Constitution, 1969. The plaintiff in this case was one of two defeated candidates for the Keta Constituency in the General Elections held on 29 August 1969 pursuant to the provisions of the Representation of the Peoples Decree, 1968 (NLCD 225) and various legislative instruments made thereunder. He brought this action against the defendant, the successful candidate at the elections, claiming:

(a) a declaration that by virtue of article 71(2)(b)(ii) and (d) of the Constitution the defendant was not qualified to be a member of the National Assembly for the Keta Constituency;

(b) an injunction to restrain the defendant from entering and taking his seat as a member of the National Assembly as long as he continues to be so disqualified.24

Article 71(2)(b)(ii) and (d) provides as follows:

"71.(2) No person shall be qualified to be a member of the Assembly who . . .

(b) has been adjudged or otherwise declared .  . .

(ii) by the report of a Commission of Inquiry to be incompetent to hold public office or that while being a public officer he acquired assets unlawfully, or defrauded the State, or misused or abused his office or wilfully acted in a manner prejudicial to the interest of the State; . . .

(d) has had his property confiscated as the result of the findings of a Commission of Inquiry;”

The defendant, Mr Gbedemah, who had already taken his seat in the National Assembly as the leader of the opposition party in Parliament admitted that a commission made findings against him but challenged the reasonableness and correctness of the findings.

 

He denied that at the time of his election he was disqualified and incompetent to become a member of Parliament under article 71(2)(b)(ii) and (d) and the findings of the commission. He stated further by way of defence that by article 102(1) of the Constitution, 1969, final judicial power was vested only in the judiciary and that the commission could not be vested with final judicial power, and consequently the Investigation and Forfeiture of Assets (Further Implementation of Commissions' Findings) Decree, 1969 (NLCD 354) was invalid in as far as it purported to elevate the findings and recommendations of the commission to a judgment of the High Court with no right of appeal. He stated in addition that the National Liberation Council (Investigation and Forfeiture of Assets) (Amendment) Decree, 1967 (NLCD 129) was also inconsistent with article 102, in so far as it purported to repeal paragraph 4 of the Investigation and Forfeiture of Assets Decree, 1966 (NLCD 72) thereby denying the defendant his right of appeal.

A preliminary objection to the original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court to entertain an action under article 71 for the enforcement of the Constitution was overruled. The court ruled that the Constitution being the fundamental law of the land, any conduct or act which did not conform to it constituted a breach of that law, and the original and exclusive jurisdiction of the Supreme Court gave it the necessary power to issue such directions or orders or writs including writs or orders in the nature of habeas corpus, certiorari, mandamus, prohibition and quo warranto as it might consider appropriate for the purpose of enforcing the Constitution. The effect of that ruling was that besides the interpretation, enforcement and defence jurisdiction conferred by article 2(1) of the Constitution, the Supreme Court had jurisdiction to hear matters in respect of disqualification from Parliament.

It was the case of the plaintiff that the defendant was "adjudged or otherwise declared" by the report of the commission of inquiry to have acquired assets unlawfully while holding public office and was ineligible on that account to be a member of the National Assembly for a period of five years from the date of the publication of the report. The defendant on the other hand claimed that he was not "adjudged or otherwise declared" in the manner contemplated by the article. Counsel for the defendant submitted that the words "adjudged" and "declared" were terms of art with a recognised legal meaning. He contended that an adjudication or declaration could only be properly made by a court of law. The court therefore found itself called upon to determine the meaning of the words "adjudged or otherwise declared" as used in article 71 of the Constitution. The majority of the court in a judgment read by Apaloo JA (as he then was) held that the words "adjudged or otherwise declared" were to be given their ordinary dictionary meaning of "found or pronounced”. In the words of Apaloo JA (as he then was):

"We think therefore that the words ‘adjudge or declare’ have no technical connotation, and in the context of article 71(2) (b) mean that the Commission of Inquiry ‘found or pronounced’ that a person acquired assets unlawfully, etc. To accede to the interpretation put up on behalf of the defendant, it would be necessary to substitute for the words ‘report of a Commission of Inquiry', the words ‘by a Court as a result of the finding of a Commission of Inquiry’. In our judgment, this would be an amendment, not interpretation of the article."25

The court having emphasised that the interpretation it put on the words "adjudged or otherwise declared" was a true reflection of the legislative will, declared the defendant to be caught by article 71(2)(b) of the Constitution. It pointed out that articles 107 and 114 of the Constitution which provided respectively that the Supreme Court and the High Court shall have supervisory jurisdiction over all courts in Ghana and "over any adjudicating authority" negated the view that it was only courts of law which could "adjudge or otherwise declare."

On the counterclaim that NLCD 129 which took away the defendant's right of appeal from the findings of the commission of inquiry; and NLCD 354, which provided a machinery for realising the confiscated assets, were void in as much as they offended against the spirit and letter of article 102 of the Constitution, the majority of the court held that not only were the Decrees valid when they were passed, but so were any acts and steps taken under them including the findings of the commission established under NLCD 72 and Act 250. It also held that the Constitution did not have retrospective effect on Decrees passed by the National Liberation Council or matters lawfully transacted under them. On the contrary, section 13(3) of the transitional provisions scheduled to the Constitution wholly validated them. The court, accordingly, further held that the Decrees did not offend against article 102 conferring final judicial power on the courts. The effect of the decision of the majority was to restrict the scope of judicial power as far as they related to the Decrees passed by the National Liberation Council.

It was the court's opinion that NLCD 129 and NLCD 354 did not take away the supervisory jurisdiction of the courts and as long as the courts' supervisory jurisdiction was not tampered with, final judicial power could not be said to have been usurped.

In a dissenting judgment, Azu Crabbe JA (as he then was) held that the provisions of article 71(2)(b)(ii) and (d) applied to the conclusions of a commission of inquiry appointed before 22 September 1969 and such a commission had no greater powers than a commission appointed under the Constitution. After extensive references to the Proposals of the Constitutional Commission, 1968, and the preamble to the Constitution, his lordship came to the conclusion that the words "adjudged or otherwise declared" were used in its technical signification as against the ordinary meaning attributed to them by the majority decision. He held the words "declared in a report" as used in sub-paragraph (ii) of article 71(2)(b) to be repugnant to the general purview of the Constitution. His lordship therefore proceeded to apply the "Golden Rule" of interpretation to modify the resultant repugnant construction and held that the words "declared by a report" meant a declaration by a court based upon a report. With respect to the judicial power, his lordship held that NLCD 129 which gave finality to the findings of the commission of inquiry and thereby deprived the defendant of a right of appeal was void as being not only inconsistent with article 102 of the Constitution but also repugnant to the Constitution itself. In his lordship's view, that interpretation would preserve the supreme jurisdiction of the superior courts over all inferior tribunals.26

The dissenting judgment of Azu-Crabbe JA (as he then was) has the propensity to give the courts wider powers over the decisions of commissions of inquiry and quasi-judicial bodies and would have afforded a broader protection for the liberty of the individual. This, of course, had to be achieved by means of legislative interpretation of article 71. It followed the Madbury v Madison27 tradition. In applying the grammatical meaning approach, the majority held the meaning of final judicial power to be restrictive only in respect of the supervisory jurisdiction of the courts.

We are of the view that from the essential attributes of judicial power spelt out by Apaloo JA (as he then was) in Akainyah v The Republic28 and adopted by Azu Crabbe JA (as he then was) in Gbedemah v Awoonor-Williams29 one can say that judicial power is not only the independent power to decide claims that raise substantial controversy between parties with sufficient legal interest in accordance with established legal principles of law but also the power to enforce them.

The court adopted a liberal view of the scope of judicial power under the Constitution, 1969 in Sallah v Attorney-General.30 That case raised a serious controversy in the Second Republic. The plaintiff sought:

"a declaration that on a true construction of the provisions of section 9(1) of the First Schedule to the Constitution (Part IV) the Government of Ghana was not entitled to terminate the plaintiff’s appointment as a manager in the Ghana National Trading Corporation."

Section 9(1) of the transitional provisions contained in Schedule I of the Constitution, 1969 provided, inter alia, that subject to the provisions of that section and the Constitution, every person who immediately before the coming into force of the Constitution held or was acting in any office (a) established by or in pursuance of the NLC (Establishment) Proclamation, 1966; or (b) in pursuance of a Decree of the NLC; or (c) by or under the authority of the Council was to be deemed to have been appointed as from the coming into force of the Constitution to hold or act in the equivalent office under the Constitution for a period of six months from the date of such commencement unless before or on expiration of that date such person was appointed by the appropriate authority to hold or to act in that office or some other office.

The question for determination was whether the plaintiff held an office "established" by or under the Council or in pursuance of a Decree of the Council or under the authority of the Council. The plaintiff contended that he did not fall within the three categories of officers affected by the provision and that the office which he held was consequent upon his appointment into the Ghana National Trading Corporation on 16 October 1967. It was argued further that there was a world of difference between the words "established" and "continue in existence." The provision of section 9(1) of the transitional provisions in the plaintiff’s submission had a specific meaning and should not affect all persons in the public service. It was also the submission of the plaintiff that the construction advocated by the defendant conflicted with article 138 of the Constitution which provided that no member of the public service shall be dismissed, removed or reduced in rank or otherwise punished without just cause. The defendant relying on the "Pure Theory of Law" propounded by Kelsen argued that by virtue of the Proclamation, 1966 and the circumstances surrounding it the old legal order was swept away and a new legal order was established from which laws and institutions derived their validity. As paragraph 3(2) of the Proclamation re-enacted all the laws of the country including the Statutory Corporations Act, 1964 (Act 323) and the Ghana National Trading Corporation Instrument, 1965 (LI 395), it was argued, all establishments in the Ghana National Trading Corporation owed their existence to the Proclamation. The plaintiff’s office therefore came within the purview of section 9(1) of the transitional provisions. Three of their lordships rejected the Kelsenian argument and decried the introduction of foreign theories and concepts into an essentially Ghanaian Constitution or statute.31 The majority of their lordships held that the word "established" as used in the Constitution was to be given its ordinary or popular meaning—ie to create or found in the sense of bringing into existence. Their lordships held that the office held by Sallah, the plaintiff, was not caught by section 9(1). In response to an invitation to the court to refer to the legislative history of section 9(1) and the proposals of the Constitutional Commission, three of their lordships in separate judgments held that full meaning could be given to the word "established" within the spirit and letter of the Constitution without resort to any aid to interpretation.

Anin JA (as he then was), in a dissenting judgment, was of the opinion that the legal effect of paragraph 2(2) of the NLC Proclamation was to abolish the old legal order and replace it with a new legal order. In his own words:

"The old legal order . . . yielded place to a new legal order . . . a veritable octopus whose tentacles covered the whole gamut of executive, legislative, and judicial powers of the state (vide paragraph 7 of the Proclamation).”32

Mr Sallah's office was validated by the Proclamation and was therefore the creation of the National Liberation Council and existed by virtue and in pursuance of the Proclamation.

What is clear from the Sallah case is that their lordships differed on the method of exercising judicial power. While the majority limited themselves to collecting the intention of the legislature from the spirit and letter of the Constitution, Anin JA (as he then was) was of the view that the legislative intent could only be collected from the legislative history of both the Proclamation and the Constitution.

Another issue which may be of interest in considering the topic under discussion was the preliminary objection to the jurisdiction of the court in the Sallah case33 on the ground that the letter dismissing the plaintiff was written on the authority of the President and the court was debarred by article 36(b) of the Constitution from questioning acts of the President as he was not liable to proceedings in a court of law in respect of his official functions. The court was unanimous in overruling this preliminary objection on the grounds, inter alia, that the article conferred on the President nothing more than procedural immunity as distinct from substantial immunity. The executive acts of the President or the cabinet were held to be that of the government. Their lordships on this occasion extended the scope of judicial power by giving it an expansive interpretation.34

However in Tait v Ghana Airways Corporation35 the court restricted its scope of the exercise of judicial power. The plaintiff in this case claimed against the defendant-corporation the following relief:

"(a) A declaration that his dismissal from the employment of the defendant-corporation, communicated to [him] by letter dated 29 May 1970 purporting to be signed by the managing director of [the] defendant-corporation, is wrongful and invalid under the Constitution, 1969, in particular articles 138 and 140 thereof.

(b) Consequential relief."

Counsel for the defendant in a preliminary objection stated among other things that the plaintiff's action was essentially one of wrongful dismissal, and did not fall within the interpretation or enforcement provisions of article 106.

The court held unanimously that having regard to the pleadings filed, the plaintiff's action was essentially one of wrongful dismissal.

The court held further that the plaintiff had failed to satisfy it that the facts raised issues of (a) the enforcement of a provision of the Constitution; (b) the interpretation of a provision of the Constitution; or (c) a question whether an enactment was made ultra vires Parliament, or any other authority or person by law or under the Constitution. Consequently its original jurisdiction under article 106 could not be invoked in the instant case.

It could be argued, that the court in arriving at that conclusion looked mainly to the form of the action and not to the substance. The gravamen of the plaintiff's case was that he had been dismissed in violation of the constitutional provisions which required a dismissal to be for a just cause. He was therefore calling upon the court to enforce his constitutional right to be dismissed only for a just cause. The case was distinguished on technical grounds from Gbedemah v Awoonor-Williams in which the court assumed jurisdiction under similar circumstances.

Another startling case of refusal of jurisdiction was Captan v Ministry for Home Affairs.36 This was a case referred by the High Court to the Court of Appeal sitting as Supreme Court under article 106 of the Constitution. The issues for determination were: (a) whether or not a citizen and an alien with a valid resident permit have equal right to freedom of movement as defined in article 24(1) of the Constitution; (b) whether any of the provisions of article 24(3)(c) operated to limit the exercise by the executive of its powers to revoke the residence permit of an alien in the circumstances enumerated in the provisions; (c) whether any provision of article 24 of the Constitution generally operated to require the executive in revoking the residence permit of an alien under the Aliens Act, 1963 (Act 160) to assign reasons for such revocation; and (d) whether article 173 of the Constitution has any relevance to the exercise by the executive of its powers to revoke a residence permit under the Aliens Act. The constitutionality of Act 160 was being called into question.

The court held that article 24 did not enure to the benefit of the plaintiff as an alien. After extensive references to the Proposals for a Constitution for Ghana, international law and the United States Constitution, the court finally reasoned that Act 160 and the regulations made thereunder did no more than to regulate the exercise of power which inhered in sovereignity. The Constitution did not disturb either the existence or the exercise of that power but expressly upheld it. Act 160 could not be said to be inconsistent with the provisions of the Constitution. It was therefore to Act 160 that one must look for the basis of the plaintiff’s claim of a legal right. When pressed that article 175 of the Constitution required the minister in the exercise of his discretion under the Act to assign reasons, the court dismissed this pertinent argument as not applicable to aliens. Thus by the use of the constitutional fiction of inherent sovereignity and the contradictory theory that the Constitution is the fundamental law of the land and that the judiciary was itself subject to that law and could not set itself above the Constitution, the court denied the plaintiff at least the right to reasons for his expulsion under article 175. In spite of the warning that what the Constitutional Proposals intended did not control the clear and unambiguous words of a Constitution37 the court using such aids to interpretation held that article 175 was not applicable to an alien.

An analysis of the Captan case leaves us with no alternative but to argue that the judicial power of the State was restricted under the smoke screen of debatable jurisprudential theories of inherent sovereignity. In reality what their lordships did was to put themselves above the Constitution and the people of Ghana and to decide that a liberal interpretation would not be in the national interest. At the root of the apparent abstract legal rules was a policy decision not to equate the status of citizens with those of aliens. The judiciary would also seem to be avoiding conflict with the legislature and the executive.

A case that further exposes the nature and scope of judicial power as a series of policy judgments is Benneh v The Republic.38 The plaintiff was found by a commission of inquiry to have unlawfully acquired assets. He was served with an entry of judgment to attach his properties pursuant to NLCD 400. The full bench of the Court of Appeal sitting as the Supreme Court sought to determine the validity of NLCD 400. The plaintiff’s contention was that the Decree which was enacted after the coming into force of the Constitution was repugnant to articles 12 and 18 of the Constitution, 1969 in as much as it sought to acquire property without payment of compensation and therefore void under article 1(2). Counsel for the plaintiff stated that NLCD 253 which took away the supervisory jurisdiction by way of the prerogative orders sinned against the Constitution and was invalid. It was further suggested that the main features of NLCD 400 usurped judicial power and therefore contravened article 102 of the Constitution and were void on that account.

The majority of the court in a judgment read by Apaloo JA (as he then was) was of the opinion that NLCD 400 having been enacted after the Constitution, 1969 came into force had to conform to the provisions of the Constitution. In the view of the court, this depended on the meaning of "forfeited to the State" – that is on what date the forfeiture took place. The court held that the date was the earliest date that the commissioners authenticated their findings by appending their signature to the report. The forfeiture must thus be deemed to have taken effect on that date which was before the promulgation of the Constitution rather than the date of the publication of the commission's report. It was not therefore NLCD 400 that expropriated the plaintiff’s property without compensation, and NLCD 400 was accordingly not invalid. Commenting on the concept of "judicial power", Apaloo JA (as he then was) said that the term was an abstract and complex legal term which did not lend itself to any conceptual definition. Judges and legal scientists were not agreed on the legal concepts on which the term revolved.

In a strong dissenting judgment, Azu Crabbe CJ was of the opinion that the effect of paragraph 1 of NLCD 400 was that it was to be construed as one with NLCD 72, that was as if every part of each of the two Decrees had been contained in one. The plaintiff’s assets were deemed to have vested in the State on 27 September 1969 when NLCD 400 was passed and not upon the signing of the commission's report. It was further the opinion of his lordship that the commission was a fact finding body whose findings were not binding upon the government until it decided to give effect to them. Therefore if the commission's findings became effective only upon the publication of their report, then paragraphs 2 and 3 of NLCD 400 were ultra vires article 18(1) of the Constitution; and paragraph 4 was also ultra vires article 12(2) as acquiring the plaintiff’s property without compensation, and article 102(1) as being an erosion or usurpation of the judicial power of the courts. NLCD 400 was consequently inconsistent with the Constitution and was void and of no effect.39

In apparent reference to what his lordship thought were considerations of social or public policy which influenced the decision of the majority, he said that he also thought that the social or public policy of NLCD 72 was to correct the wrongs of the past and to ensure probity and moral rectitude in public life. He however felt that he had to heed salutary warnings of many distinguished judges of the common law about the dangers of resorting to public policy in the construction of statutes.40

The lesson we may draw from this case is that the courts will expand or restrict the scope of judicial power depending (a) on the particular case put forward by the parties before it; and (b) in the exercise of judicial power in respect of specific cases, they employ general principles to achieve policy goals. Judicial power is not a mechanical concept on which judges and legal scientists are agreed but their nature and scope depends on the public or social policy considerations which the courts think would effectuate the spirit and letter of the Constitution. Thus whilst the majority of their lordships claimed that they were not influenced by public policy but the use of the rules of interpretation which are designed to discover the legislative will, they could not avoid the real public policy underpinnings of their decision when they said that the policy of NLCD 72 and articles 67 and 89 of the Constitution had the object of ensuring uprightness in public life. Azu Crabbe CJ who heeded the salutary warning of distinguished judges of the common law was also forced to base his decision on the need to favour the social or public policy of not confiscating property without compensation or final redress by the judiciary.41

Constitution, 1979

The Constitution, 1969 was succeeded to by the Constitution, 1979. The provisions of the latter Constitution on judicial power as pointed out earlier were similar to those of the former. It would however be helpful to discuss how the Supreme Court perceived judicial power under the Constitution, 1979.

The cases of Tuffour v Attorney-General42 and Bilson v Apaloo43 demonstrate the Supreme Court's perception of its role in the enforcement of the Constitution through the use of judicial power under its original jurisdiction. In Tuffour v Attorney-General the court was called upon to determine, inter alia, whether: (a) as a full bench of the Court of Appeal sitting as the Supreme Court,  it could hear the plaintiff’s claim in an action involving the interpretation of a constitutional provision that on the coming into force of the Constitution, 1979 the Hon Mr Justice Apaloo was deemed to have been appointed the Chief Justice of Ghana and as such became the president and member of the Supreme Court; and (b) whether his purported nomination by the President for approval by Parliament of his appointment as Chief Justice and member of the Supreme Court and his vetting by Parliament were in contravention of the Constitution and the laws of Ghana and therefore null and void.

The court by a unanimous decision dismissed the preliminary objection based on lack of jurisdiction and also upheld the plaintiff's claim. It held, inter alia, that even though the court was precluded from exercising the Supreme Court's jurisdiction under article 2, section 3 of the transitional provisions scheduled to the Constitution conferred on the court's jurisdiction to deal with any issue falling within the ambit of article 118 regulating the original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court, in particular for the purpose of enforcing the Constitution under article 118(a). It also held that the Constitution, by article 1(3), conferred on any citizen of Ghana the right to see to it that the constitutional order was maintained. One of the methods by which a person could determine whether a person was seeking to do this was to seek an interpretation of the Constitution as to the meaning or effect of a particular constitutional provision or provisions. It held further that the question as to whether or not the Hon Mr Justice Apaloo was the Chief Justice was not a private right. The interest of the plaintiff was a constitutional right exercisable by all citizens of Ghana by virtue of article 1 of the Constitution. The plaintiff under article 1 need not have a community of interest with any person or authority. His community of interest was with the Constitution.44

On the substantive claim itself, the court held that the phrase "shall be deemed" to have been appointed in article 127(8) meant that a justice of the superior court of judicature (that one composite institution) holding office as such immediately before the coming into force of the Constitution should continue to hold the office as if he had been appointed by its process. The Chief Justice was a member and head of the superior court of judicature. He was a member of the class of persons or justices referred to in article 127(8). Upon the coming into force of the Constitution, therefore, the incumbent Chief Justice, by virtue of article 127(8) and (9), became the Chief Justice and under article 114(1) the head of the judiciary.

In assuming jurisdiction in this case, the full court like its predecessor under the Constitution, 1969 was giving an expansive interpretation of judicial power under article 118. In introducing the concepts of constitutional right exercisable by all citizens, and community of interest with the Constitution, the court was departing from the concept of locus standi as understood in Ghanaian constitutional jurisprudence. The court was providing the right to seek an advisory opinion from it under the guise of constitutional interest.45 Its interpretation of article 127(8) can only be justified on grounds of policy as to the extent to which the executive should be allowed to appoint its own Chief Justice in a transitional period where there is an incumbent Chief Justice. The decision meant that a constitutional regime must be bound to accept the Chief Justice appointed under a military regime. In our view the liberal interpretation placed on article 127(8) was contrary to the express provisions of the article. However, the policy of continuity has now been clearly expressed under section 3 of the transitional provisions allowing all judges of the superior courts to continue in office as if appointed under the Constitution, 1992.

The natural reaction to the Tuffour case and its expansion of the concept of locus standi was Bilson v Apaloo.46 The plaintiff by his writ invoked the original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court under article 2(1) of the Constitution, 1979 for a declaration, among other things, that Apaloo CJ acted in contravention of article 114(3), section 3(1) of the transitional provisions and article 121(2) of the Constitution, in empanelling five judges of the Court of Appeal instead of three to hear and determine the suit in the Tuffour case. By so doing, the court was not duly constituted and thus lacked the requisite jurisdiction. The purported declaratory judgment or decision of the Court of Appeal in the Tuffour case was therefore null and void and of no legal effect because the whole trial contravened article 44(9) of the Constitution.

In the judgment read on behalf of the majority of three of their lordships, it was held, among other things, that the panel of five judges that heard the case fell within the competence of the Court of Appeal as established under article 114(5) of the Constitution and it was constitutionally and regularly empanelled. The court arrived at that decision by comparing the language of articles 115(2) with that of 121(2) of the Constitution. It was of the opinion that in such a comparison one detects a change in language. While article 115(2) stated that the Supreme Court "shall be duly constituted ... by not less than five Justices thereof', article 121(2) stated that the Court of Appeal "shall be duly constituted by any three Justices thereof" In the opinion of the court, the phrase "shall be duly constituted" when construed in its ordinary or grammatical sense, means "shall be rightly or properly formed or established." The language of article 115(2) set a minimum quorum of five without fixing a maximum. The words employed in article 121(2) provided for the court's quorum simpliciter without specifying the maximum number of members who could sit on the Court of Appeal. It also held that the effect of the declaratory judgment in the Tuffour case was to restate what had in the contemplation of law been the existing constitutional position, namely the Hon Mr Justice Apaloo was the de jure Chief Justice at all material times. The pendency of the Tuffour case did not therefore affect, and could not have affected the defendant's constitutional position as the Chief Justice. In their lordships' opinion, it could not be said with any legal or constitutional justification that the defendant was unable to perform the functions of his office immediately before or even during the pendency of the Tuffour case. The plaintiff’s case was dismissed on four grounds: First, the plaintiff's claim was in reality an appeal masquerading as an action for declaration. Secondly, the Tuffour case was res inter alios acta as neither the plaintiff nor the defendant was a party to the suit. The plaintiff could not therefore be a proper party to a declaration to have the Tuffour case quashed on an alleged ground of constitutional contravention. Thirdly, article 2(1)(b) contemplated an “act” or "omission" of a person which was alleged to be inconsistent or in contravention of a constitutional provision. In their lordships view, in this case, no act or omission of a person was specified in the pleadings. Finally, article 44(9) did not create a right of action, it merely conferred a qualified immunity from suit on the President while in office, but saved action brought under article 2 and by the prerogative writs.

The judgment of the majority of their lordships, in our humble opinion, strained the English language in its comparison of articles 115(2) and 121(20) in arriving at the conclusion that the Court of Appeal could be duly constituted by more than three judges.

The contradictions in the majority judgment founded on a historical practice of the Court of Appeal and what Taylor JSC called the trinity of prudence, convenience, and logic naturally found exposure in the dissenting opinions of Adade and Taylor JJSC. The dissenting justices by clear and convincing argument held that the Court of Appeal sitting as the Supreme Court could only sit as a court of three and not more. Adade JSC in response to the use of the previous practice of the Court of Appeal sitting as a court of five when exercising the powers of the Supreme Court, rightly pointed out that if the Court had been sitting as a court of five and no one raised a finger it did not mean that a finger could never be raised. "It has been raised now and it cannot be forced down."47 Taylor JSC was of the opinion that the argument was to the effect that a practice of the court could run counter to clear constitutional provisions and yet receive the court's collective sanction.48 Taylor JSC held that under the new constitutional order all courts with the exception of the Supreme Court were mandatorily prohibited by article 44(9) from calling into question, the acts or purported acts of the President when they do not involve breach of fundamental human rights or the operation of the prerogative writs. He was of the view that the defendant by empanelling a Court of Appeal to question such acts in the circumstances of the Tuffour case acted in breach of the Constitution. He was thus of the view that since the empanelling of the Court of Appeal was unconstitutional, the Supreme Court could, suo motu, vacate the judgment. The most devastating response from Taylor JSC to the majority decision was that if the Chief Justice was the de jure Chief Justice then it followed that at the commencement of the suit the Supreme Court which was to consist of a minimum of five judges had been established and the right of the Court of Appeal to sit as a Supreme Court had been extinguished. Consequently, the Court of Appeal which heard the Tuffour case was unconstitutional. The majority thus had their own decision looking them in the face without an answer.

A reading of the case clearly shows that the policy considerations which weighed with the majority in employing convenience, prudence and even logic to dismiss the case would appear to be that the executive should not have the right to change an incumbent Chief Justice even in the teeth of a clear provision which might permit it. The minority view would appear to be supportive of a policy of an executive under a new constitutional order appointing the Chief Justice. In that view, the President was entitled to nominate a new Chief Justice if a nominee was rejected by Parliament. The lack of consensus shows clearly the varying ways in which each individual judge perceives what is or is not in the public interest.49

In contrast with the Tuffour case the tendency on the part of the court to restrict the scope and exercise of judicial power was again manifest in Fattal v Minister for Internal Affairs.50 The two plaintiffs acquired Ghanaian citizenship by naturalisation under the Ghana Nationality Act, 1971 (Act 361) after they had stayed in Ghana for several years. The Supreme Military Council (SMC) passed the Ghana Nationality (Amendment) Decree, 1978 (SMCD172) which revoked the citizenship of the plaintiffs. On 22 August 1978 the Commissioner for Internal Affairs issued under Act 160, two executive instruments which authorised their deportation on 23 March 1980 when the Constitution, 1979 had already come into force. The plaintiffs invoked the original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court under article 2(1)(a) of the Constitution, seeking a declaration, among other things, that as SMCD 172 purported to revoke the citizenship of the plaintiffs without obtaining a judicial order of the High Court as required by Act 361, section 10 thereof was ultra vires as being contrary to the National Redemption Council  (Establishment) Proclamation, 1972 as amended, and the Constitution, 1979 and its continued operation was inconsistent with, or in contravention of chapters 5, 9 and 12 of the Constitution, 1979; and was therefore null and void. They also contended that SMCD 172 constituted a usurpation of the judicial power vested in the judiciary and a legislative judgment, and was therefore inconsistent with the Constitution 1979, chapts 5, 9 and 12. Finally, the plaintiff sought a declaration that their continued deportation from Ghana was unconstitutional.

The court by a majority of four to one held, inter alia, that since 1966, military governments in Ghana had always reserved for themselves the legislative power to either enhance or curtail the judicial power vested in the courts. In the absence of a written constitution which delimited the powers of the various organs of State, a military government was at liberty to do what it liked by Decree which had the force of law. Unless there were constraints, restrictions or limitations on the legislative power, the laws enacted by the legislature must be enforced. It was not therefore open to the court to declare SMCD 172 ultra vires in view of the clear provisions of section 3(3) of the Proclamation, 1972. It also held that SMCD 172 was not inconsistent with or in contravention of the provisions of chapters 5, 9 and 12 of the Constitution on two grounds, namely: (i) SMCD 172 was an existing law and forms part of the laws of Ghana as categorised in article 4(9) of the Constitution but article 4(5) made it clear that the operation of the existing law was not to be affected by the coming into force of the Constitution. In their lordships' opinion, therefore, even if SMCD 172 was assumed to be still operative it should not be affected by the Constitution. Notwithstanding the fact that SMCD 172 was on the statute book as part of the existing law, the court was of the view that it was no longer operative. Whatever was intended to be done by the Decree, cancellation of the plaintiff’s naturalisation had been done and completed. The Decree had been spent and therefore could not operate as inconsistent with or in violation of article 1(2) of the Constitution. As a precaution, the court stated that even if SMCD 172 were still operative, its repeal could not restore the certificate of the plaintiffs in view of the Interpretation Act, 1960 (CA 4), s 8.

The majority held further in respect of article 89(1) of the Constitution that it prohibited legislative judgment after the commencement of the Constitution and not before. Consequently, SMCD 172 which was passed before the commencement of the Constitution could not be void and of no effect because the Constitution operated prospectively and not retrospectively.

The decision of the majority restricted judicial power by straining the meaning of "existing law" in order to uphold the right to deport a person in pursuance of a law made before the coming into force of the Constitution. The policy consideration here seems to be consistent with the inherent sovereignty doctrine of the executive to deport unless there is clear legislative prohibition. Taylor JSC on the other hand was to adopt a policy of expanding the role of the courts in protecting the rights of people who have acquired Ghanaian citizenship rather than rendering them stateless without a hearing by a court of law.51

This discussion of the scope of the exercise of judicial power under the Constitution, 1979, which is similar in all material particular with the Constitution, 1992 would be incomplete without reference to Kwakye v Attorney-General.52 That case deals with the effect of sections 15 and 16 of the transitional provisions scheduled to the Constitution, 1979 which are almost similar to sections 34 and 35 of those scheduled to the Constitution, 1992.

The plaintiff was listed in a press release purported to be issued by the AFRC and carried by the national newspapers on 13 October 1979 as one of 51 persons tried and convicted in absentia by the special court established under the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Special Courts) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 3), as amended by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Special Courts) (Amendment) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 19). He issued a writ in the Supreme Court for a declaration that he was never tried, convicted or restricted by any court established under AFRCD 3 and that the purported imprisonment of 25 years imposed on him, as published in the national press, was an infringement of his fundamental human rights, inconsistent with chapter 6 of the Constitution, void and of no effect. The Attorney-General's preliminary oral motion to strike out the plaintiff’s case in limine on the grounds, inter alia, that the facts pleaded by the plaintiff, admitted that the AFRC took or at least purported to have taken a judicial action against him and accordingly sections 15 and 16 of the transitional provisions, particularly section 15(2) forbade the court from entertaining the plaintiff’s action or granting the plaintiff any remedy was overruled. The court was of the view that the defendant who contended that the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court had been ousted ought to provide factual basis for the application of those provisions. The Attorney-General obliged and later called three witnesses. The first witness for the defendant was the only person present during the alleged trial of the defendant in absentia. The Chairman of the AFRC also gave evidence and said that the AFRC dealt with the plaintiff’s case by reviewing and confirming the sentence passed by the special court. The third witness tendered in evidence the press report in which the plaintiff’s name appeared as having been sentenced to 25 years' imprisonment. The defendant led no evidence showing that any witness was called before the special court nor was evidence led or presented to the court in proof of the charges read against the plaintiff in compliance with section 5 of AFRCD 3 dealing with the trial procedure of the special court. The plaintiff led no evidence. The issue for the determination of the court was therefore whether the jurisdiction to grant the declaration sought by the plaintiff was ousted by section 15(2) and (3) of the transitional provisions.

The majority of the court held among other things that a judicial action taken within the true intendment of section 15(2) of the transitional provisions, meant a judicial action which satisfied the requirements of the law both substantive and procedural. A "judicial action purported to have been taken" meant an action which was not a judicial action properly so called but which looked like, was intended to be, or which had the outward appearance of a judicial action. A purported judicial action was therefore one whose claim to regularity, both procedurally and substantively was inaccurate but whose intention to be a judicial action was clear. Consequently on the facts of the case, although the evidence led by the defendant did not establish or prove any judicial action by the AFRC or any person in the name of the council, it sufficiently established a judicial action purported to have been taken by the special court, a "body authorised by the AFRC against the plaintiff." In the view of the court, it was at least evident that when the special court met, it intended to try the plaintiff under AFRCD 3 as amended by AFRCD 19, by relying on his file and to convict and sentence him if found guilty.

Taylor JSC in a dissenting judgment held that even the evidence of the defendant's witnesses, showed that no evidence was led at all in proof of any of the charges. There was no evidence on which the special court could even make findings and convict and sentence. In his lordship's view the special court did not even purport to try the plaintiff but only proceeded to pass sentence without trial. He was also of the opinion that the AFRC did not authorise the act of the special court to bring it within the purview of section 15(2) and (3) of the transitional provisions. He held further that the effect of section 15(3) of the transitional provisions was to prevent non-compliance with "any procedure prescribed by law" being used as a necessary pre-condition for the operation of the ouster clause in section 15(2). In other words an irregularity in a trial could not prevent the ouster clause from having its intended effect.

Anin JSC who also dissented held that the evidence adduced by the defendant did not amount to proof of the trial in absentia. In the event he concluded that there was no proof of any judicial action taken or purported to have been taken by the AFRC or its agents.

The majority of their lordships gave the words "a purported action" its literal meaning and were satisfied that the procedure prescribed for the special court was novel and therefore accommodated the purported action as falling within the meaning of section 15(3). As Apaloo CJ pointed out, in the civil law jurisprudence a court could properly act on a dossier placed before it as evidence upon which it could make a finding of guilt and it should not therefore be difficult to hold at the very least that the AFRC purported to take judicial action; especially if the finding was confirmed by it. The majority thus restricted the exercise of judicial power by dismissing the plaintiff’s action. However in the unanimous decision of the court which obliged the defendant to provide factual basis for the application it had expanded the scope and the exercise of judicial power.53 It should be pointed out that if the court had held otherwise on the substantive issues, the result would have led to a multiplicity of actions and a mass declaration of nullity could definitely have had repercussion for the good governance of the State. The minority of their lordships, reasoning in a typical stable and developed common law tradition of Europe and the United States of America, were for due process as understood in those developed jurisdictions. Surely the public policy consideration of developed countries might not meet the needs of an underdeveloped third world country struggling to learn the rules of democracy in the infant regime of the Third Republic. Their lordships did not articulate the above policy reasons but judges like other ordinary humans bear in mind the consequences of their judgment in ensuring the necessity of order in social life. We believe such policy considerations will continue to guide the courts in the application and the interpretation of the Constitution, 1992 which will be our next subject for consideration.

The Constitution, 1992

It has already been pointed out that the provisions on judicial power under the Constitution, 1992 were in many respects the same as those of the Constitution 1969, and that of 1979. We do not expect a radical change in the perception by the judges of the scope and effect of judicial power under the Constitution, 1992 unless there is a considerable shift towards a liberal and progressive approach to interpretation, enforcement and therefore the defence of the Constitution. But as we have attempted to demonstrate, the whole issue of judicial power, its scope and its exercise is in fact a device to choose between competing public and social policy objectives that would foster good governance so as to ensure stability. The burden of the judiciary in the exercise of final judicial power would of course be onerous. The preamble and the directive principles of state policy, the key to an understanding, enforcement and defence of the Constitution, introduce new concepts and ideals which though non-justiciable, indicate the aspirations of the people. The new concepts of unity, stability, probity and accountability, regional and gender balance definitely pose new challenges. The fundamental human rights provisions have also been considerably enlarged and this will open the floodgate for enforcement or interpretation of the Constitution and declarations of acts of the executive or the legislature or any person or authority as being null and void. The original jurisdiction to enforce fundamental human rights will remain in the High Court but if the Supreme Court should follow the decisions on the interpretation of articles 106(1) and 117(1) of the Constitution 1969 and that of 1979 as a guide in its interpretative approach with regard to the Constitution, 1992, then the original jurisdiction of the High Court may be circumscribed on the grounds canvassed in Gbedemah v Awoonor-Williams and thus give almost concurrent jurisdiction to both courts. A plaintiff may thus shop to be heard in one of those courts. The issues raised in Sallah v Attorney-General will still be with the courts particularly in respect of section 8 of the transitional provisions scheduled to the Constitution, 1992. Section 4 of the transitional provisions attempts to abate the crisis situation which was created for the infant government under the Constitution, 1979 that resulted in the case of Tuffour v Attorney-General. The members of the Consultative Assembly urged, and the Assembly inserted the requirement of parliamentary approval for appointments to the Supreme Court. It was intended to avoid the experience of the Tuffour case and therefore section 4 of the transitional provisions was inserted to safeguard the appointment of judges who have or would be appointed to the Supreme Court before the coming into force of the Constitution on 7 January 1993. This specific provision for judges of the Supreme Court was therefore provided. However, article 145(2) has increased the retiring age of judges of the Supreme Court and the Court of Appeal from 65 to 70 years as obtained under the Constitution, 1969 and that of 1979. Section 8 of the transitional provisions which deals with the continuation of existing offices appears to be controlled by sub-section 2 of that section which states that a person who before the coming into force of the Constitution would have been required under the law in force to vacate his office at the expiration of a period of service shall vacate his office at the expiration of that period. Though this appears to be a general provision it may appear to control not only subsection 1 of section 8 but also section 4 dealing with appointment, and thereby retirement of judges. An issue of interpretation may thus arise in respect of the time for retirement of all judges of the Supreme Court who would in fact appear to be covered by section 4 of the transitional provisions.

Article 125(1) for the first time in our constitutional history premises the administration of justice on the powers of the people from which such powers are directly derivable. The scope and effect of judicial power would appear to have been radically changed to ensure substantive justice instead of procedural justice on the current technical rules of evidence and probably the traditional aids of interpretation which at times defeat the aims of substantive justice. The introduction of the concept of public tribunals as part of the superior courts of justice shows a clear intendment that the technicalities of the present judicial process would have to be looked at by Parliament in the formulation of new rules of evidence.54 It is yet to be seen whether judicial power would be invoked on the grounds that justice is not being administered in the interest of the people. Unfortunately the difficulty in defining the people may limit the scope of such actions. The judicial power would continue to be exercised in determining rights between parties to a suit or an action and would directly speaking be binding only on parties to a cause or matter. By the rules of precedent however inferior courts would be bound by the decisions of the Supreme Court.

As we have attempted to demonstrate in our discussion of preceeding Constitutions in this country, the scope and exercise of judicial power is limited to the parties to the suit - a declaration,  order, decision or direction of the Supreme Court on enforcement of the Constitution is not directed to the world at large but to the parties in the suit. The rest of the people are not affected until they have a case before the court. The effect of judicial power will therefore be on the parties to the controversy directly. But there is no doubt that decisions of the Supreme Court serve as a guide to actions or omissions to all citizens.

Section 34 of the Transitional Provisions

One cannot do justice to the discussion of the scope and effect of judicial power under the Constitution, 1992 without making a comment on section 34(1) of the transitional provisions. This provision has generated a lot of heat and debate. That section which states: "34.(1) No member of the Provisional National Defence Council, Provisional National Defence Council Secretary, or other appointees of the Provisional National Defence Council shall be held liable either jointly or severally, for any act or omission during the administration of the Provisional National Defence Council."

We must admit that this section has no companion in the previous Constitutions we have discussed. It is a new provision intentionally adopted by the Consultative Assembly and approved by the sovereign will of the people at the referendum of 28 April 1992. The PNDC has been ruling this country for upwards of ten years and no rational person would expect the same previous transitional provisions. The most important issue is not the provision in section 34(1) but how the Supreme Court will interpret and enforce it. It has been argued that it is a blanket indemnity for all PNDC members, secretaries and other appointees for any acts done while the PNDC was in authority. Presumably this argument is premised on the position the Supreme Court took in Kwakye v Attorney-General55 by giving the words of section 15 of the transitional provisions scheduled to the Constitution, 1979 their literal meaning. It is however our submission that section 34(1) can be construed to exclude crime, and civil liabilities not necessarily and exclusively incidental to the office or functions of a PNDC member, secretary or appointee. In fact, the preamble and directive principles of state policy which constitute the basis for the understanding of the Constitution and the transitional provisions would not permit a construction that will give government functionaries unlimited indemnity. If we have the likes of the Hon Mr Justice Taylor and the Hon Mr Justice Anin on the Supreme Court, the concept of total indemnity cannot easily be read into that provision. It can be construed as being limited to acts necessarily and exclusively incidental to the office or function of a functionary.56 It would be sad indeed if the Supreme Court in future gave this section an interpretation to cover every act. The scope and exercise of judicial power being dependent on policy consideration for good governance and accountability, it is our hope that the section would not cover every act done when a person held office whether the act be official or personal.

The Public and Political Office Holders (Declaration of Assets and Eligibility) Law, 1992 (PNDCL 280) appears to take care of some of the concerns expressed on the diverse literal meaning of section 34(1).By section 3(1) and (2) of PNDCL 280, all PNDC appointees named therein are to declare their assets when they cease to hold office or at the end of their term of office. The Ombudsman is empowered by section 8(1) and (2) to receive complaints on false declarations and can investigate and take such action as he deems fit if the allegation is proved. Of course the action that the Ombudsman can take includes confiscation of the property to the State. Thus even where the issue involves property acquired unlawfully there is a remedy. We are also of the opinion that that crime may then be prosecuted. The argument that the indemnity can be raised to prevent any vetting by Parliament is also foreclosed by PNDCL 280.

Members of the Consultative Assembly which drafted and considered the Constitution and the transitional provisions did not in fact intend to give a blank cheque to PNDC appointees. But just as Azu Crabbe JA (as he then was) who was a member of the Constituent Assembly, 1969 and chairman of its business committee could not impose his understanding of what the Constituent Assembly understood by article 71 in Gbedemah v Awoonor-Williams, the matter of the interpretation of section 34(1) has to be left ultimately to the courts.57

It is our hope that PNDCL 280 would be read as part of the history of the constitutional making process and which will inform an interpretation of section 34(1) of the transitional provisions of the Constitution. Taylor JSC adopted such an approach in respect of AFRCD 3 and AFRCD 19 which were passed before the coming into force of the Constitution, 1979 for the purpose of finding out the object and purpose of section 15 of the transitional provisions in the Kwakye case. May that precedent be followed in respect of PNDCL 280.

It must be said that under articles 1(2), 2 and 130 of the Constitution, 1992 the enforcement and defence functions of the Supreme Court are achieved largely through the process of interpretation, the making of orders and the giving of such directions as the court may deem appropriate for giving effect, or enabling effect to be given to declarations it makes. Every citizen is enjoined to obey any order of the court or carry out the terms of the order or directions. Penal sanctions follow a refusal to obey or carry out the orders of the court. By this process of enforcement, judicial power defends the Constitution, 1992 in a general sense. However in the exercise of its original jurisdiction under article 130 which is similar to articles 106 and 118 of the Constitution, 1969 and that of 1979 respectively, the courts have the power to defend the Constitution in the exercise of its judicial power. Thus in Gbedemah v Awoonor-Williams,58 the Court of Appeal, sitting as the Supreme Court, held that the provisions of article 2(1) related only to enforcement of the Constitution in the event where any enactment was in conflict with it but the article did not prevent the Supreme Court from suppressing any act or conduct which was calculated to subvert the Constitution itself. The court stated that article106(1)(a) gave the Supreme Court the requisite power to uphold the supremacy of the Constitution. The court then referred to its power to issue the prerogative orders under articles 28(2) and 104(3) and opined that reading those together with article 106 the court could use them to enforce the Constitution against an applicant. The counterpart of articles 28(2) and 104(3) of the Constitution, 1969 are articles 33(2) and 129(4) of the Constitution, 1992. This clearly shows that the line between enforcement and defence is very thin. In enforcing the Constitution by the issue of orders or directions under article 2(2) to give effect to the Constitution, 1969, a court was in fact also defending the Constitution. The position of the court may have depended on the fact that the enforcement provisions under the Constitution, 1969 did not include mandatory obedience to the order or decision followed by penal consequence for non-compliance. If this be the basis for the distinction by their lordships in the Gbedemah case, then it is our submission that under the Constitution, 1979 and the Constitution, 1992 the mandatory provisions for compliance by the parties and the penal consequences of non-compliance confer a mandatory exercise of judicial power to suppress any act or conduct susceptible to subverting the Constitution. In performing such functions, the courts are defending the Constitution in the exercise of judicial power. Thus the Constitution, 1992 can be defended by a court in the particular case before it under article 2(3), (4) and (5) or under article 130(1). The scope of judicial power is thus more extensive. The Supreme Court also has power to commit for contempt.59 So far we have found no instance of the use of the penal provisions of the Constitution, 1979 in the decided cases. We would therefore have to bide our time under the Constitution, 1992. Be that as it may, it is clear from the Gbedemah and related cases discussed above that the use of the original jurisdiction of the court to defend the Constitution in particular cases is also subject to the use of judicial power as a policy device to choose between conflicting social and public policy interests.

CONCLUSION

The Constitution of any nation is not a static document. It represents the ideals and aspirations of the people. It is supposed to outlive the framers and the citizens of the country at the time it is made. It is therefore dynamic. Citizens of an era which never adopted, enacted or promulgated the Constitution will be bound by it. Thus the Constitution can be said to be sovereign only by a tacit continual renewal of its supremacy. This theoretical concept of tacit concensus renewal has its jurisprudential objections.60 If the Constitution is law which the judiciary can identify and apply by enforcement or interpretation and defence, then is it the law as interpreted by the Supreme Court which is the law or the Constitution itself? In our opinion the Constitution itself is a guide to achieving the ideals and aspirations of a people. It is therefore a compendium of social, economic, cultural, spiritual and political compromise based on the economic foundation of society in the form of policy objectives of a people for the sharing of power and the distribution of wealth. It is a superstructure reflecting the economic structure of society.61 There is no general agreement on what the economic structure of society ought to be in the Constitution. But there is apparent continuing social stratification in our present day society. We therefore require an “impartial” arbiter between the contending class interests. This power position of an umpire, as we have shown, is best entrusted to an “independent” judiciary which exercised the judicial power of the State. In view of the very nature of the Constitution, the judicial power cannot be exercised even under the smokescreen of rules of interpretation, enforcement and defence without reference to the contending public or social policy underpinnings of the Constitution that reflect a society which is dynamic. Judicial power must therefore be dynamic.

Whether judicial power, its scope and effect in the enforcement, interpretation, and defence of the Constitution, 1992 would be an instrument in this regard will depend on what lessons have been learnt in the constitutional history of this country by the Supreme Court. It is our hope that our courts would shed off their conservatism and approach constitutional interpretation with a view to maintaining the stability and survival of the Constitution.
