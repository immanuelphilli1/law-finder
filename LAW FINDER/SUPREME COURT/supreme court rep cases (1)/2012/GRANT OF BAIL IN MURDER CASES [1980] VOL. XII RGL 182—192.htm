GRANT OF BAIL IN MURDER CASES [1980] VOL. XII RGL 182—192

BIMPONG-BUTA S. Y.

 

 

THE 1979 decision of Taylor J. (as he then was) in Brefor v. The Republic1 raises anew the very important issue as to whether or not a person charged with the offence of murder is entitled to be granted bail pending his trial.

Before the coming into force of the Constitution, 1979, there were two divergent views on the matter founded on the provisions of article 15 (3) (b) and (4) of the Constitution, 1969, and section 96 (7) of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30) as amended by section 2 of the Criminal Procedure Code (Amendment) Decree, 1975 (N.R.C.D. 309). It is provided by article 15 (3) and (4) that:

“15. (3) Any person who is arrested, restricted or detained, . . .

(b) upon reasonable suspicion of his having committed, or being about to commit, a criminal offence under the law of Ghana, and who is not released, shall be brought before a court within twenty-four hours.

(4) Where a person arrested, restricted or detained in any circumstance as is mentioned in paragraph (b) of the immediately preceding clause is not tried within a reasonable time, then, without prejudice to any further proceedings that may be brought against him, he shall be released either unconditionally or upon reasonable conditions including in particular such conditions as are reasonably necessary to ensure that he appears at a later date for trial or for proceedings preliminary to trials2”

However section 96 (7) of Act 30 provides3:

“96. (7) A court shall refuse to grant bail—

(a) in a case of treason, subversion, murder, robbery, hijacking, piracy or escape from    lawful custody; or

(b) where a person is being held for extradition to a foreign country”

One view of the legal effect of the above statutory provisions was given by Taylor J. in Dogbe v. The Republic.4 His lordship held that although the court was disabled by the clear and unambiguous provisions of section 96 (7) of Act 30 from granting bail in murder cases, having regard to the provisions of article 15 (3) (b) and (4) of the 1969 Constitution, it was quite clear that in all cases, including murder, if an accused on a murder charge, was not tried within a reasonable time, then he was entitled to be released on bail.5

It is to be noted that Taylor J.’s view that a person charged with murder could be granted bail notwithstanding the mandatory provisions of section 96 (7) is also supported by Aboagye J. in Prah v. The Republic6 but for different reasons. In this case, Aboagye J. held that the mandatory provisions of section 96 (7) were applicable only where the accused has been properly charged with murder. He therefore held that in the circumstances of the case, since the applicants, by their affidavit in support of the application for bail, had denied the charge of murder but no affidavit in opposition had been filed by the Republic, the applicants’ denial was deemed to have been admitted. Consequently the applicants could not be said to have been properly charged and would therefore be admitted to bail. The same conclusion was reached by Roger Korsah J. in Seidu v. The Republic7 where his lordship held that the “provisional charge” of murder was unknown to the criminal law of Ghana and that there being no proper charge of murder preferred against the applicants, they would be admitted to bail, the mandatory provisions of Act 30, s. 96 (7) notwithstanding. His lordship held that those whom the legislature intended section 96 of Act 30 to cover were those against whom, the charge of murder preferred could be said by the court to be supported by evidence which the prosecution professed to have, and not persons against whom there was no legally acceptable evidence connecting them with the charge of murder.

It is evident that in the Prah and Seidu cases, the High Court in effect held that the words of Act 30, s. 96 (7) were mandatory and completely disabled the court from granting bail in murder cases where an accused was properly charged with murder. In the Seidu case Roger Korsah J. held that the phrase “shall refuse” in Act 30, s. 96 (7) meant that the High Court should not exercise its power to grant hail in murder cases. Thus the Prah and Seidu cases do not in fact accept the interpretation earlier put on Act 30, s.96 (7) by Taylor J. in the Dogbe case.8

The exponent of this divergent view is Osei-Hwere J. who in Donkor v. The Republic9 sharply criticised and refused to accept the interpretation put on Act 30, s. 96 (7) by Taylor J. in the Dogbe case. In the Donkor case, the applicant was provisionally charged with the offence of subversion contrary to the Subversion Decree, 1972 (N.R.C.D. 90), s. 1 before a district court which remanded him in custody. He therefore applied to the High Court for bail because there had been unreasonable delay in prosecuting his case in terms of article 15 (3) (b) of the Constitution, 1969, which in the view of his counsel, prevailed over Act 30, s. 96 (7) which prohibits the grant of bail pending trial in cases of “subversion, murder, robbery, hijacking, piracy or escape from lawful custody.”

His lordship held that article 15 (3) (b) and (4) of the Constitution, 1969, was no longer operative because the constitution including Article 15 had been suspended by section 2 (1) of the 1972 Proclamation: that the Constitution, 1969, was no longer the supreme law but a mere enactment under the provisions of the Interpretation Act, 1960 (C.A. 4), s. 32 as amended by the 1972 Proclamation, s. 28 (3). He therefore held that even if article 15 (3) (b) and (4) were operative as a rule of law saved by section 3 (2) of the Proclamation, it was inconsistent with the later enactment, i.e. Act 30, s.96 (7) as amended by N.R.C.D. 309, which must, in accordance with the principles of construction of inconsistent sections of two statutes, be made to impliedly restrict or repeal in part the operation of article 15 (3) (b) and (4) of the Constitution.  His lordship therefore held that article 15 (3) and (4) would cover all cases of offences except those crimes specified in Act 30, s. 96 (7), i.e. subversion, murder, etc.

In the instant recent case of Brefor v. The Republic, Taylor J. criticised the decision of Osei-Hwere J. in the Donkor case.  He held that far from article 15 (3) (b) and (4) being inconsistent with Act 30, s. 96 (7) , they were enactments in pari materia empowering the

High Court to grant bail in murder cases in cases of unreasonable delay.

Now in the view of the writer, since the provisions of article 15 (3) (b) and (4) of the Constitution, 1969, have been re-enacted by article 21(3) (b) and (4) of the Constitution, 1979, and since under article 1 (2) of the Constitution, 1979 “This Constitution shall be the supreme law of Ghana and any other law found to be inconsistent with any provision of this Constitution shall, to the extent of the inconsistency, be void and of no effect” the divergent views as to the legal effect of Act 30, s. 96 (7) on the issue of grant of bail in murder cases is now of mere academic relevance.  It is suggested that notwithstanding the existence of Act 30, s. 96 (7) on the statute book, a person charged with murder and not “tried within a reasonable time” within the meaning of article 21 (4) (a) of the Constitution, 1979, is entitled to be granted bail pending his trial.

The question is what is meant by the phrase “not tried within a reasonable time” in article 21 (4) (a) of the Constitution, 1979? The article provides10:

“21.(4) Where a person arrested, restricted or detained in any circumstance as is mentioned in paragraph (b) of Clause (3) of this article is not tried within a reasonable time, then, without prejudice to any further proceedings that may be brought against him—

(a) he shall be released either unconditionally or upon reasonably conditions, including in particular such conditions as are reasonably necessary to ensure that he appears at a later date for trial or for proceedings preliminary  to trial…”

It should be pointed out that the meaning of the phrase “not tried within a reasonable time” in the analogous article 15 (4) of the Constitution, 1969, was considered by Taylor J. both in the earlier Dogbe and the later Brefor cases.  In both cases, his lordship refused to grant bail because it was not shown that the applicants had “not been tried within a reasonable time.” In the Dogbe case his lordship said11:

“In my view an unreasonably delay necessarily means that the person on whom it is incumbent to act has been unreasonable in not acting timeously as Lord Watson points out in the Hick case (supra). Such a person must necessarily be at fault.  Is the delay in trying the applicants the fault of the court or the Attorney-General, because these are the organs responsible for timeous proscution? I think not.”

In the Brefor case Taylor J. again quoted and relied on his definition of unreasonable delay in the Dagbe case.

It is quite clear that by this definition, his lordship did put a lot of premium on the existence of fault either on the part of the prosecution or the court.  Thus in the Brefor case, the applicants who had been in custody for more than three years on a charge of murder were refused bail because it was not the fault of the court or the prosecution that they have not been tried within a reasonable time.

It is suggested (with the greatest respect) that his lordship was in error in confining unreasonable delay to only cases of fault on the part of the trial court or the prosecution. It is suggested that by the very nature of the court process which admittedly is very slow12, the number of judges and magistrates available to hear cases, the number of court premises and earlier cases pending on the cause list, lack or difficulty of police or prison difficulties in obtaining transport to convey remand persons to and from courts, the failure of counsel to appear in court due to unavoidable circumstances and the very fact that a person facing a murder charge has been on remand for more than three years as happened in the Brefor case and lack of prospect for an immediate trial, are compelling reasons contributing to the fact that an accused person has “not been tried within a reasonable time” within the meaning of article 21 (4) (a) of the 1979 Constitution. In Gyakye v. The Republic13 the applicants on a murder charge applied for bail. Coussey J. (as he then was) having found that there was no indication as to when the trial of the accused would start and that it was unlikely that they would be tried soon, held that the applicants had not been tried within a reasonable time within the meaning of Article 15 (4) of the Constitution, 1969. Again, in Owusu v. The Republic14 Andoh J. held that three years' incarceration of the accused charged with abetment of murder without trial, meant that the accused had not been tried within a reasonable time as was required by article 21 (3) (b) and (4) of the Constitution, 1979, and that any argument to the contrary could not be taken seriously especially when the incarceration was said to affect the health of the application. Andoh J. clarified the position when he said:

“Awaiting trial for three years and more is clearly such a delay as by the Constitution, 1979, a court shall be bound to grant bail. It is a situation like this, an abuse tantamount to suppression or oppression, which the Constitution seeks to check or avoid.”15

The interpretation put on the words “not tried within a reasonable time” by Coussey J. in the Gyakye case and by Andoh J. in the Owusu case is in accord with criminal justice that a person should not be subjected to punishment in any form until he has been found guilty by a court of law. It certainly gives comfort to accused persons charged with murder who through no fault of any person find themselves languishing in jail for years on remand. Needless to say, the decision in the Gyakye and Owusu cases is more preferable to that in the Brefor case.
