THE PLACE OF THE SUMMONS FOR DIRECTIONS STAGE [1980] VOL. XII RGL 188—192

NII-APONSAH D. A.

 

THE purpose of this note is to throw a searchlight on the place and significance of the summons for directions stage in a civil trial.

It there happened that rival claims to the ownership of a parcel of land was pending before the court. The plaintiff by his solicitor had filed a summons for directions in which he had set out all the major controverted issues, as in fact should, with respect, be expected by any practitioner familiar with drawing up of issues for trial. To the surprise and disappointment of your writer, a rather senior legal practitioner took objection not on any of the usual grounds, e.g. not pleaded; conducive to futile result; irrelevant, etc. but on the pretext that all the major issues calling for specific findings of fact should be reduced into a single issue, to wit, whether or not the land was owned by the plaintiff or by the defendant.

Thus, according to that practitioner, all the findings of fact upon which the judge could objectively form the conclusion that the land belonged to the one or the other side should be dropped and the judge be left to say which side he favoured to win without being constrained to spell out the issues upon which he was to come to that ultimate conclusion.

To this writer's mind, a greater invitation to repeated miscarriages of justice one could never contemplate! That objection underscored the disturbing fact that even at the highest reaches of the profession, there must be some who are still at a loss as to the place and indispensable importance of the stage for summons for directions in a civil action. To be frank, most of us thought little of the importance of that stage when we were young practitioners. But with the advantage of maturity we are now in a position to assert without any trepidation that the stage for summons for directions is the most crucial one in the civil trial next to the skill of opposing counsel, the intelligence and clarity of the witnesses and the foibles, fairness and competence of the trial judge!

The scant regard which most practitioners cherish for that stage is shown by the fact that many defence counsel on whom summons for directions are served, take liberties with them and rush to appear in another court in respect of what appears to them to be "more pressing" matters. They little imagine that they could win or lose their clients' case through the content, omissions or couching of the issues for determination. To be clear about the rationale behind this stage of a civil action therefore we feel bound to emphasise the following points:

It is common place that the issues for trial form the first part of the summons after the invitation to attend the court or judge, and the second part is taken up by the orders which the court is required to make to further the trial. The orders segment sub-divides into: (a) call for an early trial and (b) a statement of the probable number of days the trial would occupy and (c) the kind of orders for costs to be agreed upon before the hearing—whether the costs should be in the cause with liberty to apply or costs in any event, etc.

All interlocutory orders that ought to be made to ripen the case for hearing are made at this stage, e.g. orders for discovery, interrogatories, surveys, etc. or for disposal of the case upon a legal argument provided the decision will dispose substantially of the point of law in the whole action: see Order 25, rr. 2 and 3 of the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954 (L.N. 140A).

The importance of these as ancillaries to due process cannot be over-emphasised but these need not detain us further, as the announced object of this piece is to mount the searchlight on the importance of the first stage in registering or achieving justice, not according to the subjective evaluations of a trial judge, but largely upon objective and verifiable set of facts elicitable from the morass of the evidence given—set in perspective by the judicial observations of the demeanour of the witnesses.

What, then, are the subtle considerations that make the statement, content and angle of the issues for determination so overridingly crucial:

(1) It helps a trial judge to do self-analysis of material upon which he might have been impelled to base his judgment. Asking himself whether he was really called upon to decide a point that forces itself on his mind would help him to take the plunge and delve into it cursorily or in extenso or exclude it entirely from his considerations. Thus, the issues not only check deviations, straying and a plunge into irrelevancies or officiousness.

(2) A deviation into an extensive consideration of non-issues itself would denote either a veiled bias or a failure of justice. By combating deviations therefore the issues help resist bias and consequent failure or miscarriage of justice. Thus, it helps check or detect impertinent digressions and makes it manifest whether a judgment has been against the weight of evidence or not. It helps the Bench in line with the issues at stake.

(3) The statement of issues compels the making of express findings on those issues, unless a fundamental point like capacity, so disposes of the substratum of all the other issues that further consideration of them would be plainly superfluous. In the absence of such fundamental finding there must be an express or specific finding on each point which has been agreed upon for determination. Where the judge fails to do so, his omission will automatically be exposed by a comparison of the topics he chose to deal with and the issues left untouched by him whether by accident, inadvertence or design.

(4) Where however the trial judge faithfully pronounces expressly on the agreed issues, this achievement is also conspicuously made manifest by such a comparison as already mentioned in the immediately foregoing point.

(5) The statement of issues can in no small way help in the arduous task of distillation of elusive rationes decidendi from the sometimes imperviable morass of obiter dicta.

(6) The statement of issues must limit the extent to which the cross-examination might legitimately range or ramble. If a cross-examination strays into non-issues whose extensive investigations at all might culminate in inconclusive or irrelevant determinations the judge ought to call the examining counsel's attention thereto and ask him to come to the issues or advise himself as to the fruitfulness pertinence or helpfulness of the course he was pursuing.

(7) Much of the precious time of the court would be saved by pertinacious regard being given to the issues before questions for cross-examination were couched. Thus, the statement of issues saves or must help save time and labour and speed up trials in our courts.

The foregoing considerations postulate that the issues must be set out with ample brevity and precision or such specificity as to leave no room for doubt as to what exactly the judge is required by the parties to ponder upon and settle. It must neither be ambiguous, prolix, pointless nor lead to indecisive results.

The requirements of justice also demand that the issues, which when found, must compel a decision one way or the other, must not to jumbled together or obscured with over-simplification such as whether or not A or B owns a disputed land. If such an over-simplified issue were adopted, the question would still remain what combination of findings had compelled the trial judge to reach his particular determination. If the issues are not set out with sufficient precision for him, he is left to use his discretion to pick and choose what best suited him or attracted him most and not necessarily those points whose findings would compel one decision or another.

The judge's predilections would thus dangerously be given too large a room to have its play. Instead of binding the judge, so to speak, to determine points on which the parties as such feel so sore, the judge would be left to indulge his ego to a certain extent which might be great or small. That was precisely the complaint of counsel for the appellant in the recent case of Allotey v. Quarcoo.1 Counsel argued inter alia that the trial judge had erred in introducing new issues for trial beyond the only one issue set down by the trial judge himself, i.e. whether a family meeting called in connection with five charges preferred against the respondent came to an end or not. In allowing the appeal, the Court of Appeal coram: Anin J.S.C. and Edusei and Mensa Boison JJ.A. held that the charge levelled against the trial judge was well-founded and justified. The court per Anin J.S.C. said:

"The eight new issues introduced by the learned judge and adjudicated upon by him in favour of the plaintiff-respondent considerably widened the area of controversy and resulted in the infringement of the fundamental rule laid down in such cases as Dam v. Addo [1962] 2 G.L.R. 200, S.C. that a court must not substitute a case proprio motu nor accept a case contrary to, or inconsistent with, that which the party himself puts forward . . . In the present case ... the opposing party took elaborate care to expose and attack the plaintiff's defective pleading and even twice moved for the striking out of the writ and claim for disclosing no reasonable cause of action. By his ruling on the motion, the learned judge himself acknowledged the correctness of most of the criticisms levelled at the plaintiff's pleadings and confined the trial of the action to the solitary factual issue set out . . . The parties had therefore every right to expect that the judgment would be confined to that one issue. Instead, the appellants have had the misfortune of being adjudged on new issues not previously pleaded by their adversary; and have in the event been condemned without an opportunity being afforded them to adduce evidence to meet the new issues stated in the judgment."

Since the parties themselves had agreed to the adoption of only one issue in Allotey v. Quarcoo, the introduction of new issues by the trial judge was, with respect, deservedly faulted by the Court of Appeal. But it is submitted that it would have been a different case if the trial judge had been left unconfined to any particular issue. It would not have been the trial judge's fault but that of the parties if the trial judge had been left unassisted, for in such an instance, he could deal only with questions put before him according to the best of his own inclinations. He might humanly have an oversight of one or more issues which might have exercised the parties most. But the human tendency towards oversights would be minimised if the statement of the issues were sufficiently and clearly set out for the guidance of all!

From the foregoing, it must become obvious how dangerous and subversive to justice to respond to that senior practitioner's invitation to over-simplify issues on a disputed land into whether or not A or B is the owner. It should also be clear that to omit an issue upon whose finding will turn the decision one way or the other, e.g. capacity, could be fatal!
