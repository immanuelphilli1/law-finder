LOCUS STANDI TO SUE FOR STOOL LAND MONEYS [1981-82] VOLS. XII & XIV RGL 204—209

Woodman Gordon R.

 

Gyamfi v. Owusu1 arose after stool lands had been apparently acquired by the Government of Ghana. The omanhene whose stool's lands were affected withdrew the claim to compensation made by his predecessor in office, and instigated three other persons to claim on the ground that the lands were their ancestral properties. As a result they were paid ¢1.5m., a substantial portion of which they passed clandestinely to the omanhene. The plaintiffs brought action for money had and received on behalf of the man against the three recipients, and the omanhene was joined as a co-defendant in his private capacity. The High Court gave judgment for the plaintiffs, ordering the defendants and co-defendant to pay into court the compensation they had received.2

One defendant and the co-defendant appealed. At this point an extraordinary defect in the apparent "acquisition" was revealed, as a result of which the appeal was allowed on grounds which had not been examined in the High Court.  However, the Court of Appeal also determined a number of questions of more general significance.  This comment is concerned primarily with these, although for completeness the immediate ground of the decision will be mentioned subsequently. The general issues fall under the heads of two questions: first, who is entitled to sue to recover compensation for stool land; and secondly, can a chief be sued for an account of the proceeds of stool land?

Were the plaintiffs competent to sue for the compensation? The defendants argued that by customary law only the stool occupant was entitled to bring an action for stool moneys. The court accepted that this was an established general rule.  In reply, the plaintiffs relied upon Kwan v. Nyieni,3 the leading decision on the power to sue on behalf of a family, which specified a number of exceptions to the general rule that only the head was entitled to sue.  The plaintiffs argued that the holding should be extended to actions on behalf of stools, and that, if this were done, they would fall within the exception whereby other members might sue to preserve the corporate property if the head (or chief) and some of the elders failed to take the necessary action. Charles Crabbe J.S.C., giving the principal judgment, seems to have been sympathetic to the argument. Archer J.S.C., in a briefer, concurring judgment, doubted whether family property could be safely equated with stool property, and held, "This court would therefore decline the invitation to extend the exceptions in Kwan v. Nyieni to stool property."

However, the plaintiffs' argument foundered on a further objection to their standing.  The defendants argued that everyone who might have been competent under customary law to sue for stool moneys, including even the chief, had been excluded by legislation.  They relied upon the Administration of Lands Act, 1962 (Act 123), s.17(1) of which provided:

"17.(1) All revenue from lands subject to this Act shall be collected by the Minister and for that purpose all rights to receive and all remedies to recover that revenue shall vest in him and... no other person shall have power ... to exercise any such right or remedy."

The Act defined "revenue" for this purpose very widely.  It provided that the minister was to pay all sums collected into a stool lands account, and made provision for disbursements from the account according to schemes to be formulated and published for the benefit of the localities in which the lands were situated.4 These provisions seemed clearly to deprive even the chief himself of the capacity to sue for sums such as those in issue in this case.  The court had no doubt that "revenue" as defined included these sums.5

Charles Crabbe J.S.C. discussed the effect on these provisions of the Constitution, 1969, art. 164. This required the "revenue" from stool lands to be paid into stool lands account, from which disbursements were to be made according to schemes of the same type as those provided for in Act 123. He held that the 1969 provisions superseded those of 1962 whenever they were inconsistent. However, the 1969 provisions did not state who was the proper person to collect the "revenue" or to make the disbursements, and therefore the 1962 authorisation of the minister to perform these functions must have remained in force. In contrast, Archer J.S.C. held that the 1969 provisions "substituted the Lands Commission for the Minister" in these respects. Whichever view is correct, it seems likely that the Constitution, 1979, art. 190(2) superseded the previous provisions totally by conferring the functions expressly on the office of the Administrator of Stool Lands.6  However, it has remained the statute-law from 1962 to the present day that state officers alone, not chiefs or other persons entitled under customary law, may sue for moneys paid or payable in respect of stool lands. It may now never be necessary to decide whether the rules in Kwan v. Nyieni should be extended to stools.

In the present case the statutory provisions had a curious result. They had clearly been infringed when money in issue had been paid by the State to the defendants.  This wrongful payment was likely to prevent the money from being disbursed under an approved scheme, which might have eventually benefited the stool community including the plaintiffs. However, according to the statute no-one but an officer of the State could put matters right by suing to recover the money from the defendants. The plaintiffs might perhaps have sued the minister or the Lands Commission to compel the performance of their obligations in respect of the money, but it is not clear whether a remedy would have been available to them.7

Assuming for the purpose of argument that the plaintiffs could have sued the co-defendant chief for stool moneys, could they have compelled him to account, a procedure which would have been necessary to the remedy sought? The holdings just discussed rendered this a hypothetical issue, but it nevertheless received some discussion. The court considered that the general rule that the head of a family could not be sued for an account of family funds, except after he had been removed from office, applied to the chief.8 However, the plaintiffs argued that the customary law had been changed by the Constitution, 1969, article 164 (1), which provided, "all stool lands in Ghana shall vest in the appropriate Stool on behalf of, and in trust for, the subjects of the Stool." They contended that this created a legally enforceable trust, whereby the chief as trustee became liable to be sued for an account by any of the beneficiaries.  Although this provision is no longer in force,9 there remain enactments declaring lands to be held  "in trust."10  The court rejected the argument largely on the ground that section 17 of Act 123, discussed above, excluded all private actions for "revenue" from stool lands. However, Archer J.S.C. also said in reply to the argument that article 164(1) "intended no such thing. The article merely declared what the customary law has been for ages." Thus this provision, and others analogous to it, do not create trusts in the strict common-law sense. They merely declare the general principle that the persons referred to should exercise their powers with due regard to the interests of those for whose benefit they act. The other  interpretation would have effected a radical change in the land law, and possibly have impeded the efficient conduct of government by  imposing extensive and detailed obligations on "trustees" which would have been legally enforceable at the instance of large numbers of beneficiaries. 

Thus the court concluded on a number of grounds that the plaintiffs could not sue the defendants for this money, even on the assumption that it had been properly paid to the defendants. In fact it had not been properly paid, because of a saga of irregularities. The three defendants had made a collusive, unfounded claim which should not have been accepted.  Even if the co-defendant, as paramount chief, had made the claim it should not have been accepted because such moneys are no longer payable to chiefs.

There was yet a further, prior difficulty: it was now discovered that the land was not validly acquired by the State.11 Evidence from an official of the Lands Department had stated that the acquisition was made by a legislative instrument under the Wild Animals Preservation Act, 1961 (Act 43). That Act does not confer powers of compulsory acquisition. The officer also apparently claimed that the State had acted under the authority of the State Lands Act, 1962 (Act 125).  However, it had not complied with the procedure required by that Act. The court also held that Act 125 could not have been used to acquire stool land because section 1 thereof conferred the power to acquire only "any land . . . other than land subject to" Act 123.12  With respect, this last holding should perhaps have been qualified. It seems not to have been brought to the court's attention that the State Lands Act, 1962 (Amendment) Decree, 1968 (N.L.C.D. 234) empowered the State to apply Act 125 to stool land by making a written declaration that special circumstances existed by reason of which it appeared expedient so to do.  However, despite this it seems clear that the State had not acquired the land in question. Therefore no compensation had been payable to anyone, not even to the minister under section 17 of Act 123.

The court expressed unease at having to reject the plaintiff's claim, since this could have enabled the defendants to retain money to which they were not entitled. After the lower court had given judgment for the plaintiffs, the defendants had been compelled to pay the money into court pending their appeal. The theoretically correct procedure to solve the problem would perhaps have been for the money to be repaid to the defendants; for the State to have sued them for its return on the ground that it was held on a constructive trust and was "had and received to the use of" the payer;13 and for the process of acquisition and payment of compensation to be started afresh. The court avoided this cumbersome and litigious course.  It recorded that the defendants' appeal was allowed, but ordered that the money should remain in court until a valid acquisition was completed. After this, the court indicated, an application by the Administrator of Stool Lands for the money to be paid into the appropriate stool land account would be favourably received.  Thus the court sought to unscramble the confused state of affairs in the case, having used it for some informative rulings on points of law concerning payments for stool lands.
