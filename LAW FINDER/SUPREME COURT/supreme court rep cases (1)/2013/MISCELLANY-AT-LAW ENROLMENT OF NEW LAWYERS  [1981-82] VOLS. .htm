MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: ENROLMENT OF NEW LAWYERS  [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 17—27

 

It has been customary for His Lordship, the Chief Justice, the Hon. Mr. Justice F. K. Apaloo, to deliver a speech at the formal meeting of the General Legal Council for the enrolment of new lawyers in the country. On that occasion, 17 December 1982, his lordship in a congratulatory speech, dealt with a number of issues affecting the judiciary vis-à-vis the revolutionary organs of the 31 December 1981 Revolution. He made trenchant observations on the P.N.D.C. Government's attitude to the judiciary and the law; he also dealt with the issue of whether in view of the challenges to the judiciary by the revolutionary organs of the Revolution, i.e. the P.D.C.s and the W.D.C.s, the traditional courts have lost their value and whether the legal profession has any future in Ghana. He also made some observations on the performance of the People's Tribunal set up under P.N.D.C.L. 24. The Review presents the edited speech of the Chief Justice.

Speech Delivered by the Chief Justice

We meet here today to discharge a statutory function. That duty was imposed on us by section 7 of the Legal Profession Act, 1960 (Act 32). And it is to hold a formal meeting to enroll and call to the Bar newly qualified lawyers.

I take this opportunity of welcoming into our midst our guests—those who did us honour by accepting our invitation. In a society which desires to be ruled by laws rather than men, this healthy interest in the law is encouraging. It is probable that to some of the guests we have here this morning, the law offers no particular attraction. And that your presence here this morning is to give moral support to a son or daughter, friend or relative who is being called to the Bar. It requires some effort to do even this and to all of you, I extend the warm welcome of the General Legal Council. I hope your interest in the law and in the administration of justice in this country will outlive the very short ceremony we have witnessed here this morning.

But my main task is to welcome the new members who have today joined our ranks and to share a few thoughts with them. The legal fraternity, like a natural family, is always the gainer by the addition to its ranks of new members and new talent. I congratulate you warmly not only for contemplating the entry into what we perhaps, immodestly refer to as the learned profession, but that you translated what may well have been your childhood dreams into deeds. For five long years most of you went through the arduous training that today justifies your entry into one of the oldest and respected professions. Very many congratulations indeed!

I must also congratulate your parents, guardians and any persons who encouraged you to achieve what you have achieved today. Some may have made personal sacrifices so that you may become what you are today. To them also go my warm congratulations. Like infant children, we all must, at some point in our lives, rely on the help and encouragement of others. My only prayer is that our new lawyers will all make a success of the profession and prove themselves worthy of the sacrifice.

Having successfully gone through your legal studies and have been called to the Bar, I cannot but assume that you have some grasp of the law. I would say, perhaps without disrespect, the rudiments of the law. At the Bar, your real learning of the law will begin—possibly with each case that you are confronted with. Human affairs vary with each man's face. So are his problems. These you must study afresh. No two cases are exactly alike. Each case must be studied together with its special problems. If you delude yourselves into thinking that the study of law ends with your call to the Bar, you cannot lay a surer foundation for professional failure. In other words, you must apply in the practice of the law, the same diligence and industry that you applied to your studies. Experience shows that many more cases are lost by insufficient preparation than by want of intrinsic merit.

I believe that today, as of old, many lawyers would want to test their legal skills by forensic practice at the Bar. But I do not address my exhortation of unceasing industry to them alone. Some of you will no doubt practise in these courts. Others may feel their legal talent better fitted them to be public officers, diplomats, company or corporation lawyers or even politicians. Wherever you find yourselves, do not forget what Longfellow wrote long ago, namely:

"Heights by great men reached and kept,

Were not attained by sudden flight.

But they, while their companions slept

Were toiling upwards in the night."

It is now necessary to turn to, what I would call, matters of the moment. I am sure, on an occasion like this, the citizens of this county would expect me to review the year and say how the legal profession has fared since the 31 December Revolution. When I addressed the law students of the University of Ghana during their Law Week in May this year, I attempted an analysis of what I saw as the attitude of the Provisional National Defence Council towards the existing legal system. It is impossible to conceive of any ordered society without law, even if that society is said to be undergoing a revolution. So the Provisional National Defence Council (Establishment) Proclamation, 1981, not only saved all laws in existence before the Revolution, but it continues the appointment of all judges and other judicial personnel with their pre-revolution powers and duties.

The Proclamation gave notice that public tribunals would be established to try specified offences. That intention was carried into effect by the passage of the Public Tribunals Law, 1982 (P.N.D.C.L. 24). That tribunal was to be not only a trial court but also a final court because its decisions are not subject to appeal or open to review either by the ordinary courts or indeed by the P.N.D.C. itself. Someone told me that unlike the ordinary courts, the public tribunal is a law unto itself. That really is not my point—the Chairman of the P.N.D.C. has said the public will be left to judge which of the courts is preferable and for that judgment, we should wait.

The point I make is that the P.N.D.C. has left practically untouched the legal system and the pre-revolution laws. I used the word "practically" advisedly, because P.N.D.C. Laws 1 and 19 creating the Citizens Vetting Committee and the Special Military Tribunal respectively, gave limited judicial powers to these two bodies. That aside, the P.N.D.C. has left the courts and the legal system alone. Indeed, on one occasion when I wrote to the government about the interference with court functions by armed personnel, I received the written assurance of the P.N.D.C., that these would be stopped.

I am afraid, I am bound to say that the promise of freedom from interference of the courts has not materialised. In spite of the legal guarantee provided by the Proclamation, and written assurances given to the judiciary, some persons and organisations have behaved and continue to behave as though there were no laws and that the courts had either ceased to exist or had no right to exercise their ordinary criminal or civil jurisdiction.

Some of the first effects of the Revolution were attacks on the courts mostly in landlord and tenant and other property cases. Tenants who lost their cases in court were able to get soldiers to stop the execution of the courts’ orders by beating up the magistrates who gave the orders and the staff who attempted to carry out the orders. This was mostly at the magistrates' level, but the High Courts had their share of the disturbance. In one gruesome case at Cape Coast, a chief who was a party in a chieftaincy dispute was taken away by soldiers as he left the High Court and shot outside the town. We never got to know what happened to his murderers.

A new element was later introduced into the situation. This was the belief, which soon became prevalent, that there was a P.N.D.C. directive that no tenant should be ejected. It has never been known to anyone in the judiciary whether the P.N.D.C. or anyone associated with it ever issued such a directive. In any case, the belief intensified the interference in the functioning of the courts and the attacks on the staff. People’s Defence Committee members and soldiers took it upon themselves to stop the execution of any order of recovery of possession. I had a petition from an eighty-year-old woman who, following a report made to Mr. Amartey Kwei, the erstwhile member of the P.N.D.C. by her tenant, was, together with her daughters, taken to Burma Camp and beaten for attempting to recover her room from the tenant after a successful law suit. One of Mr. Amartey Kwei's orders was that the house should be confiscated to the State and the tenant should now act as caretaker for the State while the woman and her children became tenants. That injustice has since been redressed.

Our magistrates, who were not aware of any P.N.D.C. directives to that effect, sought clarification from me. As a result of representations made to the government, the Secretary for Justice and Attorney-General, on the authority of the P.N.D.C., publicly refuted the claim that there was such a P.N.D.C. directive. However, this did not improve the situation and I still get reports of court orders being interfered with for the same reasons.

So far as I have said, the interference with the courts had been directed mostly at the magistrates' courts. Then in July, three High Court Judges were abducted from their homes and murdered in cold blood. This naturally threw the whole of the legal profession into a state of shock and panic because it seemed to foreshadow an organised attempt to eliminate judges. It led to a suspension of the sittings of all the courts and many of the superior court judges went overseas to recover from the shock. At least six of them have not yet returned and the working of the Superior courts has been greatly hampered thereby.

On the murder of the judges, I have been made aware of some criticism made of the judges as a group and the Chief Justice as its head. It was asked why the judges did not resign en bloc or at least put up a more strongly worded statement than they in fact did. I am prepared to concede that these questions were asked by well-meaning citizens who feel well disposed towards the judiciary.

But these questions are not difficult to answer. A person resigns from public service usually on a point of principle or if he happens to be a member of the government, because of his inability to subscribe to a major government policy for which he and his other government colleagues have collective responsibility. What issue of principle is involved in the murder of the judges? That a dastardly crime was committed, there was no doubt. That the perpetrators deserve to be identified and punished, seems clear enough. The government's publicly stated position was that these culprits would be traced and punished in accordance with the laws of this country. The Chairman of the P.N.D.C. gave public expression to his suspicion that the perpetrators might well be persons opposed to the government or persons with ostensible sympathy for it. He said neither would be spared. In response to public demand, the government set up an independent investigation board to trace the murderers so that they can be brought to book. If present rumours are anything to go by, this board has identified some of the culprits and the Attorney-General has statutory responsibility to invoke the legal sanction against these wrongdoers. This is now awaited.1 What point of principle is involved to justify either the Chief Justice or the judges walking out on their jobs? And what benefit to the public would result thereby?

The truth is that the ordinary citizens who daily invoke the assistance of the courts in protecting their rights would be the losers. The judges who are matured citizens are knowledgeable enough to understand these issues. And not one of the nearly 40 judges of the superior courts has resigned his office on account of the regrettable events of July last.

The other criticism of the judges in connection with the murders brought to my notice, is that the judges did not react by a strongly worded statement. Those who held this view cannot have realised both the anguish and dilemma in which all the judges were placed by this sad event. When the disappearance of the judges was made public, the judges at a hurriedly convened meeting appointed a delegation of nine of its members to meet the P.N.D.C. They did so on 3 July 1982 and expressed their shock and abhorrence at the abductions. The delegation was assured that the government, for its part, was doing its best to locate the whereabouts of the judges and set them at liberty. When their murders were announced on Sunday 4 July, the judges again convened an emergency meeting the next day, i.e. 5 July. It was there that their anguish was matched only by the dilemma in which they found themselves. It was plain the heinous crimes of murders were committed. And if the murderers were found and prosecuted as the Chairman promised, these trials would be conducted by judges.2 If the judges by a statement reacted in intemperate language, questions may arise if they were not all disqualified by reason of legal bias from participating in any future trials. As they saw it, the best course was to express their horror in guarded terms and to announce the suspension of normal judicial work for an indefinite period out of respect for the memory of their colleagues. This is what was done. If the judges by a very violent statement against the wrongdoers who were then unknown, disqualified themselves from trying them in the future, were new persons inexperienced in judicial work to be appointed purposely to try these culprits? I think these criticisms, perhaps well-intentioned, were based on a faulty knowledge of the facts. Even for the limited period that the judges stayed away from the courts, I received a number of petitions by ordinary citizens who complained that hardship was being caused to them by the non-sitting of the courts.

Now, following the murders, the interference with the functioning of the courts has developed into what may be described as its logical conclusion. It has now taken the form of the closing down of the courts by members of W.D.C.s and P.D.C.s. Their reasons are that they don't like the way the magistrates try cases or that the magistrates are not revolutionary enough. In one town, the W.D.C. demanded that a woman who had been accused of stealing a fowl should be banished from the town. The magistrate explained that, as the woman was a citizen of the town, she could not be so banished. This was one of the charges which led to the closure of his court; they said he was not trying P.D.C. cases well.

So far, five courts have been closed in this way and it was with some dismay that I learned that, in some cases, the police were present but did nothing to stop the closing down of the court. In one town, the W.D.C. members who closed the court gave the keys to the police chief there. Strangely enough, not only did the police chief refuse to give the keys to the magistrate, but he went as far as to demand the surrender of any duplicate keys that the staff might have. He said he would return the keys to the staff after the two sides had settled the matter! In another town, the police stood by because they said their duty was to maintain law and order and not to take sides. But one would have thought that preventing unauthorised persons from forcibly closing down a court in session would be classified as maintenance of law and order and not as taking sides.

Quite apart from these interferences with the courts, there have been assaults on lawyers. One lawyer was dragged out of a family tribunal and brutally beaten by soldiers. The soldiers had been instigated by that lawyer's own client! In one regional capital, a so-called progressive group invaded the chambers of the lawyers and took away all their briefs and other papers. All these matters were brought to the notice of the government.

I am glad to be able to say that the Chairman of the P.N.D.C. has publicly condemned these acts. However, the public denunciation has not had the desired effect for the simple reason that the criminals and perpetrators have not been punished. Experience shows that mere warning and admonition unaccompanied by strong actions is usually futile and tends rather to invite ridicule and contempt.

After the chairman's statement, a further development has taken place. In some places the W.D.C.s and P.D.C.s are holding their own courts. I am told that they prefer their own charges, adopt their own procedures and impose their own fines which sometimes run into thousands of cedis.

I have been asked whether these courts are lawful. My answer is, of course, that they are not. First of all, no law has set up P.D.C. courts. A court is legal only when it is created by law. Secondly, in a trial in the regular courts, a person knows the law he has infringed and the penalty he is likely to suffer. And he is given the opportunity to defend himself by counsel if he chooses to have one. None of these prerequisites exists in the P.D.C. courts. And no one knows what happens to the fines or to whom they are accounted for. Fines exacted by the ordinary courts are paid into the general revenue of the State. It is obvious that the so-called accused persons pay these fines for fear of physical molestation. If a person refused to pay such a fine or even to attend a P.D.C. court, he would be perfectly within his rights and the government should take steps to make it possible for loyal citizens to assert their rights against crude force.  

The basic philosophy of the Revolution is said to be to transfer power to the people. What is precisely involved in this has not been fully explained. A columnist of The Mirror of 30 October 1982, gave expression to his conception of it in what strikes me as a balanced article. If you will permit me, I will quote some of the more important things he said in some detail. He wrote:

"The concept of transferring popular power to the people is not unique to Ghana. Elsewhere in the world whenever the concept has been practised the idea has been to involve the greater majority of the people in the national decision-making process of their country.

In the early days of the 31 December 1981 revolutionary process, the Chairman of the P.N.D.C. emphatically re-echoed this idea. The way to achieve this noble goal is to ensure that the majority of the people are able to effectively influence the national decision-making process.

The popular concept of People's Defence Committees, etc., therefore, is to ensure that through these basic units of popular power, the central government can take decisions and promulgate laws which fairly reflect the popular sentiments and realities of the people at the local level.

Never has it been conceived that the basic units of popular power were to function as independent, self-contained units of power, with law-making authority rivalling that of the central government.

The basic units (P.D.C.s and W.D.C.s) find a common expression in the National Defence Committee (N.D.C.) whose role, besides co-ordinating the activities of the basic units, is to advise the P.N.D.C., the government of the land on the basis of information and suggestion fed to it (N.D.C.) by the basic units.

The P.N.D.C., upon such advice, accordingly promulgates relevant laws either dismissing corrupt public officials, closing down public institutions or setting up new ones at the district or national level.

This is the only known and accepted way in which the people are supposed to take part effectively in the decision-making process of the country.

It is on the basis of this, I believe, that the P.N.D.C. has since its inception, enacted series of laws and issued directives whose purpose it is to spell out and clearly define the legal boundaries within which affairs of State are to be transacted.

Anything short of this is lawlessness and is not in conformity with any known procedure anywhere else in the world. Recent events, however, seem to suggest that this splendid idea of involving the people in the exercise of national power is being misconstrued to mean that the basic units are to constitute localized power units, self-contained and independent, with power to enact laws, etc. This is most unfortunate.

It has even been suggested recently by some people that the P.N.D.C. is only a caretaker government, which at the appropriate time will be expected to hand over power to the people. Whatever this means is yet to be effectively explained.

It is in furtherance of this mistaken belief that several groups of individuals have taken actions which, in fact, should have been taken only by the P.N.D.C. upon the advice of the basic units.

It must be clearly realised that history has as yet not produced a society in which there is no central government. Revolutionary governments are no exception to this rule . . .

All revolutions have the aim of transferring power to the people, but this, it is suggested, is effected by involving the people in decision-making, and not by creating pockets of authoritative power units with the same powers and functions as the central government.

No successful revolution can be made without a firm, dynamic and powerful and effective central power. That power must, of course, reflect that people's power by responding to the basic units, not by abandoning power to them.

It is in the light of this that the P.N.D.C. is being called upon to come out early with defined guidelines clearly describing the legal boundaries within which the basic units are supposed to function. It must also clearly indicate what it means by transferring power to the people. It is suggested that the practice whereby P.D.C.s and W.D.C.s are able to dismiss State employees, close down institutions unilaterally, is unacceptable. It is further suggested that in a revolution it is not only necessary to do the right thing; it is even more vital that the right things are done in the right way."

Forgive me if I have inflicted a columnist's view on you in too much detail. But the writer seems to me to have delivered himself of matters peculiarly appropriate to a proper appraisal of the current events in this country. He has also put up a point of view, which you may think, makes an intelligent contribution to the contemporary politics of Ghana—a viewpoint that can be looked at with profit. It certainly would be unwise to dismiss it lightly. I invite you all to read this article, ponder over it and conclude for yourselves which of these sentiments you agree with or which of them you find yourself in disagreement with. As lawyers, you have been trained to do objective analysis of issues before pronouncing a judgment.

I have been asked the question whether, in view of the challenges to the judiciary, the courts have lost their value and whether the legal profession has any future in Ghana. I said at the beginning that I would attempt to peer into the future of the profession. I am no crystal-gazer and can only judge by experience and observation.

In the first place, much of what is going on, which some people describe, not inaccurately as lawlessness, is the result, not of any genuine disaffection with the courts, but of gross ignorance. The ignorance is well illustrated by this incident.

A writ was issued by a plaintiff claiming about ¢200.000 from a corporation. The corporation's solicitor, realising that the corporation had a bad case, advised that it should negotiate with the plaintiff and settle the matter out of court. This was agreed and while the solicit or was negotiating with the plaintiff's solicitor, he was summoned to go to the P.N.D.C. Information Office. Obviously, the plaintiff had gone to seek extra-legal solution to the problem. When the corporation’s solicitor went to the information office, the soldiers asked him to produce the money. What money? he asked. The ¢200,000 that was to be paid to that man, they replied. But, he said, no agreement has been reached. They did not understand, so, finally he produced a copy of the writ which bore the figure of ¢200,000 and asked, "Is this what you mean”? The soldiers said "Yes." And he explained to them that that was only a writ showing the amount which the aggrieved person was claiming from the corporation. The matter had not been settled yet. It has not even gone to court at all!

But talking of ignorance, listen to this story. I am told that during demonstrations against professionals in Kumasi, one placard read, "ALL PROFESSIONALS ARE FOOLS." Another near by read, “WE ARE ALL PROFESSIONALS.”

The second fact which needs mention in this connection is what I have already said, that the people who are disturbing the functioning of the courts are only a small fraction of the population.

Finally, it seems to me, what is going on is largely the result of economic necessity. Ghanaians are suffering great economic hardship and, as is well known, a hungry man is ruled by the need to survive and not by reason or considerations of fairness. If a tenant cannot pay his rent and his landlord gets a court order to take the room from him, then (regardless of the reasonableness or otherwise of the court order) he will welcome anyone who says there is a P.N.D.C. directive that no tenant should be ejected. Also, if, in these hard times, a man loses a case and his property is to be sold but there are armed soldiers ready to stop the sale, he will turn to them regardless of whether the court’s decision is fair or not. It is simply a question of survival and not a loss of confidence in the courts.

I have been asked by someone to assess the performance of the public tribunal. My judgment may well be coloured. However, my first reaction is that it has not, in the least, diminished the popularity and importance of the regular courts even in criminal matters. Although its activities get the headlines, it is doing only about one-thousandth of the work being done by the regular courts. But as it is not subject to supervision by the courts and there is no appeal from its decision it is impossible to make a fair evaluation of the quality of their decisions. I think the persons best suited to pass a judgment on these tribunals are either the persons who witness their proceedings or anyone who has access to their records. I do not feel qualified to do so.

It is my hope that all these difficulties in the way of the profession will clear with time. The ship is indeed rocking and reeling at this moment but that must not be allowed to portray a gloomy future for the legal profession. No nation can afford to do without the institutions of the judiciary, law and lawyers. Even now, we have persons described as progressive lawyers who are apparently acceptable.

It is true that lawyers often come in for criticism especially during times of change. Some of the criticism is deserved and I have myself, for example, deplored the activities of some lawyers in their handling of insurance claims. However, by and large, they are indispensable instruments for achieving a fair balance between the needs of society and protection of the individual.

Well, ladies and gentlemen, this is not intended to be a real policy speech, or what in American parlance will be "a state of the Union" message. It is only to be a welcome and congratulatory address to our young lawyers. Before I am done, let me make this observation which I have consistently made to new law graduates. You are heirs to a great heritage and it is not an unreasonable ambition to have an eye on history, to reflect what reputation you will acquire and by what you will be remembered hereafter. Most of the giants of the profession that we met are no more. Sooner or later, those of us who are temporarily entrusted with responsibility will pass it on to you. Each of you must put to himself or herself the question "by what would I wish to be remembered?" I trust the profession will treat you kindly, so that when you leave it as we all must do some day, you will leave behind a reputation which future generation of lawyers would be proud of.
