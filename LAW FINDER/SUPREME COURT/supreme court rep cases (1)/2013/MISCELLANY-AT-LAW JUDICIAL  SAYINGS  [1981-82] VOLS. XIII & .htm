MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: JUDICIAL  SAYINGS  [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 14—17

 

THE Review introduces, as part of the Miscellany-at-Law, commencing from this volume, memorable and very short comments by some judges of the superior courts, on various aspects of the law. Here we sample some thought-provoking comments by some judges on matters like technical objections, ingratitude, official good faith, judicial notice of facts, old widow of a customary marriage, demeanour of a witness, cost of living, marriage standards, findings of facts of a commission of inquiry, rights of the individual, injustice, parent and child relationship and divorce.

Cost of Living

“The wages of the lower income group is quite inadequate to withstand the strain and stress of the rising cost of living. It is not a matter of choice, but of forced circumstances, that today the so-called lower class live beneath the subsistence level and, at best, from hand to mouth.”—per Osei-Hwere J., as he then was, in Adabo v. Ghana Consolidated Diamonds Ltd., High Court, Cape Coast, 19 June 1979, unreported.

"The cost of living has reached such a high pitch that the average earnings of the average person would seem to be insufficient for the essentials of life. To lead a decent life now, the average worker, not self-employed, must either engage in additional legitimate trade or business outside his normal work or have recourse to dishonest means to augment his earnings.”—per Taylor J., as he then was, in Haruna The Republic, High Court, Tamale, 8 December 1978; digested in [1980] G.L.R.D. 19.

Demeanour of a Witness

“It is not sufficient for a court to say that ‘from the demeanour of the witness the court, finds his testimony unreliable.’ The demeanour which actuated this impression must be expressed . . . To give the carte blanche to a court to accept or reject the testimony of a witness upon a reason that is unexpressed, would, in my view, be to pander to injustice."—per Roger-Korsah J. in Mansah v. Donkor, High Court, Kumasi, 11 January 1980; digested in [1980] G.L.R.D. 91.

Divorce

" Indeed in making the total breakdown of marriage beyond reconciliation as the sole key to divorce, the law has shown the strongest desire as far as it can in maintaining the sanctity and indissolubility of the marriage bond.”—per Sarkodie J. in Danquah v. Danquah [1979] G.L.R. 371 at p. 377.

Findings of Commission of Inquiry

"While we concede that the government was not bound by the findings and recommendations of the commission of inquiry, it is equally not permissible in law for the government to purport to act on a finding or recommendation where none exists.”—per Edusei J. A. in Quayson v. Attorney-General, Court of Appeal, 20 July 1981; digested in [1981] G.L.R.D. 22.

Individual Rights

"It is not the intention of the State to take away the fundamental rights of the individual without just cause. It is the protection of such rights which make the individual feel like belonging to the State."—per Ampiah J. in Armah v. Lands Commission [1979] G.L.R. 79 at p. 83.

"The court in the execution of its duty to protect the citizen's liberty always proceeds on the well-known principle, . . . of the primary necessity in the administration of the law to establish a healthy balance between the need to protect the community against crime and the need to protect individual citizens against abuse of executive power."—per Akufo-Addo C.J. in State v. General Officer Commanding the Ghana Army; Ex parte Braimah [1967] G.L.R. 192 at p. 200, C.A. (full bench).

Ingratitude

"The judge was surely not commenting on the criminal responsibility of the appellant but on his moral responsibility towards his benefactor. But ingratitude is never an ingredient in a criminal charge."—per Cecilia Koranteng-Addow J. in Mensah v. The Republic [1979] G.L.R. 523 at p. 535.

Injustice

"Any country that countenances injustice may achieve some temporary respite; . . . such respite is illusory and the nation is without doubt poised on a path that may lead to violence. The peace and tranquillity that it needs for orderly progress and development must surely elude it as long as it is capable of tolerating injustice”—per Taylor J.S.C. in Kwakye v. Attorney-General Supreme Court, 10 November 1981; digested in [1981] G.L.R.D. 87.

Judicial Notice of Facts

"To take judicial notice of a fact, . . . the judge has to be convinced that the matter is so notorious as not to be the subject of a dispute among reasonable men or that the matter is capable of immediate accurate demonstration by resort to readily accessible sources of indisputable accuracy . . . World inflation is a matter of public notoriety but the extent to which each country is affected is not a matter of which judicial notice can be taken."—per Cecilia Koranteng-Addow J. in Mensah v. The Republic [1979] G.L.R. 523 at p. 538.

“It is common knowledge that the economic conditions in the country at the moment have reached a stage where the average person is finding life really unbearable. The matter is so notorious that I propose to take judicial notice of it."—per Taylor J. in Haruna v. The Republic, High Court, Tamale, 8 December 1978; digested in [1980] G.L.R.D 19.

"One fact which is judicially noticed is that in the traditional set up of this country, the celebration of a festival is an important customary function in which the chief of the area concerned, plays a vital indispensable role." —per Twumasi J. in Hackman v. Obresi V, High Court, Cape Coast, 25 September 1981; digested in [1981] G.L.R.D. 61.

Marriage Standards

"The trial judge attempted in his judgment to set new standards of conduct and morality higher than those set up in the Marriage Ordinance, Cap. 127 (1951 Rev.). What is relevant is the standard set in the Marriage Ordinance."—per Jiagge J.A. in In re Estate of Agyepong; Poku v. Abosi, Court of Appeal, 6 July 1981, unreported.

Official Good Faith

"Official good faith cannot in the administration of the criminal law be a substitute for evidence and proof "—per Akufo-Addo C.J. in State v. General Officer Commanding the Ghana Army; Ex parte Braimah [1967] G.L.R. 192 at p. 200 C.A. (full bench).

Parent and Child Relationship

"The mere relationship of parent and child is not sufficient to show pecuniary loss, and does not even raise a presumption of such loss. The question of dependency is one of fact and cannot be presumed from the legal obligation to support."—per Andoh J. in Vormawor v. Akpedo, High Court, Ho, 6 June 1978 unreported.

Technical Objections

"I think that when technical objections which do not go to the root of the matter stand in the way of justice, they must be swept aside like so many impediments obscuring the paths to justice."—per Roger Korsah J. in Adjei v. Foriwaa, High Court, Kumasi, 18 March 1980; digested in [1981] G.L.R.D. 32.

Widow of Customary Marriage

"The defendant is now old and is deteriorating in health. There is no question that she could remarry and it appears she has no intention of taking a formal divorce from her husband's family. It would therefore, be unfair and against good conscience in such circumstances for the successor or the husband's family to divorce her formally and give her a send-off.”—per Sarkodie J. in Hayford v. Moses, High Court, Sekondi, 8 November 1979; digested in [1980] G.L.R.D. 81.
