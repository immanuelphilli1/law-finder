FOUNDING SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE ON LICENCE, UNJUST ENRICHMENT AND ESTOPPEL* [1981 & 82] VOL. XIII & XIV RGL 135—166

SAMPSON OWUSU

 

I INTRODUCTION

OUR indigenous system "abhors greed and ill-gotten gain."1 In explaining this basic tenet of our customary law Ollennu J. (as he then was) said:

". . .a question which in Ga folklore the chief and his elders put to Mr. Spider, who, after he had sat upon a tree on his land and watched Mr. Squirrel make farms on the land, quickly jumped down as soon as the crops were ready for harvesting and claimed ownership of the farms. The chief and the elders asked him: "Tsono asoo ni anaa nmo?—meaning literally: `Can a person just sit on a tree on his land and then become the owner of farms made by others on that land?"2

It is therefore startling to discover that the defendant-appellant (hereafter called the defendant) in Quagraine v. Adams3 obtained judgment to recover her land together with all the improvements effected on it by the plaintiff at the instance and with the encouragement of the defendant. The Court of Appeal sought to rationalise and justify the award of compensation in lieu of specific performance on the doctrine of licence, unjust enrichment and proprietary estoppel. The object of this article is to examine some aspects of these concepts with a view to establishing that on the authorities and in principle the plaintiff should have been awarded specific performance.

The facts in Quagraine v. Adams were these: The plaintiff who was orphaned at a tender age, was informally adopted by the mother of the defendant. Consequently, both parties lived and grew up as brother and sister. At a family meeting, the plaintiff who had had a successful practice as a pharmacist intimated that he ardently needed a plot to put up a dwelling-house. The sister, the defendant, offered to convey to him her plot of land, on the understanding that other members of the family would use and enjoy the house when completed. She consented to his starting the building and assured him that she would in due course get the land conveyed to him.  She demanded and the plaintiff paid her ¢21 as compensation for her economic trees which were destroyed before the building could commence.

On the faith of this, the plaintiff started to build in 1969, and the defendant was aware of his building activities on the land. she waited till the six-bedroom house had reached roofing level, when she demanded that the name on the building plan should be changed to that of a female member of the family. This was refused. She then stopped the construction, and at an ensuing family meeting in 1970, she requested the plaintiff to submit an account of his expenses on the building for reimbursement. A bill for ¢8,000 as the market value of the ¢3,000 spent, was presented in 1974 after protracted negotiations and solicitations by other family members to get the defendant to rescind her decision had failed.  In a claim for specific performance or in the alternative damages for ¢3,000, which was amended with the court's leave to ¢8,000, the trial court allowed the claim for ¢8,000 and held that since there was no enforceable contractual relationship, specific performance could not lie. On appeal by the defendant, her counsel contended that since there was no contract, as determined by the trial court, the plaintiff was a trespasser and that the defendant could not have been in breach of any contract to attract the award of ¢8,000 damages.

II LICENCE AS A DEFENCE TO TRESPASS

As was expected the Court of Appeal did not find it difficult to dismiss the contention that the plaintiff was a trespasser. The other two Justices of Appeal did not even find the need to comment on that frivolous claim. They, however, expressed approval for the views of their brother Mensa-Boison J.A. who, relying on Thompson v. Park said4:

". . . the law is clear beyond argument that at common law until a licence is revoked a licensee does not commit trespass if he enters and remains on the land for the purpose agreed upon, even if, as in the present case, the licence was gratuitous."

Undoubtedly this is in concert with common sense and principle.  It sounds unconscionable for one to grant a permission for a defined activity and then turn round to found a claim on that very activity for which permission was granted.  Thus in Timber & Transport Sawmilling Co., Ltd. v. Kataban Timbers Co., Ltd.5 the Court of Appeal referred to the ruling of the trial court to this effect:

" . . . up to July 1963, Gliksten had neither withdrawn their earlier consent to the feeder road project nor specifically objected to the operation of that project by the plaintiffs . . . It follows that the plaintiffs were not trespassers . . ."

Having thus rightly dismissed the contention that the plaintiff was a trespasser, the Court of Appeal had to unravel the knotty issue of the award of ¢8,000 damages in lieu of specific performance.  This problem became compounded by the fact that the Court of Appeal upheld the finding of the trial court that there was no contract.  Hence the need for a search for a basis other than a breach of contract to buttress the award of ¢8,000.  Jiagge and Mensa-Boison JJ.A., proceeded on the doctrine of proprietary estoppel while Edusei J.A., based himself on unjust enrichment.  Incidentally, the plaintiff did not cross-appeal on the trial court's refusal to award him specific performance.  This could be one of the reasons why the doctrine of licence was only considered as a defence to trespass.  That it is possible to found specific performance on licence6 was not adverted to. The question then is could the plaintiff have been given specific performance on this doctrine which has been described as equitable licence?

Equitable Licence

Licence is a permission to do what would otherwise be unlawful. It is not an easement because it does not require a dominant tenement and as an interest in land, it can only exist in equity7 and may confer a general right to occupy land.8 It differs from a lease in that it does not  purport to effect any transfer of common law interest in land,9  hence it is said that rights of a licensee are not adverse to that of his licensor compared with that of a lessee and a lessor.10 Ordinarily, a licence, unlike a lease. would not envisage the actual taking of an exclusive possession of property by the licensee.11 It can take the form of a permission to erect and use an advertisement hoarding12 to view a race13 or the letting of bookstalls on a station platform.14 Licence has been categorised into: (1) a bare licence; (2) licence coupled with a grant and (3) contractual licence. A bare licence is granted gratuitously, and even if it is under seal, it can be revoked at any time with impunity.15 Licence may be merely an ancillary to a grant of an interest16 in land. Such a licence will be irrevocable17 if the interest so granted is properly and effectively transferred. The third classification, contractual licence, has provoked a lot of discussion18 and seems to introduce a new19 concept in the law of property.

This arises where a bare licence or a licence not coupled  with a grant is supported by a consideration. At common law such a contractual licence can be revoked on a reasonable notice, and the licensee ejected before the expiration of the period contemplated by the licence. The licensor does not incur any tortious liability if after the expiration of the notice he uses reasonable force to eject the licensee.  Since he has no interest in the land he should not remain there after he has beeen duly asked to leave.  He could, however, sue for a breach of contract. One such action would have, in the estimation of Phillimore L.J., attracted a contemptuous award of 6d.,20 admission fee to cinema in those days.  This is the nature of the right of a ticket holder at a cricket match or at a cinema show. In Wood v. Leadbitter21 the plaintiff, who paid for a ticket for admission to a racecourse, was wrongfully ordered to leave.  When he refused he was forcefully ejected.  He failed in an action for assault.

Equity took a contrary stance and afforded a better protection to such a licensee.  This equitable rule has its origin in Frogley v. Earl of Lovelace22 which provided the basis for the ruling in Hurst v. Picture Theatres Ltd.23 to the effect that if a man has paid for his seat at a place of entertainment it is not competent for the proprietor of the place to withdraw the licence and eject the spectator if he has entered and behaved himself quite properly. Injunction would lie under such circumstances to restrain the proprietor (licensor) from revoking the licence. Since equity would regard as done that which ought to be done, in the eyes of equity, the affairs of the licensor and licensee should be regulated as if the injunction had already been granted.  The licence, therefore, becomes irrevocable and the paid spectator cannot be ejected even with reasonable force. Any interference with his person in an attempt to throw him out, even after he has been given sufficient notice to leave, would be condemned in damages for assault and in appropriate case an injunction would lie.

This principle of equity was found to be in direct conflict with Wood v. Leadbitter, from which the facts of Hurst's case are hardly distinguishable.  In Hurst's case the plaintiff paid for a ticket to watch a cinema show; when he was requested to leave in the mistaken belief that he had entered without paying, he refused and was ejected.  He succeeded in an action for assault and false imprisonment. The English Court of Appeal could have simply resolved (as it eventually did), this conflict on section 25 (11) of the Judicature Act, 187324 by giving precedence to the rule of equity.

As Buckley L.J. said25:

"The position of matters now is that the Court is bound under the Judicature Act to give effect to equitable doctrines. The question we have to consider is whether, having regard to equitable considerations, Wood v. Leadbitter is now law, meaning that Wood v. Leadbitter is a decision which can be applied in its integrity in a Court which is bound to give effect to equitable considerations.  In my opinion, it is not."

Buckley L.J. was, however, not content with this; he felt the urge to grapple with this obstacle that only  a licence coupled with a grant is irrevocable. His lordship side-stepped this road-block by putting an undue strain on the word “grant” to include the “right to see"26:

“That which was granted to him was the right to enjoy  looking at a spectacle, to attend a performance from its beginning to its end . . . His right to go into the building was something given to him in order to enable him to have the benefit of that which had been granted to him, namely, the right to hear the opera, or see the theatrical performance, or see the moving pictures as was the case here. So that here there was a licence coupled with a grant."

To clothe a person who merely has a right to enjoy the sight of a particular spectacle on certain premises with an interest in those premises is definitely to stretch the word "grant" beyond permissible limits. But the innovation is most welcome. In the view of the Court of Appeal in Hurst’s case, Wood v. Leadbitter turned on a point of pleading in that it was an action for assault; the old system of pleading could not have allowed a contractual issue on which an injunction could have been grounded to be added.

The germ of this new doctrine seems, therefore, to lie in the reasoning of the Court of Appeal in Hurst’s case that such a licence involves, ". . . a contract not to revoke the licence until the play had run to its termination."27 This being a negative term in the contract, injunction can issue to prevent its breach.

There is a considerable force in these arguments in favour of this new principle. But it had not been received favourably.28 It was subjected to an incisive condemnation by Latham C.J. in the  Australian case of Cowell v. Rosehill Racecourse Co., Ltd.29 The learned Chief Justice of Australia in pointing out the reductio ad absurdum of Hurst’s case remarked:

“ The right to see a spectacle cannot, in the ordinary sense of legal language, be regarded as a proprietary interest. Fifty thousand people who pay to see a football match do not obtain fifty thousand  interest in the football ground . . . The Statute of  Frauds would be applicable. A  person who bought a reserved seat might be held to have what could be called 'a term of hours' in the seat.”

There is nothing inconceivable in having one million interests in a football ground constructed to one million seating capacity if that is the only possible means of avoiding injustice.  It, however, appears from a clear understanding of the nature of this "right to see" that the injunction in this particular context does not necessarily or invariably operate to confer a proprietary right30 as such.  It is a device used to give an effective and more efficacious relief to prevent wanton disregard of one's promise.31

This fact renders superficial the argument that an injunction would be an ineffective relief unless it is possible for the Chancellor to be at the elbow of the licensee.32 For the essence of the doctrine does not lie in the issuance of injunction which is merely employed to buttress the award of substantial damages for assault. The dissenting view of Evatt J. in Cowell's case lends colour to this point. The learned judge said that a contrary rule would enable the licensor to take advantage of his own breach to buttress his claim that the licensee was a trespasser.

The problem of overcrowding and the need to enable the licensor-proprietor to refuse entry in the interests of the "safety of the audience as a whole or in order to secure observance of the law without subjecting the licensor to the possibility of numerous actions for assault" as envisaged by Latham C.J. is undoubtedly well-founded, but can be easily resolved on the discretionary nature of equitable remedies. If there are countervailing factors which make it unreasonable to presuppose the availability of injunction then since the court would take such factors into consideration, and on those grounds may not grant it,33 the basis for an action for assault would be non-existent.

The question of complying with the Statute of Frauds would not  be an insurmountable issue.  For the courts' approach to this statute has been pragmatic and where necessary, the courts have contradicted the express terms of a statute to advance its object.34 Needless to say, the authorities35 have established, beyond doubt, that the Statute of Frauds does not apply to licences.

It is submitted that Hurst's case represents good law and the Ghana courts should not hesitate to follow it. Bannerman J., in allowing an appeal from a decision of the Kumasi Circuit Court which affirmed the findings of the trial local court that a grant of land to the plaintiff-appellant by the defendant-respondent was a licence and that the respondent was entitled to revoke the licence and to deprive the appellant of the land, said36:

"In equity, a licensee who has given valuable consideration is treated as though he had a valid grant and since the Supreme Court of Judicature (Consolidation) Act, 1825 it is even irrevocable."

This principle has received the blessing of Viscount Simon in the House of Lord's case of Winter Garden Theatre (London), Ltd. v. Millennium Productions Ltd.37 Luganda v. Services Hotel Ltd.38 shows that where the licensee has been wrongly evicted a mandatory injunction can issue, where the circumstances demand, to facilitate his re-entry.  In Verrall v. Great Yarmouth Borough Council39 the Court of Appeal had no compunction in awarding specific performance to enforce a licence.  The Conservative Party dominated council contracted to hire a hall for two days' use to the National Front. Subsequently, the council became dominated by the socialists who purported to revoke the licence. The court was not restrained by such restriction as the transient nature of the contract, impossibility of constant supervision and the fact that specific performance does not lie for the enforcement of a contract which does not confer an interest in land.  For it was realised, as Lord Roskill put it, that,40 "it is the duty of the court to protect, where it is appropriate to do so, any interest whether it be an estate in land or a licence by injunction or specific performance as the case may be."

Thus on the basis of the doctrine of contractual licence the plaintiff in Quagraine's case could arguably have procured specific performance, a more efficacious remedy than the award of ¢8,000. It should, however, be noted that for such a licence to be irrevocable, it "must have the necessary qualities of a contract, binding upon the parties: It must be supported by consideration, and must in other respects be the subject of a contract."41 Lack of consideration in Quagraine's case was and would have been an unfortunate handicap, just as it was a formidable obstacle in the way of the plaintiff in Crabb v. Arun District Council42 in the pursuit of an equitable remedy.

Consideration as a requirement for the creation of contract at common law serves to prove the intention of the promisor to be bound legally.43 But consideration has been relegated to the role of a formal44 and not substantive requirement.  For this requirement has been insisted upon at the expense of its main object, i.e. intention to be bound.45 Where there is a manifest intention to be bound and there is reliance on it, it is suggested that equity should not doggedly follow the law, but rather give effect to the intention, if that is the only way to avoid injustice.46 After all equity has been able to make six exceptions to its maxim that equity would not aid a volunteer.47 Since the substance but not the form of a particular transaction would fascinate the Chancellor, if the intention to be bound is evident then lack of consideration, which could then come in as a matter of formality, should not be allowed to defeat that intention. Lord Denning M.R.'s dictum in Vaughan v. Vaughan48 tends to support this conclusion. The learned M.R. said49:

"To establish a contractual licence there must be a promise which is intended to be binding; and which is either supported by consideration, or is intended to be acted on and in fact acted on: see Dillwyn v. Llewelyn and Plimmer v. Wellington Corporation."

In Quagraine's case the defendant allowed the coconut trees on her land to be cleared for a compensation of ¢21 which was paid by the plaintiff and received by her.  That was an eloquent testimony of her intention to be bound, and the court could have proceeded on this to found a contractual licence, capable of attracting an equitable remedy, such as specific performance or mandatory injunction.

At this formative stage it is rather too early to attempt a  definite sketch of the full contours of this doctrine. But it can be stated wit h some confidence that the doctrine arises where by virtue of a contract50 enforceable at common law one acquires a right to enter and remain on or use the land of another for a defined purpose, without conferring on the licensor either expressly or impliedly51 the right of revocation during the period contemplated by the parties. Equity intervened in this regard only to supplement the ineffective common law remedy of damages, without any pretence at establishing a new right not congnisable at common law. Where one cannot, therefore, on common law principles, establish a contractual nexus he cannot sue for an  equitable remedy to protect his licence, unless in the alternative the circumstances  justify the invocation of the doctrine of proprietary estoppel (to which we will soon advert.) Where the ingredients of proprietary estoppel are present the licence thereby conferred cannot be countermanded. Injunction would lie to restrain the licensor from revoking or ejecting the licensee, who in an appropriate case could procure specific performance.

Mensa-Boison J.A, without considering this novel doctrine of equitable licence, ruled that the licence given to the plaintiff was revocable and that the licensee was only entitled to a reasonable time in which to remove himself and his property after the licence had been revoked52 and that since the construction was at roofing level "the plaintiff could only remove himself from the land but not the unfinished building." For this ruling, no authority was cited, and on e wonders whether the learned Justice of Appeal menant legal or physical impossibility to “remove . . . the unfinished building." If the plaintiff could have all the economic trees on the land removed, he could as well have had the unfinished building demolished and the debris scrapped. If this was, however, meant to be a statement of law it would impinge on the authority of Danquah v. Wuta-Offei.53 In this case the ruling of the West African Court of Appeal allowing the first defendant, who was adjudged a trespasser, to “enter the land and remove whatever he may have Put thereon," at the cost  which included a building then completed at the cost of £7,700 was upheld by the Privy Council.  It would be equally inconsistent with Asseh v. Anto, where the supreme court, in the words of Korsah C.J. with whose judgment van Lare and Sarkodee-Addo JJ.S.C. agreed, observed54:

"There is ample authority for the view that the legal maxim, quicquid plantatur solo solo cedit is not applicable to land held under native tenure. Once permission of the owner has been obtained to build a house or to farm on family land the house or farm remains the property of the licensee and his heirs and successors until the house is demolished or destroyed, when the land would revert to the owners."

Ollennu could, therefore, not have been right when he defined land to include55 "things on the soil, which are enjoyed with it, e.g. rivers . . . trees or as being artificially fixed to it like houses, building structures, whatsoever." This maxim quicquid plantatur is an English law doctrine based on the feudal concept of land tenure designed to safeguard the inconvenience of giving the soil to the heir and the building on it to the next of kin of the deceased, and to avoid the destruction involved in severance from the freehold.56 These feudal considerations have no relevance in our indigenous system.57 The rigid application of this maxim would only benefit land speculators;58 they may not make land easily and readily available to intending developers yet they stand to claim any improvement on the land, which might not have been effected by them. This could be a disincentive to entrepreneurs and may not augur well for agricultural, industrial and urban development. The exceptions, which have been engrafted on, and which may soon swallow up this maxim, seek to advance these socio-economic needs. It is, however, true that an outright rejection of this maxim would lead to a situation where one who was not able to develop his land might have to concede the use of it to a rich man who might have intruded and in the teeth of opposition from the owner developed the land, and later insisted on his right to the use of his improvement as distinct from the land on which it is. It is submitted that in the case where a trespasser cultivates annual crops i.e. fructus industriales on the land of another and he cannot protect his interest or development by any statutory provision59 or rules of equity, he should be allowed to nurse and harvest the expected crops and thereafter within a reasonable time, clear the plants where necessary, remove any temporary structure and vacate the land, leaving it as far as practicable, in the same condition as he found it. He should be made to pay damages for his trespass, economic rent for the use of the land and a reparation for any injury to the land. The problem becomes more enigmatic when the improvements consist of permanent crops, i.e. fructus naturales and buildings or fixtures. Economically, particularly in terms of foreign exchange, the country may lose, considering the various imported inputs which would be wasted if the trespasser is ordered to remove his crops or building, which may have to be destroyed. To give the owner of the land an option to pay for improvements would, in many cases, be asking for the impossible.  For if he had had that much money the land might not have lain fallow, so as to entice a trespasser.  It may equally not be fair since he would then be ordered to pay for an alleged improvement which he may not want.  And to ask the developer  to buy the land60 would be forcing the owner to sell what he may not want to  part with, and it would open the flood-gate for the exploitation of the poor by the rich.

It would certainly take an extreme case for a trespasser to complete development of a land with buildings or permanent economic trees without the acquiescence or consent of the landowner.  Since such cases would be very few and far between, the economic loss to the country will be extremely insignificant. Coupled with this, is the need for entrepreneurs to be circumspect. It is therefore suggested that in the case of permanent crops or buildings, the developer should be given reasonable time to remove any such accretion and vacate the land. There should be an award of damages for his trespass, reparation for injury caused, and if he derived any income from the use of the land, he should be made to pay for economic rent. Where there was an entry with the consent of the landowner, but the terms of the permission' were exceeded the developer should, to that extent, be treated as a trespasser as stated above.

It, therefore, means that much would depend on the intention of the parties as disclosed by the terms of the permission or contract and the surrounding circumstances. If it is not intended that the accretion should form part of the land the developer should have the right to remove the improvement not only at the end of the term envisaged by the licence, tenancy or lease but within a reasonable time thereafter. Where the developer is permitted to enter and use the land for an indefinite period it is possible to plead acquiescence (e.g., proprietary estoppel) against the landowner or rely on the doctrine of equitable licence to found an equitable relief, which may take the form of a right to the use and enjoyment of the improvement until it is destroyed by an act of God or wear and tear. Where the case lacks any of the ingredients of either the doctrine of licence or estoppel and it cannot be subsumed under the protection of any statute, the developer should be allowed a reasonable time to remove his improvements. The maxim, quicquid plantatur should, therefore, not be a fetter on the exercise of the powers of our courts in the dispensation of justice.61 The injustice sought to be inflicted on the plaintiff in Quagraine's case was deeply felt by the court but it was restrained by the maxim quicquid plantatur and was also moved by the lack of contractual nexus to refuse specific performance. These could have been surmounted by relying on the doctrine of proprietary estoppel or equitable licence with some modification in the application of the doctrine of consideration in equity as indicated above.  Incidentally the plaintiff had an iron-cast case for the invocation of proprietary estoppel; but before examining this doctrine let us have a quick look at the doctrine of unjust enrichment62 on which Edusei J. A., based his judgment.

III UNJUST ENRICHMENT

That was a safe refuge for two reasons, viz. firstly, the existence of contract was immaterial63 not only as to the question of liability but also to the issue of the appropriate measure of damages;64 and secondly, the concept is so wide and vague as would make one suppose that it can conveniently accommodate almost all such claims which result from the unconscionable conduct of a defendant. The concept of unjust enrichment undoubtedly confers on judges "a power to act at their discretion in each individual case.65 Holdsworth viewed this concept of unjust enrichment as so vague  that its application can result in a system where "a lawyer asked to advise on a doubtful case will be obliged to study, not so much the principles of the law, as the mentality of his judge."66 The vaguenes of this concept had even been preceived from the bench. According to Hamilton L.J,67 "To ask what course would be ex aequo et bono to both sides never was a very precise guide."

It is little wonder that as early as 1915 some castigating remarks touching on the validity of this  doctrine surfaced. Hamilton L.J concluded that,68 “Whatever, may have been the case 146 years ago, we are not now free in the twentieth century to administer that vague jurisprudence which is sometimes attractively styled 'justice as between man and man'." And the development of this concept was conceived as69 "a history of well-meaning sloppiness of thought".

It was not until 1942 that the principle of unjust enrichment appeared to have gained acceptance in the House of Lords where Lord Wright remarked70:

"It is clear that any civilized system of law is bound to provide remedies for cases of what has been called unjust enrichment or unjust benefit, that is to prevent a man from retaining the money of  some benefit derived from another which it is against conscience that he should keep".

Edusei. J.A. , who relied heavily on this doctrine evinced a strong urge to rule in favour of the plaintiff at all cost. When the learned Justice of Appeal was met with the quasi-contract theory as the basis on which this doctrine of restitution rests, by an inductive process of reasoning, he  merely rejected it and stuck to the unjust enrichment theory without any explanation, except that that appealed to him “more to be in consonance with the proved facts than to base it on the quasi-contract theory."

If a restitutionary claim were  based on quasi-contract  theory it might be necessary to prove an implied promise to repay as a condition precedent to any relief.71 This obviously would have been impossible for the plaintiff in the Quagraine case. For it could not by any stretch of imagination have been established that there was that understanding, tacit or otherwise, that if the defendant repented of her promise to convey the land as promised, she would reimburse the plaintiff for any expenses incurred on the land.  This was an impediment in the way of Edusei J.A. and that was probably why he quickly jumped at the alternative but rather vague concept of unjust enrichment as the basis of a restitutionary claim.

There was, however, no need for his lordship to have followed his judicial hunch. For, this controversy72 over what the basis of restitution was, had been resolved by the House of Lords in favour of unjust ciifichment.73 The "implied contract theory" has been discarded in Canada74 and has been considered as a fiction in U.S.A.75 Lord Cotton in In re Rhodes76 could not see the rationale for implying a contract by a lunatic supplied with necessaries when the lunatic could not himself contract in express terms. In the words of Lord Cotton the "expression 'implied contract' is erroneous and very unfortunate." On this "implied contract" theory, let Lord Atkin have the last word. Having referred to "these fantastic resemblances of contracts invented in order to meet requirements of the law as to forms of action which have not disappeared," his lordship then commanded that77: "When these ghosts of the past stand in the path of  justice clanking their mediaeval chains the proper course for the judge is to pass through them undeterred." This point has therefore become academic78 because of the preponderance of authorities in favour of unjust enrichment theory and not because, as Edusei J.A. stated, in a number of cases, "both theories would normally lead to the same conclusion." For the need for a search for the basis of a principle becomes urgent and imperative when the principle is being stretched to its limits by abnormal and not normal cases, and it is realised that the foundation of that principle cannot support changing circumstances where its application maylead to absurd and unjust results. The implied contract theory is certainly too restrictive to be sanctioned by our courts. The unjust enrichment theory as adopted by Edusei J. A. is therefore more acceptable.79

It is, however, unfortunate that there was no attempt to analyse this principle to determine how best it could squarely accommodate the facts of the Quagraine case. By the blanket application of the principle, Edusei J.A. not only obscurred the basic elements of the principle but justified the fears of the proponents of the implied contract theory. For, as Holdworth, in subscribing to the implied contract theory, said,80 "Better a system which is too rigid than no system at all". But this doctrine has not been left in the air; the fact that it is vague does not mean that there are no conditions precedent to its application.81 It can only be invoked where (1) the plaintiff has conferred some benefit on the defendant; (2) who stands to be enriched at the expense of the plaintiff; and (3) that it would not be just to allow the defendant to retain the benefit.82

The six-bedroom building which had been constructed up to ceiling level would improve the value of the land. But whether it was a benefit to the defendant was taken for granted. It would certainly not be a benefit if it were to be pulled down on expert advice as not having been properly located, or, where the defendant’s intended use of the land would certainly involve the demolition of that thing which was considered a benefit.83 It is however, established that the expenses incurred by the plaintiff need not result in a corresponding benefit to the defendant.84 But to mulct the defendant in damages for having received a benefit which would turn out to be a liability would be a clear case of  travesty  of justice.

If in the course of advancing one’s selfish interest he confers a benefit on another he should not be entitled to restitution.85 According to Goff and Jones86:

". . . to grant restitution in every case, where a plaintiff had acted in his own self-interest would be to open a Pandora's box of claims, in circumstances where self-interest should be its own incentive and reward."

How Edusei J.A. might have resolved these related problems was left in the realms of a guess.  The award of "damages" equally deserves some attention. The doctrine calls for restitution, i.e. to be given back what one has given away.87 Should the plaintiff be given back just what he has spent, which may sound in the value of the accretion?  If so, should it be the actual value of his expenditure or the current market price of the improvement? Should credit be given for some marginal profit or interest on the amount spent?  And, if so, what should be the rate of interest? Should it be the bank rate of interest or should account be taken of the inflationary trends in the country? Where the claim proceeds on unjust enrichment the relief should result in restitutio and not damages strictu senso.88 Provided that when the term "damages" is used the principles governing the calculation in restitutionary claim are borne in mind as distinct from those employed in the case of tort or breach of contract, it is immaterial that this term is used "in the absence of any term of convenient brevity for describing monetary payments to which a party rescinding may be entitied."89

The claim and judgment for ¢8,000 for something which cost the plaintiff only ¢3,000 required a better explanation than the formalistic reason based on "account stated."90 This can be understood as having turned on the fact that the defendant did not question the plaintiff’s claim for that amount but that explanation is not instructive enough. This issue was pertinent; for the defendant was questioning the basis of the award of the inflated sum of ¢8,000 where there was no breach of contract, in which case the remedy was to be assessed by reference to the value of the defendant's promise. On the other hand, a restitutionary claim should be measured by the value of what has been executed and received by the defendant. As it has been rightly stated.91 "A suit in contract is based on the notion of redressing a breach of contract; a suit in quasi-contract is based on the principle of preventing unjust enrichment."

The unanimous holding of the court that it would be unjust to permit the defendant in the Quagraine case to have the building without payment of its value can therefore be supported in principle. It should be the fair market value of the accretion, not merely as at the time of trial, but as at the time of judgment.92 For experience has shown that there is usually an undue delay in the delivery of judgement after addresses by counsel. According to Kom,93 in one such case judgment was reserved for not less than three-and-a-half years. Bearing in mind the hyper-inflationary conditions prevailing in the country, it would be true to say that the value of any improvement on property would not ever be the same after a lapse of one year, not to speak of three-and-a-half years. The purchasing power of a monetary award will correspondingly depreciate and the successful plaintiff would be worse off, while the defendant will be enriched by the difference between the appreciated value of the improvement as at the time of the trial and the time of judgment. There will be only a partial restitution if the difference in the value of the improvement is not accounted for. Since the doctrine seeks merely to prevent unjust enrichment it is submitted that if a fair market value of the alleged improvement is less than the expenditure the plaintiff should be given an amount equivalent to the actual market value and not the cost of his expenditure and where the reasonable value exceeds the actual cost of improvement, the improver should have the reasonable value of the improvement, i.e. the difference between the improved and unimproved value of the land, for that is the extent of the benefit which the courts seek to deny the defendant. This restitution pure and simple.

It is suggested94 that the exact relief should depend on the quality of the mistake, the extent of expenditure and the nature of the defendant's knowledge, and that where the defendant has had an opportunity to "reject the services" he should be deemed to have accepted them and should therefore be made to pay the reasonable value of the services even if that is in excess of the increased value of the land. Essentially they are some of the basic elements of the equitable doctrine of proprietary estoppel. Admittedly, however, three are equally some of the factors which would bear on the question whether it would be unjust to allow the defendant to retain the benefit. But it would be cutting across the bounds of restitution and stepping on the threshold of the doctrine of equitable estoppel to steal an equitable plank to buttress an award, which may not be restitutionary nor equitable. It would be  intelligible if the appropriate distinctions between these doctrine are maintained. It is in view of this that one would consider Edusei J.A.'s excursus into and emphasis on the doctrine of unjust enrichment unnecessary since this case could have been decided on the equitable doctrine of proprietary estoppel.

In viewing the claim from the angle of unjust enrichment the learned Justice of Appeal unduly limited, if he did not rule out, the plaintiff’s chances of getting a proprietary relief. For restitutionary claims, "are generally personal and seek to impose upon the defendant a personal obligation to pay the plaintiff a sum of money."95 The law of property forms no part of the law of restitution, although the  historical development of the forms of action has at times blurred the boundaries of the two subjects. It would be seen that cases where restitutionary claims involving land have succeeded did invariabley have elements of acquiescence, constructive trust, equitable lien or tracing.96 As already noted, a restitutionary claim seeks to recover what has been conferred by the plaintiff on the defendant and not to enforce the promise of the defendant. Thus it would have been difficult for the plaintiff to proceed on the basis of  unjust enrichment to recover in specie the promised land.

Where one notionally severs the land from the improvements thereon, it would not be possible to allow the plaintiff to enjoy the use and occupation of the improvement without the land onto which the improvement is attached. That would be giving the  plaintiff something in excess of his improvement, which is the only thing the court is urged to make the defendant disgorge and not his land. If the conduct of the defendant gives rise to equities which can only be satisfied by declaring an interest in the land in favour of the plaintiff then such equities should be specifically pleaded and established. Respondingly, Edusei J.A missed the nauseating but small fly by trying to hit with a sledge-hammer. Could he have succeeded by employing the narrower concept of proprietary estoppel which the other two members of the bench used?

IV PROPRIETARY ESTOPPEL

To rely on unjust enrichment one need not prove fraud against the defendant, who may even not be at fault in any respect. But for proprietary estoppel, the conduct of the defendant97:

"must amount to fraud, and in my view that is an abbreviated statement of a very true proposition.  A man is not to be deprived  of his legal rights unless he has acted in such a way as would make it fraudulent for him to set up those rights. What, then, are the elements or requisites necessary to constitute fraud of that description?  In the first place the plaintiff must have made a mistake as to his legal rights. Secondly, the plaintiff must have expended some money or must have done some act (not necessarily upon the defendant's land) on the faith of his mistaken belief. Thirdly, the defendant, the possessor of the legal right, must know of the existence of his own right which is inconsistent with the right claimed by the plaintiff. If he does not know of it he is in the same position as the plaintiff, and the doctrine of acquiescence is founded upon conduct with a knowledge of your legal rights. Fourthly, the defendant, the possessor of the legal right, must know of the plaintiff's mistaken belief of his rights. If he does not, there is nothing which calls upon him to assert his own rights. Lastly, the defendant, the possessor of the legal right, must have encouraged the plaintiff in his expenditure of money or in the other acts which he has done, either directly or by abstaining from asserting his legal right. Where all these elements exist, there is fraud of such a nature as will entitle the Court to restrain the possessor of the legal right from exercising it, but, in my judgment, nothing short of this will do."

The various ingredients of this doctrine were succinctly itemised and applied to this case by Mensa-Boison J.A. as follows:

"(a) expenditure of the sum of ¢3,000 in constructing the building so far on the defendant's land;

(b) the mistaken belief that the defendant would fulfil her promise to convey the land to the plaintiff;

(c) knowledge on the part of the defendant that the plaintiff was expending money on the construction to profit the plaintiff himself, although the defendant well knew she was not intending to keep her promise to convey; and

(d) the fact that there is no bar to the equity."

This is unexceptional as it casts the doctrine into its proper relief. There is no gainsaying that this jurisdiction is discretionary. Consequently, if the plaintiff had by his conduct or otherwise manifested that he was not worthy of the Chancellor's aid, the claim would have failed.98 The plaintiff’s delay in presenting the particulars of expenses to the defendant could have operated to bar the equity.99 But the Court of Appeal did rightly exonerate him. It was a common ground that there was an undue delay. "This," according to the court:

"can be understood because as the evidence goes the plaintiff went round soliciting relatives and other persons to intercede with the defendant. The plaintiff must have hoped the intervention would succeed, and it was when at last he realised all was in vain, that it became prudent to present his bill and that was some four years later."

The knowledge of the defendant of her own title, which she knew she would not transfer to the plaintiff, coupled with her notice of the adverse rights being exercised by the plaintiff was of paramount importance. For the element of fraud,100 on which this doctrine is founded, emanates from such knowledge. The more so when after the event the defendant sought to recover the land with the improvements. Where such knowledge cannot be imputed to the defendant the doctrine cannot be invoked against him.101 In the same way if the defendant is ignorant of the activities which are not compatible with his title he cannot be estopped.102 In Suleman v. Johnson,103 Verity C.J., Ag.  President of the West African Court of Appeal said'104:

"It is only . . . when it comes to the owner's knowledge that the tenant has alienated or is attempting to alienate the land that the question of acquiescence can arise.  The owner is not in possession, and . . . is not concerned, therefore, with the acts of the tenant unless and until he becomes aware that those acts are inconsistent with and, therefore, a denial of the overlord's rights."

It, therefore, becomes necessary not only to find out whether the party sought to be estopped was aware of the activities alleged to be repugnant to his title but also to determine whether these activities were actually not compatible with the title involved. A long continued occupation of land105 even without paying tribute"106 is not incompatible with the title of the ultimate owner.107 Construction of roads in the locality where the defendant's ancestors had lived over 200 years was not accepted as capable of ousting the ultimate title. For, "apart from the question of ownership there was the other obvious incentive to build the road, namely, that it facilitates the marketing of the occupier's produce.108 In Apapam Stool v. Attaa109 where the defendant referred to the fact that they had occupied, farmed and improved the land, Ames J.A. said:

"But each of these is no more than what every occupier of farm land under customary tenure may be expected to do, in the normal practice of good farming and husbandry, including where necessary the erection of buildings."

Where a pledgee improves the pledged property with the knowledge of the pledgor, he does not thereby acquire an indefeasible title by way of estoppel as this is not the basic understanding of a pledge.110 However, disposition of interest in land may be considered as inconsistent with the title of the owner.111 In Fiscian v. Nelson112 putting up a dwelling-house in a residential area was accepted as a challenge to the title of the owner.  This case governs Quagraine's case.  But the Court of Appeal merely assumed that the building activities of the plaintiff were adverse to the title of the defendant.  But in our traditional set-up it is not unusual for a member of a family to buy land and allow another member to develop it for the common use of all the members of the family.  It is in this regard that the evidence as it appears in Mensa-Boison's judgment "that when the defendant offered the plot to the plaintiff it was understood by all that the plaintiff was to build for himself although the family expected to have some use and enjoyment in the normal course of things" should have been given some weight. That being the basic understanding, it might not have been easy to impute to the defendant knowledge of any activities which were adverse to  her title, since where the title was, might not have mattered to the defendant, provided it remained in the matrilineal family. Hence her insistence on the documents being prepared in the name of a female member of the family. This will, however, sound a specious argument if it is considered that the main objective and incentive to the plaintiff was the transfer of the title to him. It was certainly fraudulent on the part of the defendant to keep mute, and by her silence encourage the plaintiff in his building activities after she had had a change of mind and thereafter sought to recover the land together with the plaintiff's improvement thereon. This might have been after she had conferred with her husband who expressed a similar desire to put up a house on the same plot.

It could not have equally been argued, as counsel in Crabb v. Arun D.C.113 contended, that since the plaintiff incurred the expenditure in a belief in an expectation of a title to be transferred to him by the defendant in the future, as opposed to a belief in an existing title, proprietary estoppel would not arise. For as Scarman L.J. rightly observed in Crabb v. Arun D.C.114 that distinction founded on the views of the majority in Ramsden v. Dyson has been discarded and the dissenting view of Kingsdown L.J. to the effect that this doctrine could be invoked even if the mistake arises from, "expectation, created or encouraged . . . that he shall have a certain interest" has prevailed. It follows from this that where the claimant is aware of their respective rights the doctrine will not lie. In the words of Ollennu115:

"If this were not our law, what would happen is that when a man owns land but is not able to develop it, due to poverty or other causes, any rich man or any man in position, knowing full well that the land does not belong to him, can just enter on to that land, spend money by building on it or by improving it in some other way, and then claim the benefit of laches. If that were the law the rich and powerful would exploit the poor and lowly, and the country would be in chaos."

Thus mere expenditure is not enough; the expenses should have been incurred in the belief of a right or expectation of such a right. The evidence on the genuineness of the plaintiff's belief in the expectation of title to the land was quite clear and so compelling that a remedy which would have enabled him realise this belief would have been preferable. The question then is could specific performance have been granted?

V SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE

Estoppel does not normally give rise to a cause of action116 Lord Denning having been shocked by a claim based on estoppel, expressed a realization that,117 "there are estoppels and estoppels.  Some do give rise to a cause of action. Some do not.  In the species of estoppel called proprietary estoppel it does give rise to a cause of action." But the respective positions of parties to suits in Ghana involving the doctrine of proprietary estoppel have been such that the doctrine has invariably been pleaded as a defence;118 and have not necessitated the award of affirmative relief.  It could be this trend in the Ghanaian cases that led Ollennu J. to misconceive, if I may say so with respect, the true effect of the doctrine by saying paradoxically that,119 "The correct position is that the true owner loses his right to assert his title and to recover possession of the land; not that the stranger acquires title to it, though in actual fact he does thereby acquire title to the land."

This being a paradox, it is not easy to comprehend. It, however, appears to be based on the premise that in law the stranger cannot thereby acquire title to the property.120 This can be easily rejected as being contrary to the authorities.121 Even in principle this cannot be supported. For it is in the application of this doctrine that equity has manifested itself as most flexible.122 For, once the equity and its extent have been established, the court, in considering the appropriate relief, "has to determine what is the fair order to make between the parties for the protection of the claimant."123 There are no constraints on the remedy to be awarded. The court's discretion in the choice of remedy is only limited by the circumstances of the case.124

One can hardly therefore fathom the basis for the denial of specific performance to the plaintiff in the Quagraine case. The learned Justice of Appeal confused this doctrine with contractual licence and justified his denial of specific performance in terms which are based on the fallacy that only a contract can attract specific performance. Mensa-Boison J.A. said, "Now the relief in equity is not based on the claim for specific performance. For as there was no consideration there was no contract to be enforced specifically." He was, therefore, of the view that the appropriate remedy was "the sum of ¢8,000 which is likewise supported by the same equity, or a declaration that the fee simple of the house was vested in the plaintiff." With much deference to the learned Justice of Appeal, this is a gross underestimation of the potentialities of the doctrine of proprietary estoppel and it cannot be correct. Declaration of title can be efficacious in certain cases, but if specific performance is the only remedy which can give effect to the equity, the court should feel free to issue it. It has also been made crystal clear that the equity does not depend on the availability of contract:

"The equity of the donee and the estate to be claimed by virtue of it depend on the transaction, that is, on the acts done, and not on the language of the memorandum, except as that shews the purpose and intent of the gift."125

There was no controversy on the promise of the defendant to convey the land to the plaintiff; it was on the faith of this that he incurred the expenditure. This promise could have been specifically performed on the orders of the court. In Pascoe v. Turner, Dillwyn v. Llewelyn and Thomas v. Thomas126 specific performance was issued for the enforcement of such promise which gave rise to the equity.  On the basis of this doctrine:

". . . if O has made an imperfect gift of the land to A, as by merely signing an informal memorandum127 or uttering words of abandonment,128 the court will compel O to perfect the gift by conveying the land to A."129

Even if from the circumstances it cannot be inferred that a gift was intended, the party guilty of acquiescence may be compelled to convey the land on condition that the mistaken improver pays him the unimproved value of the land.130

The maxim "equity would not aid a volunteer" could not have been an impediment. And one should not be made to feel that that was the restraint which led the learned Justice of Appeal to say that "there was no consideration" (as it appears in the quotation above). For the quotation from Pascoe v. Turner,131(a)  which was cited and applied by him had dispelled any doubt on this issue:

"About the rules of the court there can be no controversy.  A voluntary agreement will not be completed or assisted by a court of equity; in cases of mere gift, if anything be wanting to complete the title of the donee, a court of equity will not assist him in obtaining it, for a mere donee, can have no right to claim more than he has received.  But the subsequent acts of the donor may give the donee that right or ground of claim which he did not acquire from the original gift."131(b)

Equity by means of this doctrine of proprietary estoppel has made an invaluable inroad into the doctrine of consideration. In Dillwyn v. Llewelyn, the court was prepared to accept that, "the expenditure supplied a valuable consideration and created a binding obligation."132 According to Roscoe Pound133:

"In the case of donees in parol gifts of land [the Chancellor] felt that the donees were not getting something for nothing in view of the expenditures made on the faith of the gift, and that there was something akin to deceit if they did not get the land."

Much as this is viewed as a welcome exception to the maxim, equity will  not aid a volunteer, the attempt to describe the expenditure as a consideration should be resisted.  For it cannot be treated on any fair construction as quid pro quo134 as such expenditure would not normally be incurred in exchange for the land.135 To call it consideration would denature the concept as it is popularly known in law.

From a careful observation one may be inclined to the view that the plaintiff was content with the award of monetary relief; in which case the basis for his claim for specific performance became questionable. For, generally, specific performance would only issue where damages would not be adequate. It is noteworthy that there was no cross-appeal on the trial court's denial of specific performance. Coupled with this is the plaintiff's tender of a bill of ¢8,000 and the form in which his claim was cast, i.e. making specific performance an alternative relief, which tend to show that plaintiff had long resigned himself to the situation and that the monetary award would be adequate to him. In fact, it seems Jiagge J.A. would have granted specific performance but for the fact that she observed that there was no cross-appeal and that the plaintiff sought an order for ¢3,000 damages as an alternative relief to specific performance.  No one would cavil with the stance of Jiagge J.A. in not granting specific performance for the simple reason that there was no appeal on the trial court's refusal to issue the same. But Jiagge J.A.'s other view which purports to establish that where a plaintiff claims damages as an alternative relief he should be denied specific performance provokes doubt, which could only have been dispelled if she (the learned Justice of Appeal) had buttressed it with an authority. A case in point is Dowling v. Betjemann136 where the contention that the plaintiff had claimed damages as an alternative relief was sustained as a successful defence to specific performance.  In that case, Sir Page-Wood over-simplified the claim thus137:

"'You have broken the agreement, and I insist that it shall be cancelled, and that my picture shall be restored.' I do not say that that contention might not have prevailed.  But upon the bill as it stands the Plaintiff says he is quite satisfied with the agreed price of £300, and it is only in the event of that not being paid that he asks for the restoration of the picture. That reduces the contest to a mere money demand."

The authority of Dowling v. Betjemann has been diminished by the suggestion that it "cannot . . . apply in ordinary actions at the suit of the purchaser for specific performance of a contract . . . "138 It also appears that that decision turned on the insistence placed by the plaintiff on the performance of the contract instead of restoration of the picture.  Specific performance was, therefore, denied because from the proceedings the plaintiff showed beyond any peradventure that he wanted money and not because by the form of the claim he sought monetary compensation in the alternative.  Any such rule would undermine the wisdom in specifying all possible reliefs open on any case in the pleadings, since in giving default judgment the court is139:

"precluded from acting upon that which involves a reference (which [the court] is not entitled to make) to the contract itself, the rule being that [the court] can only give the relief which the pleading justifies upon the face of it; nothing else may be looked at: Smith v. Bucham140 and Young v. Thomas."141

It is therefore submitted that in considering the issue of adequacy of damages the court should not give any or any undue weight to the form of the claim so as to deny specific performance where a certain sum is alternatively claimed.  In Senior v. Pawson142 an action for mandatory injunction to pull down a newly erected building on the ground of obstruction appeared to be well-founded, yet the equitable relief was refused on the grounds of delay and that the plaintiff had also made an offer to take a monetary compensation.

This line of cases can be contrasted with Greene v. West Cheshire Rly. Co.143 where the court made it clear that it would not refuse specific performance although the plaintiff might have a concurrent remedy in damages or might have entered into negotiations for a money compensation which had failed. In Evans Marshall, & Co. v. Bertola S.A.144 it was being urged that the negotiations that took place between the plaintiff and the defendant showed that damages were adequate remedy .or at least were so regarded by the plaintiffs and that they should be so regarded by the court. According to the court145:

"To hold that unsuccessful negotiations to settle a dispute might be held to debar parties from subsequent enforcement of remedies otherwise open to them would not encourage sensible businessmen to come to settlement."

Sachs L.J. would therefore be more inclined by recent authorities to jettison the adequacy of damages principle and replace it with the question,146 "Is it just, in all the circumstances, that a plaintiff should be confined to his remedy in damages?" It is proposed that in determining whether specific performance should be granted or not our courts should adopt this new test propounded by Sachs L.J. instead of the adequacy of damages principle, which may only be just a factor, among others, to be weighed in determining this question.

It, however, appears that Mensa-Boison J.A. would have come to the same conclusion even if he had applied this principle of Sachs L.J. Having posed the question, "what then is the minimum equity the respondent is thus entitled on the facts of the case to do justice?" He considered that "the sum of ¢8,000 . . . on the present-day values is not so substantial as to warrant a declaration of title to the respondent." With much deference, this approach is not commendable. This sum of ¢8,000 was arrived at in 1974.  Assuming that that was the market value of the improvement as in 1974 (as Jiagge and Mensa-Boison JJ.A. held), it cannot be reasonably contended that in April 1977 when the suit was initiated and in April 1979 and December 1981 when the High Court's and the Court of Appeal's judgments were delivered respectively, it remained the same.  The current economic forces should have been taken into account.  It would be just and fair to take the value of the improvement as at the time when it was effected and compare it with the value of the land as at the same time, and then determine whether the improvement as compared with the land is so considerable as to allow the improver to take the land and the improvement.  There is much force in the view that an expenditure of ¢1,000 on an asset worth ¢3,000 cannot give rise to such equities as to call for specific performance or declaration of title.  In Lee-Parker v. Izzet147 the court did not consider the sum of about ¢650 spent on property valued at ¢4,750 as sufficient to burden the property so as to call for a proprietary remedy.  In Jones v. Jones.148 the amount of ¢1,000 spent on ¢4,000 worth of property was not enough.  But the same cannot be said of an improvement costing ¢3,000 on an asset which may not have been worth more than ¢500.

One would wonder why in Inwards v. Baker149 the expenditure of ¢150 in 1950 excluding labour costs could attract specific performance but ¢3,000 (i.e., about £1,500) in 1969-70 could not operate to vest title in the plaintiff in the Quagraine case.  In fact, in Pascoe v. Turner the amount of not less than ¢230 and not more than ¢1,000 spent on redecoration, repairs and improvements of the plaintiff's house was found by the court to be "much less than in some of the cases in the books." But the court looked at the circumstances of the case and awarded specific performance against the plaintiff, who was ordered to convey a fee simple interest to the defendant, the counter-claimant. The economic forces operating in our country should, inter alia, have weighed with the courts in determining whether to give a proprietary or monetary relief.  It is trite to state that under inflationary conditions money depreciates and landed property appreciates in value.  When, therefore, the appreciated value of the property is not taken into account, and the court is merely influenced by the amount stated as having been spent, without comparing the cost of expenditure with the unimproved value of the property, untold injustice would be done.

The right of other members to use and enjoy the property as was  understood would have posed a problem in determining the interest to be conveyed or conferred on the plaintiff.  It is certain that since this was the basic understanding it could have been a fetter on the title to be conveyed.  But it appears that that understanding was not grounded on any "right" as such.  At best it could be viewed as the normal privilege accorded and exercised by members of a family to use and enjoy the self-acquired property of one of them.  Be that as it may, Lord Denning has made it clear150:

"from Blimmer's case in particular, that the equity arising from the expenditure on land need not fail 'merely on the ground that the interest to be secured has not been expressly indicated. . .the court must look at the circumstances in each case to decide in what way the equity can be satisfied'."

VI CONCLUSION

The award of ¢8,000 could not have satisfied the equity, and the court should have aimed at, as far as possible, fulfilling the expectations or belief nursed in the plaintiff by the defendant.  Declaration of title comprehending all the interest the defendant possessed in the land, or specific performance conditioned on payment of a reasonable sum as the price for the land would have prevented the defendant from reaping where she did not sow.  It must have been painful for the plaintiff to  realise that the ¢8,000 court award he won could not in 1979, when the judgment was given, buy on the open market 100 bags of cement to complete even a foundation for a six-bedroom bungalow, and that a plot of land for the same purpose which he could have bought for about  ¢200 or less in 1969 sold at ¢1,500 in 1979. No one will envy a party who has obtained this sort of Pyrrhic victory. One can but take consolation in the fact that if the plaintiff had cross-appealed on the denial of specific performance the decision might have been different. But it is unpleasant to remark that such an important case turned on a technical rule of procedure.

The circumstances of the Quagraine case afforded an opportunity to our Court of Appeal to declare the inapplicability of the maxim quicquid plantatur solo solo cedit, and to disclose its attitude to this burgeoning doctrine of equitable licence and proprietary estoppel as  capable of attracting the affirmative relief of specific performance.  The doctrine of consideration should have taxed the attention of the court in this case. To insist on consideration in the application of the doctrine of equitable licence would have the tendency of undermining the very purpose of equity's intervention in this area. It is rather unfortunate that the legislative attempts,151 to modify the common law doctrine of consideration stopped short of getting rid of this requirement in the formation152 of contracts. Edusei J.A.'s approach confirms, and can be relied upon for the view that restitution can attract a personal remedy, but the Principles to be employed in assessing this monetary recompense under restitution were regrettably sacrificed to the technical rule on "accounts stated." Edusei J.A. did not even intimate obiter, that under restitution, a proprietary relief will lie in such cases as the one under review.  For estoppel, one can safely rely on the dictum of Jiagge J.A. that but for the alternative claim for ¢3,000 and lack of cross-appeal, the defendant would have been "estopped from asserting her rights to the land." This would have had the effect of vesting the plaintiff with some interest in the land and a declaration of title as conceived by Mensa-Boison J.A. would have cleared any doubt on the possibility of founding a positive relief on estoppel.

This case raised for decision some significant questions of wide importance which must await another Court of Appeal decision. In providing answers to these questions our courts should not overlook broad policy considerations in the context of current economic problems and agricultural and urban developments, which cannot be achieved if land is not made freely available. And in tailoring their decisions to suit the economic realities of the country they should not only forget that illiteracy is predominant in this country but also that people "Who lack negotiating strength or professional advice and assistance"153 abound. Even legislators have felt the compelling need for the courts to contradict the words of a statute if only to avoid fraud by expressly retaining the doctrines of part performance and constructive trust in some recent enactments.  In their attempts to suppress fraud in all its manifestations the court should not display an excessive judicial self-restraint in enforcing statutory conveyancing requirements. Any undue insistence on such formal requirements may have the adverse effect of not only undermining the confidence one should expect in commercial and family or private transactions,154 but indirectly promote fraud.
