MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: LEGAL AID [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 33—34

Bimpong-Buta S. Y.

 

The need to give legal aid to indigent poor litigants and accused persons, ignorant of the legal process cannot be over-emphasised.1 Yet one sees, from the decided cases, very little invocation of the provisions of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372), s.110 (2) which provides:

110. (2) " Subject to the provisions of this section a Circuit Court or a District Court may also with the prior approval of the Chief Justice assign a lawyer by way of legal aid to any party in any proceeding whatsoever before the Court where it appears to the Court desirable in the interests of justice that that party should have legal aid and that he has not sufficient means to enable him to obtain the services of a lawyer."

His Lordship, Taylor J. (as he then was) must therefore be commended for invoking the power of the court to grant legal aid to the appellant in the case of Kpekobo v. The Republic.2 His lordship in that case said:

"It seems to me that in order to safeguard the liberty of the citizens in whose interest the laws of the Republic are promulgated, it is essential that that indigent and illiterate persons ignorant of the legal process and accused of serious crimes carrying harsh penalties, are not left to their own devises but are assigned learned counsel by way of legal aid. The Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372) s. 110(2) makes adequate and ample provision for this contingency even in the lower hierarchy of our judiciary. It is about time circuit court judges and district court magistrates turned their attention to these provisions in order to avoid unnecessary injustices now becoming too frequent in their operation of the criminal law."

Needless to say, the law has made adequate provision as to legal aid for the poor in society. It is for the accused and more particularly, members of the Bar in their capacities as amicus curiae to draw the attention of the court to the necessity of invoking the provisions of Act 372, s. 110 (2) whenever it becomes clear that a very poor person on arraignment before the court for a serious offence, stands in need of legal aid.

(31 December 1982)

FOOTNOTES

1. See (1973) 5 R.G.L. 161 and (1975) 7 R.G.L. 62.

2. High Court, Bolgatanga 14 August 1979; unreported.

MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: MR. E. KOFI TETTEH RETIRES [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 30—31

On 1 September 1968, Mr. E. Kofi Tetteh, then a Principal State Attorney at the drafting section of the Attorney-General’s Department, Accra, was appointed Editor of the Ghana Law Reports. He served continuously as the Editor and Head of the Council for Law Reporting until 1 January 1979 when he left the council on secondment to the Government of Botswana as Principal Legal Draftsman under the Commonwealth Fund for Technical Co-operation on a three-year contract. Mr Kofi Tetteh formally retired as the Editor of the Ghana Law Reports as from 1 January 1982.

Mr. Kofi Tetteh was appointed the Editor of the Ghana Law Reports at a time when the General Legal Council had responsibility for the publication of the Ghana Law Reports. It was Mr. Tetteh who advocated for and in fact pressed for the establishment of a totally independent statutory body charged with the responsibility for the preparation, editing and publication of the Ghana law Reports. His arguments for the establishment of such a body were convincingly presented in his article “Law Reporting—Problems and Prospects” published in the Review of Ghana Law.1 Mr. Tetteh must be given the credit for the establishment in 1972 of the Council for Law Reporting under the Council for Law Reporting Act, 1971 (Act 366), which was subsequently replaced by the Council for Law Reporting Decree, 1972 (N.R.C.D. 64). As the first Chairman of the Council for Law Reporting, the Hon. Mr. Justice F. K. Apaloo, wrote in his preface to the first volume of the 1972 Ghana Law Reports:

This Act [Act 366] has no preamble to indicate the reasons which necessitated its passage. But it is reasonable to surmise that this legislation was brought into being because Parliament seems to have had sight of and have been convinced by an illuminating article in the Review of Ghana Law by Mr. E. Kofi Tetteh—Editor of the Ghana Law Reports. In that article, the author forcefully and persuasively argues for the establishment of an independent Council to be charged with law reporting. . . .

The independence of the Council from the General Legal Council has occasioned certain highly desirable results. It has enabled the Council to formulate policy geared only towards the improvement of the quality of its work, it has made it possible to send some of its legal staff for training overseas. . . Its financial autonomy from the General Legal Council has also enabled it to employ additional staff and acquire equipment necessary for the furtherance of its work."

If Mr. Tetteh contributed quite immensely towards the establishment of the Council for Law Reporting, he also saw to the solution of its teething problems and the regular publication of the Ghana Law Reports. He edited and saw to the publication of the 1963-68 and 1971-78 Ghana Law Reports. Not only that—he saw to the training at home and abroad of law reporters and gave much encouragement to young members of the Bar interested in taking law reporting as a career.2

When the time comes to relate the history of law reporting in Ghana, Mr. Kofi Tetteh would occupy a pride of place. He was a very hardworking officer who carried out his duties as the Editor of the Ghana Law Reports with much devotion and efficiency. The Council for Law Reporting is very much indebted to him. The Review salutes him for good work done and wishes him a very happy retirement.

FOOTNOTES

1.  See [1970] 2 R.G.L. 180.

2. One of the lawyers appointed in 1972 as a law reporter and sent to London on attachment course in law reporting at the instance of Mr. Kofi Tetteh, is Mr. S.Y. Bimpong-Buta, the present Editor of the Ghana Law Reports.
