PURCHASE BY A HUSBAND IN THE NAME OF HIS WIFE DEVIOUSNESS OR GENEROSITY? [1981 & 82] VOLS. XIII & XIV  RGL 195—199

Woodman Gordon R.

 

IN the customary law of conveyancing documents have limited importance. An instrument of the most formidable appearance is, at most, evidence of a customary-law formality.1 Even when a transfer takes effect under common law by a deed of conveyance, customary law may nevertheless override the words identifying the grantee, so that, for example, a conveyance to "X" may take effect as a conveyance to the family of which X is head and from whose funds the purchase price is provided.2 However, under "pure" common law the terms of a documentary conveyance are given great respect. Here a deed of conveyance to "X" is both necessary and conclusive in passing an interest to X. One of the few qualifications to this common-law rule is formed by the equitable doctrines of presumed resulting trusts.3 These do not affect the legal interest, which duly passes to the grantee named in the conveyance, but they can raise an equitable interest in some other person intended by the parties to take the benefit. The question of intention—roughly, whether it was simple generosity or devious self-interest which activated the person, other than the express grantee, who provided the consideration-may be established by recourse to presumption which arise in certain circumstances, and which may be rebutted by evidence of the actual intention in a particular case. Since it is not uncommon in Ghana to purchase property in another's name, or gratuitously to transfer property to another, these being the circumstances in which the presumption of a resulting trust arises, the increasing use of strict common-law conveyancing has produced a line of cases in which resulting trusts have been alleged to exist. The latest is Ramia v. Ramia.4

The plaintiff had purchased a plot of land and had had it conveyed by the vendor to his wife, the defendant. He had then erected a substantial building in his wife's name. He thereafter managed and controlled the property. He now sued, arguing that the defendant held the legal title on a resulting trust for him, and claiming that she be ordered to convey that title to him as the sole beneficiary.5 Since there had been a purchase by the plaintiff in the name of the defendant, the circumstances gave rise to the initial presumption of a resulting trust. The wife argued that this was rebutted by the contrary presumption of advancement, by which a man is presumed to intend a gift when he purchases in the name of his lawful wife. (She seems to have claimed that their children also were beneficiaries, although, since their names did not appear on the conveyance, there can hardly have been grounds for applying to them the doctrine of advancement, the sole function of which is to rebut the initial presumption of a resulting trust and to cause the equitable interest to pass with the legal. The decision reached excluded any need for discussion of the point.)  The principal issue was whether the evidence rebutted this presumption of advancement in favour of the wife. The High Court and the Court of Appeal both held that it did not, and gave judgement against the plaintiff.

Two Ghanaian cases were expressly distinguished. In Kwantreng v. Amassah6 a father had conveyed an interest in land gratuitously to his daughter, and in reply to a claim of a resulting trust the daughter had relied on the presumption of advancement applicable as between father and child. Her argument was rejected on the ground that the father's total control and enjoyment of the property proved that there had been no intention of advancement in fact.  The resulting trust therefore took effect. In Ussher v. Darko7 a man had acquired property in the name of a woman, and in reply to a claim of a resulting trust she sought to rely on the presumption of advancement as between husband and wife. It was held that this could not arise in favour of a woman who, as in this case, was not the lawful wife of the purchaser.

There have been a few other reported cases. In  Quartey v. Armar8 a husband claimed that property vested in his wife was held on a  resulting trust for him. She disputed the claim on the ground that she, and not her husband, had provided the consideration. She did not raise the argument of advancement.  The husband succeeded as to one property, and the other was found to be held in trust for both parties jointly. In Quist v. George9 there was an unsuccessful attempt to rely on the presumption of advancement.  In the circumstances the presumption would not have rebutted the particular resulting trust in issue, but in any case the advancement alleged would have been by a mother-in-law in favour of her son-in-law and by a wife in favour of her husband, neither of which is in a category giving rise to a presumption. In Reindorf v. Reindorf10 a wife had purchased property in the joint names of herself and her husband.  It was held that the legal interest was held by them jointly on a resulting trust for the wife alone. Again it was held that there could be no presumption of advancement by a wife in favour of her husband.  In Sasu-Twum v. Twum11 a man had acquired two houses in the name of his son.  It was held that the presumption of a resulting trust was rebutted by the presumption of advancement. Finally, in Harrison v. Gray, Jnr.12 a house purchased by a woman in the name of her prospective husband was held to be subject to a resulting trust in her favour, the possibility of a presumption of advancement in favour of a husband being again denied.

In Ramia v. Ramia the Court of Appeal cited a dictum from the English case Re Paradise Motor Co. Ltd.,13 as providing a convenient summary of the rules on onus of proof. These are essentially that, when the circumstances give rise to a presumption, either of an intention to create a resulting trust or of an intention of advancement, the onus shifts to the party disputing the presumption to show an actual intention to the contrary. The Court of Appeal found that the presumption of advancement in this case was in fact confirmed by the evidence, which showed that there had been an orally expressed intention to make a gift. Consequently the issue of onus was not decisive. However, one may perhaps question the statement in the leading judgment, that the wife and children could benefit from the presumption of advancement "provided an intention to benefit her could be discerned from the contemporaneous circumstances . . ." or "if a clear expression of a gift to the wife is established." It is submitted that, since the presumption itself determines the onus, it does not require to be supported by evidence of an intention to benefit the wife.  Rather, the presumption is decisive unless rebutted by evidence of a contrary intention.

The court directed its attention to the further question of the type of evidence, which could amount to a rebuttal. It cited with approval the English decision in Shephard v. Cartwright,14 also followed in Kwantreng v. Amassah and Sasu-Twum v. Twum. The rule is that acts of a party done after the conveyance cannot be relied upon to that party's advantage. Otherwise there would be a danger of self-serving conduct being used to attempt to change retroactively the effect of the conveyance, whereas it is at the moment of the conveyance that a resulting trust arises or is excluded. Thus in the present case the husband's control of the property subsequent to the conveyance could not contribute to his claim of a resulting trust.

If there continue to be cases turning on these doctrines it will become increasingly important to determine the extent to which peculiarly Ghanaian factors may affect their operation. Both the initial presumption and the presumption of advancement were originally conclusions about the probable intentions of purchasers and grantors in English social circumstances.  Every Ghanaian case gives some guidance on their applicability in Ghana. The present case confirms that generally all the English rules are thought appropriate, including those which concern personal and family relationships.15 The leading judgment even seems to follow recent changes made by English courts, as a result of English social change, in the weight of the presumptions.16 However it mentions that, in determining questions of intention, a court should take account of the local practice whereby a husband may control his wife's property to enable his expertise to be exercised in the matrimonial interest.  Thus it would seem that the exercise of control is less likely in Ghana to indicate the holding of a beneficial interest.

One important adaptation to Ghanaian circumstances appears. In every Ghanaian case hitherto in which the presumption of advancement between husband and wife had been discussed, the couple have been married under the Marriage Ordinance (Cap. 127).17 In Ramia v. Ramia the parties had been first married under customary law, and subsequently, as it was held, had contracted a valid marriage under the Marriage of Mohammedans Ordinance (Cap. 129).18 Edward Wiredu J. A. expressly upheld the application of the doctrine to parties to non-monogamous marriages and would have held it applicable even if the parties' Moslem marriage had not been proved, on the basis of their prior subsisting customary-law marriage.  It thus appears that the doctrines of resulting trusts may come to be more frequently applied between parties whose relationships are in other respects governed by customary law, and that when this occurs the common law is to recognise marriages under customary law or the Marriage of Mohammedans Ordinance and to give them the same effect as Ordinance marriages.19

However, it is submitted that in such cases it will be necessary not to overlook the prior question: is the common law in any respect applicable to the relationship between the parties with regard to this property?  It is to be expected that frequently all relations between persons married under customary law will be governed by customary law.  When that is the case there will be no ground to apply the doctrines of resulting trusts, which are part of the common law as distinct from customary law.  For example, in Abebreseh v. Kaah,20 where a man had acquired a house with the aid of substantial contributions from his customary-law wife, the court considered only the rules of customary law, and concluded that the parties held the house as customary-law co-owners: the common-law doctrines were not mentioned. The law seems to be that, as between customary-law spouses, the law applicable in a property dispute is that which they may be taken to have intended to govern their transaction in respect of that particular property.21 If, however, on this approach it is concluded that the common law generally applies, the rules of common law may still take into account any specifically customary-law aspects of their conduct.

It may become necessary to reconsider generally the assessment of evidence of actual intention in Ghanaian circumstances. In addition to the local practice mentioned in Ramia v. Ramia, another possible difference between Ghanaian and English circumstances may be a greater frequency in Ghana of purchase in the name of another as a means of making a gift. Anecdotal evidence suggests that, in preference to making wills, people sometimes acquire properties in the names of their children and other dependants as a means of providing for them, especially when the law of intestacy is unlikely to meet their needs22 Thus an intention to make a gift may be more probable in Ghana than in England.
