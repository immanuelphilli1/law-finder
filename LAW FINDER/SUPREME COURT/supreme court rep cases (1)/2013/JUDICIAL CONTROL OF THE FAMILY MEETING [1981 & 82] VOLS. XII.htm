JUDICIAL CONTROL OF THE FAMILY MEETING [1981 & 82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 222—226

Woodman Gordon R.

 

IN the field of public law there has been controversy over the extent to which the courts may pronounce upon the proceedings of other judicial bodies which, according to statutory provisions, are "not to be questioned." Accompanied by less public furore, the courts have faced similar questions about the proceedings of customary-law family councils.

In Allotey v. Quarcoo1 the plaintiff sought to question the proceedings of a family meeting which had allegedly removed him from office as one of two joint family heads. The meeting had been properly convened to consider deposition charges against him, but at a certain stage there had been angry exchanges and some disorder, and the plaintiff had left. The parties were in dispute as to whether the meeting had after his departure validly deposed him. The trial court ruled that on the face of the pleadings there was one triable issue of fact, namely "whether the meeting which allegedly removed the first plaintiff ended abruptly or rather came to a conclusion as required by law." The court held that the alleged deposition of the plaintiff had been contrary to customary law and was void, because the meeting had failed to comply with the fundamental principles of the administration of justice, had continued improperly after the plaintiff's withdrawal and had not reached its decision on the basis of a proper vote.2 The defendants appealed primarily on the ground that the decision was not confined to the issue set down for trial. The Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, holding that, on the solitary issue set down, the evidence showed that the family meeting had continued after the plaintiff's departure, and had reached a decision to depose him. The court held:

"In this case the propriety of the decision reached, not having been pleaded, was not an issue for the court to adjudicate upon. In any event, it is within the domestic jurisdiction of the family to decide on what they regard as good grounds for deposing their head of family, and unless there is clear proof of substantial departure from the tenets of natural justice, the courts will not interfere."

The dictum indicates the principal issue which case-law has to resolve concerning this class of family decision. The courts might perhaps have decided that they would make no adjudication whatsoever of internal family matters, even to the extent of declining to identify the lawful head of family in disputed cases. Had they done this, they would have had to exclude from their repertoire of customary-law rules every rule which made reference to a head of family. This would have excluded substantial areas of customary property law from the effects of state recognition and enforcement. It would, for example, have been necessary to devise different rules on the alienation of family property, perhaps to the effect that a purported alienation was void unless all adult members had concurred. The present rules, giving a preponderant voice to the head of family, require that the courts determine who is head in disputed cases. However, in accepting this need the courts have resolved generally to accept without question the decision of the family. They have then been confronted with numbers of cases in which litigants have found it in their interests to argue that the family's decision was too unreasonable to be recognised. The courts have not declined altogether to listen to such arguments, but Allotey v. Quarcoo shows that generally they will listen only in extreme cases, the independence of the family being respected in all ordinary circumstances.

This is the latest in a line of cases in which the identification of the head of a family required a decision on a disputed allegation that a former head had been deposed. In such cases there is an irreducible minimum issue which a court cannot avoid. Even if it be asserted that a family has unfettered discretion in deposing a head, it is still necessary to decide whether a head has in fact been deposed: and this necessitates the setting of a legal definition of deposition against which the particular facts can be measured.

It would seem necessarily the law that the party alleging a deposition must prove the requisite facts. So far the courts have not held deposition to be impossible in any system of customary law.3 They have indicated that the essence of deposition is a decision of a family meeting.4 Further clarification of this requirement has indicated that a "family meeting" means a meeting to which all the principal members are invited,5 and which is representative of the family as a whole;6 and that "decision" includes a resolution passed by a majority vote.7

In Allotey v. Quarcoo it was admitted that a valid meeting occurred. The factual issue for trial was whether that meeting continued to the point where a valid decision was reached. The courts have not altogether abstained from questioning decisions of family meetings. However, as Allotey v. Quarcoo confirms, once a decision of a family meeting is proved, the burden of proving invalidity shifts to the party seeking to avoid the decision.8 There is authority holding further that the decision can be declared void if it is shown to lack certain characteristics. It has been held that the head of family must have been given an opportunity to answer the charges brought against him, by receiving due warning of the purpose of the meeting and a hearing at the meeting.9 Accordingly, in Allotey v. Quarcoo a reference to "the court's lack of jurisdiction to pronounce on the merits of the decision of the family" was followed by the qualification that the decision had been reached "at a fully representative meeting and after due notice of the meeting and the charges given beforehand." According to some authorities it was also essential for it to have been established at the meeting that the head had been guilty of misconduct, or had become unfit for the post.10 However, in Allotey v. Quarcoo the court confined the extent of legitimate judicial concern. The trial judge had stated that he would consider whether there had been a violation of any fundamental principle of the administration of justice, especially the rules of natural justice. The Court of Appeal said that in this "he stated the limited supervisory role of the courts in family elections and depositions a shade too broadly." It laid down the rule that "the courts [will] interfere with the family's decision only when there has been a complete denial of justice or a substantial denial of justice."11 This is perhaps still not the most precise specification possible, but it nevertheless constitutes a clarification and determination of the law.

The decision permits more judicial intervention than in proceedings subject to the more stringent statutory exclusions. In Kwakye v. Attorney-General12 the statutory provision stated that "no . . . judicial action taken or purported to have been taken by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council or by any person in the name of that Council shall be questioned in any proceedings whatsoever," and that this was to have effect "notwithstanding that any such action . . . was not taken in accordance with any procedure prescribed by any law."13 All members of the Supreme Court hearing the case accepted that the court could not avoid determining whether a particular act amounted to a "judicial action taken or purported to have been taken. . ." While the court could not allow such action to be "questioned," it had to decide whether a particular action came within the "unquestionable" category. Similarly, even if a court were totally excluded from questioning the propriety of a family's deposition of its head, it could not avoid the need to determine whether a family had in fact made a deposition. The competence of courts in the two instances thereafter diverges. The statutory provision in issue in Kwakye v. Attorney-General bars all further questions whatsoever, whereas the customary law in issue in Allotey v. Quarcoo allows the validity of the act to be questioned on the limited grounds mentioned above.

Another analogy may be drawn with other types of family decisions, those to appoint a particular member as head and to alienate family land. The courts appear to have decided that when any such act is in issue they will ascertain whether a family decision has been reached, but will generally not question the propriety of the decision. However, there is some authority for each category of decision for the view that in extreme cases the courts may hold a decision to be void as being based on wrong grounds.14 Thus the courts are gradually demarcating the boundaries of the family's autonomy. It appears to be far-flung, leaving few grounds on which family decisions may be questioned in the courts.15
