BOOK REVIEW: CRIMINAL PROCEDURE IN GHANA [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 236—237

by A. N. E. Amissah, M.A. (Oxon) (Sedco Publishing Ltd. Ghana) 1982 xiv and 388 pp.

BIMPONG-BUTA S. Y.

 

THE good and very pleasant news that Mr. Justice Amissah has won the 1982 Ghana Book Award for Law for his latest book: Criminal Procedure in Ghana as announced on 10 November 1982 by the Ghana Book Development Council, came as no surprise to all those interested in the administration of law and justice in Ghana. The 1960 Criminal Procedure Code (Act 30) of Ghana has been subjected to numerous amendments since it was enacted more than twenty-two years ago. It has therefore been very difficult to know what the law is at any particular point in time. There have been occasions when decisions of the courts have not reflected what the statutory law says on the issue in question. For example, section 324 (3) of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, provided that, "No appeal shall be entertained against conviction by an accused person who has pleaded guilty and has been convicted on his plea." It was on the basis of this section of the Code that the High Court in 1974 in the case of Watara v. The Republic [1974] 2 G.L.R. 24 held at p. 29 that, "where the accused unequivocally pleaded guilty, there can be no right of appeal against conviction." That holding by the court was erroneous, in legal parlance, given per incuriam, simply because unknown to the court, the whole of section 324 of Act 30 had been repealed on 22 September 1971 by the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372). This unsatisfactory state of affairs in the field of criminal procedure in Ghana, brought about by numerous amendments to Act 30, has been expertly and neatly done away with by the author. Thus for a comprehensive statement of the law of criminal procedure in Ghana, i.e. statute law as explained and expounded by decisions of the superior courts of Ghana, Justice Amissah's book (which is a product of his rich experience as a retired Justice of Appeal in Ghana, teacher of the subject for many years at the Faculty of Law, University of Ghana and Director of Public Prosecutions) is to be regarded as a classic.

As the author states in his preface to the book, the arrangement "is to start from a report of a criminal offence and to take it through the various institutions which it would have to go through until the resulting case is finally determined." The author in a continuous narrative of ten chapters, deals with: The courts with criminal jurisdiction; the Attorney-General and criminal proceedings; the police and the institution of criminal proceedings; the indictment or charge sheet; the trial, matters connected with criminal proceedings; miscellaneous modes of trial; punishment and appeals; reserved questions and revisions and reviews.

Since the book was published in 1982, one would have expected that chapter 10 of the book dealing with Appeals and Reviews (or an additional chapter) would have dealt with the vexed question of whether the courts having criminal jurisdiction, especially the superior courts in Ghana as described in chapter 1 of the book, have the power to review decisions by the special courts created by Decrees (i.e., A.F.R.C. D. 3 and 23), enacted by the military government of the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council. This procedural issue was not specifically raised and discussed by the author. For instance, Taylor J. in the High Court on 19 May 1980, in the case of Republic v. Special Tribunal; Ex parte Forson [1980] G.L.R.D. 63 had held that the fact that the special tribunal set up under A.F.R.C.D. 23, acting within its jurisdiction, had utilised insufficient or inadmissible evidence in convicting the applicant, would not result in the applicant's conviction being a nullity but voidable. And that the conviction founded on such insufficient or inadmissible evidence could not be questioned by the review process of certiorari. It may, however, be conceded that the reference made by the author in chapter 1 of the book with regard to the power of the High Court to exercise the power of judicial review over military tribunals, may apply mutatis mutandis to decisions by the special courts. One may also agree with the author's conclusion at p. 13 that the courts with criminal jurisdiction in Ghana have supervisory powers of review over military tribunals (which admittedly fall under the same category as special courts) on the basis that “a residual jurisdiction to determine the limits within which these tribunals operated still resided in the courts."

Be that as it may, even though the author does refer to some provisions of the Ghana Constitution, 1979, pertaining to criminal procedure, he does not raise the all-important issue of whether or not a decision by a special court is subject to judicial review vis-á-vis section 15 (2) of the transitional provisions of the Constitution, 1979. Section 15 (2) which has been continued in force by section 25 of the Provisional National Defence Council (Establishment) Proclamation, 1981.

If the intention of the author was to re-state a comprehensive law of criminal procedure in Ghana as at December 1979, then the criticism that no reference was made to the effect of section 15 (2) of the transitional provisions on the power of the courts to review decisions of the special courts might appear unjustifiable. But neither in the body of the text nor in the preface does the author seek to state the law as it stood in December 1979.  This information becomes even more compelling since 1982 has been stated in the book as the year of its first publication. The reasonable assumption, in the light of lack of information as to the effective date of the stated law, is that the law as at 1982 was intended to be stated. If this criticism is taken, then it is respectfully suggested that the next edition of the book might take the issue raised into amount.

This review would not be complete without alluding to the unenviable position in which the author finds himself in writing about some of the criminal cases decided by him when he sat as an additional judge of the High Court. It would be recalled that Mr. Justice Amissah was the trial judge in the controversial trial of Dr. I. L. Ohene-Djan, a lawyer, and his co-accused for the offence of murder. The trial judge's acceptance of the accused's change of plea of not guilty to murder to one of a plea of guilty to the lesser offence of manslaughter under section 239 (2) of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, led to an "uproar" in the words of the author at p. 111. It is to the credit of the author that he discusses the legal issues raised from his acceptance of the resort to a plea of guilty to a lesser offence, in a detached manner, notwithstanding the unjustifiable and regrettable newspaper criticism that both the trial judge and the prosecutor were more concerned with protecting a member of the legal profession than serving the interests of justice. Given the controversial nature of the acceptance of the plea of guilty of murder to a lesser offence of manslaughter and the powerful extra-judicial pronouncements by the then Chief Justice before the conference of the Ghana Bar Association on the rightness or otherwise of the trial judge's application of section 239 (2) of the Code, your reviewer would respectfully agree with the author's conclusion at p. 112 that, "it is left to a higher court to decide, upon argument, that the course taken by the court was legally wrong."

Criminal Procedure in Ghana is lucidly written, neatly printed and attractively bound both in hard cover and paperback. The references to the Ghanaian decided cases and the English cases (where appropriate) are correct and very relevant. The book is an indispensable tool for researchers, teachers, students and legal practitioners interested in criminal law procedure not only in Ghana but in all common law jurisdiction in Anglo-phone Africa. Both the author, Mr. Justice Amissah and the Ghanaian publishers of the book deserve high congratulations for presenting a masterpiece on the criminal procedure in Ghana.

14 December 1982
