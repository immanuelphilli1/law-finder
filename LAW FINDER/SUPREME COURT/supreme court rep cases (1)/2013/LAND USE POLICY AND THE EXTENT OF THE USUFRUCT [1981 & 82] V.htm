LAND USE POLICY AND THE EXTENT OF THE USUFRUCT [1981 & 82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 200—204

WOODMAN GORDON R.

 

Amatei v. Hammond1 contains observations on the current development of the usufruct (also called the "determinable title" and “proprietory occupancy"2), the interest which a citizen of a stool or other customary law community acquires by the occupation and use of vacant communal land. These observations concern the continuity of the interest in circumstances of changing land-use, and the extent of the area over which a citizen may acquire the interest. They tend towards the curtailment of the citizen's rights. This aspect of the case raises issues of importance to the future of the customary-law scheme of interests in land.

The plaintiff, a member of the Ashanti Blohum Quarter of Osu, had earlier farmed on the land in dispute, which was under the control of the mankralo of the Quarter. Subsequently, the area, being near the town, was divided into building plots, and the plaintiff then obtained an express grant of the land from the acting mankralo. He built on the land, and his title was challenged by the defendant, who claimed through an earlier grant from a mankralo.  It was held that the plaintiff's express grant was void because the acting mankralo had been irregularly appointed, while the defendant had failed to do anything on the land after the grant to him.

The court was able to cut through the obscurities in the earlier dealings by reaching a decision on two grounds based on more recent events.  First, it held that the plaintiff had a better title by virtue of his possession. Secondly, it applied the Land Development (Protection of Purchasers) Act, 1960 (Act 2)3. The plaintiff had received his express grant and built upon the land in good faith, and would suffer hardship and injustice if he were ejected.  The court therefore exercised its power under the Act to protect him.  It was not strictly necessary to adjudicate the plaintiff's alternative claim to title based on his earlier farming activities.  However, the court stated that he lacked title. The interest which he had previously held through his farming had been lost when the land was carved into plots for the whole community. The court acknowledged that this view was contrary to Ashiemoa v. Bani4 and Donkor v. Danso,5 but suggested that they should be "looked at again."

This is a policy issue. There is a conflict between two general objectives which the law may pursue: that supported by the two cases cited, of the enhancement and protection of private property rights; and that preferred by the instant case, of the distribution of land in such a manner as to promote intensive use by as many persons as possible.  For the former objective it may be argued that both economic development and justice require that those who acquire usufructuary interests should be permanently secured in their enjoyment of the land. If the law does this they will have an incentive to develop the land, since they can be confident that they will gain the benefits of the development.  Moreover, the usufruct is alienable, as the court in Amatei v. Hammond acknowledged, and if the land market is to operate efficiently a prospective investor who proposes to purchase a usufruct and develop the land needs also to be sure that he will acquire an indefeasible title. Further, it would derogate from the customary privileges of citizens if their community could deprive them of this inherent right simply by deciding that the land should be put to a new use. This would tend to weaken the cohesion of the traditional communities. For the objective preferred by the court it may be argued that indefeasibility of title would enable the usufructuary who happens to be in occupation of land with special potential to "hamper all future developments of the area occupied by him," as the court put it.  Moreover, no injustice or loss of reasonable expectations need ensue from the termination of his interest, since he would be entitled to one of the building plots carved out of the land, or to alternative farmland.  Indeed, it may be unjust to allow an individual to enjoy the enhancement in value of his land which he has not himself produced.

These are the possible policy arguments. The legal authorities have held frequently that, as a general rule, a community may not grant to another rights in land occupied by a usufructuary.6  A number of cases hold that the general rule applies in the particular circumstance of a change in land use, such as the conversion of rural land into outskirt land or the adoption of a layout for orderly development.7 However, the contrary view has been held in two cases8 and is expressed by Professor Kludze for the northern Ewe.9 There has also been a slender line of cases holding that a stranger who purchases land from a community may dispossess a member in occupation on payment of reasonable compensation.10  Thus, while the weight of the authorities seems to support the indefeasibility of the usufruct, they are not unanimous.

It is suggested that an analysis of the function of the courts in establishing the rules of customary law may perhaps clarify the nature of the decision which will soon fall to be taken.  Decisions of the courts on customary-law issues are in theory based upon the customary rules observed by the people. The popular practice in the present area may be discerned from a consideration of the evidence given in the cases and general social factors.  Generally, no doubt, people recognise that it is in the general interest to maintain indefeasible the rights of citizen usufructuaries. However, it seems likely that in certain circumstances a community may decide that it would be of general benefit for certain land to be taken from citizens and put to new uses. It is clearly possible for the usufructuaries concerned to accede individually to requests to surrender their interests. It is likely, therefore, that such requests will sometimes be made, and will be discussed between the parties.  However, the notion of negotiations between free and utterly independent parties is hardly likely to provide an accurate characterisation of these discussions. There will always be some pressure on the usufructuary to accede. In some circumstances the pressure may be eminently resistible, as when a large section of the community can be persuaded that their individual rights will be at risk on future occasions if the proposal is accepted, and that little is to be gained by taking the land.  In other cases the pressure may be irresistible, such as when the community is generally unanimous behind its leaders in desiring the surrender, there are strong and obvious benefits to be gained by the community, and the usufructuary is offered generous compensation. In terms of rules, therefore, there may be classes of circumstances when it is generally thought right, or reasonable, or even obligatory, for the usufructuary to accede; in terms of practice, it may be possible to say that in such circumstances he always does.

The orthodox theory of customary law, that the courts "apply" existing customary law, cannot hold in such situations.  A court cannot reproduce the complex negotiations and subtle social pressures which would determine the outcome if there were no courts.  A court always has to give judgment for one side or the other, and to place the power of the state on that side.  In the present class of cases it has either to decide that the usufructuary still holds his interest, or that the interest has ceased—and in either case to ensure that the decision is effective. If the proposed analysis is correct, the courts have to make law on this subject.11 If, as has been suggested, the authorities are not yet conclusive, the courts still have some discretion in determining what the law is to be. The immediate arguments on each side have been mentioned.  They seem to reflect differing views of the type of social ordering to be desired in Ghana.

In Amatei v. Hammond the court also discussed a related problem. The citizen acquiring by implied or express grant vacant communal land for his farming activities has not normally been limited in the area of land he might acquire. Given a reasonable abundance of vacant land, and technologically simple farming methods, this presented no problem. These factors are no longer always present.  The court stressed the possibilities of commercial, mechanised farming, and said: "If . . .a person with the necessary resources and equipment is permitted to rely on this inherent right to clear miles and miles of stool land, it would not be long when other subjects of the same stool would be deprived of any share of the land." It suggested that the commercial, mechanised farmer should be required to obtain an express grant in the form of a lease, and that the community might then decline to give him as much land as he might request.

Here the policy conflict seems to be primarily between the objective of passing land immediately into the control of those who will develop it, and the objective of an equitable distribution even at the cost of delay in land utilization. Behind this is the conflict between the development of capital-intensive farming in large units, and of farming by large numbers of small-holders. There may also be a fear that the citizen who acquires an inordinate area may hoard it for speculative purposes. Another consideration may be that, once land is acquired by citizen usufructuaries, it ceases to be available for transfer to strangers by grants which can result in payments benefiting the community.

Again it seems that the authorities leave room for discretion. Although there are cases asserting that an express grant is not normally required for farming, and that no limits may be placed on the citizen's right,12 a number of cases from areas where land was in strong demand have asserted that express grants were required.13 A decision of the Supreme Court in 1963 asserts that it was never the law that a citizen might freely farm "as far as his cutlass could carry him.14 When express grants are made, they frequently take the form of common-law leases, although it has been strongly argued that the terms of such leases are ineffective in so far  as they purport to restrict the customary-law rights of the citizen.15 Perhaps this is another class of situation in which the rules in practice are flexible, leaving room for complex assessments to prevail according to what is deemed reasonable in various circumstances. Here, some at least of the difficulty arises from modern developments. And here also the courts may soon have to perform their function of establishing rules of customary law for application in the courts. If Amatei v. Hammond is an indicator of the future, the courts will here also prefer some measure of equality to development through unrestrained free enterprise in the accumulation of individual property. The usufruct, which has been regarded by some as equivalent to the fee simple,16 may move into decline.
