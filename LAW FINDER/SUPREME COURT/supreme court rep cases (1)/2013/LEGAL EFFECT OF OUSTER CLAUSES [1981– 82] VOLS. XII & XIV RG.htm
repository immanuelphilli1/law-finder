LEGAL EFFECT OF OUSTER CLAUSES [1981– 82] VOLS. XII & XIV RGL 113—134

Bimpong-Buta S.Y.

 

I INTRODUCTION

AN ouster clause may be embodied in a written contractual document or in a statutory provision.  In relation to a written contract, a clause may be inserted which purports to deny the power of the ordinary courts to settle any legal disputes which may arise between the parties or alternatively to the effect that the jurisdiction of the courts may be postponed for a given period or until after the breakdown of an arbitration. And in a statutory provision, a clause may either in express terms exclude the power or jurisdiction of the ordinary courts of the land from dealing with particular issues or matters,1 or take away the power of the  ordinary courts to review2 decisions or judgments of inferior  courts.3  Kom refers to the former denial of jurisdiction as "partial ouster clause" and the latter as "complete ouster clause.”4  The purpose of his article is to examine in detail the legal effect of ouster clauses in contractual documents and in statutes.

II OUSTER CLAUSES IN PRIVATE DOCUMENTS

As already described, an ouster clause in a written agreement may seek to do away with the power of the courts to interfere in the determination of the parties' legal relations or the parties may agree to resort to the courts only after the breakdown of negotiations or arbitration.  Whilst the former type of clause is unenforceable, illegal and void on grounds of public policy,5 the latter is valid and enforceable. f the parties' legal relations or the parties may agree to resort to the courts only after the breakdown of negotiations or arbitration.  Whilst the former type of clause is unenforceable, illegal and void on grounds of public policy, [5] the latter is valid and enforceable.

In Addison v. Brown,6 the parties, husband and wife, were married in England. They had three children.  Differences later arose between them. In 1947, i.e. prior to an agreement to bring divorce proceedings in the United States, they entered into a maintenance agreement under which the husband was to pay the wife twenty dollars per week maintenance (five dollars for the wife and fifteen dollars for the children). The agreement contained an ouster clause to the effect that:

“The provisions of this agreement shall continue in full force and effect and shall be conclusive with respect to the husband's obligation to pay to the wife . . . the sums hereinbefore provided by him to be paid.  Neither the husband nor the wife will make application to any court in such proceedings if any, for any different relief, but this agreement may be embodied in and made part of such judgment or decree.  If any judgment of any court shall otherwise provide. . . the provisions hereof shall control notwithstanding the terms of any such judgment."

After the issue of the decree of divorce by the courts in California, U.S., the husband defaulted on the payments of the weekly maintenance as agreed upon.  The wife sued in England for the arrears of maintenance. The husband contended that the action was not maintainable in an English court because it was void and contrary to public policy in that it purported to oust the jurisdiction of a foreign court, i.e. the U.S. courts. It was held that although the maintenance agreement would have been contrary to public policy, and therefore, void, if it had purported to oust the jurisdiction of the English courts, public policy did not forbid the wife from suing on the agreement in the English courts merely because the agreement purported to oust the jurisdiction of a foreign court.

In so holding, the court distinguished the earlier Court of Appeal case of Bennett v. Bennett.7 In this case, the wife sued for divorce claiming maintenance for herself and a young son.  Before a decree for a divorce could be granted, she entered into an agreement with the husband who agreed to make financial provision for the wife and child, in consideration of which the wife agreed not to proceed with her claim for maintenance and also not to present any further petition for maintenance.  The husband later defaulted on the payments for maintenance under the agreement and the wife sued him for the arrears.  It was held that the wife's action was not maintainable because the agreement sued on was contrary to public policy.  In the words of Lord Denning L.J.8:or maintenance and also not to present any further petition for maintenance.  The husband later defaulted on the payments for maintenance under the agreement and the wife sued him for the arrears.  It was held that the wife's action was not maintainable because the agreement sued on was contrary to public policy.  In the words of Lord Denning L.J.[8]:

"An award of permanent maintenance on a divorce is peculiarly a matter for the Divorce Court, and the jurisdiction of that court in regard to it cannot be ousted by the private agreement of the parties.  The reason lies in public policy."

In Baker v. Jones9 the court had to consider the legal effect of an exclusionary clause in the constitution of an unincorporated body; namely, the British Amateur Weightlifters' Association (B.A.W.L.A.). By rule 34 of its constitution, the government of the association was vested in a central council, whose powers were set out in rule 40. These powers included, by rule 40 (vii), "to be the sole interpreters of the rule of the B.A.W.L.A. and to act on behalf of the B.A.W.L.A. regarding any matters not dealt with by the rules." It was also provided by rule 40 (viii) that, "the decision of the central council in all cases, and under all circumstances, shall be final." Lynskey J. sitting in the Queen's Bench Division held that while a tribunal or the council of the association could, by the exclusionary clause in the constitution be made the final arbiter on questions of fact, the council or the parties to such a contract could not prevent its decisions on questions of law (such as the interpretation of the rules of the association) being examined by the courts, and, accordingly, he held that the first part of rule 40 (vii) and (viii) were contrary to public policy and void.

The same conclusion was reached by the court in Re Wynn's Will Trusts. Public Trustee v. Newborough10 where the testator, in a purported exclusionary clause in his will, authorised his trustee "to determine all questions and matters of doubt arising in the execution of the trusts of this my will . . . and I declare that every such determination whether made upon a question actually raised or only implied . . . shall be conclusive and binding upon all persons . . . It was held that such an exclusionary clause was void and of no effect because it was contrary to public policy being an attempt to oust the jurisdiction of the courts to interpret the will and to control the administration of the testator's estate.

On the issue of an ouster clause in an agreement being regarded as invalid on the grounds of public policy, it has been noted that it "is uncertain whether an agreement which provides that an arbitrator’s award shall not be questioned even on the ground of misconduct on the arbitrator’s part is contrary to the public policy.”11  In Tullis v. Jacson,12 a clause in a building contract provided that the valuations, certificates and awards of the architect should be final and binding on the parties and should not be set aside "on any ground or for any reason, or for any pretence . . . or insinuation of fraud . . ."  It was held that, in the absence of fraud on the part of either party, the agreement was valid and not void as being against public policy.  Chitty J. in driving home the point said:13

“To put an illustration, suppose a gentleman who is going to have a house built for him enters into a complex agreement with the contractor, in the performance of which innumerable questions may arise, says, ‘Will you agree with me (for if you will, I will agree with you) that nothing on earth shall upset the certificate that is given?’ Why is that unfair or against public policy?”

However, the decision in Tullis v. Jacson was not followed in the modern case of Re Davstone Estates, Ltds’ Leases Manprop, Ltd’s v.  O'dell.14  In this case, a clause 2(iii) of a lease provided that the lessee was to pay to the lessors, the sum of £15 as a contribution toward the expenses incurred by the lessors in performing their covenant which  under clause 3 included keeping the interior and exterior walls of the building in good and substantial repair. A proviso to clause 2 (iii) was to the effect that the expenses, as reasonable and properly incurred by the lessors in maintaining the building (as certified by the surveyor to the lessors), should be final and not subject to challenge in any manner whatsoever. Subsequently, the lessors carried out considerable work arising out of defective workmanship done on the building which was, in fact, constructed by them.  The lessors sued for expenses thereby incurred as certified by the surveyor. The lessee refused to pay on the ground that the surveyor's certificate was not so conclusive: first on the true construction of the lease and secondly and alternatively, on the ground that a provision making it so conclusive, would make it conclusive on a question of construction of the lease (which was a question of law) and so completely oust the jurisdiction of the courts on a question of law and therefore void as contrary to public policy.  It was held that on the true construction of clause 2 (iii), the question, what were expenses within the ambit of clause 3 of the lease was a question of law which was excluded from the surveyor's conclusive decision and therefore not a matter for decision by the surveyor's certificate.  It was also held that if the proviso to clause 2 (iii) applied to questions of law and, therefore, to questions of construction whether the defects remedied by the lessors were within the provisions of clause 3, then it was void as contrary to public policy in purporting to oust completely the jurisdiction of the courts on questions of law. held that if the proviso to clause 2 (iii) applied to questions of law and, therefore, to questions of construction whether the defects remedied by the lessors were within the provisions of clause 3, then it was void as contrary to public policy in purporting to oust completely the jurisdiction of the courts on questions of law.

The point which these cases seek to emphasize is that, whilst parties to a contract and domestic tribunals are free to determine to finality the facts governing the relationship of the parties to the contract, they cannot determine to a finality, to the exclusion of the courts, the legal rights of the parties.  As Lord Denning L.J. put it very clearly in Lee v. Showmen's Guild of Great Britain15:

"Although the jurisdiction of a domestic tribunal is founded on  contract, express or implied, nevertheless the parties are not free to make any contract they like. There are important limitations imposed by public policy.  The tribunal must, for instance, observe the principles of natural justice. They must give the man notice of the charge and a reasonable opportunity of meeting it. Any stipulation to the contrary would be invalid . . . Another limitation arises out of the well-known principle that parties cannot by contract oust the ordinary courts of their jurisdiction . . . They can, indeed, make the tribunal the final arbiter on questions of fact, but they cannot make it the final arbiter on questions of law."

And in Leigh v. National Union of Railwaymen,16 rule 3 (5) of a trade union rules assigned the interpretation of the rules of the union to the executive committee and rule 3(6) purported to provide that  no order or decision of that body should be questioned, reversed or suspended save by way of appeal under the rules.  It was held that the plaintiff's claim for interim injunction to restrain the holding of elections for president of the union would be granted even though the plaintiff had not exhausted or embarked on the domestic rules afforded by the rules of the union since those rules were not binding on the court so as to oust its jurisdiction to grant such relief as it considered fit.

As to the situation where an exclusionary clause in a document or a written contract is valid and enforceable, we may refer to the recent local case of In re Timber and Transport Kumasi-Krusevac Co., Ltd.; Zastava v. Bonsu.17 Under a written agreement, a Yugoslav firm entered into a written agreement with a Ghanaian timber company (T & T. Co., Ltd.), whereby the Ghanaian company agreed to transfer part of its shareholding in T. & T. Co., Ltd. to the Yugoslav firm, which for its part, agreed to manage T. & T. Co., Ltd. under a new name: T & T Kumasi-Krusevac Co., Ltd. An exclusionary clause in the agreement provided that it should remain irrevocable for ten years, and that no member or director of the newly named company could present a petition or make an application to the court seeking the winding-up or the liquidation of the company or in any way attempt to bring the existence of the company to an end.  Notwithstanding clause 15, an application for the official winding-up of the company and the appointment of the Registrar of Companies as the provisional liquidator was brought before Amua-Sekyi J. before the expiry of the ten years.  A preliminary objection founded on clause 15 was raised.  In upholding the objection, the trial judge ruled that the legal effect of clause 15 was that a period of at least ten years must elapse before any of the parties to the agreement could apply the Court under the Bodies Corporate (Official Liquidations) Act, 1963,18 s.41, for the official winding-up of the company.  Amua-Sekyi J. pointed out that it was not the court which had decreed that the petitioners should not come before the court until after the expiration of ten years; the parties themselves had so agreed. That ruling was upheld by the Court of Appeal, which held that, on the facts of the case. No reasons prevented the parties from entering into such a contract as was contained in clause 15 and that indeed there was much to be said for that clause as the parties would wish to maintain the company as a going concern during the currency of the agreement.

The Court of Appeal, nevertheless, allowed the petitioner's appeal and remitted the petition to the High Court for hearing on the grounds that it appeared possible that there had been a fundamental breach of the agreement by the respondents who had allegedly shown by their conduct that they no longer regarded the contract as binding, and if that were the case, the petitioners would be justified in treating the agreement as at an end.

One cannot cavil at the opinion of the Court of Appeal that the petitioners would be justified in treating the whole agreement as void if there had been a fundamental breach. It is, however, submitted that on the facts of the case, especially in relation to the exclusionary clause which was upheld by the Court of Appeal itself, it appears the court was, with the utmost respect, in error in allowing the appeal. It is suggested that further hearing of the winding-up petition could not have been proceeded with before the High Court in the face of the exclusionary clause which the Court of Appeal decreed as still binding on the parties. It appears that the court should rather have upheld the dismissal of the suit by the trial judge on the ground of the exclusionary clause, with a direction that the petitioners might bring a fresh action setting aside the whole agreement on grounds of fundamental breach.

If parties to written contract, are not permitted in law to oust the jurisdiction of the courts in the determination of legal issues, what is the legal position where the legislature seeks to oust the jurisdiction of the superior courts to review the decisions of the inferior courts?19

III OUSTER CLAUSES IN STATUTES

It is not the purpose of this article to examine in detail, ouster clauses which specifically take away the power of the ordinary courts to determine particular issues. The law may create special bodies to determine such issues.20 The law is however, well-settled, that decisions or orders by such bodies made in excess of their jurisdiction, are amenable to the supervisory powers of the High Court.21  What we propose to examine in detail is the legal effect of a statutory provision which takes away the power of the superior court to review decisions or orders of tribunals or bodies with limited jurisdiction.

If the legislature has the power to hand down ouster clauses which deny the right of the High Court to exercise its prerogative orders over decisions of inferior tribunals, then such a power of the legislature, must contend with the well-established presumption of law, i.e. the presumption against ousting an ouster of judicial review.

The law is thus well-settled that it is only by an exceptionally strongly worded formula that the legislature can deprive the High Court of its supervisory powers over decisions of inferior tribunals. Such supervisory powers of judicial review received the blessing of both the 1969 and 1979 Constitutions of Ghana. Thus article 126 of the Constitution, 1979, provided:

"126. The High Court of Justice shall have supervisory jurisdiction over all inferior Courts in Ghana and any adjudicating authority and in the exercise of its supervisory jurisdiction shall have power to issue such directions, orders or writs including writs or orders in the nature of habeas corpus, certiorari, mandamus, prohibition and quo warrant as it may consider appropriate for the purposes of enforcing or securing the enforcement of its supervisory powers."

In Republic v. Military Tribunal; Ex parte Ofosu-Amaah,22 Abban J. (as he then was) held that statutory provisions seeking to take away a subject's right of appeal or to oust the ordinary jurisdiction of the High Court  must be construed strictly.  Therefore where such provisions were reasonably capable of having two meanings, that meaning which had the effect of preserving the ordinary jurisdiction of the court should be taken.  On the specific issue of the true effect of the ouster clause in section 4 (7) of the Subversion Decree, 1972 (N.R.C.D 90), providing that, "The decision of a Military Tribunal appointed under this section shall be final and no appeal shall lie from such decision," Abban J. held that it was intended to prohibit appeal only and the words "no appeal" in the section could not be construe to include the words " no application for prerogative writs." He therefore concluded that the exclusionary words were insufficient to preclude the court from considering the application for an order of certiorari to quash the proceedings and the decision of the military tribunal.23  The legislature at the time of this decision, the National Redemption Council (N.R.,C.), apparently took note of the decision and in counteraction, promulgated the Subversion (Amendment) (No. 2) Decree, 1973 (N.R.C.D. 191), to the effect that24:

“(8) No Court shall [after 24 July 1973] entertain any action or proceedings whatsoever for the purpose of questioning any decision . . . of any Military Tribunal . . . and for the removal of doubts, no Court shall entertain any application for an order or writ in the nature of . . . certiorari . . . in respect of any decision, judgment, findings, order or proceedings of any such Tribunal.

In determining the true legal effect of section 4 (7) of N.R.C.D, 90, Abban J. relied heavily on the well-established common law proposition that the ordinary jurisdiction of the High Court to issue prerogative orders is not to be taken away by the legislature without express words to that effect25 This proposition was re-emphasised by Anin J.S.C. in his dissenting opinion in Kwakye v. Attorney-General26 where he held that although the right of the subject to have access to the Courts might be taken away or restricted by statute, the language of such a statute must be jealously watched by the courts and should not be extended beyond its least onerous meaning unless clear words had been used to justify such an extension.

The common law rule is well illustrated by R. v. Paddington Valuation Officer; Ex parte Peachey Property Corporation Ltd.27  The applicants, aggrieved at the assessments of their premises in a valuation list made under the Rating and Valuation Act, 1925, sought an order of certiorari to quash the whole valuation list as invalid in law. The respondents, the rating authority, argued, inter alia, that the statutory code for altering the assessments had been provided by the Local Government Act, 1948, ss. 40-49 and the Lands Tribunal Act, 1949, ss. 1 and 2, and that that code was the proper and only remedy for the applicants as the rate payer. The argument was rejected by the English Court of Appeal which held that the existence of the statutory remedy for the correction of particular assessments in a valid valuation did not preclude the rate payer, who was seeking to attack the whole list from applying for the remedies of the prerogative orders, for in such a case the remedies were more convenient, beneficial and effective than the statutory procedure. The Act did not take away the court's Jurisdiction to grant such relief. In the words of Lord Denning M.R.28:

"I am therefore of opinion that the existence of the statutory remedy is no bar to this application.  The case falls within the general principle that the jurisdiction of the High Court is not to be taken away without express words; and this applies both to the remedies by certiorari and mandamus."

Given this general principle, what is the true meaning of the phrase “shall be final” appearing in section 4 (7) of N.R.C.D. 90 which was the subject-matter of construction in the Ofosu-Amaah case?  Whilst Abban J. in this case concerned himself with the true legal effect of the whole ouster clause, his lordship did not address his mind specifically to the legal effect of the words "shall be final." Perhaps his lordship was right in this, given the interpretation accorded to the whole exclusionary clause in the section.  The issue had been dealt with by the Ghana Court of Appeal in Ahenkora v. Ofe29 where the court held that the provision in the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, No. 8 of 1952, s.8, to the effect that a decision of the Governor-General in respect of a report of a committee of inquiry "shall be final and conclusive” was to be interpreted as meaning that the decision was a final determination of the issues as between the parties but not final and conclusive in the sense that it ousted the general supervisory jurisdiction of the courts.

Furthermore, the legal meaning of the phrase “shall be final" had been provided by the English Court of Appeal in R. v. Medical Appeal Tribunal; Ex parte Gilmore,30 where it was held that the remedy of certiorari was available to quash a decision of the medical tribunal for an error on the face of the record, notwithstanding the provision in section 36(3) of the National Insurance (Industrial Injuries) Act, 1946,31 that "any decision of a claim or question [by the medical tribunal] ¼shall be final.” The court thus held that those words meant only that the decisions specified could not be the subject of appeal or reference, and that they did not suffice to exclude applications for certiorari, whether for error on the face of the record or for excess or lack of jurisdiction. Denning L.J. (as he then was) made the position very clear when he said32:

"I find it very well settled that the remedy by certiorari is never to be taken away by any statute except by the most clear and explicit words.  The word ‘final’ is not enough.  That only means 'without appeal.' It does not mean without recourse to certiorari.  It makes the decision final on the facts, but not final on the law.  Notwithstanding that the decision is by a statute made ‘final’ certiorari can still issue for excess of jurisdiction or for error of law on the face of the record.”

It is clear from the discussion so far that the presumption in question is highly rebuttable.  If in the words of Denning L.J. in the Ex parte Gilmore, certiorari "is never to be taken away by any statute except by the most clear and explicit words," then the courts must give effect to an ouster clause such as in N.R.C.D. 191, which clearly and explicitly denies the High Court jurisdiction in respect of judicial review of a decision by an inferior tribunal.  And this brings us to the discussion of the true ambit of a statutory clause which prohibits judicial review of a judgment or order of an inferior tribunal.

Legislation in Ghana, especially during military regimes, is replete with instances where the law-making body has attempted to preclude the superior courts from resorting to the issue of prerogative writs such as mandamus and certiorari with a view to reviewing the decisions and judgments of tribunals with limited and defined jurisdiction.  As earlier pointed out, it was the High Court's restrictive interpretation of section 4 (7) of the Subversion Decree, 1972, which apparently led the N.R.C. to pass the Subversion Decree (Amendment) (No. 2) Decree, 1973 (N.R.C.D. 19) making it expressly clear that no court should be entitled to entertain any application for the issue of prerogative orders to challenge or review any decision by a military tribunal.  It appears that N.R.C.D 191 was the forerunner of more of such legislation which increase in tempo during the military regime of the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (A.F.R.C.), culminating in the enactment of the controversial section 15(2) and (3) in the transitional provisions of the Constitution, 1979.33

There was a respite so far as promulgation of such ouster clauses was concerned during the period of constitutional rule from 24 September 1979 to 31 December 1981. But the advent of the regime of the Provisional National Defence Council (P.N.D.C.), saw the promulgation of yet two more ouster clauses: The Citizens Vetting Committee Law, 1982,34 s.11 and the Public Tribunals Law, 1982,35 s.9(1). This latter section, like the earlier ouster clauses passed by the A.F.R.C., provided that:

“9. (1) No court or other tribunal shall have jurisdiction to entertain any action or proceedings whatsoever for the purpose of questioning any decision, judgment, finding, ruling, order or proceeding of a tribunal set up under this Law; and for the removal of doubt, it shall not be lawful for any court to entertain any application for an order or writ in the nature of habeas corpus, certiorari, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto, injunction or declaration in respect of any decision, judgment, finding, ruling, order or proceeding of any such tribunal."

Needless to say, this ouster clause is very clear and total: it seeks to prevent the High Court from exercising its supervisory jurisdiction under article 126 of the Constitution, 1979,36 over any decision by the public tribunal.  Similarly, any order handed down by the dreaded Citizens Vetting Committee (C.V.C.), in the exercise of its jurisdiction under P.N.D.C.L. 1, cannot, it appears, by virtue of section 11 of the Law be questioned in the High Court.

The question that needs to be raised and examined is whether the P.N.D.C. can, in law, create a tribunal with limited and defined jurisdiction with no other court having power to question whether any decision by such tribunal has been taken in accordance with the law as laid down or continued in force by the P.N.D.C. itself?  Where an inferior tribunal such as the Citizens Vetting Committee37 or the Public Tribunal commits an apparent illegality in the exercise of its jurisdiction, are the superior courts powerless to intervene and do justice in the matter?38  The law requires that a public tribunal set up under P.N.D.C.L. 24 shall, inter alia: give an opportunity to the accused or his counsel to cross-examine witnesses for the prosecution and to call witnesses to prove his innocence,39 not convict an accused on hearsay evidence alone,40 give the accused or his counsel the right to address the tribunal before the decision of the tribunal;41 and in its proceedings and decisions be guided by the rules of natural justice.42  More importantly, the law provides further that non-compliance by the public tribunal with the rules governing the mode of trial creating it, shall not render a trial invalid unless—and this must be stressed-a substantial miscarriage of justice has been occasioned.43 If the public tribunal does not comply with the mandatory requirement that it shall be guided by the rules of natural justice when handing down grave punishments like death by firing squad and if because of non-compliance with the rules governing the mode of trial, the trial is rendered invalid because of the occasioning of a substantial miscarriage of justice, is there no court under the judicial system empowered to pronounce on such invalidity?  Is the ouster clause in section 9(1) sufficient to oust the High Court from questioning any such substantial miscarriage of justice which has rendered the trial invalid by virtue of section 7 (17)?44

The answers to this problems may be gleaned from four recent local cases: Republic v. Special Tribunal; Ex parte Forson;45 Darkwa v. The Republic;46 Republic v. Director of Prisons; Ex parte Shackle ford;47 and Kwakye v. Attorney-General.48  In the Forson case, the applicant was convicted and sentenced to three years' imprisonment by the special tribunal set up under A.F.R.C.D.23, s.l. He brought an application for certiorari to quash the judgment of the tribunal as being null and void.  Counsel for the respondent raised a preliminary objection to the court's jurisdiction founded, inter alia, on an ouster clause in A.F.R.C.D. 23, s.7(2). The application was dismissed by the trial judge, Taylor J. who held that the tribunal had properly exercised its jurisdiction under section 3 of A.F.R.C.D.23, and the fact that the tribunal, acting within its jurisdiction, had utilised insufficient or inadmissible evidence in convicting the applicant did not result in the conviction being a nullity but voidable.  He held further that the conviction founded on such insufficient or inadmissible evidence could not be quashed by certiorari. He also held that even though the reception of such evidence amounted to a mistake of law, such a mistake of law, even if it was apparent on the face of the record, would not affect the operation of an ouster clause so long as the inferior tribunal had acted within its jurisdiction. The only remedy available to the applicant was to appeal against the conviction.

In so holding Taylor J. relied on the English Court of Appeal case of Pearlmen v. Keepers and Governors of Harrow School.49 In this case, the appellant applied to a county court judge for a reduction in the ateable value of his leasehold premises on the grounds that the installation by him of a modern central heating system was a "structural alteration . . . or addition" within the meaning of paragraph 1(2) of schedule 8 to the 1974 Housing Act.50  The application was refused by the county court judge. On appeal it was held that, since the judge had misconstrued the meaning of the words quoted, he had erred in law and wrongly deprived himself of jurisdiction to determine the matters in respect of which jurisdiction was conferred.  Further, although his determination was made "final and conclusive" by the Act, the remedy of certiorari was not excluded and accordingly an order would be made quashing the judge's decision.

We may observe that the majority in the Pearlman case per Lord Denning M.R. pointed out51 that the distinction between an error of law entailing absence of jurisdiction, and an error of law made within jurisdiction (which Taylor J. was at pains to make in the Forson case) was very fine and argued that the distinction should be abolished. Lord Denning suggested the way out52:

“The way to get things right is to hold thus: no court or tribunal has any jurisdiction to make an error of law on which the decision of the case depends.  If it makes such an error, it goes outside its jurisdiction and certiorari will lie to correct it.”

 

This view was, however, not shared by Geoffrey Lane L.J. in his dissenting opinion.  He said that he was unable to see how the determination by the county court judge, assuming it to be erroneous, could properly be said to be a determination which he was not entitled to make. In his view, the judge was properly acting within his jurisdiction even though, in the course of it, he made a wrong determination as to what amounted to a “structural alteration, extension or addition.”

The distinction drawn by Taylor J. in the Forson case between jurisdictional and non-jurisdictional error is wholly supported by the Privy Council decision in South-East Asia Fire Bricks v. Non-Metallic Union53 which was not cited to his lordship. In this case, the Privy Council had to consider the legal effect of the Malaysian Industrial Relations Acts, 1967, s.29 (3) (a) providing that:

“... an award of the [Industrial] Court shall be final and conclusive, and no award shall be challenged, appealed against, reviewed, quashed or called in question in any court of law."

The facts were that a dispute arose between the appellant company and the respondent, a trade union. It was referred under the 1967 Act to the industrial court which made an award in favour of the trade union. On an application for certiorari to quash the award, the Privy Council, as the final appellate court, held against the company that the words of the ouster clause in section 29 (3) (a) were wide enough to oust the jurisdiction of the High Court to review the decision by certiorari, although they would not have excluded the jurisdiction of the High Court if the industrial court had exceeded its jurisdiction. The Privy Council further held that, in determining whether certiorari was still available to correct an error of law on the face of the record, notwithstanding the ouster clause, a distinction was to be drawn between an error of law which affected jurisdiction and one which did not. It held that, having regard in particular to the fact that the Malaysia Act had (apart from the ouster clause in section 29 (3) (a) which was clearly intended to exclude certiorari by making the opinion of the Attorney-General on a question of law referred to him by the industrial court binding on the court) the award by the industrial court was not subject to review merely on the ground of error of law on the face of the record.  Since the award in question was within the jurisdiction of the industrial court, the Act had, any errors of law contained on its face notwithstanding, effectively ousted the jurisdiction of the High Court to quash the award.  More importantly, the Privy Council held that the suggestion by Lord Denning M.R., that a distinction should be drawn between an error on which the decision depends, and one on which the decision does not depend should be discarded.

As a consequence of the general distinction drawn between jurisdictional error of law as opposed to a non-jurisdictional error of law, Taylor J. in the Forson case made a definite pronouncement, albeit obiter, on the legal effect of the ouster clause in section 7(2) of A.F.R.C.D. 23 by holding that such a clause would be ineffective to oust the court's supervisory jurisdiction if the special tribunal had acted without jurisdiction or in excess of its specified jurisdiction.  His lordship also cited inter alia, three English cases: Ex parte Bradlaugh;54 R. v. Hurst; Ex parte Smith55 and the well-known case of Anisminic Ltd. v. Foreign Compensation Commission.56

The Bradlaugh case turned on a provision that a magistrate had to be satisfied with two named conditions before issuing an order for a warrant of search. The magistrate had overlooked one of the said conditions and therefore his order was set aside as a nullity. The court in this case per Mellor J. said,57 “It is well established that the provision taking away the certiorari does not apply where there was an absence of jurisdiction." Continuing he said,58  “The consequence of holding otherwise would be that a metropolitan magistrate could make any order he pleased without question." And in the Smith case, the statute in question provided that, ". . . no judgment or order of any judge of county courts . . . shall be removed by appeal, motion, certiorari or otherwise into any other court whatever" except in the manner mentioned by the Act.  The court per Lord Parker C.J. held that certiorari would lie against a county court judge if he had acted without jurisdiction, notwithstanding the provisions of the County Courts Act.

The real locus classicus cited by Taylor J. in the Forson case, on the subject, is the House of Lords decision in Anisminic Ltd. v. Foreign Compensation Commission.59 In this case the court had to construe section 4 (4) of the Foreign Compensation Act, 1950, which provided, "The determination by the Commission of any application made to them shall not be called in question in any court of law." The appellants' claim to compensation was dismissed by the commission.  In determining a claim by the appellants, the commission considered that it was bound by the relevant order to determine whether the appellants had a "successor in title" and if so whether such successor was a British national.  The commission felt obliged to dismiss the claim when it concluded that the appellants had a successor in title who was not a British national.  The House of Lords held that, since the order did not in fact require the commission to make any determination about "successors in title" or their nationality, the commission had based its decision “on some matter which, under the provisions setting it up, it had no right to take into account." Therefore the apparent or purported determination was a nulliy and no determination at all and the ouster clause would not apply. The House of Lords also held that, if the inferior tribunal had merely made an error of law which did not affect its jurisdiction and if its decision was not a nullity for some reason such as breach of the rules of natural justice, then the ouster clause would have been effective.

The law also appears to be well settled that an ouster clause, whatever its scope, is effective only if a tribunal satisfies the conditions precedent for its application.  The recent decisions by the Court of Appeal in Darkwa v. The Republic;60 the High Court in Republic v. Director of Prisons; Ex parte Shackleford61 and the Supreme Court in Kwakye v. The  Republic62 clearly illustrate  this point.

In the Darkwa case, the Court of Appeal (coram Charles Crabbe J.S.C., Edusei and Edward Wiredu JJ.A.) had to construe the legal effect of an ouster clause in section 1(3) of the Committee of Inquiry (Recent Disturbances in the Police Force) (Implementation of Findings) Decree, 1979,63 which prohibited the courts from questioning by certiorari any act done by the A.F.R.C. or the Inspector-General of Police (I.G.P.) under section 1(1) of the Decree on the basis of a recommendation by the committee of inquiry.  The committee of inquiry in its report, made adverse findings against the appellant, an assistant commissioner of police. She was subsequently retired from the Police Force by a letter signed by the Chairman of the A.F.R.C. and stated to be based on the recommendations of the committee of inquiry and in the exercise of the powers conferred by A.F.R.C.D. 12, s. 1. She therefore appealed to the Court of Appeal against the findings of the committee.64  The court in allowing the appeal set aside the adverse findings and recommendations of the committee of inquiry and ordered the reinstatement of the appellant. More importantly, the court held that the power conferred on the A.F.R.C. and the Inspector-General of Police (I.G.P.) by section 1 (1) of A.F.R.C.D. 12 was equal dependant on the existence of a recommendation made by the committee and that the recommendation having been set aside, there was no longer any legal basis or jurisdiction justifying the exclusion of the appellant from the Police Force.

While the ouster clause in the Darkwa case was excluded from application by the Court of Appeal on the grounds that the conditions precedent for its application had not been satisfied, the Supreme Court by its majority decision in Kwakye v. Attorney-General declined to strike down the ouster clause in section 15(2) of the transitional provisions to the Constitution, 1979, holding that the conditions precedent had been satisfied.

Before examining in detail the Supreme Court's decision in Kwakye v. Attorney-General, it is pertinent to refer to the earlier High Court decision in Republic v. Director of Prisons; Ex parte Shackleford65 where Cecilia Koranteng-Addow J. had ruled on the legal effect of the ouster clause in the same section 15 (2) of the transitional provisions to the Constitution, 1979, which provided that:

"(2) For the avoidance of doubt it is hereby declared that no executive, legislative or judicial action taken or purported to have been taken by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council or by any person in the name of that Council shall be questioned in any proceedings whatsoever, and, accordingly it shall not be lawful for any Court or other tribunal to make any order or grant any remedy or relief in respect of any such act."

The applicant in this case applied for a writ of habeas corpus under the Habeas Corpus Act, 196466 challenging his continued detention by the military authorities.  The respondents, by their return to the writ, sought to justify the detention on the grounds that the applicant had been detained in prison by virtue of a warrant of commitment allegedly issued by the special court set up under the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Special Courts) Decree, 1979,67 and that the applicant had been tried, convicted and sentenced to three years' imprisonment by the special court.  They therefore raised a preliminary objection to the court's jurisdiction to entertain the application on the grounds, inter alia, that its jurisdiction had been ousted by virtue of section 15(2) of the transitional provisions. The applicant, on the other hand, swore to an affidavit to the effect that he was never brought before the special court which was not physically in existence at the time of his alleged trial and that the alleged warrant of commitment was in fact, a forgery. On these facts, the court ordered the release of the applicant and held that there must be a factual basis for the application of the transitional provisions, i.e. there must be evidence from which the court could determine that the applicant was tried or purported to have been tried by the special court and that the alleged warrant of commitment was issued by that court or any person authorised by it. In the absence of such evidence, the court held that its jurisdiction had not been ousted by the ouster clause in section 15 (2) of the transitional provisions.

Even though the decision by Cecilia Koranteng-Addow J. in Ex parte Shackleford that there must be a factual basis for the application of the ouster clause in section 15(2) of the transitional provisions was not cited to the Supreme Court in the Kwakye case, it arrived at the same interpretation by its unanimous preliminary ruling.68  The court held that the defendant who was invoking the ouster clause in section 15 (2), must produce facts which showed that the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council took or purported to have taken judicial action against the plaintiff.

In this case, the plaintiff had sued claiming that his purported trial, conviction and imprisonment by the special court set up under the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Special Courts) Decree 1979,69 as amended by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Special Courts) (Amendment) Decree, 1979,70 was void and of no effect.

The Supreme Court is its final judgment (with two judges of the seven-member panel dissenting) dismissed the plaintiff’s claim and held that, even though on the evidence placed before it, there was no proof of judicial action taken, i.e. an action satisfying the requirements of the law both procedurally and substantively, there was proof of a purported judicial action taken, i.e. an action which looked like, or was intended to be, or which had the outward appearance of a judicial action.  The majority took the view that there was a purported trial because it was satisfied with the evidence before it that, when the special court met, it intended to try the plaintiff under A.F.R.C.D.3 as amended by A.F.R.C.D. 19, by relying on his file, and to convict and sentence him if found guilty.  The court by its majority decision also held that the effect of section 15(3) of the transitional provisions was that compliance with "any procedure prescribed by law”71 was not to be regarded as a necessary pre-condition for the application of the ouster clause in section 15(2).  The court in so holding, distinguished the House of Lords decision in Anisminic Ltd. v. Foreign Compensation Commission. On this, the Supreme Court per Apaloo C.J. said:

“The court [in the Anisminic case] held that a determination meant plainly a determination and not a ‘purported determination’. If the statute had provided an ouster in relation to purported determination' as well, that would have made a world of difference. In our own constitutional provision, the constitutional shield extends not only to the judicial action of the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council but to its purported' judicial action as well."

In the light of the evidence placed before the Supreme Court as to the trial of the plaintiff before the special court, it is plausible to argue that there was some sort of a trial.72 The evidence given by one of the witnesses called by the defence was that he was present during the trial in absentia of the plaintiff by the special tribunal.  According to that witness, the charges against the plaintiff were read by the prosecutor apparently from a file containing the dossier on him and that a panel of more than five persons conferred for about 20-25 minutes and thereafter adjudged the plaintiff guilty.

But there was a very important issue raised by Taylor J.S.C. in his dissenting opinion which was not faced squarely by the majority opinion in the Kwakye case: was it necessary for special tribunal to hear evidence before convicting the plaintiff?  There was no evidence that in the course of the purported trial before the special tribunal any witness was called to give evidence against the plaintiff. In his dissenting opinion, Taylor J.S.C. took the view that the only way a court could try a case was to have evidence either orally or by affidavit or any other way expressly provided by law.  In the absence of any of these, there could be no trial or appearance of a trial or a purported trial. In his view the need for calling evidence was "not just a procedural requirement, it [was] a substantive requirement." If it is conceded that the calling of evidence was a sine qua non of a trial by the special tribunal under A.F.R.C.D. 3, s. 5, then one may be unable to defend the majority opinion that there was a purported trial since the evidence laid before the Supreme Court showed conclusively that no witness was called to give evidence against the plaintiff during his trial in absentia.73

Charles Crabbe J.S.C., in his opinion, concurred in the majority view that there was a purported trial, yet he took the view that notwithstanding the plaintiff's absence, there must have been a trial all the same, i.e. the relevant evidence to establish guilt must be given and received by the special court.  In his view, the special court under A.F.R.C.D.3, would, in the absence of the relevant evidence, be unable to "dismiss a charge brought against any person tried in absentia where in the opinion of the court the charge is frivolous or vexatious" as provided by section 5 (12) of A.F.R C.D.3.

What is the legal effect in the face of an ouster clause, where the alleged hearing by an inferior tribunal is proved to be a nullity?  The law appears to be settled that where an act by an inferior tribunal is proved to be illegal, the existence of an ouster clause would not prevent the High Court from quashing the illegal act.  In the words of Lord Reid in Anisminic Ltd. v. Foreign Compensation Commission,74 "No case has been cited in which any other form of words limiting the jurisdiction of the court has been held to protect a nullity." And in the Kwakye case, Taylor J.S.C. in his dissenting opinion held that the act of unknown, nameless persons assembling and without hearing evidence sentencing the plaintiff in absentia (as the evidence brought before the Supreme Court showed) was not a judicial act but an illegal act.  Such an illegal act could not be said to be "a judicial action taken or purported to have been taken" by the court within the meaning of section 15 (2) and (3) of the transitional provisions.  In the words of Taylor J.S.C., "an action can be a judicial action or a purported judicial action if not capricious or illegal . . ." In so holding, his lordship relied on the Israeli Supreme Court decision in Orthodox Coptic Bishop of Jerusalem v. Minister of Police.75  In this case, the members of the Ethiopian Church had illegally deprived the petitioners and the Coptic Church from having access to a passage leading to the Holy Sepulchre in Jerusalem.  The petitioners complained that the Minister of Police had refused to allow them to remove the Ethiopian locks to regain possession.  The respondent minister raised an objection to the jurisdiction of the court—contending that matters relating to the Holy Places were not justiciable by the court, because, a British Statute still in force, i.e. the Palestine (Holy Places) Order-in-Council, 1924, art. 2, had provided that "No cause or matter in connection with the Holy Places . . . shall be determined or heard in any court in Palestine." And article 3 had also provided that "If any question arises whether any cause or matter comes within the preceding article, such question shall . . . be referred to the High Commissioners . . . whose decision . . . shall be final and binding. . ." The Israeli Supreme Court rejected the contention of the respondent that the ouster clause had deprived it of jurisdiction and held that if it declined jurisdiction, it would in effect legitimize a manifestly illegal act and encourage lawlessness by religious community.

IV CONCLUSION

There is much to be said for ouster clauses both in private documents and in statutory provisions.  The courts should give their blessing to an agreement of parties to settle their disputes and differences amicably outside the courts, except that, as has been earlier demonstrated, it is against public policy for parties to reserve to themselves final authority on the resolution of legal issues.

The rationale for the formulation of an ouster clause in a statutory provision, especially in penal legislation, may be founded on (a) the need for a speedy trial for alleged economic and political crimes in a revolutionary period; (b) the need to assign the determination of certain legal issues to a special body with the requisite expertise and knowledge on specific matters; and (c) the need to avoid frivolous and vexatious proceedings after due determination by special bodies empowered by law to deal with such matters.

But as our discussion has amply demonstrated, there is nothing sacrosanct in ouster clauses especially in penal legislation.  The law appears to be well settled that an ouster clause would be inoperative: if a tribunal acts without jurisdiction or in excess of its specified jurisdiction; if the conditions precedent for the application of the ouster clause are non-existent; if a tribunal commits an illegal act such as the refusal to take in evidence against required by law to be mandatory and as one writer has authoritatively shown76 if the tribunal is improperly constituted or commits breach of natural justice or where its decision is tainted with fraud.

It may therefore be stated that however wide the ouster formulation, it is the duty of the superior courts to ensure, in their exercise of the conferred supervisory powers, that the courts or tribunals created by statutes, with limited powers or jurisdiction, do not go beyond their boundaries.  If one may borrow the words of Lord Wilberforce in the Anisminic case,77  "What would be the purpose of defining by statute the limit of a tribunal's powers if, by means of a clause inserted in the instrument of definition, those limits could safely be passed?" If it is conceded by the proponents of ouster clauses that these tribunals have no power or jurisdiction to exercise a power not conferred by law; then it must also be conceded that the High Court must have the power to so declare.  At the end of the day, the essential issue is summarised in the following dictum by Taylor J.S.C. in his dissenting opinion in the Supreme Court case of Kwakye v. The Republic:

“The idea that any statutory institution, authority or tribunal of inferior jurisdiction, is outside the control of the judiciary, is surely incompatible with the essence and a regime of democratic system; it undermines the rule of law, it is subversive of orderly government and is an erosion of the people's liberties...”
