JURISDICTION IN CHIEFTAINCY MATTERS [1981 & 82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 209—218

Bimpong-Buta S. Y.

 

The decision of the Court of Appeal in Tobah v. Kweikumah1 appears to have settled any lingering doubts as to whether the High Court has a concurrent jurisdiction with the judicial committees of the traditional councils and regional houses of chiefs in determining as a court of first instance a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy. The plaintiffs in this case had obtained a default judgment in the High Court to the effect that the defendants had no right to nominate or appoint or outdoor any person as the chief of the Agona Division in the Ahanta Traditional Area. A motion by the defendants to set aside the judgment on the grounds that the High Court had no jurisdiction to hear the claim was dismissed. The trial court ruled that the High Court had original jurisdiction in "all matters" under section 14 (1) (a) of the Courts Act, 1971,2 and that it had properly exercised jurisdiction in the matter. An appeal by the defendants from that ruling led to the decision of the Court of Appeal in Tobah v. Kweikumah.

 

Before examining this decision, it is pertinent to refer to the statutory provisions dealing with the conferment of jurisdiction in a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy and the previous judicial interpretations placed on these provisions. The Chieftaincy Act, 1971 (Act 370), s. 15 (1) provides that3:

"15. (1) Subject to the provisions of this Act and to any appeal therefrom, a Traditional Council shall have exclusive jurisdiction to hear and determine any cause or matter affecting chieftaincy which arises within its area, not being one to which the Asantehene or a Paramount chief is a party."

And section 23(1)(a) of the Act confers on a regional house of chiefs "original jurisdiction in all matters relating to a paramount stool or the occupant of a paramount stool. . ."4 It is also provided by the Courts Act, 1971, s. 52 that:

"52. Notwithstanding anything to the contrary in this Act or any other enactment the Court of Appeal, the High Court, a Circuit and a District Court shall not have jurisdiction to entertain either at first instance or on appeal any cause or matter affecting chieftaincy."

It was in the light of the above statutory provisions regarding jurisdiction in a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy that the High Court gave its decisions in Republic v. Boateng; Ex parte Adu Gyamfi5 and Moosi v. Boateng6 to the effect that it had concurrent jurisdiction with the traditional councils and regional houses of chiefs in a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy.

In Moosi v. Boateng, Quashie-Sam J. construed section 15 (1) of the Chieftaincy Act, 1971 and section 52 of the Courts Act, 1971 (both of which clearly precluded the High Court from determining a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy) in relation to the Constitution, 1969, art. 113 (1) which provided that:

113.(1) The High Court of Justice shall have jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters and such other original, appellate and other jurisdiction as may be conferred upon it by this Constitution or any other law."

He held that the effect of article 113(1) was that Parliament had the right to only enlarge the jurisdiction of the High Court in civil and criminal matters as already conferred by article 113(1) and not to restrict it. He therefore concluded that both section 15 (1) of Act 370 and section 52 of Act 372 sinned against the Constitution, 1969, art. 113 (1) in so far as the jurisdiction of the High Court was concerned. In so holding, the court followed the decision of Hayfron-Benjamin J. in Republic v. Boateng; Ex parte Adu Gyamfi. In this case, his lordship referred to articles 102 (2) and 113 (1) of the Constitution, 1969, and construed them as vesting the High Court with original jurisdiction in all matters.7

A plain reading of articles 102(2) and 113(1) of the Constitution, 1969, as a whole does not, with the greatest respect, bear the interpretation placed on them.8 Article 102 (2) provided9

"(2) The Judiciary shall have jurisdiction in all matters civil and criminal including matters relating to this Constitution, and such other matters in respect of which Parliament may by or under an Act of Parliament confer jurisdiction on the Judiciary."

 

The "Judiciary" referred to here was defined by article 102(1) of the Constitution, 1969, as consisting of (a) the Superior Courts, i.e. the Supreme Court, Court of Appeal and the High Court and (b) "such other inferior and traditional Courts as Parliament may by law establish."10 It is respectfully submitted that it was all these courts-both superior and inferior which were vested with "jurisdiction in all matters civil and criminal" within the meaning of article 102(2). And article 113 (1) of the Constitution, 1969, relied on by Hayfron-Benjamin J. did not vest the High Court with jurisdiction in all civil and criminal matters. It merely provided that the High Court "shall have jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters and such other original appellate and other jurisdiction as may be conferred upon it by this Constitution or any other law."11 It is strongly suggested that the true legal effect of articles 102 (1) and 113 (2) of the Constitution, 1969, as re-enacted by articles 114(5) and 125(1) of the Constitution, 1979, respectively is that the legislature had the power to apportion jurisdiction in all civil and criminal matters amongst the superior and inferior courts. And the distribution of jurisdiction in chieftaincy matters was effected by the subsequent enactment of the Chieftaincy Act, 1971 (Act 370) and the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372). If the interpretation here placed on these articles is correct, then the conclusion may be drawn that the High Court decisions in Moosi v. Boateng and Ex parte Adu Gyamfi were wrong in so far as those decisions were founded on misconstruction of articles 102 (2) and 113 (1) of the Constitution, 1969.

In any case the plain meaning of section 15 (1) of Act 370 and section 52 of Act 372 is that the High Court is precluded from determining (as a court of first instance) any cause or matter affecting chieftaincy. That was the interpretation reached by Abban J. (as he then was) in Republic v. Tekperbiawe Divisional Council; Ex parte Nene Korle II12 where his lordship, after referring to section 15(1) of Act 370 said13:

"I think the contention of the learned counsel for the respondents that the traditional council has exclusive jurisdiction to adjudicate on chieftaincy disputes is well-founded."

The statutory provision which has created doubts of interpretation (having regard to the clear and explicit language of section 15(1) of Act 370) is section 14 (1) (a) of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372) which provides:

"14. (1) Subject to the provisions of the Constitution and any other enactment the High Court of Justice shall have—

(a) an original jurisdiction in all matters."

The legal effect of this provision was in fact not determined by the earlier High Court decisions in Moosi v. Boateng and Ex parte Adu Gyamfi which conflicted with that reached by Abban J. in Ex parte Nene Korle II. That determination was happily and for the first time made by the Court of Appeal in the case under reference, i.e. Tobah v. Kweikumah. The trial judge had ruled that there was an open conflict between sections 14 (1) (a) of Act 372 under which the High Court had original jurisdiction "in all matters" and section 52 of the Act providing that the High Court had no jurisdiction to hear chieftaincy matters. The trial judge further ruled that the provisions of section 14 (1) (a) appeared to re-enact the provisions of article 113 (1) of the Constitution, 1969, and since section 52 was inconsistent and therefore in conflict with that constitutional provision, it was to be regarded as void and of no effect by virtue of article 1 (2) of the Constitution, 1969. He therefore decided to follow the earlier High Court decisions in Moosi v. Boateng and Ex parte Adu Gyamfi and concluded that the High Court had jurisdiction under section 14 (1) (a) to entertain a chieftaincy dispute.

That decision was disaffirmed, on appeal, by the Court of Appeal. It held that since the plaintiffs' claim before the High Court related to their right to appoint a chief that was "a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy" within the meaning of section 113(1)(c) of Act 372 and that under the Chieftaincy Act, 1971, and the Chieftaincy (Proceedings and Functions) (Traditional Councils) Regulations, 1972,14 reg. 2, the judicial committee of the Ahanta Traditional Council had the exclusive jurisdiction to hear the matter. It also held that there was no conflict whatsoever between the provisions of sections 14(1)(a) and 52 of Act 372; that the trial judge had misled himself by ignoring the first part of section 14 (1) (a) which made the original jurisdiction of the High Court "subject to the provisions of the Constitution and any other enactment." It held therefore that the "all matters" in section 14 (1) (a) was subject to the provisions of the Constitution and any other enactment such as section 52 of Act 372 and section 15 of Act 370.

It should be stressed that the Court of Appeal in Tobah v. Kweikumah pointed out that the supervisory powers of the High Court exercised by way of the common law writs as conferred under Act 372, s.20 and the Constitution, 1969, art. 11415 were clearly distinguishable from the ban placed on the jurisdiction of the High Court in chieftaincy matters. The court per Jiagge J.A. said:

"The action before the court was not a prerogative writ seeking to correct some mischief in an inferior court or tribunal. The claim before the High Court was one in which the law provided that the traditional court should have exclusive jurisdiction and the High Court has no concurrent jurisdiction to deal with the matter."

The decision in Tobah v. Kweikumah is very important for two reasons: Firstly, it has resolved the conflicting decisions of the High Court as to whether the High Court had concurrent jurisdiction with the traditional courts in hearing chieftaincy disputes. Secondly, it affirms the well-established law that the High Court has supervisory powers over inferior courts or bodies such as a traditional council, which has exercised or purported to exercise its exclusive jurisdiction in a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy as conferred by the Chieftaincy Act, 1971.16 Thus the law is well-settled that where a traditional court exercising its exclusive jurisdiction in a chieftaincy matter is wrongly constituted or where a decision is obtained by fraud or duress, the High Court in the exercise of its supervisory powers can intervene by orders of certiorari or prohibition.17 If a traditional council commits breaches of the rules of natural justice before making a declaration of destoolment, the High Court could issue an order of certiorari to quash the declaration.18

But it must be pointed out that the Court of Appeal in the Tobah case did not address its mind to the specific issue, i.e. can the High Court issue an order of quo warranto in respect of a chieftaincy matter in the exercise of its supervisory powers under article 126 of the Constitution, 1979? When can an order of quo warranto be issued by the High Court? The law is well-settled that when a person acts in an office to which he is not entitled, an injunction in the nature of quo warranto can be issued by the High Court to restrain him from so acting and if necessary, declare the office to be vacant. And the following essential requirements must exist, i.e. (a) the office must be one of a public nature; (b) the office must be created by statute; (c) the holder must have already exercised the office, i.e. there must be an usurpation of an office, a mere claim to exercise is not enough; (d) the remedy is not available to a plaintiff where he has been guilty of acquiescence in the usurpation of office or undue delay; and (e) the office must be substantive, that is independent and not merely ministerial to another's will.19

If these are the essential requirements for the issue of a quo warranto, can it be issued by the High Court whenever a person complains that another person is behaving and acting as a chief when, in fact, he is not? The High Court has given conflicting decisions as to whether it can issue an order of quo warranto in respect of a complaint involving a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy.20

In Republic v. Boateng; Ex parte Adu Gyamfi,21 the applicant applied for prohibition and an injunction in the nature of quo warranto to restrain the respondent from exercising the functions of Chief of Akwatia. In opposing the application, the respondent swore to an affidavit that he had been customarily elected as a chief of Akwatia and that his election had been approved by the Akim Abuakwa Traditional Council. He also argued that since the subject-matter of the application was a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy, the court had no jurisdiction to hear it under paragraph 66 of the Courts Decree, 1966 (N.L.C.D. 84).22 The trial judge, Hayfron-Benjamin J., refused the application for prohibition but granted an order of quo warranto to restrain the respondent from acting as a chief. In granting the application, his lordship did not raise and therefore did not consider the issue of whether or not the office of a chief is one created by statute-an essential requirement for a grant of quo warranto. The order was made on the grounds that chieftaincy was a public institution guaranteed under the Constitution, 1969.

In Republic v. Sagoe II; Ex parte Baffo V,23 the applicant claiming to be the rightful mankrado of Agona Kwaman as previously determined by the judicial committee of the Nyakrom Traditional Council, brought an application for quo warranto to restrain the respondent from performing the functions of or styling himself as the mankrado of Agona Kwaman. Osei-Hwere J. granted the application on the grounds that chieftaincy was a public institution guaranteed under the laws of Ghana and that the respondent had been unlawfully exercising the traditional functions attached to the office of the mankrado. Unlike Hayfron-Benjamin J. in Exparte Adu Gyamfi, Osei-Hwere J. laid down as an essential requirement, the fact that the office being usurped must be one created by statute but like Hayfron-Benjamin J., he did not raise and therefore did not consider whether or not the office of a chief is one created by statute. His lordship in following the decision of Hayfron-Benjamin J. said,24 "That an injunction would lie to restrain a person from exercising the functions of a chief has been fully vindicated in Republic v. Boateng; Ex parte Adu Gyamfi."

Even though the previous High Court decisions in Ex parte Adu Gyamfi and Ex parte Baffo V were not referred to by Twumasi J. in Hackman v. Obresi V,25 he had no hesitation in granting an order of quo warranto to restrain the respondents from celebrating an annual festival at the village of Otuam as authorised by a person who claimed to be the elected chief of the village, pending the determination of the main issue of the proceedings, i.e. an inquiry into whether or not there was a chief of Otuam. In this case, Twumasi J. accepted the Court of Appeal decision in Tobah v. Kweikumah that the High Court had no jurisdiction to entertain a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy. Nevertheless, he held that the High Court had, under article 126 of the Constitution, 1979, supervisory jurisdiction over the operation of the Chieftaincy Act, 1971, to ensure that any person who assumed the functions of a chief under the Act satisfied the essential conditions laid down by section 48 (1) of the Act, i.e. that he "has, in accordance with customary law, been nominated, elected and installed as a Chief or as the case may be appointed and installed as such and whose name for the time being appears as a Chief on the National Register of Chiefs." He therefore held that the High Court was entitled to issue an order of quo warranto to restrain any person who acted in an office, such as that of a chief, in which he was not entitled to act, from so acting. In the instant case, he held that he must necessarily make an inquiry as to whether or not the respondents did satisfy the conditions prescribed by the Act. His lordship pointed out that the High Court could not be accused of going outside the orbit of its supervisory jurisdiction if it embarked upon such an inquiry provided that it did not go further to determine and hear any matter relating to the nomination or election of any person as a chief. In the words of Twumasi J.:

"If the argument that the High Court has no Jurisdiction over chieftaincy matter means that it cannot even make an inquiry under the Act, how can the court exercise its supervisory powers with regard to complaints or information or motions in the nature of quo warranto ... How can the High Court grant an injunction restraining any person from acting as a chief (an office created by statute) if the court is denied the power to find out as a fact whether the particular person is a chief or not?"

The decision by Twumasi J. in Hackman v. Obresi V that the position of a chief is an office created by statute and therefore subject to the supervisory order of quo warranto is far reaching! That conclusion was not reached by Hayfron-Benjamin J. in Ex parte Adu Gyamfi; neither was it made by Osei-Hwere J. in Ex parte Baffo V. Their lordships in these earlier cases merely held that chieftaincy was a public institution under the laws of Ghana.

However, a contrary decision was reached by Adadevoh J. in Republic v. Akyaamaa; Ex parte Manu.26 The applicants in this case brought an application for quo warranto to restrain the first respondent, the Queenmother of Kwae in the Akim Abuakwa Traditional Area, and the second respondent, the Gyasehene, from usurping the office and functions of the applicants in their capacity as kingmakers of Kwae and also to restrain the third respondent from acting as the chief of Kwae. The court found that the third respondent had been nominated by the queenmother and installed as a chief of Kwae in accordance with customary law and that there was, in fact, still pending before the judicial committee of the Akim Abuakwa Traditional Council, a prior action by the same applicants challenging the election and installation of the third respondent.  His lordship held that the effect of the Constitution, 1979, was merely to guarantee chieftaincy as a public institution;27 that the effect of the Chieftaincy Act, 1971 (Act 370), was not to create the office of the chief but merely to deal with the structures and functions of the various houses of chiefs and the proceedings affecting chieftaincy and that the office of a chief as defined by Act 370, s. 48(1) was not created by statute but by customary law.28 He therefore held that since the third respondent had been elected and installed as a chief in accordance with customary law and not by virtue of any statute, an injunction in the nature of quo warranto would be refused.  He also held that if there was any dispute about the nomination of the third respondent as a chief, the High Court had no jurisdiction under Act 372, s. 52 to hear such a dispute which should be properly determined by the judicial committee of the Akim Abuakwa Traditional Council.

The position then is that there are conflicting decisions in the High Court as to whether its supervisory powers in respect of quo warranto can be exercised in a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy.  If it is conceded that the office of a chief is not a creation of a statute but it is a public institution created in accordance with customary law and usage as decided by Adadevoh J. in Ex parte Manu, then the previous decisions in Ex parte Adu Gyamfi; Moosi v. Boateng; Ex parte Baffo V and Hackman v. Obresi V with regards to the grant of quo warranto are, with the greatest respect, made in error and cannot be supported.  Besides it is very difficult, if not impossible, to grant an order of quo warranto without making a determination affecting a cause or matter affecting chieftaincy within the meaning of section 113(1) of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372). A clear example of this difficulty is evidence by Ex parte Adu Gyamfi where Hayfron-Benjamin J. before granting the order of quo warranto, held that it was clear from the respondent's own affidavit that he was not nominated by the queenmother or by the royal family and therefore his election and installation as a chief were irregular and contrary to custom. It is suggested that the line which Twumasi J. sought to draw in Hackman v. Obresi V between the hearing and determination of a dispute relating to chieftaincy and a mere inquiry as to whether any person has wrongly assumed jurisdiction under the Chieftaincy Act, 1971, is too fine and should, with the greatest respect, be discarded. In the opinion of your commentator, the resort to applications for quo warranto in the High Court is a veiled attempt to prosecute a chieftaincy dispute in the High Court. Such a veiled attempt was frowned upon by the Supreme Court in Yiadom I v. Amaniampong.29  In this case, the plaintiff sued, invoking the original jurisdiction of the Supreme Court under the Constitution, 1979, for a declaration that the defendant had disqualified himself from continuing in office as a paramount chief because of some adverse findings made against him by a committee of inquiry. In dismissing the claim, the Supreme Court held that notwithstanding the way in which the claim had been couched, it was in substance, a claim to have it declared that the defendant had ceased to be a paramount chief by operation of law. It was held that, that was clearly a dispute which ought to be determined according to customary law and usage. The Supreme Court therefore concluded that since it did not have concurrent jurisdiction with a regional house of chiefs in a chieftaincy dispute, it would decline jurisdiction to determine the plaintiff's claim.

The inescapable conclusion is that the High Court, under the present state of the law, cannot exercise jurisdiction in a chieftaincy matter no matter how the claim is formulated. It is suggested that the supervisory jurisdiction of the High Court in relation to quo warranto as affecting a chieftaincy matter under Act 372, s. 20, must be construed as an exercise of jurisdiction by the High Court at first instance and therefore excluded from application in relation to a chieftaincy matter under section 52 of Act 372.
