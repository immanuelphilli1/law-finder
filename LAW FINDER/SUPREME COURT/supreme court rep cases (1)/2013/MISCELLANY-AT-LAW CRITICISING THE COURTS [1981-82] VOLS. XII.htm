MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: CRITICISING THE COURTS [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 31—33

 

The law is quite clear that criticism of the court and of judicial decisions, however rumbustious, whether or not in good taste, and despite its inaccurate statement of fact, is not a contempt of court but is within the limits of the inalienable right of every individual to freedom of speech: see R. v. Commissioner of Police of the Metropolis;. Ex parte Blackburn.1 Thus the law recognises the right of a person to criticise the judgment of our judges provided the criticism is fair and made in good faith.

But what is the position where a critic goes beyond the bounds of fair criticism and comments on the decisions of courts or where a litigant in his pleadings refers to irrelevant matters which tend to scandalise the courts and judges generally and bring into contempt, the machinery and administration of justice itself? The answer was given by the Supreme Court in Bilson v. Apaloo,2 in its judgment delivered on 8 May 1981. After holding, inter alia, that there was no justification or substance whatsoever in the accusations of serious judicial impropriety, bias, and pre-judgment levelled against the five justices who had heard and determined the case of Tuffour v. Attorney-General3, the court in its concluding judgment said per Anin J.S.C.:

"Before concluding this judgment, we must comment briefly on some unsavoury aspects of the case. While learned counsel for the plaintiff properly confined his oral arguments to the above issues of law, unfortunately his pleadings extended beyond them and raised completely irrelevant matters which tended to scandalise the courts and judges generally and to bring into contempt the machinery and administration of justice itself.

Worse still, it was disclosed in the statement of defence and admitted by the plaintiff in his reply that prior to the hearing of the case in this court, a press conference was launched by the plaintiff in Accra on 4 February 1981 whereat, it is alleged, utterances were made tending to defame not only the defendant but other judges as well. Mercifully, learned counsel for the plaintiff thought the better of his client's pre-trial public utterances and newspaper advertisements and steered clear of them in presenting his legal arguments. He is to be commanded for his restraint. There was consequently no opportunity for the defendant's counsel to advert to these extraneous but serious matters, which tend to scandalise the courts and judges and destroy the public confidence in the administration of justice. Publications imputing bias and improper motives to judges are regarded as contempt and are duly punished. This court will in future not hesitate to exercise its undoubted constitutional powers (see article 114(6) of the Constitution, 1979) and mete out fitting punishment to offenders where their contempt is established.

This warning is, however, not intended to stifle legitimate and reasoned criticism of a judgment in any case or of the administration of justice generally; for we acknowledge the legitimacy and indeed the need for reasoned criticism in a democracy. If such criticism is informed, pertinent, fair and not malicious, it can only assist us in improving on our methods and in improving the administration of justice. Obviously, reasoned criticism of judgment made in good faith is in the public interest; but scurrilous abuse and the unwarranted imputations of bias and improper motives to judges are not conducive to the public good but can only destroy the public confidence in the judiciary and in the administration of justice generally. We cannot condemn it too strongly. We fully endorse Lord Denning M.R.'s wise dicta on reasoned criticism of judges and judgments in R. v. Commissioner of Police of the Metropolis; Ex parte Blackburn [1968] 2 Q.B. 150 at p. 155, C.A.:

We do not fear criticism, nor do we resent it. For there is something far more important at stake. It is no less than freedom of speech itself.

It is the right of every man, in Parliament or out of it, in the Press or over the broadcast, to make fair comment, even out-spoken comment, on matters of public interest. Those who comment can deal faithfully with all that is done in a court of justice. They can say that we are mistaken, and our decisions erroneous, whether they are subject to appeal or not. All we would ask is that those who criticise us will remember that, from the nature of our office, we cannot reply to their criticisms. We cannot enter into public controversy. Still less into political controversy. We must rely on our conduct itself to be its own vindication".

The Supreme Court must be commended for making it plain its attitude to criticisms levelled against courts of law in respect of their decisions and judgments. It is hoped that the judges in Ghana would accept criticisms of their decisions, however rumbustious, in good faith. What the judges also expect of critics and commentators of their decisions is simply, honest and fair criticism, also made in good faith.
