TITLE REGISTRATION IN GHANA [1981& 82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 51—72

Asante-Ansong S.

 

I—WHAT IS REGISTRABLE: LAND, ESTATE IN LAND OR FACTS?

THE term "Land Registration" though firmly anchored in the Ghanaian jurisprudence is a misnomer. In this country land is held for one estate or the other.  In the words of Ollennu1:

"It may be vested in more than one person at one and the same time; but the right title or interest vested in the one may be different materially from the title and interest vested in the other or others; e.g., one person may have the absolute ownership of land vested in him, another person may have the right to immediate enjoyment of the beneficial interest in the same land vested in him, while yet another may have vested in him a right to use the land for a specific purpose, e.g. licence to build, to fell palm trees, to grow seasonal crops or to fish in a creek for a specific number of years."

With characteristic poignancy, Bentsi-Enchill also opined2:

"But let it be observed that the notion of owning an interest in the land rather than owning the land itself is present in somewhat different form in the distinction between group ownership and the member's interest in group-owned land reduced to his occupation."

And in very apt and expressive words, Professor Cheshire wrote of the English system3:

"Perhaps the most vivid is the invention and development by the common law of the doctrine of an estate in the land as something distinct from the land itself, a distinction which has enabled proprietary rights to be moulded with a flexibility wholly unknown to the legal systems that derive from Rome."

The conceptual similarity between our system and the English feudal land holding is obvious, though the historical circumstance of one is different from the other. In this a theory emerges which allows land to be viewed as a dual phenomena.  In one case the attention is focused on the land as a physical object, an immovable property; in the other, the mind dwells on the owner's interest as a metaphysical abstraction. The two notions neatly blend in such expressions as "usufructuary," "allodial," "leasehold," "freehold," etc. Under title registration, it is a prerequisite to determine which of the two notions shall be registered.  But Woodman, however, introduces another item into the equation when he wrote,4.  "Thirdly, in establishing a system of registration it is necessary to decide who is to be responsible for initiating the process of registering a particular fact." If it is facts which ought to be recorded in the register then it becomes necessary examine what constitutes those facts. Initially, it is submitted, with great deference, that to use the term "fact" to describe land in our first conception of land as a physical object is certainly to strain language. However, the existence of interests like the allodial, freehold, leasehold, etc. in land taken at the notional level may be regarded as facts. This view does not accord with the philosophical position that5:

"One of the most obvious differences between facts and their ingredients (and, on reflection, one of the most puzzling) is that whereas things, qualities and events are immersed in the world of space and time, facts are somehow frozen and abstracted from the spatio-temporal continuum."

These legal facts are dependent on time and space. They have a nature of either being temporary or permanent.

In the Australian case of Sander v. Twigg6 Holroyd J. held, "Leasehold land is brought under the Act temporarily, that is, until the lease is determined, freehold land permanently." And with temporary facts also it is important to determine what extent of temporariness should be registrable since not all the facts can meaningfully be recorded as titles. Weekly or monthly tenancies or the customary arrangements where a right to occupy and enjoy land for a specific period is allowed need not be made registrable titles for the mundane reason that the frequency of their birth and the rapidity of their disappearance will suffocate and choke whatever system of title registration is envisaged. Thus though the term "fact" is too imprecise and uncomfortable to use in any scheme of title registration which seeks to provide state indefeasibility of those titles, yet the conceptualisation of estates or interests in land as legal "facts" amplifies the doctrine which views land as a dual phenomena and underscores the ambiguity in the popular term "Land Registration."

The fundamental question as to what should be the subject of registration of land or estate in land has never been analytically pursued in this country.  This is owing, in part, to the fact that the present system allows for the registration of instruments which merely affect land.

That system over-simplifies the subject of land registration. It may be argued that the introduction of a general system of title registration need not involve any significant change in the indigenous system of land tenure and that the registration can be of the titles known to the indigenous law. However, to postulate that registration should be of the titles known to the indigenous law, it is submitted, is to argue that it is the estates which are known to the land law which must be registered.  In the English land registration system, "only interests in land in respect of which a proprietor can be registered" are interests capable of existing as legal estates.7

From the proposition that it is estates or interests in land which are registrable one may pose this question: Suppose A is granted Blackacre for 50 years by B stool.  A registers his interest and by effluxion of time the lease is determined after 50 years.  Since A initiated the procedure for the registration and Blackacre was brought under the system primarily for A's estate, the register in respect of Blackacre, ex facie, will cease to be at the expiration of the lease.  To obviate this difficulty and create a permanent register for Blackacre, it should be provided under the system that the remainder dormant interest of B stool be made part of the title.

II—CLASSIFICATION OF REGISTRALE ESTATES

On account of the towering position of Ollennu in this branch of our law, the urge has always been irresistible to refer to him first in any attempt to state any part thereof.  On estates he writes8:

"The various types of ownership known to customary law may be grouped under six heads.  They are:

1. paramount title, sometimes called absolute, final, radical or allodial title;

2. sub-paramount title;

3. determinable estate otherwise called possessory or usufructuary title;

4. tenancies or interest in land which can be owned by a person who is not the owner of the paramount, sub-paramount or the determinable estate, these include abusa, the abunu, the annual crop tenant;

5. licences, e.g., the right to occupy and enjoy land for a specific period or for an indefinite duration of time;

6. pledges."

There are other estates known to our system which obviously have not been included in the above categorisation due probably, to the fact that they emanated from the common law and English conveyancing practices. The lease which forms the bulk of grants which come to the Land Registry is of common law origin.

There are also individual freehold in the non-Akan areas.9 The government's absolute interest in some lands constitute estates Which like the stool or oman lands need to be considered separately from the other estates in our tenure.  This is so for, in considering what estate to be accorded registrable titles, the vacant government or stool or oman land poses a unique problem. It is the owner of the estate who initiates registration in all the known systems, the English10 as well as those introduced by Sir Robert Torrens in Australia11 at the turn of the last century.  In the system anticipated, the government or the stool will have to initiate the registration of the titles to those vacant lands. Registration will doubtlessly entail great expense. it is economically unsound to expect the stool or the government suo muto to  incur the expenditure of registering a vacant land only for the sake of putting it  on the register.  It is here that the bugbear of title registration rears its head. If all the stools should have to register all their lands Compulsorily, the perennial boundary question will surface. Litigation will assume greater proportions. The system is bound, in those circumstances to wither away in a quagmire of law suits. However, contrary to what  Woodman said, when he stated,12 “. . . this is one of the areas where texts on English law are not greatly helpful to an understanding of Ghanaian law" it is to the foreign systems where we may seek light to illuminate our path. To understand the present system of merely registering instruments affecting land, we may not need any external guidance. But even with this system which is not one of our indigenous customary arrangements, can it be said without qualification that we do not need example? However, it is submitted that we shall have to fall on outside aid when the present simplistic system is jettisoned and a system of recording in a public office from time to time, transactions with, or the fact of ownership of some interest in land; and a state guarantee of indefeasibility in respect of those registered titles is introduced.

But the point about vacant stool, oman and government lands should not be lost to us.  It is submitted that the difficulty would be obviated by providing that land while it remains vested in the government or stool, and in which no private person has acquired any interest should not be registered or conversely, it is only land in which persons have acquired rights that can be registered 13.

The six categories of estates enunciated in Ollennu's classification cannot all be made independently registrable titles under the system envisaged by the present writer. The allodial is the highest title. Under title registration, the allodial will include the absolute titles held by individuals or stools and the transfer of them. The so-called sub-paramount estate has different potential in the states where it exists. In the Ashanti Region where the unique position of the Golden Stool gives overriding authority to the Asantehene, it has a limited and subordinate nature. However, in the Kwahu and Akim Abuakwa States where "this interest assumed a status virtually indistinguishable from the stool's dominium"14 it may be registered as the allodial title itself and the paramount stool's jurisdictional interest may be recorded in the encumbrance register.  In Ashanti, it may be registered as an encumbrance. The usufructuary is a customary estate, which the impact of modern commercial farming and socio-economic factors, has lessened in status.  It is not larger than a lease of 50 years, when its owner decides to incorporate his interest in a document and the user is agricultural or any purpose incidental thereto. For residential user, the usufructuary is 99 years like other leases. It is submitted that like the lease, the usufruct should be made a registrable title under the name customary lease.  But the life of registrable leases need not be less than twenty years. The point here is that the non-registrable lesser interest may either be recorded as an encumbrance or a notice in the certificate of title. The abusa or abunu tenancies and the customary licences need not be accorded registrable independent titles. These arrangements intrinsically do not affect the land or the estate in the land. They relate to the crops cultivated and nurtured by the tenant or the licensee. Laconically, Ollennu observed,15 "An abusa or abunu tenant's interest is the right to cultivate the land and to enjoy the proceeds; he does not acquire title or estate in any portion of the land." Furthermore, apart from the few which were presented in the registry when litigation arose about them these agreements are not usually put in writing.

Under section (1) of the Conveyancing Decree, 1973 (N.R.C.D. 175), it is provided:

"1. (1) A transfer of an interest in land shall be by a writing signed by the person making the transfer or by his agent duly authorised in writing, unless relieved against the need for such a writing by the provisions of section 3."

This provision has not improved the documentation of these agreements. It should not, therefore, surprise anyone why the scheme for recording the oral transfers in the district court registries under the Decree has still not been implemented.  The scheme was created without due regard to the administrative and technical problems involved in its implementation. The need for providing in the district court registries surveying sections or departments which could undertake the checking,  preparing and comparing of the maps or site plans which will accompany the transfers to be recorded; as well as the technical and professional staff to manage the registries appear to have been overlooked. Even if the system had worked, it could not have had any beneficent influence on title registration by Way of certainty or assurance of title. is provision has not improved the documentation of these agreements. The pledge is a species of the mortgage. It exists as a charge or encumbrance on the title; and cannot have independent existence.

Under section 10 of the Limitation Decree, 197216 provision is made for any rights in land which are not pursued within twelve years after those rights accrue, to be statute-barred.  The practical effect of this provision is that where a stranger or subject occupies a portion of any land without let or hindrance for twelve years, he acquires a legally enforceable interest in that piece of land.  Under title registration this interest has to be accorded registrable title.  It is suggested here that this interest be accorded the possessory title. To recapitulate, it is suggested that our registrable titles shall consist of:

(a) the allodial;

(b) the usufructuary or customary lease;

(c) the lease; and

(d) the possessory title.

III—OVERRIDING INTERESTS

Under section 2 of the Minerals Act, 1962 (Act 126) and the Forest Ordinance (Cap. 157), rights in our forests and timber and in all minerals are vested in the President in trust for the people of Ghana. These interests which are always binding on any acquirer of any other estate in our land may be treated as overriding, interests.17  This is to argue that even though there will be no reference whatsoever to them in the register, any purchaser of a registered estate would be bound by them.  The common law rights in profit à prendre, water courses, water18 and the sea beds will be treated as overriding interests. Easements, covenants and public rights of way may be recorded as encumbrances but in the case of easements they must exist in alieno solo, and have some natural connection with the estate as being for its benefit 19 while leases of less than 20 years duration and other minor interests which will not be accorded the status of registrable titles may be put on the certificate of title as notices.

IV—THE REGISTRABLE ESTATE

An estate may either be identified by a general or specific description and a map made to statutory specifications.  Section 4 of our Land Registry Act, 1962 20 provides:

"4. No instrument, except a will or probate, shall be registered unless it contains a description (which may be by reference to a plan) which, in the opinion of the registrar, is sufficient to enable the location and boundaries of the land to which it relates to be identified or a sufficient reference to the date and particulars of registration of an instrument affecting the same land and already registered."

The Survey Act, 196221 also provides under section 16:

"16.  A surveyor may, if the boundaries of a land under survey have not already been correctly marked in the prescribed manner by the owner of the land, mark out the boundaries of the land according to the manner of marking prescribed by regulations."

The regulations require that the markings must be to the minute accurate.  Although this tradition of preparing plans and maps exists everywhere in the world, it is only in the Torrens and the countries which have adopted the latter system which use it in their system of registration.  Under section 114 of New South Wales Act, 1900, it is provided:

(1) The Registrar-General may require the proprietor applying to have any land brought under the provisions of this Act, or desiring to transfer or otherwise to deal with the same or any portion thereof, to deposit at the registry office a map or plan of such land, certified by a licensed surveyor in manner aforesaid.

(2) If the said land or the portion thereof proposed to be transferred or dealt with is of less area than one acre then such map or plan shall be on scale not less than one inch to two chains.

(3) If such land or the portion thereof about to be transferred or dealt with is of greater area than one acre, but not exceeding five acres, then such map shall be upon a scale not less than one inch to five chains.

(4) If such land or the portion thereof as aforesaid is of greater area than five acres, but not exceeding eighty acres, then such map or plan shall be upon a scale not less than one inch to ten chains.

(5) If such land or the portion thereof as aforesaid is of greater area than eighty acres then such map or plan shall be upon a scale of one inch to twenty chains.

(6) If such proprietor neglects or refuses to comply with such requirement it shall not be incumbent on the Registrar-General to proceed with the bringing of such land under the provisions of this Act nor with the registration of such transfer or lease.

(7) Subsequent sub-division of the same may be delineated on the map or plan of the same so deposited, if such map or plan be upon a sufficient scale in accordance with the provisions herein contained, and the correctness of the delineated of each such sub division shall be acknowledged in manner prescribed for the case of the deposit of an original map or plan.

(8) Such maps or plans shall, if required by the Registrar-General, be in duplicate.22

The type of accurate plans and maps required under the Torrens Acts and to some extent under our present system ties registration to surveyors. With this type of plan the descriptions of the registrable estates embodied in the conveyance or lease need to be specific and technically accurate. This makes it urgent, among other matters, for the surveyor to establish the ownership of the thin lines existing between contiguous or adjacent owners. There is also the fact that whenever a transfer of a registered estate is being made a new survey has to be carried out to ascertain those boundary lines.  In a shifting kaleidoscope of development and re-development, of zoning and re-zoning the surveying exercise can become very complex. In an advanced area like Victoria in Australia, the system provides the academic student with much intellectual satisfaction. The flats of a skyscraper when they come to be transferred are defined and described with the particularity and accuracy required by the Acts. 

Ghana does not experience any land famine at present and the culture of skyscrapers is yet to dawn here.  However, the new flats which are being built for workers by the Social Security and National Insurance Trust and other corporations will present a problem for the registrar if the tradition of accurate or specific description is maintained without modification.

The English on the other hand operate the general description instead of the specific. Section 76 of the English Act23 provides:

"76.  Registered land may be described-

(a)  by means of a verbal description and a filed plan or general map, based on the ordinance map; or.

(b) by reference to a deed or other document, a copy or extract whereof is filed at the registry, containing a sufficient description, and a plan or map thereof; or

(c) otherwise as the applicant for registration may desire, and the registrar, or, if the applicant prefers, the court, may approve, regard being had to ready identification of parcels, correct descriptions of boundaries, and, so far as may be, uniformity of practice; but the boundaries of all freehold land and all requisite details in relation to the same, shall whenever practicable, be entered on the register or filed plan, or general map, and the filed plan, if any, or general map shall be used for assisting the identification of the land."

The general description in practice requires that the registrable property is identified by reference to its number and the street along which it is situate or by reference to the name of the locality where there are no streets. To illustrate, where Blackacre is a freehold and is situated on Nyamebekyere Road and numbered 15, it will be described as "Number 15, Nyamebekyere." This avoids the unnecessary technicalities as well as the cumbersome need for frequent re-surveying. It has, however, its problems. There ought to be a systematic naming of all streets and a strict adherence to planning rules and regulations in the towns and cities. With farm lands and others, the records on them available in the Lands Department ought to be re-cast to show some symmetry and new grants in the same localities must be by reference to the existing ones where linkage is physically possible.  The tradition of specific description has taken a firm root in this country and to suggest a change requires an over-powering reason or argument. First, the simplicity of the general plan; secondly, it's also inexpensive. It could be linked with our postal system to greater advantage.  The specific description is afterall not as accurate as its protagonist would want us believe.  Take a random description in a lease from the Lands Department Registry with all its garbage:

"All that piece or parcel of land known as Plot No. 11, Block P in the Gosokrom layout situate in the Sunyani District of the Brong-Ahafo Region of the Republic of Ghana bounded on or towards the north-east by Plot No. 12 and measuring 150 ft. on that side on or towards the south-west by a road and measuring 105 ft. on that side thus enclosing an approximate area of 14.625 square feet be the same several dimensions little more or less as the same piece or parcel of land is more particularly delineated on Plan No. L.D.B. 84/SWI: 1883 attached hereto and thereon shown edged pink."

The two traditions can conveniently be married under our system without much difficulty.  In the cities and towns and for all residential or industrial users the general description must be adopted.  And the specific can take care of the farms in the forest and other areas for the time being.

V—THE PROPRIETOR

Who may register an estate?  The owner of any of the estates discussed above may apply to the registrar for registration of that title. Under the English system, for instance, it is provided24:

“4. Where the title to be registered is a title to a freehold estate in land—

(a) any estate owner holding an estate in fee simple (including a tenant for life, statutory owner, personal representative, or trustee for sale) whether subject or not to incumbrances; or

(b) any other person (not being a mortgagee where there is a subsisting right of redemption or a person who has merely contracted to buy land) who is entitled to require a legal estate in fee simple whether subject or not to incumbrances, to be vested in him;

may apply to the registrar to be registered in respect of such estate, or, in the case of a person not in a fiduciary position, to have registered in his stead any nominee, as proprietor with an absolute title or with a possessory title:

Provided that-

(i) Where an absolute title is required the applicant or his nominee shall not be registered as proprietor until and unless the title is approved by the registrar;

(ii) Where a possessory title is required the applicant or his nominee may be registered as proprietor on giving such evidence of title and serving such notices, if any, as may for the time being be prescribed;

(iii) If, on an application for registration with possessory title, the registrar is satisfied as to the title to the freehold estate, he may register it as absolute, whether the applicant consents to such registration or not, but in that case no higher fee shall be charged than would have been charged for registration with possessory title."

The Torrens system provides in the various Acts that only proprietors in whom the fee simple of the land is vested in possession can bring an application for the land to be brought under the system.25

Provisions are available in both the Torrens and English systems which prescribe also the procedures for the registration of transactions subsequent to the initial or first registration. The transfer of whole or part rent charges and mortgage or legal charges, etc. are adequately provided for.26 Because our present system is principally concerned with instruments rather than estates and their proprietors, the issue as to how many names should appear on the register or certificate of title has never been squarely faced. But the complex and heterogeneous nature of corporate ownership of immovable property in Ghanaian society necessitates some form of control on the number of proprietors whose names should appear on the title register and the certificate. The customary law doctrine27 that neither the chief nor the head of family acting independently can give a valid discharge of stool or family property underscores the need for the control.  It is submitted that in the case of stool property, the chief and two other principal elders could appear on the register as proprietors.  With any other case, four names must be the limit.  The death or destoolment of heads of family or chiefs should be brought to the notice of the registrar for the necessary amendments.

The Conveyancing Decree, 1975, has changed the previous positions where there was a presumption of joint tenancy to that of tenancy in common.  The Decree provides under section 14 (3):

"(3) A conveyance of an interest in land to two or more persons, except a conveyance in trust, shall create an interest in common and not in joint tenancy, unless it is expressed in such a conveyance that the transferees shall take jointly, or as joint tenants, or to them and the survivor of them, or unless it manifestly appears from the tenor of the instrument that it was intended to create an interest in joint tenancy."

The reason for this disastrous turn as appears in the Conveyancing Decree, 1973, Memorandum, p. (iii) is "to bring the presumption into conformity with the probable intentions of parties to such transactions in Ghana." The argument in favour of the change is entirely theoretical. No evidence from any field research was offered to buttress that position. On the contrary, the trend towards monogamous marriage and the desire of both the educated and illiterate Ghanaians to bequeath their estate to their children offer a stronger case against tenancy in common.  On the purely economic plane, for a developing country such as Ghana to tie down capital as manifested in section 14 (3) of N.R.C.D. 173 is suicidal. To the student of land registration, it is a retrograde step. Too many names cumber the register. In a patrilineal system the effect of the section can lead to absurd results.

If A in patrilineal system dies intestate and his Blackacre vests in his seven children as tenants in common and each child produces three more children the proprietorship register will be choked after two generations. The existing law on the capacity of infants, lunatics and incorporated bodies is settled, but with the other amorphous organisations such as the unorthodox churches, football clubs, etc. their capacity is not easily determined.  But whenever any of these bodies decide to own and register an immovable property the position is (as stated by Azu Crabbe C.J.) in the case of Ghana Muslims Representative Council v. Salifu28:

"It is a principle of the common law that property can be vested only in definite persons, and where a body of persons desire to hold and enjoy land, there are two methods by which this can be done: (a) an Act of Parliament or a charter must be procured to turn the indefinite body of persons into a definite, albeit an artificial, person, called a corporation, or (b) the land must be vested in trustees upon trust to hold and manage the land for the benefit of the proposed beneficiaries."

Apart from the common law arrangements, the Trustees (Incorporation) Act, 1962 29 provides such bodies as have no legal personality, with capacity after registration under the Act.  The Act does not limit the number of persons which may be sufficient for any given trust.  With title registration this becomes imperative.  In the Ghana Muslims Representative Council's case, for instance, there were as many as eight trustees.  This means that all the eight trustees must act jointly and together to be able to acquire or give a good discharge of any immovable property of the council.  This number is too large.  It is submitted that it should be made possible under our title registration to restrict, where there is a superfluity of names, the number to four or less.  The wisdom in keeping the number small must be apparent.  It is easier for four or less people to meet and execute a deed than eight or more. Behind the whole idea of title registration lies the need for simplicity and quickness in transferring landed property.  Thus Professor Cheshire tersely and pungently observed: 30

“One of the results of this desire was to give an impetus to land legislation, and it will be as well to state at the outset the main idea which lay at the back of the legislation that resulted.  It was nothing more than a desire to render the sale of land as rapid and simple a matter as in the sale of goods or of shares. A layman knows that if he desires to transfer to another the ownership of a chattel, such as a motor car or a picture, the only requirement is the making of a contract which names the parties, records their intention, describes the article to be sold and states the price to be paid”

VI—THE JUDICIAL VERSUS ADMINISTRATIVE PROOF OF TITLE

There are two broad methods by which title can be proved for registration purposes: the judicial and administrative.

First, the judicial approach. This means that the title of the land intended to be brought under the system has to be proved before a court or before the registrar acting in a judicial capacity in a manner similar to the procedure in a civil trial.  Under the Concession Ordinance,31 provision was made under section 6 for the court to inquire into and certify as valid or invalid any concession. The procedure under the Concession Ordinance has characteristics like those under the Torrens legislation.  The draftsman of our Land Registry Act, 1962, appears to have been largely influenced by the various judicial methods available under the Torrens system. The Part IV of the Act which has not yet been brought into force has close linguistic and structural affinities with most of the Torrens Acts.32 In our Act, the registrar was to have combined the purely ministerial function with a judicial authority. In his judicial capacity, his decision were to be appealable to the High Court.  Professor Kwamina Bentsi-Enchill went to much pains to castigate the registrar's function arguing that that function could be more conveniently and effectually performed by the normal courts which have facilities all over the regions33  However, in the Torrens system, which the late Professor appears to admire so much, the function is performed in less contentious case as by the registrar. The procedure for proving title under the Torrens Act may be said to have three main objects in view:

(a) To ascertain the title put forward by the applicant. This is a matter of conveyancing and land law.  The exercise is purely ministerial.

(b) To ascertain the land to which the title relates in so far as it can be delineated on a map or plan. This is primarily the work of the surveyor. And with the specific tradition that  boundaries are proved strictly. 

(c) To discover adverse claims to the land.  This, if adverse claims are discovered, leads to litigation.

With the first of the three enumerated objects it is proposed to defer consideration until the discussion of the administrative procedure.   The surveying requirement for ascertainment of the land which is the subject of registration has been considered. The procedure for actuating the third aim of the Torrens Act is elaborate. The application is advertised in the Government Gazette in Sydney and the districts after the officers of the registry have cleared all the doubtful points about the title. The advertisement lasts a month during which time anybody whose interest may be prejudiced by the registration has to enter a caveat.  It is only persons who can bring themselves under any one of the under-mentioned categories who may caveat against registration:

(1)  persons who have the right to concur in or make an application in respect of the same land;

(2)  the interest of the caveator must be an estate or interest in land; and

(3)  the interest of the caveator must be such as can be recorded in the certificate of title.

Upon a caveat being lodged nothing more can be done by the registrar until the caveat is either withdrawn by the caveator, or lapses or an order is made by the court with respect to it.  After the caveat has been lodged, the caveator may do one of the following:

(a) he may take proceedings directly against the applicant to establish his title to the land and notify the registry; or

(b) he may take proceedings against the registry officer and obtain an injunction restraining them, absolutely or until further order, from bringing the land under the system.

On the other hand, the applicant, may either state a case for the opinion of the court or take out a summons for the caveator to show cause why the caveat should not be removed.  If the title of the applicant is finally approved of, the applicant will be granted a certificate of title34:

Application for first registration under the English system must in ordinary cases be made by delivering at the registry an application in writing in one or other of the forms prescribed by the Land Registration Rules, 1925, stating what nature of title is required35. The application must be accompanied (unless the registrar otherwise directs) by -

(i) all such original deeds and documents relating to the title as the applicant has in his possession or under his control, including opinions of counsel, abstracts of title, contracts for or conditions of sale, requisitions, replies, and other like documents, in regard to the title, and the registrar may require the applicant and his solicitor to make separate affidavits or declarations that all documents and facts affecting the title have been disclosed;

(ii)  a copy or sufficient abstract of the latest document of title, not being a document of record;

(iii) sufficient particulars, by plan or otherwise, to enable the land to be fully identified on the Ordinance Map or Land Registry General Map; and

(iv) a list in triplicate of all documents so delivered36  or if the land is leasehold, the lease, where it is in the possession or control of the applicant, and in other cases a copy or abstract thereto, or other sufficient evidence of its contents, must be delivered with the application37.

Where the applicant has no documents of title in his possession or under his control, and the registrar is satisfied that he is in possession or receipt of the rents and profits of the land, a statutory declaration by him as to the time for which he and his predecessors have been in such possession or receipt may be taken as prima facie evidence of his right to apply for registration as first proprietor.38  The prescribed forms of  application for first registration include declarations to the effect that a full disclosure of incumbrances of all kinds affecting the title to the land and of all facts material to such title has been made.   All incumbrances, etc. to which the land is subject are to be entered on the register whether the title registered be absolute, qualified or possessory39.

Where the application is for an absolute or good leasehold title, the title will be examined by the registrar, or by an examiner of titles, in accordance with the usual conveyancing practice40, but the examination may be modified as the registrar thinks fit where (1) the land has been sold or purchased under an order of the court; or (2) it shall appear to the registrar that the title has been sufficiently investigated on a transaction for value41. Before any registration is completed with absolute or good leasehold title an advertisement of the intended registration must be inserted in the London Gazette and in such local papers as the registrar may require, but unless the registrar otherwise directs, such advertisements are not necessary where the applicant is a purchaser on a sale, completed within the year preceding the application of land in a compulsory area, or the applicant is an original lessee.42 Any person may object to the registration, and any such objection is to be heard and decided by the registrar subject to appeal to the court43.

No registration with absolute title can be made unless and until the registrar approves of the title to the freehold, but if the applicant so desires, he may be provisionally registered with possessory title pending the investigation of his title44. No registration of leaseholds with absolute title can be made unless and until the title both to the leasehold and to the freehold, and to any intermediate leasehold, have been approved by the registrar; but for registration with good leasehold title it is sufficient if the registrar approves with good leasehold – it is not necessary  for the applicant to prove the title to the freehold45.

Whatever be the nature of the title with which the land is registered, on completion of the registration, a land certificate is handed to the applicant or, if he prefers, deposited in the registry46. The land certificate contains a description of  the land, the nature of the title with which it is registered, i.e. absolute, qualified, good leasehold or possessory and copies of the entries in the register and of the filed plan, if any.47 The land certificate, if not deposited in the registry, must be produced to the registrar on every entry in the register of a disposition by the registered proprietor, and a note of such entry officially endorsed thereon.48

The procedure for registering instruments under the Ghanaian Land Registry Act consists of:

(a) The instrument on presentation has to be proved by the oath of the grantors, or of the grantee or one of the grantees or one of the subscribing witnesses.49 A probate or judge's certificate may be registered without proof.50

(b) That the registrar places on the instrument a certificate which specifies the year, month, day and hour of proof of presentation and registration.51

(c) Then the registrar files a duplicate copy of the instrument in the registry.52

The inadequacies of our system are apparent.  Not only is it dilatory, but it is also inefficient to the extent that it does not provide devices like notices,53 cautions, 54 inhibitions,55 restrictions,56 etc.; and of course the content of the registered instrument is not guaranteed.  The new title registration in Ghana may adopt the English system in so far as the proof of the title of the property to be registered is concerned.  The reason behind this submission is not hard to find: It is inexpensive since it is intrinsically less litigious.  It is cheaper and quicker-the hallmarks of a proper title registration system.

VII—THE STATE GUARANTEE OF TITLE

The hub of title registration is that the title of the property which has been brought under the system is indefeasible and state-guaranteed. This concept has two aspects.57

These are positive and negative warranties. A negative warranty assumes that no title of equal validity to the registered title is in existence since registration is the one and only method of gaining a title. This means that titles under the system until they have been successfully attacked and removed from the register remain paramount.  The security of title which exists under such a system is due to the facility with which the antecedent chain of title for many years back can be examined.  Each link in the chain of title has to be strictly and conclusively proved.  The weakness in a system which advocates a negative warranty only consists in the possibility of the failure to prove the validity of any one transference in the chain.

On the other hand, a system which postulates a positive warranty guarantees the title.  The state provides an assurance fund from which owners whose interests may be prejudiced by a wrongful or improper registration are to be indemnified.  There are difficulties with this system too, since it allows estates of validity equal to the registered title to be created.  It is submitted that our system should combine the two warranties so that our registered titles shall be comprehensively assured.

The State assurance of title is based on the doctrine of ordinary insurance.  The concept of insurable interest, however, is not part of title registration. The fees charged by the English Registry for instance, are graduated in the same manner as insurance premiums. Under the Ghanaian present system, however, the fee is flat.58

The reason for the graduation of the fees is apparent.  The value the properties which will be registered is different and the compensation that will be paid when anything goes wrong will not be the same!

VIII—THE REGISTER

Under the system operating in Ghana there is no separate document you may meaningfully call a register in the sense that the term is applied to title registration.  There is a large notebook in which such basic information as the name of the grantor, grantee, the terms of lease, rent or value and the nature of instrument are recorded.  The so-called title file contains the mutation sheet and a copy of the instrument registered.

The English certificate of title comprises three sections.59 The first part, the property register, contains all interests beneficial to the property. The property is described in this part. Such interests as dominant rights of way, right of air, mines and such like appear in the property register. The proprietorship register contains the name of the registered proprietors and notices, if any are entered.  In the third part of the register, the charges register, will be found all. incumbrances on the registered property, charges, servient rights of way, restrictions, cautions, inhibitions and all disabilities attached to the property are recorded here.  In the English system when a property is mortgaged by a legal charge the certificate of title which was issued to the proprietor on first registration is retained in the registry and a new certificate of charge is prepared for the chargee. The detailed discussion of some of the features of the English system makes it possible for meaningful comparisons to be made and pertinent conclusions drawn as to what form our title registration should take.  The adoption of the features highlighted here will not affect our land tenure system in any substantial way. Our conveyancing practices will undoubtedly need undergo fundamental changes. But most of the changes that the introduction of title registration will entail will be taken care of by the draftsman of the relevant legislation; by way, of preparing the forms to be used.  The problem that solicitors may face in this connection is that they will have to familiarise themselves with the new forms.

IX—THE EQUITABLE DOCTRINE OF PRIORITY

The doctrine is pithily stated in the Latin maxim: Qui prio est tempore porior est jure.  Our land registry has operated basically on a combination of two principles.  First, no instrument shall be effective unless it is registered.60  This means that to the extent that an instrument which affects interest in  land was executed by the rightful owner of the estate contained therein and all the other legal requirements have been met; the instrument is effective. In any other circumstance the instrument is ineffective, registration notwithstanding. It bears mention, however, that by the authority of Ntem v. Ankwandah61 an instrument may be deemed to have been constructively registered when it is presented for registration and the registrar without lawful authority declines to register it.  In the case of Ntem the facts were that the plaintiff bought a piece of land from the Akumajay stool and when she presented the instrument for registration, the registrar refused to register it arguing that Nii Ayikai III who executed the deed with a number of the stool elders was then not gazetted. The Court of Appeal, per Apaloo C.J. stated62:

"The question is: What position resulted from the refusal of the land's registrar to register the document? As I said before, the land's registrar has no power to refuse to register the plaintiff's document on the ground ascribed to him, namely, that the plaintiff's vendor was not gazetted.  That reason is tantamount to saying that the plaintiff's grantor had no power to make the grant.  Such a power was conceived for the registrar by section 20(b) of the Land Registry Act, 1962.  But for reasons which I do not care to speculate upon, when the Act was brought into operation, the registrar's power to refuse registration under section 20 (a) and (b) was excepted.  The Land Registry Act (Commencement) Instrument, 1965 (L.I. 450), which brought the Act into operation states:

‘The 1st day of May, 1965, is hereby appointed as the day on which Part IV (except paragraphs (a) and (b) of section 20) of the Land Registry Act, 1962 (Act 122) shall come into operation.'

These paragraphs of section 20 have not been brought  into operation since. That being so, the land's registrar could not lawfully refuse to register the document. On the contrary, section 11 of the Act not only obliges the registrar to keep a  register but imposes a duty on him to register ‘all instruments presented to him in the prescribed form.' He could refuse to accept for registration copies of instruments on grounds stated in section 12 (4) of the Act but these have no bearing in this case.  I do not doubt that if the plaintiff had brought mandamus proceedings to compel the registrar to perform his statutory duty of registering the instrument, she would have succeeded.  As is often said, the object of that remedy, is to supply defects of justice: see Halsbury's Laws of England

(3rd ed.), Vol.  II, p. 84.

I do not consider that the fact that the plaintiff did institute proceedings to compel the public officer to perform his statutory duty should constrain us to hold that her title is invalid.  Registration of a deed under Act 122 does not constitute a state guaranteed title.  As the deed was stamped, it was admissible in evidence in proof of title and that title was adjudged, registration aside, to be good.  I think the only way in which justice can be done as between these parties, is to hold that the plaintiff's title was constructively registered.  Equity regards as done, that which ought to have been done."

This classical example of judicial legislation has brought into sharp focus the weakness and the near uselessness of our system.  To provide for mere negative warranty which allows a title of equal validity co-exist, as it is in our case, makes the system a trap for land purchasers. And the registered instruments are not guaranteed!

The second principle on which our system operates is that regulation is notice in rem63  These two principles have been the foundation on which the late Professor Bentsi-Enchill erected his abstruse race notice-statute scheme.64 Not only does the race-notice arrangement not add anything to the achievement of assurance of title, but it also smacks of a pirate rule which allows the smarter and stronger pirate who reaches a booty first to take it. Priority under English land registration is combined with the search for applications for first registration. Here the search protects and gives priority to the applicant for a period of fourteen days. During the fourteen days period the first registration will be completed. But with the registration of charges or mortgages65, the basic rule of priorities normally applies.  However, transactions emanating from the Crown66, the councils, benefices and ecclesiastical commissioners can take precedent over or postpone an earlier charge. The whole arrangement has made the doctrine of priority the handmaid of title registration.  It is submitted that our envisaged title registration should draw inspiration from the English system rather than be bugged down by the esoteric and impractical race-notice arrangement.

X—SETTING UP A LAND REGISTRY

The foregoing discourse sets out in bald outline the theoretical basis of a legislation for the establishment of title registration in Ghana. The administration of the proposed registry needs to be described briefly. Title registration is essentially conveyancing requiring a thorough knowledge of the land law.  In the examination of the titles the registrar may have to act, in some cases, in a quasi-judicial capacity. It is principally for these two reasons, among others, that a judge of the Court of Appeal or a lawyer of not less than twelve years standing should be made the Chief Lands Registrar.

There shall be ten registries at all the regional capitals. It is in expedient to start registration to cover the whole country simultaneously.

The reason is not far to seek.  There is just not available in the country the army of technocrats and professionals who can efficiently operate the system at present.  But article 189 (2) (b) of the Constitution, 1979, provides that the execution of all documents shall be made in the regions.  The policy underpinnings of the sub-article is to decentralise all matters affecting land administration.  For this reason, it has been argued that title registration should start in all the regions at the same time. Thus by this argument every area in the country will be a compulsorily registrable area! I differ on this score.  To begin with, the Constitution does not mention title registration either directly or impliedly.67  And it bears mention that our Constitution transcends the normal practice in constitution making where only the framework for the administration of the country is set out.  In our Constitution, details about even where documents affecting land should be executed have been provided for!

Thus the failure to mention title registration in the Constitution takes that activity out of its ambit.  In that circumstance, title registration may be started through a piecemeal approach rather than the so-called revolutionary method. The gradual process should recommend itself to any keen student of land registration for in all the cases where the process has been rushed the system did not work well, if it worked at all. The history of the English Land Registry teaches a great lesson in this regard. The fiasco of the registration of oral grant under our Conveyancing Decree is a living example. It is therefore submitted that when the Act has been passed, registration can start in the Greater Accra, Ashanti and Brong-Ahafo Regions initially.

Each registry has to be headed by a Regional Lands Registrar who shall be a lawyer of not less than ten years standing. There shall be an administrative manager for each region who shall not be below the rank of a Principal Assistant Secretary.  The land registry should properly have been under the Chief Justice,68 but since the Land Commission has exclusive jurisdiction over land administration, it will in the scheme of things live under the umbrella of that commission.  The emolument of the Chief Lands Registrar shall be charged on the Consolidated Fund.  This will insulate him from political manipulation.

The conditions of service of the Chief Lands Registrar ought to be the same as that of an Appeal Court Judge, but he may not retire on his salary.  This security in tenure of office will attract lawyers of the right calibre to work in the registry.

XI—ECONOMICS OF TITLE REGISTRATION

The cost of setting up a title registration will be paid by the various fees which the registry will charge in no time. But what is more important are those imponderable benefits which the assurance of title will give to commerce, industry and farming.  Investors will not need to worry about the validity of the title of the land they build their industries on.  The interminable delays which have characterised the present system will belong to history.  One thing which title registration does that it takes the whole activity from bureaucratic red-tapism to commerce where the concept of profit dominates.  Here, efficiency and dispatch are the hallmarks. 

But it is pertinent to point out that care should be taken in the preparation of the forms that may be used in the application for first registration and subsequent dealings.  To provide for too many forms has its problems.  This will require large areas for storage. There is not only the expenditure in building the storage, but the cost of paper has to be in the fore-front of our consideration.  The main statute as well as the regulations should be prepared with two main points in view- to wit, economy of size and clarity in legal draftsmanship.  Too many sections in any piece of legislation daunts even the academic recluse to read, let alone the practitioner who has to spend most of his time in the court room.
