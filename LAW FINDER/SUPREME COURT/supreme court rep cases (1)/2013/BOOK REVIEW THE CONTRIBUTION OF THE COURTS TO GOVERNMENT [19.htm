BOOK REVIEW: THE CONTRIBUTION OF THE COURTS TO GOVERNMENT [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 239—243

 

by A. N. E. Amissah, M.A (Oxon). [Oxford Clarendon Press 1981 x and 382 pp.]

DR. KWAME FRIMPONG*

IN undertaking this review, I found myself in an uncomfortable position for the fact that I have had a great deal of admiration for Mr. Justice A. N. E. Amissah (as he then was). I owed my graduate studies both in Ghana and in the United States basically to his influence and direction at the time when he was the Dean of the Faculty of Law of the University of Ghana. In addition, he has been an outstanding public figure in Ghana. His rapid and successful career at the Attorney-General's Department has been unsurpassed and has won the admiration of many. He has been one of the most dynamic, articulate and able judges in the history of the Ghanaian judiciary. Finally, he has been a great legal scholar. In this sense I was not too sure whether I could draw a distinction between a possible biased or over-critical review of his work. It is hoped that an objective appraisal of the book has been achieved.

The book can be divided into three main parts. The first part, chapters 1 through 4, deals generally with the functions and experiences of the judiciary under a variety of nations. Considering the functions of the court under chapter 1, the author identifies, among other things, dispute settlement, law enforcement and the protection of constitutional rights.  In addition, he points out two other functions which have direct bearing on the court’s relationship with the other branches of government, namely, judicial review and advisory opinion which is of a modern invention.

The court in the pursuit of its role invariably runs into direct conflict with the executive.  The relationship in this light is fully examined in chapter 2.  From the nature of the discussion and on the basis of the world-wide examples provided, the author leaves the reader in no doubt that the executive’s uneasiness to judicial decisions (especially when they affect the policies of the ruling authority) is a normal phenomenon with most societies.  In order to avoid or minimize the unpleasant experience of the court’s decisions going against them most governments try to find a way of getting their supporters or sympathisers on the bench. A classic example of this, cited by the author was President Roosevelt’s “court packing plans" (p. 52).

Conscious of the dangers posed by the executive's action, particularly in relation to the individual liberty, the author reveals that most of the independent Commonwealth African countries have tried various methods, independent of the executive, in the appointments to the bench. This is aimed at securing the independence of the judiciary. But from the author's point of view, it seems no approach offers a full-proof solution.

The judiciary under colonial period in the former Commonwealth African countries is taken up in chapter 3. In this period, one could hardly draw a distinction between the executive and the judiciary as the latter formed an integral part of the former and, in fact, actively participated in most of its functions. The concept of the independence of the judiciary was thus meaningless. The problem posed by this uncomfortable relationship and the whole impact of colonialism, Amissah argues, have transcended the colonial period into the post-independence era resulting in a situation where the independent countries cannot find ready answers.

The problem after independence is highlighted in chapter 4. In almost all cases the British left the scene by imposing on the newly independent nations, contrary to her colonial practice, a government of separation of powers where the judiciary was expected to be independent of the executive. But the author points out that damage had already been done. The judges, used to the old practice, had become subservient to the executive and therefore could not assert its independence. Further, trained under and used exclusively to the English law, the judges relied solely on English decisions and precedents at the expense of local ones.

Amissah here discusses in depth an interesting but a controversial issue: Whether political consideration is to be taken into account in the appointment of a judge as a means of ensuring the outcome of his decisions. He does not strictly take sides but merely provides the arguments for and against situations warranting the consideration of political inclination. While the discussion generally is academically interesting, it is doubtful whether its extent is worth the while. What Amissah fails to identify is that in the final analysis an appointed judge remains himself as an individual. He may choose to go any direction as Amissah concedes in the case of the appointment of Chief Justice Warren by President Eisenhower. An appointed judge, as his oath requires of him, should be himself, independent and impartial in his decisions.

On this issue, Lord Denning has written. "Suppose the future Prime Minister should seek to pack the Bench with his own extreme political colour. Would they be tools of his hand?" He answers with an emphatic "No." "Every judge in his appointment discards politics and all prejudices." (What Next in the Law, London, Butterworths, 1982 at p. 330.) And the author at p. 105 seems to concede this point. If any judge fails to behave in the way that Lord Denning sees him or as Justice Amissah wants him to be then it is simply the fact that he is “human being” with all his weaknesses. As such nobody can clearly foresee the future behaviour of an appointed judge. This makes any consideration that goes into the appointment of a judge with the aim to achieving a particular result a futile exercise.

In chapters 5, 6 and 7, the author examines the role of the judiciary under the first three governments in Ghana since independence. Under the Nkrumah era, 1957-66, the independent and impartial judiciary established by the independence Constitution that the citizen looked up to for the protection of his liberty and fundamental rights failed him miserably in the Re Akoto case. The same judiciary to re-assert itself during the Second Treason Trial case by acquitting the accused persons charged with treason. The Nkrumah regime reacted sharply by passing a law which not only nullified the court's decision but also gave the President the power to remove judges. From this time onwards, the independence of the judiciary was completely destroyed.

Nothing of a major significance occurred regarding the court's actions during the military rule of 1966-69. The only case worthy of consideration, as the author points out, was the Salifu case in which both the Supreme Court and the High Court reached different conclusions regarding the executive's interference with individual liberty. Surprisingly, some measure of judicial independence was discernible during this period of military rule, far from the normal nature of military rule. The author suggests that the seemingly independence of the judiciary was due largely to the fact that the judiciary recognised its limit: that it was operating under a military regime which had no constitutional restraint in its dealings with the judiciary. So long as the judiciary refrained from decisions which might embarrass executive, its independence was ensured.

The court found new power as soon as a constitutional government was established in 1969. Under the Busia regime, the court came out boldly by throwing its weight solidly behind a citizen’s claim that his dismissal by the government was unlawful. The executive expressed its displeasure when the Prime Minister in a radio and television broadcast condemned the decision.

In his final chapter Amissah argues that it is too late for the Commonwealth African countries to throw away the established court system  inherited from the colonialists  It is not only impracticable, but also unrealistic for the countries to go back to the pristine times. He refers to the example of Malawi which has attempted to introduce traditional courts. But as the author points out this is only a mixture of modernity and the traditional system.

The author is certainly ambivalent in his comments or opinion on the Nkrumah regime’s attitude to the judiciary. He condemns the regime in no uncertain terms when at p. 178 he writes:

“The writ of habeas corpus fell into disrepute as an instrument for the protection of individual liberty. Just before Queen Elizabeth of England visited Ghana in 1961, it was thought that, as a security measure, all hardened criminal should be rounded up and kept in custody during her stay. A large number of ex-convicts, some having served their terms for offences wholly unconnected with violence of any kind, were arrested and detained. Queen Elizabeth's visit did not last more than three weeks. At the time the regime was overthrown in 1966, most of these persons were still in detention. The Act [The Preventive Detention Act] in the end was no more a security measure. It had simply become an instrument of terror.”

However, Amissah later in his book presents a rather more favourable picture of the Nkrumah regime. For instance, he writes at pp. 319-320:

"The exercise of arbitrary power is often associated with a state of no law. The supreme despot does just what he pleases no matter what the law says. Thus, those who think of Nkrumah as an autocrat tend to picture a situation of arbitrary disregard of the law. He imprisoned Ghanaians without due process of law. He dismissed judges capriciously. He set aside the ordinary rules accepted as ensuring fairness in the case of political trials. And at least on one occasion he refused to accept or to implement the decision of a court. But none of these measures was taken in Nkrumah's time without the backing of law. For imprisonment without court process Parliament enacted the Preventive Detention Act 1958. The Constitution of 1960 was amended in 1964 after a referendum with near-unanimous support to confer on the President the power to remove judges of the superior courts for whatever cause might seem to him sufficient . . . Nkrumah's Ghana may not have entirely satisfied Dicey's rule of law in the sense that no man may be punished or be lawfully made to suffer in body or goods except for a distinct breach of law established in the ordinary legal manner before the ordinary courts of the land, but in all the examples given above his formal observance of legal punctilio is undeniable."

The statement on the referendum certainly conveys the wrong impression to the reader. It gives the impression that the referendum was a genuine one that gave the President certain powers, and if he exercised them they were legitimate since they were traced directly to the citizens by a “near-unanimous support.” That is far from the truth. This particular referendum and similar elections under Nkrumah were marked by naked rigging. I recall as a secondary (high) school student in form four how we were virtually dragged to the polling station to cast our votes. We were all under the voting age of 21 and had not been registered as voters. In the booth was a policeman who directed us to put the ballots in the "Yes" box. It was widely known that this practice was widespread in the country and Amissah cannot gloss over such flagrant abuse of the voting system by merely referring to the result of the referendum. As regards Amissah's reference to Nkmmah's "observance of legal punctilio": One may wonder at the significance of the statement. It is hoped, Amissah is not suggesting that "legal backing" necessarily makes actions defendable. Otherwise we may have no quarrel against the apartheid policies in South Africa or against those of the Nazi Germany. Everything is and was backed by law in those countries respectively.

The other criticism in Amissah's book centres on his overblowing of the Busia regime's adverse reaction to the Court of Appeal's decision in the Sallah case. In the first place, Amissah’s comparison of the Sallah case with the Second Treason Trial case under the Nkrumah regime is out of proportion. The fundamental issues in the two cases are not related. While individual lives were at stake in the Second Treason Trial case, in the Sallah case, it was a matter of the proper interpretation to be place on the provision of the Constitution, 1969, dealing with the continuance in office of public officers under the Busia regime. Further, while Nkrumah resorted to his usual method in nullifying the court's decision, Busia contented himself with howling—a toothless bulldog. This practice by Busia, as the author identifies in his earlier chapters, is normal with all countries which practise the theory of separation of powers—the executive and the judiciary are constantly at loggerheads.

The author analyses the question, "What should be the role of the court in these countries?" In one sense, he rightly suggests that, “The function of the protection of the individual from the abuses of power by government and its agents has so far been less well appreciated by the executive” (p. 363). In other words, the author would like to have courts which stand firmly behind individual liberties, and that the executive should accept this important role played by the courts. But at p. 368 he writes, "There is a need for lawyers and judges who are sensitive to the values of the society . . . A possible contribution by the courts . . . could be made the judges removing from the public mind the view that their primary interest is in legal technicalities." That certainly is an unfortunate statement. It has almost always been in respect of the court's desire to protect the individual against governmental abuses that has resulted in frictions between the executive and the judiciary and thereby giving the executive the excuse to resort to the use of the phrase "legal technicalities." It is true "there is need for the courts to appreciate the ultimate goals of the society and within that context to play a positive role in their achievement." Those "ultimate goals" cannot, however, be determined solely from the executive point of view. And certainly none of those goals is more paramount and more enduring thin the protection of the citizens, especially against excesses by the government. It should not be forgotten that in the final analysis the other "ultimate goals" are to enure to the benefit of the citizens, without whom one cannot talk of the existence of a society.

It has to be conceded that Amissah chose an uneasy topic. The judiciary, as an institution, is the most complex and delicate aspect of government as Amissah’s thesis clearly demonstrates. While the judiciary has to play its part of the role (subtle or in a conspicuous manner) in the governing of the nation, it must maintain its independence from the other branches of government.
