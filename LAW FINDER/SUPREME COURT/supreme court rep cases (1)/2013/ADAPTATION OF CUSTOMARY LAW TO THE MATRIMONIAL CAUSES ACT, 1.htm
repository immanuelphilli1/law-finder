ADAPTATION OF CUSTOMARY LAW TO THE MATRIMONIAL CAUSES ACT, 1971 [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 218—222

 Woodman Gordon R.

 

A decision on an interlocutory application in Adjei v. Foriwaa1 discussed important principles in the Akan customary law of marriage. It arose from events both tortuous and rancorous. The plaintiff was the customary-law wife of the defendant, by whom she had six children.

The marital relationship deteriorated, and the defendant married another woman. The plaintiff brought an action claiming, as far as is relevant, a declaration that a certain house was the parties' joint property and their matrimonial home, an order for maintenance for herself and the children and an injunction restraining the defendant from ejecting her from the house. The defendant himself initiated proceedings against the plaintiff and her parents for an order to compel them to dissolve the marriage customarily, or alternatively an order of dissolution, and an order ejecting the plaintiff from the house. As a result of the defendant's proceedings the plaintiff and her children were ejected under an order of court. Subsequently the two actions were consolidated, and the defendant was ordered pending their final determination to reinstate the plaintiff and her children in the chamber and hall of the house which they had previously occupied. The plaintiff did not choose to return, but three of the children did.

The court described the motive for this last order and its results thus:

"The said order was made in the belief that it would be possible for the parties herein to live, if not in amity, at least without interference from, or molestation by each other. This apparently has not been the case."

In the year following the order, there was a series of quarrels and disputes between the three children and the defendant and his new wife. Consequently, the defendant now applied for an order that the plaintiff and the three children give an undertaking to be of good behaviour during the pendency of the suit.

The court first considered which principles of law were applicable. Under the English law which applied in matrimonial proceedings concerning marriages under the Marriage Ordinance (Cap. 127), a court could order a spouse to leave the matrimonial home or otherwise restrain a spouse from molesting the other, even if they held property rights in the house. Did it have this power when the proceedings concerned a customary-law marriage? The Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971 (Act 367), s. 41 (2) provided that "on application by a party to a marriage other than a monogamous marriage" the court was to apply the provisions of the Act to that marriage. Had the defendant made an application to the court to apply the provisions of the Act to his marriage? His action had been a petition for the dissolution of the marriage. Since the customary procedures for dissolution did not include a court action, he must be deemed to have made such an application.

However, the exercise of this jurisdiction under the Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971 (Act 367). does not entail the total ouster of customary law.  Section 41 (2) provides that in such a case:

"(2) On application by a party to a marriage other than a monogamous marriage, the court shall apply the provisions of this Act to that marriage, and in so doing, subject to the requirements of justice, equity and good conscience, the court may—

(a) have regard to the peculiar incidents of that marriage in determining appropriate relief, financial provision and child custody arrangements;

(b) grant any form of relief recognised by the personal law of the parties to the proceedings, either in addition to or in substitution for the matrimonial reliefs afforded by this Act."

The court accordingly considered the customary-law rules concerning occupation of the matrimonial home by a wife and children. It held that customary law knew no personal right of occupation of the matrimonial home by a wife, and that since the children in this case belonged to their mother's family they also could have no such personal right. A court might for the protection of the children order the father to provide accommodation, but their continued occupation of the matrimonial home must be subject to good behaviour.

No authority was cited, and there does not appear to have been any case-law bearing directly on these issues. However, there is case-law on the rights of Akan wives and children in the matrimonial home after the man's death intestate. The rationale of the rules in this case-law is that the obligations owed by a man during his lifetime pass to the family which inherits his estate. Therefore the cases impliedly state the customary-law rules applicable during a man's life. The earliest cases asserted that the widows and children were entitled to accommodation. This was not expressed as a right specifically in the matrimonial home, and some cases suggested that it might be satisfied by the provision of accommodation in a house other than one individually owned by the deceased.2 The children's right was said to be subject to good behaviour.3 The children's right of accommodation has been repeatedly asserted since those cases,4 and a few cases add mention of a widow's similar right.5 However, there has been some emphasis on the children's right to maintenance and education, and the widow's right to maintenance.6 It seems possible that the law is moving towards the conclusion that the wife and children are entitled essentially to maintenance and in the case of children's education, but that in appropriate circumstances maintenance may be provided partly or wholly in kind in the form of free accommodation. The cases did not clearly indicate whether accommodation had to be provided in kind if it was available, but they did not attach the right to any particular accommodation owned by the man, such as the matrimonial home. The statement in the present case summarised above seems to clarify the law by leaving the form of provision to the discretion of the husband and father, except where a court deems it necessary to order that the obligation be met by the provision of specific accommodation. In this regard, customary law has moved to a conclusion similar to that of English law, which after some controversy has also concluded that a wife has no proprietary interest in the matrimonial home purely by virtue of her status as a wife, although she may have a personal right therein against the husband.7 This outcome avoids the growth of a new category of encumbrances on land which might impede its marketability; and it gives the wife's right a pecuniary character, a conclusion which many would regard as economically more efficient in a money economy.

The court therefore concluded that the plaintiff did not have a customary-law right to remain in the house; even less did her children, who had returned on the strength of an order which had been granted to their mother, who herself had continued to stay out. It was thus necessary for the court to exercise its jurisdiction under the Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971, having regard to the customary law. It may be supposed that, if the wife or children had had a specific customary-law right in the house, the court might have hesitated to override this right. The absence of any such right meant that there was no fetter on the court's discretion on an application for equitable relief. The court proceeded to argue that the conditions in the house had become such that the children should be compelled to leave. It held that, although the defendant had not asked for their ejectment, the court had power to make such order as conscience dictated and the justice of the case demanded. It therefore ordered the children to vacate the house within 28 days.

The decision thus clarifies one aspect of the rights of the customary-law wife, and begins the working-out of the rules and principles by which the Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971 and the customary law of marriage will be adapted to each other, and parties enabled to make use of the opportunity to petition under the Act in respect of customary-law marriages.8
