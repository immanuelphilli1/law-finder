THE LAW AS AN INSTRUMENT OF TRADE POLICY IN GHANA [1981-82] VOLS.  XIII & XIV RGL 190—195

Ansa-Asare K.

 

IT is provided for by section 1 (1) of the Imports and Exports Act, 19801 that:

"1. (1) No person shall import goods into Ghana for commercial purposes unless the person—

(a) is registered under—

(i) the Registration of Business Names Act, 1962 (Act 151); or

(ii) the Incorporated Private Partnerships Act, 1962 (Act 152); or

(iii) the Companies Code, 1963 (Act 179); or

(b) is a body corporate established by or under any enactment; and

(c) has been issued with an import licence under this Act for the purpose."

To give teeth to Act 418, the Imports and Exports (Special Licence) Regulations, 1980,2 was passed.  One of its objects was to enable Ghanaians who have money abroad to import goods to supplement government efforts in affording adequate protection to the consuming and purchasing public.3

On the strength of the aforementioned laws, traders brought into the country all kinds of foreign commodities in flagrant disregard of the provisions of the law.  Such traders sold their goods at the non-controlled prices with impunity because neither Act 418 nor L.I. 1248 had teeth to bite4 However, a government statement released to the trading public on 30 November 1983, drew the attention of every trader to the existence of Act 418 and L.I. 1248 in the statute book. The government threatened to enforce the law by confiscating goods imported but not covered with documents required by the law. In the words of the Acting P.N.D.C. Secretary for Trade, Mr. Ato Ahwoi,5 "Being a trader does not only mean travelling to bring in goods for sale but also being conversant with trade regulations in the country as enshrined in Legislative Instrument 1248." Mr. Ahwoi further said that the government only wanted to see to it that the law was enforced.

Mr. Ahwoi's, sentiments, which will form the basis of your commentator's discussion, are by no means new. For example, successive governments since independence in 1957 have exhorted traders to be law abiding.6 At different times the approach has varied considerably.

Thus, in Nkrumah's days it was considered that the Ghana National Trading Corporation should be the main importer and distributor of some specified commodities.  In Acheampong's days, and now, traders are barred from dealing in some goods specified in the Schedule to the Price Control Decree, 1974.7 It can be said, therefore, that the question of how traders should go about their business has been the subject of much concern and remains one of the most difficult problems with which successive governments in post-independence Ghana have had to grapple.

From the point of view of government, there is a growing sense of urgency in the trading scene for controlled trading in the country. The upshot of it all is that government expects far greater popular legal consciousness and knowledge about the law of trading in the country. Afterall, once the law is enacted, it is presumed to be within the knowledge of the addressees. But this is by no means the case.

In modern times promulgation is a generally accepted practice. The present general rule is that immediately a law is enacted, it is binding on the addressees. The application of this general rule gives rise to the rule ignorantia juris neminem excusat. Although this rule seems harsh, it is commonly defended as a matter of utility8; and further, it has been argued, "it may be doubted whether many substantial hardships result from it."9

It is not the intention of your commentator to hold brief for traders who are not law-abiding. However, he will question the applicability of this rule to a trading public the majority of whom are illiterates. It is the writer's considered opinion that our trade regulations are not popular; most traders are ignorant of our trade laws. The writers has commented elsewhere10 that both the trading and purchasing public have little or no knowledge about our trade regulations. This ignorance is a delicate problem that requires delicate handling. It is not enough for government to sit on the fence and confiscate goods imported into the country in breach of L.I. 1248, or any trade regulation for the time being in force. The Provisional National Defence Council11 Government's high-handed policy on confiscation of goods under its special exercise could be argued to be backed by trade regulations, which the traders do not deny. However, the trading public contends that the mere act of promulgation - in one way or another - of trade regulations does not render them conscious of the law. This argument is plausible and seems to suggest that there is a divergence between popularisation and actual knowledge of the law. In other words, in a country where majority of traders are illiterates the presumption that once the law is promulgated, the addressees are aware of its meaning, should not be so. In reply, government echoes Blackstone's view that when laws are published:

"...  it is the subject's business to be thoroughly acquainted herewith: for if ignorance of what he might know were admitted as a  legitimate excuse, the laws would be of no effect, but might always be eluded with impunity"12

For government, therefore, ignorance of our trade regulations is no bar to enforcement of the law.  Since our trade regulations have been enacted and published, they carry a message and, like most laws, seek to regulate the conduct of the trading public.  The presumption of knowledge, therefore, defies questioning.

In reply, the trading public contends that this presumption is merely fictional, albeit convenient and possibly necessary.  Laws must not only be published: they must actually be communicated.  As Friedman put it, ". . . a bare resolution, confined in the breast of the legislator, without manifesting itself by some external sign, can never be properly a law."13 But policy makers might reply that, for them, the manner in which notification of the law is made was a matter of very great indifference. And that so long as there was some fair external manifestation, then the requirement was satisfied; it was then incumbent upon the trading public to become acquainted with the contents of trade regulations.

This issue of a divergence between the presumption and communication of the law has to be critically evaluated.  The writer is of the view that it was time the P.N.D.C. Government took steps to ensure that all enacted laws are communicated to the addressees.  It is arguable that a legislative act is effective even without communication. This is a purely Austinian concept. Austin's view is reflected in this paragraph14:'

"It is true, that Acts of Parliament are printed and may be had by those who choose to buy them; but this is not promulgation; for, before an Act is printed, and whether it is printed or not, it is a statute, and legally binding. If the printing were a promulgation, in the proper sense of the term, it would be a necessary consideration precedent to the existence of the law as binding on those whom it concerns.  The printing is, in this case, merely intended to refresh the memory of the parties whom it concerns; who, being all of them present at the enactment of the law, were then and there sufficiently informed of its existence and purport. Presence by ourselves and presence by our representatives are manifestly the same thing."

Bentham appears to have taken a somewhat different view of things. He strongly advocated for popular legal education. For him:

"The principal sort of instruction which governments owe to the people, is knowledge of the laws.  How can we require laws to be obeyed, when they are not even known. How can they be known unless they are published under the simplest forms, so that each individual may read for himself the enactments which are to regulate his conduct."15

Bentham's views will repay careful study because the writer believes those views to be relevant to the Ghanaian situation, if only because the majority of the people for whom the laws are meant are illiterates.

There is no doubt that Austin's views still reign supreme with most modern governments. But even at face value, Austin's sentiments may be said to express in extreme form the legal fiction earlier referred to.  It is surprising that such a view has found favour with post-independence Ghanaian regimes. At the very best, this legal fiction, originated by Blackstone,16 is impracticable,17 presumptious and unrealistic. It is not only grossly unfair but smacks of high-handed government action.

SUGGESTIONS

It is suggested that the government should device means whereby promulgation of laws would be accompanied by communication. The term "communication" contains the sense of a more positive effort to ensure that the legal message is received. Special arrangements should officially be made by the government to ensure that laymen and traders become familiar with the law.  For instance, there is an urgent need for our trade laws to be written in our local languages. Again, the mass-media, including the Press, should consider creating columns where newly promulgated laws can be published. Further, proposed legislation should receive wide publicity in both English and the local languages. A less formal objection to the immediately preceding suggestion will be that a commercial press publishes material which will have popular appeal.

CONCLUSION

This short note has attempted to show that traders have little or no knowledge of some or all of our trade regulations. The traders contend: that they know "next to nothing" of trade regulations.  In reply, the government believes that traders know "enough to get by." As examples, the government argues, traders are ignorant of L.I. 1248, but know enough to import essential commodities to sell to known customers at the non-controlled prices; that, traders know that it is a crime to sell above the controlled price, or hoard, or smuggle. The government is thus encouraged to assume that traders are aware that trading is regulated by law. Granted that this is the situation, this presumption by itself is not enough.

If it is accepted that our laws are couched in the English language by a select-few educated elite, then the government should ensure as a matter of urgency that our traders are not simply left to find out trade. regulations for themselves. The provisions of L.I 1248 or Act 418 should not remain in the breasts of the law-maker but should be brought to the actual knowledge of the trading public. In other words, there is need to communicate laws to the addressees in a language that they understand. This way, justice will not only be done, but it will be seen to have been done.
