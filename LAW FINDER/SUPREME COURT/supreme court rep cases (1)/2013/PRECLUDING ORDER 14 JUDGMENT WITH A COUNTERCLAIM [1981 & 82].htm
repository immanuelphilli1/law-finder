PRECLUDING ORDER 14 JUDGMENT WITH A COUNTERCLAIM* [1981 & 82] VOLS. XIII & XVI RGL 172—183

Sampson Owusu

 

INTRODUCTION

SUMMARY proceedings under Order 14,1 are devised to obviate the necessity for a full trial when the claim can be said to be effectively beyond dispute. According to rule 1 of Order 14, after appearance has been entered to a writ of summons especially endorsed with2 or accompanied by a statement of claim, the plaintiff can move the court for judgment on his claim provided the claim does not fall within the exclusionary clause of Order 14, r.13.3  One is thus spared the time and cost of going the whole hog of pleadings and trial.4  A problem arising under this provision, and which this comment seeks to discuss, is whether in such a proceeding under Order 14 the defendant can preclude summary judgment by relying on a counterclaim which is not in any way connected with, but exceeds the plaintiff's claim. 

Undoubtedly it would not conduce to justice to allow recovery of a claim which had been controverted without the issue thereby raised being determined. But it is completely a different thing, and certainly unjustifiable in our present judicial system, to sustain the view that where the defendant sets up a counterclaim which has no connection with, but overtops the plaintiff's claim, the right to summary judgment should be refused.  Should the plaintiff, suing as a head of family to recover an admitted purchase price of family land be denied summary judgment just because long before the sale and the appointment of the plaintiff as head of family he allegedly libelled the defendant?  Should the fact that the defendant's counterclaim for defamation exceeds the plaintiff's demand make any material difference? This would certainly be vexatious.

The problem is strikingly exemplified in Inspector-Plastico Moulds Ltd. v. Atico Ltd.,5 in which Amissah J.A. sitting in the High Court gave judgment to the plaintiff in spite of the defendants' counterclaim, which overtopped the plaintiff's claim.  But the Court of Appeal consisting of Apaloo, Lassey and Archer JJ.A. on a slightly different set of facts came to a contrary decision in the case of Accra Furniture and Rubber Foam Co., Ltd. v. Indart S.P./A. of Rome6 where Amissah J.A.'s decision in the Inspector-Plastico Moulds Ltd. case was not cited. It will be contended in this comment that the Court of Appeal's decision in the Accra Furniture's case does not, in the respectful view of the present writer, involve any departure from the principle laid down in the Inspector-Plastico's case. But the grounds of the Court of Appeal's decision cast doubt on the authority of the Inspector-Plastico's case, and therefore call for a detailed examination of these two cases. Before an attempt is made to demonstrate the ground on which these two cases can be reconciled with a view to determining the principles enunciated in them, it may be worthwhile to examine briefly the concept of counterclaim and set-off.

THE CONCEPT OF COUNTERCLAIM AND SET-OFF

It was not possible under the common law for a defendant "to defeat an action by attacking the plaintiff in any form."7 Where the defendant had a claim against the plaintiff, he had to initiate his own separate action.8  With respect to claims arising out of the same transaction, the defendant had a right of reduction, which, when insisted upon, had the effect of reducing or altogether extinguishing the claim of the plaintiff.  As Parke B. held9:

"It must . . . be considered, that in all these cases of goods sold and delivered with a warranty, and work and labour, as well as the case of goods agreed to be supplied according to a contract, the  rule which has been found so convenient is established; and that it is competent for the defendant, in all of those, not to set-off, by a proceeding in the nature of a cross action, the amount of damages which he has sustained by breach of the contract, but simply to defend himself by shewing how much less the subject-matter of the action was worth, by reason of the breach of contract . . ."

Nevertheless, the rule remained that a defendant was not entitled to receive a judgment for damages for a positive claim against a plaintiff. This common law restriction was relaxed by statutes10 which, in certain specified situations,11 permitted set-offs of defendants' claims in abatement or reduction of plaintiff's demands.  

Before these statutory interventions, equity allowed set-offs of one debt against another as a shield for a defendant against a plaintiff's claim. This was permitted where it would be unjust and unreasonable to award a specific relief without mitigating or reducing it by reference to a related obligation of the plaintiff to the defendant. It existed, "in cases where the party seeking the benefit of it [could] shew some equitable ground for being protected against his adversary's demand."12  Equitable set-off thus operates much more widely than legal set-off.  It is limited only by the bounds of reason, and therefore not subjected to any arbitrary restriction.13 Unlike the common law, it is not confined to cases where the claim of the defendant is for a liquidated amount14 nor where the respective claims of the plaintiff and the defendant can be rightly considered as mutual15 provided, of course, that "the equity of the bill [impeaches] the title to the legal demand."16 Such equity will arise where, according to Spry17:

"the claim of the defendant has been brought about by or has been contributed to by, or is otherwise so bound up with the rights which are relied upon by the plaintiff that it would be unconscionable that he should proceed without allowing a set-off."

Generally, a claim of set-off should then necessarily have to exist between the same parties and in the same right.18

But equity like the common law allows set-off to be used only as a defence, "as a shield, not as a sword."19  The defendant cannot by proceeding by way of set-off recover anything against the plaintiff. Since it operates only as a defence it lapses when the plaintiff obtains judgment or the action is stayed, discontinued or dismissed.20  It is not possible for a defendant to advance any claim which cannot qualify as a statutory, common law or equitable set-off.  To advance any other claim, he is required to institute his own action.  To avoid such multiplicity of actions, the Judicature Act, 1873,21 made it possible for the defendant to set up and maintain any claim he might have against the plaintiff in the very action instituted by the plaintiff.  This claim is denominated counterclaim.  The procedure of attacking the plaintiff by means of a counterclaim had been re-enacted, reinforced and amplified by the rules of court.22

Counterclaim therefore embraces both the set-off and other claims. Every set-off is a counterclaim, but not every counterclaim is a set-off.  Essentially, a counterclaim is a cross-action; and the effect of the all-inclusive language of the stature is to allow the defendant to admit the claim of the plaintiff and simultaneously, subject to the power of the court to order a separate trial,23 insist on the prosecution of a claim which has no affinity24 with the subject-matter of the plaintiff's claim, and which may even exceed the claim of the plaintiff.  It need not be made against the plaintiff in the same capacity as that in which he sues.

A factor which compounds the difficulty for the plaintiff-applicant who invokes the court's jurisdiction under Order 14 is that he has the onus of convincing the court of the absence of any genuine triable issue.  It has therefore become expedient for the opposing party to raise all issues that the court might otherwise dispose of as uncontroverted and not content himself with pointing to a single genuine issue of facts.  Such issues relied upon by the opposing party to ward off an application for summary judgment include counterclaims, which may even not have any bearing on the determination of the original claim of the plaintiff.

The problem presents itself in an acute form when the value of the subject-matter of the counterclaim exceeds the value of the plaintiff's claim. But the possibility of such counterclaim being put in as red herring cannot be ruled out.  For one can always resist an application under Order 14 by showing that "there is an issue or question in dispute which ought to be tried or that there ought for some other reason to be a trial of that claim."25 It might appear from this short analysis that the court would be inclined to try an otherwise uncontroverted claim only when there is an equitable set-off.  But equitable set-off does not cover situations where the defendant sets up a counterclaim which  is not connected with, but merely exceeds the plaintiff's claim.  The problem then is whether the defendant has set up a counterclaim, albeit larger than the plaintiff's claim, which can be brought within his provision. For reasons which will appear hereafter, the courts have treated such Order 14 applications involving unresolved questions of law and bills of exchange differently from the other cases. It is therefore proposed to examine this problem under these various sub-headings.

CASES NOT INVOLVING BILLS OF EXCHANGE

In Accra Furniture & Rubber Foam Co., Ltd. v. Indart S.P./A. of Rome26 counsel for the defendant-appellant contended, inter alia,27 as stated in the headnote that,28 "since there was a counterclaim for an amount larger than the sum claimed in the writ, judgment should not have been entered for the respondent company for the amount claimed . . ." The  Court of Appeal consisting of Apaloo, Lassey and Archer JJ.A. in  sustaining the appeal, relied on Court v. Sheen29 for the proposition that30: 

"where as in this case, there was a claim and a counterclaim for an amount exceeding the claim, the plaintiff is not entitled to immediate judgment for the amount admitted, inasmuch as the amount  could only be admitted subject to the counterclaim."

This proposition is susceptible of a wider interpretation if it is juxtaposed to Court v. Sheen, and seen in the light of the reasoning of counsel for the defendant-appellant.  In Court v. Sheen it is reported merely that the defendant admitted certain items of the plaintiff to the amount of £100 but set up a counterclaim for a much larger amount.  The court held that the plaintiff was not entitled to immediate judgment for  the amount admitted, since it was only admitted subject to the counterclaim, which might turn out to be larger in amount. The particulars of the counterclaim do not appear in the judgment. Given the sparseness of the report it may be questioned whether the ruling could, for example, entitle a defendant to leave to defend a claim for ¢10,000 special damages for the price of goods sold and delivered to him, merely because he enters a counterclaim for ¢10,000 general damages for a defamation alleged to have been committed by the plaintiff long after the delivery of the goods. Yet this appears to be the implication of the argument presented by counsel for the defendant-appellant.

The Court of Appeal in the Accra Furniture case referred to Order 21, r.16 which provides that:

"16. Where in any action a set-off or counterclaim is established as a defence against the plaintiff's claim, the Court or a Judge may if the balance is in favour of the defendant, give judgment for the defendant for such balance, or may otherwise adjudge to the defendant such relief as he may be entitled to upon the merits of the case."31 

But upon careful consideration it was found that the provision was not applicable.  For, according to the Court of Appeal, neither the claim nor the counterclaim could be held established32 unless it was determined on the merits by the adduction of evidence.  It is submitted with respect that the Court of Appeal erred in holding that a claim can only be established by the adduction of evidence.  Affidavit with or without documentary evidence can competently be employed to establish a claim as is normally done under Order 14.  Even with a counterclaim nothing prevents the defendant in an appropriate case from proceeding under Order 14, r.12 of L.I. 1129 for a summary judgment against the plaintiff on his counterclaim.  By this means the counterclaim can be established by an affidavit evidence. When summary judgment is given in respect of the claim and the counterclaim, the  application of Order 21. r.6 of L.N. 140A or Order 14, r.12 of L.I. 1129  will be apposite.33 It therefore appears that the ambit of the principle enunciated in the judgment can only be discerned from the facts of the case.  The plaintiff caused a writ to be issued specially endorsed with a claim for ¢72,550.77 being the price of goods sold and delivered to the defendant.  The plaintiff then sought leave to enter judgment against the defendant under Order 14, r.l. This was countered by the defendants with an affidavit in opposition filed by the managing director of the defendant company, who deposed that "the only amount the defendant company admits owing to the plaintiffs is N¢12,792.08 but the plaintiffs owe the defendant a much bigger amount for which the defendants intend to counterclaim".  The defendants had leave to counterclaim for the sum of N¢200,000 being unliquidated damages for "breach of promise."34 The trial court allowed the plaintiffs-respondents to sign judgment for the amount admitted, the counterclaim notwithstanding; judgment was also entered for the disputed amount with a stay of execution. 

On appeal, it was observed that the counterclaim of the defendants appellants was raised as a defence to the plaintiffs respondents' claim.  The defendants contended that the respondent company were in breach of contract and were liable in damages to them (the defendants).  The Court of Appeal stated that it did not wish to express any opinion  "on the merits of the counterclaim and could say no more than that there is a chance of it succeeding. We think, like the judge, that that claim was at least plausible."35 The order of the High Court granting leave to sign immediate judgment was therefore set aside.  The Court of Appeal was also of the opinion that since the counterclaim was only controverted in the affidavit of the plaintiffs' solicitor who did not have any personal knowledge of the facts, the application for summary judgment could have been dismissed on that ground.

Order 14 is designed, as noted above, to dispense with the need  for a full trial where there is no arguable or triable issue.  Therefore, when the court found the counterclaim to be a defence which had a chance of succeeding, it necessarily followed that it tendered a triable issue to the original claim.  The dismissal of the application for summary judgment on this ground would have been unexceptionable. However, the reference to Court v. Sheen and the wide proposition emanating therefrom was rather unfortunate.  The authority of the decision is further vitiated by the fact that the issue was not fully argued, since there was no appearance for the plaintiffs -respondents.  What is more, Court v. Sheen on which the Court of Appeal relied for this astonishingly  wide proposition had been doubted by the English Court of Appeal.36  The principle, it is submitted, is that leave to defend a claim under Order 14 should be granted only when the counterclaim raises an arguable or triable issue.  Where, on the other hand, the counterclaim cannot in law or equity operate as a defence37 but merely asserts a demand to a payment in excess of the claim of the plaintiff, there would be no triable issue, and it would not conform with principle to deny the plaintiff's right to immediate judgment.

We may cite Morgan & Son, Ltd. v. S. Martin Johnson & Co., Ltd.38 as an example of a case where a defendant succeeded in defeating an application for summary judgment on a claim for a certain sum for the storage of vehicles by putting up a tenable counterclaim.  This was counterclaim for loss arising from the plaintiff's breach of duty as bailee, or alternatively breach of contract for storage of vehicles, one of which the plaintiffs had negligently delivered to a wrong person or allowed to be stolen.39

Where then the counterclaim arises from, and is so connected with the subject-matter of the original claim that it would not be equitable to allow the plaintiff's claim to be satisfied without taking into account the defendant's counterclaim, the court will grant leave to defend. In another case, Diplock L.J. said40:

"I think, having listened to the argument before us, the learned  Judge must have been persuaded that the counterclaim did not relate to the subject-matter of the agreement in which case his order might well have been justified.  I think, however, that if one looks at the evidence it is sufficiently plain that the whole of the counterclaim does relate to the same subject-matter as the agreement and since one cannot say that the counterclaim is bogus or a sham  whatever its ultimate merits may turn out to be, I think the defendants-appellants here were entitled to unconditional leave to defend." 

It therefore becomes imperative to establish some nexus between the claim and counterclaim.  In Rotheram v. Priest41 the plaintiff sued for arrears of rent, and the counterclaim was for libel.  The counterclaim was found to be completely unconnected with the original cause of action and could not, therefore, militate against the plaintiff's application for summary judgment.  A counterclaim which is entirely foreign to the original cause of action can hardly be a defence in law or equity.42 If the counterclaim fails to contradict the plaintiff's claim in a material respect, and merely advances a fresh claim which does not emanate from the same facts as the plaintiff's claim, there will be two uncontradicted, distinct and independent claims.  It will be observed that as immediate judgment on such a claim will rather expedite litigation and will not undermine the object of avoiding multiplicity of actions.  For such a counterclaim, which does not qualify as a defence or set-off, does not abate, but proceeds to separate trial when judgment is given on the original claim.43

BILLS OF EXCAHNGE CASES

In the case of Inspector-Plastico Moulds Ltd. v. Atico Ltd.,44 the plaintiffs sought summary judgment on a bill of exchange for the sum of £1,416.5s. An application for judgment under Order 14 was resisted by the defendants who relied on a statement of defence instead of an affidavit as provided by Order 14, r.3.45 In their defence, the defendants put up a counterclaim for £798.10s. special damages and £1,000 general damages for breach of warranty.  Amissah J.A. ignored the breach of Order 14, r.3. The learned Justice of Appeal was of the view that an application for summary judgment on a bill of exchange could only be defeated by fraud, absence or subsequent failure of consideration, and any of the defences recognised by Order 21, r.2.46 Since none of these was disclosed by the defence, the court awarded judgment and allowed the counterclaim to proceed separately. With respect, there cannot be any conceivable ground on which the decision can be impugned.

The defendants in this case alleged that since a jewel mould which the plaintiffs delivered to them was defective they had suffered loss.

But the defence did not disclose that the mould was the consideration for the bill.  Amissah J.A. observed obiter47:

"The position would have been different if it had been alleged that the goods tendered had not been of the contract description and had been rejected. For then there would have been a total failure of consideration, which, as I have said, is a defence to an action on a bill." 

The defendants then argued that, since the total amount of the counterclaim was larger than the amount claimed on the bill, they should be given leave to defend.  This contention was founded on Court v. Sheen.  But, as Amisah J.A. noted,48 Court v. Sheen had been impliedly overruled by the English Court of Appeal because the report was short and did not disclose the ground of the decision.  The fact that the counterclaim was in excess of the original claim was therefore of no moment. To the learned Justice of Appeal the crucial point was that49:

"The defence to the claim is not one of those mentioned in the Bills of Exchange Act, 1961 (Act 55), nor does it allege any other fact which would incline me to allow leave to defend.  The counterclaim does not improve matters because it does not give me any compelling reason why the plaintiffs' judgment should be postponed." 

The courts have always considered actions involving bills of exchange as a special case.  In bills of exchange cases two transactions are involved, one pertaining to the bill of exchange, and the other dealing with a wider transaction to which the issue of bill of exchange is incidental.  The issue of a bill of exchange, according to Roxburgh J. gives rise to:

"an independent contract within the wider contract in pursuance of which it was executed and not dependent as regards its enforcement on due performance of the latter."50

An application for summary judgment on a bill of exchange therefore can only be defeated by a defence to the inner contract constituted by the bill of exchange. If none is open, the defendant should not be allowed to protract the proceedings.  To allow a counterclaim per se to defeat an application for summary judgment just because it exceeds the original claim would open the floodgates to factuous counterclaims, tagged with sums inflated with a view to delaying the evil day, and the utility of Order 14 would be eroded.  Thus, unlike the other cases, such as Accra Furniture & Rubber Foam Co., Ltd., an application under Order 14 for judgment on a bill of exchange can be defeated only by a cross-claim founded on fraud, total failure of consideration and matters which are strictly, directly and exclusively pertinent to the issue of the bill.  The fact that the counterclaim exceeds the original claim is completely immaterial.

CASES WHERE THE COUNTERCLAIM RAISES A SERIOUS QUESTION OF LAW

The counterclaim may only raise a difficult question of law which may not be convenient to determine in a summary manner.  If the counterclaim raises a dispute relating to law and the facts on which the plaintiff founds his claim are not controverted, then judgment should be awarded to the plaintiff with a stay of execution, pending the determination of the vexed legal question.  Any contrary course would put the plaintiff to unnecessary expense in proving his claim.  This, it is submitted, is the ratio decidendi of Sheppards & Co. v. Milkinson and Jarvis.51

In that case the plaintiffs were seeking to recover the sum of £7,500 advanced to the defendant in pursuance of certain agreements.  The counterclaim alleged that they had been breached by the plaintiffs, and had been obtained by fraud.  The nature of the alleged fraud was not disclosed. But counsel for the defendants:

"had shadowed forth a claim for damages for breach of duty by the plaintiffs, and it certainly was a question whether where shares were entrusted to a broker for sale he was entitled to sell similar shares belonging to himself in priority to shares entrusted to him to sell.  That was the breach of a duty alleged against the plaintiffs, and it was a question which, for the benefit of the public and the instruction of the Stock Exchange, ought to be thoroughly sifted. It was impossible to say that there was no prospect of the counterclaim succeeding."52

The Court of Appeal, although awarding judgment to the plaintiffs, granted a stay of execution pending trial of the counterclaim. The fact that the counterclaim overtopped the plaintiffs' claim cannot have been the basis of the decision, which was rather that there was a legal question of overriding importance to be decided. 

CONCLUSION

The court can exercise its discretion to refuse an application for summary judgment only when there is an arguable or a triable issue.  For the fundamental right to trial does not vest one with the right to insist on the trial of non-existent issues.  The imposition of absolute mandatory rules would trammel the free and just exercise of this discretion.  However, since the overriding policy consideration of preventing  unnecessary delays in litigation is salutory and cannot be achieved without proper guidelines, the discretion cannot be left unfettered. Hence  the need to formulate principles which will facilitate the pursuit of this policy. 

The view that, where the counterclaim overtops the original claim, leave to defend must follow as a matter of course, although canvassed in some decisions, appears contrary to Order 14 and likely to lead to abuse.  On the other hand, if in exercising this discretion the courts consider factors which constitute equitable and legal defences, this  will not only advance the policy but will be consistent with the letter of the rules.
