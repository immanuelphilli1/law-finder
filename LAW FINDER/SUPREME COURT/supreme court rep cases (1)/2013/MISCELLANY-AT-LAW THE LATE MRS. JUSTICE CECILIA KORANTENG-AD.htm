MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: THE LATE MRS. JUSTICE CECILIA KORANTENG-ADDOW, MR. JUSTICE F. P. SARKODIE AND MR. JUSTICE K. A. AGYEPONG: THE CASE OF THE JUDICIAL MARTYRS [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 1—14

 

THE very foundations of the administration of law and justice were shaken and the machinery of justice in the courts of Ghana was in fact halted for some weeks when the shocking and painful news was broadcast to Ghanaians on the night of 4 July 1982 by the Chairman of the Provisional National Defence Council, Flt.-Lt. J. J. Rawlings, to the effect that three eminent and very competent judges of the High Court of Ghana and a retired army officer had been abducted and subsequently murdered by some unknown assailants. The three High Court judges who were kidnapped from their Accra homes on the night of Wednesday, 30 June 1982, were Mrs. Cecilia Afran Koranteng-Addow, Mr. Justice Fred Poku Sarkodie and Mr. Justice Kwadwo Agyei Agyepong. The retired army officer abducted and murdered together with the three judges was Major Sam Acquah.

There was public outcry and expression of total condemnation of the murders both at home and abroad. The Chairman of the Provisional National Defence Council, Flt.-Lt. J. J. Rawlings, in a nation-wide broadcast on radio and television on the night of 4 July 1982 said, "We condemn these acts from the depths of our hearts," adding that "they are against all the principles which the current revolutionary process is designed to advance." Various other organisations including the National Council on Women and Development, the Christian Council of Ghana, Conference of Catholic Bishops, the Ghana Bar Association and workers condemned the gruesome and wicked murders in no uncertain terms and clamoured for an independent body to investigate the wanton and brutal murders. In fact, a delegation of the African Bar Association led by its Secretary-General, Hon. Debo Akande, arrived in Accra on 16 July 1982, and called on the P.N.D.C. Government to conduct an inquest into the murders and also to conduct an independent public and judicial inquiry to track down the culprits. The Ghana Bar Association after its emergency general meeting held on 5 July 1982 at the Supreme Court Buildings, Accra. issued the following statement:

"1. The association condemns in the strongest possible terms the recent criminal abduction and heinous murder of the three High Court Judges and the GIHOC group personnel manager, Major Acquah (Rtd.). The association further condemns in no less strong terms the now obvious pattern of abductions and murder of various Ghanaians all over the country by persons who have so far remained unidentified and unpunished.

2. The association urgently calls upon the P.N.D.C. Government to take immediate steps to ensure public safety and the enforcement of law and order, and meanwhile emphatically to discourage any propaganda from any source whatsoever designed to incite Ghanaians to challenge lawfully constituted authority or the law at large in the name of the Revolution.

3. The association extends its deepest condolences to the families of the four deceased persons and all other victims of similar criminal brutality. The association calls upon His Lordship the Chief Justice as the Head of the Judiciary, to open a book of condolence as soon as possible in the Supreme Court in memory of the deceased judges.

4. The association having furthermore reviewed the circumstances relating to the death of the four deceased persons demands of the P.N.D.C.:

(a) the immediate institution of a coroner's inquest to ascertain as far as possible the facts about the abduction and murder of the four deceased persons; and

(b) a public inquiry into similar killings all over the country since 31 December 1981.

5. Lastly, the association is immediately declaring two weeks of mourning in memory of the four deceased persons and of others who have been similarly abducted and murdered since 31 December 1981. Meanwhile, no member of the Ghana Bar Association will appear before any court, tribunal, commission or committee of inquiry, save and except the coroner's inquest."

The Government of the P.N.D.C. responded and appointed an investigating body, the Special Investigations Board (S.I.B.) under the Special Investigations Board (Kidnapping and Killing of Specified Persons) Law, 1982 (P.N.D.C.L. 15), headed by the former Chief Justice, the Hon. Mr. Justice Azu Crabbe, to investigate the killing of the three High Court Judges and the retired army officer.

he S.I.B. conducted a public inquiry into the murders and later submitted a 55-page final report to the Attorney-General and Secretary for Justice, the Hon. Mr. G. E. K. Aikins. In its final report, the S.I.B. found that there was no evidence from which the inference could be drawn that the Chairman of the P.N.D.C., Flt.-Lt. Rawlings, had fore- knowledge of the kidnapping and murder. The S.I.B., however, made adverse findings against ten named persons. In paragraph 120 of the report, the S.I.B. stated:

"There is evidence, which, if believed, would support a conclusion that the following persons: L./Cpl. Michael Senya, L./Cpl. Gordon Kwowu, Johnny Dzandu, Tonny Tekpor, L./Cpl. Nsurowuo, L./Cpl. Gomeleshio joined the conspiracy with the criminal purpose of killing the three High Court Judges and the retired army officer."

And in the last paragraph of the report, i.e. paragraph 340, the S.I.B. wrote:

"On the evidence before us we find that Captain Kojo Tsikata (Rtd.), Special Adviser to the P.N.D.C., Sgt. Daniel (now Ahmed) Alolga Akata-Pore, and Joachim Amartey Kwei, were all implicated in the conspiracy to kidnap and murder the three Justices of the High Court and Major Sam K. Acquah (Rtd.)."

Notwithstanding these findings, the Attorney-General and Secretary for Justice, issued a statement on the report to the effect that in his opinion, there was no sufficient evidence, on the basis of the report, on which to prosecute five of the ten persons against whom adverse findings had been made by the S.I.B., namely, Lance-Corporals Gordon Kwowu, Nsurowuo, Victor Gomeleshio, Sgt. Alolga Akata-Pore and Captain Kojo Tsikata (Rtd.). He however, decided to prosecute five persons before the public tribunal set up under the Public Tribunals Law, 1982 (P.N.D.C.L. 24), namely: Lance-Corporals S. K. Amedeka, Michael Senya, Johnny Dzandu and Tonny Tekpor who were charged, tried and convicted on two charges of conspiracy to commit murder and murder; whilst Joachim Amartey Kwei, the former member of the P.N.D.C., was charged, tried and convicted for conspiracy to commit murder. The public tribunal sentenced all the five convicted persons to death by firing squad: Joachim Amartey Kwei and Ransford Johnny Dzandu were executed on 18 August 1983 whilst Michael Senya was executed on 31 August. Tonny Tekpor who was arraigned before the public tribunal as the fifth accused, had in fact earlier been executed by firing squad for his role in the June 19 abortive coup against the P.N.D.C. The fifth person, Lance-Corporal S. K. Amedeka, was also tried, convicted, and sentenced to death by firing squad in absentia, having broken jail during the June 19 abortive coup.

And so it was that the Hon. Mrs. Justice Cecilia Koranteng-Addow, the Hon. Mr. Justice F. P. Sarkodie and the Hon. Mr. Justice K. A. Agyepong passed away into eternity. A special funeral service for the late three judges and the retired army officer was held at the law Courts Buildings, Accra, on Wednesday, 7 July 1982. The very solemn funeral service was attended by members of the government, Bench and Bar, the Diplomatic Corps, the families of the deceased and thousands of mournful Ghanaians from all over the country.

Glowing tributes were paid at the funeral to the late judges by the Hon. Chief Justice, Mr. Justice F. K. Apaloo, the Hon. Attorney-General and Secretary for Justice, Mr. G. E. K. Aikins, and lastly by the then President of the Ghana Bar Association, Mr. E. D. Kom.

Tribute by the Chief Justice

This is not the first time that it has fallen to my lot to pay tribute to a departed one. I have had this duty imposed on me on a number of occasions. But this one is sadly unique and I cannot bring myself to do it without a feeling of intense grief. The circumstances in which the judges met their end, are not now news. It would be more than human if any portrait that I paint of them is not coloured by the shared feeling of grief which we are all experiencing at the present time. I would mention them not in their order of seniority in our records, but according to their ages: Mr. Justice K. A. Agyepong happens to be the oldest in age. Born at Oyoko, Ashanti on 17 November 1926, he will be 56 next November. After primary school in his home-town, he went to Mfantsipim, Cape Coast, in 1942. When he finished there in 1946, he was retained to teach there. He was obviously a good English student and he taught English, Latin and Mathematics in his alma mater. When he left Mfantsipim, he held short teaching appointments at Ghana National College, Cape Coast, and at the Accra High School. But teaching was not his destined career. In October 1955, he entered the English Inns of Court and pursued a course in law. He became a fully fledged lawyer in February 1960.

Upon his return to Ghana in 1961, he entered private practice in Kumasi. It was at that time, as it still is, a very competitive Bar with such stalwarts as Justice Prempeh, Justice Siriboe, Justice Adade, Victor Owusu, Tottoe and Effah—the greats of those days. But  Agyepong showed at an early age his capacity for industry and his determination to hold his own against the best. As a judge of the High Court in Kumasi in 1961. I am able to say this of my own knowledge. 

In 1977 when the Judicial Council decided to elevate a lawyer of proven merit and integrity from the Ashanti Bar, his name was submitted by the then presiding judge. The Judicial Council had no difficulty in endorsing his candidature. Accordingly, on 27 September 1977, he was appointed a judge of the High Court. He has been stationed in Accra ever since. His performance on the bench satisfied me that the Judicial Council's selection of him was right. It was plain he was in love with his judicial work and sat in court long after official hours.

I do not know what other interests he had but one that I can say judging from his work, is that sitting and hearing cases in court gave him a great deal of satisfaction. His capacity for work was prodigious and it was my habit to push on to him cases not on his normal schedule. He accepted without hesitation. The only complaint the lawyers make of him is that he tends to be a little garrulous. That is not a serious handicap in a judge but that was to be expected of a man who began in a profession in which talkativeness is an asset.

His willingness to accept extra burden without demur was illustrated in May 1979 when in the midst of heavy judicial work-load, he readily agreed to serve as a chairman of a committee of inquiry with Mr. Dade and Rev. Kwansa to inquire into the shooting incident at the Accra Railway Station of a university student.

Having been in Accra continuously for five years, he was due to be transferred. On 17 May 1982, I wrote notifying him of his transfer to Cape Coast in the ensuing legal year in October. I did not then realise that he was being returned to the one town—Cape Coast—whose scholarly environment decided his academic bent. I was told later that he was delighted at the idea. Little did Justice Agyepong or I know that he would not return to Cape Coast. He was a simple and unassuming man and it is hard to believe that he would have seriously harmed anyone or any would have designs on his life. But the cruel fate that befell him on Wednesday, 30 June 1982, is now stale news. And he will not go to Cape Coast in October. He will this day be sent to his permanent resting-place at Osu and he would lie with some of the departed judges, the Cousseys, Korsahs, the van Lares and the Manyo-Planges who also have made that place their permanent earthly home.

Mr. Justice F. P. Sarkodie—Our other departed brother who lies before us is Frederick Poku Sarkodie, born at Koforidua on 19 August 1929, he would have been 53 next month. He began his legal career as an Assistant State Attorney in the Attorney-General’s Office in January 1961. He rose through the ranks—so to speak, and in May 1971, had become a Principal State Attorney.

My acquaintance with him began in the middle 60s when his assignment was to appear for the State in criminal matters before the Court of Appeal. As an advocate, he was cool and collected. Although driving his points home with conviction, he was the very embodiment of courtesy. That gentleman of a lawyer, we would say, if we knew he was to appear in a particular case.

In May 1971, he was elevated to the High Court and joined what we call the judicial fraternity. He worked conscientiously and well and is reputed to have in abundance one of the most treasured qualities that many judges lack—judicial taciturnity. He would listen for hours, unsmiling and with attention to legal argument without showing the least sign of boredom. He has a habit of delivering his judgments on time because he had a firm grasp of principle and could make up his mind firmly and without vacillation.

Because of his great experience in criminal work, many lawyers, myself included, thought that he would have no truck with civil work. But that is incorrect. There were occasions when he came to me, when on criminal schedule, to ask to be assigned civil work in addition. I believe he enjoyed these more because of the variety of cases on every subject.

Many persons outside the legal profession believe, quite wrongly, that lawyers, as a rule, have little truck with religion or hardly concern themselves actively with church matters. That may be true of some lawyers. It is not true of Fred Sarkodie. He was, until his death, the Chancellor of the Diocese of the Koforidua Anglican Church. That he combined his temporal with his spiritual duties, is shown by a letter he addressed to me on 5 March 1982. He wrote:

"I should be grateful if you would grant me permission to travel to Freetown, Sierra Leone, to attend the Synod of the Province of West Africa (Anglican Church) in my capacity as the Chancellor of the Diocese of Koforidua."

That permission was granted and as he told me on his return, he quite enjoyed the discussions and the ennobling companionship of his religious colleagues.

Some of us were privileged to see the other part of his life—at home with his wife Yvonne whom he adored and his darling children. Their welfare was his dearest concern. A few years ago, before I became Chief Justice, he unburdened his heart to me about the great distress he was in because his posting was uncertain and he had to commute between Accra-Ho and some times Tamale. He said the prolonged parting from his wife and children was causing him great unhappiness

But now as ill-luck had made it and as cruel fate had ordained it, he has parted from them for good only to be re-united with them as we Christians believe, on resurrection day. Can the anguish of this family, whose breadwinner, counsellor and father who was gunned down in the prime of his life, ever be measured? I would not want to attempt it. To us mortals with our limited human vision, we can be forgiven if we say it is a loss that can never be repaired.

Mrs. Justice C. A. Koranteng-Addow—And the third departed member of our judicial family is a lady and the youngest of the three. She is Cecilia Afran Koranteng-Addow. She was born at Assin Nsuta on 24 May 1936, she attained the age of 46 only last May. At her comparatively tender age, she is the third highest-ranking lady on the Superior Court Bench ranking only after Mrs. D. Owusu-Addo, High Court Judge and our well-known Mrs. Justice Jiagge, Justice of the Court of Appeal.

Our record shows that she was educated at Holy Child School, Cape Coast, and the University of Hull, England. She graduated Bachelor of Laws with honours in June 1962. In June 1963, she was called to the English Bar at Lincoln's Inn. She had a two-year shot at private practice at the Bar between 1964-66.

In July 1966, she joined our judicial rank as a district magistrate. It was clear from her performance that she had bright prospects in her freely chosen field. In October 1971, she earned promotion as a circuit judge. She then climbed the judicial ladder fairly quickly. In April 1975, she was appointed as an acting judge of the High Court. There was little doubt that her appointment to the substantive post was only a question of time. It came two years later, in October 1977 when she was appointed to the Superior Court Bench as a High Court Judge.

As an acting High Court Judge, she was posted to Kumasi on 1 July 1975. The Bar was apprehensive whether she could cope in view of the volume of litigation in the Ashanti capital. Not only did she cope but when she was transferred to Accra in 1977, so she could join her husband, then Attorney-General, a delegation of the Ashanti Bar called on me and pleaded with me to let her remain in Kumasi. They could not conceive of the smooth flow of work without her. I could not oblige. In Accra, as in Kumasi, she took her judicial duties seriously and I regularly received with intense pride glowing reports about her judicial competence. Though not robust in build, she was extremely robust in mind and had guts. She was endowed with one of the qualities that is indispensable in a good judge—namely, the courage of her conviction. It did not matter on what particular schedule she was posted, either in the criminal court, land court, on company and industrial duties or probate and matrimonial schedule. She always delivered the goods, as they say.

In January last year while she had just left the probate, divorce and family schedule, I asked her to give me an opinion whether the High Court can entertain a petition for divorce of a marriage celebrated under customary law. She wrote what I can only describe as a dissertation on the subject and traced the High Court's jurisdiction from 1876 through the Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971, through to the present Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372), and expressed as her opinion that, that jurisdiction exists. On 14 January 1981, I acknowledged her opinion in a letter, paragraph 2 of which reads:

"I appreciate the promptitude you accorded my request and am particularly impressed by the depth in which you analysed the matter and the process of reasoning by which you came to your clearly correct view. I am grateful for it and have nothing but the highest admiration for the clarity of thought and the legal scholarship exhibited in your approach."

This letter was placed in her personal confidential file and it is from it that I made the above quote. I don't think I need say anymore about her legal ability.

She was, consistent with her upbringing, extremely high-minded and innately refined. I believe the profession would agree that her reputation for integrity is impeccable. She was not without fault. I had on occasions queried her for delayed judgments and reminded her of the much-quoted judicial slogan—justice delayed is justice denied or as others put it justice is sweetest when it is freshest. She often pleaded in mitigation, her other obligations. She was a housewife as well as a mother and had a house to keep. That, of course, I always understand.

Cecilia, as her friends call her, was deeply religious and I am proud to say that I share with her not only the same profession but the same religion and we all belong to the same parish to boot. With her friends, family and judicial colleagues, you can well understand the sense of personal loss, I feel at her very tragic end. But as they say, that which is fated must be and what we cannot help we must endure

These three judges who lie before us motionless have each in turn, pronounced death sentences on persons proved guilty before them of committing offences for which our law prescribes death sentence. But they all have been cruelly put to death, as far as we know, for committing no offence. It is impossible to conceive of a clearer case of judicial martyrdom. In that manner, they will ever remain in our legal annals.

Before I am done, permit me to do one last act in their memory. I invite the Chief Registrar to step forward and call out the cases listed before them for trial today—7 July 1982. (At this point, the Chief Registrar stepped forward and called three cases listed before Justices Agyepong, Sarkodie and Koranteng-Addow for Wednesday 7 July 1982. The Chief Justice then asked. "Where are the judges?" And the Chief Registrar replied—“All the judges are absent.”). His Lordship then continued his tribute and concluded:

The Chief Registrar says all the judges are absent. They are not, in fact, absent. Here they lie, not absent—but present in another form. They have all been translated into a happier, peaceful world, and with them sooner or later we will all be re-united. Until we meet them, I know that I echo the feelings of my judicial colleagues when I say farewell! May your deaths remain to us the monuments of judicial courage and may judges yet unborn draw both solace and inspiration from the selfless devotion to duty that you have all singly and collectively shown. And may your deaths be symbols of a new beginning of the hopes and aspirations which our founding fathers enshrined in our national motto—Freedom and Justice.

Tribute by the Attorney-General

It is with a heavy heart that I stand before you this morning to pay tribute to these three honourable judges and the retired major whose untimely death was reported to have taken place between the night of Wednesday, 30 June 1982 and the morning of Thursday, 1 July 1982. The whole of Ghana was shocked by the announcement on Sunday, 4 July that these sons and a daughter of the soil of Ghana had met their death at the hands of assassins who had forcibly removed them from their various residences and brutally murdered them in cold blood.

When it was announced that they had been kidnapped, everyone had high hopes that they would return alive. This was because it was unheard of in the annals of the social history of this country for anyone to have been murdered in such circumstances. It is unbelievable that persons against whom these innocent men and woman had no grudge or quarrel, could dish out such outrageous treatment to their bodies. We are saddened, we are shocked, we are dismayed at this melancholy news. May they rest in peace.

One cannot pay tribute to these illustrious children of Ghana without recalling the singular contributions made by all of them in their respective fields of human endeavour towards the reshaping of our society in Ghana. These three judges of the Superior Court of judicature, namely. Mrs. Justice Cecilia Koranteng-Addow, Mr. Justice Fred Sarkordie and Mr. Justice K. A. Agyepong can be regarded as shining examples on the Bench. They proved themselves in the type of decisions they gave during the period they all served as judges. To members of the Bar, the expectation that they would get justice whenever they appeared before any of them was always current. It is well known that many members of the Bar were even satisfied if they had to lose cases before these judges because they were more than convinced and assured that justice would be done or seen to be done at least when finally judgment was delivered in their cases. It is saddening to note that three brilliant judges of our beloved country who have made singular contributions to the advancement of the law should have their careers cut short by terrorists who cannot be said to appreciate that the Ghanaian society will certainly miss them. The heinous crime that was committed against them will forever remain in the minds of Ghanaians as the most dastardly that had ever been perpetrated on our land. It is in fact unprecedented. Whatever decisions they might have taken daring their career as judges were taken in furtherance of the enrichment of the law through what might appear to others to be a complex process of interpretation.

It has been said in many quarters that they were killed because of certain judgments they had given on certain controversial issues in our legal system. We are inclined to believe that this was what the perpetrators of the heinous crime would want us to believe and accept as the truth. Who could be said to have nursed such grievances against them six months after the accession into power of the Government of the Provisional National Defence Council to warrant such an inhuman and barbarous act as was said to have been committed on or about 1 July 1982? That day certainly was the darkest in the history of this country.  For, it is inconceivable that innocent persons could virtually be dragged out of their various homes leaving their poor wives, husband and children behind only to meet their untimely death at the hands of cold blooded assassins who had no conscience.

It is worthy of note to recollect that these three judges had great concern for the deprived citizens who appeared before them. One can readily recall that Mr. Justice K. A. Agyepong in 1977 took the historic decision which led to the release of most of the persons who were detained under the regime of the late Mr. Kutu Acheampong. Several important decisions of similar nature were taken by the other judges which made anyone regard them as having concern for oppressed persons who appealed to them for redress. It is our hope that their departure from this earth will inspire other persons who have been left behind to aspire towards more noble ideals that will further the cause of human advancement on this earth.

I have personally known all the three judges for a considerable length of time. With Fred, I had worked for some time at the Attorney-General's Department before his appointment as a High Court Judge. He was quiet and pleasant person to work with who considered his personal appearance to be of prime importance to him. The least said about Cecilia the better, because her absence reminds me of a great loss I have incurred in my life. She was affable and kindly, had time for every person. She was prepared to take on, sympathise and carry part of everybody else's problems and burdens. I got to know Kwadwo Agyepong in 1970 when I was transferred to Kumasi, and worked with him at the Bar. One cannot lose sight of the love, patience and friendly attitude he exhibited towards all at the Bar. The departure of these illustrious persons are indeed a great loss.

This tribute cannot be completed without mentioning the role which Major Sam Acquah has also played in the reshaping of this country. Until his untimely death, he was the Director of Personnel at GIHOC, and had been working in that establishment since his retirement from the Army. As an officer, he was brilliant and was highly commended during the regime of the late Dr. Kwame Nkrumah. He served with distinction as a military intelligence officer after receiving training in several countries. Many army officers who served under him would recall that he was a disciplined man, honest and very hardworking. He was very loyal and very reliable. He worked assiduously on any issue that was referred to him, and could always be expected to produce solutions to the most intricate of problems. By his death, Ghana has lost one of her most patriotic sons who still had so much to give towards the further advancement of our society. It was only last week, Tuesday, that his appointment as Managing Director of Bonsa Tyre Company and Ghana Rubber Estates Ltd. (formerly Firestone Ltd.) was approved by the Committee of P.N.D.C. Secretaries. His loss will forever be felt by all who had to deal with him when he was alive. May he also rest in peace.

Ghanaians will forever remember all these four distinguished personalities, and we hope that our society will strive to produce persons of equal merit to keep the torch they have lit burning.

Tribute by the President of the Ghana Bar Association

My Lord the Chief Justice, my Lords, ladies and gentlemen, we are gathered here today to pay tribute to three High Court Judges who met their death in circumstances that defy description.

I have known Mr. Justice K. A. Agyepong from his secondary school days at Mfantsipim. He was a year ahead of me and was my house prefect in Balmer House. I shared the same junior dormitory with him. At Mfantsipim School in those days, a person would not be considered for the post of a prefect if his class work was not exemplary, and John Kwadwo Agyei, as he was then known, was a brilliant scholar. He left the school in 1946 but came back later and this time as a member of the teaching staff. This was when he was preparing himself for his intermediate degree of Bachelor of Arts.

After his education in the then Gold Coast, Mr. Agyepong left for Britain on Cocoa Marketing Board scholarship to read law at King's College, University of London. He came in 1955 when I was in my last year at the same college, and we continued our friendship from where we had left off. He was a member of the Honourable Society of Inner Temple and after being called to the Bar in 1960 he returned home to be enrolled on 10 February 1961 and did his pupilage in the chambers of the late Amankwatia. He established a successful legal practice in Kumasi until his elevation as a High Court Judge on 27 September 1977.

The impression of my colleagues about Mr. Justice Agyepong is that he was capable, patient, understanding and particularly bold and hardworking. He very often sat without a break from 10.00 a.m. to 4.00 p.m., provided counsel agreed to stay. It is not difficult for counsel to tell how his mind was working because some times at the stage of summons for directions, he would ask counsel whether he had considered the decision of the Court of Appeal in such and such a case. He often turned his court into a tutorial class when he was hearing a case that involved an intricate or novel proposition of law. All counsel in the court would contribute their views. This he did in the interest of the development of the law along the correct lines. He was a Methodist and took religion seriously. This, according to him, enabled him to have his vision clear and his direction in life correct.

Unlike Mr. Justice Kwadwo Agyei Agyepong with whom I attended the same secondary school, Mr. Justice Fred Poku Sarkodie was a student of Adisadel College, Cape Coast, a few miles from Mfantsipim. After completing his education in the then Gold Coast he went to the United Kingdom to read law. He was a member of the Honourable Society of Middle Temple. He was called to the Bar on 12 July 1960 and was subsequently enrolled in Ghana on 6 December the same year. He started work at the Attorney-General's Department where he distinguished himself as an efficient and capable prosecutor. From there he was appointed High Court Judge.

Mr. Justice Sarkodie was by nature a perfect gentleman, peaceful and friendly. During trials he asked few questions or none at all and counsel never knew how his mind was working until he read his judgment. As a judge, he was hardworking, fair and firm. Few lawyers or litigants, if any, could complain genuinely about his handling of cases or his judgments. He was born and bred an Anglican and I hasten to add a practising Christian. He was the Chancellor of the Diocese of Koforidua, one of the dioceses carved out of the Accra Diocese on 18 October 1981.

Mrs. Justice Cecilia Koranteng-Addow attended OLA and Holy Child Secondary School, Cape Coast, after which she entered the University of Hull. She so distinguished. herself in her university days in Britain that she won the admiration of H.M. Queen Elizabeth II who invited her to dinner at Buckingham Palace. She was called to the Bar in 1963 and she returned home in 1964 to engage in Private legal practice.

After a few years private practice, she joined the Bench as a district magistrate. From there she rose through the ranks until she was appointed a High Court Judge in 1977. As a High Court Judge, Mrs. Justice Cecilia Koranteng-Addow had her court always under perfect control and she was very firm but understanding and affable. Her sense of justice was keen and she worked very hard sitting for long hours in order to dispense justice without delay.

I was in her court just as a listener during her hearing of the case of Pennie v. Egala. She sat overtime and completed the hearing within two days, the court was addressed and she delivered a very learned and well-reasoned judgment reported in [1980] G.L.R.D. 24. What impressed me most during this trial was her firm control of the situation, her alertness of mind and sense of responsibility. She was a devout Catholic and what Americans call "Born again Christian." May the Lord have mercy on her soul.

With the untimely death of Mr. Justice Kwadwo Agyei Agyepong, Mr. Justice Fred Poku Sarkodie and Mrs. Justice Cecilia Koranteng-Addow, the judiciary in Ghana has sustained an irreparable loss, for they were all very efficient, hardworking and trustworthy judges. They stood for justice but they were the victims of injustice; they all showed Christian love and charity but they were all victims of hate and vengeance and lastly, they showed mercy but they received none in return. One cannot help agreeing with Richard Baxter, the hymnist, when he said, "My knowledge of (that) life is small, the eye of faith is dim. But,’ its enough that Christ knows all and I shall be with Him." We hope and pray that in the court of their Maker they would have justice, love and mercy, the ideals they lived and died for. The Ghana Bar Association mourns with the Bench at the loss of their colleagues and we share your grief and loss. We have the consolation that so long as justice according to law properly so called is administered in these courts, so long will the spirits of the three go marching on even though their bodies may lie smouldering in the grave.

To the bereaved family, the Bar Association expresses its deepest and sincere condolences at the untimely death of the three judges. We have already condemned in no uncertain terms their abduction and murder, we have called on the government to institute a public inquest. This request we understand is being seriously considered.

The Review salutes these gallant judges who will go down in history as the judicial martyrs of Ghana. MISCELLANY-AT-LAW: THE LETTER AND SPIRIT OF THE CONSTITUTION [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 28—29

What is meant by the phrase " letter and spirit" of the Constitution, 1979? Could the term whatever it means be used as an aid in construing provisions of the Constitution? In Tuffuor v. Attorney-General,1 the court gave an answer to these questions.

The court having held that on the coming into force of the Constitution, 1979, the incumbent Chief Justice, the Hon. Mr. Justice Apaloo, became the Chief Justice and the Head of the Judiciary by virtue of the provisions of article 127(8) and (9) and that it would be clearly inconsistent with the provisions of article 128 to remove him as Chief Justice by recourse to article 127(1), said per Sowah J.S.C.:

" The ideals which the framers of the Constitution were at pains by the letter and spirit of this Constitution to establish ought to be respected and adhered to. They are justice and fair play, abhorrence of arbitrariness and discrimination; victimisation and vindictiveness, the protection of the individual and his fundamental human rights within the walls of the Constitution. We believe that it was in pursuance of these ideals that the framers of the Constitution formulated their proposals amongst which are article 127(8) and (9) and which were approved by the Constituent Assembly. Conformably with these ideals, the court finds both by its letter and spirit that upon a proper and true construction of article 127(8) and (9), the Chief Justice was amongst the class of justices who were deemed to have retained the various offices upon taking their oaths. It is this letter and spirit which animate our judgment." Earlier in its judgment, the court per Sowah J.S.C. had said:

"A written Constitution such as ours is not an ordinary Act of Parliament. It embodies the will of a people. It also mirrors their history. Account, therefore, needs to be taken of it as a landmark in a people's search for progress. It contains within it their aspirations and their hopes for a better, fuller life.

The Constitution has its letter of the law. Equally, the Constitution has its spirit. It is the fountain-head for the authority which each of the three arms of government possesses and exercises. It is a source of strength. It is a source of power . . . Its methods of alteration are specified. In our peculiar circumstances, those methods require the involvement of the whole body politic of Ghana. Its language, therefore, must be considered as if it were a living organism capable of growth and development . . . A broad and liberal spirit is required for its interpretation. It does not admit of a narrow interpretation. A doctrinaire approach to interpretation would not do. We must take account of its principles and bring that consideration to bear in bringing it into conformity with the needs of the time."

NO CASE TO ANSWER [1981 & 82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 90—112

Osew E. A.*

I. INTRODUCTION

MOST legal practitioners, if not all, have at one time or the other, attempted the precarious game of "Submission of No Case to Answer" as a short circuit to a whole trial.  It is worth doing so in the proper circumstances. For, if the submission is upheld, counsel's client is freed from giving evidence under the suspicious eye of the Bench and from being cross-examined to the glee of the prosecution.  More advantageously, it takes out the defendant from the likely danger of entangling himself in the complicated web of evidence thus helping the prosecution in drowning him in the abyss of guilt.  Dr. Glanville Williams puts the effect of a successful submission of no case to answer in these words1:

"Not only does a successful application protect the defendant from going into the witness-box, where he might be compelled to make damaging admissions, but it also prevents the prosecution from making good deficiencies in its own evidence by cross-examining the other witnesses for the defence."

II. CIRCUMSTANCES JUSTIFYING A SUBMISSION OF NO CASE TO ANSWER

A submission of no case to answer is made when the prosecution has called evidence and closed its case and before the defence gives evidence. Circumstances justifying such submission are well stated in Storey v. Storey where it was held2:

“There are, however, two sets of circumstances under which a defendant may submit that he has no case to answer. In the one case there may be a submission that, accepting the plaintiff's evidence at its face value, no case has been established in law, and in the other that the evidence led for the plaintiff is so unsatisfactory or unreliable that the court should find that the burden of proof has not been discharged."

This definition was amplified in the Practice Direction of the English Divisional Court per Lord Parker C. J. thus3:

"Those of us who sit in the Divisional Court have the distinct impression that justices today are being persuaded all too often to uphold a submission of no case.  In the result, this court has had on many occasions to send the case back to the justices for the hearing to be continued with inevitable delay and increased expenditure. Without attempting to lay down any principle of law, we think that as a matter of practice justices should be guided by the following considerations.

A submission that there is no case to answer may properly be made and upheld: (a) when there has been no evidence to prove an essential element in the alleged offence; (b) when the evidence adduced by the prosecution has been so discredited as a result of cross-examination or is so manifestly unreliable that no reasonable tribunal could safely convict upon it.

Apart from these two situations a tribunal should not in general be called upon to reach a decision as to conviction or acquittal until the whole of the evidence which either side wishes to tender has been placed before it."

This common law rule is codified by the Ghana Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30), ss. 173 and 271 which give the discretion to the court (in a summary trial) and to the judge (in a trial on indictment) respectively, to acquit the accused or call upon him to make his defence depending on whether or not the court or the judge is of the opinion that a case has or has not been made against him after the close of the case for the prosecution. The sections are fully discussed later in this article.

III. WHAT IS A PRIMA FACIE CASE?

The ground upon which a defendant is called upon to open his case is when, as earlier stated, the evidence adduced by the prosecution has reached a degree of proof which in legal parlance is referred to as a prima facie case.  In a nutshell, a prima facie case means an allegation sufficiently supported by evidence as to call for an answer from the other party.  In other words, it is one which calls for an investigation. We may here refer to some definitions of prima facie evidence.  The Dictionary of English Law defines it thus,4  "Prima facie [means] at first sight; on the face of it." It goes further to distinguish the literal definition from the legal one.

"Prima facie evidence [means] that which, not being inconsistent with the falsity of the hypothesis, nevertheless raises such degree of probability in its favour that it must prevail if believed by the jury unless rebutted or the contrary proved." [Compared with], “conclusive evidence, on the other hand, is that which excludes or at least tends to exclude, the possibility of the truth of any other hypothesis than the one attempted to be established."

It was also thus defined by Ghose J. in an Indian case, Sher Singh v, Jitendranath Sen as5:

“It (prima facie) means that there is ground for proceeding ... but a prima facie case is not the same thing as ‘proof ’ which comes later when the Court has to find whether the accused is guilty or not guilty. ‘Proof ’ is nothing but belief according to the conditions laid down in the Evidence Act, and it is an obvious fallacy to say the magistrate has found a prima facie case, therefore, he believes the case to be true in the sense that the case is proved."

In the same case, Lart-Williams J., gave his version,6 "The evidence discloses a prima facie case when it is such that if uncontradicted and if believed it will be sufficient to prove the case against the accused." And over a century ago a famous English judge, Willis J., distinguished between no evidence and reasonable evidence7:

"It was formerly considered necessary in all cases to leave the question to the jury if there was any evidence, even a scintilla, in support of the case; but it is now settled that the question for the judge (subject of course to review) is, . . . not whether there is literally no evidence, but whether there is none that ought reasonably to satisfy the jury that the fact sought to be proved is established."

And, Lord Devlin in our era comments8:

“. . . there is in truth a fundamental difference between the question whether there is any evidence and the question whether there is enough evidence . . . It is the business of the judge as the expert who has a mind trained to make examinations of the sort to test the chain of evidence for the weak links before he sends it out to the jury; in other words, it is for him to ascertain whether it has any reliable strength at all and then for the jury to determine how strong it is."

Professor Rupert Cross also holds this view9:

“The test to be applied by the judge in order to determine whether there is sufficient evidence in favour of the proponent of an issue, is for him to enquire whether there is evidence which, uncontradicted, would justify men of ordinary reason and fairness in affirming the proposition which the proponent is bound to maintain. This test is easy to apply when the evidence is direct, for the question whether witnesses are to be believed must be left to the jury, but it is necessary somewhat vague when circumstantial evidence has to be considered. In that case, little more can be done than to enquire whether the proponent's evidence warrants an inference of the facts in issue or whether it merely leads to conjecture concerning them."

Like grave offences, before a circumstantial evidence reaches a prima facie case, the evidence adduced against the defendant should reach a relatively high standard of proof. This is what Cross says as earlier noted above and Devlin J. agrees with him.

In R. v. Atter,10 someone murdered the deceased without any proven motive with a weapon which was never discovered; the accused was with the deceased at about the time of her death and the accused's clothing when subjected to benzidine test gave a positive reaction to the presence of blood, but the expert witness was not prepared to state that the blood was human blood. Devlin J. (as he then was), at the close of the case for the prosecution, upholding counsel's submission of no case to answer, stated, inter alia:

"Where one has a case where the evidence is purely circumstantial then I must satisfy myself, in my judgment, that there is some piece of evidence that is more than mere suspicion, that there is some piece of evidence which would justify me in saying that points to the accused.  You cannot put a multitude of suspicions together and make a proof of it."

“The Ghana case of The State v. Ali Kassena seems to be on all fours with Atter; and the Supreme Court, in its ruling, quoted Devlin J.’s dictum with approval. The learned trial judge in Ali Kassena, in ruling that there was a case against the accused, stated11":

“There is of course no eye-witness account of the act resulting in the death of the deceased but in my opinion this is not a sine qua non.  There is evidence that accused showed hostile intentions towards deceased and followed him up to his house. The deceased was found dead the same night that accused was alleged to have threatened him. The clothes and palms of the accused were found to be stained with human blood. In these circumstances I do not think I would be justified to withdraw the case from the jury. In my opinion a prima  facie case of murder has been made against the accused. The submission of no case is overruled and the accused is called upon to make his defence to the offence charged”.

The Supreme Court, however, reversed the decision of the trial judge on appeal, basing its ruling mainly on the ground that the prosecution failed to prove that the human blood found in the accused’s clothing and palms was the blood of the deceased and also that the accused was the only person who had the opportunity of lying in wait to inflict harm on the deceased.

Contrary to the popular acceptance of the Supreme Court judgment in this case, the writer, with the greatest respect, is inclined to support the trial judge’s ruling that, the evidence led at the close of the case for the prosecution was sufficient for the accused to be called upon to make his defence, on the following grounds:

(a) The Supreme Court seemed to have viewed the case as if the whole trial had gone through to a finish. Its judgment would have been without question if the defence had given evidence and the trial judge  had directed the jury to convict without proper direction on blood evidence or the evidence on the whole; but the ruling, as stated above, coming at this stage where the prosecution has closed its case, shows a prima facie case. For, at this stage, unlike at the end of the whole case, the standard of proof required is not  “ beyond reasonable doubt” but sufficient evidence.

(b) Even though it may be dangerous to leave evidence smacked with suspicion to the jury, it is submitted that the jury has the opportunity of listening to the trial judge’s summing-up on the facts and on the law before giving its verdict at the end of the whole case; and modern juries are reasonable and experienced enough to discern the substance from the chaff.

(c) There is also the opportunity for the accused to appeal if he is aggrieved that he has been wrongly convicted by the court of first instance.

(d) There is a fine distinction between Ali Kassena and Atter in that, unlike the former, in the latter case it was not categorically stated that the blood found on the accused was human blood.

These points of criticism are confined to the instant case, for the writer agrees with the Supreme Court in the Ali Kassena case that the standard of proof in very serious cases and in cases where evidence is circumstantial should be high.  In the words of Denning L.J. (as he then was) in Bater v. Bater12 on the standard of proof in criminal cases:

"It is of course true that by our law a higher standard of proof is required in criminal cases than in civil cases. But this is subject to the qualification that there is no absolute standard in either case. In criminal cases the charge must be proved beyond reasonable doubt, but there may be degrees of proof within that standard.

As Best C.J., and many other great judges have said,  ‘in proportion as the crime is enormous, so ought the proof to be clear’ ... a reasonable doubt is simply that degree of doubt which would prevent a reasonable and just man from coming to the conclusion. So the phrase ‘reasonable doubt’ takes the matter no further.  It does not say that the degree of probability must be as high as 99 per cent or as low as 51 per cent.  The degree required must depend on the mind of the reasonable and just man who is considering the particular subject-matter.  In some cases 51 per cent would be enough, but not in others. When this is realized, the phrase ‘reasonable doubt’ can be used just as aptly in a civil case ... as in a criminal case . . . The only difference is that, because of our high regard for the liberty of the individual, a doubt may be regarded as reasonable in the criminal courts, which would not be so in the civil courts."

IV. SUBMISSION OF NO CASE IN LAW

According to the authorities, submission of no case to answer will succeed in law when the essential ingredients of the offence have not been proved by the prosecution at the close of its case.

Firstly, there is the need to prove the essential ingredients of the offence charged. For example, in murder, the intention to cause death by unlawful harm must be proved;13 in receiving, proof of knowledge of the accused that the property was obtained or appropriated by any crime and that there was possession direct or constructive14; in robbery, stealing must be accompanied by use of force or harm or threat of criminal assault with intent;15 in the offence of defrauding by false pretences the ingredients are false representation . . . either with knowledge that such representation is false or without belief that it is true16; in extortion by a public officer, the demand should have been made by a public officer under the colour of his office and the thing obtained should be money or of valuable consideration with the knowledge of the accused that he is not lawfully authorised to demand or obtain it17; and in the offence of perjury, proof that the statement was made or verified upon oath before a court or a public officer and that the maker knew it to be false in a material particular, or had no reason to believe it to be true18.

Secondly, it is imperative that these essential ingredients of the offence be stated in the particulars of the charge or indictment, failure of which is fatal to the case of the prosecution.  And, for the case to stand or fall it will depend on whether or not the said particulars go to the essence or form the gravamen of the offence charged. Two cases of the Supreme Court clearly illustrate this proposition. The first is Donkor v. The State19 and the second Akowuah v. Commissioner of  Police20. In Donkor, the appellant appealed against a conviction of  extortion by a public officer.  There Ollennu J. (as he then was) stated21:

“The law on this point, i.e. the proper interpretation of Section 112 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30) has been explained in some detail in the Akowuah case. We may restate it as follows. A charge has two parts: Part I must state the specific offence with which the accused person is charged and Part II should give particulars of the charge.  The particulars required in Part II are of two kinds: (i) particulars which are only necessary for giving reasonable information as to the nature of the charge and (ii) particulars which are of the essence or form the gravamen of the offence charged. As to particulars in (i), an omission to aver the first type of particulars, namely, those only necessary for giving reasonable information as to the nature of the charge, renders the charge merely defective. That defect however may be cured by evidence which supplies the omission. As to the particulars in (ii), an omission of the second category of particulars from the charge, i.e. those particulars which are of the essence of or form the gravamen of the charge is fatal, because without them the charge would disclose no offence.  Such an omission cannot be cured by evidence, it can only be cured by amendment."

The Criminal Procedure Code, 1960,22 s.233, however, to some extent, comes to the aid of a badly prepared indictment. The section which states that no indictment is to be held insufficient for certain omissions provides:

“233. No indictment for any offence shall be held insufficient for want of the averment of any matter unnecessary to be proved, nor for omitting to state the time at which the offence was committed, nor for stating the time imperfectly, nor for stating the offence to have been committed on a date subsequent to that of the indictment, or on an impossible day, or on a day that never happened, nor for want of the statement of the value or price of any matter or thing, or the amount of damage, injury, or spoil, in any case where the time, value, or price, or the amount of damage, injury, or spoil is not of the essence of the offence."

But section 202(2) of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, as amended by section 2 of the Criminal Procedure Code (Amendment) Act, 196523 states:

“202. (2) Every indictment shall contain and shall be sufficient if it contains a statement of the offence with which the accused is charged, together with such particulars as may be necessary for giving reasonable information as to the nature of the charge and notwithstanding any rule of law to the contrary, it shall not be necessary for it to contain any further particulars than the said particulars."

It is submitted that section 233 of Act 30 does not apply to all omissions.  It refers only to matters not going to the root or the essence of the charge; for where the omission is one affecting the gravamen of the offence charged the section cannot save it where amendment has not been made.24

Act 30, s.110 (2) also insists that an objection by the defence when a charge or an indictment is defective should be taken at the commencement of the trial or earlier.  Notwithstanding this provision, it will still be appropriate to raise the objection at the close of the prosecution's case (the time of submission of no case) provided that the omission or the ill-framed charge is substantial and goes to the essence of the offence charged, and till then, no amendment has been made by the prosecution.  For, objection can be raised at the end of the trial before judgment and even on appeal.25  While not explicit on indictment, the Act allows the prosecution, in summary trial, to amend a defective charge but it should be done before it closes its case.26

Section 406 of Act 30 also states that no finding or sentence shall be reversed on appeal or review by reason of error, omission in the charge or irregularity in the complaint, summons, warrant, charge, proclamation, order or judgment, or other proceedings, etc.  It is, however, submitted that the section will not avail the prosecution after submission of no case where an amendment has not been made earlier; because the section envisages a situation where the whole trial has been completed and the objection is first raised on appeal; but not at the stage of the submission of no case.

Submission of No Case Wrongly Overruled

There is a great difficulty here and the law is still not certain and settled in a situation where a judge or a court overrules wrongly, a submission of no case to answer.  The effect of the adverse ruling may be that in giving evidence the defence may make admissions that could incriminate him and lead to a conviction. This problem faced Kingdon C.J. and his colleagues of the West African Court of Appeal years ago in the currency case of R. v. Akinpelu Ajani.27

In his elaborate judgment his lordship reviewed the earlier English authorities by quoting in extenso from Darling J.'s judgment in R. v. Power28:

“The authorities in this court on this point are not in accord.  A statement made by myself when delivering the judgment of the Court in Rex v. Joiner29 was not followed by Lord Alverstone C.J., in Rex v. Fraser.30 In that case Lord Alverstone said: ‘Where an objection is taken by counsel unsuccessfully, and he then calls evidence, this Court is not bound to disregard the effect of that evidence.' In Rex v. Pearson,31 where the conviction was quashed, I said: ‘Of course, if admissions by the defendant had supplied the evidence that was wanting, we should not have interfered with the jury's verdict,’ and in Rex v. George,32 Phillimore J. said: ‘As the case proceeded to the end the Court of Criminal Appeal are of opinion that they ought to look at the whole of the evidence.’ It is true that in Rex v. Joiner, Darling J., said: ‘If there was no evidence’ (of the offence) ‘then it was the duty of the Court to withdraw the case from the jury.  We have no right to look and see whether any evidence of larceny was elicited after the close of the case for the prosecution, when counsel for the prisoner submitted that there was no evidence to go to the jury.’ At present the Court is not prepared to follow Rex v. Joiner, but prefers the reasoning in the other two cases.’ This Court agrees that Lord Alverstone’s statement of the law was accurate, and that the judgment in Rex v. Joiner did not put the matter rightly.  It is therefore impossible to quash the conviction in this case on the ground that the case for the prosecution disclosed no evidence against the appellant. As a result of the evidence given by the other defendant, there was a case against the appellant. But the conviction of the appellant must be quashed on the ground that there was misdirection and non-direction by the Commissioner in his summing-up. The defence raised by the appellant was not put to the jury."

Kingdon C.J. continued his own judgment,33 after this extensive review:

"And that is how the law stands today, and it appears to amount to this, that in cases where the prosecution has made out no case against an accused, but in spite of that he is called upon by the Court to enter upon his defence instead of being discharged—

a) If at the close of the case for the prosecution his Counsel, if he was represented, made no submission, he can be properly convicted upon evidence subsequently given; and

(b) If at the close of the case for the prosecution, he, being unrepresented and probably completely ignorant of procedure, made no submission, he can be properly convicted upon evidence subsequently given; and

(c) If at the close of the case for the prosecution he or his Counsel made a submission which wrongly overruled then, if either he or his Counsel took any part in the subsequent proceedings, an appeal against a conviction resulting from those proceedings will fail; but

(d) If at the close of the case for the prosecution he or his Counsel made a submission, which was wrongly overruled, and then refused to take any part in the subsequent proceedings, he will be ‘quite safe’, i.e. apparently certain to get a possible conviction quashed on appeal.”

In this difficult case, where former authorities were uncertain, Kingdon C.J., may not be blamed for not stating the law positively. He, however, quashed the conviction in these words34:

“At the close of the case for the prosecution there was no case for the appellant to answer and he should have been discharged. But since he was called upon for his defence and took part in the subsequent proceedings this Court has looked at the evidence against him on this count as a whole and finds that it consists exclusively of the uncorroborated evidence of accomplices, and since it is clear that the trial Judge failed to direct himself properly as to such evidence, the conviction on count three  must be set aside on this ground also.”

It is submitted that on (d) above, instead of the defence refusing to participate in the subsequent proceeding after his submission has been wrongly overruled, the impasse may be avoided if he at this stage files an ‘interlocutory’ appeal which should be determined before the trial proceeds.35

The Ghanaian courts seem to have taken a more firm stand in this difficult circumstances of wrongful overruling of no case to answer than the English courts. This is illustrated by the following cases: R.v. Osabu and Maaseyo36, Nyarko v. The State37 and Donkor v. The State38.

In R.v Osabu and Maaseyo, a strong appellate court (van Lare Ag. C.J., Granville Sharpe J.A. and Adumua-Bossman J. ), faced with the same problem, after reviewing the English authorities and the West African Court of Appeal case of Ajani, held, inter alia, that  a distinction should be drawn between the situation where, on a submission of  “no case’’ ,  there is some evidence which could go to the jury, and the situation where there is no evidence at that stage to go to the jury. In the former case, if there is subsequent conviction, the appellate court is entitled to look at all the evidence in the case. This, even if the submission was wrongly rejected, will include all the evidence subsequently admitted so that, notwithstanding the wrongful rejection, the appellate court may exercise the power conferred upon it by the Court of Appeal Ordinance, 1957,s 10 (1), which empowers the dismissal of an appeal which would otherwise be successful if in fact no substantial miscarriage of justice has occurred.  In the latter case, however, where there was no evidence that could have gone to the jury at the time of the submission, the wrongful rejection of the submission amounts to a wrongful decision on a point of law. This being so, the evidence put in consequently was not part of "all the evidence in the case," and the appellate court was thus effectively precluded from considering it and hence from considering whether it was a fit case for the proviso in section 10 of the Ordinance to be applied.39

In the Supreme Court case of Nyarko v. The State, at the close of the case for the prosecution (a case of Indian hemp) it had failed to establish the essential elements of the crime against the second and third appellants, namely, that both or either appellant was in possession of the hand bag and had knowledge of its contents.  The court, therefore, gave approval to the decision in Osabu and Maaseyo, by holding that if at the close of the prosecution's case, no evidence has been led incriminating or connecting the accused with the offence and a submission of no case is made which is wrongly overruled by the trial judge, the appellate court is not entitled to take into consideration any subsequent incriminating evidence adduced against the accused.

And in the case of Donkor v. The State, where the charge was extortion contrary to section 239 of Act 29, the Supreme Court, allowing the appeal, held that since the prosecution elected not to lead evidence in proof of the matter charged, there was no triable offence before the court.  The submission of no case made on behalf of the appellant should have been upheld and the fact that some of the evidence given by the appellant, after he had been wrongly called upon for his defence, seemed to supply some of the omissions of the prosecution, did not change the legal position that no offence had been alleged or proved by the prosecution.

THE RIGHT OF THE DEFENCE TO SUBMIT NO CASE AND THE RIGHT OF THE PROSECUTION TO REPLY

The Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, does not expressly state that the defence has the right to make a submission of no case to answer.  It is also silent on the right of the prosecution to reply to a no case submission.  Sections 173 and 271, however, give the discretion to the court (in summary trial) and to the judge (in trial on indictment) to decide whether or not to call upon the accused or direct that he should be called upon to make his defence.

In the case of Commissioner of Police v. Osei Yaw Akoto,40 the right of submission and reply became an issue in respect of section 173 of Act 30. The prosecution, after a magistrate had freed the accused on a submission of no case, argued, on an appeal to the High Court that, on a proper interpretation of section 173 of Act 30, the defence had no right to make a submission of no case and conceded also that the prosecution had no right of reply.

Dismissing the application, Akainyah J. (as he then was) said41:

"I hold therefore that the proper interpretation of the section is that the court’s power to rule that there is no case to answer and the right of the defence to make it so appear to the court by the submission of no case to answer are not mutually exclusive.  In my view, they are coexistent and concurrent and counsel for the defence is entitled to make a submission of no case to answer.  I think the point taken by learned state attorney is not only a novelty but it is also not supported by any authority.  The application is therefore dismissed summarily.”

Eleven years after this finding, the Akoto case was reviewed by Taylor J. (as he then was) in an abortion case, Republic v. Akosah42 where counsel for the first accused, after submitting that there was no case to answer, sought to stop the prosecution from replying. In his ruling the learned judge came to these main conclusions:

(a) That the Akoto case did not decide that the prosecution had no right of reply. The impression created by the headnote of the report is erroneous.

(b) That even  though it was the opinion of Akainyah J. that defence, counsel should have the chance of submitting no case, the authorities cited by him in support of his ruling do not have close relevance to that issue.43

(c) That nowhere in Act 30 or in case law is it stated that the defence has the right to submit no case, even though in Ghana, following the English practice, such privilege or concession may be exercised at the discretion of the court or judge given them under sections 173 and 271 of Act 30.44

(d) That it stands to reason, equity and justice that if the privilege to submit no case is given to the defence, the same privilege should be extended to the prosecution to reply. "The scales of justice (says his lordship) cannot be evenly balanced if equal opportunities are not given to the parties”45.

He, therefore, overruled the objection, and held that the court could, in the exercise of its discretion, grant prosecution counsel the privilege of replying to a submission of no case with a view to obtaining assistance from counsel if needed.46.

The writer is in agreement with the learned judge on the strong points so far made. However, his lordship went further to state47:

"If in this situation defence counsel insists that he must be allowed to make a submission of no case, it is my view that it is within the discretion of the judge to agree or refuse and if he refuses in the rightful exercise of his discretion, I cannot see on what basis it can be said that he has erred in law, if the enabling provision is section 271 of Act 30."

It is respectfully submitted that even though sections 173 and 271 of Act 30 give the discretion only to the court or the judge to decide to call upon the accused to answer or acquit him at the close of the case for the prosecution, the writer would recommend that the law be amended to give the right of submitting no case to the defence.  Similarly, the right of reply should be granted to the prosecution.  In this respect, it is pertinent to refer to section 132 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30), as substituted by the Criminal Procedure Code (Amendment) Decree, 1976 (S.M.C.D. 56) which states:

"132.(1) In cases where the right of reply depends upon the question whether evidence has been called for the defence, the fact that the person charged has been called as a witness shall not of itself confer on the prosecution the right of reply.

(2) Any of the following officers when appearing personally as counsel for the prosecution shall, in all cases, have the right of reply, that is to say,—

(a) the Attorney-General, the Deputy Attorney-General, the Solicitor-General, the Director of Public Prosecutions or the Chief Parliamentary Draftsman;

(b) any Chief State Attorney, Principal State Attorney, Senior State Attorney or State Attorney;

(c) any Police Officer,  being a person of not less than three years standing as a lawyer.”

It is to be noted that S.M.C.D.56 particularly refers to addresses at the end of the whole case. However, it is referred to here to emphasize the need for a reply by the prosecution even to the defence’s submission of no case after the former has closed its case.48 This may prevent the occurrence of the few instances where the discretion may be used by the court or the judge against the defence either deliberately or through an error of law or wrongful assessment of evidence which may not have occurred if the defence’s right to submit and the prosecution’s right of reply had been exercised.

CAN THE PROSECUTOR CALL A WITNESS AFTER A SUBMISSION OF NO CASE?

An affirmative answer was given in R. v. McKenna.49 There M was charged with unlawfully exporting articles made of iron and steel. At the close of the prosecution's case, when it had been proved that M exported a steam-roller, counsel for the defence submitted that there was no case to answer, since no witness had said steam-rollers were made of iron and steel.  The judge, therefore, allowed a witness to be called to depose to this fact and this was upheld on appeal. In another case"50 the court held that save in special circumstances, a prosecution witness should not be called for the first time after the defence has closed, but it is in order to call one on a matter of formal proof after a submission of no case.

It is submitted that this practice should be rarely invoked; it should be resorted to only when it is by calling or re-calling such witness that justice could be done in arriving at a decision in the case and without prejudice to the parties.

CAN THE DEFENCE APPEAL IMMEDIATELY AFTER THE OVERRULING OF SUBMISSION OF NO CASE?

The usual practice is that after the overruling of a submission of no case by the defence, the trial is continued until it is completed.  In case the accused is convicted he may make the overruling of his submission of no case one of the grounds of appeal. And, the previous authorities cited show that where the submission was wrongly overruled by the trial court, the conviction would be quashed51".

However, in some cases where, as stated by Kingdon C.J. in the Ajani case, the defence may not like to continue with the trial until the issue whether the submission should succeed or not has been resolved, it is submitted, in the interest of justice and to avoid the danger of supplying evidence for his conviction when he opens his defence, he should not hesitate to appeal at this stage.

IS A SUBMISSION OF NO CASE INTERLOCUTORY OR FINAL FOR PURPOSES OF APPEAL?

In Atisu v. The Republic52 Andoh J. ruled that a submission of no case to answer was an interlocutory matter for the purposes of appeal, and in accordance with section 19 (4) of Act 372, the High Court is not properly seised with jurisdiction to entertain the appeal since leave to appeal was not obtained first from the district court or from the High Court. And, in Yevu v. The Republic53 it was held that there was no right of appeal under section 324 of Act 30 for an interlocutory order. In the same case it was held, obiter, that it is doubtful whether the statutory right of appeal from an interlocutory decision or order from a district court under section 19 (4) of the Courts Act, 1971, includes the right of appeal from the district court rejecting a submission of no case after the close of the prosecution's case; since the resultant deluge of cases, if it were so, would hold up trials, create arrears by impeding quick determination of cases and it would be contrary to judicial and public policy.  And, a High Court has recently held that since virtually there is no right of appeal against a ruling of a submission of no case, an application for leave to appeal against such a ruling should not be entertained by any court54.

It is respectfully submitted that, the Blay case is an extreme statement of the law and if followed, would in some cases, cause injustice to defendants whose submissions have been wrongly overruled. The obiter pronouncements in the Yevu case that receiving such applications on no case to answer would choke the courts with cases is no good ground either to disallow such appeals.  All will depend on the merits of each case55".

It is true that interlocutory matters apply to civil cases and that the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960, is silent on them; yet as an interlocutory matter implies a matter incidental or ancillary to a substantive suit which has not been disposed of, by analogy a submission of no case to answer could be conveniently classified as such; hence the need, following the practice in civil cases, to ask leave before appealing on its rejection by the trial court.  However, it is submitted that the accused should have the right to appeal after a submission of no case has been rejected, even if in civil cases, such leave is necessary.  It is therefore suggested that section 324 of Act 30, as amended by section 19(4) of Act 372, should be further amended to give the accused the right of appealing after a rejection of no case to answer as it is now done when the whole evidence has been given and the accused has been convicted.

CAN CERTIORARI QUASH A RULING ON A SUBMISSION OF NO CASE?

This problem came up for consideration in The Republic V. Accra Special Court; Ex parte Akosah56.  There the accused  took out the prerogative writ of certiorari to quash an adverse ruling of his submission of no case.  It was deposed to in the supporting affidavit that there was an error of law by the trial judge.  In answer to this ground the court, following several English authorities,57 held that the High Court can by certiorari quash the decision of an inferior tribunal, if the tribunal, acting within its jurisdiction, makes an error of law apparent on the record. Other than that the procedure should be by way of appeal, if permissible.  Archer J. (as he then was) had earlier made the same point.58

On whether or not at the stage of submission of no case the judge could make a finding of fact, it was held in the same case that the trial judge was right in not making a finding of fact which he was legally not bound to make at the close of the prosecution's case.  Finding of fact could only be made after the close of the case for the defence, because at the close of the case for the prosecution, all the facts in issue were legally rebuttable facts and displaceable by witnesses for the accused.

CAN A SUBMISSION OF NO CASE BE UPHELD IN COMMITTAL PROCEEDINGS?

Sowah J. (as he then was) was faced with this problem in State v. Bisa.59 The respondent was charged with murder and arraigned before the District Court, Ho, for committal and  subsequent trial at the Ho Criminal Sessions.  After counsel’s submission of no case to answer, the accused was discharged in conformity with section 184(5) of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960. The State appealed and the court held as stated in the headnote, that:—

“(1) the duty of the district magistrate under section 184(4) and (5) of the Criminal Procedure Code is to examine the summary of evidence and decide whether the charge is maintainable.  If it is, then he should commit the accused for trial; if not, he should discharge him;

(2)  It is not the duty of the district magistrate to decide whether the prosecution have made out a prima facie case for the accused to answer.  That is the province of the trial court after the close of the oral testimony given on behalf of the prosecution;

(3)  The phrase ‘if . . . there is a case . . . to answer' appearing in section 184 (4) of Act 30 must be given a restricted meaning.  A case to answer can only mean that if after due examination of the summary of evidence the magistrate comes to the conclusion that there is evidence, even a scintilla, to support the offence of which the accused stands charged, he must commit.  The credibility of witnesses does not arise at this stage."

It is submitted, with respect, that where it becomes necessary that the preliminary inquiry is not confined to examining only the summary of evidence but reaches the stage of hearing and the prosecution calls evidence and closes its case it will be proper for the defence to submit no case and the magistrate to uphold such submission if he finds no prima facie case has been made out against the accused.

NEED TO CURTAIL SUBMISSION OF NO CASE IN CRIMINAL CASES

Dr. Glanville Williams has recently argued for the minimisation, if not abolition, of the procedure in criminal trials of submission of no case to answer, particularly in magistrate courts. He argues that60:

"The opportunity to submit ‘no case’ is one of the features of our legal procedure giving it the appearance of a game of skill rather than of an inquiry into truth.  Its most objectionable aspect has been largely abolished in civil matters by judicial decisions, and should be abolished in criminal matters also. Let the judge decide whether there is evidence to go to the jury, but let him decide it only after all the evidence is in. In magistrates' courts, particularly, there is no rational place for a submission of ‘no case’. There is no sense in requiring lay magistrates to try to decide a case not on the basis of what they think but on the basis of what a hypothetical jury might think, and the present law provides a plenitude of opportunity for confusion, miscarriage of justice and public misunderstanding.

The abolition of the application to dismiss would not affect the present power to control perverse juries, nor would it affect the power of the court ¼ to bring a prosecution to a halt not because of the rights of the defendant but because its continuation would be a waste of the court’s time.

If legislation were introduced to abolish the present right to submit ‘no case’, this limitation of the rights of defendants could profitably be balanced by a declaration that all statutes and rules of law purporting to shift a burden of proof to the defendant operate to shift an evidential burden only, and by a repeal of the local legislation shifting the burden of proof where the police search vehicles.”

Perhaps few practitioners in Ghana would wholly agree with the learned professor on this issue!

SUBMISSION OF NO CASE IN CIVIL CASES

In a civil suit the defendant may make a submission of no case at the close of the plaintiff’s case as is done in criminal cases. But while in criminal cases the doctrine of election has no place, in civil matters such submission is fettered by the doctrine.

The doctrine is one of choice, and it enjoins that a defendant before making a submission of no case should state his position clearly to the court whether or not he would call evidence. If he formally announces that he will lead no evidence, a ruling or judgment may be given against him if his submission fails, just like a defendant who abandons a suit after the plaintiff has successfully proved his case. Where the defendant elects to call evidence, the judgment or ruling must be given only after he has put his side of the case across to the court. If he is not put to his election at all he can still give evidence as if no submission has been made.61

At least two considerations justify the requirement. In the first place the judge sitting alone, has to determine the facts as well as the law, and he ought not to be asked to express his opinion until the case is completed. Secondly, the parties may be put to extra expense if the judge ruled in favour of the submission before the evidence was complete, for if the Court of Appeal were to decide against his ruling, a new trial would be necessary so that the party who made the submission could call his evidence.

In a civil case with a jury neither of the above considerations applies. Accordingly, it has been held that the judge has a discretion in such cases, and he may rule on the submission without putting the party who makes it to his election whether to call evidence or not.

In Young v. Rank, Devlin J. (as he then was) reviewing the civil authorities on election, said, among other things62:

"Besides the distinction drawn in Alexander v. Rayson between cases tried by a judge alone and those tried by a jury, there is this further matter which I think of some importance: in a case tried by a judge alone the Court of Appeal has a complete power of rehearing, and if evidence for the defendant has not been taken and the court disagrees with the ruling of the trial judge it is, in effect, prevented from exercising its power of rehearing and has no alternative but to send the case back for re-trial, which will result in additional costs to the parties.  In cases of trial by jury, the Court of Appeal has no power of rehearing, and, if the verdict of the jury is set aside for any reason, the court has no power except to send it back for re-trial.  Accordingly, it appears to me that one of the advantages which flows from a submission of no case in a trial before a judge alone does not arise in the case of a trial by jury”.

As to the position in Ghana, we may refer to the case: Boakye v. Asamoah63 (a case of contract, indemnity and contribution) where Osei-Hwere J. discussed the doctrine of election, and held, as stated in the headnote, as follows64:

"(1) There were two grounds under which a defendant might submit that he had no case to answer, namely (a) accepting the plaintiff's evidence at its face value, no case had been established in law and (b) the evidence led for the plaintiff was so unsatisfactory or unreliable that the court should find that the burden of proof had not been discharged.  Where the submission was based on the former ground, the defendant would be bound by his election not to call further evidence and no new trial would be ordered where the ruling was upset on appeal. However, if it were based on the latter ground, the appellate court might, as in the instant case, decide the case without sending it back; but in other cases, it might find itself unable to come to a just conclusion on the findings of the court below; in which case the only course to be adopted in the interests of justice was to order a new trial even if the defendant had elected to stand on his submission.  At such new trial the defendant was not bound by his election and so might give evidence.

(2) The well-recognised general rule of practice was that defence counsel would not ordinarily be allowed to submit no case unless he told the judge that he intended to rely on the submission alone without calling evidence. If the judge or magistrate overruled the submission, judgment would then be entered for the plaintiffs. However, a submission of no case ipso facto did not mean that counsel would lose his right to call evidence if his submission failed, he would only lose the right if he definitely elected not to call evidence. The practice, however, amounted to no more than a direction to the judge to put counsel who desired to make a submission of no case to his election and to refuse to rule unless he elected to call no evidence. Consequently, if through oversight or misapprehension as to the nature of counsels argument, the judge did not put counsel to his election and counsel did not expressly or impliedly elect, counsel would be entitled to call his evidence just as if he had never made the submission. Since the defendant had not been put on his election, the trial magistrate was entirely wrong when by his ruling on the submission of no case, he denied to the defendant his right to call evidence.

(3) The said rule of practice was however not inflexible but discretionary; if it was likely to save the litigants expense, time and trouble, the judge should allow the defendant to make his submission unfettered by the condition that he should call no further evidence.”

In Tepretu v. Akpetey,65 a customary action for seduction, François J. (as he then was), sitting at the High Court, Ho held:

“(1) Where there is a submission of no case the practice is to require the defendant to elect whether to call evidence or not before a ruling is given. Generally, the whole of the evidence must be before the court before a ruling is given.  Where the defendant elects to call evidence the ruling must be given only after he has put his side of the case. Even if he is not put to his election he can still give evidence as if no submission has been made.

(2) Where the right of election has not been exercised it is unjust to dismiss a plaintiff’s action without hearing the defendant’s story. Since a prima facie case has been established in this particular case, the defendant should have been called upon to make his defence”.

It is submitted that the doctrine of election should be abolished in civil cases when a party wants to submit no case to answer. This should be so whether in a magistrate court or before a judge sitting alone or with a jury, so that the position becomes clear and simple in civil suits as in criminal cases where the doctrine is not applicable. If the defendant or a party finds that there is no prima facie evidence against him in law and on the facts, he may submit no case to answer unfettered.  If his submission is upheld that is the end of the suit; if he is overruled, the case continues and he can call evidence.  There are several grounds for the abolition of the doctrine, namely.

(i) That previous cases on the doctrine of election are complicating and sometimes uncertain.

(ii) That the doctrine may not be understood or properly applied by counsel or a party and such a slip or oversight may affect the justice of the case.

(iii) That the long procedure of cases going on appeal and re-trial being ordered in some cases may be curtailed.

(iv) The object of every trial is that justice may be done, and justice cannot be said to be done where a party in a suit is prevented from calling evidence because he promises on an application that lose or win he shall not call evidence.

(v) That recent cases show the tendency of judges to allow cases to go a full circle notwithstanding the election promise; for the bench, rightly indeed, are now more interested in hearing all the evidence to enable them to arrive at a just and fair decision.

CONCLUDING REMARKS

In both criminal and civil cases, it is submitted, there should be no fetters on submission of no case to answer; provided the circumstances demand it. Discretion of the judge, notwithstanding, one party should have the right to submit no case and the other the right to reply.  This dual right of parties should be allowed to operate whether the case is being tried summarily or on indictment and irrespective of the personalities involved.  The doctrine of election in civil cases should be abolished.  Where a court overrules a submission of no case it is desirable that reasons for doing so are given.  Although an appeal could be taken by a party or his counsel at this stage of the trial, it should be resorted to rarely and only where subsequent evidence by the defence could seriously be adverse to him.  However, the fact that a submission was overruled should be one of the grounds of appeal if the party feels the court did so wrongfully. Finally, counsel should be wary in making a submission of no case and should attempt it only where there is ample chance of success. It should not be routine; for where it fails, it weakens not only what might be a brilliant address at the end of the whole evidence but might subtly prejudice a party's case, particularly where it is being tried with a jury.
