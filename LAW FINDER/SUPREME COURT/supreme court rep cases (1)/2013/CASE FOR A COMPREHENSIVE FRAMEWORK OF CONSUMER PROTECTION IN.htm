CASE FOR A COMPREHENSIVE FRAMEWORK OF CONSUMER PROTECTION IN GHANA*  [1981 & 82] VOLS XII & XIV RGL 73—89

Kwaku Ansa-Asare*

 

INTRODUCTION

IN an earlier contribution,1 the present writer drew attention to the acute shortage of consumer goods which has led to high prices in the markets.  The current shortages have enabled traders to sell essential commodities at the non-controlled prices as can be evidenced from the prices of goods on the markets.  The fact that unfair trading practices still persist as is evidenced by high prices and arrests for hoarding and profiteering cases can only demonstrate that resort to law to curb rising prices has had only a marginal impact. The question is whether, and to what extent, the law can protect the consumer in current Ghanaian circumstances.

The law and practice of consumer protection in Ghana is hardly developed and may be said to be in an unsatisfactory state.  Its future is contingent upon the development of controlled trading in the country. Previous attempts to enhance consumer protection have been half-hearted, and it remains uncertain whether recent attempts to encourage "consumerism"2 will ensure a high level of consumer protection.

However, current market practices as well as the ignorance of the consuming and buying public regarding their rights under the Ghanaian private sales law necessitate immediate consideration of a comprehensive legislation dealing with the subject under discussion.  In what follows, the writer will draw attention to some of the areas in which the law and practice of consumer protection is said to be defective.

II PRICE CONTROL

The process of economic change in Ghana has brought a considerable increase in the proportion of people who now depend upon others in order to obtain their consumer requirements.  In other words, the market dependence of consumers has increased, and one of the problems that has arisen as a result has been that consumers have now come to stand in need of legal protection in connection with their consumer sale contracts.3  Especially is this so with regard to the legal regulation of prices.  It may be argued that unfair trading practices such as hoarding and profiteering have been in existence even before the termination of colonial rule.  As long ago as 1938, the shortage of some commodities such as sugar, flour and cutlasses led to high prices.  As noted by Dennis Austin4:

"The effect was made worse by a system of petty retail trade which exaggerated the rise. Thus the price of sugar in the main stores was now 8d. a 1b, . . . but when sold a cube by cube on the small wayside stalls it was 3s.; the price of imported tinned fish and meat, candles, matches, and cigarettes was similarly inflated by a local system of multiple retail trade."

The shortages enabled the market forces of supply and demand to have free play. The colonial administration resorted to the law to keep prices within limits. It passed the Control of Prices Regulations in 19495 which forbade traders to sell their wares at the non-controlled prices. Since then, successive governments have been using the law as the main mechanism to keep prices within limits.6 One regrettable result of the shortages has been that consumers willingly give away the protection that the law would otherwise accord them. Personal experience and life in, and among the Ghanaian people, reveal that traders sell consumer goods to known customers who would not report them to the law enforcement agents.  Again, personal experience and the number of cases which have come up for resolution in the courts provide a reliable support for the claim that the proportion of complainants who are prepared to enforce their rights is very small. This encourages traders to assume that they can sell their goods at exorbitant prices with impunity and get away with it.  Such an assumption on the part of dishonest traders does not only reduce the protection the law seeks to accord the consumer, but also the practice is unfair to the honest trader who endeavours to maintain high standards and has, at the same time, to compete with the dishonest traders operating in the consumer trade.  Accordingly, successive legislatures have resorted to the sanctions of the criminal law in its search for control.  First, the legislature's resort to the criminal law is to protect consumers by ensuring that they buy their consumer goods at the controlled prices. Secondly, the use of the criminal law ensures that the expensive and time-consuming process of discharging the dishonest trader is entrusted to public servants whose duty it is to enforce price control regulations. If the provisions of the criminal law can enable the consumer to obtain full compensation, it will be unnecessary for him to rely on the civil law remedies: they will be superfluous.  But above all, the resort to criminal sanctions is to squash unfair practices before they mushroom. Sad to say, this effect has not been achieved yet.

It is not the purpose of this article to discuss the provisions of the current Price Control Decree, 1974, in detail.  However, it is proposed to draw attention to its salient features.

In a memorandum accompanying the Decree, the National Redemption Council declared that it would ensure that essential goods are available for the consuming and buying public at moderate prices; and that it would not tolerate profiteering, hoarding, smuggling and speculation.  Its section 1 provided for the issue of price control orders. Under section 2, the penalty for contravening a price control order is a fine not exceeding ¢1,000 or imprisonment not exceeding five years or both.  Where the illegal profit exceeds twenty per centum of the controlled price, or where the offence relates to certain specified essential commodities, or where the offender has a previous conviction within the last two years, the offender is to be imprisoned without the option of a fine.  Section 3 gives instances of what may amount to an offence under section 2. These instances include selling or offering to sell above the controlled price, having goods and refusing to sell them at the controlled prices and any other circumstances showing intention or willingness to sell above the controlled price. Under section 4, the court which convicts the offender will forfeit not only the goods in respect of which the offence was committed (other than goods sold to the victim) but all similar goods in the possession or control of the offender. The court will also order the offender to pay to the complainant double the price which he charged that person for the goods. In default of immediate payment the offender would be imprisoned for a term not exceeding six months. Its section 5 provides thata market trader who is convicted of a price control offence shall automatically forfeit his or her market stall, and shall be banned from any  further trading in that market for a period of twelve months. This fair trading provision seeks to eliminate dishonest traders from the consumer sale business, thereby allowing the more law-abiding traders to operate.

Section 6 makes it an offence to hoard goods, and the prescribed punishment is imprisonment for a term of not less than three months and not exceeding five years without the option of a fine.  All goods concerned are to be forfeited.  Section 7 gives examples of situations amounting to hoarding in the context of the Decree.  These instances include keeping of goods in excess of one's immediate requirements, or in a manner likely to cause a shortage.7 Personal observation and life among the Ghanaian trading community demonstrate that very often a trader would refuse to treat on the sole ground that the owner of the goods exposed to the view of the consuming and buying public is not present.  Consequently, the Decree, by its section 7 makes this unfair trading practice an offence which can be punishable by mandatory imprisonment.  Section 8 gives power to authorised police officers in uniform to take possession of goods which a trader is unreasonably refraining from selling to a consumer-buyer who needs them. Such goods, if seized, may be sold to the public by any person authorised by the Inspector-General of Police or regional secretary, and the proceeds of sale are to be forfeited to the Republic unless the Inspector-General of Police or a regional secretary otherwise directs.  It is also an offence to smuggle out of Ghana goods specified in the schedule to the Decree.  Those include tinned foods, rice, sugar, milk, flour, soap, matchets, textiles.  The penalty is imprisonment for a term of not less than three months and not more than ten years without the option of a fine.  The goods concerned are to be automatically forfeited and the vehicle may also be forfeited.8 Where a juvenile under seventeen years does any act in contravention of section 1, his parent or guardian or the owner of the goods shall be deemed to have committed the offence. It will be no defence to say that the act of the juvenile was unauthorised.9 Section 12 provides that the trading, consuming and buying public are entitled to complain to any police officer of any contravention of a price control order or any instance of hoarding or smuggling. The law places on the police officer a statutory duty to investigate immediately and take steps to prosecute the offender. This provision seeks to squash high prices before they mushroom. If suppliers are reported and disciplined, it will scare other suppliers to mend their ways. Section 13 gives the usual related powers of entry, inspection, production of documents and seizure. Section 14 makes it an offence to obstruct or insult an inspector or fail to comply with his lawful requests.

It will have been noted that the Price Control Decree, 1974,10 sought not only to enable consumers and buyers to buy at the controlled prices, but also, to prevent dishonest traders from trading altogether. It was an ambitious penal legislation that purported to curtail unfair trading practices in the country. The question is whether or not the Decree has achieved its intended effects.  In other words, has the Decree been effective in its design to eliminate unfair trading practices and to enable the consuming and buying public to obtain their requirements at moderate prices?  In the words of Richard S. Tur11

"Effectiveness is no univocal concept but the following aspects are central: first, that conduct by and large conforms to that prescribed in the legislative rules; secondly, that in the event of conduct not conforming to such rules an appropriate sanction is visited on the wrongdoer or an appropriate remedy is available to whomsoever may be injured in his person or property thereby, or both; and thirdly, that the social objectives or purposes of the law are realised."

As has been said, if the Price Control Decree, 1974, can enable the consumer-buyer to obtain full compensation, it will be unnecessary for him to enforce his rights in the courts. Proceedings will be instituted on his behalf by the State.  The advantage of resort to the criminal law sanctions is that it not only removes some of the terrors of litigation such as pleading and the hearing in open court, for the litigant, but also, recovery of "compensation" will be automatic once the trader is convicted.  Interestingly, the recompense that the consumer-buyer would be entitled to is not the conventional compensation of monetary damages for loss incurred either as a result of misrepresentation or breach of the consumer sale.  Section 4 (b) of the Price Control Decree, 1974, provides;

"4. Where a person is convicted of an offence under section 2, the Court shall, in addition to any other punishment ordered under that section,—

(b) order the offender to pay forthwith to the person to whom he has sold goods above the controlled price, a sum equal to double the price which he charged that person, or in default of such payment to be imprisoned for a term not exceeding six months."

Clearly, a consumer who lodges a complaint of an improvident bargain will reap some benefit from the fact that the trader has sold above the controlled price: the consumer-buyer is entitled to double the sum of the price charged for the goods sold by the trader.  Thus, in essence, the Price Control Decree, 1974, enables the court to order a convicted trader to make the refund specified under section 4(b) of the Price Control Decree, 1974. More importantly, however, the section does not create a right to compensation. This is partly because, to borrow from a somewhat different context12:

"criminal offences are not created for the purpose of providing consumers with compensation, as is pointed out in the Review of the Trade Descriptions Act, 1968, paragraph 281: 'We believe that compensation is primarily a matter for the civil law, and that an award of compensation under the powers of the criminal Courts Act should be regarded as a windfall rather than a right which itself justified prosecution under the Act'."

The nature of the compensation under the Price Control Decree, 1974, is nothing more than a windfall to the consumer-buyer who complains about profiteering at his expense, and whose "assailant" is ordered by the court to refund the illegal profit, on conviction.  Thus, where the trader fails to refund the illegal profit to the consumer-buyer and goes to prison in default, the consumer is entirely without remedy. It even boggles the mind that a consumer-buyer who has contravened the price control order not to agree to buy above the controlled price or from a source other than a commercial house or designated supermarket should be "compensated" as specified under section 4(b). But it can be argued that in such a case since the consumer-buyer has availed himself of section 12 of the Price Control Decree, 1974, he only concluded the consumer sale to enable him lodge a complaint for the arrest and prosecution of the trader.

However, we cannot avoid the conclusion that this "windfall" is of little value to the consumer-buyer.  He does not need this. What he needs is a full legal protection such that he can obtain his consumer requirements at the controlled prices, as well as obtain redress in civil claims arising from supply of defective products. Above all, in current, Ghanaian circumstances, what the consumer really needs is a system whereby dishonest traders can be legally restrained from trading altogether unless and until they mend their ways. The provisions of the Price Control Decree, 1974, seek to achieve this as is evidenced by section 5(1),(2)(a)-(c) and (3). But by themselves, the provisions are not enough.  Personal observation and the current Ghanaian experience show that traders can always adopt new practices to sidestep the law. For instance, personal experience reveals that some traders fill up large enamel bowls with goods and cover them with garri to escape discovery. Others pretend that they sell crabs when in actual fact they have concealed pilchards in the bowls.13  It is, therefore, necessary for the law to find devices to curb such trade malpractices and unfair trading practices.

Thus, although the legislature's intention in resorting to the criminal law through the enactment of elaborate price control regulations has been to enable the consumer-buyer obtain his requirements at the controlled prices, clearly it has not proved to be so as is evidenced by the current prices in the local markets and small kiosks of petty traders from whom the majority of the consuming and buying public obtain their requirements.  Further, the Price Control Orders have not enabled the consumer-buyer to obtain compensation in the event of a breach of consumer sale because the Price Control Decree did not create a right to compensation.  Only a refund of a sum equal to the excess profit or double the price charged can be obtained, but even then only upon the conviction of the seller and by order of the court.

Clearly, therefore, although the criminal law sanctions, discussed so far, in theory, do a great deal to protect the consumer-buyer, they have not done enough to protect the consumer in practice, as the foregoing discussion has shown.

III CIVIL LAW

In addition to resort to the criminal law, there is also the civil law through the law of sale of goods and contract which attempts to assist the consumer-buyer in his consumer transactions with traders.  For instance, the Sale of Goods Act, 1962,14 imposes certain obligations15 on traders and other suppliers of goods. It further restricts attempts to exclude or cut down implied conditions as to quality and fitness.16 Furthermore, the civil law provides for remedies on breach of a consumer sale.17 Unfortunately, the Sale of Goods Act, 1962, was drafted before the explosion of the current problems which the consumer now  faces as a result of chronic shortages which have led to improvident bargains and supply of shoddy goods. The language of the Sale of Goods Act, 1962, regarding for instance, the condition of quality and fitness, fundamental obligation of seller and buyer as well as remedies of seller and buyer is more appropriate to a contract between two businessmen than to a  contract between a businessman and a consumer. This is most evident in the remedy the law seeks to provide the consumer where there has been a breach of either a condition or warranty on the part of the seller or supplier of goods. Under the present Ghanaian circumstances, the remedies which the consumer-buyer most wants are a right to have the defective goods replaced or repaired by the seller, but this is not provided for by the Sale of Goods Act, 1962.  In Ghana, as life among the trading community vividly shows, the policy to refund is a matter of business practice; there does not appear to be any legal right to have the goods repaired or replaced.  The language of section 49(1) purports to enable the consumer to get his money back in at least three cases specified in that section.  But this protection would seem to be in respect of bulk purchases—in fact, by a businessman, not a consumer. Even then, section 51(1) reduces this protection.  It provides, "The buyer may not reject goods which he has accepted." Section 52 then specifies three types of acceptance.  It provides:

"52. The buyer is deemed to have accepted the goods when—

(a) he intimates to the seller that he accepts them; or

(b) he does not within a reasonable time after delivery of the goods, inform  the seller that he rejects them; or

(c) he wrongfully refuses or neglects to place the goods at the disposal of the seller after notifying the seller that he rejects them."

This section raises a further unsettled point in our consumer law. Clearly, the section does not purport to deal with consumer sales which are the concern of this contribution.  The section is clearly dealing with bulk sales between businessmen and not for instance, between a consumer-buyer and a seller. Finally, although section 53 purports to enable the consumer-buyer to maintain an action for damages against the seller for non-delivery, this is yet another area which is of little importance to the consumer-buyer, the sole ground for this view is that the provision does not purport to deal with consumer contracts.  Section 54 bears out this assertion. It provides:

"54. (1) The measure of damages in an action under section 53 of this Act is the loss which could reasonably have been foreseen by the seller at the time when the contract was made as likely to result from his breach of contract.

(2) Where there is an available market for the goods in question the measure of damages is prima facie to be ascertained by the difference between the market or current price and the contract price . . ."

It is clear, therefore, that consumer sales between a consumer and a seller are not covered by the Sale of Goods Act, 1962.  In the result, the consumer's only option is to contact the trader for either a replacement or repair.  He will not tend to think of court proceedings because the cost of proceedings may exceed the amount in dispute.18  However, in the face of current Ghanaian circumstances of acute shortages, the consumer will not complain about the high prices at which he obtains his requirements, even though there is a provision that purports to enable him make a complaint to a price control official or the police.19

Another remedy which the law purports to provide is contained in section 5(1) of the Contracts Act, 1960.20 It provides:

"5. (1) Any provision in a contract made after the commencement of this Act which purports to confer a benefit on a person who is not a party to the contract, whether as a designated person or as a member of a class of persons, may, subject to the provisions of this Part, be enforced or relied upon by that person as though he were a party to the contract."

Unfortunately, the draftsman did not make his intention clearer. It can be argued that the provision purports to confer a benefit on consumers who cannot establish a privity relationship with the seller. But since this intention is not clearly expressed, it would appear that strict intention of a conferment of benefit will be insisted upon.  Professor Date-Bah has observed that the phrase " any provision " is used synonymously in the Act with the phrase "any term."21 He concludes that the Ghanaian Law "recognises a ius quaesitum tertio arising by way of contract, where there is an intention to confer a benefit on a third party.22  This observation would seem to take account of difficulties which occasionally arise in ascertaining whether a contract, for example, for a meal supplied at a restaurant, is made with a husband, or his wife, or both. This provision can effectively be of utility to the consuming and buying public if it is re-written to take account of consumers who would not be able to establish any privity relationship with the seller.

Thus, judged by the notion that the law would provide an appropriate remedy for an injured or aggrieved consumer, our contract law has not been effective in protecting the consuming and buying public.

IV THE COURTS

The statute book bears eloquent testimony that the law gives rights directly to the consumer but, "all too often he is powerless to enforce them."23

Ultimately, it is the courts, and only the courts, which can ensure observance of the legal rules by making certain that appropriate sanctions are visited on persistently dishonest traders, or by providing an appropriate remedy to whomsoever may be injured in his person.  Although it has been said that unfair trading practices and high prices still persist, the fact that some traders have been convicted demonstrates that the market does not always win.  The following illustrative cases which came before the Accra District Magistrate Court namely: Republic v. Asiedu,24 Republic v. Aduhene,25 Republic v. Ntim,26 Republic v Abena,27 Republic v. Boye,28 Republic v. Sowah,29 Republic v. Afenu30 and Republic v. Opoku31  in which the traders were either fined or sentenced to varying terms of imprisonment, show that, occasionally, an appropriate sanction is visited on persistent offenders against the price control law.  Thus in Republic v. Asiedu the accused was fined ¢100 or six months' imprisonment. She paid the fine.  She had failed to mark the price of mosquito coils which she sold contrary to section 2 (1) and (4) of the Price Control Regulations, 1976 (L.I. 1068). The accused in Republic v. Aduhene was fined ¢500 or in default six months' imprisonment.  She paid the fine.  She had increased the price of pepsi cola from 70p. to 80p. without the prior consent of the Prices and Incomes Board contrary to sections 1 (2) and 5 (1) of the Prices and Incomes Regulations, thus making an illegal profit of 10p.  In Republic v. Ntim, the accused increased the price of some goods without the prior consent in writing of the Prices and Incomes Board contrary to sections 1(2) and 5(1) of the Prices and Incomes Regulations, 1973 (L.I. 805). She paid the fine of ¢1,500 imposed by the court. Similarly, in Republic v. Abena, the court fined the traders ¢150 each or three months' imprisonment in default, for failing to mark the price on a set of pyrex bowls they were selling. Predictably, they paid the fine. So did the accused in Republic v. Boye, who was fined ¢300 or a day's imprisonment for increasing the prices of goods without the prior consent in writing of the Prices and Incomes Board.  The accused in Republic v. Sowah who sold a cake of guardian for ¢3 instead of 85p. was sentenced to a month's imprisonment.  In Republic v. Afenu, and in Republic v. Opoku, the accused persons both received a month's imprisonment sentence each for selling goods above the controlled price.  Both of them were, in addition, ordered to refund twice the illegal profit to the buyers, or in default two months' imprisonment. Although the cases cited show that the law seems to ensure that appropriate sanction is visited on offenders, yet they represent the tip of the iceberg.  In seeking to observe and enforce the observance of the price control laws and orders, the courts have not been active enough.  Rather they appear to be lenient with some of the traders. Some of the scales of punishment have not been a deterrent.  As was observed by the Abraham Commission32:

"indeed, they could be construed as an invitation to the offence. In any case, the fines imposed are so paltry that profiteers, completely undissuaded anticipate them in their price demands as additional occupational hazards."

The cases cited in which the court imposed fines on the wrongdoers illustrate our assertion.  We have already discussed a few of the reported cases that went on appeal in the high courts.33 These cases, except Acquah v. The Republic,34 are illustrative of the anti-consumer protection attitude of some of our judges.

It has already been stated that the decision in Acquah v. The Republic35 seems both technically correct and practically convenient. The reason for this view is that the decisions in the other cases noted36 would inhibit consumer-protection.  There is, therefore, clearly much to be said for the actual result arrived at in this case. It is obviously desirable that suppliers of consumer goods should sell according to the spirit of the existing price control laws.  There seems no good reason why their activities should not be checked by the courts.  On this note, it is useful to mention that the decisions in Adom v. The Republic,37 Republic v. Dufa38 and Osei v. The Republic39 must not  be followed. In general, these cases have shown that the courts have displayed no very favourable attitude towards the consumer. Some of the decisions have rather construed the protection away.  It will be hardly surprising if some consumers decide to abandon the courts. But there is still some hope for the consumer.  Certainly the last word has not been said.  And one may hazard the guess that the glare of public agitation and the threat or actual enactment of corrective legislation will have some effect of inducing our judges to modify their anti-consumer protection attitude.  To this extent, it can be asserted that the law has not vigorously ensured that appropriate sanction is visited on offenders.  Thus, it has not effectively protected the consuming and buying public.

On the other hand, the law has provided an appropriate remedy for consumers who have been injured as a result of using or consuming defective products. For instance, the plaintiffs in Aboagye v. Kumasi Brewery Limited40 and Acheampong v. Overseas Breweries Ltd.41 were awarded compensation for injuries caused them on consuming defective beer manufactured by the defendants. Although the illustrative cases are scanty, they amply demonstrate that in some cases the law has provided an appropriate tort-based remedy for injured consumers. Judged by this notion of effectiveness, the law has protected the consumer-buyer in some respects.

However, on net balance, the law has not effectively achieved its overall aim of ensuring that:

(a) the consumer-buyer bargains in the shadow of the law;

(b) the consumer-buyer obtains his requirements at the controlled price;

(c) the consumer-buyer gets the remedies of replacement, repair or refund for being supplied defective goods; or that

(d) the consumer-buyer obtains compensation where a repair, replacement or refund cannot be made.

These have been some of the areas in which the Ghanaian law and practice of consumer protection has been defective.

V THE FUTURE OF THE LAW OF CONSUMER PROTECTION IN GHANA

We have in the foregoing pages, considered the extent to which the law and practice of consumer-protection in Ghana is defective.  We have seen that, as can be evidenced by life among the Ghanaian trading public, as a result of the persistence of unfair trading practices, such as hoarding and profiteering, the resort to even the criminal law sanctions has not effectively protected the consumer. It is believed, nonetheless, that it would be desirable for the Ghanaian legislature to pass a comprehensive statute to guide the consuming and buying public in their daily confrontation with the market forces.

Recommended Legislation

As a first step in reform, policy-makers must bring some sanity into our private sales law. There remains a need to develop a comprehensive framework of consumer-protection which will eliminate the anomalies and inconsistencies which still exist in the spate of consumer-protection legislation in our statute book.  Legislation is called for to protect the varied interests of the consumer. Whether this new legislation should take the form of a comprehensive consumer-protection dealing with all the main issues discussed in this study, or whether there should be isolated statutes dealing with each issue separately, is not a question that should detain policy-makers unduly. There would be advantages in a comprehensive legislation, such as the reduction in cross-references to other statutes.  For instance, at present, there are isolated statutes, namely, Price Control Decree, 1974,42 Contracts Act, 1960,43 and Sale of Goods Act, 1962,44 which purport to deal with consumer transactions. On the other hand, the pressure of time on the legislature and the length of a comprehensive Bill would probably make it easier to pass piecemeal legislation.  Either way, the legal content would be similar.

What would our proposed "Consumer Protection Law" contain? First and foremost, it would contain provisions dealing with the definition of consumer goods, consumer sale and a consumer.  The form of the definitions should not be different from that adopted in an earlier article.45

Secondly, it would contain a rule that all consumer goods sold must be of good quality whether or not they were sold "by description or examined by the consumer." This calls for a further rule that would prohibit any limiting clause which would attempt to exclude this right given by the "Consumer Protection Law."

Furthermore, there would be a provision on remedies to the consumer if the goods are faulty.  An important provision that the Consumer Protection Law would contain is a remedy that if the goods cause damage, anyone who is injured by them should be entitled to a claim, even though he is not a party to the contract of sale.  In part, this would be based on section 5 (1) of the Contracts Act, 1960,46 but considerable clarity could be achieved by improving upon the wording of the existing law.  It could be in this form47:

"1. One who sells or supplies any consumer product in a defective condition which causes injury or damage to the user or consumer or to his property is subject to liability for physical harm thereby caused to the ultimate user or consumer, or to his property if:

(a) the seller is engaged in the business of selling such consumer product; and

(b) it is expected to and does reach the user or consumer without substantial change in the condition in which it is sold.

2. The rule stated in subsection (1) applies although:

(a) the seller has exercised all possible care in the preparation and sale of the product; and

(b) the user or consumer has not bought the product from or entered into any contractual relation with the seller."

The real purpose of such a provision is to make it easier for everybody who uses or consumes the goods purchased by the consumer or buyer to secure compensation.

Again, there could be a provision that if the goods are faulty and thus worthless than they should be, the buyer ought to get it replaced or repaired or a cash refund. It should be emphasised that the consumer or buyer is not interested in getting damages but in getting the goods that he wants and in avoiding the risk of injury.  Thus, if the seller is unable to do this, then the consumer or buyer ought to have a right of action or opportunity, to claim compensation.

Again, the law could contain provisions on what to do with bad goods which have already reached the market or are in daily use.  The new provision would ensure that such goods are not only withdrawn, but also, destroyed.

We have seen that a major cause of consumer weakness in the present law lies in the inadequate enforcement of the many laws in the consumer or buyer's favour.  The price control statutes and orders as well as the law on quality contained in the Standards Decree, 1973,48 all operate through the medium of the criminal law.  The individual consumer need not launch a court case and no initiative is needed beyond a complaint of a contravention of a price control order or a standards rule49 to the police. But life in Ghana among Ghanaians, shows that most of the personnel of the price control squad and the Ghana Standards Board are passive in the sense that they are not active in inspecting and searching for practices which might offend against the law.  This attitude may in part explain the present weakness in the enforcement of protection enacted in these statutes and orders. Policy-makers must, therefore, find an alternative enforcement machinery to ferret out consumer complaints and grievances.  In other words, there is need for an agency that will play an active role in consumer affairs.

We suggest, as a reform measure, the establishment of a Consumer Ombudsman such as the one in Sweden.  Such an officer must be a lawyer. His supporting staff must include police officers, price inspectors and standards personnel.  Among other responsibilities, these consumer officials must intervene in improvident bargains and hear consumer complaints.  If this idea is implemented, it will put in place full-time officers whose sole responsibility will be to initiate or bring to light consumer complaints.  In addition, these officers can seek voluntary compliance by educating the selling community on the need to comply with consumer-protection legislation.  They can also issue legally binding "cease and desist orders" against dishonest traders.50 Our known experiences indicate that it is only if a consumer agency takes an active role in seeking out improvident and harsh consumer bargains that the consumer will be able to voice an effective complaint.  In turn, this agency is likely to be a watchdog of consumer interests.  Any information to be collected, too, by the agency will provide more data on consumer problem solving.  This is likely to provide a basis for a systematic surveillance of the market-place.

Another area which needs a recasting is the courts.  Since consumer disputes in this country involve small claims, we need to provide a machinery different in procedure from other varieties of civil litigation. We suggest the creation of "market courts" that will be active, such as the type in Sweden.

The real purpose of such a provision is the expeditious and inexpensive resolution of many of those consumer grievances that now fall outside the practical ambit of the ordinary courts.  Afterall, few consumers are willing to litigate their disputes in the ordinary courts. As has been stated already, a factor which affects a consumer in ventilating his grievances is financial costs. Thus, if market courts are established, the law would in particular, discourage or prohibit the use of legal representation.  In addition, our proposed law would seek to simplify court procedure by the introduction of a less formal and speedier method of adjudication. Since most consumers will not want to go to court even if the legal impediments are removed; because they will pay costs if they lose,  our law would contain a provision that where, for instance, the claim will not exceed ¢200, no costs should be recoverable by either party (except the cost of issuing the summons). The purpose of this proposal lies in the fact that it will reduce the possible risk that an unsuccessful small claimant would have to pay the other side's costs, as well as encourage more litigation, although it would also mean that the small claimant's victory could be pyrrhic if the costs are more than the value of the consumer sale.  Such a court, should not wait till cases come to them.  Provisions should be made to enable their officials ferret out offences against the law for resolution in the market courts. This means that our law would contain provisions that would give the usual related powers of purchase, entry and seizure, testing and prosecution to the consumer ombudsman and his staff in aggregate as well as officials of the market courts. Afterall "The enjoyment of traditional as well as new social rights presupposes mechanisms for their effective protection.  Such protection, moreover, is best assured by a workable remedy within the framework of the judicial system."51

Again, our law would contain a provision that would require the consumer ombudsman to prohibit persistently dishonest traders from trading altogether. Our known experience bears eloquent testimony to the fact that unfair practices including selling above the controlled price, refusing to sell, hoarding and displaying goods but refusing to sell because "the owner is not present" persist. Our law would, thus, contain a provision to specify such practices as unfair trading which would be a ground for the consumer ombudsman to take an action in the market court.

Publicity

We have seen that both the consuming and buying public do not bargain in the shadow of the law due to their ignorance of the private sales law. "It is not enough to introduce legislation and codes of practice to bolster the consumer in his perpetual confrontation with the business world. Such rights are useless unless he knows of them and can exercise them.52 Our law would thus contain a provision empowering the consumer ombudsman to publish such information and advice in asserting the interest of the consuming and buying public.  Information of the type in question would inform the consumer and seller of their respective rights and obligations in respect of goods. It would also advise the consumer of his position after he has been given a raw deal.

It should be conceded, however, that legislation is not an omnipotent remedy for social evils. Under the current Ghanaian circumstances, the mere passage of law would not automatically produce the results intended.

Thus, in addition to recasting our private sales and price control laws, Ghanaians should, as a people, alter what is bad; instead of demanding a law to alter it.
