BOOK REVIEW: THE DISCIPLINE OF LAW [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 237—239

 

by The Rt. Hon. Lord Denning M.R. [London: Butterworths & Co. Ltd. 1979 xxii and 331 pp.  Price: paperback £5.95 net; hard cover £9.95 net.]

OSEW E. A.

FOR the past five years Lord Denning has speedily enriched English legal literature by writing five important and interesting books. These are: The Discipline of Law (1979); The Due Process of Law (1980); The Family Story (1981); What Next in the Law (1982); and The Closing Chapter (1983).

The Discipline of Law, the subject-matter of this Review, was written to mark the 80th birthday of the Master of the Rolls (as he then was). The theme of the book, according to him, is:

". . . that the principles of law laid down by the Judges in the 19th century—however suited to the social conditions of the time—are not suited to the social necessities and social opinion of the 20th century. They should be moulded and shaped to meet the needs and opinion of today."

It may be rightly said that, to a large extent, he, despite odds and even opposition from his colleagues, has succeeded in moulding a considerable branch of the law to suit the social and economic needs of our century.

The book is treated under seven main topics. Part One deals with the construction of documents; Part Two, with misuse of ministerial powers; Part Three, locus standi; Part Four, abuse of group powers; Part Five, the High Trees case; Part Six, negligence; and Part Seven, the doctrine of precedent.

Though not against precedent as such, Lord Denning is highly opposed to its irrational and unfair application. He would not, therefore, stay at the House of Lords where conservatism was highly in vogue. On the other hand, he would prefer to remain in the Court of Appeal where there was some chance of doing good. No wonder that in the first and last cases in which he took part as Lord of Appeal in Ordinary (1957-62) he dissented.

But for his courageous decisions and famous dissents which earned him the acolade of an iconoclast judge, such celebrated cases decided by the House would not have reached there. Among them are: Candler v. Crane Christmas & Co. [1951] 1 All E.R. 426, C.A. on negligent statements; Bonsor v. Musicians' Union [1954] 1 All E.R. 822, C.A. on trade union disputes; Conway v. Rimmer [1967] 2 All E.R. 1260, C.A. about Crown privilege; Padfield v. Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food [1968] A.C. 997, C.A. about ministerial discretion; and Schorsch Meier GmbH v. Hennin [1975] 1 All E.R. 152, C.A. about judgments in foreign currency.

In dealing with the construction of documents in Part One, Lord Denning is inclined to the method or approach by the "intention seekers" who go by the purpose or intent of the makers of the document as opposed to the "strict constructionists" who stick often to the traditional rule. In the construction of contracts, he favours the "presumed intent" as opposed to the traditional "implied term."

On ministerial powers (Part Two), he deals with statutory and other claims by ministers of State, government departments, tribunals, etc., that seek to protect their actions under "ouster clauses" and special privileges. All these must act within the law. And so, in Pearlman v. Keepers and Governors of Harrow School [1978] 3 W.L.R. 736, he held that "ouster clause" apart, the courts could by means of certiorari set aside any determination of a tribunal which has erred in law. Also, in Laker's case [1977] 2 All E.R. 182, he rejected the claim of a minister that he had a "prerogative power" to prevent the courts from looking into a case of a private man undertaking the business of airways.

Locus standi is the topic for Part Three. The main consideration here, according to Lord Denning, is the "interest" of the applicant in the particular case. Thus in Blackburn v. Attorney-General [1971] 2 All E.R. 1380 he upheld his application for a locus standi, even though his main action seeking to restrain Britain’s entry into the Common Market did not succeed.

Part Four deals with the abuse of group powers. This is twofold: Powers against the group's own members and against outsiders. Here also his opinion finds favour with the Labour Relations Act, 1974.

This Review would be incomplete if the popular topic of students—The High Trees case—is omitted. Lord Denning in this book, clarifies the principle by referring to his judgment in Combe v. Combe [1951] 2 K.B. 215, C.A. where at p. 220 he said:

“Seeing that the principle [in the High Trees case] never stands alone as giving a cause of action in itself, it can never do away with the necessity of consideration when that is an essential part of the cause of action. The doctrine of consideration is too firmly fixed to be overthrown by a side-wind. Its ill-effects have been largely mitigated of late, but it still remains a cardinal necessity of the formation of a contract, though not of its modification or discharge.”

On the law of negligence discussed in Part Six, the Master of the Rolls traces its history and comments that this is the area of the law which twentieth century judges have done well to develop most. Donoghue v. Stevenson apart, Lord Denning has also, in his own way, done well in extending the principle to negligent statements in his famous decision in Candler's case, which was approved by the House of Lords in Hedley Byrne & Co. Ltd. v. Heller & Partners Ltd. [1964] A.C. 465.

Apart from the main theme of law reform, The Discipline of Law has other attractions. The language and style in which the story is clothed is worth noting. In simple, clear and colloquial language, the great judge is able to state the law in the way that the layman can understand. In his staccato and laconic style, he succeeds in telling the world his views of English law as it was before his days, during his time, and, as it should be in the future.

Humour comes next. It is not only what to say that matters but how to say it. Lord Denning is aware of this and in his book he expresses humour in writing as he does in speech.

It will be of interest to know that the barrister's privilege of not being liable for negligence in the conduct of a case is protected by Lord Denning in his decision in Rondel v. Worsley [1969] 1 A.C. 191 which was affirmed by the House of Lords. And, lawyers are advised by him to give great thought and study to the use of language which at p. 5 he describes as "the tools of trade." And at p. 8, he warns counsel when addressing the court in these words:

"Remember also that, whatever the tribunal, you must give a good impression. Your appearance means a lot. Dress neatly, not slovenly. Be well-groomed. Your voice must be pleasing, not harsh or discordant. Pitch it so that all can hear without strain. Pronounce your consonants. Do not slur your words. Speak not too fast nor yet too slow. All these things are commonplace but they are so often forgotten that I warn you against the mistakes I see made daily. No hands in pocket. It shows slovenliness. No fidgeting with pencil or with gown. It shows nervousness. No whispering with neighbours. It shows lack of respect. No 'ers' or 'ums.' It shows that you are slow-thinking, not knowing what to say next. Avoid mannerisms like the plague. It detracts attention. Don't be dull. Don't repeat yourself too often. Don't be long-winded. All these lose you your hearers; and once you have lost them, you are done for. You can never get them back—not so as to get them to listen attentively."

Some critics have these comments about The Discipline of Law. The Guardian observes, "His reputation is founded on a rare combination of intellectual brilliance, a vivid style of speaking and writing and unquenchable zest for reforming the law.  His book reflects these qualities.”  And, Lord Hailsham describes the book as “an ebullient, readable and vigorous defence of his own judicial career . . .”

For the law student, the academic lawyer, the practitioner and the judge, The Discipline of Law should have several attractions.  Even the layman may find it fascinating.
