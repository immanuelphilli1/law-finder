FAMILY LANDS AS "STOOL LANDS" [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 187—190

WOODMAN GORDON R.

 

A relatively clear issue arose in Okwan v. Amankwa,1 concerning the statutory meaning of "stool land." If the same dispute arose in contemporary circumstances it could raise a more difficult issue.

It may help to propose partial definitions of the terms in issue.  Statutory definitions apart, it is suggested that a "public stool" is a stool which represents a community or an office within a community consisting of more than one family; a "private stool" or "family stool" represents a single family or an office within a single family; and a "stool family" is a single family from which by custom the occupant of a particular public stool is selected.

The plaintiff sued in November 1979 for accounts of moneys collected by the defendants from lands of a stool family.  The defendants raised a preliminary objection to the plaintiff's capacity to sue, relying on the Administration of Lands Act, 1962 (Act 123), s. 17.  This provides  that all revenue (which is widely defined) "from lands subject to this Act" shall be collected by the minister.  Section 31, the interpretation section, provides, as far as is relevant, that "'Stool land' includes land controlled by any person for the benefit of the subjects or members of a stool, clan, company or community, as the case may be ."  The court decided that, at least as far as was relevant to the present case, "lands subject to this Act" meant "stool land" as defined.2

The plaintiff argued in reply that land of a stool family was family land, not "stool land."  The court upheld the plaintiff's argument and overruled the objection, relying on a number of cases which had distinguished between family stool and public stools, and which had held statutory provisions regarding "stools" to be inapplicable to family stools.3 The point seems well-established on these authorities.  Moreover, as the court pointed out, it was unlikely that the legislature had intended the State to control the budgets of stool families, an action which it described as "ridiculous."

The 1962 provision was amended in 1969.  The Constitution, 1969, art. 164(2), pointed that all payments from "stool land" were to be paid into a stool lands account in respect of the stool concerned.  Disbursements from the account were to be as determined by the regional council, or, on appeal, the Lands Commission.  Article 172(1) provided:

"'Stool land' includes any land or interest in, or right over, any land controlled by a Stool or Skin, the head of a particular community or the captain of a company, for the benefit of the subjects of that Stool or the members of that community or company."

The 1969 provision remained the law until 1979.4 The Constitution, 1979, art. 190(2) provided that all payments from "stool land" were to be paid to the Office of the Administrator of Stool Lands.  Article 213(1) provided:

" 'stool land' includes any land or interest in or right over, any land controlled by a stool, the head of the particular community, or a family for the benefit of subjects of that stool or the members of that community or family."

(The emphasis is mine.) "Stool" was defined consistently with this as including, among others, persons having control over family land.5 The Lands Commission Act, 1980 (Act 401), s. 4(2) re-enacted article 190(2).  It did not repeat the new definition of "stool land," but presumably was to be read with reference thereto.

These provisions were all repealed by the Provisional National Defence Council (Establishment) Proclamation (Supplementary and Consequential Provisions) Law, 1982 (P.N.D.C.L. 42), ss. 65 and 66. Section 48 (1) provides for payments from stool lands to be paid to the Administrator of Stool Lands.  The definition section, section 63 (1) provides:

" 'stool land' includes any land or interest in or right over, any land controlled by a stool, the head of the particular community, including a family as known to customary law for the benefit of the subjects of that stool or the members of that community.

"Stool" again has a consistent definition.

What would be the result of the objection raised in Okwan v. Amankwa if it referred to payments due after the commencement of the Constitution, 1979?  A case can be made for the view that all family land in the ordinary sense is now "stool land" for the purpose of statutory controls on stool land.  The consequences would be very far-reaching.  The statutory controls affect not only the collection of payments in respect of stool land, but also the making of assurances of stool land.6  Moreover, it is likely that stool lands as defined are "public lands," and so have been vested in the P.N.D.C. "on behalf of and in trust for the people of Ghana and their communities," and are subject to various other controls.7  This would be a startling development to have occurred without express recognition, through the medium of a definition section.  Its results would be more extensive than the alleged results described as "ridiculous" in Okwan v. Amankwa.

It may be reasonable to argue that, since the legislation makes provisions for stool lands, not "stool and family lands" it cannot have been intended to include all family lands within stool lands.  The contrary view would involve artificiality in the use of the term "stool," and would produce impractical results. Therefore, just as "stool" has been held to mean "public stool," so "family" now must be restricted to some sort of "family community."

It may be surmised that "family" was inserted in the legislation from 1979 onwards as a replacement for the word "clan," which was thought unsatisfactory, and to ensure that control was extended to communal lands analogous to stool and skin lands in those areas which did not have the institutions of stools or skins.  On this argument, "family lands" are within the definition only when the "family" consists of more than one "family" in the usual sense of the term as used in areas where stools exist. This usual sense may be taken to be limited to groups in which the precise kinship relationships between members can be traced, as distinct from those where members are known to be related but where precise links have been forgotten.  A better distinction than this may be found, but provided such distinction can be defended, the current law can be interpreted in terms not very different from the earlier law.  Its precise scope may need to be worked out in cases argued in terms of the circumstances in particular land-holding systems.  The new definition has set a difficult task.
