ENGLISH TECHNICALITIES IN A GHANAIAN SETTLEMENT OF LAND [1981-82] VOLS. XIII & XIV RGL 183—187

Woodman Gordon R.

 

TESTATORS frequently wish to leave interests in land not in separate portions to individuals but to a group of relatives together. There are various ways of achieving this result.  Property may be devised to a customary-law family as family property, in which case customary law determines the membership of the group and the mode of administration of the property. Such a devise may be qualified by express provisions ousting the customary law on particular issues, such as provisions which specify the membership of the family, or the person who is to be head for the purpose of this property, or which vary the normal rules of management.  Yet another possibility is to employ the rules of common law to produce the desired result, as by providing that there shall be a trust, a joint tenancy, a tenancy in common or a succession of estates (a settlement). However, it is unusual to grant an entail, either in possession or as a future interest, because this estate embodies the peculiarly English notion of family enjoyment, whereby only one individual in each generation, determined by male primogeniture, enjoys the property to the exclusion of all others. The novel forms of words used by testators for these purposes have given rise to problems of interpretation in a line of cases.1 The latest is Arbenser v. Hesse.2

The testator died in 1942, leaving property "unto and to the use of my daughters and to their heirs being issues of their respective bodies as tenants in common," with a gift over in the event of the failure of such heirs. The plaintiff, an issue of the body of one of the daughters (specifically, her son), sued the daughters (including his mother) for a declaration that the devise had passed immediate interests to the daughters and the heirs of their bodies as tenants in common in equal shares.

The court held, following English authorities, that where a will used technical legal terms, it must be presumed that the testator intended them to carry their technical meaning. It is to be expected that such terms will normally appear as a result of the will being drafted by a professional lawyer. The presumption seems to assume (reasonably, it is submitted) that the lawyer will have used the appropriate technical language to give effect to the client's instructions.

The court next dealt with the relatively minor point of the significance of the words "unto and to the use of." It was pointed out that they had once been necessary in voluntary deeds of conveyance. A voluntary grant simply "to X" would have given rise to a resulting use in favour of the grantor, which the Statute of Uses, 1535, would have "executed", that is, would have converted into a legal estate of the same type as the grantor's equitable estate, and thus rendering the grant of no effect. The words, "unto and to the use of" prevented a resulting trust from arising, and it became the practice to include them for safety in all conveyances, even those for valuable consideration. As the court pointed out further, the Statute of Uses, 1535, ceased to apply in Ghana on the enactment of the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372), which also brought into force in Ghana the English Law of Property Act, 1925, s.60 (3), which removed the need to use words to prevent a resulting trust from arising.3  It was concluded that the words ensured that the devisees took both legal and beneficial interests. However, it might also be suggested, with respect, that the words were never necessary in wills. The doctrines of resulting trusts have never applied to wills, which, because they always consist entirely of gratuitous grants, would have suffered extensive and devastating consequences if they had been subject to the doctrines.

The most difficult question was the effect of the remaining quoted words. The court cited the analogous English cases. They are few, and they do not seem to settle all aspects of the question. It seems likely that English lawyers have generally avoided using this form of words because it was realised that their effect was uncertain. Wilkinson v. Spearman4  is very briefly summarised in the reports. It appears that, in construing a devise to the testator's two daughters and the heirs of their bodies, the court held that the daughters took a joint estate for life, and that there were "several inheritances" on the death of the survivor; but during the lifetime of the survivor she was solely entitled by virtue of her right to survivorship.5  In Doe (Dec'd.) Littlewood v. Green,6  there was a devise of the testator's two nieces "as joint tenants, and their several and respective heirs and assigns for ever." It was held that the court could give due effect to the words by giving the nieces a joint tenancy for their joint lives and the life of the survivor with a remainder in fee simple to them as tenants in common in equal shares. In Forest v. Whiteway7 the devise was "to Martha and Jane, to hold to them, their heirs and assigns, for ever; but in case the said Martha and Jane shall both die without issue, then" there was to be a gift over to others. The words following the semi-colon indicated that Martha and Jane were not to have the fee simple absolute.  The court held that they must be intended to take as joint tenants, and stated that it would follow Wilkinson v. Spearman and Doe (Dec'd.) Littlewood v. Green.  However, the editorial note summarises the decision as holding that, on the death of the survivor, Jane, without issue, the entire property passed to Martha's issue to the exclusion of Jane's devisees.  This entails that the remainder was granted to the issue of Martha and Jane, whereas if Doe (Dec'd.) Littlewood v. Green had been followed it would have belonged to Martha and Jane and so Jane's share would have passed to her devisees.  Wilkinson v. Spearman, as far as the record goes, does not seem clear on this point.

From these authorities two propositions seem to follow without difficulty.  First, such words pass two distinct gifts.  Secondly, the gift to the named devisees vests in possession first, and the other is a remainder which vests in possession on the expiry of the prior gift.  These were sufficient to dispose of the plaintiff's claim in Arbenser v. Hesse.

So long as daughters survived, the gift "and to their heirs etc." had not vested in possession. It also seemed clear that the gift to the daughters was of a joint tenancy, since at the time of the will this was presumed in the absence of words of severance indicating that a tenancy in common was intended. (This rule has since been reversed by Conveyancing Decree, 1973 (N.R.C.D. 175), s.14(3).)

A further, more difficult question could arise; what exactly is the nature of the remainder? The judgment in Arbenser v. Hesse at one place follows Doe (Dec'd.) Littlewood v. Green in holding that this was a remainder to the daughters as tenants in common. Naturally, on this view they held an interest which would never vest in possession in them, because it was postponed to their life estate. However, it would be vested in interest during their lifetimes, and each would therefore be competent to dispose of her share in this future interest either inter vivos or by will. At another place the judgment says that the remainder was to the heirs of the bodies of the daughters, that is, to the daughters' lineal descendants living at the death of the last surviving daughter. This follows the editorial summary of Forest v. Whiteway. It would not permit the daughters to deal with the remainder. The two English cases seem irreconcilable.

It seems possible that the view expressed in Doe (Dec'd.) Littlewood v, Green was determined by the rule in Shelley's case, although the rule was not expressly referred to. By this rule a grant to a person (or persons) for life followed by a remainder to the heirs or the heirs of the body of that person passed to that person both a life estate and a remainder in fee simple or fee tail.8 Frequently, the life estate and the remainder merged, giving the person a fee simple or fee tail in possession, but this did not occur if it would have had the effect of changing the nature of the rights acquired in any way. The circumstances in the present class of cases were in the latter category, and it is noteworthy that it is the very instance given as an example of non-merger under the rule in Shelly's case by  Challis9:  

"A limitation to two men and the heirs of their two bodies begotten, gives them a joint estate for their lives, with benefit of survivorship to the survivor, and to each an estate tail in a moiety; and there is no merger of the estate for life in the estates tail."

The reason for excluding merger was that, to enable the right of survivorship (intrinsic to joint tenancy) to operate, neither of the remainders could be permitted to vest in possession until the survivor had died. The remainder of the first man to die could therefore not vest in possession on the termination of his prior right of enjoyment, and therefore could not merge with it. It is unclear why the rule in Shelley's case was not applied in Forest v. Whiteway.

If the argument just put is correct, it would seem that the rule was not applicable in Arbenser v. Hesse. It was admittedly part of Ghanaian law at the time of the will.10  However, it applied to a will only when the will evinced on intention to benefit all the heirs or heirs of the body. A Ghanaian testator being unlikely to desire to create an entail, the court considered that the intention of the instant testator had been to make a gift to those issues of each daughter living on her death in fee simple as tenants in common.  The rule in Shelley's case being inapplicable, that part of the judgment which follows Forest v. Whiteway is to be preferred.
