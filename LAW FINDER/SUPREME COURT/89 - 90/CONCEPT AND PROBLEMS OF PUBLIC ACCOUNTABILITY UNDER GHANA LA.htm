CONCEPT AND PROBLEMS OF PUBLIC ACCOUNTABILITY UNDER GHANA LAW [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 70— 94

*Hon. Taylor Justice JNK

 

INTRODUCTION

Public accountability is a universal concept, but the problems that arise in the course of its application in particular jurisdictions do stamp it with unique characteristics.  In the treatment of this subject we wish to stress that our contribution necessarily deals with the concept of public accountability as it impinges on phases of Ghana's constitutional history, touching pre-colonial times, the colonial era and the independence years. The independence years cover our experiences of spasmodic epochs or periods of constitutional rules and military regimes. I dare say there are experiences which we share in common with many of our West African brethren.

In all the periods of our constitutional history from pre-colonial days to modern times, concepts of public accountability dictated by unstable ideological norms and very subtle cultural values all of which are difficult to insolate pervaded the body-politic.  An attempt to grasp the significance and the consequential problems posed by these norms and cultural values is the burden of our contribution.  Public accountability as a concept is a principle which embraces the examination of the conduct of individuals, institutions and indeed even of the acts and omissions of the administrative and executive authorities1 of the state in cases of complaints that their conducts, acts or omissions have not been in accordance with the law.  The purpose of the examination is to subject improper2 and indeed unlawful conduct or activities to some form of sanctions or corrective measures.  These sanctions or measures are devices calculated to neutralise the further undesirable effort of their offending activities,3 or to force violators of the legal order to reverse their actions and make reparation for the loss or damage if any, which their illegal conduct has caused.4  The principle necessarily involves accountability in public life and under the law and consequently the judiciary as the acknowledged mouth-piece of the state with the traditional responsibility in an appropriate case of pronouncing on the legality of the conduct of every member of the society, is the umpire and a key figure in the concept of public accountability.  The judiciary is encouraged and structured to fulfil this role, by a recourse to a system of hierarchical courts armed with procedural machinery devised for the purpose.5

It is of some importance to note that the doctrine of public accountability, as a dynamic concept is a universal phenomenon. It is indeed an alternative way of looking at the problem of the ordering of society by means of law. It raises questions which are synonymous with the perennial problem of the rule of law as every state in the world sees it.  The ideal, I suppose, is attained if every member of society no matter his status in the political or constitutional set up in the country is publicly accountable in all his actions on the premise of complying with a priori criteria specifically provided to superintend all actions under the law. As an ideal concept it eschews anarchy and extols peace, harmony, law and order.

In its essence public accountability is attained in the utilisation of the judicial process by employing the services of judges and magistrates sitting in courts to discipline the entire population and other organisations as well as state institutions.  In the nature of things because of some inherent and limiting factors inhibiting total reliance on the judicial process, not all activities in the state are subject to the superintending powers of the judiciary.  In other words not all activities in the state are justiciable. The implication of this doctrine of non-justiciability of some issues by recourse to judicial power, is in effect a repudiation of a general and all embracing principle of public accountability in all areas of public activity.  In the circumstances, the areas which are exceptions to the concept deserve to be closely examined for they are the constant source of embarrassment and confrontation between the estates of the realm, particularly between the executive and the judiciary. Any attempt to grasp the significance and the consequential problems posed by the cultural norms and values that shape the concept is the burden of our contribution.

Factors Delimiting Public Accountability

One of the factors delimiting the power of the judiciary to call in question all activities in the state without exception is the constitutional understanding that draws a sharp distinction between the political role of the legislature and the due process requirement of the judicial system.

Our concept of the proper role of the judiciary was articulated in Ghana recently on 15 August 1979 when the court observed in Tinieye v The Republic that:

"If a legislative body is engaged in oppressive legislation it is no concern of the courts.  Any judge who finds it abhorrent to give effect to legislation which he considers obnoxious had his remedy by resigning.  The remedy of the people is to change the legislative body; but as long as the body is the institution in which the legislative powers of the state have come to rest, then its Decrees, Acts or enactments are entitled to be given full legal force by the courts."6

The position in Ghana is that in the absence of constitutional prescription legislators engaged in legislation which the people consider to be wrong are completely immune from the judicial process and therefore legally outside the purview of the coercive apparatus of public accountability.  And it is in this light that one can view the survival of the Preventive Detention Act, 1958 before the 1966 coup as well as probably, the unpopular decision of the erstwhile Supreme Court in Re Akoto.7

Re Akoto, is a case in which the judiciary was called upon to review the discretion given to the executive by the said Preventive Detention Act, 1958 (Act 17) to detain persons without trial.  In that case, the applicant invoked the jurisdiction of the court by way of habeas corpus and the court held that the English Habeas Corpus Act of 1816 was "a statute of general application" and therefore applicable in Ghana. Under that Act, the courts had power to inquire into the truth of the facts deposed to in a formal answer to a writ of habeas corpus.  The executive however, claimed in this case that it need not satisfy the doctrine of public accountability by submitting the facts upon which the detention was based to judicial scrutiny in accordance with the prescription of the said 1816 Act.  It merely baldly claimed that the detention was necessary to prevent the applicants from "acting in a manner prejudicial to the security of the state."  The court although not bound by English authorities rested its decision on the English war time cases under the famous Regulation 18B(1) exemplified in Liversidge v Anderson.8

This decision was indeed regrettable, for the English courts and the Privy Council would seem to have restricted the Liversidge v Anderson ratio to war time cases involving state security and the ratio does seem to be of limited application as subsequent cases in Britain amply demonstrate.9 It is paradoxically an ironic vindication of the doctrine of public accountability that the executive which with the acquiescence of the judiciary repudiated the doctrine, pilotted through our Parliament a Habeas Corpus Act which in unequivocal terms reinstated the principle of public accountability and in effect overruled and rejected the Re Akoto ratio.10

HISTORICAL PERSPECTIVE

(a) Pre-Colonial Era

This was a period in the constitutional history of our people, when the individual or group able to organise or wield naked force, controlled the lives of men within the jurisdictional area where the sword of the said individual or group held sway.  The wielders of the sword were publicly accountable to none but themselves. Our ancestors in those far off days were pawns in a political configuration and permutation which they hardly grasped. Our past is not unique, for almost every country has passed through this phase.  History, it is said, repeats itself; and the history and events of the past three decades in Ghana have such frightful resemblance to our pre-colonial past as is revealed by the writings of Reindorf in his History of the Gold Coast and Asante that we must respect it; indeed the pre-colonial period was truly an era in the political and social evolution of our people when as a general proposition it would seem might was right and right was might.  No doubt the ethical and moral norms which pervaded and dictated their conduct were determined in the said period of our forefathers by subtle and enigmatic forces.  The nature and the modus operandi of these forces do not lend themselves easily to any precise and accurate formulation and articulation at this distance of time mainly because the factual foundations for their exposition has been blurred and lost to us in the dim and uncertain history of our dismal past.

A close study of Reverend Reindorf's researches as are disclosed in his said work does demonstrate that the concept of public accountability as we have it now was then in no way known; it was however in a fragile stage and was dictated by our heterogeneous customary laws.

(b) The Colonial Era

The next stage is the development of public accountability in the colonial era and a useful starting point is 6 March 1844, on which date the principle of public accountability in an amalgamated form was unequivocally accepted formally by many of our chiefs on the premise that "the first objects of law are the protection of individuals and of property."11

On this date the first hesitant step was taken of laying the foundation for the fundamental principle of the accountability concept in our criminal law reflected in the latin maxim: nullum crimen sine lege, meaning nothing is a crime unless made so by law.  And so on that date with the consent of a number of the chiefs of our tribes,  it was decided that:

"Murders, robberies and other crimes and offences will be tried and inquired of before the Queen's judicial officers and the chiefs of the District moulding the customs of the country to the general principles of British law."12

Thus our various tribal laws were subjected to British ideas and out of the hotchpotch was to stem ultimately the common law of Ghana.13  The first formally reported cases of the application of the concept of public accountability following the formal acceptance of the Bond of 1844 by a very important segment of our coastal peoples is in a case on 30 January 1845 in which an accused person was subjected to the modern concept of public accountability, when he was found guilty of committing rape on a small girl.  He was ordered to pay a fine of eight dollars as satisfaction and to be imprisoned for one month; he was also to pay one dollar costs.14  Before this case, however, Governor Maclean had exercised jurisdiction of an undefined nature in many matters and had clearly laid the foundation for the modern doctrine of public accountability, for on 1 February 1830 two natives of the land: Mr De Graft and Mr Sam were imprisoned by Governor Maclean15 in exercise of a jurisdiction the precise nature of which as well as the constitutional foundation for which was totally unclear. 

The great constitutional question as to whether the jurisdiction of traditional authorities to vindicate their brand of public accountability have been superseded completely by the jurisdiction of the court system established by the Supreme Court Ordinance, 1876 and subsequent legislation surfaced in the years that followed the enactment of the said Supreme Court Ordinance. The statutory courts established by the Ordinance made praiseworthy effort to solve the question by recourse to the common law principle of interpretation.16  It is, however, doubtful whether the question was ever satisfactorily settled for it seems the problem still tasks our present superior appellate courts.17

One significant feature of the colonial era is the purposeful thrust of the concept of public accountability. It became a product of British colonial policy and it was fashioned and calculated to advance the metropolitan economic interests of Britain. The precolonial concept of public accountability as was protected by the heterogeneous customary laws changed its character. From this period onwards, till the end of colonialism the customary laws of Ghana in their pristine character were denied the attributes of true laws. They ceased to be yardsticks without more, for operating the concept of public accountability. They only qualified to be considered as laws for that purpose after they had been repeatedly proffered to the courts by evidence and they had been subjected by the courts to a test as to whether they were compatible with, or repugnant to natural justice, equity and good conscience.18

This imperialist approach to the public accountability concept continued until independence on 6 March 1957 and lingered on for a few more years until the Republican era began on 1 July 1960 following the promulgation of the First Republican Constitution. On the coming into force of the said constitution the imperialist device of assessing the legal validity of the indigenous customary laws by the yardsticks of natural justice, equity and good conscience was constitutionally repudiated and the customary laws of the various tribal communities became part of the laws of the country and as of now the only test for their validity in contemporary Ghana is whether they fulfil those conditions which under the general law of Ghana must be fulfilled by all customary laws.  All the customary laws of the various tribal communities are therefore part of the laws of Ghana and accordingly are now no more subject to the imperialist repugnancy provisions of natural justice, equity and good conscience.19

(c) The Independence Years

The next stage in the narrative of the constitutional history of the concept of public accountability is the independence years. This period was characterised by concepts of constitutionalism which oscillated between three apparently irreconcilable theories of adjudication. The first being the willingness to entertain the doctrine of the total sovereignty of Parliament, a sovereignty developed by Britain and articulated by William Blackstone. It was founded on the premise of a supremacy so transcendent and absolute as to be unlimited in time and scope and which consequently cannot be confined either to persons or causes within any bounds.20 A Ghanaian critic of this concept of legislative supremacy reflecting general Ghanaian attitude to this alien ideology has said that: "legislative omnipotence is a logical abstraction and ought not to be applied to practical issues."21  Probably in the fullness of time the Ghanaian electorate will repudiate this frightening dogma so foreign to our traditional values and so subversive of the concept of public accountability.  For us it seems this theory is nothing but a prescription for revolutions. The second theory is the supremacy of the Constitution confining all donees of power within constitutional boundary as was expounded by Chief Justice Marshall in Marbury v Madison22 as representing the cornerstone of American constitutional doctrine.  The convulsion posed by this dichotomy in the Ghanaian legal order was fostered by incessant imposition of military regimes on the body-politic necessitating structural adjustments in the constitutional order and inhibiting smooth functioning of the judicial process devoid of dilemmas.  In the wake of military regimes a third theory usually surfaces.

The third theory can be seen as vying uneasily to impress itself on Ghanaian constitutional practice. It has so far been precariously confined to vindicating a concept of public accountability in the domain of the criminal law.  It is clearly predicated on the premise of official suspicions that the judiciary, functioning traditionally as an independent estate of the realm, does not operate some areas of the criminal law to the satisfaction of the executive.  Its relentless dogma is that the criminal justice system must be operated under the supervision of the executive and in the paramount interest of the greatest number of the citizens even if in the process individual rights suffer.23 This novel constitutional theory which is frowned upon by Ghanaian jurists, seems to have sprung from the Soviet Union following the famous October Revolution.  It has been styled "Socialist legality" and it is gradually feeling its way into our law through a chain of public tribunals projected to operate throughout the country dispensing justice in vindication of its brand of public accountability in selected areas of our criminal law.24

The reality of the amorphous constitutional order in the Republican era is reflected in the results of court decisions depending as they have to on the constitutional theory that informs the decisions.  As a random illustration, it is quite clear that the decision on 8 July 1968 of Anterkyi J in Director of Prisons; Ex parte Salifa25 a was animated by the Marbury v Madison ratio and of course, on orthodox application of appropriate legal criteria it can perhaps hardly be faulted.  In that case by the Proclamation, 1966, which established the NLC it was provided specifically that decrees of the court shall be numbered and published in the Gazette. Salifa was detained on the strength of a document which was neither numbered nor published in the Gazette and Anterkyi J sitting at the High Court, decided on an application for habeas corpus that the document had failed to satisfy the provisions which made decrees valid and as the document was therefore not a decree, Salifa was ordered to be set free. On his re-arrest on the same facts on the strength of a similar document neither published in the Gazette nor numbered, Charles Crabbe J held on 15 July 1968 in Republic v Director of Special Branch; Ex parte Salifa25b at the Accra High Court that the document was a decree and refused to set Salifa free.  There is little doubt that Charles Crabbe J must have been influenced by the idea that the Blackstonian doctrine of absolute parliamentary sovereignty highlighted by the positivism of John Austin was applicable to the case, having regard to the view he took of the then subsisting constitutional structures set up by the Proclamation, 1966.

It is doubtful whether a firm constitutional system with a clear unequivocal mandate as to the constitutional theory which the judiciary should apply in resolving issues affecting the liberty of the subject and the expectations of the citizens has been either projected or clearly achieved in Ghana. As the Salifa cases illustrate, in the absence of a discoverable theory pervading the application of legal criteria to adjudication, the logical application of the concept of public accountability will be unpredictable.  As Oliver Wendell Holmes the famous American jurist so aptly put it:

"Theory is the most important part of the dogma of the law, as the architect is the most important man who takes part in the building of a house.26

The problem is neither semantic nor academic. The soul writhing-experience of our legislature in modern times as well as the Salifa cases bear testimony that this is quite a serious practical problem. At all times when people have adopted a constitution, they would seem to prefer to limit legislative sovereignty by constitutional provisions. The judiciary however does not seem to be structured after revolutionary military take overs along any ideological lines to enable it to accept the challenge and legally react to the novelty posed by such revolutions. The pity of it all is that the structural adjustments that follow such military coups are moulded with the past performance and good faith of the judiciary so discredited, that the new order cast dark shadows on the potential capacity of the judiciary to function effectively in a military regime to advance the military concept of public accountability.

A few words on the dramatis personae in the area of the scenes depicted by the public accountability doctrine can throw considerable light on the true nature of the concept. These players as I have already noted, are the citizens, the legislature, the executive, the judiciary, the security forces and the administrative class.

THE EXECUTIVE AND ADMINISTRATORS

Under the common law system of justice as we have inherited it in Ghana,27 the law is no respecter of persons. As the English constitutional theorist, Dicey put it, "the idea of legal equality" is "[t]he universal subjection of all classes to one law administered by the ordinary courts." This view in effect means in his words that: "every official . . . is under the same responsibility for every act done without legal justification as any other citizen."28

It is, however, in our opinion not very realistic at least in Ghana to equate the "ordinary citizens" responsibilities for their actions with the liabilities of members of the executive for actions they take in their executive capacities as Dicey would seem to have unwittingly done. Our experience in Ghana in the years following independence does falsify that equation because the executive in our country is in a really very strong position in the governmental power structure. Dicey's rapturous pronouncement therefore does not seem to apply to our constitutional experiments from independence to the present time; it failed to draw the necessary distinction between public accountability of executive acts under the law as our courts have applied it since independence and the public accountability of the ordinary citizen during the same period.  With humorous cynicism the average Ghanaian sees the alleged executive equality as more equal than the citizens.  It is the type of equality that defies mathematical equations and tips the scale on the side of the executive.

The control for instance which our Attorney-General as a member of the executive exercises in his discretion over criminal prosecutions is immune from the public accountability concept, whether its exercise is an abuse of the discretion or not.29  However, the special position which the Republic occupies in litigation when it accepts the vicarious responsibility in law for the acts of its officials is highlighted by the nature of the statutory immunities and reliefs which it enjoys.30  Thus under the State Proceedings Act, 1961 (Act 51) officials acting in their official capacities would seem to be at times capable of avoiding the sanctions imposed on ordinary citizens by the public accountability concept.  Furthermore, the executive are at times prepared to exploit and do indeed deliberately exploit all possible constitutional means to escape public accountability.  This tendency of the executive to avoid public accountability is a psychological reaction to the prevailing norm in our society by which authority enjoys general adulation.  The effect of this is that any appearance of subjection to law for the purpose of public accountability through the supervisory powers of the courts is viewed by some segments of the general public as an erosion of the powers of the executive authorities. Naturally the executive authorities resent this public tendency of apparently denigrating their powers and they do everything in their power to neutralise it.  They brace themselves to show where "power lies."

An illuminating illustration of this syndrome is afforded by the celebrated case of Balogun v Edusei.31 On Friday, 17 October 1958, Mr Krobo Edusei, the then Minister of the Interior issued deportation orders against Wahabi Balogun and three other men and authorised the Acting Commissioner of Police, Mr Madjitey, to arrest them without warrant and deport them to Nigeria. Accordingly, on Saturday, 18 October 1958 the four men were arrested. On Monday, 20 October 1958, at 8.15 a.m. counsel filed an ex parte notice that the court would be moved for a writ of habeas corpus on the ground that the four men were Ghanaians and therefore not subject to deportation and also by a writ of certiorari to quash the deportation orders.

On hearing counsel, the court directed that notice of the motion be given to the Minister, the Acting Commissioner of Police and the Director of Prisons; and 30 October was fixed for the adjourned hearing. The Minister of the Interior, Mr Krobo Edusei, was informed by the counsel for the applicants and by the Attorney-General's office of the proceedings in the court.  His reaction was that he would wait for the notices of the court order to be served on him, but that he would deport the four persons forthwith and in the afternoon of the same day he made special arrangements and deported the four persons, before the notices were served on him on 31 October 1958.

On 11 November 1958 counsel for the four men filed notice of motion of an application that the Minister of the Interior and the Acting Commissioner of Police be committed for contempt of court.  On 22 December 1958, although the application for certiorari was dismissed, as regards the application for attachment and committal for contempt, the court held firmly that the Minister of the Interior, Mr Krobo Edusei and the Acting Commissioner of Police, Mr Madjitey, were guilty of contempt.  Committal was accordingly ordered but execution was stayed until 9 a.m. on 24 December 1958. On that date before 9 a.m. Parliament held an emergency session and passed into law the Deportation (Indemnity) Act,32 which received the royal assent before the court sat.  By section 2 of the said Act, Krobo Edusei and Madjitey were:

"indemnified from all penalties for contempt of Court and exonerated from all penalties in respect of any action taken by them in carrying out the deportation order ... after the institution by the persons named in those orders of proceedings by way of Habeas Corpus."

Smith J the presiding High Court Judge had no option but to give effect to the legislative fiat and therefore he made no order, but in the best tradition of the judicial function he remonstrated:

"By the passing of this Act, I take it that the Court's finding that the respondents are in contempt is not challenged by Parliament, but that the intention is to neutralise any consequential order that I might make.  It is plain that Parliament prefers that the respondents should not apologise and it has passed that Act in order to nullify any order which I might make in the absence of the apology.  The Courts of Justice exist to fulfil not to destroy the law and it would not make sense for me to record an order which is incapable of being carried out.

As to the deportations, while the applications for habeas corpus was still sub judice I cannot over-emphasise the undesirability of interference by the Executive with the functions of the Court.  Persistent indulgence in such a practice could not have any other than the most serious ill-effect on the well-being of the country. Decisions of a Court are as binding upon the Executive as the Laws which Parliament passes are binding upon the ordinary citizen and it is the Court that enforces upon the people obedience to these laws thereby, aiding Parliament in the ordering of the country."33

The case is a striking instance of the generally well-known executive predilection in Ghana to avoid the judicial application of the concept of public accountability to executive actions.  Other instances since independence can be seen in the aftermath of the famous case of Sallah v Attorney-General34 and in the 1958 case of Larden v Attorney-General (No 2).35  The case of State v Otchere36 which was declared null and void by an Executive Instrument37 is an illuminating example of executive predisposition against public accountability.  It is quite apposite as portraying the extent to which the executive was prepared to go to circumvent the judicial process to achieve improper aims and objectives contrary to acceptable traditional legal criteria. Like most statements of general propositions there are exceptions to this executive tendency and in two cases: Tuffour v Attorney-General38 and Bilson v Apaloo,39 the executive of the Third Republic accepted with good grace and admirable tolerance the decisions of the final appellate court given against the stand which they had taken in the two cases.

This can be contrasted with the shrill displeasure of the executive of the Second Republic in the Sallah case and their radio and television protestation that no Court would persuade them to accept such unfavourable judicial decisions with equanimity. Their indignant reaction was followed by their lack of interest as the executive organ of the state, to cause the security forces to put before the Supreme Appellate Court, persons whose presence, the court had requested for them to show cause why they should not be committed for contempt in perjuring themselves in the Sallah case.

Theoretically, however, as against the executive and administrative organs of the state the mechanism for ensuring public accountability in all cases where these organs exercise adjudicating functions is the supervisory jurisdiction of the Supreme Court and the High Court by means of orders, directions or writs including writs or orders in the nature of habeas corpus, certiorari, mandamus, prohibition and quo warranto as the Supreme Court or High Court considers appropriate for the purpose of securing the enforcement of its supervisory powers.40 Another procedure for securing public accountability by questioning executive and administrative actions is the action for a declaratory judgment41 thus the 1957 Larden case42 was in substance an action for a declaratory judgment. The citizen if injured by the action of a state official or a person exercising traditional or statutory authority is in theory not debarred from taking a writ in vindication of his rights guaranteed by the public accountability concept, and this procedure has been followed times without number since the establishment of our Supreme Court in 1876.43

It is clear that executive and administrative actions can only be harmoniously subjected to the sanctions of the public accountability concept if the executive authorities eschew arrogance and self-importance and are prepared to have confidence in the good faith of the judicial disposition which was articulated by Smith J in the Balogun case that the judiciary is poised to fulfil the law and not to destroy it.  This ideal prerequisite for public accountability can however only be realised if the judges and magistrates also succeed in inspiring the development of a culture of respect for law, by the rational and moral foundations on which they rest their decisions.

In this respect it is worth remarking that the Ghana judiciary has not been insensitive to the responsibilities of the executive. The executive and the people, however, are entitled to know the considerations which dominate the approach of the judiciary in ensuring public accountability, and in State v GOC Ghana Army44 in what must be regarded as the locus classicus, the Full Bench of the Court of Appeal stated the applicable principle when the Chief Justice said:

"The court in the execution of its duty to protect the citizen's liberty always proceeds on the well-known principle, at any rate as acknowledged in democratic countries, of the primary necessity in the administration of the law to establish a healthy balance between the need to protect the community against crime and the need to protect individual citizens against abuse of executive power. Subject to the limits imposed on this twofold protection by the establishment and maintenance of the requisite balance, the scales are to be held evenly, at any rate in normal times, between the community that is the state and the individual and there can be no question of "leaning over backwards" so to speak, to favour the state at the expense of the citizen or to favour the citizen at the expense of the community.  And the court's vigilance in protecting the citizen against any encroachment on his liberty by the executive becomes meaningful and real only when pursued on the basis of this principle.

Naturally any extension of the powers of the executive that tends to impinge upon the liberty of the citizen is viewed with the greatest suspicion, for such extended powers are in their operation fraught with serious dangers."

THE LEGISLATURE AND THE JUDICIARY

The role of the legislature to enact laws is the exercise of political discretion and as a general proposition it rarely brings to the public accountability concept in regard to the contents of its legislation.  Where, however, the constitutional theory that informs its legislative acts is anchored on the Blackstonian concept of legislative supremacy, then strictly the enactments of the legislature are not subject to public accountability and are completely outside the pale of judicial supervision.45  It is where the legislative power is derived from a constitution which mandatorily sets limits to its exercise that the exercise of the said power is subject to public accountability and becomes justiciable to test its compliance with the constitutional mandate in the tradition exemplified in the Marbury v Madison ratio (supra) of the American Supreme Court.  As Marshall CJ of the American Supreme Court succinctly put it in that case: "a law repugnant to the constitution is void."46 Thus in Ware v Ofori-Atta,47 Murphy J sitting in the High Court in Kumasi came to the conclusion that the Statute Law (Amendment) (No 2) Act, 1957 passed by Parliament soon after independence and which received the Royal Assent on 30 December 195748 did not comply with the constitutional mandate contained in the provisions of section 35 of the Ghana Independence Constitution49 and he therefore held that the Act was invalid.50

It does happen at times that legislatures purporting to exercise unbridled legislative powers have inadvertently passed laws inconsistent with other laws already on the statute book.  On the principle of the Latin maxim - lex posterior derogat priori, the last enactment in time prevails over the first and the courts in the exercise of their judicial power have invariably refused to give effect to the first in time as Murphy J had occasion to do in Tsiboe v Kumasi Municipal Council.51

The relationship between the judiciary and the legislature with regard to the application of the concept of public accountability by the judiciary to proceedings in Parliament, has been considered by the Court of Appeal, sitting as Supreme Court, in the celebrated case of Tuffour v Attorney-General.52  In that case the plaintiff, a university lecturer sought a declaration, inter alia, against the Speaker of Parliament that the application of the procedure in article 127(1) of the Constitution, 1979, to the Chief Justice and his purported vetting and rejection as Chief Justice by Parliament were in contravention of the Constitution. The Speaker was therefore a nominal defendant, Parliament being the state organ intended to be reached by the suit.  Article 127(1) provides:

"The Chief Justice and the other Justices of the Supreme Court shall be appointed by the President by warrant under his hand and the Presidential seal,

(a) in the case of the Chief Justice, acting in consultation with the Judicial Council;

(b) in the case of the other Justices of the Supreme Court, acting on the advice of the Judicial .

(b) in the case of the other Justices of the Supreme Court, acting on the advice of the Judicial Council, and with the approval of Parliament."

The Chief Justice of the pre-1979 Constitution was vetted and rejected by Parliament as Chief Justice under the Constitution, 1979, and declaratory action was instituted by Mr Tuffour, the university lecturer for a judicial decision that the President and Parliament were amenable to public accountability for their actions.53  The President for submitting the name of the Chief Justice for approval and Parliament for instituting the vetting procedure to enable it to approve or disapprove of the President's nomination. The court unanimously stated that there were two important principles governing the relationship that subsisted or should exist between Parliament and the courts:

"(a) that the court can call in question a decision of Parliament; but that the courts cannot seek to extend their writs into what hapens in Parliament; and

(b) that the law and custom of Parliament is a distinct body of law and as constitutional experts do put it "unknown to the courts'."

It affirmed that "the courts do not and cannot inquire into how Parliament went about its business" and concluded that, "the courts cannot therefore inquire into the legality or illegality of what happened in Parliament" and "[i]n so far as Parliament has acted by virtue of the powers conferred on it" by the Constitution, "its actions within Parliament are a closed book."  The Speaker was accordingly on this reasoning, struck off the action by the Court of Appeal on ground of non-justiciability and consequential absence of public accountability.54  However, for stated reasons, it reversed the rejection by Parliament.55

Of course the holding in effect that Parliament has control of its domestic proceedings and that such proceedings are non-justiciable is quite consistent with the common law approach to the problem. Lord Coleridge CJ in Bradlaugh v Gossett56 attempted to explain the rational foundation for this position in a case not too divergent from the Tuffour case, he said:

"If injustice has been done, [by the House of Commons to its members] it is injustice for which the Courts of law afford no remedy . . . The history of England, and the resolutions of the House of Commons itself, shew that now and then injustice has been done by the House to individual members of it. But the remedy, if remedy it be, lies not in actions in the courts of law . . ."

Under a constitutional democracy however, which has mapped out the limits of parliamentary powers, it is doubtful whether the doctrine of non-justiciability and non-accountability as is illustrated in the views of Lord Coleridge in the Bradlaugh case can survive, if the courts come to a conclusion that Parliament has indeed perpetrated an injustice on a citizen.  It is in this light that the final decision of the Court of Appeal in the Tuffour case57 can be viewed, for the resounding foundation of the decision was articulated by Sowah JSC, as he then was, in these memorable words:

"The ideals which the framers of the Constitution were at pains by the letter and spirit of this Constitution to establish ought to be respected and adhered to. They are justice and fairplay; abhorrence of arbitrariness and discrimination; victimisation and vindictiveness; the protection of the individual and his fundamental human right, within the walls of the Constitution."

Many a time in the constitutional history of Ghana the legislature itself either by tradition or by statutory or constitutional provision has had the modalities for the exercise of legislative power and therefore the governing principle for public accountability of legislative acts spelt out. In such a case failure to keep within the parameters thus provided may invalidate a statute. This is quite consistent with the English case of Bowles v Bank of England58 where the English court held that a resolution of one House of Parliament could not amount to law, because one House acting alone did not constitute the Parliament recognised by the English courts. Thus in our own local case of Mekkaoui v Minister of Internal Affairs,59 a statute, the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council Decree, No.42 was declared invalid by the Supreme Court for its failure to meet  the yardstick for validity expressly provided in the Armed Forces  Revolutionary Council Proclamation, 1981.

Perhaps the gravest problem perilously approaching confrontation which arises in the judiciary-legislature relationship is the  phenomenon of statutory provisions specifically and explicitly purporting to take away the jurisdiction of the courts to enforce  the concept of public accountability. One of the earliest instance of the problem occurred in Tamakloe v Mitchell60 and it involved the construction of section 50 of the Supreme Court Ordinance, 1876 [since repealed], which afforded statutory protection to district commissioners in the performance of their duties.  The section is  in the following words:

"No action shall be brought against any Commissioner in respect of any act or order bona fide performed or made by him in the execution or supposed execution of the powers or jurisdiction vested in him."

Purporting to shelter under this provision the defendant district commissioner had the plaintiff, Tamakloe "arrested and imprisoned for what the commissioner thought was disrespect to him as the Queen's representative in the manner in which he saluted him in a street." At the divisional court in Accra, Sir Joseph Hutchinson  CJ, held that section 50 and indeed the "supposed execution" provision in the section did not protect the district commissioner or insulate him from public accountability for his action and he was mulcted in damages for false imprisonment.  This case can be contrasted  with the case of Kwakye v Attorney-General61 where a contrary conclusion was arrived at by the Supreme Court on the peculiar provision of  the transitional provision of the Constitution, 1979 ousting the jurisdiction of the court.62  It is often said that the judiciary is the least powerful estate in the realm. The legislature controls the finances of the Republic and the executive has the coercive forces of the state at its disposal. The court has neither the purse nor the sword and for this reason, the conclusion, a non sequitur, is drawn that it is the weakest in the governmental power structure.63  I venture to think that this view is a misconception.

The judicial function is predicated on an adjudicatory process  which rests on rationally time tested foundation. The judge is enjoined  to anchor every single decision he makes, even discretionary ones,  on articulated reasons which are open to public examination. And  the existence of a hierarchy of appellate tribunals to subject judicial  reasoning to further scrutiny elevates the judicial process to a level at which the legislature and the executive can never hope to compete seeing that their decisions are rooted on numerous, subtle and divergent considerations which defy rationalisation at all times. Habitual  adherence to the judicial tradition restrains the judges from giving way to tendentious popular views.64 These popular and unexamined views may be momentarily overwhelming but resisting them on rational grounds demonstrates ultimately the truth in the oft-quoted aphorism that the pen is mightier than the sword.

The Citizen and the Public Accountability

A smooth functioning of the public accountability concept demands of the citizens a certain level of awareness and a reasonable sense of their duties and responsibilities. This evolves either slowly or can only be achieved by devising for the purpose a form of public education. The matter is really very grave because living conditions which were the order of the day a few decades ago have been displaced by new living conditions which the money economy of the colonial power has imposed on us.  These have brought to us now living standards which we can only maintain by abandoning our old methods of working for a living.  We must now, to survive, resort to revolutionary means of earning a living consistent with emerging new norms which are foreign to our traditions.  And yet the preparatory arrangements and education which should be made to prepare us as citizens by ensuring a smooth transition from our old ways to the new roles we are to play have been neglected by both the imperial power and the indigenous regimes.

The result is that many of our citizens have been confused and a class of people ill-equipped to contribute to national development is fast emerging in our society.  Their unmistakable identity as a class is the badge of poverty which they openly display in all our towns and villages.  Even those who are supposed to be well-off have to resort to questionable means to earn a living.  The scarcity of employment opportunities drive many an honest man to criminal activities and the response of executive authority to vindicate public accountability is the application of stringent sanctions of the criminal justice apparatus.  Realism and pragmatism have deserted us.

When a hardworking honest man is able to earn from all legitimate endeavours only ¢5,000 a month, but his pressing essential minimum commitment is ¢12,000 a month, it is surely stretching credibility to unacceptable level to believe that if he is a custodian of ¢1,000,000 of public money, he may not succumb to the temptation to help himself to some of this public fund to escape his embarrassment and fulfil his pressing obligations. Surely, if his sick son needs vital medical attention for a fee in order to save his life and the man is unable to obtain it lawfully by way of loan or his salary or other lawful employment, will a criminal justice system with a stringent concept of public accountability persuade him to ignore the threat to his son's life?  Is it not quite clear that such a person will be prepared to suffer the sanctions and humiliations imposed by our concept of public accountability under such circumstances rather than to see the life of his son ebb away? How does the concept of public accountability under the criminal law deter such a person and be a rational apparatus for desirable social purposes?

When persons obstinately and with reckless disregard for consequences continue to engage in criminal activities in open defiance of law, then the prevailing concept of public accountability has broken down and become irrelevant and extravagant. The problem then becomes one of an intelligent inquiry as to the reasons for the persistent conduct.  The enquiry must be directed to find out whether what moves the culprits to engage in proscribed conduct is stronger than the natural desire to abide by the beneficent precept of public accountability.65

It seems obvious that moral and ethical principles notwithstanding if hard work in lawful employment does not give the average citizen access to the where withall to meet his essential obligations, then his natural inclination, fuelled by his commonsense will be to defy the principle of public accountability.  He will end up being a parasite on the economy and devise strategies by which he can subvert the economy to enrich himself.66 He will easily find other citizens sympathetic to his plight and legislation that operates the public accountability concept with a blind eye to this social problem merely promotes oppression, creates disgruntled citizenry and unwittingly encourages revolution.

Disgruntled citizens are unhappy elements in the society and their productive capacity is minimal.  Their activities undermine national development and harsh criminal legislation calculated to promote public accountability in them becomes quite meaningless if the law does not address itself to the cause of their being disillusioned. Public accountability operates smoothly as I have already indicated in an atmosphere where the citizen is aware and indeed appreciates and gladly accepts the level of his duties and responsibilities as are mapped out explicitly or implicitly by the state.  Punishment in vindication of public accountability must bear relationship to proscribed conduct and be humane enough to consider all the circumstances that have led to the infraction. Certain forms of punishment are by civilized standards known to be cruel and unusual and insensitive to the humanity that separates us from the animal kingdom.

The imposition, for instance, of the death penalty in vindication of public accountability is a defeatist procedure; it is nothing but a bewildering confession by the authorities that they have a problem for which they have neither the intellectual capacity nor the inclination to devise a solution.  We have had the death penalty with us for countless generations and have never succeeded in getting it to advance any social goal.

In these days when the moral and ethical foundation of society is so much enlightened, the death penalty is an anachronism and is incapable of enhancing the public accountability concept. Public infliction of punishment, cruel measures, harsh sentences and exorbitant fines in vindication of public accountability are strategic sanctions which the criminal justice system in other climes and jurisdictions have needlessly operated to their sorrow and under the influence of benign and enlightened social changes have abandoned for reasons that can hardly be faulted. As a distinguished American jurist Mr Justice Frankfurter put it, such strategies are nothing but "foolish and harsh manifestations of coercion."67

When young and impressionable minds are witnesses to public executions and degrading punishments meted out to persons suspected or convicted of crime, their respect for the sanctity of human life and the dignity of the human person must necessarily suffer.68 Such young and immature persons destined ultimately to administer the criminal justice system in justification of the beneficial effect of the public accountability concept will surely be poor material for fulfilling this ideal purpose. They will on the contrary, cause suffering and unhappiness and let loose on the society citizens disgruntled and bitter; tools for revolutions; habingers of perpetual convulsions in the body-politic and therefore potential danger to the peace tranquility and welfare of the people.
