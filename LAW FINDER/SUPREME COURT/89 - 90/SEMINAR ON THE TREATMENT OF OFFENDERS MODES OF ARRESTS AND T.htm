SEMINAR ON THE TREATMENT OF OFFENDERS: MODES OF ARRESTS AND TREATMENT OF COMPLAINANTS AND SUSPECTS UNDER OUR LAW [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 1173—179

Alhaji Nuhhu Billa

 

*

Arrest

Whenever one talks of arrest, it is the policeman who comes to one's mind. Arrests are associated with policemen but not all arrests are made by policemen. What constitutes an arrest in police jargon is the taking or restraining a person in order that he or she may be available to answer an alleged or suspected offence or crime. The term arrest was defined by a judge in a leading case, Christie v Leachinsky [1947] AC 573, as the beginning of imprisonment. It normally leads to detention which may be prolonged. Arrest involves depriving a person of his liberty and his right to move about freely. It has been defined in legal textbooks as: the total restraint of the liberty of another, whether by constraining him or compelling him to go to a particular place, or by confining him in a particular place, or by detaining him in a public place.

The purpose of arrest is to bring an arrested person before the court, or to prevent him from committing a crime, or in some cases, to remove a person suffering from mental disorder or in need of care and protection to a place of safety.

Section 1 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30) states that "All offences under the Criminal Code and, subject to the provisions of any enactment, all other offences shall be enquired into, tried and otherwise dealt with according to this Code." It is the function of the police to enquire into criminal offences. This function enables the police to interview witnesses who are connected with crimes, and suspects who may also be arrested and interrogated in connection with offences. One may further ask to know from where the police derive their authority to arrest offenders.  These powers are contained in the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30), s 10(1) and (2). It is the duty of all members of the police service to make themselves familiar with their powers of arrest which is governed by the Criminal Procedure Code.

Generally speaking, no arrest shall be made unless either:

(a)  an offence has been committed or about to be committed;

(b)  the arrest is necessary to prevent a breach of the peace;

(c)  there is reason to believe the suspect may abscond or may be difficult to trace;

(d)  there is reason to believe a further offence may be committed if the suspect is not arrested; and

(e)  the arrest is necessary for the immediate investigation of the alleged offence.

The police should avoid premature arrests. Hasty arrests frequently hamper the investigation and spoil what might otherwise be a successful case. Besides, it may cause embarrassment.

Arrest without Warrant

Every citizen including the police in whose presence an offence or a breach of the peace is being or is about to be committed, has the right to take reasonable steps to make the person who is breaking or threatening the peace refrain from doing so, and those reasonable steps would include detaining him against his will. This is not a duty only reserved for the police but it is a duty of every citizen. Under our laws, a policeman can arrest any person who commits an offence in his presence; the police may also arrest any suspect under any reasonable suspicion. Private persons may also arrest without warrant for any offence in the nature of violence or the use of force against another which is committed in their presence. A private person may arrest without warrant for any offence involving stealing or fraud which is committed in his view. He can also arrest for any offence involving injury to public property. Our laws empower the owner of a property to arrest any person who tries to steal or injures his property or trespasses to his property. Any district magistrate may arrest or direct the arrest in his presence of any person whose arrest upon a warrant he could have lawfully ordered if the facts known to him at the time of making or directing the arrest had been stated before him on oath by some other person.

Warrant of Arrest

It is a written authority signed by a justice directing the person to whom it is addressed to effect an arrest and bring the arrested person before a court. Normally, a justice will not sanction the issue of a warrant of arrest unless a complaint or charge has been made upon oath. He must satisfy himself that there are grounds for the arrest of the suspect or the offender. Once the warrant has been issued and directed to the police, any police officer may execute it at any time.  The police have powers of discretion when arresting offenders without warrant. Several factors are taken into consideration. All persons committing serious or indictable offences shall be arrested without any warrant. The police can exercise discretion not to arrest offenders who have committed minor or indictable offences. Such persons may be proceeded against by way of summons provided that the police is satisfied with their true names and addresses. If the police officer has reasons to doubt their names and addresses, he may arrest.

How Arrests are Made

Generally, any police officer or other person making an arrest shall actually touch or confine the body of the person to be arrested. He must also inform the suspect of the reasons for the arrest. Since his freedom of movement is going to be restricted, it is fair that the offence is made known to him. The police or the officer making the arrest will point out the offence the suspect has committed to him. After that the police officer should note any reply that the suspect would give. The law does not deal specifically with the manner in which an arrest shall be made, although it provides powers to facilitate an arrest, eg to enter or break out of premises, to force and to call for assistance. A police officer must not do anything that cannot be justified by law, eg use of unnecessary force. But an arrested person cannot complain that he has not been informed of the reason for his arrest, when he himself contributed to the situation which made it impracticable to inform him, eg a violent or disorderly prisoner. These principles equally apply to private persons who arrest on suspicion.

Under our laws, when any private person makes an arrest, he should hand the suspect over to a police officer who will re-arrest the suspect or the private person should hand him immediately to the nearest police station for investigation. As soon as it is practicable, the suspect should be searched and placed in safe custody, likewise all articles other than necessary clothing found on him.

Treatment of Complainants

Ghana, today, with a population of about 14,000,000 people has 610 police stations and posts dotted all over the country.   The doors of these police stations are always open day and night.  Any member of the public shall be entitled to make a complaint, either in writing or calling at the station personally. It is the duty of the police to assist any member of the public who reasonably needs their help. The functions of the police under our laws, include prevention and detection of crime, apprehension of offenders, maintenance of public order and the safety of persons and property.  By virtue of these functions, the public are at liberty to call on the police.

A police officer must always be polite, however rude a person may be to him, he must keep his temper and sense of humour.  He should remember that the man who controls his temper controls the situation. It is the duty of the police to listen patiently to any member of the public who reports at the police station with a complaint. After listening to the complaint, it should be recorded and the station officer informed for an immediate action to be taken.  After interrogating the complainant, the station officer can refuse cases as being civil, trivial or vexatious and decline to arrest the suspect. In some cases, it will necessitate the complainant accompanying the police and also providing the means of transport. Lack of transport in nearly all the police stations has hampered effective police action. In most cases, the public become irritated when the police fail to take expeditious action on complaints.

Treatment of Suspects

A suspect or prisoner is any person involved in a criminal case and who is in police custody. Subject to the overriding duty to see that persons in police custody do not escape, there is also the duty to see that they are treated humanely and allowed to exercise all the rights to which they are entitled. On arrest, if a suspect refuses to speak when being interrogated, the police cannot compel him to do so. His refusal to speak should be recorded and this brought to the notice of the station officer or any senior police officer present. The police service instructions give ample guidelines as to how prisoners in police custody should be treated. All personal searches should be done with care and decency. Male prisoners should be searched by male police officers while no female prisoner shall be searched except by a female. Every person when arrested is presumed innocent until the contrary is proved. For this reason, the police have been told to be cautious when dealing with suspected persons. When a juvenile is arrested, all practicable steps are taken by the police to inform a parent or guardian. If the case is not of a serious nature, the station officer may release the juvenile to the parent or guardian on condition that he or she should produce him at an appointed date and time.

Not all prisoners are placed in cells. The decision on this point is one for the officer in charge of the station and is dependent upon the nature of the case, whether bail is to be given, and the length of time of probable detention. In accordance with the police service instructions, if a prisoner is placed in cell, the following general principles apply:

(a)  appropriate station record is to be made of the fact, as well as of times of supervisory visits, meals supplied and visitors;

(b)  a female prisoner must be lodged in the cell used for females, supervised by a police woman;

(c) precautions may be necessary in the case of some prisoners, eg removal of laces, braces, ties, socks, trousers and shirts to avert possible suicide;

(d) the frequency of visits of inspection by the police, mainly casual but occasionally for greater re-assurance, will depend upon the circumstances of the case.

Special care is taken of prisoners who may be ill.  In some cases, doctors are called to attend to them while in police custody.  In most serious cases, prisoners or suspects are taken to the nearest hospital for an immediate check up.

Police officers are responsible for the custody of suspects and prisoners and are duty bound to assist so far as is practicable in the exercise by suspects of any of their accepted rights. These rights should be distinguished from facilities or privileges which suspects may claim or request but which may not be readily available in some police stations.  The officer in charge has to exercise his discretion according to the circumstances of particular cases.

Under our laws, all written statements must be made under caution. Police are always advised to conform to the Judges' Rules.  These provisions are intended to ensure that a statement is voluntary and that it consists of what the suspect wants to say, not what a police officer wants him to say. The provisions of section 120(2) and (3) of the Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323) should be adhered to by the police. The purpose of any interview is to obtain from the person concerned his explanations of facts and not necessarily to obtain an admission.

Police Cells

When the colonialists stepped on the Ghanaian soil, the police service was one of the first institutions they established to help them in their colonization. Every police station that was built, also had a cell or cells for the confinement of suspects and prisoners.  It is regrettable to say that all the cells in our police stations are not good enough except, perhaps Legon police station.  I say this because it is the only police station that has decent cells and toilet facilities.

Transport

The question of the use of transport is one which has always to be considered, not only when an arrest has to be made but also in the case of the conveyance to the station of a person arrested, especially if taking the arrested person through crowded public places is to be avoided, to avert possible escape. The public often criticises the police for unnecessary handling of suspects and prisoners.

At times, suspects and prisoners are unnecessarily kept in police custody or cells for several days, awaiting transportation to the nearest prisons for confinement. Some of these treatments are not deliberate, but are due to circumstances beyond the capabilities of the police, eg court orders, lack of transport and many others. With the establishment of police watch-dogs certain police constraints and other abuses are taken care of by reports to Headquarters and other legal agencies.
