SEMINAR ON THE TREATMENT OF OFFENDERS: TYPES OF SENTENCES [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 156—160

Her Lordship Mrs. Akoto-Bamfo*

 

Introduction

A court of law is under a duty to pass sentence on any person whom it finds guilty and accordingly convicts of an offence. The type of sentence imposed on the convicted person depends on the nature of the offence, the penalty set by the law and the jurisdictional limits of the trial court as provided by the Courts Act, 1971 (Act 372) as amended by the Courts (Amendment) Law, 1987 (PNDCL 191). Again the type of sentence depends on the view that the judge or magistrate takes of the conduct of the offender. Every sentence should therefore be traceable to a specific statute, legislation or executive or legislative instrument. Once the statute has prescribed the sentence, it is illegal to exceed the limits: see Republic v Addo [1974] GLR 254. In that case an offence punishable by a fine only was punished by a sentence of three months. This was declared illegal and thus subsequently quashed on appeal. Generally, therefore even though a court has a discretion as to what punishment to impose, this discretion must be exercised on well-known principles and in accordance with rules of reason and justice and not according to private opinion.

The types of sentences which the courts impose include death, imprisonment, fines, detention, probation, discharge with or without conditions, order of recognisance for keeping the peace and compensation and liability for police supervision.

The Death Sentence

The Criminal Code requires the imposition of the death sentence in cases of treason, murder and aggravated piracy under sections 46, 180 and 194 respectively of the Criminal Code, 1960 (Act 29).

Furthermore, PNDCL 78, s 16(1) requires the imposition of the death sentence for such offences as may be specified by the council and in respect of cases where the tribunal is satisfied that grave circumstances meriting such a penalty have been revealed. Under these laws persons have been sentenced to death by firing squad for ritual murder, economic sabotage, etc.

The death sentence cannot be passed on juvenile offenders and pregnant women. In the case of the former, the juvenile offender is detained in such a place and manner as directed by the Secretary for Justice and in the case of the latter a life imprisonment is substituted therefor. By virtue of the Criminal Procedure Code (Amendment) Law, 1988 (PNDCL 206) which amended section 304 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30) the sentence of death shall either be by hanging or shooting by firing squad. Depending on the particular circumstance surrounding each case, the Head of State upon receipt of all relevant documents relating to the trial and the conviction could either have the death warrant sealed or pardon the convict upon terms.

Imprisonment

The maximum sentences that can be imposed are set out under section 296 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30).

Offences are classified. We have first degree felonies, eg forgery, altering forged documents, robbery and rape. Where the punishment is not specified the person shall be liable for imprisonment for life or for any lesser term.

Where an enactment declares an offence to be a second degree felony, eg kidnapping, etc and the punishment is not specified, the person is liable for imprisonment for ten years or for any lesser term. Where a crime is labelled as a misdemeanour, eg libel, assault, abduction, etc the term of imprisonment where not specified, shall be for three years or less.

The Criminal Procedure Code (Amendment) Act, 1965 (Act 261) which amended the Criminal Code declared certain offences involving dishonesty, eg stealing, fraud, etc to be felonies punishable by a term of imprisonment of 25 years or less. The trial judge or magistrate has a discretion in all these cases to impose a fine or even discharge the accused either absolutely or upon conditions. Certain enactments, notably the Pharmacy and Drugs Act, 1961 (Act 64) prescribe a minimum sentence of five years for unauthorised possession of Indian hemp unless circumstances do exist which require the imposition of a lesser term. A sentence of three years or more could be imposed with hard labour unless the court otherwise directs. Where a person is convicted for a term of imprisonment for three years or more, any public office held within the jurisdiction becomes vacant.

The Punishment of Habitual Criminal Act, 1963 (Act 192) states that where a person who is not less than 20 years of age is convicted for an offence other than that for murder and who has had at least two previous convictions for either a felony or misdemeanour the High Court after satisfying itself as to the criminal habits of the convicted person shall pass a sentence of preventive custody for not less than ten years. This power is vested only in the High Court.

Committal by District to Circuit Court for Enhanced Sentence

Section 178 of Act 30 empowers the district court, after conviction where it takes the view that its powers of sentence are not sufficiently adequate to deal with the convicted person, to commit  him to the circuit court for sentence. Provision is made under section 300 of Act 30, for increased punishment to be imposed on a person who has already been convicted of a similar offence previous conviction.

Fines

An enactment may impose a fine only as a penalty, eg an offence under section 296 of Act 29, eg throwing rubbish, defacing public notices, loitering, etc. The court in such circumstances has no authority to impose a higher penalty but may as a means of enforcing payment of the fine fix a term of imprisonment in default. Where the amount of fine is not stated, it is subject to the limitations of the powers of the court. Some of the fines are ridiculously low having regard to the fact that there are parallel systems running in the country at the moment and having regard to the current value of the cedi. A magistrate or judge faced with such a sentence may be tempted to impose a custodial sentence. It is seriously recommended that some of the fines should be reviewed.  Again all the fines are stated in the old British currency; it has always been an issue as to the rate at which to convert, eg whether at forex bureau rate, etc.

Recognisance for Keeping the Peace: Section 299 of Act 30

Where a person is convicted of any offence other than an offence for which the sentence is fixed by law, the court may instead of or in addition to a fine or any such punishment, order the convicted person to enter into a bond either with or without sureties for keeping the peace and of good behaviour. The court may order that a term of imprisonment be served in default.

Costs and Compensation

Under section 139(1) of Act 30, in addition to any other penalty, the court may after conviction order the person to pay such reasonable cost as the court deems fit. By virtue of Act 30, s 148, a person convicted of a felony or misdemeanour may be ordered by the court to give compensation to any person injured by the offence. If the conviction is after a summary trial the award shall not exceed ¢50. (Again this should be looked at.)

There seems to be no limit after trial upon indictment (it should be noted that a greater percentage of the trials are done summarily). Again where the sentence is imposed for an offence involving dishonesty, the court may make an order for a return of the property not recovered and payment in default of the value of the property. This is in exercise of the court's civil jurisdiction. A court may make an order under this section even though the value of the property or money exceeds the jurisdictional limits of the court.

Probation

Where a person is arraigned before a court and the charges are proved but the court takes the view that owing to factors like the age, character, health and mental conditions of the offender or any peculiar circumstances surrounding the case, it is expedient to release the offender on probation, the court may make the order. The order requires the person so ordered during the operation of the order to submit to the supervision of a probation officer assigned to the district or area. The period of operation shall not be less than six months and not exceeding three years. The order must contain directions as to residence, etc as the court may deem necessary. A court may make the order in addition to the imposition of costs for damage or compensation for loss. An order may be made in respect of a person above 17 years but the court shall make the order in circumstances where the person undertakes to abide by the order.

Absolute or Conditional Discharge

A court may after a conviction take the view that it will be inappropriate either imposing a fine or releasing the person on probation. In such circumstances it may either discharge the person absolutely or with conditions. Where he is only warned, it is an absolute discharge, the familiar expression being cautioned and discharged. He may also be discharged upon a condition that he commits no further offence within a specified period.

Deportation

An alien who is sentenced to a term of imprisonment not exceeding three months may be deported upon a recommendation made by the trial court to the Chief Justice.

Conclusion

In conclusion I wish to make the following suggestions, namely  that a re-classification of offences has to be done. Act 261 has to be looked at in view of the fact that long sentences do not necessarily reform the convict. Again, Act 192 has to be looked at. Offences which do not involve dishonesty, eg assault, causing damage and for which the payment of compensation would adequately satisfy the complainant should be made non-custodial offences.  In such cases instead of imposition of a term of imprisonment in default  other forms of sentence like communal labour for specified periods have to be looked at and it must relate to the offence, so that where a person is convicted of causing damage, he works at a construction site for periods under supervision.

The scope of section 318 of Act 30 must be enlarged, to allow monthly payment in cases in which sentences can be suspended. The rules relating to fines should be looked at. Fines should be increased, eg section 297 and others. Again other terms of punishment should be looked at, eg communal labour. Where the circumstances require a person to be sentenced to a jail term it should be reasonably short especially where the court's discretion is not fettered.

In the long term, the general economic conditions have to be improved since many a person has turned to crime basically for survival.
