LA STOOL LAND DISPUTE [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 338—348

Woodman Gordon R.

 

INTRODUCTION

Nartey v Mechanical Lloyd Assembly Plant Ltd1 is a recent addition to the line of land cases which marks the growth of Accra, each stage of expansion causing land values in newly peripheral areas to rocket, this leading in turn to litigation over titles.  This Comment examines three of the many issues argued in the case.2 The transactions and other events in issue are set out italicized in chronological order in the course of the discussion.

The Power to make Grants of La Rural Land

The land in dispute was La rural land situate in the village of Frafraha (or Fafraha), about 30 kilometres north of La town.  Some time after its conquest by the La people, settlements had been made in the area and control established over it by members of the Agbawe family, based in the Agbawe quarter of La town.  At the time of the dispute Nii Okpoti Adjaye Komey (referred to as OK) was the head of the Agbawe family in Frafraha and the Mantse of Frafraha.  The plaintiff (the respondent in the Court of Appeal and appellant before the Supreme Court) sued for a declaration of title, damages for trespass, recovery of possession and an injunction restraining the defendant-company from entering the land.

He based his case on the earliest relevant transaction, a documentary conveyance of April 1976 (exhibit B), executed in favour of the plaintiff by OK on behalf of the Agbawe family of Frafraha.  The plaintiff commenced farming on the land.  An issue was posed as to whether the plaintiff’s grantor OK had been competent to grant the land, or whether only the La stool could have validly made such a grant.  The argument was clouded by a tendency to state the issue as being whether the land was stool land or family land, or as to whether it was owned by the La stool or the Agbawe family. Thus Owusu v Manche of Labadi,3 in which it had been held that compensation on the compulsory acquisition of La rural land was payable to the La stool, not the family in possession, was cited as authority for the conclusion that such land was stool, not family land.  This formulation of the issue would seem to have presented a false dichotomy. The allodial title is frequently vested in both a head stool and its sub-stools or constituent families. In such circumstances rights may be exercisable variously by the head stool, by the appropriate sub-stool or family, or by both either jointly or in the alternative.  As Adade JSC held:

“[W]here land belonging to or attaching to a paramount stool is in the care and possession of a sub-stool, control often becomes a shared responsibility between the paramount stool and the sub-stool. The quality of the responsibility and therefore of the privileges which each enjoys in relation to the land may depend on local practices.”

Owusu v Manche of Labadi, if considered in detail, explained Adade JSC, would be seen to confirm such a dual exercise of rights in La lands, although it was held that the particular right to receive compensation in that instance was vested in the La stool. The point in issue in the present case concerned the quite distinct right to make grants of the land.  Almost all the judges in the case accepted that the authorities tended towards establishing that the La stool alone held this right. The leading case on the neighbouring Osu rural land, Akwei v Awuletey,4 determined that the Osu stool alone was competent to grant such land.5 The La case, Adoaku v Nyamalor6 held that it was right to rely on an Osu case because the customary laws of the two areas were similar.  However, that case concerned quarter (or town) lands, which in Osu, and presumably also in La, are subject to different rules from those governing rural lands.  Owusu v Manche of Labadi, if not directly on the issue, was indirectly favourable to the claim of the La stool.  Curiously, no mention was made of the case most directly on the point, Nii Adjei Onano v Okoi Noi,7 which held that La rural land might be granted by the La stool alone.  In the view of the Court of Appeal and the dissentient minority in the Supreme Court the authorities had conclusively determined the law.8 In the view of Taylor and Wuaku JJSC, members of the majority in the Supreme Court, the authorities were strongly indicative of the law.  The other member of the majority, Adade JSC, alone concluded on the authorities that sub-stools always had the power to make such grants.9 For Taylor and Wuaku JJSC, the authorities did not so much provide an answer as would pose a problem, when viewed in relation to certain evidence in the case. This evidence showed clearly, but inconveniently, that over a lengthy period many plots in Frafraha and adjacent areas had been the subject of grants by the local heads of the Agbawe family, and virtually none had been granted by the La stool. Moreover, although aware of this practice, the La Mantse had failed to asset effectively any claim to make such grants.  Thus, whether or not the law laid down by the courts had been in accord with the previous social practice, it was clear that many transactions entered into subsequently to the judicial declarations had been based on a contrary view of the law. Any such situation raises the question whether the courts can and should apply some legal doctrine which will give effect to the expectations of the parties to such transactions even if the courts cannot pronounce the transactions to be fully valid.

In the present case, two such possibilities seem to have been considered. First the evidence of the practice of the granting of rural land by sub-stools might have been taken to be evidence of customary law, showing that the authority to make such grants was vested in the sub-stools.  The difficulty here was that the existing case law supported the contrary view.  It is doubtful whether the courts are entitled to accept further evidence of customary law on an issue which falls within rules already established by authority. It is also questionable whether it would be good policy to allow authoritatively settled questions of customary law to be re-opened in this way.10  This approach was not fully adopted.  Secondly, there was a possibility of employing the doctrine of estoppel by acquiescence to modify the strict legal conclusion in the light of social reality.  The doctrine has frequently been used for this purpose in other contexts.11  In the present case, Taylor JSC, in whose reasoning Wuaku JSC concurred, examined the doctrine as it had been expounded in a number of English and Ghanaian cases, and argued that the La stool had, by acquiescing over a long period in the granting of rural lands exclusively by sub-stools, impliedly represented to persons in the position of the plaintiff that a good title might be obtained by taking a grant from a sub-stool.

This differs from the many other instances of application of the doctrine. Usually a party is estopped as against another from asserting title to a particular plot as a result of acquiescence in acts done in respect of that plot.  Here the argument was that the La stool was estopped as against all members of the public who had taken grants of plots of rural lands from sub-stools as a result of its acquiescence in grants by sub-stools of other plots.  Nevertheless, there would seem to be little reason in principle why the doctrine should not apply in this way.  Perhaps the only objection may be that the mistaken belief in such a case is an error of law.  It is not certain on previous authority whether a mistake of law can found a plea of estoppel by acquiescence.12a  It is arguable that the objection is weaker in a case like the present where the law in question is a local rule with a relatively small area of operation.  It may be unreasonable to expect members of the public to be aware of all such rules.

A nice problem is encountered by one who would seek to determine the legal proposition or propositions for which the Supreme Court decision is authority.  Two members of the court, Taylor and Wuaku JJSC, out of the three who decided in favour of the plaintiff, held that a grant of La rural land by a sub-stool was ineffective except when the La stool was estopped by acquiescence from disputing it.  The two dissentients, Francois and Amua-Sekyi JJSC, held that such a grant was always ineffective. The other member of the majority, Adade JSC, held that it was generally effective.  Thus four of the judges held that grants by a sub-stool would be invalid in the absence of acquiescence by the La stool.  But these four included only two out of the three who upheld the plaintiff’s appeal. Moreover, this near consensus postulates a condition—the absence of acquiescence—which appears unlikely to be often met.  It seems as if there will be few instances arising for decisions in the near future where it will not be possible to establish acquiescence, although the La stool may assert and publicise its rights and so exclude the plea of acquiescence in respect of grants taken from sub-stools in the future.  The conclusions on this issue left doubt as to the strength of the plaintiff’s title.  From this followed the next issue.

The Burden of Proof in Land Cases

All the judges found it necessary to examine the basis of the defendant-company’s title, notwithstanding that the defendants’ counterclaims were not pursued beyond the High Court. The following events were proved.  The land referred to at each stage comprised or included the subject-matter of exhibit B to which the plaintiff's claim related. 

(a) On 8 May 1976 a lease (called both exhibits Rl and CA1) was executed in favour of Mr R A Darko (D), executive chairman of the defendant-company, by the La Mantse. D died in September 1977; (b) on 10 June 1978 the plaintiff discovered that workers of the defendant-company had entered the land and were working there.  He expelled them; (c) in August 1979 a documentary conveyance (exhibit F) was executed in favour of the defendant-company by OK and Atofotse Tetteh Nukpa (TN), the head of the Agbawe family in La, on behalf of the Agbawe family of Frafraha; and (d) subsequently the defendant-company took possession of the land and refused to leave.

While it might not be easily deniable that there were weaknesses in the plaintiff’s title, it was incontrovertible that there were weaknesses in that of the defendant-company.  The plaintiff’s problem arose from his having a grant from the sub-stool, not the La stool.  But the La stool had not made a grant to the defendant-company.  It had made a grant to D, but there was no clear evidence that the defendant-company was successor in title to D or that the grant was taken on the company’s behalf.  The grant to the defendant-company (exhibit F) was, like the plaintiff’s, not made by the La stool.  Any claim in reliance upon it conflicted with any claim based on the grant to D.  Various other defects will be mentioned later.

For the decision of such disputes, where each side’s case has weaknesses, there appears to be two different guides. A general principle of common law is that a plaintiff in a civil action need do no more than establish his claim on the balance of probabilities to succeed.12b This suggests that a plaintiff with a weak case may succeed against a defendant whose case is even weaker. But a line of cases typified by Kodilinye v Odu13 propounds the apparently conflicting principle that in land cases the plaintiff can succeed only on the strength of his own case, not on the weakness of the defendant’s.  In the present case, the majority of the Supreme Court adopted an argument set out in Ricketts v Addo14 which sought to reconcile the two principles.  It stated that, according to the rule in Kodilinye v Odu, a plaintiff could not succeed unless he positively established some grounds on which a court could reasonably uphold his claim.  But once this initial requirement had been met, the claims of the two parties must be weighed against each other, a process which required the weaknesses of each to be taken into account.15

The resolution of this issue indicates the importance of a clear answer to the further question, what must the plaintiff prove?  The present case was treated as primarily an action in trespass, the claims to damages, recovery of possession and an injunction all being related to this tort. The action is directed at the protection of possession. To succeed a plaintiff needs generally to prove only that he was in possession of the land, and that the defendant directly interfered with that possession.16 Thus in the present case, the majority of the Supreme Court recounted the plaintiff’s farming activities on the land, showing that he had been in possession from the time of event (a) in April 1976 until event (d) in or after August 1979. (Event (c), the entry by the defendant-company in June 1978 had been temporary, and had been reversed by the plaintiff’s assertion of his right.)17 Thus the plaintiff discharged the initial burden on a plaintiff in trespass.

This argument was challenged in the dissenting judgment of Amua-Sekyi JSC in two respects.  First it was contended: “The most that can be said for the plaintiff here is that he probably entered the land some three weeks before the defendants did . . .”  The dates of events (a) and (d) were cited.  With respect, this argument seems to substitute for the fact of possession, on which the action in trespass is based,18 the receipt of a conveyance, which may have no effect on the facts on the ground.  The second counter-argument of Amua-Sekyi JSC was to the effect that the possession protected by the action of trespass:

“. . . is not possession for a day or two, a week, a month, or even a year . . . It is rather long, peaceful, undisturbed possession over a considerable period of time, long and peaceful enough to raise a presumption that the occupation of the land must have a lawful origin.”

Again one must respectfully question this argument. The action in trespass protects the fact of possession, not a title which may be inferred from possession. The authorities show that the action can be founded on relatively slight acts of possession over a short period.19

The plaintiff who proves these facts is prima facie entitled to succeed.  It is not necessary to prove title.20 However, the outcome of a trespass action may not eventually turn on the fact of possession. It is a good defence that the defendant held a title which legally empowered him to do the acts complained of, that is, which conferred a better right to possession than the plaintiff’s. This defence is frequently raised, and is the procedure whereby title to land is so often put in issue in trespass actions. In principle the onus is at this juncture on the defendant to prove the title on which the defence is based.  However, relatively slight evidence is sometimes enough to nullify the effect of the plaintiffs bare fact of possession, and so to compel the plaintiff in turn to produce evidence of his title.  Hence it is sometimes said that the defendant by pleading title puts the plaintiff in trespass to the proof of his title.  In an action for a declaration of title the plaintiff’s initial burden is different.  The plaintiff here is not seeking protection for possession, but a declaratory judgment stating that he holds a specified title.  Therefore the onus is on him to show that he holds that title. When a claim in trespass is coupled with a claim for a declaration of title, as in the present case, it is quite possible for the former to succeed and the latter to be dismissed.21 However, in the present case, this question was not raised.

Consent and Concurrence to Grants of Stool Land

Taylor JSC noted a formidable list of possible defects in exhibit R1 or CA1.  It was unregistered under the Land Registry Act, 1962 (Act 122) and it infringed the Administration of Lands Act, 1962 (Act 123), s 8, the Stamp Act, 1965 (Act 311), and the Illiterates Protection Ordinance (Cap 262).  Just one of these provisions will be considered here.22

The Administration of Lands Act, 1962 (Act 123), s 8, as far as is relevant, provides that “any disposal of any land [for consideration] by a stool ... shall be subject to the concurrence of the [Secretary for Lands] and shall be of no effect unless such concurrence is granted,” and that a transaction contrary to the provisions of this section “shall be void.”  The requirement seems to be widely ignored.23 Exhibit R1 or CA1 had not received the secretary’s concurrence, and Taylor JSC held it to be void on this ground, among others.  The only other judge to comment on this issue, Amua-Sekyi JSC, said:

“As to the absence of concurrence of the Lands Commission, this could not have been raised by the plaintiff since it was his case that the land was family, not stool land.  Had he not challenged the title of the [defendant-company], [Exhibit Rl or CAl] would, in due time, have been referred to the Lands Commission by the Lands Department for approval. That was the practice as I knew it at the Bar.”

It would appear that two different statutory provisions were under discussion.  The words of Amua-Sekyi JSC refer to the terms of the Provisional National Defence Council (Establishment) Proclamation (Consequential and Supplementary Provisions) Law, 1982 (PNDCL 42), s 47(1) which provides that “[A]n assurance of stool land ... shall not operate to pass an interest in or right over a stool land unless it was executed with the consent and concurrence of the Lands Commission...” (This is the most recent re-enactment of a provision which originated in the Constitution, 1969, art 164(3).) If it be assumed that this provision has not superseded that of 1962 but co-exists with it, it follows that grants within their scope require the consent or concurrence of both the secretary and the Lands Commission.  It would seem reasonable to suppose that the requisite consent or concurrence may in either case be given after the parties have executed the disposal or assurance.  But it is questionable whether according to the statutory words, the transaction can carry any legal effect before such consent or concurrence has been given, even if it is likely to be given.

Exhibit B also had not received any governmental consent or concurrence.  It may have been considered that, as a grant by a head of family of family land, it was not within the statutory provisions.  It may be replied in relation to the Administration of Lands Act, 1962, that it was a “disposal ... by a stool,” namely, the Frafraha sub-stool.  In relation to PNDCL 42 it may be replied that, as suggested above, even if in a sense this was family land, it was also stool land.  Therefore this was an “assurance of stool land” vested in the La stool, as well as the Frafraha sub-stool.  Moreover, under PNDCL 42 “stool land” includes “family land.24 This aspect of the case points to the possibility that the courts may start to enforce these often disregarded provisions.25 But it also supports the reflection that a good deal of judicial clarification of the statutory terms will be required before parties to land transactions generally can be prodded into full compliance.
