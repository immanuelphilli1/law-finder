BURDEN OF PROOF IN LAND SUITS: [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 349—359

Bimpong-Buta S. Y.

 

*

1 INTRODUCTION

The law is well-settled that in criminal cases, the prosecution is required to prove its case beyond all reasonable doubt; whilst in civil cases the plaintiff must produce sufficient evidence on "a preponderance of probabilities" a phrase which has been statutorily defined as:

"that degree of certainty of belief in the mind of the tribunal of fact or the court by which it is convinced that the existence of a fact is more probable than its non-existence."1

We intend commenting on the 1989 Court of Appeal decision in Banga v Djanie2 with a view to determining whether there is a higher burden of proof beyond the balance or preponderance of probabilities in a claim for declaration of title to land.

2 IS THERE A HIGHER BURDEN OF PROOF IN LAND SUITS?

In Banga v Djanie the plaintiffs claim for declaration of title to a tract of land as family property was controverted by the defendants. Both parties led conflicting evidence of traditional history as to acquisition of the disputed land. The plaintiff also sought reliance on certain acts of the defendants as constituting estoppel such as the first defendant co-operating with the plaintiff in surveying the disputed land and also appearing at the office of the lawyer of the plaintiff’s witness to execute some documents in favour of some members of the plaintiff’s family. The trial court gave judgment for the plaintiff.

The Court of Appeal (coram: Francois JSC and Ampiah and Lamptey JJA) allowed the defendants' appeal from the decision of the trial court. It found, on the evidence, that the plaintiff failed to produce evidence of her right of title, possession and user.  The Court of Appeal, however, found that there was unchallenged evidence that the ancestors of the defendants had been in possession of the disputed land and had exercised acts of alienation of portions of the land for decades. It therefore held (applying the decision of the West African Court of Appeal in Kodilinye v Odu)3 that since the plaintiff had sued for declaration of title, she must rely on the strength of her own case but not on the shortcomings or weakness in the 'defendants' case; and that having failed to produce evidence of her root of title, possession and user, she could not rely on the acts of the defence which at best could only constitute weaknesses in the defence. Francois JSC in his opinion in support of the unanimous decision of the Court of Appeal, made some further pronouncements. His Lordship said4:

"Since a declaration of title is sought, it is essential that the usual burden of proof should be satisfactorily discharged for success. That burden was clearly laid in Kodilinye v Odu (1935) 2 WACA 336. That burden . . . ensures that the plaintiff should win on the strength of her own case which must not be propped up by weaknesses in the defence's case. The principle has for several decades been the fulcrum for determining ownership in land matters in our courts. In recent times a dangerous trend has been erupting of equating this burden with the normal burden in a civil case of measuring success by a balance of probabilities. In my view the requirement of a higher burden of proof in land matters cannot be whittled away by glosses on the principle. This quality of proof has sometimes even been equated with proof in criminal matters, i.e. proof beyond reasonable doubt. Suffice to emphasise that a high measure of proof is necessary to sustain victory in a plaintiff seeking a declaration of title to land . . . [He must] lead positive evidence to merit victory, and not merely to rely on the shortcomings of a defendant in the discharge of his obligations."5

It must be pointed out that the two judges in Banga v Djanie, Ampiah and Lamptey JJA, did not in their opinions specifically or impliedly support the view of Francois JSC that there is in law a higher burden of proof, i.e. proof beyond reasonable doubt (not on a balance or preponderance of probabilities) in a claim for title to land. Indeed, Ampiah JA in his concurring opinion must be said to have taken a contrary stance (perhaps unwittingly) when his lordship said6:

“But since the traditional evidence adduced by the parties was conflicting, the plaintiffs could only succeed if their traditional evidence when connected to recent acts of possession was made more probable."

We must, however, point out that in the subsequent Court of Appeal unanimous decision in Adzo v Tay7 (affirming the dismissal by the trial court of the plaintiffs claim for declaration of title to land) their lordships Ampiah, Lamptey and Essiem JJA cited with approval the pronouncement of Francois JSC in Banga, namely: the existence in law of a higher burden of proof in land suits - not on a balance of probabilities—but "on a quality of proof [which] has sometimes been equated with proof in criminal matters, ie proof beyond reasonable doubt."

We discern two issues from what we might refer to as the Francois principle as endorsed in Adzo v Tay: Firstly, has the plaintiff in a claim for title to land a higher burden of proof beyond proof on a balance or preponderance of probabilities?  Secondly, what is the true ambit of the Kodilinye principle that a plaintiff in an action for a declaration of title must win on the strength of his own case and not to rely on the weakness in the defence's case?

It is suggested that the Francois principle as to requirement of a higher burden of proof in land suit may (with the utmost respect) be viewed as having been given per incuriam for failing to consider the relevance of sections 10-12 of the Evidence Decree, 1975 (NRCD 323) on what constitutes the burden of proof in civil cases. The effect of these sections is to draw a distinction between: (a) the burden of proof in criminal cases, i.e. proof beyond reasonable doubt, and (b) the burden of proof in civil cases, ie proof by a preponderance of the probabilities. This is borne out by a passage in the Commentary on the Evidence Decree, 1978 where it is stated8:

"The burden of proving a fact by a preponderance of the probabilities is the standard burden imposed in civil actions. Proof beyond a reasonable doubt is the degree of proof generally required of the prosecution in criminal actions."

The matter is put beyond controversy when one compares the wording of subsections (3) and (4) of section 11. Section 11(3) of NRCD 323 provides that: "In a criminal action" the burden of producing evidence by the accused is discharged when he produces "sufficient evidence so that on all the evidence a reasonable mind could have a reasonable doubt as to guilt." In contrast, section 11(4) provides that "In other circumstances (which must patently be construed as in civil actions) the burden of producing evidence requires the production of sufficient evidence "so that on all the evidence a reasonable jury could conclude that the existence of the fact was more probable than its non-existence." If that is the true statutory standard of proof in civil actions which must necessarily include a civil action for title to land, then one finds it (with the greatest respect) hard to accept or justify the Francois principle in Banga v Djanie that the normal proof by a preponderance of probabilities in civil actions is inapplicable to land suits and that in such cases the plaintiff must prove his claim to title beyond reasonable doubt. No policy reason was given by his lordship in support of that view of the law. Admittedly, his lordship's view is supported by the 1933 Privy Council decision in Kponuglo v Kodadja9 (a case where the respondent had sued for trespass and an injunction) and where the Privy Council said10: "Has the respondent discharged the onus which rests upon him in demonstrating beyond reasonable doubt that the title to the disputed land is in him?" It appears the opinion of Francois JSC in Banga v Djanie is at best founded on the case law as it existed before the promulgation of the Evidence Decree, 1975, ss 10-12, particularly section 11(3) and (4) which came into force as from 1 October 1979.11 There is nothing in sections 10-12 of the Evidence Decree which lends support to his lordship's view that proof on a balance or preponderance of probabilities—applicable in civil cases does not apply to claims for title to land. In any case, before Banga was decided, the Court of Appeal in an earlier unanimous decision: Mante v Botwe12 gave judgment for the appellants in a claim for title to land. The court per Taylor JSC said13:

“Under section 12 of NRCD 323, "The preponderance of the probabilities in this case is certainly in favour of the appellants and . . . the lower courts were wrong to impugn their title without advancing adequate legal reasons for doing so particularly in a case in which they have been in undisturbed possession for more than 28 years."

3 THE TRUE AMBIT OF THE KODILINYE PRINCIPLE

It is therefore respectfully suggested that proof on a preponderance of probabilities in civil cases is also applicable to claims for title to land and that the standard of proof (which in the words of Francois JSC "ensures that the plaintiff should win on the strength of his own case which must not be propped up by weaknesses in the defence case") must be considered in determining the balance of the probabilities. In other words in assessing the balance of the probabilities, all the evidence (including the strength of the plaintiff's case and the weaknesses in the defence) must be considered: Everything must, as it were, be put into the melting pot. The party in whose favour the balance tilts, ie the person whose case is more probable, wins.

This view of the law is supported by the 1975 Court of Appeal decision in Ricketts v Addo14 a case which was considered by Francois JSC in the Banga case on the issue of the application of the Kodilinye principle. In the Ricketts case, the plaintiffs bought a large tract of land originally owned by two families—the W and E families. The sale transaction was entered into with the consent and concurrence of W family. The E family was no party to the sale transaction. However, the plaintiffs remained in undisturbed possession of the land for 30 years without any objection by the E family. In a subsequent action by the plaintiffs for declaration of title to the purchased land, the defendant representing the W and E families, pleaded total ignorance of all material facts. The trial court found for the plaintiffs. On appeal, the defendants contended, inter alia, that the plaintiffs ought to have relied entirely on the strength of their own case and not on the weakness in the case of the defence.

The appeal was dismissed by the Court of Appeal which per Amissah JA delineated the true ambit of the principle that in land suits, the plaintiff must rely on the strength of his own case. It was held that it would be applied where the plaintiff failed to make out a case at all and sought to rely on the defects in the case of the defendant (that was in fact what happened in the instant case of Banga v Djanie). It could also be applied where the plaintiff could put forward some sort of claim which was met by the defence of occupation or possession, in which case the plaintiff (whatever the defects in the defence case) must rely on the strength of his own projected title to the land. The Court of Appeal per Amissah JA made the following pertinent assessment of the principle15.

"It should not be forgotten that the burden of persuasion which a plaintiff has to satisfy in every civil case is no more than proof on a balance of probabilities. In a trial involving title to land like any other civil trial, therefore, if the defendant's case is measured against the plaintiff's and the plaintiff's is found more probable, a determination which necessarily involves the balancing of the strengths and weaknesses of the rival claims, the plaintiff’s case has to be accepted."

Given the Court of Appeal decision and pronouncement in the Ricketts case, as to the nature of the burden of proof in land suits, the Francois principle in the Banga case that there is a "requirement of a higher burden of proof in land cases" which "has sometimes even been equated with proof in criminal matters, ie proof beyond reasonable doubt" cannot be easily supported. Besides his lordship Francois JSC himself recognised the limitation of the Kodilinye principle when after examining the facts in the Ricketts case, in which the defence had exhibited, as his lordship put it, "emptiness relying purely on technical legal points and asserting a plea of innocence of all facts" he concluded16:

"In such a situation, Amissah JA [as in the Ricketts case] as I understand him, says there is a gnawing void in the defence which cannot be bridged by any academic considerations of the strength or the weakness of a case. There must be at least the semblance of a demonstrable case before the principle of strength or weakness can be pressed into service . . . where the plaintiff's superior strength is disclosed, this must of necessity be matched by what the defence has to offer and that 'necessarily involves the balancing of the strengths and weaknesses of the rival claims' if the defence is not a mere hollow denial."

It is interesting to observe that six weeks before Francois JSC made his pronouncements on the proper application of the Kodilinye principle in Banga v Djanie, the Supreme Court in Odametey v Clocuh17 had (per Taylor JSC, Francois and Wuaku JJSC concurring) also explained the application of the principle that the plaintiff must rely entirely on the strength of his own case and not on any weakness in the defence case. It was held (applying the Court of Appeal decision in the Ricketts' case) that if the plaintiff totally failed to make out a case for title to land, he could not (on application of the principle) rely on the weakness in the defence case. If, however, he led evidence entitling him to some relief and the defendant in giving evidence disclosed any weakness which tended to support the plaintiff's claim, the plaintiff would be entitled to rely on the weakness in the defence case to strengthen his case. The Supreme Court in the Clocuh case deprecated the mechanical application of the Kodilinye principle and castigated the trial court for failing to ascertain whether or not the plaintiff did make a case (even if a weak case) bearing in mind the fact that it was only when a plaintiff has failed completely to make a case that the Kodilinye principle would be applicable. The court in so holding cited with approval the dictum of Adade JSC in Nartey v Mechanical Lloyd Assembly Plant Ltd.18 where his lordship said:

"It is true that a person who comes to court no matter what the claim is, must be able to make a good case for the court to consider, otherwise he fails. But that is not to say that having succeeded in establishing some case, he cannot take advantage of conflicts, admissions and other weakness in the defendant's case."

4 EVALUATION OF THE KODILINYE PRINCIPLE

What is the policy justification or rationale for the Kodilinye doctrine? We must not overlook the fact that in all cases where the so-called Kodilinye doctrine has been applied to arrive at a decision against the plaintiff, the defendants have been in possession and occupation and exercising rights of ownership. That was the position in the instant case of Banga v Djanie. Ownership carries with it the right of possession, but possession does not necessarily connote right of ownership. However, ownership is an abstract relational concept; while possession is a practical demonstrable aspect of exercising ownership rights. Herein lies the problem. It is for such theoretical and philosophical and practical problems associated with ownership and possession in the common law that the law requires that the person claiming ownership, itself an abstract thing, must produce cogent and strong evidence to displace the practical evidence of possession by the defendant. The Kodilinye principle means no more than the practical resolution of this theoretical, philosophical and doctrinal underpinnings of land law whose foundations are embedded in feudal theories of allodial ownership. It is suggested that the correct statement or explanation of the Kodilinye principle therefore appears to be this: Possession is a sufficient but not a necessary condition for claims to ownership. Therefore the person claiming abstract rights of ownership against the one already in physical possession, in order to succeed, requires more than merely pointing to certain acts of the one in possession (which may be inconsistent with ownership) and therefore may weaken his case for claims to ownership. He is required to do more than that. This is not an easy task because the concrete objective fact of physical possession constitutes a prima facie case for claim to ownership. One can thus appreciate the enormity of the burden to be discharged. But at the end of the day, the weaknesses of the defendant's case will constitute part of the evidence which the claimant has to assemble in order to rebut the prima facie evidence of possession. It would seem that pointing to such weaknesses in the defendant's case without more cannot be enough. It involves weighing those acts constituting the weakness and the fact of possession against the claims of the plaintiff that he is the true owner by virtue of purchase, succession or otherwise. This is nothing more than proof on a preponderance of probabilities.

5 CONCLUSION

To conclude our discussion, we must emphasis that the Supreme Court in Odametey v. Clocuh made it very clear that its interpretation of the true ambit of the Kodilinye principle as indicated above, is supported by sections 10-12, particularly section 11(4) of the Evidence Decree, 1975 which provides:

". . . the burden of producing evidence requires a party to produce sufficient evidence so that on all the evidence a reasonable mind could conclude that the existence of the fact was more probable than its non-existence."

The Supreme Court made the position very explicit when it held per Taylor JSC (Francois and Wuaku JJSC concurring) that:

". . . the burden of persuasion in a civil case is as indicated in section 10(2) of NRCD 323 the establishment of the existence or non-existence of a fact by a preponderance of the probabilities.'''

The Supreme Court also by its subsequent unanimous decision in Akrofi v Otenge19 (a decision also supported by Francois JSC) and decided after Banga has clarified the legal position. In this case, the plaintiff, in his capacity as the head of family, sued in the High Court, Koforidua, for a declaration of the family's title to a farm sold in execution of a judgment which had been obtained against a member of the family. The High Court found for the plaintiff but on appeal, the decision was reversed by the Court of Appeal. On a further appeal by the plaintiff, the Supreme Court held (dismissing the appeal) that in an action by a head of family for a declaration of the family's title to property sold in execution of a judgment debt obtained against a member of the family and for setting aside the sale, the courts must examine the evidence with extreme caution. In such a situation, the Supreme Court held that a high degree of proof must be looked for, but on a balance of probabilities. It was thus held that on the evidence as a whole, the probabilities were that the farm belonged to the member of the family who was only hiding behind the head of family to get back the auctioned farm.20

If as held by Francois JSC in the Court of Appeal in Banga v Djanie and supported by the subsequent Court of Appeal decision in Adzo v Tay (supra) there is a higher burden of proof in land suits as an exception to the burden of proof in all civil cases, ie proof on a balance of probabilities, that view of the law appears (as earlier suggested) to be founded on the law prior to the promulgation of the Evidence Decree, 1975. In the absence of any proffered policy justification for the requirement of a higher burden, ie proof beyond reasonable doubt, the Francois principle may be considered as having been given per incuriam It does not (with utmost respect) reflect the present law on the issue of the burden of proof in land suits.21 The Court of Appeal in Banga v Djanie was bound to follow the previous decision of the Supreme Court in Odametey v Clocuh on the burden of proof in land suits and as subsequently affirmed in Akrofi Otenge.
