BASIS FOR AWARD OF FINANCIAL PROVISION [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 49—69

Professor Ekow Daniels W C

 

*

In the exercise of its discretion to award financial provision under section 20 (1) of the Matrimonial Causes Act, 19711 a court has powers under section 26(1) to rescind any disposition of any assets or property that has been made by either party to a marriage with the intention of defeating any claim for financial relief.  The Supreme Court, by a majority of three to two, so held in Ribeiro v Ribeiro2 in reserved judgments when dismissing an appeal by the appellant-husband from the unanimous decision of the Court of Appeal which had affirmed the decision of the High Court. An analysis of the judgments will show that at the highest level, judicial opinion on the powers of the court to award financial provision is so sharply divided that the intention of the legislature must be held in question. The view of François JSC3 is that:

". . . the time has come for the law-makers to take a second look at the Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971 and spell out their intentions in a language that would admit of no ambiguity or equivocation."

Much of the case was concerned with the economics of matrimonial breakdown rather than with divorce simpliciter. The petitioning wife, Barbara Ribeiro was married to her husband Charles Ribeiro in London in 1953.  There were four children of the marriage.  In 1980 she filed a petition for the dissolution of the marriage together with a prayer for financial provision.  The petition for divorce was uncontested and in January 1981 she was granted a decree of divorce by the High Court.  In March 1981, she instituted proceedings for financial relief.  "She claimed for the return of her properties, maintenance and provision for accommodation."

After some delay, the wife in 1984 started to give her evidence.  During the pendency of the proceedings it came to light that the husband had procured within the space of one day the execution of a deed of gift of some of the houses owned by him including "Haulage House" to one of his mistresses whom he had later married after the dissolution of his first marriage.

At the conclusion of the case the learned trial judge made the following findings4:

"From the evidence before me I am satisfied that the respondent [husband] owns more than ten houses, which were all acquired during the subsistence of the marriage.  Even though there is no direct evidence of contribution by the applicant for the acquisition of those houses, I think her devotedness to her duties as a housewife and her invaluable services rendered to the respondent [husband] in looking after the home, the children, some of whom were not children of the marriage, entitled her to the enjoyment of the houses which her husband acquired during the marriage."

In the exercise of his powers under section 26 of the Matrimonial Causes Act, the learned trial judge set aside the disposition of "Haulage House" to his mistress on the ground that it was made not in good faith but with the intention of defeating any claim for financial relief hence he ordered that that house should thereafter be conveyed by the husband to his former wife "by way of settlement" or as part of financial provision.  The court in addition awarded the former wife a lump sum payment of ¢150,000 as financial provision.

From this judgment the husband appealed to the Court of Appeal contending that the learned trial judge erred in law when he made an order rescinding or cancelling the conveyance by which the respondent-husband had conveyed his house on Ring Road known as "Haulage House" to his present wife and ordering that the said house be given to the wife-petitioner.

Counsel for the appellant-husband argued that in dealing with an application for financial provision and accommodation by the wife against her husband who was shown to be the owner of as many as ten houses acquired by his own resources, there was no legal basis which justified the court below to set aside the disposition of one particular house to his newly married wife and order its conveyance to his former wife, simply because the transfer of that house was executed during the pendency of the application.

It was also submitted on behalf of the appellant that the liability of a spouse to maintain the other after a decree of divorce or nullity was a general rather than a particular responsibility to provide adequate financial relief.  Further that there was no evidence of intention on the part of the husband to defeat any claim by the wife for financial provision even though the disposition complained of occurred during the pendency of the application.  Counsel also argued that the husband had ample fortune, he had the ability and capacity to satisfy any order which the court might make, therefore, the transfer of three houses including "Haulage House" to his recently acquired wife could not be fraudulent.  Counsel maintained further that the power of the court to order either party to the marriage to convey to the other immovable property as settlement was limited to such property as stood in the name of that party and not one which was registered in the name of the new wife.  Consequently, it was submitted that the learned trial judge exercised his discretion wrongly or arbitrarily by invoking section 26(1) of the Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971 to rescind the transfer of "Haulage House" because that house was disposed of in good faith to the new wife who had provided valuable consideration.

The arguments made on behalf of the appellant-husband did not find favour with the Court of Appeal which dismissed the appeal and affirmed the decision of the High Court.5 Abban JSC delivered the judgment of the court which was concurred in by his brethren Osei-Hwere and Lamptey JJA.

Firstly, on the question whether the trial judge was justified in law to rescind the disposition of "Haulage House" to the new wife, the Court of Appeal expressed its satisfaction that the order for rescission was proper.  The Appeal Court found several instances of misconduct on the part of the appellant-husband to justify the setting aside of the disposition of "Haulage House' to his new wife which his lordship observed was "done in bad faith and with the sole intention of removing that particular house out of the reach of the wife-petitioner."  It was also clear to the court that the appellant knew all along that there was the possibility that the court below might order him to settle that property on his former wife.  In its view the disposition of "Haulage House" to the second wife was a hoax, in the sense that even after the transfer the appellant continued to have legal dominion over it by receiving rent income for his own use.  The court in its reasoning found much support from a long line of English authorities dating back to the sixteenth century.6

Secondly, on the issue of the manner in which the trial judge exercised his discretion to award financial provision, the Court of Appeal rejected the appellant's contention that financial provision should be awarded on the basis of the financial contribution made by a spouse towards the acquisition of the property in question.  In the view of the court, the issue was not whether or not a party to a marriage made a substantial financial contribution; it was simply whether she was entitled to be provided with a decent accommodation as part of financial provision. As the court explained, "it was a matter which included the living standard of the parties, their financial circumstances and conduct before and after the marriage." The court added that the applicant-wife devoted all her energy and life to serve the appellant, looked after the home and cared for the children of the marriage and those born outside wedlock during the cohabitation period of 34 years.  There was ample evidence of the appellant's properties which included ten houses, one in the United Kingdom, stocks and shares and a printing press as compared with nothing owned by his wife.  In view of all this, observed the court, "was it too much to ask the appellant to accommodate such a devoted wife-petitioner in just one of the several houses . . . ?"  The appeal was dismissed.

Not satisfied with the judgment, the appellant-husband appealed to the Supreme Court.  By its decision which was reviewed with the same result,7 the Supreme Court was profoundly split on the question of the power of the court to rescind a disposition made by a spouse, with intent to defeat a financial claim and also on the matters to be considered in awarding financial provision. 

Amua-Sekyi JSC delivering the majority judgment of the court which was concurred in by his brethren Adade JSC and Ofori-Boateng JA, quickly disposed of the appellant-husband's arguments relating to the powers of the trial judge to rescind a disposition if it is satisfied that it was made with intent to defeat a claim for financial provision.  Again, the submission that the jurisdiction of the court under section 20(1) of Act 367 was contingent upon proof that the recipient was either the owner of the property to be disposed of or that he or she made a substantial contribution towards its acquisition was given a sharp rebuttal in the majority judgment. In the view of Amua-Sekyi JSC the cases cited in support of that contention, notably, Cobb v Cobb,8 Pettitt v. Pettitt9 and Achiampong v Achiampong10 were irrelevant to the issue at stake.  He emphasised that the point at issue was mainly one which related to the discretionary powers of the court to award financial provision.  Adade JSC in the course of his judgment in the application for review reached the same conclusion.11

In his dissenting judgment, François JSC posed two questions for his decision, namely (a) whether the court's powers to award financial provisions "admit of any arbitrary settlement of property upon a spouse who has no vestige of a title to it, purely on the basis that the court thinks it "just and 'equitable'"  and (b) what conditions exist under section 26 of Act 367 to rescind the disposition when there is an issue whether it was made with the intention to defeat a claim for financial relief?.  His lordship vigorously maintained that section 20 of Act 367 does not pretend to entrust the courts with powers arbitrarily to re-align property rights where there is no claim to title in the recipient party.  He declared:

"I do not think also that the facts allow the wife to press with any degree of optimism an interest in the husband's properties by virtue of mere domestic services in the house."

He adopted the reasoning in Pettitt v. Pettitt.12  After citing the New Zealand case of Hofman v Hofman,13 his lordship went to great lengths to show that "unless specifically legislated for, no court would consider loose discretionary powers as conferring a right to re-distribute a spouse's property."  Turning to the instant case, his lordship felt that it was clear "that both the law and the facts do not confer any proprietary rights in the wife of any legitimate claims over her husband's properties."  Her request for one of her husband's houses, he stated could only in the context legitimately refer to a request for adequate "financial provision."  The thrust of his lordship's judgment was that the powers entrusted to the court to award financial provision in the event of divorce or nullity of a marriage were limited to the powers of making monetary award be it a lump sum payment or otherwise, for as he reasoned, nowhere does Act 367 confer on the court a power of selection in order to identify and earmark a particular house to meet a spouse's prayer for financial provision when such a spouse has no "title or an interest in that property." ln his view, therefore, both the High Court and the Court of Appeal erred in the exercise of their discretion. Firstly, on the ground that no such legislation as the English statutes culminating in the Matrimonial Causes Act, 197314 exists here; yet it was acknowledged by him that Ghana inherited its matrimonial laws from Britain and was bound by the English matrimonial laws till recently.  Secondly, Mr Justice François observed that "Haulage House" was not a matrimonial home, hence he did not see the legitimacy behind the order of rescission.  His lordship said that the courts below erred in assuming that upon the breakdown of a marriage the entire estate of either party to a marriage came under their scrutiny and suzerainty.  He questioned the finding of the courts below that there was evidence that the appellant-husband disposed of "Haulage House" with the intention of defeating the course of justice, adding that the line of English authorities relied on was inapposite since the appellant never intended to render himself a bankrupt.  His lordship finally said that in the instant case, "a lump sum award as indicated by Lord Denning in Wachtel v Wachtel15 would have been a compelling solution in a situation of this kind where the husband was demonstrably 'affluent'."

Wuaku JSC, supported François JSC with his own judgment the thrust of which was that under the Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971, the courts do not have an unfettered discretion to award financial provision so as to order the conveyance of a particular property of one spouse to the other.  In his view the Court of Appeal therefore misdirected itself on the law.

Origin of Act 367

The split decision of the Supreme Court raises several points of general interest and importance on the basis of the powers of the court to award financial provision under the Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971. The Matrimonial Causes Act, 1971 was enacted by Parliament on the recommendation of the Ghana Law Reform Commission to make divorce proceedings much easier and to give the courts a new armoury of powers to order financial provision following the breakdown of a marriage.  These objectives were derived from the current English reforming statutes at the time of its enactment.  The Memorandum accompanying the Bill published in May 1971 claimed that "the Bill is distinctively Ghanaian."  At the same time it acknowledged that it was in many ways similar to the English law on matrimonial causes.  In moving the second reading of the Bill in Parliament, the Attorney-General said16:

"The divorce law at present applicable in Ghana is the English Divorce Reform Act, 196917 which came into force on 1 January 1971. The statute effected very considerable improvement in the law relating to divorce, and adopted an enlightened and humane approach towards this difficult human problem.  The present Bill retains the progressive features of the English legislation while adapting them to meet Ghanaian circumstances."

Historically, the relationship between Ghana and England in matters of matrimonial laws dates back to 1859 when the first Divorce and Matrimonial Causes Ordinance18 which was based on English law was passed. Thereafter the jurisdiction conferred on the courts was expressed to be exercised "in conformity with the law and practice for the time being in force in England."19  But Act 367 put paid to the linkage in the sense that Ghana has by a local enactment adopted the two main English statutory reform laws. These were: The Divorce Reform Act of 1969 and the Matrimonial Proceedings and Property Act, 197020  In the words of Ormrod J21: "These two Acts, which are clearly intended to be read together form the new code of family law." There can be no doubt that Act 367 owes its origin from English legislation.  With the greatest respect, Justice François' statement that "no such legislation exists here" must be open to question.

THE NATURE, OBJECT AND SCOPE OF FINANCIAL PROVISION

The law governing property adjustment and financial provision is set out in part three of the Act.22 It was drafted in accordance with the recommendation of the English Law Commission which contained the following drafting hints23:

"(a) The distinction in nomenclature between maintenance, alimony and periodical or lump sum payment should be abolished.  All should be described as 'financial provision' except alimony pending suit which should be renamed 'maintenance pending suit.'

(b) There should be no distinction between the powers of the court in relation to husbands and wives, or petitioners and respondents.

(c) All forms of periodical financial provision, but not maintenance pending suit, should be capable of being secured, and, if secured, of being awarded for the life of the payee or until he or she remarries whichever be the shorter.

(d) All periodical financial provision should cease on the remarrying of the payee and not to be capable of being revived.

(e) The court should be empowered to award a lump sum payment not only in respect of the future but also to enable the payee to discharge liabilities reasonably incurred prior to the institution of the suit . . . and to order any lump sums to be payable by instalment."

This explains the introduction of the expression "Financial Provision" in section 43 of Act 367.

Then also under the Act the expression "either party to the marriage" is used to refer to a husband or a wife.  It is "abundantly plain that husbands and wives come to the judgment seat in matters of money and property upon a basis of complete equality."24

Section 18 of the Act directs the court to exercise any of the armoury of powers to order financial provision. They are wide-ranging and far-reaching. Sections 19 and 22 govern the award of financial provisions for a party to the marriage and/or a child of the household. Section 20 governs the power of the court to order a transfer of property as settlement of property rights or as part of financial provision, and section 21 relates to the powers of the court to ascertain ownership of the movable and immovable assets of the parties to the marriage.  In the exercise of these functions the court is armed with two important auxiliary powers under sections 25 and 26 whereby it can by order restrain either party to the marriage (a) from leaving the jurisdiction until it is satisfied that he has made adequate provision to satisfy any award the court might make; and (b) from permitting the disposition of his asset or rescind any such disposition if satisfied that it was made with the intention of defeating a financial claim.

FINANCIAL PROVISION

The operative provision is section 19.  It empowers the court, "Whenever it thinks just and equitable" to award maintenance pending suit or financial provision to either party to the marriage after it "has considered the standard of living of the parties and their circumstances."  It is, however, section 20 which affords the courts the machinery to that end.  The expression "financial provision" is defined in section 43 to include "Maintenance and all other forms of financial support to be provided by one spouse to the other or to any child of the household."

There is no doubt that the duty imposed on the court to exercise its powers under section 19 "whenever it thinks just and equitable" was to accord to it the widest possible discretionary powers to re-adjust the financial position of the parties.  The scope of sections 19 and 20 was intended to bring about a shift of emphasis like the English statute, from the old concept of "maintenance" of the wife and children by the husband to one of re-distribution of assets and, what might be called "purchasing power"25; so that in dealing with the spouses' assets the court no longer asks: "Whose is this" but rather "To whom shall this be given."26  This approach is a complete innovation.27 The shift of emphasis had to be made with the backing of legislation, because it could not be done by the evolutionary method of case law as the speeches show in Pettitt v Pettitt.28

PRINCIPLES GOVERNING COURTS' DISCRETIONARY POWERS

The criteria by which the court may order financial provision under Act 367 fall short of adequate guidance or guidelines.  It must surely have contributed to the split decision of the Supreme Court.  Judicial wisdom, however, has warned against the dangers of loose discretionary powers in a number of decided cases.  One is that the absence of guidance on the exercise of a discretionary power may be worse than any conflict of precepts.  In another case, absolute discretion has been called a ruthless master, more destructive of freedom than any of man's other inventions.29  In a third example, a wide discretionary power, like "public policy," has been described as an unruly horse, and when once you get astride you never know where it will carry you.30  It is no wonder that François JSC in Ribeiro was prompted to say that "no court would consider loose discretionary powers as conferring a right to distribute a spouse's property."31  However, commenting on the lack of guidance in section 20 of Act 367, Abban JA in Achiampong v Achiampong,32 offered a view on the intention of the legislature to have been as follows:

"It left that to the good sense and judgment of the court.  However, in exercising that discretion, the court would have to be guided by law; the discretion meant sound discretion guided by law not vague, arbitrary or fanciful."

It may be observed that under the English Matrimonial Causes Act,33 the court is directed to have regard to various criteria laid down in the Act. They include (a) the income of the parties, (b) their financial needs, (c) their standard of living, (d) their age and duration of the marriage, (e) their physical and mental conditions; and (f) their contribution to the welfare of the marriage and any contribution made by each by looking after the home or caring for the family.  A provision such as this has the advantage of minimising divergencies of legal opinion; but not all the factors mentioned are of recent origin in matrimonial causes legislation.  As long ago as 1891, when considering the scope of the powers of the court to award maintenance "as it thought fit," Lindley LJ identified in Wood v. Wood34 the following factors:

"The circumstances which have to be taken into account are (1) the conduct of the parties; (2) their position in life, and their ages and their respective means; (3) the amount of the provision actually made; (4) the existence or non-existence of children, and who is to have the care and custody of them; (5) any other circumstances which may be important in any particular case."

It is therefore submitted that the direction given to the court under section 19 to discharge its duty as it thinks just and equitable however inadequate that may be, can be made to work in the meantime according to the guidelines given by Lindley LJ.

What needs to be remembered is that these principles have varied from decade to decade.  Therefore the court is expected to take into account the human outlook of the period in which they make their decisions as regards "the conduct of the parties" and also the "other circumstances" of the particular case.  As Sachs LJ rightly observed in Potter v. Potter35: "In the exercise of any of such discretion the law is a living thing moving with the time and not a creature of dead or moribund ways of thought."

The extent to which the conduct of a party is to be taken into account as a factor to modify the result of an award has not been free from debate.  In Wachtel v Wachtel36 the English Court of Appeal went to great lengths to clarify the issue.  Lord Denning said:

"It has been suggested that there should be a 'discount' or 'reduction' in what the wife is to receive because of her supposed misconduct, guilt or blame (whatever word is used).  We cannot accept this argument.  In the vast majority of cases it is repugnant to the principles underlying the new legislation, and in particular the Act of 1969.  There will be many cases in which a wife (though once considered guilty or blameworthy) will have cared for the home and looked after the family for very many years. Is she to be deprived of the benefit otherwise to be accorded to her . . . because she may share responsibility for the breakdown with her husband?  There will no doubt be a residue of cases where the conduct of one of the parties is in the judge's words . . . 'both obvious and gross,' so much so that to order one party to support another whose conduct falls into this category is repugnant to anyone's sense of justice.  In such a case the court remains free to decline to afford financial support or to reduce the support which it would otherwise have ordered."

Similar sentiments had been expressed by Mr Victor Owusu, then the Attorney-General during the consideration stage of our Matrimonial Causes Bill.37 He said:

"We would expect a certain standard of decorum from the woman whilst she is living on the charity of the former husband.  I think it is unwise to allow the woman to continue to draw this permanent alimony in spite of this open misconduct.  When it has become clear that the woman is flirting openly with a gentleman or gentlemen, it will be proper for the paying party to go to the Court . . . to say that because of certain circumstances the Court should rescind or vary such order for the payment of the alimony."

But short of cases falling into this category the power of the court is a financial, not a moral exercise. Conduct is only relevant where it would be inequitable to disregard it.38

APPLICATION OF THE RULES

(a) Maintenance Pending Suit

The court may award to either party maintenance pending suit formerly known as alimony.  If it is applied for under section 19, it will usually be awarded when either party to the marriage has filed their affidavits of income and property pursuant to the Rules of Court.39  Generally, the amount to be awarded should be sufficient to bring the income of the applicant, if any, up to one fifth of the joint incomes of the husband and wife.40  In present-day circumstances, the amount would be greater.

(b) Financial Provision on Divorce and Nullity

The power to award financial provision to either party to the marriage on a decree of divorce or nullity depends entirely on the statutory provisions in sections 19 and 20. Firstly, in the exercise of its discretion the court must "think it just and equitable to do so."  Secondly, it has to consider what may be deemed to be a reasonable provision having regard to "the standard of living of the parties and their circumstances."  It must surely be the case that the primary duty for the court is to exercise its powers so as to place the parties ". . . in the financial position in which they would have been if the marriage had not broken down and each had properly discharged his or her financial obligations and responsibilities towards the other."41

Admittedly the intention of the legislature is merely implicit in Act 367; but the common legislative intent of ensuring that a spouse is given a reasonable provision from the family assets is explicitly endorsed by section 13 of the Wills Act, 197142 where it is provided that "when an application is made by a surviving spouse the High Court may, taking account of all relevant circumstances and notwithstanding the provisions of the will, make reasonable provision ... for the needs of such spouse."The provision includes the payment of a lump sum or periodical payment and a grant of an estate or interest in immovable property.  A similar intent is discernible from the Intestate Succession Law, 198543 which "aims at giving a larger portion of the estate of the deceased to his wife and children." In the light of the two analogous examples, it is submitted that financial provision should be awarded on the basis that a wife forms part of the husband's economic activity.44  This has been the case for centuries.  One may well echo a fourteenth century old rhetoric45: "When Adam delved, and Eve span, who was then a gentleman?"  In relatively recent times, Sir Jocelyn Simon, President of the Probate, Divorce and Admiralty Division defended the modern approach to the financial responsibilities of husband and wife with these reasons46:

"In the generality of marriages the wife bears and rears children and minds the home. She thereby frees her husband for his economic activities.  Since it is her performance of her function which enables the husband to perform his, she is in justice entitled to share in its fruits."

TYPES OF ORDERS

(1) Periodical Payments

An order for periodical payments, for example on a monthly basis or by instalments is usually made after a decree of divorce or nullity in cases where there are no resources other than the current income of the parties to the marriage.  They are usually unsecured and they can last until the remarriage of the recipient spouse or for the joint lives of the spouses.  Section 27 empowers the court to vary or rescind any such order as it thinks fit.

Where the court has reason to believe that a party ordered to make a payment or payments may be unwilling or unlikely to pay, it may order that party to give reasonable security for any payment or payments ordered.47  The essence of an order for secured payment is to ensure that the person against whom an order is made is to provide assets movable or immovable which shall be charged with the payments.  One advantage over unsecured periodical payment is that the order can be enforced even if the paying spouse disappears or ceases to earn.48

The quantum of the award is determined on the principles outlined in Part III of Act 367.  The court must perform its duties under the Act and consider each of the relevant factors mentioned therein, bearing in mind the result of applying "the one-third rule."49  Sir Jocelyn Simon P tried to state the underlying judicial principles as follows50:

"In Stibbe v. Stibbe51 Lord Hanworth MR described the 'rule of one-third' as 'a sound working rule' . . . yet 'not an absolute rule' and again as 'a guide.' The reason why the 'rule of one-third' so often worked out soundly and fairly was because in a typical case the court was concerned with three groups of needs, Ú those of the wife, those of the husband and those of the children for whose support the husband was liable."

It gives a very useful starting point but it is in no way a rule of law.52 Lord Denning MR observed in Wachtel v Wachtel53 that in spite of criticism". . . it has retained its attraction for a very simple reason: those who have to assess maintenance must have some starting point.  They cannot operate in a void.  No better starting point has yet been suggested than the one-third rule."

This approach was adopted by Cecilia Koranteng-Addow J. in Clerk v. Clerk.54  She then explained the method of assessment which is reported as follows:

"In assessing maintenance, by way of financial provision, the method which had been adopted and used by the courts was what has been described for convenience as the 'one-third rule.' Under that method the income of both parties should be added and divided by three and the dividend should be used as the starting point in determining what the husband must pay to the wife."55

(2) Property Settlement Orders

It is provided by section 20(1) and (2) of Act 367 that the court's armoury of powers can be exercised in one of two ways:

(a) it may order either party to pay to the other party such sum of money as settlement of property rights or as part of financial provision; or

(b) it may order the transfer or conveyance of movable or immovable property of one party to the other party in order to achieve a re-adjustment or re-distribution of the family assets.

(a) Lump Sum Order

The power of the court to order a lump sum payment came into force in Ghana in 1963.56  It is unlike periodical payment order which can be rescinded or varied from time to time.  But in many respects it is similar to a property settlement order; for section 27(2)(b) stipulates that where a decree provides for a money or property settlement in lieu of financial provision and that settlement has been executed, the court shall not have the power to review or vary the order subsequently in the event of the remarriage of the payee or after the death of the payer. This view of the law was adequately explained in Ribeiro by Adade JSC with these words:

"But where payment is made in gross or in a lump sum, then surely section 28 becomes inoperative, it cannot affect, as it were, the 'unspent portion' of such lump sum payment, already in the hands of the recipient."57

Even though the circumstances under which it can be ordered are various, in Wachtel v. Wachtel58 Lord Denning was able to restate a few of the principles as follows:

"One thing is, however, obvious.  No order should be made for a lump sum unless the husband has capital assets out of which to pay it Ú without crippling his earning power. Another thing is this: when the husband has available capital assets sufficient for the purpose, the court should not hesitate to order a lump sum.  The wife will then be able to invest it and use the income to live on. This will reduce any periodical payments; or make them unnecessary.  It will also help to remove the bitterness which is so often attendant on periodical payments.  Once made, the parties can regard the book as closed.  The third thing is that, if a lump sum is awarded, it should be made outright."

In adopting these principles, Sarkodee J in Aikins v. Aikins59 added these words:

"Such a sum when granted is not affected by subsequent remarriage and it is not reduced because a wife is likely to re-marry.  But it is not without its drawbacks from the point of view of the recipient.  For example it is an order made once and for all and cannot be varied when circumstances change.  The payee cannot go back to the court and ask for more."

These observations were endorsed by Adade JSC who read the majority judgment of the Supreme Court in Ribeiro after the application for a review.

(b) Property Adjustment/Settlement Order

Section 20(1) empowers the court to order the transfer of the property of either party to the marriage to the other party either as settlement of property rights or in lieu thereof as part of financial provision.  Here the court will be concerned with the assets of either spouse on the basis of what would be a just and equitable re-adjustment of the property of the spouses.60  The nature of the power to order transfer of property is similar to the power to award a lump sum. It is not limited to property acquired during marriage it includes property in possession and reversion.61  It is often awarded as an alternative order to a lump sum payment.  The alternative order of settlement of property though not often used may be made in order to secure the payment of periodical payment orders.  In such a case the ownership of the property need not change hands.

(3) Ascertainment of Property Rights

In Ribeiro, the Supreme Court was sharply divided in its opinion on the question of property rights between the spouses.  The primary issue of contention was whether section 20 of the Act could be used to re-allocate the property of either spouse.  The majority decision expressed an affirmative answer whilst the minority view was vehemently opposed to that idea.

It needs to be said that the power of the court to ascertain the ownership of the property of either spouse is contained in section 21 of Act 367.  It states:

"When a decree of divorce or nullity is granted, if the court is satisfied that either party to the marriage holds title to movable or immovable property part or all of which rightfully belongs to the other, the court shall order transfer or conveyance of the interest to the party entitled to it upon such terms as the court thinks just and equitable."

It is this section which preserves the long established principle of separation of property in our laws.  It connotes that "married people have no community of goods, but each hath his or her particular property."62  Further, that each spouse retains what he or she acquired before and during marriage; that each can freely dispose of that property and be entitled to the income therefrom.63  This principle which was of customary law origin was given statutory recognition by the Married Women's Property Ordinance64 which declared the right of a married woman to hold and deal in property independent of any husband to whom she might be married.

From its wording and context, the court when adjudicating under section 21 should have to be guided by legal principles which were developed under the English Married Women's Property Act of 1882, rather than by case law which has grown out of the Matrimonial Proceedings and Property Act, 1970. What would be in dispute under section 21 is whether a particular asset or property was in fact acquired by the husband, or the wife, or by both of them jointly.

Disputes such as these were under English law resolved by reference to the Married Women's Property Act, 1882.  Touching on that Act, Lord Morris said65:

"The procedure was devised as a means of resolving a dispute or a question as to title rather than as a means of giving some title not previously existing.  One of the main purposes of the Act of 1882 was to make it fully possible for the property rights of the parties to a marriage to be kept entirely separate. There was no suggestion that the status of marriage was to result in any common ownership or co-ownership of property.  All this in my view, negatives any idea that section 17 was designed for the purpose of enabling the court to pass property rights from one spouse to another.  In a question as to the title to property the question for the court was—'Whose is this' and not—'To whom shall this be given'."

This was the line of approach adopted in the dissenting speeches of François and Wuaku JJSC in Ribeiro.  It has to be remembered that the wife-petitioner in that case did not make a claim of title to any property.  She asked for financial provision and accommodation.  So the court did not have to ask itself the question "Whose is it" but rather "To whom shall this be given."

(4) Avoidance of Dispositions

Under section 26(1) of Act 367 the court has power to restrain either party to the marriage from permitting the disposition of his or her assets or to set aside any such transfer of assets if it is satisfied that such a transaction is not for valuable consideration and is intended to defeat claims by a spouse for financial provision.  It is important to stress that subsection 2 confers the power of the court on any asset of either spouse.  Consequently, it can make any order affecting the assets so as to preserve or maintain that asset or property while the suit is pending in the court.  It must be obvious that any attempt by a spouse by any enforceable contract to fetter the future discretionary power of the court would be rightly interpreted as a step intended to usurp the power of the court.  In Kemmis v Kemmis,66 for example, where the judge found that a husband had procured the execution of mortgage to a bank in order to defeat a financial claim by the wife, he observed that the bank knew of that design and was therefore not entitled to possession.  It is submitted that the majority judgment of the Supreme Court stated the correct law on this point, but much of the controversy would have been avoided if an application for the interim protection of the assets or property of the spouses had been made together with the claim for financial relief.

CONCLUSION

The scope of the powers given to the court under Act 367 to award financial provision in the event of a breakdown of a marriage is wide and far-reaching.  The supporting provisions were enacted to ensure an equitable re-adjustment of the property of partners in marriage in much the same way as the freedom of testamentary disposition has been cut down by section 13 of the Wills Act, 1971 (Act 360) and the Intestate Succession Law, 1985 (PNDCL 111).  It is now regarded as a question of a fundamental human right; this is why the 1979 Republican Constitution ordered that "No spouse may be deprived of a reasonable provision out of the estate of a spouse whether the estate be testate or intestate."67  The matrimonial laws of the country cannot be expected to swim against the tide of socio-economic reality.  Whether the objective should be achieved by means of further legislation or judicial activism is a matter which calls for serious consideration.
