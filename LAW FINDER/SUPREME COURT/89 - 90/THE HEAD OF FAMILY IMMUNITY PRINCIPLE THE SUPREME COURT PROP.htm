THE HEAD OF FAMILY IMMUNITY PRINCIPLE: THE SUPREME COURT PROPOSES, THE PNDC DISPOSES [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 361—369

Woodman Gordon R.

 

Early in 1985 another great judicial battle was fought over the accountability at customary law of the head of family. It was won by the proponents of the “immunity principle,” according to which the head was immune from actions for accounts. But the opposition was not routed, and at the end it seemed quite possible that in subsequent litigation it would rally, deny the conclusive force of the judicial decision, and fight again. Then later in the year the legislature, a peace-keeping force which (unlike some) has peremptory and irresistible authority, intervened and concluded the contest by abolishing the immunity principle.

In Hansen v Ankrah1 the plaintiffs were the head of two of the three sections of the Mantse Ankrah family of Accra. The first defendant was the head of the entire family.  He had received two large sums of money on behalf of the family. He had failed to pay one of these into a designated bank account to the credit of the family as, the plaintiffs alleged, the family meeting had instructed. The other he had paid into this account, but he had then, the plaintiffs claimed, made unauthorised disbursements from it. The plaintiffs sued for: an order that the head account for all the sums he had received on behalf of the family; an order that he pay into the designated family bank account such sum as the accounts might reveal him to have received for the family; and a declaration that he was liable to be removed from office for breach of trust.  Before the hearing of the suit they applied for interlocutory orders. These were to require that the sum which had not been paid into the family bank account be paid in forthwith, and to restrain until judgment any disposition of family funds or other property. The plaintiffs claimed that these orders were necessary to ensure the preservation of the property during the pendency of the action. The interlocutory application was opposed on the basis of a preliminary defence objection to the action on the ground that the first defendant as head of family could not be sued for an account by the plaintiffs. The High Court granted the plaintiffs’ application. The Court of Appeal unanimously upheld the defence submission, set aside the interlocutory orders, and, on the ground that in consequence of this holding the substantive action was bound to fail, dismissed the action. The Supreme Court dismissed an appeal by the plaintiffs, by a bare majority of three (Sowah JSC and Francois and Mensa Boison JJA) to two (Apaloo CJ and Taylor JSC).2 This result may not have been conclusive of the law. Taylor JSC urged that the Supreme Court when differently constituted at a later date should feel free to decide the question differently, since constitutionally the court was empowered to depart from a previous decision when it appeared right to do so.3 This note consists of a few comments on the decision of the Supreme Court.

There were considerable and detailed differences between the members of the court regarding both the effects of the authorities and the policies to be favoured. But there was a consensus on a fairly precise definition of the immunity principle, the correctness of this principle being the legal question in issue. The general notion of the principle as concerning the accountability of the head of family needs to be defined in five respects.

Firstly, the issue concerned the particular mode of accountability by court order.

Even the judgments of the majority, propounding the immunity principle, accepted that a head of family might be under a duty to render accounts at a family meeting. Thus Sowah JSC said: “In our view, there is no question that a head of family is accountable to the family at a family meeting for his stewardship.”4 It was implied that this undoubted duty was enforceable by the further rule that a head of family who failed to perform the duties of the position could be removed from office by the family.5

Secondly, the issue did not concern the liability generally of the head to be sued by family members. The minority showed, and the majority did not question the contention that, for example, the head could be sued for a declaration that a specified property was family property and not the head’s individual property. The issue concerned the liability to be sued for an account.

Thirdly, the issue was not whether a person who was a head of family was in all circumstances immune to an action to account. Strong authority holds that a person who is a head of family is so liable in respect of property which he or she holds on behalf of a person or group other than the family of which he or she is the head. For example, property vested exclusively in a certain family or group may be placed in the custody of the head of a wider lineage of which the owning family or group is a segment. The head of family who is entrusted with such property is not the head of the owning family, nor even a member of it, and has no discretion to allocate to himself any benefit thereof. Hence particular restrictions and liabilities may be imposed on him, including a liability to be sued for an account by those beneficially entitled.6 Similarly a head of family may be liable to be sued for an account if the head or family have bound themselves contractually to pay a specific proportion of family funds to any person, such as the child of a deceased male member of a matrilineal family.7 While authorities on these situations were cited in the present case, it is submitted that they were relevant only as analogies. The issue was limited to the possible immunity of the head in respect of property vested in that family of which he or she was the head, and apart from any liability under any social contractual obligations.

Fourthly, the issue concerned an action by principal members of the family. It was generally accepted that other members, ie junior members, were not empowered to sue. Thus Taylor JSC, while arguing against the immunity principle, stated that it would not normally be open to junior members to bring an action. On the other hand, it appears that the majority, in accepting the immunity principle, would not have restricted it to cases, such as the present, where only a majority of principal members sued. They would have applied it also to bar an action brought by the unanimous consent of all the principal members. This may be concluded from the nature of the reasoning which prevailed. For example, Francois JA (as he then was) stated approvingly: “Sarbah’s statement of the customary law, rules out entirely any legal suit against a head for account while in office.”

Fifthly, the issue concerned an action brought at a particular stage in the family dispute. Even the minority of the Supreme Court who would have allowed the action indicated that they would not have done so had they not been satisfied that serious attempts had first been made to settle the dispute and secure a statement of accounts within the family. Among the majority, Francois JA suggested that the immunity principle would not extend to bar an action if it was necessary to preserve family property and attempts to act within the family had been obstructed; although the other two members of the majority gave no indication that they would permit an action at any stage prior to the removal of the head from office. After deposition the action is possible, it is universally agreed, but of course at this time it is no longer an action against a head of family.

Thus the issue of the immunity principle on which the Supreme Court was divided, strictly defined, was whether the head of family was immune to a suit brought by all or a majority of the principal members of the family for an order to present in court a statement of account in respect of property vested in that family after attempts to secure an accounting outside court had failed. The arguments on this issue were of two main types.

First, there was extensive and detailed argument over the effect of the authorities. Disagreement was expressed as to which view was supported by a preponderant weight of authority, either for the customary laws of particular ethnic groups, or for Ghanaian customary laws generally.8 It is not possible to summarise here the differing analysis of the many cases and texts. The judgments need to be read by any one wishing to make a full appraisal of the authorities.9 However, even if the authorities had been unanimously on one side, they could not have absolutely bound the Supreme Court to decide the issue in one way. Statute provides: "The Supreme Court shall not be bound to follow the decisions of any other court.10

Secondly, therefore, there was argument as to the policies which were appropriate for the judiciary to follow in establishing the law. All members of the majority attached weight to the policy of maintaining the authority of the head of family on account of the many socially important functions of the head as representative of the family. Thus Sowah JSC argued:

“[T]he core of Ghanaian society is the family and . . . the head symbolises the hopes and aspirations of that family . . .  [I]t is irreverent and improper for a member to bring him to court while he occupies the office . . . The head of family must of course have the capacity to handle the routine orders and disorders of the daily life of the family.”

Related to this was the policy of ceding to the family a degree of autonomy and self-government. Francois JA applauded the conclusion “that the courts should not arrogate to themselves decisions which customary law and the common law have for centuries reposed in the families themselves.” Among the majority, Francois JA, who found near unanimity in past authority for the immunity principle, emphasised the policy of maintaining continuity in judicial decision-making.11 However, the other members of the majority did not rely on advocacy of any broad judicial policy, either of continuity or of innovation12

The dissenting members of the court advanced policy arguments for rejecting the immunity rule. Apaloo CJ, unlike Taylor JSC, seems to have concluded that the past authorities gave some support to the rule. But they both argued that the policy of maintaining continuity in the law should carry less weight than other policies which supported the head’s liability. Thus Apaloo CJ held: “Refusing to follow an obviously unjust precedent cannot rightly be construed as judicial legislation.” Noting that the authorities from some ethnic groups supported a principle of liability to account, the minority judgments adverted to the policy of ensuring uniformity in the various customary laws, implying that this might require the overruling of some which indorsed the immunity principle.13 They attached weight also to the policy that no wrong, including a breach of trust by a fiduciary, should be without a judicial remedy; and to the policy of infusing probity and accountability into public and private life.14

This Comment will not advance yet another theory of the content and rationale of the law on this issue. But a suggestion may be put as to the nature of the judicial function in this case. Much discussion, both practical and academic, has assumed that the function of the courts in the determination of questions of customary law consists of the discovery, recognition and application of the practised customary law. Any divergence between “practised customary law” and “judicial customary law”15 is avoidable and to be avoided. It is suggested that this assumption is based upon a fundamental misconception of the process. Customary rules which structure social life outside the courts cannot, by their very nature, be “recognised” and “applied” by courts without change, because they are not rules for the guidance of the courts. Thus in the present instance the social practices outside the courts constituting the relations between a head of family and the principal members could not possibly, because they existed only outside the courts, include rules determining the remedies to be given by the court. At the most, evidence of social practice, for those communities in which customary adjudicative tribunals existed, might have shown whether and in what circumstances such a tribunal used its particular powers and procedures to compel a head to present a form of account. But those tribunals, their procedures and remedies were far from identical with the state courts and their peculiar powers, procedures and remedies. It follows that a state court, in deciding an issue of customary law such as whether it may issue an order for accounts to a head of family, necessarily creates a new rule16a

It does not follow from this view that the court has an unfettered discretion to create any rule it pleases. It is generally accepted in the legal profession that the customary law should be designed to promote consistently a certain body of accepted policies. Thus a court engaged in the creation of a rule may obtain firm guidance from settled rules on related issues. An examination of these will often clarify the nature of the accepted policies pursued in that area of law. This was acknowledged in the present case. The judgments advocated various policies, but all sought to show that those policies were acceptable by reference to authorities. Thus the judgments mentioned established rules on some of the issues which were outside the scope of the particular question in the case, but concerned the liability of the head of family in other respects. The judgments which cited them recognised that those authorities were not directly applicable. The implied argument was that the rule to be formulated for the present case should accord with established underlying legal principles, so that the law would promote consistently the same policies.

For example, the judgments noted that by existing authority it was clearly possible for accounts to be ordered in some cases governed by customary law. The implication was that, if this had never occurred, consistency would have required at least that weight should be given to the argument that the remedy was excluded altogether from customary law cases. However, since the remedy was sometimes available, the policy of consistency required an examination of the degree of similarity between the present issue and those of past decisions. Thus, since there was authority on the liability of a chief to be sued for an account of stool funds,16b it was thought relevant to debate both the precise effect of the authority and the closeness of the analogy between a chief and a head of family.17 So again the need for general consistency of principle was the basis of argument when Apaloo CJ and Taylor JSC contended, and Francois and Mensa Boison JJA disputed that weighty authority held the head to be a fiduciary or even a trustee, and that fiduciaries were generally liable to be sued for accounts. When arguments based on such specific and relatively similar issues emerged as inconclusive, then the judgments referred to broader and less precise principles such as those relating to accountability in public life. But still the object was to ensure consistency between the new rule and existing law, not to promote the judges' personal policy preferences.

Ultimate agreement as to the law to be laid down eluded the court. There seems to have been ineradicable doubt as to which view would be more consistent with the underlying principles of customary law at the present state of their historical development. There has been a transition from an economic order in which the lineage was the principal unit of production and consumption to an order in which the individual can secure a monetary income by individual effort, and the unit of consumption is increasingly the conjugal family. Consequently, a lower proportion of property is today held by families. This trend is reflected in rules of customary law developed by the courts. Thus the presumption in favour of family property is now relatively weak, and newly acquired property is more likely to be vested in the individual or individuals who have purchased it, and less likely to be vested in their families.18 However, it is not certain how the rules on accountability of the head can best be constructed consistently with policies related to this trend. It can be argued that, family property being less significant today, no serious harm will be done if the head is not compelled to account for its use; and that the cohesion of the family will be better maintained in the face of disintegrating tendencies if the courts refrain from intervention in internal family matters. The latter argument featured prominently in the judgment of Francois JA. On the other hand it can he argued that there is a greater probability today that a head will have divided loyalties and will be tempted to misuse family property for the benefit of himself personally, his conjugal family, or a few individual family members; and that family and general social cohesion will be maintained only by strict control of the administration of family property to ensure that all benefit. The latter argument was emphasised in the judgment of Taylor JSC.

Later in 1985 the legislature declared its own decision as to the rule which current social conditions required. The Head of Family (Accountability) Law, 1985 (PNDCL 114) effectively reverses the decision of the Supreme Court. Section 2 provides that a family member “may apply by motion to a Court for an order compelling the Head of family to render account . . . of the family property to the family . . .” Section 3 also provides that “a Court shall have jurisdiction to make an order compelling the Head of family to render account . . .”19

The right to apply for an order is limited by the condition that the court be satisfied that the applicant “had taken steps to settle the matter within the family and that all such attempts had failed.” This condition was attached by the minority of the Supreme Court in Hansen v. Ankrah to the rule they advocated of liability to actions for accounts. However, in another respect the law imposes a wider liability than had been contemplated even by the minority. The application may be brought by "any member of the family . . . who has or claims to have any beneficial interest in the property.” These words appear to be comprehend every member of the lineage in which the beneficial enjoyment of the property is vested, including junior members.20 The law is not a reaction to the Supreme Court decision. A draft had been published more than a year before, and two judgments referred to this, although not for crucial purposes.21 The enacted version differed from the draft in several respects, but these all seem to have been minor verbal matters.

One consequence of the law is that the decision in Hansen v Ankrah is no longer authority. It could be argued that the case therefore needs no attention, being at most of historical interest. However, history may provide lessons of relevance to contemporary action. From this case much may be discovered about the judicial function and the underlying principles of the customary law of the family.
