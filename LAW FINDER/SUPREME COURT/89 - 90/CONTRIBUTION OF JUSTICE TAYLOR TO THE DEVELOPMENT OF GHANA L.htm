CONTRIBUTION OF JUSTICE TAYLOR TO THE DEVELOPMENT OF GHANA LAW [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 212—252

Agbosu L. K.

 

*

INTRODUCTION

Speaking through Mark Anthony in Julius Caeser, William Shakespeare said of ancient Romans that "the evils" that they did lived after them. The good were often "interred with their bones." That statement might be true of the attitude of ancient Romans to their dead. It does not, however, seem to be a true reflection of most Ghanaians' attitude to their dead. The evils that a Ghanaian perpetrates in his lifetime are often interred in a "befitting burial” and in glowing tributes to his memory. The "fine things” which would otherwise not have been said about him in his lifetime would be highlighted at his funeral. It is a matter of joy and relief for us to be spared the feeling of uneasiness and the seeming hypocrisy that would have attended our efforts to pay tribute posthumously to this great man, Mr Justice Kobina Taylor. What follows is a genuine attempt to appraise in an objective manner, the works of  Mr Justice Kobina Taylor who is still alive, strong and kicking. It is not possible to deal with all aspects of his work but an effort would be made to highlight what we can consider to be some of his key contributions to the development of our law while on the Bench.

Attitude to Technicalities and the Binding Effect of Judicial Precedent

What stands out from Justice Taylor's legal publications is that he conceives of the law as a purposive ordering of human affairs. The adjudicative process therefore does not consist in a mere juristic mensuration. The judicial process involves a combination of logic, commonsense, experience, social, economic and sometimes political policy considerations. No doubt these factors ought to be the inarticulate major premises of most decisions of the courts. For a techno-juristic approach to judicial decision-making tends to stifle an orderly development of the law to meet the demands of a progressive society. Most often than not, common law judges, who tend to stand out as the most distinguished, such as for example, Lord Mansfield CJ, Lords Esher MR, Lord Atkin of Liversidge v Anderson1 and Donoghue v Stevenson2 fame, Denning and Chief Justice Coke himself, all did combine those factors mentioned above in the adjudicative process.

Mr Justice Taylor falls within the class of these great men. Like them, imbued as he is with a keen sense of justice, fairplay and the dynamic development of the law, Taylor did not allow himself, while on the Bench, to be fettered by slavish adherence to authority. So convinced is Taylor about the need to apply and interpret the law in such a way as to prevent miscarriage of justice that he would decline to follow a precedent which would otherwise be binding on him. He does not, however, do so arbitrarily. He would find a legal justification in refusing to follow it. A close examination of some of his memorable decisions would disclose his enlightened attitude to the doctrine of judicial precedent.

When Justice Taylor was elevated from the Attorney-General's Department to the Bench as a High Court judge in 1969, he was soon to be transferred to Kumasi. There, he decided one of his memorable cases, Nkrumah v Manu,3 on 7 December 1970, just about a year after his appointment. This case stands out today as one of the most comprehensive case law exposition of the customary law of defamation; and together with Billa v Salifu ([1971] 2 GLR 87) they constitute the first and the best explanation of the choice of law rules as contained in paragraph 64 of the Courts Decree, 1966 (NLCD 84) now section 49 of the Courts Act, 1971.4 It was in this case that the first signs of his greatness as a judge became manifest. In Nkrumah v Manu, the plaintiff-respondent was called a slave by the defendant-appellant during a heated quarrel in which both parties exchanged insults. In the ensuing action for slander, the district magistrate awarded the plaintiff N¢80 which he described as special damages. It was submitted on appeal that the district magistrate's description of the sum awarded as special damages meant that he had applied the common law. Hence his decision was wrong as no special damage was proved. Apparently, the legal basis for this submission is that the common law of slander requires as a basis for liability, proof of special damage. However, it was not clear as to whether the customary law or the common law was applicable. If the former was the applicable law in which case slander is actionable per se, then the decision would be right. The issue for determination therefore was whether the customary law or the common law was applicable.

Mr Justice Taylor in response to counsel's submission, showed early indications that he would never be part of decisions that are based on form and not of substance. Dismissing counsel's argument he said:

"Clearly the district magistrate may have misdescribed the award as special damages instead of damages simpliciter. But I think it will be a travesty of justice if I were to rest my decision on this unworthy ground. If for this obvious slip I were to allow the appeal as counsel has urged me to do I can hardly lay claim thereafter to any sense of justice and fair play. Surely such bewildering technical notions of law were buried long ago with Maitland's Forms of Action. I will certainly not be a party to conjuring up their ghosts to rule us from their graves."5

In determining the applicable law, the learned judge in characteristic fashion examined the case law and the opinion of  text-writers like Sarbah and Danquah. He traced the history of the choice of law rules to section 87(1) of the Courts Ordinance, Cap 4 (1951 Rev) which Ollennu J (as he then was) interpreted in Kwaku v Addo6 to mean that "unless there is anything to the contrary, native customary law should be applied in deciding this case which is between two natives." Taylor rightly pointed out that the validity of that interpretation was unexceptionable, but with the birth of the First Republic went the demise of the Courts Ordinance, Cap 4 and its successor in title, so to speak, became the Courts Act, 1966 (CA 9), s 66 which was concerned primarily with the vital question of what law, common or customary, to apply in all courts in the country. The repeal of section 84 of Cap 4 had thus "tolled the death knell" of the principle expounded by Ollennu J (as he then was) in Kwaku v Addo and in his view as from July 1960 that principle "suffered a statutory death." Taylor ended this historic excursus into the choice of law rules by concluding that the guide as to the applicable law had to be found in the Courts Decree, 1966 (NLCD 84), para 64(1) which provides:

"64.(1) Subject to the provisions of any enactment other than this sub-paragraph, in deciding whether an issue arising in civil proceedings is to be determined according to the common law or and, if the issue is to be determined according to customary law, in deciding which system of customary law is applicable, the court shall be guided by the following rules, in which references to the personal law of a person are references to the system of customary law to which he is subject or, if he is not shown to be subject to customary law, are references to the common law:-

Rule 6. Subject to the foregoing rules, an issue should be determined according to the common law unless the plaintiff is subject to any system of customary law and claims to have the issue determined according to that system, when it should be so determined."

At the district court, the guide as to the choice of law provided above was overlooked. It could be noted that NLCD 84 came into effect five years before Nkrumah v Manu was decided. Yet the determination of issues in civil proceedings had gone on without any serious advertence to these rules as a guide to the choice of law. However, Taylor took the bold and unprecedented step in this case to criticise the decision of the Court of Appeal in Attiase v Abobbtey7 in which the choice of law rules were ignored. Taylor pointed out that the principle upon which that case was decided amounted to the revival of the principle which the legislature had discarded by re-enacting section 66 of the Courts Act, 1960 (CA 9) which became paragraph 64 of NLCD 84. Referring to the latter as the only guide to the choice of law, he said:

"I am not aware of any guide; but the judicial mind is highly conservative and in Attiase v Abobbtey (supra) nine years after the legislature had deliberately disposed of the rule, the following pronouncement, obiter, and not necessary for the decision, was made by Ollennu JA and it found support in the concurrence of Apaloo and Lassey JJA."8

The learned High Court judge, barely a year on the Bench then, quoted the relevant wrong statement of the law by the Court of Appeal and posed the question: "A correct statement of the law in 1969? Surely not." Taylor did not stop there. He proceeded to demonstrate that one of the cases relied upon by the Court of Appeal in Attiase v Abobbtey, ie Ampong v Aboraa9 was incorrectly applied. He declared that Smith J did not decide any such thing in Ampong v Aboraa as the Court of Appeal ascribed to him. If indeed he did, he would have been right at that time. After 1 July 1960, however, that decision would not be good law to be applicable to cases commenced and decided after that date. Consequently he argued, by 29 July 1969, the date of Attiase v Abobbtey  “ the new doctrine enshrined in paragraph 64 of NLCD 84 was the new principle." Mr Justice Kobina Taylor conceded the fact that the authority of the Attiase case was binding on him. But on the basis of his analysis he thought that in so far as the observation of Ollennu JA could be interpreted as part of the ratio decidendi and to mean that paragraph 64 of NLCD 84 was not the true rule and that it operated side by side with the old discarded rule contained in the supposed ratio in Ampong v Aboraa, then to the extent to which the old rule was applied, that decision was given per incuriam

Applying the new guide as contained in rule 6 of paragraph 64(1), Taylor found that there was nothing on record to show that the plaintiff had claimed to have the issue determined according to customary law nor did she show that she was subject to any system of customary law. For this reason the exception to Rule 6 of paragraph 64(1) of NLCD 84 was clearly inapplicable.

The significance of this decision lies in the signals which Justice Taylor had already sent out at that early stage of his career on the Bench, that he would be carrying out his judicial functions with candour, courage and without fear or favour. Sterile technicalities and bad precedent would not be permitted to stand in his way in the adjudicative process. That bold and courageous dissection of the judgment of the Court of Appeal which he concluded was given per incuriam and thus refused to follow it would seem to be unprecedented in the courts at the lower hierarchy. But at the end of it we have the most comprehensive exposition of the choice of law rules and an excellent explanation of the customary law of slander in a single case.

However, this forthright and bold attitude may not be looked upon favourably by some of his peers, particularly those at the upper limits of the judicial hierarchy. We thus find for example, a sharp criticism of his approach in Nkrumah v Manu by Francois J (as he then was) in Sogbaka v Tamakloe.10 There the issue before the court, as it was in Nkrumah v Manu, was whether in an action for slander a party needed to elect the system of law applicable to the proceedings and whether the words complained of, ie "thief,"  "uselessman" and "non-native of Anloga" were actionable per se. The learned judge declined to follow Nkrumah v Manu. He stated:

"It seems therefore to some extent material to ascertain whether the suit is to be determined under customary law or the common law. Following Attiase v Abobbtey (supra), I am of the view that a party need not elect which law he is proceeding with. If it appears on the consideration of the whole facts that the law applicable is customary law it should prevail."11

The learned judge then expressed the view that Taylor's criticism of the Attiase case in Nkrumah v Manu was not well-founded. Relying on some authorities, he considered it undesirable for a lower court to criticise as wrong a binding authority of a higher court. He declared:

"I have always found it difficult to appreciate the reasoning which acclaims a decision as binding and authoritative and yet subjects it to criticism. The principle of stare decisis as I understand it, prohibits such public ventilation of dissent sometimes dragged to lengths of decrial . . . If this rule is not strictly enforced the authority of a precedent may be whittled away by gradual and piecemeal judicial nibbling. The judge's duty to allay doubts with positive decisions would then have turned to the creation and multiplication of doubts.”12

It may be pointed out, that such ready adherence to authority as advocated above in the face of demonstrably wrong precedent, even in the teeth of a statutory provision which makes such precedent totally wrong, is what Taylor cannot countenance. It is a credit to Taylor that his position has been vindicated by the Court of Appeal differently constituted from the one which decided the Attiase case. In Kyei v Donkor13 the Court of Appeal thought Taylor J gave the issue of choice of law a considerable thought in Nkrumah v Manu and they found "great assistance in his consideration of the matter." The Court of Appeal after considering Taylor's criticism of Attiase v Abobbtey and quoting with approval the relevant passage from Nkrumah v Manu declared:

"We are in entire agreement with this statement of the legal position as to choice of law in an action for damages for slander in respect of cases decided during the currency of NLCD 84.”

The Court of Appeal clearly rejected the Attiase v Abobbtey and Sogbaka v Tamakloe views in an emphatic manner when it declared that a claim in terms of rule 6 must:

". . . be a positive demand or request by the plaintiff that his action be adjudicated by the system of customary law to which he is subject. Such a request cannot be said to arise by implication."14

(The emphasis is mine.)

Taylor, has since the decision in Nkrumah v Manu, been consistent in refusing to be fettered by precedents, the application of which would tend to stifle the ends of justice. But such bold, frank and lucid analysis of the superior courts' decisions after which they become demonstrably clear that the proposition of law or the principles of law upon which they are decided could not be supported in law would naturally tend to irritate his colleagues at the summit of the judicial hierarchy. The case of Harlley v Ejura Farms (Ghana) Ltd15 provides a classic example. In 1977, while still a High Court judge, he was invited to sit on the full bench of the Court of Appeal as an additional judge in order to review a decision of the ordinary bench of the Court of Appeal. That was in Harlley v Ejura Farms (Ghana) Ltd where one of the technical grounds on which the application for review was opposed as not being properly before the full bench was that the applicant had failed to comply with the Practice Direction of the Chief Justice. The objection was raised that the applicant had not stated precisely the nature of the miscarriage of justice complained about and that the applicant had failed to meet the test laid down in A/S Norway Cement Export Ltd v Addison.16 The relevant portion of the full bench's decision upon which the respondents were relying to oust the jurisdiction of the court was stated by Apaloo JA (as he then was) as follows:

"True, there is a constitutional rules committee established by article 121 of the 1969 Constitution and kept alive by section 4(b) of the National Redemption Council (Establishment) Proclamation, 1972, but this committee made no rules to govern the practice and procedure for a review. We think that the practice directive issued by the Chief Justice on 15 May 1973 in consultation with his brother justices should be deemed a modification of rules 11 and 12 of CI 13 by the court within the true intendment of section 6 of the Courts (Amendment) Decree, 1972. It is not reasonable to hold that the legislature intends that the machinery of the courts should grind to a halt, if the rules committee, through inertia, oversight or otherwise, omitted to make rules to govern the conduct of any particular legal transaction. In such situations, reason suggests that the court should be enabled to formulate and give directions as to its procedure. Rule 38 of LI 218, gives the court power to act in such cases and this is repeated in slightly altered language in rule 5 of CI 13. It is not, as implicit in the contention of the applicants, an assumption of legislative power by the Chief Justice.”17

After considering the issues raised by both counsel, the full bench unanimously agreed that the application for review should succeed. Kingsley-Nyinah and Francois JJA and Abban J were content to concur in the judgment of Azu Crabbe CJ. Taylor on the other hand while in “entire agreement with the conclusion that the application for review succeeds," had not been able to arrive at that conclusion along the same line of reasoning which the Chief Justice had adopted. He therefore set down in his own words what he conceived to be the legal foundation for his own conclusions. In the course of doing so, Taylor did not only demonstrate that both the High Court and the ordinary bench of the Court of Appeal had dealt with facts and circumstances which were wholly irrelevant to the resolution of the issues involved in the case but seized the opportunity to review and severely criticise the full bench’s decision in the Norway Cement case upon which the respondent was relying to oust the jurisdiction of the full bench.

Taylor would agree with the full bench in the latter case that the only authority to make a modification to the Supreme Court Rules, 1970 (CI 13), apart from the rules committee, is the court.

He however had considerable doubts in accepting the conclusion that the Practice Direction issued by the Chief Justice should be deemed as a modification of the Supreme Court Rules, 1970 (CI 13). He could not succeed in finding the legal foundation for the modification, and he "continued to have these painful doubts."18

He said:

"I have not found it easy to cling to my view, since the Norway Cement case is a unanimous decision of the full bench. In the circumstance, I do not think it is proper for me to gloss over my doubts without giving expression to the legal reasons which have generated these doubts. My doubts arise from two main considerations, the first of which is whether power given to the court can be equated with power in the Chief Justice exercisable in consultation with his brother judges. With the greatest respect, I find it difficult to appreciate how power exercised by the Chief Justice in consultation with his brother judges can be deemed to be the exercise of a statutory power vested in the court.

The second consideration is that the Norway Cement case is a decision of the full bench and by virtue of the provisions of article 109(3) of the Constitution, 1969, the full bench is in principle bound by its previous decisions. What then is the binding effect of a previous decision of the full bench. In my humble opinion, it is only the principle or reason in a decision which necessarily supports the conclusion of the court that is binding and not merely the conclusion.”19

In other words, if the reasons do not support the conclusion then those reasons are not binding.

Here, once again as we saw in Nkrumah v Manu, Taylor was at his best. He took the opportunity to place under scrutiny another decision of the full bench of the Court of Appeal and with such force and lucidity that it became apparent that the full bench was wrong in its decision and Taylor, the High Court judge, was right although on this occasion he was doing so as an additional judge of the full bench. In concluding that it is only where the reasons support the conclusions that these reasons could be binding on a lower court and the full bench itself, Taylor found an ally and support respectively in another great judge, Amissah JA in Asiedu v The Republic20 where he had stated:

"I take it that what binds me is not everything said by the Court of Appeal, whether it be necessary to the conclusion arrived at by the court or not. It is only that which was necessary to the conclusion that forms part of the decision which is binding."

 

After further examination of other respectable persuasive authorities on the binding effect of a ratio decidendi, he demonstrated that the Practice Direction issued by the Chief Justice on 15 May 1973 in consultation with his brother justices and upon which the respondent was relying to oust the jurisdiction of the court could not be deemed in law, as the full court had decided, to have modified rules 11 and 12 of CI 13 within the intendment of section 6 of the Courts Amendment Decree, 1972. On the basis of his analysis and the authorities, he concluded that in the Norway Cement case, the principle which enabled a power given to the court to be exercised by another body was never indicated or expressed; and that in any case, the Norway Cement case was a single and the only case dealing with the construction of statutory provisions enabling the Supreme Court Rules, 1970, to be modified. In that circumstance it was difficult to say that that case laid down any principle on the issue of the modification of the Supreme Court Rules, 1970, which could be said to be binding on a subsequent court.

Mr Justice Taylor in his characteristic thoroughness and his exhaustive and meticulous approach to judicial decision-making would leave nothing to chance. He concluded this aspect of his judgment by saying that even if the opinion he had then expressed on the validity of the Practice Direction was wrong, and therefore the latter was valid, nevertheless, it would seem to him that the respondent had misconceived the legal effect of a failure to comply with the Directive in regard to miscarriage of justice. Taylor's view was that having regard to section 3(2) of NRCD 101, which clothed the full bench with jurisdiction to review and determine any decision of the ordinary bench of the Court of Appeal "where it appears to a full bench of such court that there has been a miscarriage of justice,"  the full bench could proceed to review although the applicant did not state that he was proceeding on grounds of miscarriage of justice nor did he indicate the nature of the miscarriage of justice. The reason for this conclusion, he argued, was that the Practice Direction could not overrule the statutory provision in the parent enactment. From its very name and content, he observed it was meant to be merely directives designed to facilitate the speedy hearing of applications for review.21 The significance of Taylor's judgment in Harlley v Ejura Farms (Ghana) Ltd, among other things, is that it does portray him once again as a judge with a sound logical acumen, a thorough, frank, fearless, bold, hard working and industrious judge. His judgment does not only clarify very important and specialised areas of the law but illuminates the binding effect of judicial precedent which seems to haunt some of our timorous judges who appear to tremble at the feet of the ghosts of their creators. They seem to hold them in chains when interstitial judicial decision making is called into question.

Our discussion of Taylor's attitude to the binding effect of judicial precedent and technicalities cannot be complete without reference to his dissenting judgment in Hansen v Ankrah.22 Although a dissenting judgment, it is perhaps one of his best in terms of industry, research and logical analysis. The case concerned the scope and the extent of the head of family's immunity to account under customary law. The case involved the Mantse Ankrah family comprising three branches, ie Ankrah, Ayi and the Okanta branches. They maintained an account at the Ghana Commercial Bank, High Street, Accra. It was operated by the parties on the mandate of the family. The first respondent was the overall head of the family. Any two of them could operate the account. In 1977 an amount of ¢15,968.98 received by the family as compensation for family land compulsorily acquired by the government was paid into the account. But the overall head of the family paid into his personal account and not into the designated family account a further sum of ¢160,547.40 received from the government on behalf of the family. The two appellants in their capacities as heads of the Ankrah and the Okanta branches, brought an action in the High Court against the overall head and the head of the Ayi branch for an account of their expenditure of the ¢15,968.95, part of which they claimed the respondents had spent on Homowo festivities against their express opposition. They also sought an order for the ¢160,547.40 received on behalf of the family to be paid into the designated family bank account.

The respondents raised a preliminary objection to the propriety of the action on the grounds that they were not accountable at customary law to the appellants in court. The trial judge overruled the objection and ordered the respondents to pay the ¢160,547.40 into the family account forthwith, pending the determination substantive issue.23 He further opined that Abude v Onano24 which the respondents had relied upon for their objection was of questionable authority and besides, was inapplicable because it concerned stool and not family funds. On appeal by the respondents to the Court of Appeal against the ruling, the court held, inter alia, that the first respondent as the overall head of the Mantse Ankrah family, was not accountable to the appellants and therefore allowed the appeal and dismissed the suit against the respondents.25 The appellants appealed to the Supreme Court.

All students of the customary law are familiar with the head of the family unaccountability rule supposedly established in Abude v Onano and which is claimed to be based on the interpretation of Sarbah's statement of the law. The rule is perhaps, the most criticised aspect of the customary law. Few modern commentators on the subject have had anything to commend it. The opportunity was presented in Hansen v Ankrah for the Supreme Court to jettison the discredited doctrine, but it was frustrated. However, Taylor and Apaloo CJ would never be part of it. One of the key issues for determination was whether or not the trial High Court judge was right in his view that Abude v Onano was of questionable authority. This entailed the examination of the scope and the extent of the family head’s immunity to account under customary law.

In an exhaustive dissenting judgments in which Apaloo CJ referred, probably to every text that has ever been written on the subject and in which Taylor JSC referred to 69 cases, the latter demonstrably showed that Sarbah had never meant what was attributed to him in the decided cases and that the courts have been misinterpreting what Sarbah had written of junior members of the family's capacity to sue the head on account of squandering family funds. As an introduction to the discussion of the issues raised, Taylor JSC said:

"... it is my opinion that in the Supreme Court, we are now obliged by the fundamental law governing our decisions to operate the common law doctrine of judicial precedent in a much more modern discriminating manner, and hopefully less rigidly than the other courts in our judicial hierarchy."

The task of the Supreme Court, as Taylor pointed out, had been made easier by the fact that the Supreme Court had constitutional authority to depart from a previous decision "when it appears right to do so."26 But this was not to be.

Surprisingly, the Supreme Court, by a majority of three to two, per Sowah JSC (as he then was), Francois and Mensa Boison JJA (Apaloo CJ and Taylor JSC dissenting), confirmed and approved the family head's unaccountability rule. In doing so the majority failed to take into account the socio-economic conditions which the rule reflected in Sarbah's day and age when it was supposedly formulated; and the Supreme Court achieved this result by relying on what it considered to be a long line of authority that had crystallised into the rule. The Chief Justice, Mr Justice Apaloo would not be part of it, and Taylor JSC supported him ably. In its decision, the Supreme Court, per Sowah JSC, held that the customary law rule that a head of family, who symbolised the hopes and aspirations of the family which constituted the core of Ghanaian society, was not accountable in court to the writ of a member of the family, whether a principal or otherwise, while he still held the status of a head. This rule was reasonable and justifiable because the head should have the capacity to handle the routine orders and disorders of the family. The head of the family was however, accountable to the family at a family meeting for his stewardship. Consequently, a member of the family who considered that an incumbent head of family was incompetent, irresponsible or frivolous in the handling of family matters or was dissipating family funds could ventilate his grievances against the head at a family meeting.

Before discussing the response of the minority view we deem it appropriate to point out some logical fallacy in the reasoning by which the conclusion was reached in the majority judgment. There would seem to be no logical nexus between the family head’s capacity “to handle the routine orders and disorders of the family” and his unaccountability in court to the writ of a member of the family. How does his accountability affect his capacity to handle the routine orders and disorders? This has not been made out by the Supreme Court. This would seem to be a reductio ad absurdium.

In response to the majority decision, Taylor in his usual logical and exhaustive examination of the issue in the light of Sarbah’s statement of the law and the interpretation placed on it by the courts came to the conclusion that Sarbah never meant what had all along been erroneously attributed to him. He thought that the interpretation of the rule as stated by Sarbah was taken out of context. It is apposite here to quote what Sarbah wrote:

“The head of a family has greater power of alienation over movable than he has over immovable ancestral property and family property. He can alienate such movable property in gifts to any of the members of the family, for their education, support, or getting a wife for any member of the family.

If the family, therefore, find the head of the family misappropriating the family possessions and squandering them, the only remedy is to remove him and appoint another instead; and although no junior member can claim an account from the head of the family, or call for an appropriation to himself of any special portion of the family estate, or income therefrom arising, yet the customary law says they who are born and they who are still in the wombs require means of support, therefore the family lands and possessions must not be wasted or squandered.”27

Taylor's approach to the interpretation of the above statement was to read and inwardly digest Sarbah's discussion touching the rights, privileges and duties of an Akan head of family in respect of family property. In his analysis he had regard to the whole of Sarbah's discourse under his sub-heading of "Self-acquired Property."28 He also examined his views, inter alia, in his introductory remarks in his book.29 From the analysis of the text, Taylor formulated five principles governing the duties, rights and privileges of an Akan head of family. The learned judge found it remarkable that with the exception of the admirable judgment of Amua-Sekyi J (as he then was) in the case30 in most of the judgments in which the judges had had the occasion to rely on Sarbah's statement to elucidate the alleged immunity rule, including the judgment of the Court of Appeal in Ankrah v Hansen, the very relevant first paragraph had been consistently ignored with the result that what Sarbah meant had been emasculated and virtually made vacuous.

Having examined and analysed all the texts and the cases, Taylor came to the firm conclusion that on the authority of Sarbah, senior members of a family as joint trustees for valid alienation of family property could jointly or severally claim an account from the family head either at a family meeting or before a competent court, otherwise they could not vindicate their undoubted customary right of knowing or giving their consent to a valid alienation of family property and their right could then be violated by the head of the family with impunity. He opined thus:

"In so far as the Court of Appeal in this case repudiated the absolute right of senior members on behalf of the family to sue the head qua head for account without first removing him, its appreciation of the formulation of the rule is erroneous. The maxim ubi jus ubi remedium and the customary rule disqualifying junior members from suit is nothing but an antonymous conceptualisation of this maxim."

What must be considered as Taylor's contribution to our correct appreciation of the so-called immunity doctrine is his lucid discussion of the issue of the common law principle that a claim for account cannot be made against a person who is not an accounting party. As he explained, the principle is not peculiar to our customary laws.  "It is in fact nothing more than a trite common law principle well known to the jurisprudence of many nations, ancient and modern." He did explain that the head of family was an accounting party to the whole family or the senior members, but he was not, without more, an accounting party to the junior members, consequently the use of the word "junior" by Sarbah was deliberate and understandable. "I will venture to think that if a junior member can by virtue of the lawful dealings with the family property," he observed, "brings the head within the compass of an accounting party, he can maintain the supposed proscribed action for account against the head." Indeed if the majority had had a correct appreciation of these views, they would have recognised that there was sufficient ground for discarding the discreditable doctrine. In the opinion of the majority of the court, per Sowah JSC (as he then was) since the precedent establishing the unaccountability rule has had the blessing of the superior courts of the land, it was binding upon all courts, except perhaps the Supreme Court. In any event it had so crystallised and solidified into a rule of customary law that only legislation could change it. Francois JA (as he then was) in support of that view said:

". . . if the principle of non-accountability of the head before the courts had been accepted by hallowed practice and it no longer meets the changing circumstances of a developing nation, then the answer is a change by legislation or decree and not pre-emption by judge-made reform."

Statements such as these, coming as they do from the highest court of the land imply that the judges should fold up their hands and abdicate their duty of doing justice as between parties appearing before them, because some precedent has been crystallised and had been accepted by "hallowed practice." This should be the case even where the precedent is demonstrably unjust and does not mirror the present-day social and economic conditions. This is the sort of conclusion that is totally unacceptable to Mr Justice Kobina Taylor. As he put it in Hansen v Ankrah:

“. . . to consider appearing before a court of justice as apparently infra dignitatem is a concept which casts a slur on the honourable nature of our functions and undermines the integrity of the judicial process in a manner subversive of the judicial order prescribed in section 1(1) of PNDCL 42. The reluctance to appear becomes hypocritical when it is only invoked when the liability to render account is involved."

In Taylor's view, the operation of such a rule as the Supreme Court had thus confirmed would be incompatible with national aspirations inherent in the need to infuse probity and accountability into the public and private lives of the populace as well as the necessity to discourage corrupt practices and the abuse of power, whether discretionary or mandatory as would seem to be prescribed by sections 1(1)(c) and 61 of PNDCL 42. These were the reasons why Taylor indorsed fully the view, which as he puts it, "Apaloo CJ in his inimitable way so nobly expressed in his judgment where recognising the outrageous consequences of the supposed rule," reminded the Supreme Court of its responsibility when he said:

"We have constitutional authority to refuse to be bound by a precedent which injures the innocent, benefits the guilty and puts a premium on blatant breach of fiduciary duty. To do otherwise would be an exhibition of judicial inertia wholly indefensible in this day and age."

So indefensible indeed, did Taylor find such a rule of law that, speaking for himself, he would not be a part of any such decision that sponsored that so-called rule which "perpetuates and probably will continue to perpetuate these evils which the Chief Justice so honourably deprecates." We must point out that in our humble opinion, the position taken by Apaloo CJ and Taylor JSC would seem to be unimpeachable. The majority decision of the Supreme Court would seem to be based entirely on the need to protect the dignity and the integrity of the head of family as the "fountain of honour." But that is why the decision of the majority is difficult to explain. When the head of family is being sued for an account, his integrity and honour is called into question. The court, as the independent arbiter would seem to be the best institution to restore that dignity when an innocent head would render a clean account to clear his name and restore his honour and dignity. For a head of family to refuse to render accounts to the family in court and for the court to support and protect him from submitting himself to the judicial process is to condone wrongly, moral turpitude and dishonesty. Rather than protect the family head’s integrity, dignity and honour, it would undermine it irreparably.

It should be pointed out that the unfortunate decision by the Supreme Court in Hansen v Ankrah, is a result of techno-juristic approach to the treatment of the issues involved in the case. At that level of adjudication, a dynamic and interstitial judicial decision-making is called for. Indeed, we deem it fit to illuminate some of the factors which the majority of the court failed to take into account in their decision. Importantly, the judges failed to recognise that accounting in the sense of a formal record of the debits and credits relating to the person named at the head of the ledger account, or a balance of a specified period's receipts and expenditure is an economic concept denoting a certain historically determined level of society's socio-economic development. Accounting in this formal sense imports a highly organised and complex social system of production. Keeping records of account or book-keeping has been known to many organised societies that preceded the capitalist age. But it is under the latter system that accounting as a legal and economic concept attained maturity reflecting the higher, more complex nature of its socio-economic structures than those ante-dating it. A record of receipts and expenditure is essential for determining at a glance attainable profit margins. It also helps in projecting into the future business prospects and decisions regarding future investment policy objectives.

But the written word is a necessary condition for that level of sophistication in recording accounts. Yet in 1894 when Sarbah published the result of his research, writing could hardly have been known to Fante rural communities that were organised on the family or kinship principles of sharing under, what for want of a better term, one would call subsistence economies. Under such a socio-economic system one cannot expect the evolution of a formal accounting system as an economic or a legal category.

Therefore when Sarbah's work is relied upon today as the basis for the proposition that under the indigenous law the head of the family cannot be sued for an account, that could only be a statement of principle reflecting the social norms of Sarbah’s day and age. That would certainly be anachronistic. The absurdity of such a rule in the face of the drastic metamorphosis that Ghanaian societies have undergone under the influence of Western education and its supporting capitalist ordering of society is self-evident. It is gratifying that Apaloo CJ and Taylor JSC recognised this absurdity having regard to socio-economic changes over the last hundred years. That is why Apaloo CJ points out that afterall, "the laws must adapt itself to changing social conditions and those precedents are inapplicable to modern conditions." The dissenting judgment of Mr Justice Taylor once more reveals and demonstrates his aversion to slavish adherence to authority as was shown early in his career on the Bench in Nkrumah v Manu (supra) and later in Harlley v Ejura Farms (Ghana) Ltd (supra). As he does point out often, in its traditional setting, it is not every pronouncement of an appellate or higher court that is binding on lower courts by virtue of the doctrine of judicial precedent. It is rather the ratio decidendi of the case logically and demonstrably supporting the decision that is so binding on subsequent lower courts. It is a feather in their caps that the correctness of the dissenting opinions of Apaloo CJ and Taylor JSC have been indorsed and enacted in the Head of Family (Accountability) Law, 1985 (PNDCL 114).31(a)

Burning Desire for Justice

Mr Justice Kobina Taylor's attitude to the doctrine of stare decisis is largely influenced by his burning desire to be fair and just in the adjudicative process. This seems to constitute the main source of his inspiration to carry out exhaustive, thorough and critical examination of all cases. That is why Taylor would always find an "interesting legal proposition" or an "interesting legal issue" in every case. To him no case is simple. They are all of equal importance. It is simply a question of degree. As we have pointed out earlier, such traits could be found in all the great judges who have made monumental and indelible marks on the development of the law. Their formative influence on the law has been a result of their characteristic boldness as of the kind to be found in Kobina Taylor.

It should be pointed out that it is when a judge conceives of the law in the adjudicative process as does Justice Taylor, not merely as juristic mensuration, but as a purposive ordering of human affairs, that he can play his role meaningfully in the judicial process. He would then be in a position to make the “hard coin of social facts,” and not mere “paper legalities,” the cornerstone of his judicial decision making. The recognition of socio-economic conditions as the determinants of the essence and content of the law and the realisation that they are inter-dependent and do overlap are crucial to sound and realistic judgment. It is gratifying to note that Kobina Taylor had proved himself on the Bench to have come to grips with the problems of balancing social facts with legal rules. Changing social conditions did form the inarticulate major premises of most of his decisions as the following statement in Nkrumah v Manu exemplifies31b:

"I must say though that it is about time the customary law of slander took on a more enlightened garb and moved so to speak with the demands of modern times. When village communities were small and the written word was unknown to customary law, the only means of social and commercial intercourse was the spoken word .It was therefore essential for the preservation of the peace of those small communities that idle insults which ridicule and may therefore ruin a person be discouraged by the body politic. With the very drastic changes which modern civilisation has imposed on community and rural life throughout the country it seems to me that the law has more serious problems to tackle than silly vituperation."

Hard Work and Industry

It is often said that a genius is never born. About 90 per cent of his making is the result of hard work. Mr Justice Kobina Taylor’s achievements provide a classic example of the validity of that statement. His early life was characterised by a determined struggle to succeed in life. That determination to succeed and a burning desire to get at the truth have helped to develop in him a spirit of inquiry. He has an insatiable appetite for knowledge.

This constitutes the driving force behind his impulse for hard work and industry. This leads him on to carry out thorough research on the area of law concerned in every case. Taylor would go on a voyage of discovery throughout the common law jurisdictions – from Africa to Australia, United States of America, the United Kingdom and back to Africa - Ghana. Cases, statutes, textbooks and archival materials are hardly overlooked by him if they are relevant to the case at hand. It is that kind of painstaking search and inquiry that produced Nkrumah v Manu; Harlley v Ejura Farms (Ghana) Ltd; Hansen v Ankrah and Kwakye v Attorney-General32 and many others, including Billa v Salifu33 which we think should be examined next in some detail. The purpose of doing so is to show the illuminating and the rewarding effect of Taylor's hard work and industry.

Having been transferred from Kumasi to Tamale soon after his decision in Nkrumah v Manu, Taylor was soon to establish himself as one of the most diligent and industrious judges who no serious student of the law could ignore. This was confirmed in one of his first but important judgments at Tamale in Billa v Salifu. In that case, a district magistrate in Tamale had awarded the plaintiff N¢260 in damages against the defendant for adultery with the plaintiff’s wife. In his statement of claim at the district court, the plaintiff claimed that the action of the defendant in intentionally and unlawfully having sexual intercourse with his wife, Muniratu, whose child was under age was an action contrary to Dagomba "customary rites.”

On appeal to the High Court, the meticulous judge as Taylor was, he did not assume that Dagomba customary law was automatically applicable. He drew attention to the fact that as a condition for its application, direct evidence ought to be led in accordance with paragraph 64(1), rule 6 of the Courts Decree, 1966 (NLCD 84) showing that the plaintiff was subject to a particular system of customary law and claimed to have the issue determined according to that particular system of law. This set the stage for further elaboration, explanation and application of the choice of law rules prescribed under NLCD 84. Taylor satisfied himself that the plaintiff was subject to Dagomba customary law. The problem he faced was, however, the determination of the essence and content of that law. Making an observation on the paucity of relevant material on Dagomba customary laws, Taylor said:

"Unfortunately, and I can appreciate the difficulty of counsel, no authorities of any sort were cited in support of the propositions of Dagomba customary law which were propounded with so much confidence by counsel for the parties. I did not get the Northern Sarbahs and Danquahs and as I found myself entertaining grave doubts as to the nature of the Dagomba customary law relating to adultery and the resultant rights and remedies of an injured husband in cases of adultery, I decided after I had heard the submissions of counsel and after I had consulted a few books as well as documents in the National Archives at Tamale, that it was necessary to hold an enquiry under paragraph 65 of the Courts Decree, 1966 (NLCD 84).”34

Thus for the first time since it was enacted in 1966, Taylor became the first judge to call into service paragraph 65 of the Courts Decree, 1966 (NLCD 84). By resorting to that provision in order to determine the relevant Dagomba customary law, Taylor brought together two of the key elders of the Dagomba Traditional Area supposed to be repositories of Dagomba customary law. They were the Wula-Na, the chief linguist in the court of the Gulkpe-Na who was the Dagomba Chief of Tamale and the Zobogu-Na, a Dagomba sectorial chief of Tamale. Both men were made to appear on subpoena to give their opinions, inter alia, on the nature of adultery under Dagomba customary laws and the remedies or penalties, if any, associated with it. However, Taylor was not content to rely on their evidence alone for his decision. He therefore in his characteristic, meticulous and exhaustive manner formulated, on the basis of the traditional evidence they gave, six sets of questionnaire which were submitted to the Dagomba Traditional Council for its written opinion in accordance with the proviso to paragraph 65(3)(b) of NLCD 84. Yet that was not the end of Taylor's voyage of discovery.35

Taylor also spent his time combing the archives of Tamale. The end result of the painstaking inquiry is that for the first time in Ghana we have recorded in a case, a fairly comprehensive statement of the law on an important aspect of Dagomba family law. As a result of this we now have the knowledge that under Dagomba customary law, until a mother's child has began to walk after its delivery, it is not permissible for the husband to have sexual intercourse with his wife. This is called bia naba tuuba, meaning the child's feet have come together, ie the child is able to stand on his feet. When the child begins to walk, the first sexual intercourse which the man has with his wife is called bia zugu yagbu. The term is also applicable to sexual intercourse which an adulterer has with a man's wife after she has delivered and the baby is under age and the husband has not yet had his bia zugu yagbu. This is a taboo which customarily dissolves the marriage.

These are rules the details of which we need not go into here. They are contained in the judgment to be read by all interested persons. It is interesting to observe however that the rules and the social norms vividly reflect a system of social regulations that involve a mechanism for birth control in Dagomba society. This must be of considerable interest to anthropologists, lawyers, social and health workers alike. But of considerable interest and importance is also the discovery that under Dagomba customary law an adulterer is punished by the chief and he is required to pay what is called pan kobiga (vagina money) which is an adultery fee. This is in fact, as Taylor discovered, a quasi-criminal remedy. The idea of claiming monetary compensation from an adulterer is foreign to Dagombas. Unless the husband is seeking divorce as a result of the adultery when the dowry would be returned, no direct monetary compensation is payable.36 His lordship also referred to the bihi koba wobu, the period which must elapse before a husband should have sexual intercourse with his wife where the latter has had a miscarriage or where the child dies after birth.37

The case of Billa v Salifu stands out as one of Justice Kobina Taylor's monumental decisions that makes an invaluable contribution to the development of our law and jurisprudence. The methodical and logical analysis of the research data so painstakingly and meticulously assembled exemplifies and distinguishes Mr. Justice Taylor as a great, an excellent and hard working judge. Such qualities are rare in the so-called third world countries, where economic and social problems constitute formidable obstacles to conscientious and consistent scholarly endeavours of the kind that preoccupies Justice Taylor. These qualities of his can hardly be matched by many of his peers. Time and space would not permit case to case examination of all his epoch-making decisions. We have already seen that the qualities being ascribed to Taylor here are also stamped on such decisions as Nkrumah v Manu; Harlley v Ejura Farms (Ghana) Ltd and Hansen v Ankrah in which altogether 137 cases were considered and examined by him. It is enough to state that it is apposite to highlight further some other judgments of his that do not only mirror these traits but show his concern for individual human rights.

Concern for Individual Human Rights

Mr Justice Kobina Taylor, throughout his career as a professional lawyer, has been in close contact with the criminal law. It is not surprising that he rose through the senior grades, so to speak, to the top post of Director of Public Prosecutions in the Criminal Division of the Attorney-General’s Department. That experience and his jurisprudence have helped him to recognise the potency of the coercive machinery of the state exemplified in the army, the police force and other security agencies and the judiciary itself. He has the recognition that such a formidable machinery ought to be used cautiously lest it becomes an instrument of oppression and violation of fundamental human rights. What it would seem constitutes the basic philosophical premises of Mr Justice Taylor’s perception of the criminal process in judicial proceedings is the following statement in Appiah v The Republic38. “a criminal trial in this country as in most countries is not like a game of draughts. It is rather comparable to a battle between the accused and the state in which the state with its almost unlimited resources behind it, enjoys an advantage that no accused can ever hope to match. For this reason  we in the traditional courts have been obliged for over a century to strain hard to uphold a balance that will ensure that the accused has justice and the state apparatus is not used oppressively against him."

It is his belief that it is an important function of the courts that they should relentlessly make praise-worthy efforts to champion the cause of accused persons, to ensure the rule of law and to discourage capricious and oppressive prosecutions associated with the early dictatorial proclivities notoriously characteristic of some regimes that emerged after the Second World War.39

It is during the course of his efforts to live up to such declared policy goals of achieving a balance between the state and accused persons in the adjudicative process that his courage and boldness become evident. Provided on the basis of the evidence produced by the prosecution, he becomes convinced that the accused person is not guilty according to the law or that the benefit of the doubt tips the scales in favour of the accused, Taylor would go ahead to so declare his conclusions without fear or favour. The fearlessness and conviction with which he defends the rights of the individual citizen in the courts is exemplified in the case of Kwakye v The Republic40a which we shall examine next.

The plaintiff was until 4 June 1979, the Inspector-General of Police. He escaped from custody where he was confined since his arrest following the coup d'etat that overthrew the Supreme Military Council of which he was a member. One of the reasons given by the government as to why it overthrew the Supreme Military Council was "to purge the armed forces of corruption and graft and to restore the image of the military and to deal with the accomplices of the guilty members of the armed forces." In pursuance of this policy, it established the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Special Courts) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 3), and conferred on it jurisdiction to try persons accused mainly of economic crimes and kindred trade offences.

The evidence suggests that a number of persons were allegedly tried in absentia by this court in pursuance of that Decree. On 21 September 1979, the AFRC announced on the radio that 68 persons had been tried in absentia and sentenced by the special court. On 13 October 1979, the national newspapers published the names of 51 persons who were said to have been tried in absentia. The plaintiff was listed as one of twenty-five persons who were sentenced to 25 years' imprisonment each. The plaintiff filed an action at the Supreme Court seeking a declaration that he was never tried, convicted or sentenced by the special court established under the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Special Courts) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 3), and that the purported sentence of 25 years' imprisonment imposed on him was void and of no effect.

When the case was filed the Attorney-General sought to have the suit dismissed in limine on two grounds, one of which was to pray in aid article 15(2) and (3) of the transitional provisions of the Constitution, 1979. It was argued that those provisions forbade the Supreme Court from exercising jurisdiction in that sort of case. That provision states:

“(2) For the avoidance of doubt it is hereby declared that no executive, legislative or judicial action taken or purported to have been taken by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council or by any person in the name of that Council shall be questioned in any proceedings whatsoever, and, accordingly it shall not be lawful for any Court or other tribunal to make any order or grant any remedy or relief in respect of any such act."

The Supreme Court was not satisfied that the Attorney-General had provided enough factual basis for praying in aid the above quoted provision as he had failed to file a statement of his case or otherwise provided any evidence in compliance with rule 48 of the Supreme Court Rules, 1970 (CI 13).Therefore in its unanimous ruling on 22 March 1981, the Supreme Court said, inter alia, that40b

“... the defendant who claims that this court's jurisdiction is ousted by this provision must provide a factual basis for it. We think that the defendant must produce facts which show that the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council took or purported to have taken judicial action against the plaintiff."

The Attorney-General then filed within the seven days as ordered by the Supreme Court, the alleged factual basis for praying in aid the ouster clauses relied upon. Such statement consisted of the production of both oral and documentary evidence with a view to showing that judicial action or purported judicial action within the true intendment of section 15(2) of the transitional provisions of the Constitution, 1979 had been taken against the plaintiff and that the Supreme Court was constitutionally forbidden to assume jurisdiction. The task of the Supreme Court, therefore, was the examination of the facts or evidence provided by the defendant so as to determine whether or not they support the claim that a judicial action or a purported judicial action was taken on behalf of the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council.

An example of the oral evidence produced by the defendant was the testimony of Squadron Leader Segbefia. The latter testified that he was present at Burma Camp where the charges against the plaintiff were read from a file which contained the dossier. He said the court of about four or five members conferred for about twenty to thirty minutes and thereafter handed down a verdict of guilty against the plaintiff. The court then said that it would communicate its decisions to the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council for approval. (This is a requirement of section 2(2) of the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Special Courts) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 3.) According to that witness the proceedings were recorded and would normally have been forwarded to the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council – the convening authority. The other testimony was by Flt-Lt J J Rawlings. In substance, it was that the "Armed Forces Revolutionary Council received and confirmed the sentences passed by the special court." He could not be specific on details but he said if the plaintiff’s name appeared on an official list issued by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council's Press Secretary, then the plaintiff must have been tried. The next witness was Captain Mfojoh who was the Press Secretary to the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council and was responsible for issuing press releases. He produced the press release in which the plaintiff’s name appeared with a sentence of 25 years. Apart from the evidence thus so far produced only the warrant of commitment to prison was the additional evidence produced as constituting the factual basis for proving that a judicial action was taken or was purported to have been taken within the meaning of section 15(2) of the transitional provisions of the Constitution, 1979.

It should be pointed out that none of the three persons, Segbefia, J J Rawlings or Mfojoh had any personal knowledge of the facts alleged as constituting the judicial action or the purported judicial action taken. Segbefia never claimed that he saw the file from which the charges were read. He could not even recollect what the charges were nor the provisions under which the plaintiff was charged. He said there was a recording but he never read any of the statements on the record. Though the recording could have been sent to the AFRC, it was never produced. As far as J J Rawlings' evidence was concerned it was merely hypothetical. He said if the accused person's name was included in the list to be confirmed, then he was tried. This means he had no personal knowledge of the trial either. He placed his faith on the one who made the list. But there could be an error or a deliberate inclusion of his name by a villain. The production of the press release and the warrant of commitment to prison do not necessarily, in our opinion, constitute a sufficient evidence of a trial, let alone a purported trial.

Yet surprisingly, the Supreme Court, with a majority of 5-2 with Taylor and Anin JJSC dissenting, premised their decision on the literal definition of the word "purported" and held that a judicial action taken within the true intendment of section 15(2) of the transitional provisions of the Constitution, 1979, meant a judicial action regularly and lawfully taken, ie a judicial action which satisfied the requirements of the law both substantive and procedural, while by a "judicial action purported to have been taken" was meant an action which was not a judicial action properly so-called but which looked like, was intended to be, or which had the outward appearance of a judicial action. Therefore a purported judicial action was one whose claim to regularity both procedurally and substantively, was inaccurate but whose intention to be a judicial action was clear. Therefore on the facts of the instant case, although the evidence led by the defendant did not establish or prove any judicial action by the AFRC or by any person in the name of that council, it sufficiently established a judicial action purported to have been taken by the special court, a body authorised by the AFRC, against the plaintiff.

Taylor was "scandalised" by what he regarded as an unjust decision of the majority. He would not be part of it. In Taylor's dissenting opinion, the evidence of the defendant's own witnesses showed that no evidence was led at all in proof of any of the charges. Hence the tribunal had no evidence at all upon which they could ever make findings so as to convict and sentence the plaintiff. They only proceeded to pass sentence without a trial. In his opinion, what constituted a trial or a purported trial could be found in section 5 of AFRCD 3. That section provides, inter alia, in mandatory terms that the evidence of the prosecution shall be presented to the Court and that the Court shall receive all relevant evidence on oath or affirmation in proof of the charges against an accused person. It provides, inter alia, that the Court shall be guided by the rules of natural justice. Evidence, Taylor observed was the substance of which a trial was made of and not a procedure in a trial. By breaching the provisions of section 5 of AFRCD 3 on the need for calling of evidence, there could have been no trial or a purported trial. He declared:

"In fact, by refusing to take evidence, a tribunal empowered to try a case was in effect declining to decide and is abandoning jurisdiction. Whatever it does without evidence is a nullity."

For this reason there was no trial or purported trial as the provision of section 5 of AFRCD 3 was blantantly ignored. It is remarkable that while his colleagues in the Supreme Court were content to perceive the case simply in terms of a literal interpretation and application of the meaning of  “purported judicial action," Taylor saw the issues and their implications far beyond the mechanical application of the term within the context of those provisions. He would seem to continually bear in mind the effect of the interpretation of those ouster clauses on the rights of the individual and the dangerous precedent which would thus be set by a mechanical interpretative approach. That attitude is reflected in this comment on the instant case in Appiah v The Republic41 He commented:

". . . for the first time in the recorded history of our law, we in this Supreme Court took an unprecedented decision and acquiesced in the imprisonment of a man although we were not told what he had done against the laws of the Republic.”

It should be pointed out that the crucial issue in this case is that the fundamental human right of the citizen is at stake here. As Sowah JSC conceded in the case "the trial was defective and the tribunal violated the procedural rule in a manner which went to the root of its jurisdiction." "If this was the end of the matter," the learned judge rightly observed, "the course open to me is clear. The trial, conviction and sentence are a nullity." If indeed the violation of the law prescribed to be followed by the AFRC was such that it went to the root of jurisdiction rendering the act done to be a nullity, the inevitable conclusion can only be that there was no action in existence to "resemble or look like anything or to "appear to be" anything. That seems to be Taylor's contention.

Indeed, Taylor admits that section 15(2) and (3) of the transitional provisions saved executive, legislative and judicial acts taken or purported to have been taken by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council or its lawful agents from being questioned in court. However, he pointed out that:

"The act of unknown nameless persons assembling and without hearing evidence, sentencing a man is not a judicial act. It is neither a legislative act nor an executive act. The AFRC did not authorise any such body to act. It is plainly an illegal act and section 15 in no way saves an illegal act taken or purported to have been taken ... this illegal act was not taken by the AFRC and there was before us no evidence that the special court established under AFRCD 3 took or purported to take this illegal act. Clearly, section 15 is not applicable."

In Taylor's opinion, a capricious action or illegal action could not be a judicial action and could not have the effect of an outward appearance of a judicial action or could not be considered as intended to seem or made to appear as a judicial decision.

To assemble unlawfully in secret in a caucus or as a court and without hearing evidence as approved by law, to administer unlawful secret oaths and to sentence a person "has no semblance to a judicial action." It rather resembles, so declared Taylor, "a capricious act or an illegal act."

Taylor did recognise that the AFRCD (Special Courts) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 3) was a respectable Decree. That is why it set down in comprehensive terms, dignified conditions under which an accused person was to be tried. It infused into the criminal system, adequate safeguards to protect persons accused under it. To say as the majority of the court would seem to be saying that those elaborate safeguards could capriciously be ignored with impunity, “is to denigrate the government of the AFRC" and to create the impression that a government which gave the indication that it wanted justice for the people of Ghana "was deliberately subverting justice." Taylor could not believe that was the intention of the AFRC.

For this reason, in Taylor's opinion, it was necessary for the Attorney-General, in order to prove that the AFRC took or purported to have taken judicial action against the plaintiff, to prove that the body which is alleged to have sat was the AFRC or an appointee of the AFRC or its authorised agent. Taylor argued that if the court that tried the plaintiff claimed to be an AFRC Special Court, then it had failed to satisfy the provision of section 1 of the Decree which established such a court. There was clear evidence, for instance, as Taylor pointed out, that there was no judge advocate, the presence of which is a mandatory statutory condition for the composition of such a court. This Taylor rightly pointed out, was not a defect in composition, "and the AFRC (Special Courts) (Amendment) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 19) which saves defective composition," Taylor declared, "is therefore inapplicable."

The logic of this reasoning can hardly be faulted. If a court is created by statute setting out certain conditions precedent for its composition, until these conditions are met, the court would not be constituted. So that, for instance, as in the instant case, where there was clear evidence that there was no judge advocate, it would not amount to a mere defect in composition. And Taylor was right in concluding that under those circumstances the alleged court was not constituted as an AFRC Special Court. Hence it could not have taken any action, judicial or otherwise or purported to have taken any action on behalf of the AFRC. So convinced was Taylor about the impropriety of the majority decision and the dangerous precedential consequences for the liberty of the individual and right to his property that he lamented as follows:

"In the Kwakye case, we do not have the particulars of the charge. The witness present at the so-called trial does not know the charge. We have operated a civilised court system for over 100 years. A trial, conviction and sentence have always been proved before our courts by the production of the record of proceedings. This is the first trial in the history of this country that a court has been asked to assume that there has been a trial without the production of any record at all. This is the first time over 100 years, since the establishment of statutory courts in Ghana where the highest court in the land is called upon to acquiesce in incarcerating the citizen without being told or given information as to the offence the said person has committed."

Justice Taylor could not understand how any court in the responsible discharge of its duty to protect the citizen from illegal acts that invade his liberty and property could entertain the application of such a law. Taylor confessed to being ashamed and scandalised that our courts system which is one of the best in black Africa, and which has imbibed all the best principles of the British common law tradition and which now has distilled into it by our Constitution, 1979, some of the best elements of the jurisprudence of the enlightened world can be said to be capable of entertaining the secret trial system which is the hallmark of the worst totalitarian regimes of some countries of the world. He regarded the majority decision as being a dark day indeed for the Ghanaian people and constitutionalism when the Supreme Court was able to say that the evil machine of tyranny and despotism had a place in our Constitution, 1979.

"If it is indeed so," declared Taylor "then an evil genius must be in control of our destiny - But I think not."

Continuing this frustrating lamentation he said:

"The people of this country have had a distressing past. The Preventive Detention Act of the Nkrumah era was a blot on our law and an affront to justice. The totalitarian regimes which followed military coups in which people were denied their rights to govern themselves is a shameful past best forgotten. The ‘no court' scenario of the Busia regime did no good to justice and constitutionalism in this country. Our people desire and are entitled to justice. And I would have thought the Constitution, 1979, with its excellent human rights provisions, is the culmination of the end of the woes of our citizens. It is a pity if it is not so and our citizens are as yet to continue the struggle again against the monster that injustice is.”

The sentiments to which Taylor so graphically gives expression in the above statement have guided him throughout his career on the Bench. They have influenced his perception of the issues raised in cases that involve the application of the criminal law. Those sentiments, blended with the logical analysis and evaluation of the  evidence, the relevant statutes and the general law in the Kwakye case, led him to the inevitable conclusion that a "blatant and outrageous injustice of immense proportions" had been perpetrated against the plaintiff. He rightly expressed his regret and sadness that there could possibly exist a rule of law which welcomed injustice, and declared that the majority decision was wrong in law.

Taylor would never overlook a wrongdoing. He would always spot it and castigate it. He severely admonished the Attorney-General's role in the case. He expressed his regret that the latter who represented the government that had sworn to protect the citizen was in effect urging the judiciary, the authority which was the constitutional defender of the rights of the people, to deny any remedy to the plaintiff who had overwhelmingly shown that his legal and constitutional rights had been violated. Taylor thought that the joinder of forces by the judiciary and the executive to deny justice to a citizen exuded an "odious and intolerable smell of the totalitarianism of the Akoto and Amadu Baba syndrome of the First Republic." Taylor did not regard such curtailment of the citizen's right in the interest of state security justifiable because the Constitution, 1979, and the thrust of traditional legal reasoning favoured a decision vindicating freedom and justice. These he considered to be potent allies which should press the judges "to ride roughshod over the fears and prejudices of the unfortunate segment of our society," undoubtedly a significant but an unenlightened segment, who because of their economic woes and the collapse of their living standards, would make scapegoats of their fellow citizens whose misconduct had not in any lawful way been exposed for the deterrent benefit of would-be wrongdoers.  He ended on this didactic note:

"If I do not know what A has done to be incarcerated, how do I avoid his fate?"

It must be said in support of Taylor's position that there is no point in our judges frequently making references, with great admiration, to the bold and landmark decisions of some of the great judges of the common law world that blazed the trail such as, for example, Chief Justice Marshall in Marbury v Madison,42 Justice Frankfurter in Baker v Carr,43 Lord Atkin in Liversidge v Anderson44 and others, such as Lords Denning, Mansfield CJ, Esher MR and Chief Justice Coke himself, praising their valour; but when the opportunity is presented for them to tread their path, then they recoil into their shells. It is to the credit of Taylor and a mark of greatness that he has never been found wanting on such occasions.  Hansen v Ankrah, the Kwakye case and the Appiah case have shown that he is in the class of the great judges of the common law world. 

Outraged by what he regarded as injustice to the plaintiff in the Kwakye case, he appealed to the executive to remedy the situation in the following words:

"I think the majority decision is wrong in law.  It must surely be a grave concern of both the legislature and the executive; and in my view, they must in the responsible discharge of their duties, seek to ameliorate this injustice by finding a solution to this problem since the Supreme Court has in effect declined to interfere. They must do it because any country that countenances injustice may achieve some temporary respite; but in my humble opinion, such respite is illusory and the nation is without doubt poised on a path that may lead to violence."

The sentiments expressed here must not mislead readers to think that Mr Justice Taylor has any sympathy for wrongdoers as such. He does insist that whenever a man is found guilty, particularly for dishonesty and corruption, he must not escape just punishment.  This view of the jurist is evident in the Kwakye case itself. Taylor made it clear in his judgment that the executive of the AFRC era were clearly intent on punishing the criminals who had destroyed the country.  The future security of the state in the enjoyment of  a sound economy demanded that persons who had wreaked havoc with the economy were not let loose on our society.  Therefore while declaring the alleged trial, conviction and sentence of Kwakye to be illegal, null and void, he declared and directed that if the plaintiff were one of the detractors, then by virtue of section 17 of the transitional provisions of the Constitution, 1979, his case would have been pending before the special tribunal established under the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Special Tribunal and Other Matters) Decree, 1979 (AFRCD 23). In such a case the plaintiff should be tried before that tribunal.

Indeed, Taylor found nothing wrong with the tribunal, what he deplored was the way in which the elaborate safeguards provided by the state were capriciously ignored with impunity.  For in trying to get at such economic criminals, Taylor points out, the courts must balance the security of the state as against the individual's constitutional rights and the courts must in the interest of justice and constitutionalism endeavour to satisfy both.

The message of justice, fairplay, the protection of fundamental human rights, abhorrence for dishonesty and the need for probity and accountability run through the great works of Mr Justice Taylor on the Bench. His dissenting judgment in the Kwakye case vividly and truly reflect such virtues. It is a credit to him and even to the executive itself that while the Supreme Court was unable to do justice to the plaintiff in the Kwakye case; the executive heeded the appeal made on his behalf by Mr Justice Taylor and freed him.  That is a further vindication of Taylor's position on the matter.  We salute him.

However, our discussion of Taylor's contribution to the development of the criminal law and the protection of the citizen's fundamental rights in Ghana cannot be complete without referring to his erudite and sound dissenting judgment in Appiah v The Republic.  Like the Kwakye case, the accused person in Appiah v The Republic was a public servant. He was the special prosecutor of the public tribunal created under the AFRCD 3. He was charged with extortion for demanding and obtaining from two persons, Djaba and Owusu who were fugitives from justice and had their cases pending before the special tribunal, £1,000 and  £10,000 respectively. These amounts were alleged to have been paid into the appellants' account operated with Barclays Bank International in London.

The evidence in support of the prosecution's case consisted,  inter alia, of: (a) the bank giro cheques from Djaba and Owusu paid into the appellant's account; (b) two cheques for £100 each drawn by the appellant after those payments had been  made when in fact the appellant had before the payments only £110.41 to his credit; and (c) an affidavit sworn to in London by an official of the bank which confirmed the state of Appiah's account and which the court had secured on commission. The circuit court before which the appellant was arraigned convicted him on the evidence produced. On appeal to the High Court, the appeal was allowed on the grounds that the moneys had been paid into the appellant's account by mistake and that the prosecution had failed to call Djaba and Owusu as witnesses. The State appealed from that decision to the Court of Appeal which set aside the decision of the High Court. An appeal by the appellant to the Supreme Court was dismissed by a majority of four to one with Taylor being in the minority.

One of the key issues for determination in the appeal was the admissibility or otherwise of the documentary evidence tendered by the prosecution.  In Taylor's opinion, the ratio of the case was a simple one. "The case depends entirely on the bank statements and nothing else." In his opinion, the trial judge was right in his ruling that those documents tendered were not being accepted as proof of their contents.  Taylor points out, and in our opinion rightly too, that with the exception of the bank statement and other documents obtained from England by the prosecution there was no evidence as to the contents of these documents. Taylor in his characteristic, meticulous and exhaustive manner,  examined in some detail, one after the other, the evidence of all the fifteen prosecution witnesses.

He discovered that the whole prosecution was based on documentary evidence, for no oral testimony whatsoever was led in proof of the ingredients of the offence. Objective readers of the case would readily agree with Taylor's observation that none of the fifteen witnesses had any personal knowledge at all of the contents of the bank statement and were therefore not in a better position than the court to say anything about their contents.  Taylor pointed out that the original giro credits for £10,000 and £1,000  respectively ought to be with Owusu and Djaba. Similarly the original cheques issued by the appellant for £100 each, exhibits M and N ought to be with the bank and no explanation was offered by the prosecution; and indeed the prosecution "deliberately withheld all explanation as to how such bank documents fell into strange hands and to be passed on to them."

In Taylor's opinion, and contrary to the majority view, it could not be understood how those so-called original documents could be tendered through the police witness in blatant disregard of section 60 of NRCD 323 as well as sections 163(1) and 165 of the said Decree.

Taylor argues:

“If evidence had been led, that this document was found in the possession of the appellant, his knowledge of the payment into his account cannot be controverted. The evidence led by the prosecution however is that the document was in the hands of an apparently unknown stranger, and since it is this document that could possibly impute knowledge to the appellant of the payment into the account, the circumstance that it was in possession of a stranger not shown to have received it from him is proof that the appellant was not aware of the payments. The appellant could not be said to have obtained something the existence of which he has been proved by evidence emanating from the prosecution itself that he was unaware of.”

From a close examination of the evidence, Taylor was left in no doubt, and we would say many readers of the case would agree with him, that a person or persons operating under inexplicable conditions of anonymity who did not want to reveal their motives in an honourable fashion had by some extraordinary and certainly improper means got hold of the documents or conspired to purloin or caused someone to prepare the documents to induce the authorities to prosecute the appellant. Taylor would not see such a person as the scarlet pimpernel —“a conscientious citizen” as the majority of the Supreme Court would want to portray him. The “man of conscience” whose “conscience does not permit him to operate openly but clandestinely in the fashion of conspirators” would not impress or appeal to Taylor. He cautioned the Supreme Court to be vigilant and not to permit its process to be used as an instrument of evil by any unscrupulous person masquerading as a “conscientious citizen” and seeking succour in the corridors of innocent and well-meaning prosecutors, caught in their bona fide fervent zeal to eradicate corruption from our society.

As he did in the Kwakye case, he admonished the prosecution for not having been fair and candid with the court in the best tradition of the administration of the criminal law. He observed that the prosecution was aware of the provisions of section 167 of NRCD 323 which provides that evidence other than an original writing is admissible to the same extent as an original to prove the contents of a writing if all the originals are lost or have been destroyed unless the loss or destruction resulted from the fraudulent acts of the proponent of the evidence, yet although the prosecution had the original documents in its custody since 25 August 1980 while the trial commenced in October 1980, it kept on "foisting photocopies upon photocopies" on the court and kept away the original in a trial by error attempt at overreaching the defence and hoodwinking the court in order to prove the contents of the documents by such unlawful means, for as he argued, how the original bank giro slips in the hands of two different persons - Djaba and Owusu came to be together with the police was the key to the case. "If those hiding behind screens of anonymity will not emerge into the open, I will not tolerate their pulling strings and making me dance like a marionette."

Taylor in effect concluded that on the basis of the evidence produced by the prosecution the conviction of the appellant would be tantamount to a conviction of a man with no mens rea being proved, not even on his actus reus but solely on another person's actus reus. Arguing that the appellant was never shown to have committed any criminal act in Ghana he warned that the implicit parallel of the case with the substance of the Kwakye case could not be glossed over. "Public officers carrying out their duties with enthusiasm and creating enemies for themselves, observed Taylor,  “are at risk if the conviction in this case is not set aside."

On the question of the alleged receipt of the money by the appellant, Taylor raised, suo motu, pertinent legal issues which the majority opinion did not consider at all. The question was whether or not money paid into a customer's account at a bank can be said to have been obtained by the customer. Taylor in his usual exhaustive manner examined the issue by reference to authorities.45

The conclusion to be reached from the examination of the cases shows as Taylor found, that in the law of banking, money paid into an account is received by the bank and it becomes at once the property of the bank. The bank becomes a debtor to the customer. This is what it must be, because the relationship between a banker and its customer is that of a debtor and a creditor. It is therefore quite erroneous, as Taylor rightly concluded, that when the money had been paid to the appellant's credit at the bank in London, he could thereby be said to have obtained that money. Another issue which it would appear the majority opinion did not sufficiently address was the affidavit evidence which the court had secured from London on a commission. The majority view was that the securing of the affidavit of the official of Barclays Bank International, London, by the trial circuit court was sanctioned under sections 124-127 of the Criminal Procedure Code, 1960 (Act 30). But according to Taylor no matter how section 124 of Act 30 was read, it specifically applied only to commissions issued by our courts to inferior courts in Ghana and not to any tribunal or court exercising foreign jurisdiction. That section he rightly points out, in our opinion, "gives power to the High Court or circuit court to issue commission to a magistrate in Ghana" and when issued the magistrate was mandatorily required to comply with the terms of the commission. Our High Courts and circuit courts, he pointed out, had no power to order any magistrate in a foreign country to do anything.  Having regard to the fact that our jurisdiction is territorial, it is difficult to see how this argument could be faulted.

The pity of this case, as Taylor points out at the beginning of his judgment, is that his judgment had not been seen by his colleagues and he had not seen theirs either.46 This is unfortunate. At this level of adjudication at the court of last resort, where a case as important as the present involves the liberty of the individual, not only would it have been illuminating and rewarding for written opinions to have been exchanged, but in the interest of justice and proper development of the law, such exchange and discussion of views would seem to be a must. Whatever would have accounted for this serious anomaly is difficult to say.

Whatever has happened, it is clear from the reading of the Appiah case that Justice Kobina Taylor has once again come out with distinction. He was at his exhaustive, meticulous and logical best. He has once again exhibited all the qualities that one would wish to find in a judge of a developing country: courage, probity and uprightness, hard work and industry and erudition. His contribution to our knowledge and understanding of the law is great.
