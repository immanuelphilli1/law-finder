THE QUALIFICATION AND THE CONSTITUTIONAL POSITION OF THE ATTORNEY-GENERAL [1989-90] VOL. XVII RGL 95—117

Hon Amidu Manfia A B K

 

*

INTRODUCTION

The National Commission for Democracy and the Ministry of Local Government ran a series of regional seminars on "District Assemblies and the Evolving Democratic Process" from 5 July to 9 November 1990. As the seminars progressed, it became clear that issues of constitution making were the focus of the debate. The consensus would appear to be for a constitutional democratic government even though the modalities for such an arrangement do not appear to be agreed upon. One of the important institutions for the achievement of any concept of the rule of law and democracy is the office of the Attorney-General and/or Minister for Justice. It has had a chequered history both in the past and in the post independence era and yet does not appear to have been touched upon during those seminars. Admittedly, time could not allow for a discussion of all institutions. Since the debate is an on-going one we have thought it necessary to discuss the qualification and the constitutional position of the Attorney-General and/or Minister of Justice. Before then we think it essential to set out a resume of the subject and set ourselves the scope of the burden we intend to address.

Before Ghana became a republic the Attorney-General was a public servant and his conditions of service were determined by Constitutional Orders in Council, rules and regulations governing the Gold Coast public service. No specific qualification in terms of years standing at the Bar appears to have been required.1 This public service status of an Attorney-General and/or Minister for Justice was abolished by the Republican Constitution, 1960,2 and all subsequent constitutions have maintained the non-public service status of that office.3 The governments of the National Liberation Council and the National Redemption Council, would appear to have made the Attorney-General a civil or public servant even though that office was also conjunctive with that of the Commissioner for Justice which was admittedly political in character.4 While enacting that the Attorney-general was a member of the legal service class of the civil service, the NLC appears to have negated that status in reality by a subsequent legislation making the provisions of the Civil Service Act, as amended to be subject to paragraph 6 of the Proclamation which conferred on the NLC the power to appoint a person to the office of the Attorney-General.5 Similarly the NRC conferred on the Attorney-General a civil service status. However, his appointment was to be done directly by the NRC.6 Both the NLC and the NRC introduced qualification in terms of years' standing at the Bar for appointment as an Attorney-General. The Attorney-General was to be of at least twelve years' standing as a lawyer or in exceptional circumstances, ten years.7 The NRC for the first time impliedly established the post of Deputy Attorney-General who was the official administrative head of the legal class. His standing as a lawyer was to be the same as that of the Attorney-General. But he was a category A officer under the Civil Service Act, appointed upon the recommendation of the Legal Class Board and therefore removable only under the Act.8

The Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Establishment) Proclamation, 1979 and the Provisional National Defence Council (Establishment) Proclamation, 1981 and its Supplementary and Consequential Provisions Law, 1982 (PNDCL 42) do not appear to have conferred on an Attorney-General the status of a public servant or any qualification in terms of years' standing as a lawyer.9 Under PNDCL 42, however, section 40 established a legal service as part of the public services. It is to "comprise of all lawyers in the office of the Attorney-General and other establishments in the Civil Service."10 A legal service board was also established under the chairmanship of the Attorney-General to advise the government in all matters connected with the structure and conduct of the affairs of' the legal service and in particular, in relation to the recruitment, appointment, promotion, remuneration, discipline, dismissal and other conditions of service of the legal service. The board is further empowered in its own right to make regulations by legislative instrument with the approval of the PNDC for the efficient performance of the legal service and the board.11 Pursuant to the powers of the board to make legislative instruments for the efficient performance of the service and the board, the Legal Service Regulations, 1983 (LI 1288) were purported to have been made. By the combined effect of paragraphs 1 and 3 of LI 1288 the board purported not only to re-define the Legal Service but also to confer on the Attorney-General and the Deputy Attorney-General the status of public servants and impose a requirement of fifteen years' standing at the Bar as the minimum qualification for appointment.12 By this device the status and qualification of an Attorney-General and Deputy Attorney-General would appear to have been changed, not on the advise of the board but by its legislative instrument. This state of affairs has given rise to some questions as to the limits of the powers of the government in the appointment of not only the Attorney-General and his deputy but also that of the Secretary for Justice and his deputy. An argument would thus be raised whether the appointment of a person as an Attorney-General necessarily confers on him the status of Secretary for Justice and vice versa. Two ideas about the office would arise from this argument - the separate office notion and the combined office concept. A casual consideration of these issues would indicate the confusion which the present state of the law on the qualification and constitutional position of an Attorney-General could create.

The burden of this article is therefore, firstly, to make an exposition of the constitutional status of the Attorney-General in historical perspective, and secondly, to examine in particular the PNDC (Establishment) Proclamation, 1981 and its Supplementary and Consequential Provisions Law, 1982 (PNDCL 42) to determine the legality of the Legal Service Regulations. We would further examine the arguments for both the separate office and combined office notion vis-a-vis the statutory functions of the Attorney-General and Secretary for Justice. We would conclude by arguing that both historically and realistically and in accordance with existing law, the separate office idea is and would be fraught with serious problems in any future constitutional arrangement for the country.

Constitutional Status 1950-81

Before independence, Ghana was made up of the Gold Coast Colony and a number of territorial appendages and was like most colonies, administered primarily to achieve the objects of using the material and human resources of the colonies for consolidating its own economic and political power.  The colonial legal system was accordingly fashioned to achieve control and dominion over the colonies and their resources. During the period of decolonisation, efforts were made to stem the tide and tempo of the pressure for independence by allowing participation of the colonial people in the affairs of the colony. But it reserved for itself control over sensitive political and socio-economic departments in the political structure.  The control over the legal system and its apparatus thus reflected its exploitative economic norms. Elected representatives of the colonised people could not be trusted to administer those institutions of government that made it possible for the exercise of effective control.

It was for these reasons that when the Gold Coast (Constitution) Order in Council, 1950 sought to extend to the colony some measure of self-government, the appointments of the Chief Secretary, the Attorney-General and the Financial Secretary were retained by the colonial authorities.13  The incumbents of these three offices were also to be ex-officio members of the principal instrument of policy (the Executive Council) and were to perform such functions and duties and exercise such powers as were to be prescribed from time to time.

The members of the Executive Council were styled ministers and the Governor had the authority to charge any member of the Council with responsibility for any department. However, while the representative members ceased to be members of the Council if they lost their seats in the Assembly, the Attorney-General and the other ex-officio members held their office subject to the pleasure of the Crown.14

The Gold Coast (Constitution) (Amendment) Order in Council, 1952 which amended the Gold Coast (Constitution) Order in Council, 1950 preserved the public service status of the Minister of Justice and Attorney-General and the remaining ex-officio cabinet ministers.15  They were answerable to the colonising power.

The deepening of the struggle for self-government and independence and the enormous dimensions it assumed after 1952 compelled the British Government to promulgate the Gold Coast (Constitution) Order in Council, 1954. This Constitution severely curtailed the powers of the Governor and also abolished the ex-officio membership of the cabinet.  The Prime Minister was given enlarged functional duties and powers.

The post of Attorney-General was separated from that of Minister for Justice; he remained a public service officer and was vested with his traditional functions of administering the colonial legal system. Provision was made in the Order in Council for the assignment of a minister to be responsible for the Attorney-General's Department.16

It would however appear that the British Government was so distrustful of the indigenous people that it made the Attorney-General not answerable to the Assembly. To ensure that the Minister of Justice's power over the Attorney-General was limited, it was enacted that the assignment to a minister of responsibility for the department of the Attorney-General conferred responsibility only for submitting to the cabinet questions referred to that department and the conduct of business relating to that department in the assembly.17 It is significant to note the changes over the period in question where the ministerial responsibility for the ministry of justice and the attorney-general's department was vested in one person.18

Be that as it may, with great reluctance and after a protracted struggle in which the colonial power was supported by its internal collaborators made up largely of chiefs and Gold Coast lawyers trained by the colonial power to serve and defend the colonial system and its legal order, the Convention People's Party was compelled to accept the Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, 1957 as a compromise only for the purpose of achieving "independence now." Under that constitution the British government was compelled to relinquish its hold on the vital departments of defence and foreign affairs unconditionally.19 The Attorney-General was, however, to be a public service officer and the assignment of ministerial responsibility for the department was subject to the same limitations as provided under the Constitutional Order in Council, 1954.20

The first Prime Minister, Dr Kwame Nkrumah, had to go out of his way to encourage an English lawyer committed to decolonisation to apply for the post of Attorney-General.  As the most senior and experienced lawyer who applied for the post, the late Geoffrey Henry Cecil Bing who had been invited to the Gold Coast in 1956 as general adviser to Dr Nkrumah was appointed the Attorney-General.  Co-operation between the government and the Office of the Attorney-General was achieved and this was to help the government to weather the storm of confrontation and legal battles with the opposition.

The Republican Constitutions

In July 1960, Ghana formally broke off the last vestiges of colonialism by adopting a Republican Constitution. This Constitution provided for "an Attorney-General who shall be a minister of Ghana or other person appointed by the President." The Attorney-General's powers were exercisable subject to the directions of the President and he was removable from office by the President.21  The Attorney-General thus became a member of the government and ceased to be a public servant who was unaffected by changes in government.22

This falls in line with the position in Britain where the Lord Chancellor is a cabinet minister with responsibility for justice, and the Attorney-General is also a minister and represents the Crown in all legal proceedings.  They are both political appointees and are answerable to the government of the day while still maintaining the independence of their judicial and quasi-judicial functions. They stand and fall with the ruling government.  The Lord Chancellor is a member of the House of Lords while the Attorney-General is invariably a member of the House of Commons chosen from the majority party in the House forming the government.23 They are both appointed by the Queen on the recommendation of the Prime Minister and no qualification as a lawyer is required, not even for the high office of the Lord Chancellor. In practice however, the Queen appoints the person recommended by the Prime Minister from among the Bench or such members of the Bar as hold or have held the office of Attorney-General or Solicitor-General as Lord Chancellor.  The unfettered discretion of the government in the appointment of the two officials is however unfettered by law.24

The Republican Constitution, 1960, in the absence of a bi-cameral parliamentary system followed the British tradition by conferring wide discretionary powers on the President to appoint a Minister of Justice and or an Attorney-General. It made it possible for the President to appoint a non-member of parliament as an Attorney-General if that was considered desirable.  The first Republican Constitution has been criticised for making the Attorney-General subject to the directions of the President and removable by him. But it is significant to note that without stating explicitly that the Minister for Justice and the Attorney-General is subject to the directions of government, the two Republican Constitutions which Ghana has had since the abrogation of the Constitution, 1960 made the Attorney-General a political appointee of government. The second Republican Constitution, 1969, for example, provided for "an Attorney-General who shall be a minister of state and the principal legal adviser to the Government."25

It was a blend of the presidential system of the United States of America and the British parliamentary system of government. It provided for the appointment of ministers of state and ministerial secretaries from among members of the National Assembly by the President acting in accordance with the advice of the Prime Minister. The ministerial secretaries were to assist the ministers of state in the performance of their duties. This did not require any standing at the Bar in the case of the Minister of Justice and the Attorney-General or his deputy.26 In fact the Constitution provided explicitly in article 71(1)(i) that a member of the civil service could not be a member of the National Assembly. Therefore a minister of state or a ministerial secretary could not be a civil servant. The discretion to appoint and remove the Minister of Justice and the Attorney-General under the Constitution was thus absolutely unaffected by qualification as to standing as a lawyer or status as a civil servant.27 The Third Republican Constitution, 1979, simply re-enacted the 1969 provisions with necessary amendments to reflect the presidential character of the Constitution.28 Both Constitutions apart from the requisite qualification for membership of parliament, did not restrict the power of government in the appointment, removal or dismissal of the Minister of Justice and the Attorney-General.

The Establishment Proclamations of 1966, 1972 and 1979

The Proclamation for the Constitution of the National Liberation Council for the administration of Ghana and for other matters connected therewith, 1966 provided that the Attorney-General was to exercise his powers subject to the directions of the Council.29 The substance of this provision in the Proclamation was later amended to read simply that "There shall be an Attorney-General who shall be appointed by the Council."30  This amendment was merely cosmetic in nature.  It was intended to abate the embarrassment of opponents of the previous government who had criticised the position of the Attorney-General under the Constitution, 1960. The truth of the matter in respect of the position of the Attorney-General is that he has always been an integral part of the cabinet of any government of the day.

In addition to the NLC (Establishment) Proclamation, 1966, Ghana has been ruled under three other Establishment Proclamations including the present PNDC (Establishment) Proclamation, 1981, as amended.31 All three Proclamations except NRC (Establishment) Proclamation as amended did not accord the Attorney-General the status and position of a public servant.32 In fact the provision in the NRC (Establishment) Proclamation, 1972, making the Attorney-General a member of the public service appears to have been deliberately not re-enacted both in the Proclamations, 1979 and 1981 in view of the fact that the legislature is presumed to know the existing law.33  It should however be admitted that section 9A(1)-(6) and 9B of the Civil Service Act, 1960 (CA 5) as inserted by the Civil Service (Amendment) Decree, 1974 (NRCD 277) which was made to give flesh to the public service status of the Attorney-General was not repealed by the Armed Forces Revolutionary Council (Establishment) Proclamation, 1979.  The legal status of that amendment with respect to the post of Attorney-General under the regimes that followed since 1979 should therefore have to be examined so as to appreciate the change in the status of the Attorney-General as affected by the AFRC (Establishment) Proclamation, 1979 and the Constitution, 1979.

The AFRC (Establishment) Proclamation, 1979, did not continue in existence the office of the Attorney-General established under the NRC (Establishment) Proclamation, 1972, as amended, but provided for "... an Attorney-General who should be appointed by the Revolutionary Council."34  The Proclamation, 1979, unlike its predecessor of 1972, did not provide that the Attorney-General should be a public officer even though it continued in existence the public service "as existed immediately before the fourth day of June 1979."35  Nonetheless NRCD 277 in as far as it affected the position and status of the Attorney-General as a civil servant, was not expressly repealed. It is therefore arguable that notwithstanding the fact that the Attorney-General under the regime of the AFRC derived his office and authority from the Proclamation, 1979, the AFRC was bound in law to satisfy the conditions in NRCD 277 in the appointment and removal of an Attorney-General.  It is further arguable that the Deputy Attorney-General being head of the administration of the legal class could not be removed by the AFRC except under the conditions provided under NRCD 277. The rationale for these arguments would appear to be that NRCD 277 is not inconsistent with the AFRC (Establishment) Proclamation in so far as it affected the position of the Attorney-General and the Deputy Attorney-General

and in the absence of an express repeal there is a strong presumption against an implied repeal. Until the Constitution, 1979 the position of a Deputy Attorney-General was not a live issue as there was no incumbent of that office for some years before and during the regime of the AFRC.

The mandatory language of the Proclamation, 1979 establishing the office of the Attorney-General, in our view, puts beyond a pale of doubt the intention of the AFRC not to be bound by the Proclamation 1972 and any laws amending it in so far as the position of the Attorney-General is concerned. The position of a Deputy Attorney-General could however, be said to be unaffected by the AFRC (Establishment) Proclamation. The establishment of the office of the Attorney-General also underscores the political character and importance of that office to every government and belies the civil service character of that office.

Whatever doubts might have existed as to the position and status of the Attorney-General and his deputy were laid to rest by the Third Republican Constitution, 1979.  Article 74 of the Constitution established the office of an Attorney-General who was to be a minister of state and spelt out his functions. It also spelt out the qualification for the appointment of ministers of state under article 65. They were to be appointed by the President with the prior approval of parliament from among persons who are qualified to be elected as members of parliament. The qualification and disqualification for membership of parliament were contained in article 76 of the Constitution and the only qualification in terms of years is that a Ghanaian citizen should have attained the age of twenty-one years. Subclause 2(k) expressly disqualified civil servants from being members of parliament and therefore ministers of state.

A minister of state was prohibited from holding any other office of profit or emolument whether private or public and either directly or indirectly. Under article 66 the Constitution also permitted the President in consultation with a minister of state, and with the prior approval of parliament, to appoint one or more deputy ministers to assist the minister in performing his functions. The deputy minister was likewise to be a person qualified to be elected as a member of parliament.  The tenure of office of ministers of state and deputy ministers were also spelt out. The office was to become vacant if his appointment was revoked by the President or if he was elected as Speaker or member of parliament or if he resigned from office or died.36

The Constitution also established the public service. It defined both the public service and civil service to exclude from its ambit the office of a minister of state or deputy minister of state and, therefore, the Attorney-General and the Deputy Attorney-General.37 In fact the Constitution did not expressly establish the post of Deputy Attorney-General. But in view of the fact that the Attorney-General was a minister of state, and his deputy was to assist him to perform his functions, articles 65, 66 and 68 dealing with the appointment, removal and resignation of ministers of state laid sufficient foundation for a deputy minister for justice and Attorney-General to be appointed.

The provisions of the Constitution, 1979, were in conflict with the civil service status of the Attorney-General and his deputy as provided for in NRCD 277. The Decree would thus be deemed to have been repealed by the Constitution in as far as the office of Attorney-General and his deputy were concerned.38 Those aspects of the legal class of the civil service which were continued in existence by the Constitution, were limited to posts held by the heads of the various divisions of the legal class from the Solicitor-General and below.

The Legal Service Regulations, 1983 (LI 1288)

The PNDC (Establishment) Proclamation suspended, the Constitution, 1979, but continued in existence the public services as it existed under the Constitution as at 31 December 1981.39  The public service under the Constitution as we have already indicated, excluded the Minister of Justice and Attorney-General and his deputy as members. In fact the Proclamation, 1981 did not set out any qualifications for the appointment of the Secretary for Justice and Attorney-General or his deputy or any secretary of state but left the matter of appointment and tenure of office entirely at the discretion of the government.40 It was under this Proclamation and under such conditions that the government appointed the first PNDC Secretary for Justice and Attorney-General in 1982. It would however appear that the effect of the Constitution, 1979 on the position and status of the Attorney-General as a non-civil servant was not appreciated by the draftsman, and therefore the Civil Service Act (Amendment) Law, 1982 (PNDCL 20) was enacted purporting to amend sections 7 and 9A thereof by merely listing the Attorney-General and his deputy as members of the legal class. Five months later, however, the whole of sections 9A(1)-(6) and 9B of the Civil Service Act, 1960 (CA 5) as inserted by NRCD 277 were repealed by PNDCL 42, which set up a legal service of the public service.

As indicated earlier, the legal service established under paragraph 40 of PNDCL 42 was empowered to advise the PNDC in all matters connected with the structure and conduct of the affairs of the legal service and in particular in relation to recruitment, appointment, promotion, remuneration, discipline, dismissal and other conditions of service in the legal service.  The Legal Service Board, subject to the approval of the PNDC was empowered to make legislative instruments for the efficient performance of the functions of the legal service and the Board.  The powers of the Legal Service Board were limited to only the legal service which is defined to "comprise all lawyers in the office of the Attorney-General and other establishments in the Civil Service."

The Legal Service Regulations, 1983 (LI 1288) defines the legal service to include the Attorney-General and the Deputy Attorney-General and yet excluded the Attorney-General and the Deputy Attorney-General from its definition of "lawyers in the office of the Attorney-General."

It is our view that the approach adopted by the supposed Legal Service Board in paragraph 1 of LI 1288 in re-defining the legal service to include and exclude at the same time the Attorney-General and the Deputy Attorney-General as falling within the statutory language of section 40(1) of PNDCL 42 as "lawyers in the office of the Attorney-General" and other establishments in the civil service is an indication that the "Legal Service Board" recognised the difficulty and inconsistency of bringing the Attorney-General and his deputy under LI 1288. In spite of this, the "Board" purported to confer on the Attorney-General and his deputy the status of officers in the legal service and prohibits the government from appointing, removing or dismissing them unless the conditions set down by the "Board" are satisfied by the government of the day.  This is in spite of the fact that under section 40 of PNDCL 42 the powers of the "Board" are limited only to advising the government on such matters and do not extend to legislating on them.

The Legal Service Board, if properly constituted, is a subsidiary legislative body and it is trite law that such a body cannot normally amend or add to its parent statute. Such an action would be ultra vires the powers of a properly constituted Board and therefore illegal.  It may however be argued that the PNDC as the supreme legislative body approved of LI 1288, and that it was in pursuance of that approval that the Director of Public Prosecutions who was also the PNDC Secretary for Justice and Attorney-General was promoted to the allegedly public service post of Deputy Attorney-General and therefore LI 1288 has the force of substantive PNDC law. But if that is accepted as the case then it would produce contradictory and absurd results. It would, for example, mean that the substantive Attorney-General at the time was being reduced in status to a Deputy Attorney-General who was then being promoted from the post of Director of Public Prosecutions to that post.  The mere approval of LI 1288 cannot in law give the legislative instrument the force of a substantive enactment. This is why the substantive Secretary for Justice and Attorney-General duly appointed in accordance with sections 1(4) and 12 of the Proclamation deemed it necessary to hold on to the post of Director of Public Prosecutions in order to continue to be subject to the public service conditions of service. This shows that the then Secretary for Justice and Attorney-General himself appreciated the fact that the post of Attorney-General is not a public service office under the Proclamation. If the post of Attorney-General were a public service one then the appointment of the Director of Public Prosecutions as PNDC Secretary for Justice and Attorney-General in 1982 should have been considered as a promotion out of turn. Even after LI 1288 was passed in 1983 the appointment of the Attorney-General was not considered a promotion out of turn. By some contrivance, under LI 1288 the unfortunate impression was created of the existence of a public service office of Deputy Attorney-General to which the Director of Public Prosecutions (who was also the Attorney-General) had to be promoted to enable the latter post to be filled.  The fallacy of this arrangement which we shall presently show is misconceived, is that Ghana has not, since 31 December 1981 had a substantive Attorney-General.

Be that as it may, it is our view that the abrogation of the Constitution, 1979, does not have the effect of reviving NRCD 277 in so far as the post of Deputy Attorney-General is concerned. The public service continued in force by the Proclamation, 1981 excluded that office. The amendments purported to have been effected by the Civil Service (Amendment) Law, 1982 (PNDCL 20) by inserting the post of Attorney-General and Deputy Attorney-General in the Schedules of the Civil Service Act (CA 5) which were deemed to have been repealed by the Constitution, 1979, did not restore the provision of the Act dealing with the office, functions and duties of an Attorney-General and his deputy. Consequently, the office which is mentioned in PNDCL 42 cannot be said to be a civil service post. At the time of promulgating the Proclamation, 1981, there was no such office under NRCD 277. The office now exists as an office under the Proclamation. This fact appears to have been recognised by the supposed Legal Service Board in its definition of lawyers in the office of the Attorney-General. It is therefore our submission that the purported promotion of the substantive Attorney-General from his substantive civil service post of Director of Public Prosecutions from which he was on secondment to the post of Deputy Attorney-General does not make either the Attorney-General qua Attorney-General, or in his second capacity as Deputy Attorney-General a public service officer. The acts of the government in purporting to make such a promotion were executive acts and even though the PNDC is also the legislative body, it cannot make or amend the law by its executive decisions or wishes.

The discussion of the scope and effect of LI 1288 on the position and status of an Attorney-General and a Deputy Attorney-General will not be complete without drawing attention to the composition of the Legal Service Board and the signatories to LI 1288. The legislative instrument is purported to have been signed by the Chairman and three other members of the Board. An examination of section 40 of PNDCL 42 shows that two persons who were to have been appointed by the government as Board members were never appointed and were therefore not signatories to the legislative instrument. Neither was there a Deputy Attorney-General to complete the statutory mandatory requirements for the composition of the Board. From our researches we found that the Legal Service Board has never been legally in place as the government has never appointed its nominees to the Board to date; neither has any amendments been made to the composition of the Board. This in our view raises a further issue as to the legality of LI 1288. It is our view that the law sets out mandatory requirements for the composition of the Legal Service Board by stating that "there shall be a Legal Service Board which shall consist of . . ." the persons named therein. In the absence therefore of the appointment of such members of the Board there cannot be a functional Legal Service Board, for the statutory language is not permissive. It is imperative. It stands to reason that in the absence of a properly constituted Board the power to make legislative instruments cannot exist. Any purported exercise of such a power is a nullity and the issue of a quorum does not even arise.41 Further support for this submission can be found in several PNDC laws in which both the mandatory requirements for the composition of Boards are spelt out together with the names of Board members including the number which may constitute a quorum for the transaction of business. In some of these laws it is even provided that a defect in composition or appointment of the Board are not to effect the validity of any proceedings of the Board. Other PNDC laws are couched in the same imperative language such as section 40 of PNDCL 42 with a proviso for the Board to make regulations for the conduct of its business. It should be pointed out also that even though the government has not appointed any lawyer in the office of the Attorney-General to the Board, some divisional heads of the legal service have always attended meetings of the Board as co-opted members. Despite the fact that section 40(7) does not give them a vote, they were signatories to the Legal Service (Amendment) Regulations, 1987 (LI 1342) which was also approved by the government. That regulation deals with the salaries and benefits, etc and compulsory and voluntary retirement of officers including the Attorney-General and the Deputy Attorney-General. The legal validity of that regulation under which attorneys including the Attorney-General and his deputy are supposed to receive their emoluments is gravely in doubt.

Thirdly, it could be absurd if the government, by PNDCL 42 intended that the Legal Service Board which when properly constituted, is chaired by the Attorney-General and of which the Deputy Attorney-General is a member should advise the government on their own appointment and removal. Does the government have to consult the Board before it removes the Attorney-General or his deputy from office? Is the Attorney-General to preside over any deliberations of the Board to discipline him for example? Or has the Board powers to consider disciplining the Attorney-General and his deputy and also advise the government on their dismissal? The answers to these questions are obvious and show the absurdity of any contention that LI 1288 regulates the appointment, remuneration, discipline and removal of the Attorney-General or his deputy. No government would intend any such absurdity. That is why even in the case of officers of the legal service the law gives the Board only advisory functions in connection with the structure and conduct of the affairs of the legal service, and in particular in relation to recruitment, appointment, promotion, remuneration, discipline, dismissal and other conditions of service. The proper body therefore to determine the conditions of service including the appointment, etc. of members of the legal service is the government itself on the advice of the Board. The power entrusted to the Board to make regulations by legislative instrument with the approval of the government for the efficient performance of the legal service and the Board, in our view, did not include the power to make regulations in respect of matters reserved by the law for implementation by the government. Even a properly constituted Board would thus have been acting ultra vires if it purported to legislate on matters for which it is imperatively enjoined to advise the government for necessary action.

From the foregoing analysis it is our submission that the government under PNDC (Establishment) Proclamation, 1981 confers on itself an unfettered discretion in the appointment, remuneration and removal of the Attorney-General and his deputy and any purported limitation placed on that unfettered discretion by an illegal or improperly constituted Board by way of a subsidiary legislation, LI 1288, is null and void as having been made without authority. The government as the supreme legislative body has power to make the Attorney-General and his deputy public service officers but this should be done by substantive law as was the case under NRCD 277, or by a properly constituted subsidiary legislative body. It is further submitted that in as far as LI 1288 and LI 1342 are inconsistent with PNDCL 42 and the Proclamation, 1981 they are void and of no effect whatsoever and any act done under them is void and of no effect whatsoever. Their approval by the government was done under a misapprehension of the conditions precedent to the exercise of the power of approval and was therefore also a nullity.

The Attorney-General as Minister of Justice

In our examination of the various Republican constitutional provisions on the status of an Attorney-General we demonstrated that except for a period under the First Republican Constitution, the qualification, position and the status of an Attorney-General was inextricably interwoven with the position of the Minister of Justice. Since February 1966 the same person has always exercised ministerial responsibility for the ministry of justice and the Attorney-General's Office.42  Even under the Proclamations which have intervened between the various constitutions, the position and status of the Attorney-General has, in fact, always been conjunctive with that of the Commissioner for Justice. The appointment or removal of a minister or commissioner for justice has always been at the pleasure of the government. Thus when the Commissioner for Justice and the Attorney-General was removed from office in 1975, he could not plead the Civil Service (Amendment) Decree, 1975 (NRCD 277) to maintain his supposed unique civil service position of an Attorney-General as distinct from Commissioner for Justice. Section 1(4) of PNDC (Establishment) Proclamation, 1981 confers discretion on the government to appoint such persons as it may consider necessary to take responsibility for any ministry or department of state. Paragraph 12(1) makes it imperative for the Council to appoint an Attorney-General. The government could thus in the exercise of its discretion appoint a Secretary for Justice and Attorney-General. Furthermore, the discretion to appoint and assign ministerial or departmental responsibility is unfettered and the government can in addition to assigning ministerial or departmental responsibility, assign another person responsibility as deputy to the ministerial or departmental head. This explains the appointment by the government of deputy secretaries for ministries or departments. It is our view that sections 1(4) and 12 of the Proclamation puts the government perfectly within its rights to appoint and designate a person as Secretary for Justice and the Attorney-General as it did in early 1982. This is because the Proclamation, 1981, provides only for an Attorney-General who was appointed and designated as Secretary for Justice and the Attorney-General. Any reference to the Deputy Attorney-General or Deputy Secretary for Justice can only be taken to mean any person appointed as deputy to the holder of the office created under paragraphs 1(4) and 12.

The confusion in the content and extent of the powers of the government to appoint and assign ministerial or departmental responsibility has arisen as a result of an attempt to draw an artificial distinction between the Ministry of Justice and the Attorney-General's Department. By that distinction an Attorney-General could have ministerial responsibility, but the Secretary for Justice need not be the Attorney-General. To stretch this argument further is to say that the Secretary for Justice or the ministerial head of the Ministry of Justice need not be a lawyer. In the absence of a clear definition of the functions of the Secretary for Justice and Attorney-General this line of reasoning can only lead to confusion. One may ask whether in such a case the Attorney-General as principal legal adviser to the government would be subordinate to the Secretary for Justice. If the Attorney-General is not a secretary of state but a public servant, subordinate to the Secretary for Justice, then the question would arise as to which of them, the latter or the former represents the ministry at cabinet meetings as legal adviser to government. It is submitted that the only interpretation that will achieve the object of the legislature without introducing inconsistencies, ambiguities and absurdities is that the legislature did not by the Proclamation or PNDCL 42 intend a division of the functions between a Secretary for Justice on the one hand and an Attorney-General on the other.43 In fact such separate offices were never intended. It is our opinion that if it was the intention of the legislature to create two offices it would not only have made separate budgets for them but would have also clearly defined their separate functions as happened, during the colonial era. The only known serious attempts to enshrine the separate and independent doctrine of the office of the Attorney-General in our post-independence history were made by the Constitutional Commissions of 1968 and 197844 but those proposals were summarily rejected by both the 1968 and 1978 Constituent Assemblies.45

It was not accidental that the legislature in providing for the composition of the Police Council in paragraph 27(d) of PNDCL 42 referred to the Secretary for Justice and Attorney-General as a member of the Council. Practice would also appear to support the foregoing conclusions. Suppose the PNDC appointed an Acting Attorney-General by letter without adding that he was also Secretary for Justice, further appointed a Deputy Secretary for Justice simpliciter. If we concede to the argument of separate offices, then one can argue that the Acting Attorney-General is the subordinate of the deputy secretary who was appointed the Deputy Secretary for Justice and must take ministerial instructions from him. The Acting Chief Director of the ministry would also be responsible to the deputy secretary as acting Secretary for Justice and not to his public service colleague, the Acting Attorney-General. Any one acquainted with the work arrangements in the ministry of justice would appreciate the confusion which would have followed the acceptance of that position. A narrow interpretation of the provisions dealing with the appointment and assignment of responsibility to the ministry of justice would mean that all acts of the acting Attorney-General as Secretary for Justice would be null and void. Conversely all acts of the Deputy Secretary for Justice as deputy principal legal adviser to the government would also be null and void. The broader interpretation in our view accords with commonsense.

We believe that it was for this reason that even though there was no specific provision for the appointment of a Deputy Attorney General under the Constitution, 1979, the Deputy Minister for Justice was also designated Deputy Attorney-General without a quo warranto hanging over his head. The foregoing explication of the constitutional and statutory provisions relating to the status and position of the Minister or Secretary for Justice and the Attorney-General leaves us with the conclusion that the government has a discretion to appoint a secretary, and a deputy secretary respectively for justice and who would also be Attorney-General or Deputy Attorney-General and vice versa.

It must, however, be admitted that the argument for an Attorney-General who is separate and independent of a Minister of Justice is theoretically attractive. One of the traditional functions entrusted to an Attorney-General in Ghana is the prosecution of criminal offences. The interest of justice, it is arguable, is better served if the decision to prosecute is made by a person who is not influenced by any political party consideration. An independent Attorney-General with the tenor of a judge of the superior courts would be a bulwark against arbitrariness in exercising the power to prosecute. It is for these reasons that the constitutions of some countries provide that the minister of Justice and the Attorney-General or the Director of Public Prosecutions is not answerable to any person or authority in the exercise of his function in the prosecution of offences.46 It is our view that whether the theoretical argument of the independence or separate and independent office of an Attorney-General is honoured more in their breach than their observance is dependent not on legal provisions but on the stature and personality of the holder of the office.

The independent or separate and independent concept has never been written into any of our constitutions or proclamations. As a matter of convention Anglo-Ghanaian jurisprudence has always insisted that a Minister of Justice and the Attorney-General must exercise independent judgment in criminal prosecutions.

It appears to us that, the real problems that have led to the civil or public service status and the resultant separate office argument for an Attorney-General and his deputy is the emoluments to be attached to that office. A minister, commissioner or a secretary of state need not be professionally paid or contribute to social security or the pension fund, but a civil servant or a public servant is entitled to such job security. Thus any time the years’ standing of an Attorney-General has been raised by law so has there been a proportionate hiking in his equivalent status in the judicial hierarchy and perquisites of office. In reality however the increase in year's standing on the roll of lawyers bears no relationship to competence. The operative words have always laid emphasis on date of enrolment instead of the actual practice of law. Experience and competence as a lawyer can be measured from the latter and not the former.

The constitutional history of this country demonstrates that it has been considered important that the Attorney-General must also be a minister of state or a person of equivalent political status. We have also shown that in circumstances where the Attorney-General has been stated to be a public servant that status has had significance only in terms of his conditions of service. The requirement of years' standing as a lawyer could sacrifice competence and experience to an arbitracy fancy for years' standing at the Bar per se. It is our hope that this article has made a modest contribution to exposing the problems that have beset various attempts at formulating legal provisions on the qualification, position and status of the Attorney-General in Ghana. It could serve as a guide in formulating appropriate and acceptable provisions for that office in the next Republican Constitution. It is our further hope that the legal service will also be given the necessary recognition in the definition of public services under the constitution as the role of a legal service in any democratic process cannot be underestimated.  It is our suggestion that in view of the doubtful legal validity of the Legal Service Regulations which were purportedly made pursuant to PNDCL 42, a substantive law should be enacted now to govern the appointment, promotion, remuneration, dismissal and other conditions of service of the legal service. The Attorney-General and his deputy in our view have historically and realistically and in accordance with existing law not been part of the public service and ought not to be included in any such enactment.47
