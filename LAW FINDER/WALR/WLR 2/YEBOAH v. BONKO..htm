                                                                              YEBOAH v. BONKO.  

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Central Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Cape Coast

Ollennu J.

Succession-Slavery-Devolution of property of deceased ex-slave who has been accepted into ex-master's family-Slave-Dealing A bolition Ordi­nance, c. 109 of 1951.

The plaintiff sought a declaration that she was, by native law and custom, the person properly entitled to be appointed as successor to a deceased person and for an order annulling the appointment of the suc­cessor nominated by the family of the deceased. There was a conflict of evidence as to the relationship between the plaintiff and the deceased and as to the position of the deceased within the family, although it was not disputed that the person appointed by the family to succeed was related to the deceased. The plaintiff claimed that she and the deceased were the daughters of two sisters; that the mother of the deceased had been brought as a slave from the Northern Territories; and that she (the mother) was thus not a member of the family which, therefore, had no claim upon the deceased's estate. The defendant, who was head of the family, denied that the plaintiff and the deceased were first cousins and claimed that, regardless of whether the mother of the deceased had or had not been a slave, she had voluntarily opted to join and had been accepted by the family as a member of it, and that the deceased was therefore a member of the family which was thereby, by the ordinary rules, entitled to appoint a successor to her. The Native Court of first instance found as a fact that the plaintiff and the deceased were not first cousins as alleged by the plaintiff.

At the hearing of the action before the Native Court, the family had been represented, under the provisions of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s. 22, by a linguist of the family who had provided the Native Court with a written authority from the family appointing him as their representative. The linguist, however, did not pg108  a representative capacity but gave evidence as if he were a principal. On the appeal it was argued that a linguist was not a proper person to represent a family in an action of that nature and, that the admission by the Native Court of inadmissible evidence given by the linguist made the proceedings before the Native Court null and void.

Held, (i) the principal issue between plaintiff and defendant was whether or not the plaintiff and the deceased were daughters of two sisters. On sufficient evidence the trial Native Court held they were not. This, therefore, disposed of the claim by the plaintiff to be the person properly entitled to succeed to the deceased.

(ii) If the mother of the deceased had been acquired by the family as a slave then the operation of the Slave-Dealing Abolition Ordinance, c. 109 of 1951 (formerly c. 1 of 1874) had nevertheless made her a free person and as such she had voluntarily opted to join and had 'been accepted by the family as a member of it. The deceased was therefore a member of the family and the family were entitled to appoint a successor t<> her.

Afoley A mbah v. Blabi Libra «1927) Full Court 1926-29, 241) followed.

(iii) The appointment by the family of the successor to the deceased had been carried out in conformity with native custom and therefore could not be disturbed.

(iv) A linguist is a proper person to represent his family in litigation: the Native Court, on being satisfied as to the linguist's written authority so to act, had judicially exercised its statutory discretion in allowing him to represent the family.

(v) The reception of inadmissible evidence given by the linguist did not render the proceedings before the Native Court null and void; inadmissible evidence thus received is to be struck out and disregarded. In this case, after such striking out, there remained sufficient evidence for the Native Court to be justified in reaching the conclusion it did reach.

Kissiedu v. Ankrah «1956) WA.C.A., February 25, 1956, unreported) followed .

APPEAL from a decision of the Magistrate, Winneba, on May 4, 1956, dismissing an appeal from a decision of the Native Court" B " of Agona Swedru on January 31, 1956, giving judgment for the defendant family in an action for a declaration as to the right to succeed to a deceased person's estate and for setting aside the appointment of a successor made by the defendant family.

Kingslcy-Nyinah for the plaintiff.

Sekyi for the defendant.

pg109

 

OLLENNG J. By her writ of summons issued in the Native Court .. B " of Agona Swedru, the plaintiff claimed reliefs which can briefly be stated as follows. A declaration that she is the only surviving maternal cousin and next of kin of one Adjua Penfua deceased, and therefore the proper person to succeed to her estate. In the alterna­tive, an order to annul the appointment of one Kwabina Sarfu as successor of the said Adjua Panfua; an appointment which had been made by the head and members of the Yorko family.

The plaintiff gave evidence to the effect that her mother and the late Adjua Panfuah's mother were sisters, children of a woman named A wuyeh. Her evidence in this respect was supported by her first witness, one Kwaku Nyamekye, a member of the family. It was further supported by her second and third witnesses, Kweku Prah, head of another family, the Nsona family, and Robert Odai Saah, head of another family, the Aburadzie family. Her fourth witness, however, contradicted her on this point and deposed that the plaintiff and the deceased were sisters, the children of one woman, and not cousins as the plaintiff claimed.

As far as the alternative claim is concerned both the plaintiff and her first witness admitted Kwabina Sarfu to be a member of the family to which the deceased belonged. The plaintiff, however, says that Kwabina Sarfu is not in the line of succession to the deceased.

The Native Court by an order made on January 20, 1958, per­mitted one Yaw Kyiribua to represent the defendant. This repre­sentative gave evidence on behalf of the defendant.

The first and second witnesses for the defendant are members of the family. They each gave evidence refuting the plaintiff's claim to be a first cousin of the deceased. They deposed that the mother of the deceased was a domestic to a member of the family; that she had by native custom become a member of the family; and that upon her death intestate the family was entitled to appoint a fit and proper person to succeed to her estate as they would do in the case of any other member of the family.

The Native Court, after hearing the evidence on both sides, rejected the evidence given by and on behalf of the plaintiff and accepted the evidence led on behalf of the defendant.

Whether or not the plaintiff is a first cousin of the deceased-the first issue raised in the case-is a question of fact. That question was determined against the plaintiff, the Native Court holding that the plaintiff's case .. is untrue, conflicting, and fallacious, and is therefore disbelieved."

From that judgment the plaintiff appealed to the Magistrate's Court constituted by the Government Agent, Winneba, on the follow­ing grounds: that the judgment was against native custom and natural justice; that the judgment was contrary to law and equity; that the judgment was against the weight of evidence; and that the judgment pg110 was therefore erroneous and should be set aside and the appeal allowed. No particulars of the native custom and natural justice mentioned in the first ground were given, and no particulars of the law and equity referred to in the second ground were given. However, at the hearing before the Magistrate's Court, counsel for the appellant argued: that the judgment was based on slavery and therefore contrary to law and natural justice; that the Native Court was wrong in permitting the defendant to be represented; and that the representative, giving evidence as if he were the defendant, vitiated the whole of the proceedings and rendered it null and void. The Magistrate dismissed the appeal, holding that the contentions of learned counsel were not born out by the evidence on the record, and the cases cited were not applicable

From that judgment the plaintiff appealed to this court and filed eight grounds of appeal. Before me counsel argued only the first two of those grounds, as all the other six grounds are covered by the first two grounds. The grounds argued were that the procedure adopted by the Native Court in granting leave to Yaw Kyiribua to represent the defendant was irregular and the Magistrate was wrong in saying nothing about it in his judgment after a long argument by counsel on this point, and that the main issue to be decided was the question as to whether Aba Yaa, the mother of Adjuah Panfuah (deceased), was a slave purchased directly from the Northern Territories or that the said Aba Yaa was a woman born at Swedru by Awu Yeh. Learned counsel for the appellant submitted that the Native Court was wrong in allowing Yaw Kyiribua, a linguist to the family, to represent the defendant, because the action was against the defendant in his personal capacity and because the same Native Court had previously, upon an application by Yaw Kyiribua to be made a co-defendant in the suit, held that:

" he the said Yaw IZyiribua, as linguist to the Uoko Family (the defendant's family), has no interest in the case."

I cannot accept these contentions of learned counsel. In the first place the action, as disclosed on the writ and in the whole of the proceedings, is against the defendant in his capacity as Yoko Ebusua­panyin (head of the Yoko family), for acts done by the family as a whole, and not against him personally. Certainly a linguist of the family is entitled to represent the defendant in such an action.

Secondly, the Native Court, in its former ruling to which learned counsel referred, said further:

" Assuming that he (Yaw Kyiribua) is a linguist of the said family he then has to carry an authority from the family to act as its representative as such linguist and not as a co-defendant to claim personal interest." pg111

  Now in support of his application to represent the 'defendant, Yaw Kyiribua produced an authority in writing which is reproduced at p. 24A of the record of appeal If the Native Court was satisfied with that authority, as apparently it was, then I hold that it judicially exercised the discretion given it under section 22 of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c, 98 of 1951, in permitting Yaw Kyiribua to represent the defendant.

Counsel for the plaintiff also contended that even if the permission to represent the defendant was properly granted, the whole of the proceedings were vitiated by the fact that the representative Yaw Kyiribua gave evidence as if he were the defendant himself giving evidence. In support of this submission he cited Adabla v. G'bevlo Agama and Others (1) at p. 168, and Emmanuel Osei v. Asiedu Ofei (2), two cases in which the West African Court of Appeal con­demned the practice. In reply to that argument, counsel for the defendant referred the court to Kissiedu v. Ankrah (3), where it was held that an irregularity of this nature does not make the proceedings as a whole void; it merely renders that particular evidence inadmissible.

That the representative gave evidence as if he were the defendant is clear on the face of the record. But I am not prepared to hold that the whole of the proceedings are vitiated thereby as to become a nullity. Upon the authority of. Kissiedu v. Anhrah, I hold that the evidence of Yaw Kyiribua should be completely disregarded in the consideration of this case. The exclusion of that evidence does not improve the plaintiff's case and therefore cannot affect the decision on the facts. As far as the defence is concerned, the evidence given by the first witness for the defence is precisely the same as that which is excluded. Therefore the situation of the defense remains unchanged.

As to the plaintiff's second ground of appeal, I would say that both the ground of appeal and the arguments advanced thereon are miscon­ceived. The issue raised by the plaintiff is; was the deceased a first cousin of the plaintiff or not. This is a question of fact; the Native Court found against the plaintiff on that issue. There was evidence on the record which, if believed, justifies their finding on that issue.

But even if the. issue of slavery were raised, there is sufficient evidence to show that the mother of the deceased was a domestic who, since the passing of the Slave-Dealing Abolition Ordinance, c. 109 of 1951 (formerly c. 1 of 1874), had voluntarily identified herself with and been recognised and acknowledged a' member of her former master's family, the Yoko Family. Succession to her property, therefore, must follow the same principles as succession to the estate of any free-born member of that family. See the case of Afoley Ambah v. Blabi Libra (4). As I have already pointed out, the plaintiff and one at least of her witnesses gave evidence that the Kwabina Sarfu, who was elected by the family to succeed to Adjuah Panfuah, was also a pg112 relation of Adjuah Panfuah. The procedure for the appointment of a successor to a deceased member of the Yoko Family, as given in evidence by the plaintiff and her first witness, is exactly the same procedure which, according to the evidence, was followed by the family in appointing Kwabina Sarfu the successor to the late Adjua Panfuah. The validity of the Kwabina Sarfu's appointment is therefore beyond question.

I hold that the Native Court was justified in dismissing the plaintiff's claim and entering judgment for the defendant. I also hold that the decision of the Magistrate dismissing the appeal to his court was sound. It is for the reasons given above that I dismissed the appeal on September 24, 1956; and awarded the defendant costs.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
