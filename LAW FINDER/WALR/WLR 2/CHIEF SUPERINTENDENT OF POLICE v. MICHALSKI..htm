June 29, 1956.

                                                        CHIEF SUPERINTENDENT OF POLICE v. MICHALSKI.

          Supreme Court of the Colony of Gambia

Miles J.

 

       Motor Traffic - Driving under the influence of drink - When "special reasons" exist for not ordering disqualification-Motor Traffic Ordinance, c. 155, s. 48 (2).

This was an appeal by way of case stated. The accused had been convicted by a magistrate of driving while under the influence of drink and had been fined but not disqualified from driving. The Motor Traffic Ordinance, c. 155, s. 48 (2) requires that a person convicted under the section (which includes the offence of driving under the influence of drink) sha11 be disqualified from driving for at least twelve months unless " special reasons" exist for the court ordering otherwise. The magistrate in this case had held that the nature of the accused's employment constituted "special reasons" for not ordering disqualification.

Held, "special reasons" within the meaning of the Motor Traffic Ordinance, c. 155, s. 48 (2) must be reasons special to the offence, not to the offender. There must be mitigating features in the circumstances in which the offence is committed: inconvenience or hardship to the offender or his employer are not such mitigating features.

Case referred to :

(1) Whittall v. Kirby [1947] K.B. 194.

APPEAL by way of case stated from a decision of the Magistrate's Court Bathurst on June 11, 1950, convicting the accused of driving under the influence of drink, under the Motor Traffic Ordinance, c. 155, s. 48 (1). The magistrate had fined the accused but had held that" special reasons" existed under section 48 (2) of the Ordinance

for not ordering disqualification.

Lawrence Weston, A.-G., for the Chief Superintendent of Police.

Mahoney for the respondent.

MILES J. This is an appeal by the Attorney-General by way of case stated from a judgment of the acting Colonial Magistrate sitting in the Magistrate's Court at Bathurst. The respondent was convicted 0 driving a motor-vehicle while under the influence of drink contrary  to section 48 (1) of the Motor Traffic Ordinance, c. 155. He was fined £5 but no order of disqualification was made.

Section 48 (2) of the Motor Traffic Ordinance provides that:

" a person convicted of any offence under this section shall, unless the Court for special reasons thinks fit to order otherwise and without prejudice to the power of the Court to order a longer period of disqualification, be disqualified for a period of twelve months from the date of the conviction from holding or obtaining a licence."

The Magistrate did not overlook the provisions of this subsection but he held that special reasons were present in this case, the" being, according to the case, the nature of the defendant's duties al the Atlantic Hotel, Bathurst, which require him to drive a car' connexion with the hotel catering and servicing of aircraft at Yundu~1 airport and particularly the servicing of aircraft.

The question of law which is raised by the case is whether there, was any ground upon which the acting Colonial Magistrate could hold, that there was a special reason within the meaning of section 48 (2 of the Motor Traffic Ordinance, c. 155, for not disqualifying th, defendant from holding or obtaining a licence for a period of at leas twelve months.

The meaning of the phrase " special reasons " has been considered by the courts in England in a number of cases decided under the corresponding section of the Road Traffic Act, 1930. It is sufficient to refer to one of them, namely Whittall v. Kirby (1). That case laid down that the reasons must be special to the offence and not to the offender. That means that there must be mitigating features in the circumstances in which the actual offence is committed. The hardship to the offender, although loss of his licence may mean the loss of his livelihood, or the inconvenience to his employer or to some other persons, are not matters which it is proper to take into account in deciding whether or not to disqualify.

The reasons given in the present case are eminently reasons which are special to the offender and not to the offence and were not sufficient to warrant the Magistrate refraining from imposing what the legislature intended to be the penalty in the case of a conviction for this particular offence.

Mr. Mahoney has asked that the case be sent back to the Magis­trate with a direction to consider whether there were any special reasons connected with the offence but I cannot do that on the case as stated in its present form. If Mr. Mahoney was anxious for that course to be taken he should have amended the case.

I do not propose to lay down what would amount to special reasons in a case of this sort. Indeed Lord Goddard C.J. in Whittall v. Kirby (1) said that it was difficult to visualize any circumstances in which a person would be justified in driving a motor-vehicle when under the influence of drink. It is sufficient to say that the reasons given by the Magistrate were not special reasons within the meaning of the subsection.

The answer to the question raised in the case is " no." The appeal is allowed and the case will go back to the Magistrate with a direction to impose a disqualification for at least twelve months. The exact period is a matter for the :Magistrate.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
