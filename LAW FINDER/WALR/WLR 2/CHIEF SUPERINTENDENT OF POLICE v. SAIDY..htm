CHIEF SUPERINTENDENT OF POLICE v. SAIDY. 

Supreme Court of the Colony of the Gambia

                                                                                             Miles J.

 

   

    Criminal Law-Practice-Lack of particularity of charge-Summary trial­ When lack of particularity fatal to conviction-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 23, s. 119.

Cri Criminal Law-Practice-Charge of attempt to commit a felony-Effect of failure to refer in charge to section creating full offence-Criminal Code,

c. 21, ss. 284 and 365.

The appellant had been convicted of attempted shop breaking. The charge had been drawn under section 365 of the Criminal Code (by which an attempt to commit a felony or a misdemeanor is itself a   misdemeanor and made no reference to the section of the Ordinance (section 284) by which the particular felony attempted is made an offence. In addition the charge lacked particularity in that it did not allege that the attempt at shop breaking was made with intent to commit a felon)" or what that felony was.

Held, (i) the lack of reference in the charge to the section of the Criminal Code creating the full offence was not fatal, because the section under which the charge was drawn in itself created an offence. It is nevertheless desirable, in all cases where the charge is that of attempting to commit a felony 01' misdemeanor, that the charge should in addition specify the section of the Code which creates the full offence.

(ii) In the case of summary trial a lack of particularity in the charge will not necessarily be fatal, provided the charge contains (as it contained here) particulars sufficient to give the accused reasonable information as to the nature of the charge.

R. v. 19binovia «1936) 3 W.A.C.A. 79) applied.

(iii) On the facts, any omission or irregularity that may have existed in the charge did not occasion a miscarriage of justice and therefore, by the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 312, no grounds existed for an interference with the conviction 01' sentence.

Case referred to :

(1) R. v. 19binovia (1936) 3 W.A.C.A. 79.

Appeal against conviction and sentence. The appellant was convicted on June 13, 1956, in the Bathurst Magistrate's Court on a charge of attempted shop breaking under the Criminal Code, e. 21, s. 365.

 

Ames, Acting Attomey-General, for the Chief Superintendent of Police.

    The appellant appeared in person.

MILES J. This appellant was convicted in the Bathurst Magistrate's Court of attempted shop breaking contrary to section 365 of the Criminal Code and was sentenced to four months' imprisonment with hard labour. On the facts there could be no doubt that the appellant attempted to break into a shop in the night with intent to commit a felony. He was in fact, as the trial Magistrate pointed out, caught flagrante delicto.

The only matter which has raised any difficulty in this appeal is the; form of the charge which is laid as follows:

" Attempted shop breaking contrary to section 365 of the Criminal Code.

Particulars of Offence.

On the 12th day of June, 1956, between the hours of 12 midnight and 1 a.m. in the Town of Bathurst you attempted to break into the shop of one Ebrima Jallow of No. 42 Buckle Street."

It is desirable that when a person is charged with attempting to commit a felony or misdemeanor the statement of offence should specify, in addition to section 365, the section creating the full offence. I do not regard the omission to specify the section in this case, where the charge should have been under section 284 of the Criminal Code, as fatal because section 365 does create an offence in itself, but, the charge in its present form lacks particularity. The particulars of offence, moreover, should have alleged that the accused intended to break into the premises with intent to commit a felony, specifying the felony in question.

The point to be considered is whether the omission of these par­ticulars is fatal to the conviction. In my opinion it is not. On this point the decision of the West African Court of Appeal in R. v. Ipbinovia (1) is directly in point. The judgment of K  C.J. was as follows:

" In this case all the elements which go to malie up the separate offences charged in the two counts were not set out in the par­ticulars, and if the trial had been upon information, it would have been necessary to quash the conviction on the authority of R. v. Harvey, 11 Cox 662. But this was a summary trial, and the same considerations do not apply. It is necessary, however, that the accused should have adequate notice of the charge made against him, and the Court is satisfied that in this case he had. It is desirable that all the elements going to make up the offence should be set out in the charges, but in a summary trial an

omission does not vitiate the trial provided the accused knows what he is charged with. On the merits the appellant was clearly guilty .•"

These observations apply in all respects to the present case and there could be no doubt that the appellant at the trial was fully aware of the offence with which he was charged and that the omission could not be said to have occasioned a failure of justice. This is unquestioned· ably a case in which section 312 of the Criminal Procedure Code is applicable. The appeal against conviction is therefore dismissed. The sentence of four months' imprisonment could not be said to be in any way excessive and the appeal against sentence is also dismissed.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
