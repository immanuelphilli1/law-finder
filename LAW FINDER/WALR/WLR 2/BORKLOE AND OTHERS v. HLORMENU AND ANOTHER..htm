                                            BORKLOE AND OTHERS v. HLORMENU AND ANOTHER.                                                                      

                                   Supreme Court of Ghana, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra

                                                                                  van Lare J.

 

 

Practice-Appeals-Power of appellate court to direct retrial by a Native Court-Manner of exercise of discretion-Circumstances in which exercise of discretion will be examined.

The plaintiffs had failed in the trial Native Court in their action for a declaration of title to land. On appeal to the Native Appeal Court the court had, in exercise of its powers under the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s. 50, remitted the case back for rehearing de novo. From this decision the defendants appealed. The original decision of the trial Native Court awarded a declaration of title to the defendants although they had not counterclaimed for one: it was conceded by the defendants that this part of the judgment could not, in any case, stand. The plaintiffs opposed the appeal on the grounds that the Native Appeal Court had judicially exercised its discretion in ordering a rehearing and therefore that its decision could not be inter­fered with. In ordering a rehearing the Native Appeal Court had given its reasons for so doing. These were that the lands claimed by the parties differed from those seen on a view; that the trial Native Court pg 328 must have been influenced in reaching its decision by a perpetual injunc­tion which, being void, should not have been permitted; and that an application by the plaintiffs, before judgment, for amendment of their claim had been wrongly refused.

Held, (i) where a court, charged with a discretion, gives its reasons for the exercise of that discretion, it is competent for an appeal court to examine the reasons given to discover if the discretion has been properly exercised.

(ii) The identity and location of the land was known and not in dispute. If there had not been certainty as to the land this might have given grounds for dismissing the plaintiffs' claim for lack of particularity of subject-matter: the present circumstances disclosed no valid reason for ordering a rehearing.

(iii) The perpetual injunction which, being made by a Native Court, was void and therefore should not have been admitted, related only to that part of the judgment granting a declaration of title to the defen­dants. As this part must in any case be struck out it followed that the perpetual injunction had no relevance, so that its wrongful admission could not constitute ground for ordering a rehearing.

(iv) The Native Court (Colony) Procedure Regulations, 1945, reg. 16, permits amendment during hearing but before judgment only of the " details" of the claim. In the present case the amendment sought was substantially the substitution of a different claim and therefore the trial Native Court were correct in refusing it.

(1) Ofuman Stool v. Nchiraa and Branam Stools (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 229.

APPEAL from a decision on September 19, 1956, of the Anlo Native Appeal Court ordering a rehearing of a case heard by the Aflao/Some Native Court" B " which, on June 2, 1956, gave judgment and a declaration for the defendants in an action for a declaration of title to land.

E. O. Asafu-Adjaye for the plaintiffs.

Apaloo for the defendants.

VAN LARE J. In this case the plaintiffs sued for a declaration of ownership in respect of two swampy areas. The trial Native Court dismissed the plaintiffs' case and entered judgment for the defendants on the facts, which made it abundantly clear that ownership of the two swampy areas is in the defendants' family. The issue that was joined and tried was whether the plaintiffs' families or the family of the defendants owned the areas the subject-matter of the suit. Upon the evidence of the plaintiffs themselves it was admitted that original ownership of the areas in dispute was in the defendants' family but the plaintiffs' case is that the disputed areas had been granted to the plaintiffs' ancestors by the defendants' ancestors. Now the onus became clearly fixed on the plaintiffs to establish that the defendants' family had parted with and/or transferred their ownership to the plaintiff families. It is clear on the evidence that this onus has not pg 329 been discharged and the trial Native Court was therefore justified in entering judgment for the defendants on the real matter in controversy between the parties, and that is the "ownership" of the swampy areas. The defendants' family did not counterclaim but resisted the plaintiff families' claim of ownership by leading evidence to establish that their ancestors were the original owners. The trial Native Court appeared to have considered the merits of the defence and in excess of jurisdiction in its decision decreed ownership in the defendants' family and ordered the plaintiffs to quit and to deliver vacant possession to the defendants. On the authorities it is good law that the trial Native Court was wrong in making such a decree in favour of the defendants and to the detriment of the plaintiffs when no such reliefs were claimed before the court. i'dI'. Apaloo, for the defendants' family, is not prepared to support this portion of the decision of the trial Native Court and I would therefore expunge it from the said decision.

The plaintiffs' families, dissatisfied and aggrieved by the decision of the trial Native Court in dismissing their claim, appealed to the Native Appeal Court, which set aside the decision of the trial Native Court and remitted the case back for a rehearing de novo in the exercise of its powers under section 50 of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951.

'rhe defendants have appealed to this court upon the grounds firstly that the Native Appeal Court was wrong in setting aside the judgment of the trial Native Court since the plaintiffs failed to prove their case and, that the trial Native Court was right in dismissing the suit. I think this is a sound ground for the reasons already indicated, subject to the deletion of the ultra vires declaration in favour of the defendants.

The second ground of appeal, with which I am also in sympathy, is that the Native Appeal Court was wrong in remitting the case back for rehearing de novo because it is not shown that the trial was unsatis­factory and that the discretion of the appeal court was not judicially exercised by the Native Appeal Court.

Mr. Asafu-Adjaye has referred me to Ofuman Stool v. Nchiraa and Branam Stools (1), in which the West African Court of Appeal confirmed the discretion of the Land Judge in remitting a case for rehearing de novo. It has been contended by Mr. Asafu-Adjaye for the plaintiffs before me that the exercise of a discretion must not be interfered with unless it is shown that there is no reason to support the exercise of the discretion in the way it has been exercised. While I agree with this proposition generally it is not quite correct to say that a

. discretion can be held judicially exercised upon the flimsiest reasons.

It is a well-established principle of law that where a court exercises its discretion simpliciter without assigning reasons it is difficult to interfere; but wherever reasons are assigned for the exercise of the discretion a Court of Appeal is competent to evaluate the soundness or otherwise of the exercise of the discretion in' the light of the reasons pg 330 given. Now, in the present case, the Native Appeal Court's reasons for remitting the case for rehearing de novo can be summarised as hereunder:

(1) That lands inspected by the viewers differed in description from the lands claimed and/or pointed out by the parties to the suit.

(2) That a void perpetual injunction was erroneously admitted in evidence by the trial Native Court and must have influenced its decision.

(3) That amendment of the claim sought by the plaintiffs at the close of the proceedings but before delivery of judgment was wrongly refused and should have been allowed by the trial Native Court.

Upon hearing arguments I consider that these reasons do not justify the Native Appeal Court in exercising its discretion in remitting the case back for rehearing de novo because firstly. although the boundaries of the lands inspected did not tally with the boundaries given in the writ of summons and as given by the parties on inspection, neverthe­less the identities of the subject-matter of the suit, that is to say, the two particular swamps, are not in dispute and create no difficulty whatsoever. The location of the respective swamps is known and there is no controversy about it, as the viewers' report indicates. In any case, even if there is no certainty as to the identity or particulars of the subject-matter of the suit, this would be a ground for dismissing the plaintiffs' claim for lack of particularity of subject-matter. The Native Appeal Court must not open the door to a party to do that which he had every opportunity of doing during the hearing of a claim brought by himself.

Secondly, concerning the void perpetual injunction, it is true that it must have influenced the trial Native Court because it purports to be a perpetual injunction granted by a Native Court which is not competent to make such an order; but as that part of the decision of the trial Native Court granting a declaration of title in favour of the defendants and ordering recovery of possession by the defendants is to be expunged, the power of the injunction in the decision becomes removed and ineffective and its admissibility in no way prejudices the plaintiffs' case as to the question of absolute ownership of the disputed areas.

The third reason is also capable of being dismissed because, according to Regulation 16 of the Native Courts Procedure Regulations an amendment can only be entertained in respect of " details" of the claim, and in any case this is within the discretion of the trial court to decide. In my view the amendment sought by the plaintiffs to the claim made after the close of the defendants' case, but before judgment, did not seek to supply any "details" but was rather a 'substitution of a different claim for the original one. I do not agree pg 331 with Mr. Asafu-Adjaye that the amendment was concerned with limiting the nature of "ownership" claimed to one of " possessory title." In my opinion the trial Native Court was justified in refusing the amendment of the plaintiffs' claim as no injustice could result because the effect of the amendment sought was not different in nature from the original claim and that was a declaration of title to the two disputed" swamps."

In conclusion I am of the view that the Native Appeal Court erred in the exercise of its discretion in remitting the case back to the trial Native Court for rehearing de novo as all the evidence for adjudicating upon the plaintiffs' claim of title of ownership has been presented and adjudicated upon satisfactorily and I am unable to say that the trial before the Native Court was unsatisfactory.

In the result I would set aside the decision of the Native Appeal Court and restore the decision of the trial Native Court with a variation to the effect that the following, appearing in the last paragraph of the said decision, be deleted therefrom:

" for title of ownership to the swampy area of lands in dispute. Plaintiffs accordingly ordered to quit forthwith and to give vacant possession and occupation to the herein defendants."

As to the extent as varied in this judgment the decision of the Native Court" B •• of Aflao-Some in this case is restored. The appeal is allowed with counsel's costs in this court. Costs in the court of the trial Native Court to remain. The order for costs in the Native Appeal Court is set aside.

Appeal allowed. Judgment of trial Native Court restored 1rith variation.

S. G. D.
