                                                         WELBECK v. M. CAPTAN LTD. AND HAMMOND. 

          Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Accra

Smith Ag.J.

 

 

           Native Law and Custom - Family law - Appointment of head of family­ Effect of non-attendance of principal members at family meeting-Burden of proof in displde as to validity of appointment

The plaintiff, claiming as the appointed caretaker and administrator of the property of his family, sought to recover the rents of certain family properties that were leased to the first defendants. The second defendant c1aimed to be the duly appointed head of the family and as such to be the person properly entitled to administer the family property. It appeared from the evidence that the second defendant at one time had been the head but had been relieved of his office by the family who, at a family meeting, appointed the plaintiff and one Thompson (since deceased) to administer the family property in his should. Subsequently, at another family meeting, the second defendant (jointly with another person) was reappointed head of the family. This led to litigation between the two factions in two suits that were heard before a Native Court. In the first suit judgment was given for Welbeck and Thompson; in the second suit for Hammond and his co-head, but this latter decision was reversed on appeal on the grounds that the issue was res judicata by virtue of the first Native Court judgment. This was followed by another family meeting which certain principal members of one branch of the family, supporters of Welbeck, refused to attend. At this meeting a resolution was passed appointing Hammond head of the family. For the plaintiff Welbeck it was argued that a dispute existed between the two branches of the family; that these branches had not been reconciled, as they should have been, before a meeting to appoint a head could take place; and, that the absence of principal members of the family from the meeting that appointed Hammond rendered that appointment nugatory.

Held, (i) on the facts the family members who, at the final meeting, appointed the second defendant as head of the family, were representa­tives of both branches of the family. There was thus no question of one branch of a family, in dispute with another branch, attempting to appoint It head of the family in the absence of the second branch and similarly there was no question of reconciliation to be brought about between the two branches before a head could be elected.

Ankrah v. Allotey and Others «1943) Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court, November 27, 1943, unreported) referred to.

(H) It is open to the members of a family, at any time, to make a new appointment to the headship of the family, provided this is done in due form.

(Hi) Although the absence of principal members from It family meeting may render nugatory decisions taken at such a meeting, yet this will not be so if the members concerned have been invited to the meeting but for reasons of their own have not attended.

(iv) Where a member of a family seeks to avoid a decision taken at a family meeting on the grounds that the meeting was not representative or that indispensable principals were not in attendance, the burden of proof is upon him to establish the non-representative character of the meeting or that the attendance of absent members was essential to the validity of the proceedings.

I have referred to Suit No. 1350/48 before the Ga Native Court ,. B," and the reason for dismissing the appeal. In his judgment of October 13, 1951, dismissing the appeal Coussey J. also indicated that he would perhaps have come to the same conclusion as the Native Court on the facts, as it was unreasonable for a member of the family deliberately to avoid attending a meeting and thus hold up the making of an appointment. Mr. Bossman, however, argued that there must be a move to reconciliation and pacification of the rival elements of a family before a meeting can be held to make an appointment. He cited the decision of Quashie-Idun (then acting judge) in Ankrah v. Allotey and Others (2). I think that case is distinguishable in that it only goes so far as to lay down that where two opposing sections of a family have subsequently become reconciled, a meeting of one or other of such opposing sections cannot appoint or remove a head of family. Such an act can only be done by the united family.

It seems to me that foUowing the judgment of the Native Court in Suit 1350/48 and that of Coussey .J. it is open to principal members of a family, to appoint a head in the absence of a principal who refuses to attend a meeting after invitation. ')'he plaintiff and his son in this case refused to attend the meeting although invited. According to the evidence before me " most members of the female and male sides of the family attended .... The interest dealt with was the Kreshie family." There was no dissension. So far as I have evidence the signatories to the document of resolution of November 4, 1951, are representatives of both sides of the family. In view of the evidence on this point for the co-defendant-particu!arly that of Emmanuel Borquaye-it was I think for the plaintiff to show, if such was the case, that the meeting was not representative and that members were absent whose presence was indispensable. AU that I have is the suggestion by the plaintiff that one Ababio was not present. It was not shown that his presence was essential.

I have not taken into account the proceedings at the arbitration before the Gbese Division council. I would dismiss this action by the plaintiff and give judgment for defendant and co-defendant with costs.

The meaning of the phrase " special reasons " has been considered by the courts in England in a number of cases decided under the corresponding section of the Road Traffic Act, 1930. It is sufficient to refer to one of them, namely Whittall v. Kirby (1). That case laid down that the reasons must be special to the offence and not to the offender. That means that there must be mitigating features in the circumstances in which the actual offence is committed. The hardship to the offender, although loss of his licence may mean the loss of his livelihood, or the inconvenience to his employer or to some other persons, are not matters which it is proper to take into account in deciding whether or not to disqualify.

The reasons given in the present case are eminently reasons which are special to the offender and not to the offence and were not sufficient to warrant the Magistrate refraining from imposing what the legislature intended to be the penalty in the case of a conviction for this particular offence.

Mr. Mahoney has asked that the case be sent back to the Magis­trate with a direction to consider whether there were any special reasons connected with the offence but I cannot do that on the case as stated in its present form. If Mr. Mahoney was anxious for that course to be taken he should have amended the case.

I do not propose to lay down what would amount to special reasons in a case of this sort. Indeed Lord Goddard C.J. in Whittall v. Kirby (1) said that it was difficult to visualize any circumstances in which a person would be justified in driving a motor-vehicle when under the influence of drink. It is sufficient to say that the reasons given by the Magistrate were not special reasons within the meaning of the subsection.

The answer to the question raised in the case is " no." The appeal is allowed and the case will go back to the Magistrate with a direction to impose a disqualification for at least twelve months. The exact period is a matter for the :Magistrate.

Appeal allowed.
