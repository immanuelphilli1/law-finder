                           February 27, 1957                                

ASCHKAR v. SOMUAH AND OTHERS.

                                 West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                                Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.J.A.

 

 

Landlord and Tenant-Recovery of possession by landlord-Scheme by land­lord for redevelopment of premises-Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, s. 11 (1) (e).

The defendants were tenants of the plaintiff, who sought an order for recovery of possession under section 11 (1) (e) of the Rent Control Ordinance on the ground that he desired to carry out a scheme of development. The scheme involved, inter alia, the removal of two interior walls dividing the defendants' premises from the rest of the building. The defendants resisted the plaintiff's application on the grounds that the plaintiff had no genuine intention to redevelop and had put forward his scheme as a mere pretext, with the object of evading the spirit and intention of the Rent Control Ordinance; that any scheme of redevelopment must be a scheme in the public interest and not simply in the private interest of the landlord; and that in any case the alterations proposed by the landlord did not amount to a scheme of redevelopment within the meaning of the Ordinance.

Held, (i) the Ordinance is one that restricts the common law rights of landlords, but it does not protect a tenant from eviction in all circum­stances, and if a case comes within the ambit of any of the instances where the statutory protection given to the tenant is expressly removed, it cannot thereby be said that the intention of the Ordinance is defeated. Where the court has reason to suspect the bona fides of the application it has powers under the proviso to section 11 (1) (e) to attach a time limit within which the scheme is to be carried out and to provide for com­pensation for the tenant for non-compliance. In the present case there pg 265 was ample evidence that the landlord had taken steps to implement his scheme.

(ii) The Ordinance does not require, either expressly or impliedly, that the public interest (i.e., as opposed to the private interest of the landlord), shall be a factor for consideration in determining whether or not ejectment will be ordered under the Ordinance to enable a landlord to proceed with a scheme of redevelopment.

Mitchell Y. Townend & Co. [1921] 2 K.B. 91 referred to.

(iii) The true test for determining whether a scheme of redevelop­ment put forward by a landlord is a scheme within the meaning of the Ordinance sufficient to merit the making of an order for ejectment is "can the scheme be carried out if the landlord does not recover posses­sion of the premises." The court is not required to make its decision as to whether a scheme is within the Ordinance on the basis of the extent to which a proposed scheme affects any part of the premises for which the recovery of possession is sought.

Cases referred to :

(1) Mitchell v. Townend & Co. [1921] 2 K.B. 91.

(2) J. W. Smart (Modern Shoe Repairs) Ltd. v. Hinckley and Leicester­shire Building Society [1952] 2 All E.R. 846.

APPEAL from a decision of Quashie-Idun J. on June 30, 1956, in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, granting an order of ejectment against the defendants at the suit of the plaintiff landlord.

Bentsi-EnchiU for the plaintiff.

C. O. Asafu-Adjaye for the defendants.

VERITY Ag.J.A. This is an appeal by the defendants from an order for recovery of possession made in the Land Court on the application of the plaintiff, the defendants' landlord, under section 11 (1) (e) of the Rent Cont!ol Ordinance, 1952. The ground for the application is that the plaintiff requires possession of the premises to carry out a scheme of redevelopment, the requisite notice having been given.

There are a number of grounds of appeal but the combined effect of them is that the learned judge erred in his interpretation of the relevant subsection of the statute and by reason thereof granted relief to which the plaintiff was not entitled, there being, it is submitted, no genuine scheme of redevelopment within the meaning of the enactment.

The facts appear to be that the plaintiff desired to make certain alterations to the building, of one part of which the defendants were tenants, the purpose of the alterations being to render the building suitable for certain business purposes other than those for which they were then occupied. For this purpose certain plans were drawn up and submitted to the Accra Municipal Council, a permit being subse­quently issued authorising the work, the plan for which was approved by the Accra Planning Committee. The work included the removal pg 266 of two of the interior walls which divided the defendants' premises from the rest of the ground floor of the building. It is clear therefore that the plaintiff's plan could not be carried out unless and until he recovered possession of those premises.

The question turns upon whether there was a bona fide requirement on the part of the plaintiff in order to carry out a scheme of redevelop­ment within the meaning of the statute. Particular exception was taken on behalf of the defendants to certain passages in the judgment in which the learned judge said:

" ... the court has no power to say whether the intention was necessary or genuine as long as a landlord has given six months notice of his intention to carry out a scheme of redevelopment and as long as he has also proved that he has taken steps to carry out the said scheme.

" In other words the court has no power to say that the scheme of redevelopment approved by the requisite authority is either unnecessary or not genuine."

The contention on behalf of the defendants is that if the court has no discretion in the matter, the whole purpose and intention of the Ordinance may be defeated and a tenant be evicted at any time at the caprice of the landlord, who need do no more than express an intention to carry out a scheme and give six months' notice to the tenant. I do not think that this contention is well founded. In the first place while it is clear that the intention of the statute is, as stated in the title thereof, "the control of rents and the recovery of possession of premises in certain cases," it must be borne in mind that the statute is one restricting the common law rights of the landlord and that the courts are careful therefore to limit its operation strictly to those " certain cases" to which the words of the statute apply. There is no intention, either express or of necessary implication, to protect a tenant from eviction in all circumstances, for section 11, which pre­cludes the making of any order for recovery of possession of premises to which the Ordinance applies, makes provision for the making of such an order in all cases falling within paragraphs (a), (b), (c), (d), (e), (I) and (g) of subsection (1). Where the circumstances bring the case within the ambit of any of these exceptions the protection of the tenant from eviction is expressly removed, but it cannot be said that for this cause the intention of the statute is defeated.

It is not correct to say, moreover, that the interpretation placed by the learned judge upon the words of the relevant paragraph defeats its intention nor that by reason of that interpretation the tenant may be evicted at the mere will or caprice of the landlord. Whatever may, have been the precise meaning attached by the learned judge to the word" genuine" in the context in which he used it, the implication of caprice or feigned intention is negatived by the succeeding words pg 267 " as long as he has also proved that he has taken steps to carry out the said scheme." It is to be observed that should the court remain in any doubt as to the bona fides of the application, the need for which I do not think the learned judge intended to ignore, it is within the power of the court, by virtue of the proviso to paragraph (e), to impose a condition in breach of which the tenant will be entitled to compen­sation. In the present case the learned judge imposed no such condition, and it is to be assumed that he was satisfied that the appli­cation was made in good faith. That the learned judge was justified is demonstrated by the statement made to us by counsel for the plaintiff that the scheme has in fact been carried out, a statement which counsel for the defendants was not minded to dispute.

It was further contended, however, that the learned judge erred in holding that the court had no power to say that the scheme is unnecessary, and counsel cited the case of Mitchell v. Townend (f; Co. (1), in which the court construed the words" scheme of recon­struction or improvement" where they appear in the Increase of Rent and Mortgage Interest (Restriction) Act, 1920, s. 13' (1) (c). Their Lordships there held that these words refer to some public scheme and do not cover a private project for the substitution of one kind of business premises for another. Were the provisions of the English Act identical with those of the local statute under consideration the contention of the defendants would no doubt be unanswerable for the facts of the present case would fall within the limits of the restrictions imposed by their Lordships' construction, but this is far from being the case. The words of the Act are that" the premises are bona fide required for the purpose of a scheme of reconstruction or improvement which appears to the court to be desirable in the public interest." It is clear that the court in Mitchell v. Townend & Co. (1) interpreted the words" scheme of reconstruction or improvement" in conjunction with the words "desirable in the public interest." No such words appear in the local Ordinance and I am of the opinion that it is not possible to import any question of the public interest in relation thereto. I am fortified in this opinion by the fact that in an earlier enactment, the Rent Control Ordinance, 1947, s. 9 (1) (c), a somewhat analogous provision was made in the following terms:

" ... the premises are reasonably required for any purpose which in the opinion of the court is in the public interest."

I think it is patent that by the repeal of this provision and by the substitution therefor in the Ordinance of 1952 of the words I am now concerned to construe, the legislature clearly expressed an intention to remove from consideration all regard for the particular interest of the public in relation to the right of the landlord to recover possession in the present circumstances. The decision in the case cited is therefore inapplicable, and counsel did not venture to suggest any other means whereby the necessity of the scheme could be  pg 268 determined, nor do I think that any such consideration is raised by the­words of the relevant enactment, which do not even include the words " reasonably requires" as is the case under the immediately preceding paragraph.

Subsequently to the hearing of the appeal counsel for the defen­dants, with the concurrence of counsel for the plaintiff, drew to our attention the case of J. W. Smart (Modern Shoe Repairs) Ltd. v. Hinckley and Leicestershire Building Society (2), but I think it is not necessary to say more than that this decision turns upon the construc­tion of certain provisions of the Leasehold Property (Temporary Pro­vision) Act, 1951, the terms of which are so dissimilar as to render the decision incapable of application to the present case.

Some argument was addressed to the question whether the extent of the alterations proposed by the plaintiff can properly be described as a " scheme of redevelopment" involving as they do in regard to the particular premises demised to the defendants no more than the removal of certain party walls. I do not think, however, that the true test is the extent to which the whole scheme affects that part of the building which is the subject of the particular tenancy where the premises demised are a part only of the whole property to be redeveloped. It is clear that in the present case the scheme for redeveloping the building of which the premises demised formed part could not be carried out unless the landlord recovered possession of those premises and that the matter falls therefore within the provision of section 11 (1) (c) of the Ordinance.

I would therefore dismiss the appeal with costs.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
