                                                           KWESI-WILLIAM v. ACCRA MUNICIPAL COUNCIL.

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Accra

Dennison J.

 

.

      Master and Servant - Conflict of service - Determination by employer _ Conduct inconsistent with due and faithful discharge of servant's duties.- Nature of contract between Municipal council and its employees.

The plaintiff had been employed by the defendant council as a collector of market tolls. On November 7, 1953, the defendants held a surprise check on the plaintiff's books and cash and found a deficiency of £10 14s. 0d. 'The deficiency was investigated by a committee of the council and the plaintiff gave them an explanation, but the committee were dissatisfied with this explanation and the plaintiff was dismissed from his employment. It was established in evidence that this was the second occasion on which a deficiency had been discovered in the plaintiff's books. The plaintiff in an action for damages for wrongful dismissal claimed that the apparent deficiency of £10 14s. Od. had been caused by a scuffle in which he lost toll tickets and which took place on November 4, 1953. He claimed to have reported this verbally to his superior officer, which the latter denied, and to have sent a written report to the Town

. Clerk. This letter however was not written until November 7. On his behalf it was claimed that in the absence of disciplinary regulations governing the conditions of service of municipal employees the General Orders applicable to civil servants should, by analogy, be applied. It' was argued moreover that the council were under a duty to act judicially in considering the case against their employee and that, in declining to accept his reasonable explanation, they had failed so to act.

Held, (i) in the absence of specific provisions governing the terms of the contract of service the common law governing the relationship between master and servant applies to such contracts. As between the Municipal Council and its employees no such special provisions existed and there· fore the ordinary law must apply.

(ii) On the facts of the present case the council could not be said to have acted without good reason in dismissing the plaintiff. The plaintiff', action must therefore fail.


 

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Conduct, inconsistent with the due and faithful discharge by a servant of the duties for which he is engaged, is good cause for his dismissal-Clouston and Co. Ltd. v. Corry [1906] A.C. 122, PC, " There is no fixed rule of law defining the degree of misconduct which will justify dismissal ... the question whether the misconduct proved established; the right to dismiss the servant must depend upon facts-and is a question of fact "-per Lord James, at p. 129. It would appear from recent English cases that although the master must be satisfied that good grounds exist for his summarily terminating a contract of employment, he will not be treated as under a duty to act judicially in so doing-judicially at laest in the sense that certiorari or prohibition may issue where there is a judicial act-see the remarks of Lord Goddard C.J. in Ex parte Fry [1954] 2 All E.R. 118.]

ACTION by the plaintiff for damages for wrongful dismissal under contract of service.

   AIrs. Forster for the plaintiff.

   Enchill for the defendant council.

DENNISON J. The plaintiff claims the sum of £509 6s. 8d. from the defendants in respect of his alleged wrongful dismissal from his pension· able post with the council. Regarding paragraph 2 of the statement of claim it was proved that the plaintiff was employed as a Mosquito Headman in 1939. In 1949, after army service, he was promoted to Market Collector.

The plaintiff stated that whilst on duty collecting licences from the market women on November 4, 1953, an altercation ensued. He stated that he was set on by several of these women and in the course of the scuffle he lost 107 market tickets. The tickets cost 2s. each and there­fore he was short in his accounts for that day in the sum of £10 14s. 0d. That same day the plaintiff was arrested and charged with assaulting one of these market women called Nettey; he was acquitted of this charge by the Magistrate on November 28, 1953. In evidence he stated that on the said November 4 he reported the loss of the tickets to his superior officers Mr. Welbeck and Mr. Mingle; further, that he informed them of the circumstances in which the loss had occurred. At the request of Mr. Welbeck the plaintiff stated he put the facts of his loss into writing-the report was admitted as evidence; it is dated November 4, 1953. It was admitted that it was not sent until November 7, and the reason for the delay was, according to the plaintiff, that he had to get a friend to type the letter. An investigation into the loss was held and as a result the plaintiff was informed by letter that the defendants held him responsible for the loss of the £10 14s. ad. By letter, the plaintiff then asked leave from the defendants to issue a summons against the woman Nettey, but he received no reply to this request.

On December 14, 1953, a letter was sent to the plaintiff from the defendants stating his dismissal was contemplated in view of the auditor considering that he had been guilty of fraud. In reply to this letter the plaintiff gave his explanation of the loss in a further letter. A committee was formed by the council to go into the facts of the. case. The plaintiff was summoned before the Finance Committee in respect of the allegations made against him; he failed to satisfy the committee and as a result he received a letter which informed him that he had been dismissed from his post for the offence for which he . was originally indicted, that is fraud.

It is the case for the plaintiff that this dismissal was wrongful for two reasons. First that his explanation was a reasonable one, also that it was supported by his conduct and the undoubted scuffle in which he was involved on November 4, 1953. Secondly the plaintiff claims that no proper inquiry was made into the charge of fraud, nor was he ever charged in accordance with the General Orders which apply to civil servants. Mrs Forster, for the plaintiff, argued that as on the date in question  no regulations had been made whereby a disciplinary pro­cedure was in force for employees of municipalities the said General Orders should apply, this being so, Mrs. Forster argued, the plaintiff was never properly charged, in accordance with the said General nor was the matter Investigated properly, that is the council did not use its powers of dismissal judicially. I do not agree with this latter argument. In the absence of specific legislation, such as the Medical Act of 1858, referred to in the case of R. v. The General Medical Council, Ex parte Spackman (1), the ordinary law of the land relating to master and servant is what applies and not the said General Orders.

The case for the defendants is that they were justified in dismissing the plaintiff, that they did so only after thorough and due inquiry had been made into the allegations against him, that in no manner was he unjustly treated and wrongfully dismissed.

The defendants proved that a surprise check was made on the plaintiff's books on November 7, 1953, and that this shortage of £10 14s. ad. was found; this they stated was the first intimation they received of the shortage. As stated, the plaintiff said he informed his superiors of the shortage on November 4 but Mr. Mingle, giving evidence, denied that this was so. It was proved in evidence that this was the second occasion on which the plaintiff had had a shortage, his explana­tion in both cases being the same. The coincidence of the letter dated November 4, 1953, not reaching the Town Clerk until after the surprise check had been held and the plaintiff found with this discrepancy of £10 14s. ad. was, to say the least of it, a feature which called for a satisfactory and acceptable explanation and this the defendants found the plaintiff could not provide.

From the oral and the documentary evidence in this suit I am not prepared to hold that the council did not act judicially  or without good reason so as to make the dismissal wrongful. The suit is therefore dismissed with costs to the defendants.

Judgment for the defendants.

S. G. D.

WINDSOR-AumlEY J. The defendant appeals against the decision of the Native Court" B " of Atuabo reversing the decision of the trial court, the Native Court" C " of Esiama.

In my opinion the trial before the Native Court" C " was not altogether satisfactory but it will be unnecessary for this court to consider that aspect if it appears that the Native Court" C " had no jurisdiction to hear the case.

Under the Second Schedule to the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance (c. 98) a grade" C " Native Court has no jurisdiction to hear land cases. Indeed, the Native Court" C " fully appreciated that point, and for that reason held it had no jurisdiction to adjudicate upon paragraph 1 of plaintiff's claim for £25 damages, holding that this part of claim was a land case.

The Native Court" C," however, adjudicated upon paragraph 2 of the plaintiff's claim holding that this matter was a civil dispute. 'fhe plaintiff's claim was as follows:-

Now it is well-settled law that in determining whether an action is

a land suit, the wording of the claim is largely immaterial and that the court should determine what were the real issues before the court. The second part of the case is an " oath case" but any action either relating to land or to a civil dispute may be commenced by swearing an oath. The Native Court " C" seemed to think that an oath is automatically a civil but not a land dispute. That is not so. The defendant had sworn the great oath of Ehanu that the plaintiff should II give to the defendant one third share of any rum or money which plaintiff might receive from his lessees on the land. To this oath the plaintiff responded by this action. The court must ask itself on what ground the defendant claimed a third share of rum or money, paid by the plaintiff's lessees and tenants. It is very clear from reading the record that the defendant claimed tribute by reason of his rights

of overlordship over the land. It is, therefore, to my mind clear that to adjudicate on the part 2 of the claim the court had to determine what rights, if any, of overlordship the defendant enjoyed. Tribute can only arise from some form of overlordship and a claim to such a right is clearly a matter relating to the ownership of land.

I, therefore, hold that both claims were land causes and that Native Court " C " had no jurisdiction to hear the case. Consequently the proceedings before that court and all subsequent proceedings are a nullity and are so declared. There has been no valid trial and the proceedings before all the native trial and appellate courts are set

aside.

Order accordingly.
