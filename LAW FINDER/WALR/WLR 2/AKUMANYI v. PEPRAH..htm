         Succession-Appointment of successor on an intestacy-Nature of right to succeed-Priority as between the sons of sisters of deceased.

           Res judicata-Arbitration by customary proced1Lre-Proof required 01 a.rbitration to establish res judicata in subsequent suit.

This action arose from a dispute between two nephews as to which of them was entitled to be appointed as successor to a deceased intestate. The plaintiff claimed that he had been properly appointed by the family and that this appointment had been confirmed by the head of the family: he sought a declaration ratifying this appointment. The defendant claimed that he, and not the person who had confirmed the appointment of the plaintiff, was the head of the family; that he had not confirmed the appointment of the plaintiff; and that the question of who was to succeed had been settled in two customary arbitrations which made the issue before . the Court res judicata. The Native Court found in favour of the plaintiff, holding that his appointment had been validly made in accordance with native law and custom and, that the two arbitrations could not operate as res judicata.

Held, (i) by the custom of the locality concerned the son of the elder sister has priority as regards the right to succeed over the son of the younger sister, irrespective of the relative ages of the two men. This was a finding of the Native Court which could not be disturbed, particularly in view of the apparent conflict of opinion expressed in the authorities (Sarbah, Fanti Customary Law).

(ii) In any event the right to succeed is not an absolute right. The power of appointment vests in the family, who can pass over a person with a prior right to succeed if they are satisfied in the family interest pg113

that it would be desirable to do so. In the present case the plaintiff had been validly appointed by the family and as no sufficient cause had been shown why they should not so appoint him, it was not open to the court to interfere with this appointment.
 

[EDITONAL NOTE. It is clear that the right of a person to succeed is stronger than a mere right to be the first person considered by the family for the appointment, for it is desirable that the holder of property should know who may be expected to succeed him. If he feels that the person entitled to succeed to his self-acquired property would be unsuitable it is open to him to make a will. On the other hand, as the appointed successor is custodian of the family property on behalf of the family, it is equally desirable that the family should, for sufficient reason, have power to displace the order of succession. ]

(iii) For the decision of a customary arbitration to operate as res judicata in a subsequent suit it must be established to the satisfaction of the court that: the arbitration was a valid one; the parties to it were the same as those in the subsequent suit; and the issues raised and determined in the arbitration were substantially the same as those in the suit before the court.

APPEAL from a decision of the Magistrate, Winneba, on August 3, 1956, dismissing an appeal from a decision of Gomoa Ajumako Native Court " B " on December 29, 1954, in favour of the plaintiff in an action on the right to succeed to a deceased intestate. The action had been originally commenced in, but was transferred from the Native Court" B " of Agona Swedru.

Sekyi for the plaintiff.

Hayfron-Benjamin for the defendant.


 

OLLENNU J. This is an appeal from a judgment of the Magistrate's Court constituted by the Government Agent, Winneba, sitting as an appeal court on a judgment of the Native Court "B " of Gomoa Ajumako. The claim made by the plaintiff before the Native Court in substance is for a declaration that he had been properly appointed as successor to two deceased members of his family.

The defences to that claim were first, that Kwami Nyame, who had made the appointment, was not the head of the family, the Ayensuadzi 'l'widan family; second, that the defendant was a senior member of the family, in fact the head of the family, and therefore the plaintiff's appointment by Kwame Nyame was void; and third, that in any event the matter was res judicata by reason of two arbitrations, one held by the Oman of Ayensuadzi and the other by the Omanhene of Gomoa Ajumako State. The Native Court gave judgment for the plaintiff, holding that his appointment was in accordance with native custom and that the acts of the Oman of Ayensuadzi and of the Omanhene of Gomoa Ajumako cannot operate as res judicata. The defendant's appeal to the Magistrate's Court was dismissed and his counsel has appealed therefrom to this court. pg114

Five grounds of appeal were filed. Mr. Benjamin, for the defendant, abandoned the first and the fifth of those grounds and argue the second, third and fourth. The third ground is that the judgment is against the weight of evidence, and the fourth is that the judgment is contrary to Native Custom.

As to the weight of evidence, I must say at once that counsel ha not been able to show that this case comes within the exception where a court of appeal will be entitled to set aside findings of fac made by a court of first instance and substitute its own finding upon the evidence. There is therefore no substance in that ground.

On the third ground counsel referred me to Sarbah, Fanf Customary Laws, 2nd ed., pp. 101-102, and in reply to submission: made by counsel for the plaintiff to paragraph 2 of p. 104 of the sam book. He argued that since the plaintiff admitted himself to b younger in age than the defendant, his, the plaintiff's appointment  succeed to the deceased members of the family in preference to th defendant, was contrary to custom as laid down in Sarbah. Mr. Sekvt counsel for the plaintiff, in reply to this argument referred the co~J to pp. 104-105 of Sarbah, where it is stated that as between nephew~; entitled to succeed, the son of the elder sister though younger in a~ than the son of the younger sister has preference in the matter d succession to the estate of a deceased uncle. The Native Court faun: as a fact that the custom of the area where the case arose is that between nephews the seniority must be determined by the seniority' of their mothers. I cannot interfere with that finding of fact, paticularly as the passages in Sarbah to which I have been referred b' learned counsel for both sides seem to conflict. In any case succession' is not absolutely as of right. The family are entitled to appoint younger man to supersede an elder member in certain cases. It I, clear on the evidence led for both sides that the appointment of th! plaintiff was with the concurrence of the members of the family~ The appeal therefore cannot succeed on the fourth ground of appeal

The second ground of appeal raises a question of law, namely that the claim is res judicata by reason of the fact that the matter had bee: finally decided at two arbitrations, one by the Oman and the other~ by the Omanhene as stated above. To uphold this contention I must I be satisfied among other things that there has been an arbitration' properly so called, i.e. a valid arbitration, that the parties to t arbitration are the same as the parties to the suit before me and, that the issues raised in this suit are substantially the same as the~ determined at the arbitration. Learned counsel for the defender has not been able to satisfy me on these points. It is not enough to say that there has been an arbitration. If a party relies upon an arbitration as a defiance he must prove that there was a prop submission of the dispute, that the parties willingly agreed to the matter being arbitrated upon and so on. It must be observed the pg115 the defendant never put his defence of arbitration to the plaintiff and his witnesses in order that the Native Court might have opportunity to investigate it properly. The matter was only clearly raised when the defence came on. Moreover the account of the arbitration as given by the Omanhene, the fourth witness for the defence, is materially different from that given by the defendant himself. Also it is not clear who the parties were to the two arbitrations; it appears from the evidence of most of the witnesses that Kwami Nyame, the head of the family, and the defendant, but not the plaintiff and the defendant" were the parties at the so called arbitration. Also, as to the issue before the so called arbitration it appears that the headship of the family was the issue dealt with.

 

In these circumstances I find myself unable to agree with the submissions of learned counsel for the defendant. The defendant has consequently failed to discharge the onus which was upon him to prove that the judgment of the Native Court was wrong; his appeal must therefore be dismissed. I dismiss it accordingly and award costs.

Appeal dismissed.
