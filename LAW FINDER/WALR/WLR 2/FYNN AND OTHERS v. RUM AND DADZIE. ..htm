March 15, 1957

                          FYNN AND OTHERS v. RUM AND DADZIE. .                                

                    Supreme Court of Ghana, Central Judicial Division, Land Court, Cape Coast

                                                                                          Ollennu J

 

 

   Native Law and Custom - Severance of family ties - Cutting "Ekar"­Family property undivided-Requisites for division of family property

  This action arose from a dispute between two branches of a family living in different localities. The head of the family had attempted to dispose of part of the family property without obtaining the consent of the principal members of one of the branches. In an action brought on behalf of this branch for a declaration that the land concerned was family land and could not be disposed of without the consent of the pg 290  principal members of each branch, it was argued by the head of the family that the objecting branch had severed its ties with the rest of the family and had thereby lost its right to participate in the disposal of family land.

Held, (i) disagreement or disaffection between members or branches of a family does not alone suffice to sever family ties. To be effective as severance there must be some clear and unequivocal acts showing the intention of both sides that severance shall take place. One of such acts of severance must be the partitioning of family property.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Although an independent and disassociated course of conduct is not sufficient, without more, to destroy the family tie, it does not seem open to doubt that it can afford evidence, perhaps in circumstances where no other evidence is available, that the formal act of severance has in fact taken place.]

(ii) Where family property is being partitioned on severance the act of partitioning must be performed on both sides by those principal members of the family, whose concurrence would otherwise be required to the disposition or alienation of such property.

(iii) The right and entitlement of branches, or individual members, of a family to an interest in the family property can be abandoned by voluntary acts, amounting to less than formal severance, on the part of the branch or individual concerned. Such voluntary acts, if by a branch or section of a family (but not necessarily by an individual) must include a formal declaration of the intention to abandon the family ties.

Cases referred to :

(1) Sackey v. Okantah (1916) Divisional and Full Court Judgments, 1919, p. 88.

(2) Okaikor v. Opare and Mensah (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 275.

(3) Amoabimaa and Another v. Badu and Others (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 227.

ApPEAL from the decision of the Asebu Native Court •. B " given on September 21, 1956, giving judgment for the defendant in an action for a declaration as to the ownership of certain family lands.

Abadoo for the plaintiff. Short for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. This is an appeal from judgment of the Asebu Native Court .. B " Moree.

The claim in substance is for a declaration that: land situate at Moree known and called .. Abramadzie " is the property of the Odumna family: that the Odumna family consists of three sections, namely, the Moree, Queen Anne's Point (Ekon), and Cape Coast sections: and that the head of the family at Moree cannot dispose of or otherwise deal with the said lands without the consent and concur­rence of the principal members of the family in all the three towns.

The defendant admitted that the land in dispute belongs to the whole family. But he disputed the right of the principal members of pg 291the Cape Coast and Queen Anne's Point (Ekon) sections to be consulted in dealing with it because he said they had within recent years severed connections with the family at Moree and had not been paying funeral and other family debts which arise at Moree.

The Native Court, after reviewing the evidence, made the following findings of facts: that the Odumna family had their origin from Moree; that Robina Rum, the defendant, is a blood descendant of Taakyi Bessah, the founder of the Abramadzie land ... and also is senior in line of succession; that the plaintiffs had some years previously severed their connections with the family at Moree and had formed themselves into a new family set with less to do with the Moree family; and that Kobina Rum the defendant is the accredited head of the family, having been unanimously selected as such to succeed one Kojo Annor. They then declared that the defendant as head of the Odumna family at Moree has the absolute right over the Abramadzie land, and as his elders in Moree have not questioned his acts they should enter judg· ment in his favour. There is abundant evidence led by and on behalf of the plaintiff himself in proof of the first and fourth facts found by the Native Court, namely, that the family originated from Moree and it is by marriage of female members of the family from Moree to Ekon and Cape Coast that the branches there sprang up, and that the defendant was appointed head of the entire family by the unanimous consent of all the three sections. In view of that evidence the findings of the Native Court on those two points cannot be disputed by the plaintiff. It also follows that the second fact cannot be seriously contested.

One of the grounds of appeal argued by Mr. Abadoo, learned counsel for the plaintiff, is that the Native Court wrongly rejected certain documents, namely, judgments of courts of competent jurisdiction which declare the land to be the property of the whole family. Had the defence denied the fact that the property is owned by the whole family, this point would have been important. But since that fact was conceded by the defence the rejection of those documents is immaterial.

The main point upon which the appeal turns is the third finding made by the Native Court. Severance of family ties is a very grave and serious matter which rarely occurs among our native communities; owing to its gravity, significant special forms and ceremonies are required to effect it, and the widest publicity must be given to its performance. The matter is discussed in Sarbah, Fanti Customary Law, 2nd ed.. , pp. 33-36. The Ga (Accra) form of it, called " tako mlifo," was discussed in the old case of Sackey v. Okantah (1) and by the Land Court, Accra, in Olwiko1' v. Opare and M en,~ah (2); and it was also dealt with indirectly by the West African. Court of Appeal in A moabimaa and Another v. Bad'll and Others (3). pg 292

In my opinion mere disagreement or disaffection between members or sections of the family is not enough evidence of breaking of the blood relationship. There must be clear evidence of real intention permanently to " cut" the relationship, and thereby make the parties strangers to one another. Like marriage according to native custom, which has the opposite effect, namely, uniting two different families, the act of breaking a family requires special formal rites. In the case of a family owning ancestral property the formalities of the separation must include partitioning of the family property, otherwise all the new, separate families will retain their inherent rights in the property unless there is a public declaration by a section disclaiming interest in the said property, or unless the severance of the relationship is nothing more than a few members breaking away, leaving. the family as a whole intact.

I am of the view that the third finding of the Native Court does not amount to a finding that the family ties have been broken, I am further of the opinion that if by that finding they meant that the family ties had been broken, that finding is not in accordance with native custom, and I am further of the opinion that the said finding is not in accordance with native custom. The evidence on the record does not show either that the ancestral property has been partitioned, or that the Cape Coast and Ekon sections of the family have abandoned or forfeited their rights in the ancestral property. As far as I can see, their interest remains intact.

The declaration of the Native Court that the defendant as head of the family has an absolute right over the property is misdirection. By native custom the head of the family holds the family property as trustee for the family and not as an absolute owner. To be valid, his dealings with it must be with the consent and concurrence of the principal elders of the family. Any disposition he makes of the land without the requisite consent can be set aside at the instance of the principal members of the family.

In my opinion the people who can make a valid election of a breach of the family must of necessity be the same principal members of the family whose consent and concurrence is necessary in dealing with the family property.

According to the co-defendant the election of the defendant as head of the family was valid because it was made by the whole family, and he defined the whole family as " I mean the Odumna family at Ekon, Queen Anne's Point, Brafu Yaw, Moree and Biriwa and Cape Coast." The Native Court held the election valid, implying that those who did it were principal members of the family. Therefore, for the defendant's dealings with the family land to be in accordance with native custom' and therefore valid, they must be with the consent and concurrence of those principal members of the family including those represented by the plaintiff. pg 293


    It follows that the plaintiff should have been given the declaration he sought, and that his appeal should succeed. I therefore allow the appeal, set aside the judgment of the Native Court, including the order as to costs, and substitute therefor judgment for the plaintiff for a declaration that the Abramadzie land is the property of the whole of the Odumna family including the Moree, the Ekon, and the Cape Coast sections, and that control of the said land is vested in head thereof who, in accordance with native custom, should deal with the same with the consent and concurrence of the principal members of all the component sections of the family or of such of the elders at Moree as would be empowered by all the said principal members.

I would end by commending to the parties the advice of the Native Court as to reconciliation among members of the family; and would add that this should be done in a spirit of goodwill on all sides for the benefit of the family as a whole and not for individual or sectional gains.

The appellant will have his costs in the Native Court, to be taxed, and his costs in this court.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
