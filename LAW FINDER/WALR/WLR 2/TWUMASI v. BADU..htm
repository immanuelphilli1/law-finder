                                                                TWUMASI v. BADU.

                                                      West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                                   Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.JA

 

 pg 204


Native Law and Custom-Arbitration-Essential characteristics distinguish­ing customary arbitration from negotiations for a settlement.

The plaintiff sought, in effect, recovery of possession of certain premises on the grounds of failure by a subtenant, through whom the defendant claimed, to fulfil the terms of a tenancy agreement. The dispute was taken to a third party and the case turned upon whether this constituted a binding reference to customary arbitration.

Held, the essential difference between a binding arbitration and a reference to a third party for the purpose of seeking a negotiated settle­ment is the presence or otherwise of a prior agreement to be bound by the decision of the arbitrator or negotiator. Whether this exists or not is a question of fact to be determined by the evidence: in the present case there was no evidence of a prior agreement to be found.

Ankrah and Others v. Dabra and Olaga [1956] 1 W.A.L.R. 89 followed.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Ankrah and Others v. Dabra and Olaga, quoted above, sets out in detail the three essential requirements of a binding customary arbitration and contrasts this procedure with negotiations for a settlement. Whether or not a reference to a third party comes into the one class or the other depends entirely on the facts as disclosed by the evidence. The approach of the customary arbitrator or negotiator to his task may be the same in each case: it is possible that he is concerned primarily with conciliating the parties and reaching a solution amenable to both. This approach can be distinguished from that of an arbitrator appointed under the Arbitration, Ordinance, c. 16 of 1951, who will be concerned principally with reaching a decision on the merits of the case presented to him. It may be that the use of the one word" arbitration" to describe these two different procedures has led to some confusion in the past.]

Cases referred to :

(1) Ayafie v. Banyea (1884) Fanti Law Report 38.

(2) Gyesiwa v. Mensah (1947) W.A.C.A. Cyclostyled Judgments, 1947, p.45.

(3) Ankrah and Others v. Dabra and Olaga [1956] 1 W.A.L.R. 89.

APPEAL from the decision of Manyo-Plange J. on June 20, 1955, in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, reversing the decision of the New Tafo Native Court" A," on December 2, 1954, in favour of the defendant in an action for a declaration of title and for damages.

Baidoo for the plaintiff.

Danquah for the defendant.

VERITY Ag.J.A. This is an appeal from a judgment of the Land Court allowing an appeal from the judgment of a Native Court dis­missing the plaintiff's claim in an action for a declaration of title and damages. pg 205

It is unnecessary to deal in any detail with the merits of the plaintiff's claim or the defence thereto in view of the fact that the judgments in both the courts below turned in the first instance upon the question as to whether or not the issue between the parties had been determined by an arbitrator's award. Counsel for the defendant conceded that if there had been no arbitration he would have difficulty in supporting the case set up by the defendant on its merits. It may be stated briefly however that the plaintiff's case rested upon a right or re-entry as tenant-in-chief upon the default of the subtenant, through whom the defendant claims, in fulfilling the terms of the tenancy agreement.

The evidence is to the effect that upon re-entry by the plaintiff the defendant complained to a person, who describes himself as a ., leader of the people owning land in that area," that the plaintiff had wrongly taken possession of the land in dispute. The plaintiff and the defen­dant appeared before this person who heard witnesses and made an award against the plaintiff. Although the plaintiff denies it, it would appear that the trial court accepted that he paid the sum of twelve shillings as what was described as a " stamping fee" but there appears little doubt that the plaintiff did not accept the award as binding and, after an abortive complaint to the chief, brought a suit in the Native Court.

The trial court held that a binding award had been made by an arbitrator which had not been set aside and entered judgment for the defendant. The plaintiff then appealed to the Land Court which, holding that there had been no arbitration valid according to Native Customary law, decided on the merits in favour of the plaintiff. Hence this appeal.

It was conceded by counsel for the defendant that in order to constitute a valid arbitration there must be a voluntary submission ,by both parties coupled with an agreement to abide by the award but it was contended that it is unnecessary to prove strictly any formal submission or agreement as these may be inferred from the conduct of the parties. Counsel relied in support of his contention upon the judgment of Lesingham Bailey C.J. in Ayafie v. Banyea (1), a case decided so long ago as 1884, in which case the learned Chief Justice expressed himself as being satisfied upon the facts that the defendant therein consented to his cause being referred to arbitration.

Since 1884 there have been a number of decisions of courts in the Gold .Coast on this question in more than one of which the matter has been considered at greater length than appears in the reported judgment in the case cited, although I do not think that the principle adopted in that case has been affected thereby. In 1947 this court dealt with the essentials to an arbitration according to customary law in Gyesiwa v. Mensah (2)pg 206. While declining to lay down precise rules as to such essentials or the requisite procedure I then said:

.. it is, however, clear from many decisions that there must be not only a voluntary submission of the matter in dispute but also it must be shown that it was agreed by both parties that the decision of the arbitrators will be accepted as final and binding."

I proceeded:

.. as to precisely what evidence will satisfy the court that there has been such a submission and such an agreement, that will depend upon the circumstances of the case and upon such evidence as may be adduced to establish that the requisite formalities (if any) have been observed in accordance with native law and custom. "

The matter has been dealt with again by this court as recently as last year in Ankrah and Others v. Dabra and Olaga (3) on appeal from a judgment of Wilson C.J. who had considered the matter at consider­able length. In his judgment the learned Chief Justice stated that there were three essential characteristics of such an arbitration: a voluntary submission to arbitration, a prior agreement to be bound by the award and a publication of the award; and in considering whether or not there existed in that case these characteristics his Lordship was careful to distinguish between a submission to arbitration and an expression of readiness to enter into negotiation for a settlement.

This court held that it was not possible to say that the learned Chief Justice was not justified in holding that there had been no arbitration the award upon which was binding but that

., the submission was to arbitrators who were to enquire into the dispute and attempt to effect a settlement on terms which would prove acceptable to both sides and that this was a proceeding recognized under Native Customary law."

It does not appear ever to have been disputed that in addition to the submission there must be an agreement to abide by the award, this being the essential distinction between an arbitration binding upon the parties and a reference to some third party for enquiry in an attempt to effect an acceptable settlement and I am of opinion that it is most desirable that this well-recognzed distinction should be maintained.

The real question to be determined in this case is therefore not whether any particular formalities have been complied with but whether upon the evidence the court is satisfied that there was not only a reference of the dispute to the third party but also a prior agreement that the award of the third party should be binding. The learned judge of the Land Court after a careful and detailed examina­tion of the evidence reached the conclusion that  pg 207. there is no evidence that the appellant expressly or by his conduct agreed to accept as final any judgment or decision of • the third party' and be bound by it  and although the learned judge in this respect differed from the view of the facts taken by the Native Court I incline to the view that he was right. The trial court does not appear to have addressed its mind to this aspect of the matter, but merely to have accepted that there was an arbitration upon which an award had been made and to have based its judgment upon the fact that the award had not been set aside and therefore was binding. This finding appears to beg the question which, in view of the plaintiff's denial, was whether or not there had ever been a valid arbitration and not whether, assuming such an arbitration, its award was binding

As in my opinion the learned judge was right in holding that the evidence did not disclose that there had ever been such an agreement to accept a decision of the third party as binding as would constitute a valid arbitration, and as I agree with his findings on the merits of the case set up by the defendant I think he was right to allow the appeal and set aside the judgment of the Native Court. I would therefore dismiss the present appeal with costs.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

KORSAH C.J. I concur.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
