                           February 22, 1957                                

REGINA v. GRUMAH. .

                                 West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                                Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.J.A.

 

pg 255

  Criminal Law-Insanity-Insane delusion at time of commission of crime - Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 52 (2)-Criminal procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 137.

     The appellant, who had been convicted of murder, had admitted to killing the deceased but had pleaded "not guilty," claiming that at the time he had been under a delusion that the deceased was about to kill him. When the appellant had been first arraigned, medical evidence had been given to the effect that he was unfit to plead; the trial had been twice postponed in consequence. Some seven months after the first arraignment the trial was commenced, without any further enquiry as to the state of the appellant's mind. A psychiatrist called for the defence, however, was of the opinion that at that stage the appellant was fit to plead.

        Held, an insane delusion, for the purposes of the Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 52 (2), is a state of mind, induced by mental disease, in which a false belief is held that cannot be shaken by facts. In this case there had been no sufficiently adequate direction to the jury on the subject of insane delusion. If there had been a sufficient direction, then, on the facts, the jury might probably have returned a verdict of guilty but insane. For this reason the special verdict of guilty but insane should be substituted for the verdict of guilty.

       [EDITORIAL NOTE. The Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 137, which is linked with the Criminal Code, s. 52 (2), is concerned with a person, who is "insane so as not to be responsible ... for his actions." The latter section applies the procedure of the former section in cases where an accused is "under the influence of an insane delusion of such a nature as to render him ... an unfit subject for punishment .... " It would thus appear that the Ordinance is concerned principally with whether or not the insanity is of such a nature as to make punishment pointless: it is not concerned with the form the delusion takes. Thus, A kills B while under the insane delusion that B would otherwise kill him, and A kills B while under the insane delusion that B is his brother: the section is equally applicable in each case.]

   APPEAL from a conviction for murder on January 10, 1957, before Acolatse J. and a jury at Sekondi Assizes.

     Unger, Crown Counsel, for the Crown.

     Anoba for the appellant.

      The following judgment of the court was given by COUSSEY P.

      The appellant was charged with the murder of a youth, Kwame Boadu alias Kwami Dappo, at Nkrondua village in the Western Judicial pg 256 Division on February 23, 1956. When he was arraigned on June 6, 1956, the trial was adjourned by the court for a medical statement as to his state of mind. On June 29, 1956, the psychiatric specialist reported that in his opinion the appellant was unfit to plead. On September 12, 1956, the court was of opinion that the appellant was still incapable of making his defence, owing to his state of mind, and thereupon postponed the proceedings for further medical observation of the appellant. On January 2, 1957, the appellant was again arraigned before the same judge at the Sekondi Assizes. There is no record of any further enquiry regarding the state of the appellant's mind nor of a certificate as to his fitness to plead, but on that day the appellant apparently pleaded" not guilty" to the charge and the trial proceeded.

The case for the prosecution was that while the appellant, a farm labourer, was working with the deceased and another farm labourer, the second witness for the prosecution, on their employer's farm, the appellant suddenly attacked the deceased with the cutlass he was holding and inflicted the wounds to the head and back of the neck of the deceased from which he died. There was no apparent motive for this sudden attack.

When charged with the crime the appellant made a voluntary state­ment and he also gave evidence on oath at the trial.

The defence, according to the statement of the appellant made to the police, was that at the farm the deceased suddenly told him that before sunset one of them would either live or die, that the appellant became afraid and believed there was a plot to kill him and, being afraid, he killed the deceased, before his turn came, with the cutlass he was then holding. Giving evidence on oath the appellant said he had been told by the deceased that he would be killed before going to the farm and he was afraid to accompany the deceased to cut plantains because he had a thorn in his foot and also because he was afraid the deceased would kill him if he went with him. He also said the deceased raised a cutlass before the appellant cut him with his cutlass.

The defence called as a witness the psychiatric specialist attached to the Mental Hospital at Accra who was of opinion that the appellant was fit to plead, but who could not speak as to the appellant's state of mind at the time of the act charged. He testified that a delusion is a symptom of mental disturbance and is a false belief which is unshakable by facts. "It would be a delusion," he said, " if a person believes he is going to be killed if in fact he was not going to be killed ... a delusion is a disease of the mind. "

It is not clear from the summary before us of the learned judge's summing-up to the jury, that he sufficiently distinguished the defence of self-defence, when the deceased allegedly attacked the appellant, from the defence of insane delusion, namely, that he believed he was pg 257 about to meet his death at the handsof the deceased whereupon he struck the deceased whilst that delusion was operating on his mind.

We think that if there had been a correct and adequate direction the jury might probably, on the evidence, have found that the offence was committed by the appellant under the influence of an insane delusion and have returned a verdict of guilty but insane-see the Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 52 (2) and the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 13'7. Upon the whole case we think it would not be right to allow the verdict to stand. We accordingly set aside the verdict and sentence of death and substitute the special verdict that the appellant was guilty of the act charged but was insane at the time he did the act. It is ordered that the appellant be detained in custody at the Mental Hospital at Accra during Her Majesty's pleasure.

Special verdict of guilty but insane substituted for verdict of guilty.

 

S. G. D.
