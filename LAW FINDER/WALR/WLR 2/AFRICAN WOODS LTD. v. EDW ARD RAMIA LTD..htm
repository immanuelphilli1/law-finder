May 19, 1956.

AFRICAN WOODS LTD. v. EDW ARD RAMIA LTD.

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Ashanti Judicial Division, Land Court, Kumasi

Benson J

 

 

       Concessions - Grounds for refusing certificate of validity - Concessions Ordinance, c. 136, s. 13 (2)-Concession conferring rights already the subject of a prior agreement between,. grantors and a third Party.

In May, 1953, Edward Ramia Ltd. entered into an agreement which purported to be a concession over certain lands in Ashanti. This agree­ment was not negotiated before a government agent as is required by the Concessions Ordinance, c. 136, s. 12, and for this reason was subse­quent1y held by the West African Court of Appeal never to have come into existence as a concession. In October, 1953, African Woods Ltd. entered, in due form, into an agreement with the same grantors by which they received a concession over substantially the same land as that of the Edward Ramia agreement. In each case the agreement was concerned with timber. During the statutory enquiry on the claim by African Woods for a certificate of validity it was asserted by the grantors that they had been under a misapprehension as to the location of the land involved and that they would not have entered into the second agreement if they had realized their error.

Held, (i) it is the duty of the court, at a validation enquiry, to ascertain that the parties to a concession understand precisely their obligations under it. A misapprehension by the grantors of the area of land affected by the concession is a misunderstanding of their obligations sufficient to inhibit the court from ,validating the concession.

(ii) The existence of a prior agreement, binding on the parties thereto, and affecting the land the subject of a subsequent concession, is not sufficient grounds for refusing to validate the subsequent concession, provided the grantors knew of their liability under the prior agreement.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It may be that the courts, in deciding if a grantor of a concession had know ledge of the binding effect of a prior agreementt entered into by him, will extend this principle to cases where the grantor ought to have known of such effect.

The Concessions Ordinance, c. 136, s. 13 (11), is misleading in that it suggests that an agreement concerning land in Ashanti that is not made in accordance with section 12 of the Ordinance is nevertheless a concession that will be pronounced as valid or otherwise at the statutory enquiry. The 'Vest African Court of Appeal in Edward Ram ia Ltd. v. African Woods Ltd. (1) (the judgment referred to in this case) makes it clear that where section 12 is not complied with, an agreement never comes into effect as a concession at all. An agreement falling under section 12 must therefore be distinguished from a concession that is null and void under section 8 (e.g., for failing to serve notices of the concession on the court) or that is not validated by the court for some reason contained in section 13 or elsewhere. In these instances, a concession has clearly had a period of effective life, from the date it is entered into until the date it becomes null and void or invalidated. This is explicitly recognized by section 19, and it is no doubt the point the learned judge had in mind in referring to prior agreements. But, and notwith­standing the obiter remarks of Coussey P. in Edward Ramia Ltd. v. African Woods Ltd. (1) to the effect that an agreement that never came into existence as a concession might still be effective as a demise of land and contain binding covenants, it is difficult to see that an agreement that flatly ignores the statutory requirements in the public interest set out in section 12 can ever be effective or enforceable as between the parties to it (unless perhaps it contains severable covenants)-see the remarks of Scrutton L.J. in Anderson v. Oaniel [1924] 1 K.B. 138 at p. 147. And this view is reinforced by the decision in Bissoe v. Aithie (1926) F.C. 26-29, 113 that a concession, void under section 8 for want of notice, could not be valid nevertheless as a conveyance.]

CONCESSION ENQUIRY (No. 450) on the claim by African Woods Ltd. for the validation of a concession. The claim was opposed.

Mead for the claimants.

Bing Q. C. and Blay for the opposers.

BENSON J. This enquiry has been sent back to this court for adjudication by the West African Court of Appeal. The Enquiry has already been before this court, differently constituted, in consolida­tion with Concession Enquiry No. 447. It is not necessary for me to set out in detail the facts of the case, as they appear sufficiently from the judgment of the West African Court of Appeal in this Enquiry (1). By that judgment the concession of Euward Ramia Ltd. in Enquiry No. 447, was declared invalid as a concession. It is quite clear that the land in the lease to African Woods Ltd. is part of a larger portion of land demised to Edward Ramia Ltd. and which was declared invalid as a concession by the West African Court of Appeal. There is no doubt about this and it is not denied by the claimants that this is so, according to the documents in evidence.

The opposers, Edward Ramia Ltd., were given leave to oppose on July 5, 1954. In a preliminary ruling by me, I refused leave to argue an additional ground of opposition; in point of fact ground 5 of those grounds filed on August 6, 1954, covers the additional ground, and the Enquiry has been conuucted and examination of witnesses so as to include all grounds.

I will deal with the first ground of opposition-that the land covered by the concession had already been granted to the opposers. The affidavit of the opposers filed on May 24, 1954, makes it clear that the grant referred to is the one in Concession Enquiry No. 447. As the West African Court of Appeal has declared this grant to be invalid as a concession, there can be no substance in this ground and it fails. As to ground 2-that the claimants had notice of the prior grant to the opposers. There is no substance in this ground either in view of the judgment of the West African Court of Appeal. As to ground 3-that the grant was obtained by the misrepresentation of the claimants that the prior grant was null and void. I can find no evidence that such misrepresentation was made and certainly nothing to suggest that the claimants used any fraudulent or improper means, within the meaning of section 13 (3) of the Concessions Ordinance.

Ground 4 is to the effect that the grantors have withdrawn the grant to the claimants by a letter dated December 14, 1953. Even assuming that the grantors could withdraw a grant by their unilateral act, I fail to see how this can give rise to a ground of opposition by the opposers. At any rate such withdrawal was never carried into effect and is of no legal effect. The grantors, in fact, do not contend that they have withdrawn the grant to the claimants.

The last ground of opposition is the fifth one, which reads: " Because the claimants are not entitled to a grant of a Certificate of Validity." Under this ground it has been argued that section 12 (3) and (4) of the Concessions Ordinance have not been complied with, and also that the concession is invalid by reason of section 13 (2) of the same Ordinance.

With regard to section 12 of the Ordinance I am satisfied that its provisions were all complied with. I rule also that the words "interested parties" in section 12 refer only to the parties of the concession and not to rival claimants, such as Edward Ramia Ltd. In my opinion the government agent had not to enquire into the validity of any other concession or lease, but if he had any knowledge of one, he would be quite right in drawing the attention of the parties to it and explaining its possible consequences. His duties under section 12 are to make such enquiries concerning the concession as he considers necessary, arrange the sum to be paid under the concession and have it executed in his presence. In this case I find that the government agent did 'all that was necessary under the Ordinance. I think, how­ever, that he should have refrained from giving his opinion as to the legal value of the concession to Edward Ramia Ltd., as although it is invalid as a concession, it is valid as a demise of land. Such advice may have misled the grantors as to their liabilities. There is no substance in the ground of appeal relating to section 12 of the Ordinance.

The contention as regards the fulfilment of the requirements of section 13 (2) needs very careful consideration. The main purposes of the Concessions Ordinance are to protect natives and chiefs against persons who may try to obtain rights over their lands on unfair or un favourable terms and to ensure that the concessions will be sufficiently worked and developed if granted. It is the duty of the Concessions Court to enquire into the matter and ascertain that the Ordinance has been fully complied with. I think that by the words in section 13 (2)-" that it may be reasonably presumed that they understood the nature and terms thereof "-it is intended that the court should be satisfied, not only that the nature of the concession be under­stood, but also that the parties understood exactly what land was included in the concession. I think also that it is the duty of this court to ascertain that the parties, and especially the grantors, understood precisely what their obligations were under the concession. If the grantors knew of the possibility of an action in damages by third parties and were prepared to grant the concession in spite of that danger, I do not think that that would be a ground for refusing to certify the concession. I think, however, that if the grantors were under a misapprehension as to the actual land being granted by the concession and that they did not think it had already been granted to another person, if in fact it had, then that would be a very good reason for the court to refuse to validate the concession.

 

In this instance I find from the evidence, and especially that of the Omanhene Bekwai, that the grantors were uncler the impression that the land already leased to Edward Ramia Ltd. did not include the land leased to African Woods Ltd. It is conclusively proved that in this assumption they were wrong, as the land was in fact included in the Edward Ramia Ltd. lease. I do not propose to review all the evidence on this point, but will refer to the words of the Omanhene himself where he says, .. If I had known that the land I was giving to African Woods Ltd. was land already given to Edward Rmnia Ltd., I would not have signed the concession." In my view that evidence alone would be sufficient to settle this point. It is quite clear that the circumstances surrounding the grant of the concession were generally unsatisfactory: the grantors obviously did not know exactly what they had already leased to Edward Ramia Ltd., and certainly did not appre­ciate the consequences of the lease to African Woods Ltd. or their liabilities. It is obvious also that the Bekwai Local Council would never have authorised this lease if they had been aware of all the facts.

Section 13 (4) of the Ordinance makes it clear that this court has to see that the grantors get a fair deal, as the court has to see that adequate valuable consideration is given, regard being had to the circumstances existing at the time of the Concession, etc. I rule that the grantors did not understand the nature and terms of the grant to African Woods Ltd. and that it would be wrong for this court to grant a certificate of validity to this concession. The opposition of Edward Ramia Ltd. is upheld for the reasons stated and it is declared that the concession of the claimants, African Woods Ltd., is invalid as a concession under the Ordinance. The claimants will have to pay the costs of the opposers.

Claim disallowed.

S. G. D.
