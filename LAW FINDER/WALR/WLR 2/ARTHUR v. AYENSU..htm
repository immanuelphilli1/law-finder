Succession-Intestacy-Devolution of self-acquired property-Rules of descent -Meaning of "family" in connection with succession to self-acquired property.

Succession-Intestacy-Effect vf Marriage Ordinance, c. 127 of 1951, s. 48.

This action in respect of land, the self-acquired property of a deceased intestate, was commenced by the plaintiff as head of the family group to which the deceased had belonged. The plaintiff claimed that the land, by virtue of the intestacy, became family property, i.e., the property of the family group of which he was head. A grant of letters of adminis­tration (limited to the deceased's personal estate) had been made to a niece (on the maternal side) of the deceased and the courts below had dismissed the plaintiff's action on the grounds that the administratrix, and not the plaintiff, was the proper person to maintain the action.

Held, (i) although the self-acquired property of a deceased intestate passes, by virtue of the intestacy, to the family of which the deceased was a member, the family, for this purpose, is not the wide family group to which the deceased belonged, but the immediate family of the deceased, i.e., all those persons claiming descent from the mother of the deceased or, failing such persons, all those claiming descent from the maternal grandmother of the deceased, and so on.

Mensah Lar'kai v. Amorkor and Others «1933) 1 W.A.C.A. 323) referred to.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It should be noted that this particular mode of tracing succession applies only to the matrilineal areas of Ghana.]

(ii) On the facts of the present case the niece of the deceased was the proper person to maintain the action; the plaintiff, not being a member of the immediate family, could not maintain it. 1'he niece should have commenced the action in her capacity as representative of the immediate family of the deceased, not as administratrix, her grant of letter-s of administration being limited to the personal estate of the deceased.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The above rules of descent apply in all cases where self-acquired property (i.e., property disposable by will) falls to be disposed of in accordance with the rules of native law and custom, whether it be property affected by the Marriage Ordinance, c. 127 of 1951, or not. Property affected by the Marriage Ordinance includes the real property disposable by will and all the personal property of persons, married under the Ordinance, who die intestate leaving a widow or husband or any issue of their marriage, and of the children of persons married under the Ordinance who die intestate.

In this case the learned judge speaks of disposable real property of a deceased intestate as vesting upon death direct in the persons beneficially entitled thereto. This rule will apply where the intestate is not a person affected by the Marriage Ordinance, but it would appear that the learned pg 358 judge overlooked the provisions of section 2 of the amending Marriage Ordin­ance, passed in 1951, which amends section 48 of the principal Ordinance. This amendment appears specifically to overrule Gorleku v. Gorleku (1). Section 48 (2) of the amended principal Ordinance prescribes that the property mentioned " in the preceding subsection ... shall devolve upon the adminis­trator of such deceased person's estate upon trust .... " The property mentioned in "the previous subsection" covers both the personal property of the deceased intestate and the" real property of which the said intestate might have disposed by will." It is clear therefore that since 1951 both rea] and personal property vest in the administrator.

In causing the property mentioned" in the previous subsection" to devolve upon the administrator, the amendment may, however, have further effects. The property with which the" previous subsection" is principally concerned is, as has been mentioned, the personalty of the intestate and the realty of which he might have disposed by will. But the subsection also contains two provisos, each of which is concerned with a further type of property, and it may be that these also would be held to be caught by the amendment and thereby to devolve upon the administrator. The second proviso is concerned with "that real property, the succession to which cannot by the native customary law be affected by testamentary disposition." The Ordinance is, unfortunately, not explicit, and it gives no further indication of the sort of property that is intended to be covered by this expression, although presum­ably it must refer to property which was vested in the deceased intestate immediately prior to his death. It may be that it was inserted by the legislature with a view to ensuring that family property of which the intestate was custodian or caretaker should not be caught by the operation of the section. It remains to be seen· whether the courts will hold, as a matter of statutory interpretation, that it is this type of property with which the proviso is concerned and that such property must now be caught by the 1951 amendment and vest in the administrator. If they do so hold, there will presumably be a change in the present practice whereby the administrator is appointed from the immediate, rather than the wider, family of the deceased. One solution might be the appointment of joint administrators with a view to safeguarding the interests of both family groups. It is to be hoped, how­ever, that the courts will not find it necessary thus to interpret the section: such an interpretation would put the Jaw out of accord with normal practice and behaviour.

One further point in this conllexion is that it may be that family property does not necessarily vest solely in the custodian. Conveyancing practice is not yet so far developed in Ghana as it is in England and the existence in the latter country of a conveyancing system that ensures continuity of title is not yet fully apparent in the former. It is well settled that family property cannot be conveyed away from the family by the custodian acting alone and without the consents of elders and leading members of the family, but the form of such a conveyance is not so well settled. It may well be that, for conveyancing purposes, the title to family property will be regarded as vesting in the custodian together with the elders and leading members as joint tenants. If this should be the case, there will be no gap in the title on the death of the custodian, and the difficulty apparently created by the proviso to section 48 of the Marriage Ordinance will prove to be non-existent.]

APPEAL from a decision of the magistrate, Cape Coast, on August 2, 1956, dismissing an appeal from a decision on September 8, 1955, of the Cape Coast Municipal Court dismissing an action for recovery of possession of property, for damages, and for mesne profits.

Sekyi for the plaintiff.

The defendant appeared in person.

ADUMUA-BOSSMAN J. The judgment of the Municipal Court of Cape Coast, confirmed by the Magistrate's Court constituted by the Government Agent, cannot be supported on the grounds expressed by those courts, namely, that it is the administratrix to whom letters of administration have been granted by the Supreme Court (significantly enough, to administer the personal estate only of the deceased) who is the proper person to bring and maintain this action in respect of the self-acquired real property (a house) of the deceased and in respect of which he died intestate.

It is now settled law that a grant of letters of administration does not vest the real estate of the deceased in the administrator or administratrix, and that such real estate devolves, if the deceased be married under the provision of the Marriage Ordinance, c. 127 of 1951, upon the surviving widow, children and the family of the deceased together as tenants-in-common in certain shares-see Gorleku v. Gorlelm (1);. but if the deceased be not so married under the Marriage Ordinance, then, in accordance with native customary law, upon his family. But the question is, which family? It is clearly not the larger or wider family, of which the deceased was in his lifetime a member. So in the case of Mensah Larkai v. Amorlwr and Others (2), Sir George Deane C.J. pointed out as follows:

" Now the plaintiff has claimed this land as being the head of the Larkai family. Ahuru, it is true, was called Ahuru Larkai and it seems was a member of the Larkai family; but individually owned property of his would, on his death intestate, become the family property, not of the Larkai family, but of his own family."

By his amended writ or claim the plaintiff in this case claimed ,. As head for himself and on behalf of the other members of the Anona family of John Ayensu of Cape Coast." The plaintiff, however, in the course of cross-examination, had to admit that the late John Ayensu left a niece, one Mrs. l<'austina Daniels, to whom letters of administration have been granted to administer the personal estate of the deceased, and who obviously, not by virtue of the grant to her of letters of administration but rather by virtue of her relationship as a surviving uterine niece, would be the person entitled according to native customary law to succeed the deceased and administer his self-acquired property for herself and the deceased's immediate family, pg 360

2.e., all tracing through females from the deceased's mother, or failing any such, then all tracing through females from the deceased's mother's mother or maternal grandmother ..

The main point is that the self-acquired property of the late John Ayensu, deceased, does not devolve upon the wider Anona family of which, in his lifetime, he was a member-and the head of which the plaintiff claims to be-but upon his immediate family as I have said, consisting of Mrs. Faustina Daniels and others like her, claiming through females from John Ayensu's mother.

Accordingly, in my view, the courts below were right in holding that the plaintiff in the capacity expressed in his amended claim or writ was not entitled to maintain the action, although the grounds expressed by them that Mrs. Faustina Daniels, qua administratrix, was the proper person were unsound.

Accordingly, I confirm and uphold the judgments of both courts, in so far as the decisions dismiss the claim of the plaintiff on the ground that he is not as head of the Anona family of John Ayensu the proper person to maintain the action-and I dismiss the plaintiff's appeal with costs.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
