May 25, 1956.

COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. OWUSU.

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court, Cape Coast

Manyo-Plange J.

 

 

() )

O  Criminal Law-Procedure-Accused not before court during part of hearing -Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, ss. 166 (1) and 355.

The appellant, a local authority police corporal, was charged before a magistrate with extortion, under the Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 413. The hearing was spread over several days and on one of these days, during which a witness for the prosecution gave evidence, the appellant was not present before the court. The magistrate continued the case in his absence, relying on the Criminal Procedure Ordinance, c. 10 of 1951, s. 166 (1).

Held, (i) where an accused is charged with a misdemeanour section 166 (1) of the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, permits a court to proceed at an adjourned hearing in the absence of the accused, but where the charge is of a felony such absence amounts to an irregularity depriving the court of jurisdiction.

(ii) An irregularity the effect of which is to deprive a court of

        jurisdiction cannot be cured on appeal or review by the application of                         Ii

the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 355 (a): this section is concerned only with errors, omissions or irregularities not affecting jurisdiction.

R. v. Sylvanus Samuels ( (1938) 4 W .A.C.A. 183) applied.

Cases referred to:

(1) R. v. Smith (1817) Russ. & Ry. 339, C.C.A.

(2) R. v. Gee and Others (1936) 25 Cr.App.R. 198. (3) R. v. Sylvanus Samuels (1938) 4 W.A.C.A. 183.

ApPEAL from a decision of the District :Magistrate, Saltpond, on March 29, 1956, convicting the appellant of extortion.

Short for the appellant.

Unger, Crown Counsel, for the Commissioner of Police.

MANYO-PLANGE J. There is no merit in this appeal, but unfortu­nately the learned magistrate committed an irregularity in hearing the evidence of one witness in the absence of the accused. This he had no jurisdiction to do. He purported to have acted under section 166 (1) of the Criminal Procedure Ordinance but that section only empowers him to act as he did when the charge is a misdemeanour and not a felony as in this case. This lack of jurisdiction in my view vitiates the whole of the proceedings and invalidates abinitio the conviction and sentence. Council for the Crown has referred me to the case of R. v. Smith (1) and section 355 of the Criminal Procedure Ordinance but neither in my view helps. R. v. Smith deals with the admissibility of depositions of a dead deponent and section 355 does not in my opinion cover irregularities and omissions which affect jurisdic­tion. This is in my view borne out by the cases of R. v. Gee and Others (2) and R. v. Samuels (3). The conviction and sentence are therefore set aside and the appellant is discharged.

Conviction quashed.

S. G. D.
