                            May 8, 1957                        

                                                                               REGINA v. BANDAY.                                                                       

                                                         High Court of the Protectorate of the Gambia

                                                                                  Miles C.J

 

 

Criminal Law-Murder-Intoxication-When intoxication negatives malice aforethought - Whether intention an element of the offence - Criminal Code, c. 21, ss. 11 (4) and 190 (b).

The accused was charged with the murder of his brother. The facts of the killing were not in dispute: the accused, who had been drinking, offered violence to a third person and the deceased, approaching the accused from behind, sought forcibly to restrain him, whereupon the accused, who was holding a knife, stabbed backwards with it, causing the wound from which the deceased subsequently died. For the accused it was argued that at the time he was so intoxicated as not to know what he was doing. pg 332

Held, (i) on the evidence the accused was not so intoxicated as not to know what he was doing or so as to be incapable of knowing that his action would probably cause death or grievous bodily harm: he was indifferent as to the results of his action and, therefore, by the Criminal Code, c. 21, s. 190 (Ii), malice aforethought must be deemed to be established.

(ii) As, by the provision of section 190 (Ii) of the Code, intention to cause death is not an essential ingredient of the offence, section 11 (4) of the Code (which requires that intoxication shall be taken into account for the purpose of determining whether a person charged had formed any intention, specific or otherwise, in the absence of which he would be not guilty of the offence) could be of no assistance to the accused.

Case referred to:

(1) R. v. Beard (1919) 14 Cr.App.R. 110.

TRIAL upon information. The accused was charged with murder under section 187 of the Criminal Code.

Bridges, Assistant Attorney-General, for the Crown. Forster for the accused.

:MILES C.J. The accused Tallo Banday is charged with the murder of one Cartel Banday, who was his younger brother. As to the main facts there is no dispute. On the evening of :Monday March 18, 1957, the accused, who had been drinking, started a wholly unprovoked quarrel with one Illo J amanka, who was passing in the street while the accused was sitting outside his house. In the course of this quarrel the accused seized 1110 Jamanka by the neck of his shirt and used some abusive language. He was at the time holding a matchet. The accused was eventually restrained by the deceased. He was then taken to his house by the deceased and his sister, Kumba Yell Baldeh. Eventually he forced his way out, cutting his sister's finger in the act, and proceeded to create a further disturbance. At this point the deceased came up behind the accused and seized him, whereupon the accused, who was holding a penknife, thrust his hand backwards and stabbed the deceased in the stomach.

The deceased was seriously injured and his relatives, seeing that the intestines were protruding, placed a calabash over the wound and bound it with cloth. The deceased was taken to Bansang Hospital where he was admitted at 2.50 a.m. on March 19. An operation was performed on the same morning but the deceased died at 2.00 a.m. on March 20. According to the medical evidence there was an incised wound 2 to 2t inches long penetrating the layer of the abdominal wall and causing damage to the gut. A post-mortem examination revealed a large quantity of cherray scattered over the peritoneal cavity. This was due to the fact that the calabash previously mentioned had not been properly cleaned and still contained a quantity of cherray. According to Dr. Bidwell the cause of death was generalized pg 333 peritonitis caused first by the cherray scattering over the peritoneal cavity and the dirt that went with it and, secondly, a punctured hole in the lower part of the intestine through which fluid from the intestine had oozed into the peritoneal cavity. Dr. Bidwell says that either of these two factors could have caused death by itself but the presence of the cherray and dirt caused death to occur sooner than it would otherwise have done. In these circumstances I find as a fact that the deceased died as a result of the stab wound inflicted by the accused and Mr. Forster does not contend the contrary.

The defence set up is intoxication. The accused in evidence said that on the day in question he had taken a cigarette tin full of neat rum which rendered him so drunk that he did not know what he was doing and did not know afterwards what he had done. He says he does not remember having seen any of the persons in the compound that night.

Section 11 (4) of the Criminal Code provides:

" Intoxication shall be taken into account for the purpose of determining whether the person charged had formed any intention, specific or otherwise, in the absence of which he would not be guilty of the offence."

This is, in effect, an enactment of the principle of law laid down in R.   v. Beard (1).

Mr. Bridges for the prosecution concedes, and rightly in my view, that the accused did not intend to kill or cause grievous bodily harm to his brother. He contends, however, that the evidence establishes malice aforethought within the definition contained in section 190 (b) of the Criminal Code. This is as follows:

"Knowledge that the act or omission causing death will probably cause the death of or grievous harm to some person, whether such person is the person actually killed or not, although such knowledge is accompanied by indifference whether death or grievous bodily harm is caused or not, or by a wish that it may not be caused."

There is certainly a good deal of evidence in the case for the prosecution to the effect that the accused was under the influence of drink, but this evidence is really of the vaguest possible kind. On the other hand, I am satisfied that the accused has greatly exaggerated the extent of his intoxication. He now professes to be unable to recall any of the events of that evening but it is to be observed that on March 20 in a statement to Inspector Jarrah, he gave a detailed account of the occurrence which substantially agrees with that given by the witnesses for the prosecution. Also it is clear from the evidence that the accused realized what he had done as soon as he had done it and was greatly distressed. Moreover, there is evidence from one Kebba Keita that when the accused was shut in his house he pg 334 threatened to cut the finger of the person holding the door, and in fact did so.


  No doubt a cigarette tin full of rum would have a considerable effect on a person unused to alcohol, but that is no evidence to support the accused's statement that he had in fact consumed that amount. This is no evidence aliunde as to when, where or by whom he was supplied with the rum.

The irresistible inference from the evidence is that the accused was angry at the attempts which had been and were being made to restrain him, and that in an attempt to free himself he struck out wildly with the knife at the person who was holding him from behind.

I am satisfied that the accused was not so drunk as to be incapable of knowing that his action would probably cause death or grievous bodily harm. He may even have wished that it might not be caused. A common result of the consumption of liquor is that it makes a person reckless as to the consequence of his actions. That, however, is not a defence in law.

In R. v. Beard (1), the Lord Chancellor said:

" Evidence of drunkenness falling short of a proved incapability in the accused to form the interest necessary to constitute the crime, and surely establishing that his mind was affected by drink so that he more readily gave way to some resistant passion, does not rebut the presumption that a man intends the natural consequence of his acts."

The present is a case to which the principle enunciated by the Lord Chancellor appears to apply. I appreciate that the onus on the accused is no greater than that imposed upon a plaintiff in a civil action but he has failed to discharge even that limited burden of proof. In the result I find him guilty of murder contrary to section 187 of the Criminal Code.

Accused found guilty and convicted.
 

S. G. D.
