March 14, 1957

                          SAMAN v. OTSIW ABA.                                

                    Supreme Court of Ghana, Central Judicial Division, Land Court, Cape Coast

                                                                                          Ollennu J

 

 

Native Courts-Jurisdiction-Variation of territorial jurisdiction of Native Court-Effect on pending causes concerned with territory affected by 1,ariation-Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951-Native Courts (Colony) (Reconstitution of Native Courts) (Variation) (No.4) Order, 1956.

An action concerning land was commenced in the Ajumako Native Court "B" in December, 1955. At this time the Native Court, by virtue of the Native Courts (Colony) (Reconstitution of Native Courts) Order, 1953, had territorial jurisdiction over the whole State of Ajumako, and in addition had jurisdiction as a Native Appeal Court. The action came before the court in April, 1956, and, after a numbel" of adjournments, judgment was given for the defendant in September, 1956. While the action was before the court, on April 30, 1956, the Native Courts (Colony) (Reconstitution of Native Courts) (Variation) (No.4) Order, 1956, removed the appellate jurisdiction of the court and pg 285 imited its territorial jurisdiction to "the State of Ajumako excluding Brakwa and Odoben lands within the State of Ajumako." By the same Order the Ajumako Northern Native Court" C," which already had jurisdiction over the "Brakwa and Odoben lands," was reconstituted as a "B" court. It was established that the land, the subject of the action, was within the "Brakwa and Odoben" area.

Held, (i) in the absence of any provision for pending causes in the Native Courts (Colony) (Reconstitution of Native Courts) (Variation) (No.4) Order, 1956, the Ajumako Native Court" B" was, from the effective date of the Order, deprived of its jurisdiction in matters concerning land removed from its jurisdiction by the Order, even though such causes were before the court at the date of such removal.

Nyako v. Akwa and Others (1949) 12 W.A.C.A. 465 applied

APPEAL from the decision of the Ajumako Native Court" B " on September 11, 1956, giving judgment for the defendant in an action for declaration of title to land.

Abadoo for the plaintiff.

Akufo-Addo for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. Three grounds of appeal were filed, but it has been necessary to argue only the first of them, since if that succeeds it will dispose of the matter as far as this court is concerned. That ground is :

" The land the subject-matter of dispute herein being situate at Odoben and within the particular area and jurisdiction of Odoben Native Court" B," which had then been duly constituted and functioning-vide supplementary Gazette No. 30 of 12th May, 1956-the Native Court" B " of Ajumako State at the date it heard and decided the suit herein, acted without jurisdiction and therefore the proceedings herein are null and void for want of jurisdiction. ,.

The suit commenced in the Native Court" B •• of Ajumako by a writ issued on December 9, 1955, returnable on December 23, 1955. From the record of proceedings it does not appear that the matter came before the said Native Court until April 10, 1956. After that date the case was adjourned a few times, counsel being heard on August 14, 1956, and it was concluded by a judgment delivered on September 11, 1956, against the plaintiff.

The Ajumako Native Court" B •• was constituted by the Native Courts (Colony) (Reconstitution of Native Courts) Order, 1953, made pg 286 under sections 3 and 4 of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance. It was created a Native Court" B " and Native Appeal Court; and the area of its jurisdiction is " The State of Ajumako." By the Native Courts (Colony) (Reconstitution of Native Courts) (Variation) (No.4) Order, 1956, made on April 30, 1956, the jurisdiction of the court was amended by the removal of its appellate jurisdiction, and the deletion of the area of the " Brakwa and Odoben lands" from the territ()rial limits of its original jurisdiction. The same mnending Order also abolished another Native Court, created by the former Order, namely, the Ajumako Native Court •• C," and constituted in its place the Ajumako Northern Native Court" B." This cQurt was already vested with jurisdiction over the" Brakwa and Odoben lands" removed from the jurisdiction of the Ajumako Native Court" B."

Mr. Myles-Abadoo, for the plaintiff, submitted that, since the amending Order of 1956 contained no saving clause reserving to the Ajumako Native Court" B " jurisdiction to conclude cases which were pending before it on April 30, 1956, the date when the amendment took effect, that Native Court ceased to have jurisdiction over •. Brakwa and Odoben lands" as from that date. In thus entertaining the suit after that date, the said Ajumako Native Court .. B " assumed juris­diction which did not belong to it, and the proceedings so taken before it are therefore a nullity.

Mr. Akufo·Addo, for the defendant, argued on the other hand that the plain language of the amending Order could not be interpreted to have retrospective effect and that in the absence of express words making its effect retrospective the amending Order could not affect causes and matters which were pending in the Native Court before the amendment came into force. The Native Court was therefore properly seized of the case up to its conclusion. He submitted further that the term .. Odoben lands" is vague; that evidence would be required to determine their territorial limits; and that there is nothing on the face of the writ of summons or in the evidence on the record to show that the land in dispute in this case is part of those lands, and therefore the point taken by learned counsel for the plaintiff must fail even if the amending Order could be interpreted to have retrospective effect.

In reply to Mr. Akufo-Addo's second point Mr. Myles-Abadoo referred the court to documents tendered in evidence by the defendant herself, where she admits that the land in dispute is part of the Odoben lands.

I am satisfied upon the evidence on the record of proceedings before the Native Court that the land in dispute is part of the Odoben lands, and I shall therefore consider the legal points raised by learned counsel.

The authorities are clear that a statute should not be construed retrospectively unless it so specifically provides. The question whether an Act of Parliament is retrospective in its operation must be deter­mined by the provision of the Act itself, bearing in mind that a statute is not to be construed retrospectively unless it is clear that such was the intention of the legislature-see the remarks of Jessel :M. R. in Quiltur v. Mapleson (1). The same principle is stated in volume 31, Halsbury's Laws of England, 2nd ed., p. 513, para. 670, as follows:-

" A statute is prima facie prospective and does not interfere with existing rights unless it contains express words, or there is the plainest implication to that effect."

In the case of the Colonial Sugar Refining Co. Ltd. v. Irving (2) it was held that

"although the right of appeal from the Supreme Court of Queensland to His Majesty in Council, given by the Order in Council of June 30, 1860, has been taken away by the Australian Commonwealth Judiciary Act, 1903, s. 39 (2), and the only appeal therefore now lies to the High Court of Australia, yet the Act is not retrospective, and a right of appeal to the King in Council in a suit pending when the Act was passed and decided by the Supreme Court afterwards was not taken away."

The long string of authorities draw a distinction between Acts affecting rights and Acts affecting the procedure for exercising rights already acquired. See the case of River Wear Commissioner v. Adam­son (3), and the case of In re Debtor (No. 99 of 1928) (4). I think, too, that a distinction must also be drawn between a " right" and the jurisdiction of a court to entertain or adjudicate upon that " right." The jurisdiction of a Native Court, as indeed of many other courts, is conferred by statute; it is not acquired merely by the commencement of a suit or the appearance of the parties before the court with a complaint. That jurisdiction may be exercised for so long a time as the statute confirming it remains in force. It disappears the moment the statute is repealed, unless there is a saving clause in the revocation Act itself. The jurisdiction of the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council to hear appeals from courts of the Commonwealth was con­ferred by a statute of the British Parliament. Section 39 (2) of the Australian Commonwealth Judiciary Act, 1903, merely took away the right of the Australian subject to avail himself of that jurisdiction of the Privy Council to hear his appeal. Thus, although in effect the Australian Act limited the territorial jurisdiction of the Privy Council, yet since no British Act of Parliament has limited the jurisdiction of the Judicial Committee, a subject who, before the coming into effect of the Australian Act, had vested in him a right of appeal to the Privy Council can exercise that right, in spite of the Australian Act, unless it is manifest from the Australian Act that the deprivation of the subject's right to appeal to the Privy Council was meant to be retro­spective. The position would have been different if the appeal were pg 288 pending before the Privy Council and Parliament at Westminster passed an Act, taking away the jurisdiction of the Council in appeals from the Commonwealth, without a saving clause.

In the case of Nyako v. Akwa and Others (5), the action was commenced in the Native Court" B " of Okwawu, but before hearing commenced the court was abolished and a grade" A " Native Court was substituted therefor, with jurisdiction including, but more exten­sive than, that of the grade " B " Native Court. The grade " A " Native Court heard the case as filed in the grade " B " court, the defendant having pleaded to it before the grade" A "court. It was held on the appeal that the Order abolishing one Native Court and substituting another, even though of superior jurisdiction, brought to an end all causes and matters then pending in the Native Court which was abolished. It was held further that the appearance of the parties before the new Native Court which had jurisdiction in the matter was tantamount to the commencement of a fresh suit before the new Native Court-a procedure which is warranted by the Native Court Procedure Regulations. Verity C.J., delivering the judgment of the court, stated in the course of his judgment:

" The learned judge who heard the appeal held that the suit having been adjourned when the grade 'B' court was closed and the grade 'A' court having heard the case de novo there was no irregularity. I think in this the learned judge erred. The proceedings had been commenced in one court and were heard and determined in another. N either the Native Courts Ordinance No. 22 of 1944 nor the Order-in-Council which abolished the first court and constituted the second made any saving in regard to pending causes and in the absence of any such having it appears to me that all causes pending in the grade 'B' court were brought to an end when that court was closed. The correct procedure would have been, therefore, to commence the suit de novo in the grade' A ' court."

It appears to me that that is exactly the position in this case.

The position here is stronger than that in cases which come under section 58 of the Native Court (Colony) Ordinance. In those cases a court constituted under the Courts Ordinance has jurisdiction but the Ordinance says that under certain circumstances it shall not exercise that jurisdiction. In this case, there is total absence of jurisdiction by reason of the revocation of jurisdiction which existed at one time.

I may put it this way: the Native Courts (Colony) (Reconstitution of Native Courts) (Variation) (No.4) Order, 1956, abolished the Ajumako Native Court "B" as a court for the "Brakwa and Odoben " areas of the Ajumaku State. Consequently it brought an end to all causes from that area which were pending before the said Ajumako Native Court" B " before the abolition, and it created a new pg 289 tribunal, the Northern Ajumako Native Court" B," empowered to bear actions which might be regularly brought before it from· the .. Brakwa and Odoben lands." But cases from the "Brakwa and Odoben areas" which were pending before the original Ajumako Native Court " B " before it ceased to exist, cannot be heard in the con­tinuing court, which no longer has jurisdiction over cases affecting tbe •. Brakwa and Odoben lands.

I hold therefore that the Native Courts (Colony) (Reconstitution of Native Courts) (Variation) (No.4) Order, 11:)56, brought tbe writ in this case to an end on April 30, 1956. I hold further that as a new writ in connection with Odoben lands could not issue from the Ajumako Native Court" B " for want of jurisdiction, after April 30, 1956, the appearance of the parties before the court after April 30, 1956, could not vest the Native Court with jurisdiction not belonging to it.

On June 13, 1956, when the case came before the Ajumako Native Court" B " for the first time after the amending Order had come into force, the suit should have been struck out for want of jurisdiction and with consequential order as to costs. Thus, the proceedings in the Ajumako Native Court" B " taken after April 30, 1956, are a nullity. The proceedings being void, it follows that the judgment therein is also null and void ..

For these reasons, I would allow the appeal on this ground, and I declare the proceedings, judgment and orders therein null and void. For the said judgment and orders I substitute an order striking out the suit for want of jurisdiction with costs for the defendant in the Ajumako Native Court" B " to be taxed. I make no order as to costs in this court.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
