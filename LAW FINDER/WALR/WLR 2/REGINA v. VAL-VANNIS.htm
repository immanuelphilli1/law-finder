 January 11, 1957

                                                           REGINA v. VAL-VANNIS.

                                                      West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                                   Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.JA

 

-

Criminal Law-Alibi-Failure of accused to serve notice on prosecution of intention to plead alibi-Effect of raising defence of alibi at trail ­Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 131 (1) and (3).

The appellant was convicted of theft by a judge sitting with assessors at the Accra Assize. He appealed on the ground that his applications for witnesses to be subpoenaed by the Crown and to be allowed to advance a defence of alibi had been rejected by the court and that this rejection had so prejudiced his defence that he had not had a fair trial. The Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 131 (1), requires that when an accused person, to be tried on information, intends to raise a plea of alibi in defence he shall give notice thereof, with details, to the prosecu­tor before the opening of the assize. Section 131 (3) provides that where a defence of alibi is put forward at trial without the notice under section 131 (1) having been served, the court shall require the accused to serve the necessary details upon the prosecution. In this case the appellant, half-way through the trial, made an application for the Crown to be instructed to subpoena certain witnesses for the defence and stated that he intended to plead an alibi as his defence. The application was refused by the court.

Held, failure to serve the notice required by the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 131 (1) does not prevent the defence of alibi from  pg 200 being pleaded at trial. The failure of the court to require notice and details to be served on the prosecution under section 131 (3)' of the Ordinance and the absence, on such failure, of an objection by the prosecution to a continuance of the trial, operate to the advantage of an accused, who is not thereby inhibited from calling witnesses himself. In these circumstances the trial judge was right to refuse the application for witnesses to be subpoenaed by the Crown.

APPEAL from a conviction of theft on November 14, 1956, by Quashie-Idun J. sitting with assessors at the Accra Assize.

Unger, Crown Counsel, for the Crown. The appellant appeared in person.

The following judgment of the court was given by COUSSEY P. :

The appellant was convicted by a judge sitting with assessors at the Accra Criminal Assize on two counts of stealing the sum 6f £800 during the month of September, 1954, and £450 during the month of December, 1954, and he was sentenced to three years' imprisonment with hard labour on each count, to run concurrently. The only ground of law on which leave to appeal was granted by this court was" Wrongful rejection of application to enter a plea of alibi as a defence. "

The appellant, who at his trial defended himself and has argued his own appeal, contends that the refusal of the learned trial judge to permit him to plead an alibi and to subpoena witnesses to testify in support of that defence so prejudiced him that he did not have a fair trial, and that he is entitled to have the convictions set aside, as the verdict may have been in his favour if he had been allowed to plead and establish the alibi.

The appellant was arraigned before the court on an information on October 15, 1956. He pleaded" not guilty" and was remanded on bail. The trial was resumed on November 12, when four witnesses testified for the prosecution and the trial was adjourned till the following day. On November 13 the record, as far as it is relevant to the point raised, reads as follows:

" Defendant states that he proposes to plead alibi as a defence and wishes certain people to be subpoenaed by Crown to give evidence in support of that defence.

" Crabbe refers to section 131 of the Criminal Procedure Code and states that notice has not been given of alibi as prescribed by the section.

" By Court:

" I do not grant the application at this stage. It should have been made at the latest when the defendant pleaded to the charges in this court."

The trial then proceeded to the conviction of the appellant, as stated already. pg 201

Having considered the record and section 131 of the Criminal Procedure Ordinance, c. 10 of 1951, I am satisfied that the ruling of the learned judge was correct and that the appellant was not thereby prejudiced in his defence. The appellant did not avail himself of section 131 (1) of the Ordinance, which provides:

.. If the person charged intends to put forward as a defence a plea of alibi, he shall be bound to give notice thereof to the prosecutor or his advocate with particulars as to the time and place and of the witnesses by whom it is proposed to prove it, prior in the case of a summary trial to the examination of the first witness for the prosecution, and prior in the case of trial upon information to the opening of the Assize to which the case has been committed for trial."

As the appellant had not, before the opening of the Assize, given the requisite notice of intention to plead an alibi he could not, contrary to the section, be admitted so to plead half-way through the case for the prosecution.

The appellant did, however, as he was entitled to do, in the course of his defence, both by his own evidence and that of an inspector of police whom he called as his second witness, attempt to establish the alibi that he was in custody from September 28 to October 2, 1954, and -from October 12 to December 21, 1954. The situation of an accused person who puts forward a defence of alibi without giving the notice prescribed by section 131 (1) of the Ordinance is provided for by subsection (3) of the same section in these terms:

.. If the person charged puts forward a defence of alibi without having given such notice as aforesaid the court shall call upon him to give notice to the prosecution of the particulars mentioned in subsection (1) either forthwith or within such time as the court may allow and after such notice shall have been given shall, if the prosecution so desires, adjourn the case."

It will be observed that where the accused puts forward the defence without notice of a plea of alibi, the law provides that the court shall call upon him to give notice to the prosecution as to the time and place and of the witnesses by whom it is proposed to prove the alibi. The object of this is to afford the prosecution sufficient data to counter the defence if so advised. If, however, the court does not direct that such particulars be given, and the prosecution does not object to the trial proceeding, the accused cannot complain that he has been pre­judiced in his defence; on the contrary the omission to direct him to give particulars may be to his advantage in that it may afford him greater scope or latitude both as to the time and place and the evidence he would call to support the defence of alibi. That is the position here and the appellant was not prevented from calling any witnesses he was minded to subpoena. In the event, after hearing his defence, the learned trial judge found as a fact that he did not believe the pg 202 evidence of the defendant that he was in prison at the time the complainants say they paid the first amount of £800. Both in the trial court and in this court the appellant failed lamentably to sub­stantiate his defence of alibi as to either count and he has nothing legitimately to complain of. The appeal is dismissed.

 

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
