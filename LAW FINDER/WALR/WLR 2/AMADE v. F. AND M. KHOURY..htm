June 12, 1956

AMADE v. F. AND M. KHOURY.

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court, Accra

Dennison J.

 

 

Hire Purchase-Seizure by owner under cla1lse in agreement -Payment in arrear- Whether hirer entitled to repossession on payment of arrears after seizure.

The plaintiff had entered into a hire-purchase agreement with the defendants for the purchase of a lorry. One of the clauses of the agree­ment permitted the defendants to seize the lorry in the event of default in the payments due. During the currency of the agreement the defen­dants, at the request of the plaintiff, carried out repairs to and supplied spares for the vehicle. These supplementary transactions were evidenced by two receipts which provided that the sums due under the transactions should be added to the capital payable by the plaintiff under the hire­purchase agreement. The plaintiff fell into arrear with his payments and, at a time when £20 of the original sum remained outstanding, the defendants seized the vehicle. Subsequently the plaintiff paid the out­standing £20 but the defendants refused to return the vehicle to him. The plaintiff then commenced this action for the return of the lorry or its value and damages for the unlawful seizure and detention. The defendants denied liability and counterclaimed for the sums due under the two supplementary transactions.

Held, (i) by the terms of the agreement the defendants were entitled to retake possession of their vehicle in certain circumstances: when these circumstances arose the defendants were entitled to exercise their right of seizure without recourse to the courts.

(ii) In the absence of a clause in the hire-purchase agreement entitling a dispossessed hirer to retake possession after seizure and upon payment of outstanding arrears, the plaintiffs were not entitled to such repossession.

Whiteleys Ltd. v. Hilt ([1918J 2 K.B. 808) followed.

(iii) The two subsequent transactions were collateral agreements which had the effect of enlarging the initial hire-purchase agreement. As the sum due under this agreement was thereby increased the payment and acceptance of the outstanding £20 could not operate to discharge it.

Cases referred to:

(1) DeioT v. Foli (1952) 14 W.A.C.A. 54.

(2) Whiteleys Ltd. v. Hilt [1918J 2 K.B. 808.

 

ACTION by the plaintiff-hirer to recover possession of a vehicle the subject of a hire-purchase agreement, or its value, and for damages. The defendants counter claimed for sums due under two subsequent agreements.

Boateng for the plaintiff.

Lassey for the defendants.

DENNISON J. The plaintiff's claim is for the return of a lorry, number AD. 20, or its value assessed at £1,060; also the sum of £800 damages for unlawful seizure and detention of the lorry by the defendants.

The defendants stated they were entitled to possession of the vehicle because the plaintiff had failed to pay the instalments due under clause 10 of a written hire-purchase agreement dated April 10, 1952, and admitted in this case in evidence. In addition the defendants counterclaimed against the plaintiff in two sums; the sum of £261 being the price or value of tyres supplied on sale to the plaintiffs and, the sum of £119 15s. 6d. being amounts paid by the defendants for the repair of the vehicle and which it is alleged the plaintiff is due to repay by reason of clause 6 of the agreement. The total is £380 15s. 6d. No reply was filed to this counterclaim and Mr. Lassey at an early stage asked that judgment should be given for the defendants on the counterclaim. However as the entire sum was not liquidated damages I did not accede to his request at that early stage. From the evidence adduced I am perfectly satisfied that the defendants are entitled to judgment on the counterclaim; that is to say for £380 15s. 6d., and judgment is accordingly entered against the plaintiff for that amount.

The evidence establishes, and it is not in dispute, that when the lorry was seized by the defendants under clause 10 of the agreement, the plaintiff was in arrears to the extent of £20. The defendants gave a fair amount of latitude to the plaintiff each time he was in arrears with payments. They stated that the last payment but one they received was in April, 1953, and nothing more was paid until December, 1054, that is after the defendants had had the lorry seized. The lorry. according to the defendants' evidence, was in very bad order when they recovered it and the evidence proves that they had to spend the sum of £119 15s. 6d. upon getting it road-worthy again. After the lorry had been seized in December, 1954, the plaintiff came to the defendants and paid them the sum of £20, that is to say the entire amount that was owing on the original hire-purchase agreement; the defendants accepted the £20 but refused to return the lorry to the plaintiff. The questions for decision are:-

(          a) whether the seizure by the defendants in December, 1954, was lawful ;

          (b) if such seizure was lawful whether the acceptance of the balance outstanding under the agreement created a waiver and thereby vested the property of the lorry in the plaintiff; and

           (c) whether two receipts constituted collateral written agreements whereby the original agreement was kept alive until such times as the amounts covered by the said receipts were paid by the plaintiff.

Mr. Lassey referred me to Delor v. Foli (1). In that case the judgment was given and delivered by the learned President of the West African Court of Appeal. He stated that as the purchase price of the lorry in question was to have been paid by instalments and as the hirer did not pay the instalments, the owner was legally entitled to seize the lorry and take it back into his possession. Mr. Boateng for the plaintiff argued that the facts of this case are distinguishable from Delor v. Poli (1) in that the lorry in the Court of Appeal decision was secured for a loan, and therefore in default of payment the lorry automatically became forfeited. In this case, he argued, there was an agreement between the parties and that by the acceptance of the £20 the defendants waived any rights as to possession they may have had under that agreement.

I have no doubt in holding that when the lorry was seized in December, 1954, the defendants were quite within their rights in so doing, and had only delayed the seizure in order to give the plaintiff time to pay for the lorry. It must be appreciated at the time of the seizure the plaintiff had had the possession and use of the lorry for some three years and had had two accidents with it; as a result the lorry was in a very bad state of repair. Naturally the defendants did not wish to see their property wasted any further, hence the action they took.

There is no redemption clause in the agreement permitting the hirer

to have the right to resume the hiring provided he paid the arrears of hiring up to the date of repossession. This latter clause was one of the clauses in the agreement disputed in the Court of Appeal decision of Whiteleys Ltd. v. Hilt (2). The inclusion of such a clause would appear to be necessary in order that a hirer having once made default can obtain back the vehicle upon his paying the balance of the amount due under the original agreement.

It appears to me that the hirer's rights had ceased completely when the lorry was seized in December, 1954, and the effect of his having paid the balance due does not entitle him to repossession of the vehicle. There is no hardship here because when the lorry was seized in 1954 the plaintiff was in arrears, he had also wasted the defendants' property as is proved by the repair bills.

I have no doubt at all on the evidence before me that the agreement and the receipts were read over to the plaintiff by Mr. Inkabi and that he, the plaintiff, knew full well the terms of each document. Mr. nkabi struck me as a witness of truth; futhermore I find it hard to believe that a firm dealing in hire-purchase business would not take every safeguard to ensure that the agreements were understood and appreciated.

I

The first receipt was dated July 30, 1953, and the second August 4, 1953. The two documents are clear and unambiguous on the face of them. Both state that in consideration of goods supplied to the plaintiff by the defendants the amounts owing by the plaintiff were to be added to the total shown on the agreement and it is not in dispute that the amounts secured by the receipts have not as yet been paid. In these circumstances I have no hesitation in holding that the accept­ance of the £20 by the defendants in January, 1955, does not act as a waiver of their rights under the agreement because the receipts are obviously collateral to the agreement.

The defendants brought no action against the plaintiff but once the latter did, they insisted on their strict legal rights and filed the counter­claim and this they were quite entitled to do. In no manner has the plaintiff, in my opinion, been unjustly used.

In his closing address Mr. Boateng argued that the agreement was contrary to public policy. This argument to have any usefulness should have been pleaded but this was not done. I consider it was too late for Mr. Boateng to ask me to accept this as a possible defence to the counterclaim. The agreement was entered into freely between the parties, the terms were, to an extent, carried out by both parties; if the point had been raised in the pleadings I would have held on the facts of this particular case, that this agreement could not be treated as being contrary to public policy, but this is, of course, purely obiter. In the result the plaintiff's claim is dismissed and he will pay the costs of this action both on the claim and on the counterclaim.

        Judgment for the defendant on the claim and counterclaim.

S. G. D.
