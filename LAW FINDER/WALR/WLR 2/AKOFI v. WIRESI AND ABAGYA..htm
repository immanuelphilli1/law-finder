    Practice - Parties - Representation by one person of a group of persons having the same interest in the subject-matter-Native custom-Supreme Court (Civil P1'ocedure) Rules, Ord. 16, 1'. 9,

     Practice-Appeals to West African Court of Appeal-Concurrent findings of fact in courts below.

      The defendants were sued, as representatives of their stool, by the plaintiff, as the representative of a group of strangers to the stool who, by agreement with the stool, were occupying and working stool land. The plaintiff claimed that the strangers he represented worked their land under an "abusa" agreement with the stool (an agreement, common in cocoa-producing areas, involving a tripartite division of the yield of the land of which one such third is rendered by the tenant-farmer to the grantor stool). The defendants claimed that the land concerned had been taken over by the strangers under an "ebuenu" (abunu) agree­ment (an agreement involving a division of the yield into moieties but otherwise similar to "abusa "). The plaintiff asked for, and obtained in the courts below, an order restraining the defendants from demanding the surrender of half the land cultivated by those he repre­sented, and requiring the defendants to enter into a written "abusa" agreement with those persons. pg 258

         The court, in dismissing the appeal of the defendants, noted the observation of the Native trial Court that "ebuenu" agreements are unusual where the land involved is virgin forest, and quoted with approval the remarks of Rattray, Ashanti Law and Constitution, p. 348

Held, (i), adopting the rule applied in the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council, where there have been concurrent findings of fact in two courts below the West African Court of Appeal, the court will not review the evidence in a case unless it appears to them that there has either been such a miscarriage of justice as to have made what had happened not, in the proper sense, judicial procedure at all, or such a violation of a principle of law or procedure that if corrected would prevent the finding from standing, or that the case has such other unusual features as would constrain the court to depart from its normal practice.

srimati Bibhabati Devi v. Kumar Narayan Roy [1946] A.C. 508 applied.

(ii) The estate or interest held by a stranger to stool land in land granted to him by a stool, against an annual rent or tribute of a fixed share of the yield of the land, inures to the benefit of, and (apart from forfeiture for breach of condition, etc.) is determinable only by, the grantee and his successors in title).

(iii) Where numerous persons have the same interest in one cause or matter and one or more of such persons sues or is sued on behalf of them (Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 16, r. 9), no formalities are required to establish such representation provided the appointment of the person representing is made in accordance with custom, and evidence exists as to the identity of the person he represents.

Cases referred to :

(1) Kwamin Odontwi v. Kwamin Essuman and Others (1940) Divisional Court, Central Judicial Division, September 27, 1940, unreported. (2) 81'irnati Bibhabati Devi v. K1lmar Narayan Roy [1946] A.C. 508.

APPEAL from the decision of Acolatse J., on July 20, 1955, in the Land Court, Central Judicial Division, affirming a decision of the Ajumako Native Court "B" on February 24, 1955, ordering the defendants to execute a written" abusa " agreement with the plaintiff and the persons he represented.

Blay for the plaintiff.

E. O. Asafu-Adjaye for the defendants.

COUSSEY P. This is an appeal from a judgment of the Land Court, Cape Coast, affirming a decision of the Ajumako Native Court " B " of Ajumako in the Western Province, allowing the plaintiff's claim for a declaration that the defendant was entitled to exact from the farming tenants on whose behalf the plaintiff sued, being strangers to the defendant's stool, a fixed share or part amounting to one-third only, known as abusa, of the fruits of farms cultivated by such farmers on the Odoben Stool lands. The plaintiff also sought for and was granted an order restraining the defendant from demanding from such farmers possession of one-half of such farms, by division, in contra­vention of the original agreement between the said farmers and the defendant's stool and in contravention of the native customary law, and an order that the defendant should execute a document setting out the agreement between the parties in terms of the plaintiff's claim.

It is a common form of tenure throughout the country for a landowner who has unoccupied virgin or forest land, which he or his people are unable to cultivate, to grant the same to a stranger to work on in return for a fixed share of the crops realized from the land. In such a case the tenant-farmer, although he has no ownership in the soil, has a very real interest in the usufruct of the land. The arrange­ment may be carried on indefinitely, even by the original grantee's successor, so long as the original terms of the holding are observed.

It is not in dispute that these tenant farmers were granted their lands some fifty years ago for the cultivation of cocoa by the defendant's predecessors on the Stool of Odoben.

The plaintiff farmers claim that grants were made to them on the one-third, or abusa, system and that it was only about five years before the date of the action (1954) that the defendant, exerting pressure on the tenant farmers, had altered, or attempted to alter, the terms to an actual division of the mature cocoa farms into two, the farmer to possess one-half as a share-cropping tenant and the defendant's stool t<> possess the remaining one-half as owner.

The defendant maintained that the latter was the true and original arrangement; that the abusa or one-third arrangement was unknown to the Odoben Stool and that the arrangement had always been for division of a farm into equal moieties, the stool, the landlord, taking one-half, this being known as the ebuenu system.

The Native trial Court observed that under the abusa system the tenant pays all expenses in connection with working the property, the landowner not contributing towards the cost of labour, while under the ebuenu or one-half system, the cost of making the farm is, in the first instance, borne by the landlord and the farmer-tenant is then placed in charge of the farm to maintain and improve it. "As the tenant does not contribute to the cost of making the farm, he gets only one-half of the farm."

Receipts given by the defendant's predecessor, Chief Kwesi Andam, during the months of March and May, 1934, to several farmers evidence that at that time farmers were working the land" as abusa." Some documents, mostly of later date-and it is to these that the defendant points-suggest that the arrangement had been altered in some instances, from about the year 1940, to the ebuenu or one-half system.

It is not without significance that one of the terms of settlement in September, 1940, in the suit of J{wamin Odontwi v. J(wamin Essuman and Others (1) was: pg 260 3 (a) The defendants to give up to the plaintiff in lieu of Abusa, half the cocoa trees now growing on the land .which suggests that in that instance the earlier arrangement before the dispute and settlement had been on the abusa system.

Accepting the plaintiff's evidence, corroborated as it was by some of the documentary evidence, the Native Court on the conflict of testimony preferred the plaintiff's to the defendant's case-that the stranger-farmers whom the plaintiff represented were admitted to the lands under the" abusa "system. It continued:

" and we do disbelieve the evidence of the defendants and their witnesses that they were admitted on the' ebuenu ' system which is very unusual in case of virgin forest."

The learned judge of the Land Court on appeal agreed with the finding of fact of the Native Court.

As to the observation of the trial court that the ebuenu system is very unusual where the tenant-farmer is called upon to clear unoccupied forest land, the description of Rattray at p. 348 of his Ashanti Law and Constitution may well be borne in mind. He says:

" To win a plot of land from these sombre unlit depths, to let in the rays of the sun, to reach its rich virgin soil beneath the tangle of lianas and living, or dead and decomposing, vegetation that covers the land, was a task that, with the appliances at the Africans' disposal, might have seemed almost impossible for individual effort. Gigantic trees had to be removed, huge fallen trunks over which a man could hardly climb, a few yards of whose surface alone were visible .. , The assistance of every able­bodied member of the family or kindred group had to be called in to give a helping hand. "

The defendant did not suggest that his predecessors on the stool had ever contributed to the cost of making the cocoa farms over the years.

In this case, there are concurrent findings of fact of two courts below and the plaintiff is entitled to a dismissal of the appeal unless the defendant can bring himself within any of the recognized exceptions to the rule as to concurrent findings. That rule was stated by the I'rivy Council in Srimati Bibhabati Devi v. K1tmar Ramcndra Narayan Roy and Others (2) as follows:

" (4) That, in order to obviate the practice, there must be some miscarriage of justice or violation of some principle of law or procedure. That miscarriage of justice means such a departure from the rules which permeate all judicial procedure as to make that which happened not in the proper sense of the word judicial procedure at all. ... The question whether there is evidence on which the courts could arrive at their finding is such a question of law." pg 261

It is argued for the defendant that the Native Court did not weigh the evidence as a whole, but simply said it did not believe the defen­dant's evidence while the defendant's documentary evidence was overlooked. But the Native Court and the Land Court both accepted the plaintiff's evidence, the first after a full hearing with the advantage of seeing the witnesses and the Land Court after a full argument with the assistance of counsel. Then it is argued for the appellant that the judgment of the Native Court is a nullity because it was delivered orally on February 28 but was recorded on the following day and further that the panel of members of the Native Court had no authority to adjudicate on the suit after a certain date, when, it is suggested, their warrant lapsed while the suit was part heard. There is no> evidence on the record to support either of these points and they were rightly rejected by the judge of the Land Court on first appeal.

Again, it was submitted that the farmers, for whom the plaintiff' sued, had not authorized him to sue on their behalf as they had not, in fact, executed a power of attorney empowering him to institute the action. But the plaintiff's appointment was made at a meeting of farmers according to customary practice and did not require -to be reduced into writing. The names of those persons for whom he sued was sufficiently evidenced by one of the exhibits.

Lastly, it was submitted that the defendant had been sued and judgment had gone against him as to some farmers who had not been let into their farms by his predecessors and with whom he had not interfered. We pointed out at the time when this submission was made that if any farmer who claims the benefit of the judgment appealed from, in fact holds, not from the Odoben Stool, but from another self-owning landlord who is not the agent of the defendant's stool, then this judgment would not operate against the latter, as the farmer's remedy would be to sue his own landlord who enforces terms. of occupation which, it is claimed, are not in accord with the original Jetting agreement.

In my opinion the concurrent findings of the courts below shou1d be upheld. I would accordingly dismiss this appeal.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
