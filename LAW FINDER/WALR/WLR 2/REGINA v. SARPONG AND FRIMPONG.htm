                           May 13, 1957.                              

                                               REGINA v. SARPONG AND FRIMPONG.

                                               Supreme Court of Ghana, Court of Appeal

                                                               Korsah C.J., Quashie. Idun, van Lare JJ..

 

 

Criminal Law-Practice-Trial of more than one accused each on more than one count.

Criminal Law-Appeals-Charge of minor offence in addition but alternative to charge of major offence-Effect of conviction for major offence.

Criminal Law-Unlawful assembly-Assembly in moving vehicle-Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, ss. 360 and 374.

The charges against the two appellants arose from shooting incidents that took place when they were passengers in a vehicle. The vehicle was passing a group of people when the first appellant discharged a shot-gun from it and wounded two bystanders. One of the wounded pg 343 men informed the police of the incident and later, as the vehicle was returning along the same route, the police attempted to intercept it. The first appellant again fired his gun, this time at the police as well as the bystanders, but no further injury was caused, and the vehicle with its passengers escaped. The appellants were convicted at the Kumasi Assize on all the counts preferred against them. These were: as regards the first shooting incident, attempted murder and causing a wound (these charges were against both appellants); as regards the second incident, attempted murder (against both appellants), using a firearm to cause harm (against the first appellant), aiding and abetting the use of a firearm to cause harm (against the second appel­lant); as regards the whole affray, unlawful assembly with other persons unknown (against both appellants). On the appeal it was argued, inter alia, for the defence, that unlawful assembly could not be committed on a moving vehicle.

Held, (i) in a criminal trial where several counts are preferred against several accused, it is essential not only that each count against each accused should be considered separately. A verdict on one count is entirely independent of a verdict on another count: where two counts are tried together the position is as if two juries were trying two indictments. In the present case it had not been established that the second appellant was a passenger in the vehicle at the time of the first incident. The conviction against him on the first count of attempted murder and on the count of causing a wound, therefore, could not be sustained. B. v. Appea ( (1951) 13 W.A.C.A. 143) applied.

(ii) The charge of causing a wound was an alternative to the charge of attempted murder (in the first incident) and was not founded on facts that would constitute a separate offence. For this reason, on the conviction of the first appellant on the major charge of attempted murder the alternative, minor, charge against him should have been dismissed. Similarly, as regards the second incident, the charge of using a firearm to cause harm (first appellant) and aiding and abetting such use (second appellant) were alternative and minor charges to the major charges of attempted murder and, on oonviction on the major charge, should have been dismissed.

(iii) The essence of the offence of unlawful assembly is that the persons concerned must have conducted themselves "in such manner as to cause persons in the neighbourhood reasonably to fear ... " If this requirement is met it is immaterial where the assembly takes place, so that the fact that the persons concerned were assembled in a moving vehicle was no defence to the charge.

The following judgment of the court was given by VAN LARE J.:

The appellants were tried and convicted at the Criminal Assize at Kumasi on all the counts preferred against them. They have appealed to this court against their conviction and sentence. The first three counts, which involve both men, are these: (1) attempt to murder one Kofi Frimpong; (2) attempt to murder G jSgt. Anthony Eyeson; (3) causing wound to Kofi Frimpong. The fourth count, which is the use of a firearm to cause harm to G / Sgt. Anthony Eyeson is against the first appellant only. The fifth count charges the second appellant with aiding and abetting the first appellant in committing the offence named in the fourth count. The last and sixth count, unlawful assembly, involves both accused and other persons unknown.

The following is quoted from the judgment of the learned trial judge:

" The case for the Crown is that on the 28th day of January, 1956, between 8 a.m. and 8.45 a.m. the two accused with three other persons unknown plied in an open Land-Hover (or Jeep), No. All 9438, painted with the colours of the National Liberation Movement (N.L.M.) from Ashanti New Town to the Bantama Area passing the road near the Car Washing Park (Narkama) and back on the same route. On the first journey, on reaching the Narkama Area, Fred Sarpong who was armed with an offensive weapon which appeared to be and upon discharge sounded like, a shot gun, fired twice and wounded first, Kwadjo Mensah and secondly, Kofi Frimpong. On the return journey, again on reaching Narkama, which is a place commonly known to be used by members of the Convention Peoples Party (C.P.I).), and their friends, Fred Sarpong fired twice upon the crowd there, resulting in a panic and the crowd scattered and fled. On the occasion of the return journey, Crown witnesses Sergeant Eyeson, Escort Police Constables Billa Grunshie, Ackah and Oppong had arrived in a taxi in the N arkama Area on their way to court as witnesses in a criminal case, and had been stopped by Kwadjo Mensah who made a report to Sergeant Eyeson and also showed him the wound he had sustained from the gun shot. Not only were these Crown witnesses eyewitnesses of the incident on the return journey, but Sergeant Eyeson was fired upon by Fred Sarpong as he chased the jeep, followed by Escort Police Constable Billa Grunshie, and shouted at Fred Sarpong and Yaw Frimpong by their names to stop. The jeep was driven away and lost sight of, but a live cartridge which fell from the jeep was picked up by Sergean1i Eyeson. " pg 345

The jeep had made two journeys and there were shooting incidents on each separate journey. Although the shooting incidents out of which the charges emanate may form one continuous transaction and each offence can rightly be regarded as an act done in the execution of the same design it is nevertheless necessary and important to draw a line separating the incidents connected with the earlier journey of the jeep from the latter one. In a criminal trial it is absolutely essential to consider not only each count separately but also the case against each accused must- be considered separately. A verdict on one count is entirely independent of a verdict on another count. Where even two counts are tried together the position is the same as if two juries were trying two indictments-R. v. Appea (1). Bearing in mind that each count is a separate indictment it is important therefore to examine the evidence carefully in order to ascertain whether only one or both prisoners are connected with a particular count as laid.

Now upon a careful examination of the evidence we are satisfied that there is nothing to connect the second appellant with the acts committed from the passing jeep on its first journey. There is no evidence that on its first journey the jeep had the second appellant as one of its occupants. The only person identified on the passing vehicle on the first journey was Fred Sarpong, the first appellant; in' the absence of satisfactory evidence, direct or circumstantial, that the second appellant was also an occupant of the vehicle he cannot be convicted of any offence then committed by Fred Sarpong. The learned trial judge erred in presuming apparently that when between 8 a.m. and 8.45 a.m. on the material date the jeep first passed the area the two appellants with others were in it. Apart from the identification of the first appellant, no other occupant of the vehicle was identified on its first journey, and therefore the conviction of the second appellant on the first and third counts is not supported by the evidence.

Apart from this patent misstatement of the facts it is conceded that the quotation from the learned judge's judgment fully represents the case for the prosecution and there has been no other criticism of the facts upon which the prosecution's case was based.

Mr. Koi Larbi for the appellants has argued this appeal on several grounds but we propose to deal only with those with merit or substance. It has been argued and conceded on behalf of the Crown that having regard to the nature of the counts preferred against the appellants the learned trial judge erred in convicting the appellants on all the counts.

We are satisfied that counts 1 and 3 are not separate, but alternative counts, as it was one and the same act of shooting which created the offence of attempted murder of Kofi Frimpong (first count) or in the alternative causing a wound .to him (third count). A conviction on the first count includes the minor offence contemplated by the third count pg 346 which should have been dismissed and in respect of which the appellants should have been discharged. The maxim is " Nemo debet bis puniri pro uno delicto' '-one must not be punished twice for the same offence.

     The same consideration also applied to counts 2, 4 and 5. The second count charges both appellants with the attempted murder of G/Sgt. Anthony Eyeson, who ducked the shot and was not hit or hurt. It is the - same actus reus which gave rise to the charge of the use of firearms to cause harm as an alternative charge. Because the­victim ducked and was not hit or hurt it cannot be said that a separate offence had been committed apart from the major offence of attempted murder. The more serious offence includes the minor. One must necessarily use a· deadly means of harm to be guilty of attempted murder. The second appellant was also convicted for aiding and abetting the first appellant in regard to the use of deadly means of harm but both have also been convicted of the major alternative count of attempted murder.

In the circumstances the convictions and sentences of the first appellant on the fourth count, and of the second appellant on the fifth count must be quashed and set aside.

Learned counsel for the appellants has argued that an unlawful assembly cannot be constituted in a passing or moving vehicle. We do not agree. The offence of an unlawful assembly js committed when three or more persons assemble together with intent to commit an offence. Where such an assembly takes place is immaterial. Inthis case the fact that there were people assembled on a moving vehicle \vhich plied along a certain area with one of the occupants shooting into the neighbouring crowd can leave no doubt in anybody's mind that the intent was to commit an offence, considering especially the fact that some offence indeed had been committed as the vehicle plied along. On the first journey the first appellant was seen in company with four others and on the second journey both appellants were seen amongst five others together on the passing vehicle. \Ve are therefore of the opinion that an unlawful assembly can be constituted and was constituted by the appellants and some others on the moving vehicle.

Objection has been taken to the part of the judgment which appears to deal with conspiracy when no such charge had been preferred. We are of the opinion that the passage relates to the direction of the trial judge on the consideration to be given to the offence of unlawful assembly preferred in the indictment. We think that where three or more people assemble together with intent to commit a crime there is the necessary inference that such an agreement involves a concert or common design which smells of conspiracy and the judge was therefore entitled to direct himself on this element in order to arrive at a verdict upon the charge of unlawful assembly. There is in fact no conviction pg 347 for conspiracy, an offence not preferred; it is clear that the appellants were convicted for unlawful assembly, the sixth count, and not of conspiracy.

There is overwhelming evidence in support of the conviction of the first appellant on counts 1, 2, 6 and his appeal against conviction in respect of these counts is dismissed accordingly. For reasons already stated the appeal of the first appellant against conviction and sentence on counts 3' and 4 is allowed; the convictions and sentences.on these counts are hereby set aside and quashed.

By his presence on the vehicle, and taking into consideration all the surrounding circumstances, we are satisfied that the second appellant was not a mere onlooker but a confederate and therefore amenable to the law as though he himself had a hand in the execution of the common design, that is to say in the shooting from the jeep specifically on its second journey. There is this clear evidence to warrant his conviction on the second and sixth counts; his appeal against conviction on these counts therefore fails. For reasons already given his appeal is allowed in respect of his convictions and sentences on counts 1, 3 and 5 which are hereby set aside and quashed .•.

Each of the appellants has appealed against sentence. It is not the practice of this court to interfere with sentence unless some principle is involved or it is manifestly excessive. The offences are serious ones, and it was necessary for heavy sentences to be imposed in order to check the violence prevalent in Ashanti at the time, but there are no previous convictions to justify a sentence of fifteen years' imprisonment with hard labour on the first and second counts having regard especially to the fact that the prosecution preferred minor alternative charges in respect of the same respective acts. Further­more we are of the opinion that in passing sentence on counts 1 and 2 the learned trial judge might have been inadvertently influenced by the other convictions wrongly recorded against the appellants on the alternative counts. We cannot speculate as to whether the learned trial judge would have imposed the same penalty for counts 1 and 2 had he dismissed the alterntive counts 3, 4 and 5 and discharged the appellants on those counts as he ought to have done. In the circum­stances we think that justice would be done to allow the appeal against sentence on counts 1 and 2. The sentences of fifteen years' imprison­ment with hard labour passed at the trial on the first appellant for his convictions on counts 1 and 2 respectively are reduced and we sub­stitute therefor a sentence of ten years' imprisonment with hard labour on each count; and the sentence of fifteen years' imprisonment with hard labour passed at the trial on the second appellant for his conviction on the second count is also reduced and we substitute therefor a sentence of eight years' imprisonment with hard labour on that count; pg 348 the sentence of one year's imprisonment with hard labour passed at the trial on each of the appellants on the sixth count is to stand. The sentences to run concurrently.

 

Judgment accordingly.

S. G. D.
