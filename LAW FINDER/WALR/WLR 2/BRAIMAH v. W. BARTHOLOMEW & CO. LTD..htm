The plaintiff was the divorced husband of the defendant. Prior to the divorce the husband and wife occupied land which had belonged to the wife alone before the marriage. During the marriage the husband and wife had erected buildings on the land, the cost of these being met from joint funds to which they each contributed. After the divorce the husband claimed from the defendant half the value of the buildings.

Held, the rule quicquid plantatur solo, solo cedit is displaced only where one person builds on the land of another and the latter, as a result of his laches, is debarred from asserting his own interest. Where the builder is conscious of the defect in his own title but nevertheless builds he may not avail himself of this exception ana the fact that the relation· ship between the parties is that of husband and wife makes no difference to this rule.

It is conceded in the particulars of the plaintiff's claim that the land did in fact belong to the defendant and that the buildings were erected from funds jointly owned by the plaintiff and defendant. This concession is no doubt based on my finding in a previous action between the parties in which the question of title was decided. I will deal with the case, therefore, on this basis.

The property at present consists of a compound surrounded by a fence made of disused drums. There are three houses, one of which contains a shop, and there is a kitchen, formerly used as a bakery, at the rear of the compound.

The plaintiff's valuer has estimated the present-day value of the buildings as: the building on the right £80, the bakery £40, the fencing £6. The plaintiff has omitted the second house on the left side of the compound, possibly owing to a failure of recollection since he last saw the property about a year ago. From my inspection I would say that it is approximately the size of that on the right and I will assign the same value, namely £80. The valuer's evidence as to the other buildings was not challenged and I accept his figures. The fruit trees are more difficult to assess but I will put their total value at £25.

The question is wbether on this state of the facts the plaintiff has an enforceable claim either in law or equity. Where buildings are erected or trees planted on land belonging to another the maxim " quicquid plantatur solo, solo cedit "applies. The only exception to this rule is where a man builds on land supposing it to be his own and the real owner perceiving his mistake abstains from setting him right and leaving him to persevere in his error. In such a case a court of equity will not allow the real owner to assert his right to the land and buildings, see Ramsden v. Dyson (1) and Attorney-General to Prince of Wales v. Collom (2). But as was pointed out by Clare L.C. in Iienney v. Brown (3) at p. 519:

" as to the equity arising from valuable and lasting improvements I do not consider that a man who is conscious of a defect in his title, and with that conviction in his mind expends a sum of money on improvements is entitled to avail himself of it."

In the present case there can be no doubt that the plaintiff was well aware that the land belonged to his wife, having been acquired by her before the marriage. The buildings were erected out of money accruing from the joint proceeds of the business carried on by husband and wife, no doubt in the expectation that this relationship would continue. There was nothing in the nature of fraud on the part of the defendant.

Does the fact that the parties were husband and wife make any difference? I think not. The position is set out by the learned author of Halsbury's Laws of England, 2nd ed., vol. It>, p. 674, para. 1076, as follows:

" Whether a husband is entitled to reimbursement of expendi. ture in respect of improvements of his wife's estate depends upon the circumstances. If the money was expended by him in the belief that the property was his own, he is entitled to charge for improvements, but must account for the rents received by him. If he has an interest in the property, and incurs the expenditure in respect of that interest, he is not entitled to reimbursement, though his interest may determine sooner than he expected, nor can he claim for money expended on improvements of the wife's estate if he has been permitted to receive the rents and profits, In any case the burden of proving that the circumstances are such as to entitle him to reimbursement lies on him."

Mr. Forster has been unable to cite any case where a spouse erecting buildings on the land of the other party to a marriage out of joint moneys has been able to recover a contribution in respect thereof. The plaintiff's claim accordingly fails.
