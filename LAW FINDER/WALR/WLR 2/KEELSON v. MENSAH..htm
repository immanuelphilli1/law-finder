Native Law and Custom - Family property - Person to sue or be sued on behalf of family-Headship of family put in issue.

Native Law and Custom-Family land--Land held for a determinable estate by one family from another-Circumstances that will cause forfeiture and reversion.

The plaintiff, as head of a family, sued the defendant, as head of a family linked and associated with the plaintiff's family, for the return of certain lands. It was established that the land concerned had, at some remote time, been the subject of a usufructuary gift (Le., a deter­minable estate, determinable, inter alia, by waste) from the plaintiff's family to the defendant's family. With time the links uniting the two families had loosened and the plaintiff sought to advance the non­fulfilment by the defendant's family of certain customary observances­notably a failure to attend or contribute to the cost of funeral rites-as waste sufficient to cause forfeiture. In the action before the Native Court of first instance the defendant denied the plaintiff's claim and contended also that he was not the head of his family and was therefore the wrong person to be sued.

Held, (i) except in very limited circumstances only the head of a family may sue or be sued in an action involving family property. pg 272  Where a defendant, sued as head of his family, puts the headship in issue, proof of the defendant's headship becomes an essential ingredient of the plaintiff's case.

(ii) A mere loosening of the ties linking families together is not. sufficient act of waste to cause forfeiture of land held as an estate at will by one family of another.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It is clear that an estate of this nature is deter­minable by abandonment, or by becoming bona vacantia as a result of the extinction of the grantee family, or by a purported alienation in denial of the grantor's residual interest. The present case appears to indicate that there can be few incidents, or waste, causing forfeiture apart from the three general ones just mentioned. There may, however, be others in particular

· localities, by local as opposed to general custom.]

APPEAL from a decision of the Essarkyir Native Court" B " on September 22, 1956, giving judgment for the plaintiff in an action for the surrender of family land.

Sekyi for the plaintiff.

The defendant appeared in person.

OLLENNU J. In this suit, commenced in the Native Court" B " of Essarkyir, the plaintiff claimed an order upon the defendant to surrender to him his ancestral land known and called Adasemadze, and in default £50 damages. The plaintiff sued in his capacity as head of the Twidan Stool family of Ekumfi Ekrawfo, and sued the defendant as the head of the Adwinadze family of Ekumfi Adansi.

The extent or identity of the land is not in dispute. On April 4, 1956, when the defendant appeared before the Native Court for the first time on the writ, he pleaded not liable generally to the claim, and contended specifically that he was not the head of his family and that he had consequently been wrongly sued. The Native Court heard arguments on both sides, but made no ruling: all it said was:

" Case to proceed-submission heard by both sides."

After the close of the evidence, the Native Court inspected the land in dispute.

In a short judgment delivered on September 23, 1956, the Native Court stated as follows:

" Upon the evidence adduced by both plaintiff and defendant with their witnesses which are on record the court became satisfied with the undermentioned points:

(1) That the defendant could not prove as to how his ancestor or elder came by the said land Adasemadze in accordance with evidence on record.

(2) That the defendant's exhibit put to evidence does not show how the defendant's elder got the land Adasemadze, in  pg 273 clause 4 in his possession whilst clause 6 explains the defendant's elder's possession.

(3) That the court believe the defendant's elder got the land Adasemadze as a gift through brotherhood in family affairs, as evidenced by the plaintiff, since native customs or usages show the oneness of Twidan, Adwinadze and Abiradze families or clans.

(4) That there were no witnesses at the land inspection to prove that the land actually belonged to the defendant whilst on the other hand the plaintiff had witnesses to prove ownership of the land. Evidences of the defendant'~ witnesses do not speak of clearing of virgin forest by the defendant's elder or ancestor.

(5) That according to the land inspection report the defendant did not cut any boundary marks but admitted the plain­tiff's boundary marks as the correct dimensions of the land Adasemadze. "

In view of the above facts, the court entered judgment in favour of the plaintiff for £15 for pacification, with costs to be taxed. Damages were struck out.

Dissatisfied with that judgment the defendant appealed to this court on four grounds which together involve only two material points: namely that the defendant was wrongly sued as head of the Adwinadze family, and that the judgment was against the weight of evidence.

There is no doubt that the claim is against the defendant as head of his family, and not against him personally. It is a well-settled principle of native custom that, except in very special circumstances, only the head of the family can sue or be sued in respect of family property. Although the defendant put in issue the capacity in which he was sued, the plaintiff did not lead any evidence whatsoever in proof of his allegation that the defendant was the head of the Adwinadze family and the Native Court did not direct itself on that most important issue.

Now to the weight of evidence. The findings of fact made by the Native Court seem to show some confusion of thought on the question of onus of proof. They appear to indicate that the onus was upon the defendant to prove his title to the land and that he failed to discharge that onus. The writ of summons is framed in the form usually employed in Native Courts. It calls upon the defendant to show cause why he should not surrender the land to the plaintiff: in other words to prove his title to the land. 'l'hat form of writ does not shift the onus. The proper direction is that the onus is upon the plaintiff to prove his title. The defendant may have no title at all to the land; nevertheless, the plaintiff must fail unless he is able to establish his own title. pg274

In fairness to the Native Court, it must be pointed out that the third of the findings of fact made by them amounts to a finding that the plaintiff had discharged the onus upon him. By that finding they accepted the evidence of the plaintiff that the defendant's predecessor acquired the Adasemadze land not by cultivation of the virgin forest but as a gift made to him by the plaintiff's ancestors. But in spite of this positive and unqualified finding of fact the Native Court entered judgment for the plaintiff. It is difficult to support that judgment unless a further finding is read into it, namely, that the family ties, by reason of which the gift was made, had been broken, so that the gift failed and the land reverted to the family of the donor.

Assuming, however, that that further finding of fact is a necessary inference from the judgment of the Native Court, I must say that such a finding is not warranted by the evidence. The evidence on the record of the severance of family ties is very meagre; it was given by the plaintiff in his evidence-in-chief, in cross-examination, and in his answers to the Native Court. In his evidence-in-chief he said:

., About two years ago, we called on one Bedzi Gua who was feeding in that land and told him/her that it was through brother­hood that we gave you this land but since the link of brotherhood is broken, do not fell palm trees on that land for we shall require it back. To this Bedzi Gua replied that he/she was going to inform Kofi Mensah-the defendant herein-and some elders."

Under cross-examination he said:

" Yes we have had funerals which you have not attended when called. One Nyameabah when passing to Otabanadzi was called by my elders and questioned as to this breaking of the link of brotherhood and this Nyameabah replied that he has no one to come down with, hence he does not come."

In answer k) the Native Court he stated as follows:

.. My demand for the land is that the present people have broken the link of brotherhood existing between my family and theirs, hence my demand. Because formerly they used to attend funeral libation at Ekrawfo when we lose a member and we attend when they lose theirs. \Ve pay money as help but do not share internally with them. When Nyameabah was called and asked of the reason why people of Adansi did not help the funeral of ours, he replied that he had no people to attend with, hence he has stopped attending funeral libations. "

This is not evidence which can support a finding that family relationship has been broken, necessitating determination of a gift of land made by one branch of the family to another branch. The Native Court was therefore wrong on the face of its third finding in giving judgment for the plaintiff. For these reasons I allow the appeal, set aside the judgment and order of the Native Court, including the order pg 275~as to cost, and substitute therefor an order dismissing the plaintiff's claim. The defendant will have the costs of this appeal and costs in the Native Court to be taxed.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
