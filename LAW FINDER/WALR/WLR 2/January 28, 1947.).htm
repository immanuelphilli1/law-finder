January 28, 1947

WILLIAMS v. DEPUTY COMMISSIONER OF INCOME TAX.

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Western Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Sekondi

Hooper J.

 

 

( ),

1/1 Income Tax-Ascertainment of income- Whether legal expenses an allowable deduction-Income Tax Ordinance, c. 27 of 1943, ss. 10 (1) and 11 (a).

The appellant claimed a deduction under the Income Tax Ordinance, c. 27 of 1943, s. 10, for the purposes of ascertaining his taxable income, of money expended by him in legal expenses, as a party to litigation over the estate of a deceased person. He claimed that a successful termination to the litigation would result in income accruing to him and asserted that the legal costs were necessary expenditure for the purposes of obtaining that income. The Commissioner of Income Tax refused to allow the deduction.

Held, the legal expenses concerned were not incurred by the appellant in the production of his income but came in the category of private expenses, under the Income Tax Ordinance, c. 27 of 1943, s. 11 (a), and /IS such were not an allowable deduction for tax purposes.

Case referred to :

(1) Southwell v. Sarill Brothers [1901] 2 K.B. 349; 17 T.L.R 513.

APPEAL under the Income Tax Ordinance, c. 27 of 1943, s. 58 (1) against a refusal of the Commissioner of Income Tax to allow expenses incurred in litigation as a deductible allowance for income tax purposes.

The appellant appeared in person.

Christian for the Deputy Commissioner of Income Tax.

HOOPER J. In this case, Mr. F: A. Williams appeals against his assessment to Income Tax by the Deputy Commissioner of Income Tax for the Income Tax year 1945-1946.

The grounds of the appeal are as follows:

" 1. Because the sum of £700 spent by the appellant on the prosecution of the appeal J A.Taylor v. J.            Annu Essuman and F. A. Williams depending on appeal from the West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session, in the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council in England was and is a proper amount deductible from the income of the appellant F. A. Williams for the year 1945/1946 assessment, and the respondent, the Deputy Commis­sioner of Income Tax, was wrong in law in refusing to make or allow the said deduction.

"2. Because the said sum of £'100 was an expenditure reasonably and properly incurred by the appellant F. A. Williams in connection with the estates of Mrs. K. A. Taylor nee Whilhilmina Awoonor Williams and Dorothy Essuman Gwira in the Colony and involved in the said action and appeal and should therefore have been allowed by the respondent."

To this the respondent replied on October 29, and contended that: "the expenditure of £700 referred to by the appellant is not a disbursement or expense being money wholly and exclusively laid out 01' expended for the purpose of acquiring the income on which the assessment for the year of assessment 1945-1946 is based. "

At the hearing, the appellant stated that he had paid the whole sum claimed by the Income Tax Authorities, that is, £108 10s. 0d. Re claimed exemption, however, from income tax with respect to the sum of £700 which he had incurred in litigation over the estate of his late daughter in the case of Taylor v. Essuman and Williams, which is pending before the Privy Council. The appellant claimed that the expenditure of this sum should be exempt from taxation under the income tax legislation of the Colony and that the sum he had paid by way of tax with respect to the sum of £'100 should be refunded by the Income Tax Commissioner, who had refused to do so. The appellant relied upon subsection (1) of section 10 of the Income Tax Ordinance which provides that a deduction is allowed with respect to all outgoings and expenditure "'holly and exclusively incurred in the production of the income. In reply to the court, the appellant stated that he had received no income yet with respect to the sum of £700 expended on the litigation, because no decision in the case had yet   been given by the Privy Council.

           In reply, :Mr. Christian, for the respondent, submitted that there was a clear difference between:

(a) money spent in the production of the income; and (b) money expended by way of capital investment.

The £700 expended by the appellant was money spent by way of capital investment and was for the purpose of securing property in which a certain speculative element. was involved. The application for exemption could, in any case, only be made after the property had accrued in fact. Mr. Christian refered to Southwell v. Savill Brothers (1). Mr. Christian added that if the sum for which the money was expended is in fact realised, it is still a capital sum and not income.

Upon consideration I have come to the conclusion that there is no substance in the appeal.

The £700 has not been employed either for purposes of earning his income as a professional lawyer or of earning an income by any other means which may be open to a person acting as barrister and solicitor in the way in which that is normally done. The appellant has been faced with the necessity of expending money in securing legal advice and assistance in connection with property in which he is interested. The expenditure of money in payment of fees to lawyers, or otherwise for legal purposes, is an obligation which every­one must enter into ,,,ho avails himself of the services of legal advisers or practitioners. If it could be shown that the Income Tax Ordinance contains any provision to the effect that fees paid by private individuals to their legal advisers, or other expenditure for legal purposes, are exempt from income tax in cases where the taxpayer hoped to make money as the result of an action before the court, there might be some substance in the case put forward' by the appellant; but I can find nothing in the Income Tax Ordinance to this effect. Indeed, if it were so, it would mean that all those taxpayers who were engaged in litigation in the hope that a judgment might perhaps be given in their favour whereby they may obtain a sum ()f money, could all be entitled to exemption from the payment of income tax in respect to the fees and other moneys expended for purposes of bringing the action. In any case, the point seems to me to be resolved by the clear terms of item (a) of section 11 of the Income Tax Ordinance, which provides that no deduction shall be allowed with respect to private expenses. I hold that the Deputy Commissioner of Income Tax was right in refusing to allow any deduction.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
