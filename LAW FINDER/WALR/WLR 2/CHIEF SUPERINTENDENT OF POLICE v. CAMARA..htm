                                      CHIEF SUPERINTENDENT OF POLICE v. CAMARA.

                                                              High Court of the Protectorate of the Gambia

                                                                                         Miles C.J.

 

 

Criminal Law-Deportation of aliens-Recommendation for deportation of convicted alien - Necessity for proving status as alien - Deportation (Aliens) Ordinance, c. 59.

The appellant was convicted of cattle theft and W1t6 recommended by the magistrate for deportation. The alien status of the appellant was not alleged by the prosecution at the trial, nor was it admitted by the appellant.

Held, where the prosecution desires to seek a recommendation for deportation of a person accused of an offence, the alien status of the accused, as this is defined by the Deportation (Aliens) Ordinance, c. 59, s. 2, must be alleged and put in issue at the trial, unless it is admitted by the accused. pg 208

APPEAL from a conviction for cattle stealing and a recommendation for deporation as an alien, given in the Magistrate's Court in the Western Division on October 13, 1956.

Bridges, Assistant Attorney-General, for the Chief Superintendent of Police.

The appellant appeared in person.

MILES C.J. This appellant was convicted jointly with another man in the Protectorate Court of the Western Division on a charge of cattle theft contrary to sections 252 and 255 of the Criminal Code. He was sentenced to six months' imprisonment with hard labour and the Magistrate made a recommendation for his deportation under section 24 of the Repatriation of Convicted Aliens Ordinance, 1949 (now the Deportation (Aliens) Ordinance, c. 59, s. 5).

The appellant has appealed against conviction and sentence and also the making of the recommendation of deportation. So far as the conviction is concerned there is no substance in this appeal. The appellant was identified in the act by a man to whom he was personally known. There was also evidence given by a witness that the appellant offered the bull for sale to him. There is no ground therefore upon which the conviction could possibly be disturbed.

The appellant has four previous convictions for stealing, the last conviction being for stealing cattle for which he was sentenced to fifteen months' imprisonment with hard labour. In the circumstances the sentence of six months' imprisonment, awarded by the Magistrate, appears to me to be somewhat lenient. However, although with con­siderable hesitation, I am not prepared to say that it was so inadequate as to amount to an error in principle.

It is clear that the appellant's principal complaint is against the making of the recommendation for deportation. Th~re was, in fact, no evidence before the Magistrate that the appellant was an alien. I have repeatedly said that if it is desired to ask for a recommendation for deportation, evidence must be given that the accused person is an alien, as defined by section 2 of the Ordinance, unless this is admitted by the accused. The recommendation is not supported in the present case by Mr. Bridges and it is accordingly set aside. The appeal against conviction and sentence is dismissed.

Appeal dismissed. Recommendation for deportation set aside.

S. G. D.
