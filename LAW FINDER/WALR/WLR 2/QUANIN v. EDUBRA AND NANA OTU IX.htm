QUANIN v. EDUBRA AND NANA OTU IX  

                                   West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                                 Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.J.A.

 

.

Native Courts-Jurisdiction-Land cause-Dispute before Native trial Court with territorial ,jurisdiction-Intervention by head of neighbouring state - Whether ,jurisdiction of trial COU7't ousted - Whether intervention created conflict of loyalties sufficient to make dispute not properly cognizable by trial court-Native Court (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s. 58.

Practice-Transfer of cause from Land Court to competent Native Court ­ Native Court (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s. 54 (1).

The plaintiff sought a declaration requiring the defendant to render tribute to him in respect of land belonging to the plaintiff but in the possession of the defendant. The action was commenced in the Native Court with territorial jurisdiction over the area containing the land. The co-defendant, who was Omanhene of the neighbouring state, intervened by leave and, on an application to a magistrate, had the action trans­ferred to the Land Court. The application was made on the grounds that the real issue in dispute was whether the land concerned was part of the co-defendant's state or the state to which the plaintiff belonged. It was argued that since the territorial jurisdiction of the Native trial Court (the court of the plaintiff's state) was thus brought into question it was beyond the competence of the Native trial Court to hear the case. It was further argued that the nature of the dispute involved the Native Court panel members in a conflict of loyalties and interests sufficient to make the action not properly cognizable by the Native Court. The Land Court transferred the matter back to the original Native Court on the ground that it was a court competent to hear the dispute. The Native trial Court found for the plaintiff against both defendants, and this finding was upheld on appeal to the Land Court.

Held, (i) the action at the commencement was between two parties living within a state and concerning the incidents of tenure of land there. The Native trial Court, with jurisdiction over the territory of the state, was therefore properly seised of the matter and its jurisdic­tion would not be ousted by intervention from outside the state. pg 245

(ii) The dispute was not between states as to the boundaries and extent of state territory, nor even between stools of different states as to the extent of stool lands. It was a dispute between a state on the one hand and a subject of another state on the other. A dispute of this nature raised no presumption of a conflict of loyalties as would make the dispute not "properly cognizable" by the court with competent territorial jurisdiction.

(iii) The power of re-transfer, conferred on a Land Judge by the second proviso to section 54 (1) of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, is properly exercisable when, inter alia, the original transfer into the Land Court has been made on untenable grounds, or when a Native Court with competent jurisdiction has come into existence subsequent to the transfer into the Land Court.

APPEAL by the co-defendant from a decision of Acolatse J. given in the Land Court, Central Judicial Division on January 11, 195G, upholding the finding of the Native Court" B " of Asin Atandanso on September 29, 1955, granting a declaration of title to the plaintiff. The defendant did not appeal.

Anin for the plaintiff.

Awoonor- Williams for the co·defendant.

COUSSEY P. This is an appeal, by the co-defendant only, from a judgment of the Land Court, Cape Coast, confirming on appeal a judg­ment of the Native Court "'B " of Asin Atandanso in an action in which the Native Court found against the co-defendant, who had inter­vened in the suit, that the plaintiff was the owner of the Benda land in dispute (referred to in the writ of summons and delineated on the plan) and against the original defendant that he was liable to pay tribute to the plaintiff for the occupation of cocoa farms on the plaintiff's said land.

The appeal was filed on questions of law and fact, but we intimated to Mr. Awoonor-Williams, counsel for the co-defendant that we could find no reason to disturb the findings of fact of the trial Native Court. Mr. Awoonor-Williams therefore confined his argument to grounds (a), (b) and (c) of the appeal. These raise the question whether the Native Trial Court had jurisdiction and was competent to hear the suit. If the trial Native Court had no jurisdiction its judgment is a nullity and also that of the Land Court confirming that judgment.

In support of the appeal it is argued that the area of land in dispute' is claimed both by the State of Asin Atandanso and the State of Abura and, consequently, a Native Court of neither State had jurisdiction or was competent to try the suit, as any panel member of either Native Court would be interested in the suit on one side or the other. Further, that the land in dispute, by the very fact that it was in dispute between  pg 246

the two States, could not be said to be within the territorial limits of the State of Asin Atandanso. The Native Court "B" of Asin Atandanso thus could not have jurisdiction over the suit by virtue of the land being within the area of its territorial jurisdiction. It is further argued that the co-defendant, as an Omanhene or head of the State of Abura, was not amenable to the jurisdiction of the Native Court of a neighbouring State.

These points were not taken in the court of first appeal nor at the trial in the Native Court but Mr. Awoonor-Williams reminded us that a point of jurisdiction can be raised at any stage even in the Court of Appeal.

Turning to the record of the appeal, it will be seen that the action originated in the Native Court on May 7, 1954, in a claim by E.ofi Quanin, Head of the Asona family of Bosumadwi, against Edubra of Jakai for an order to pay tribute. On June 18, 1954, Nana Kwesi Egyie of Bosumadwi was joined as co-plaintiff on his application, and Nana Otu IX, Omanhene of Abura was joined as co-defendant on his applica­tion. On October 11, 1954, the Magistrate_, Cape Coast, on an application by the co-defendant, filed on July 7, for an order of transfer, reported the suit to the judge of the Land Court who, by an order dated October 19, 1954, transferred the suit to the Land Court for hearing.

The grounds advanced in the co-defendant's application for the order of transfer were (a), that the suit refers to a portion of land in dispute between a subject of Asin Atandanso State and the Stool of Abura, and (b) that the suit affects two States. It is observed that on October 11, 1954, before the Magistrate, both parties agreed that neither the Native Court of Asin Atandanso nor the Native Court at Abakrampa (Abura) had jurisdiction over the suit.

On March 7, H)55, the action was called on before a judge of the Land Court, Benson J., when according to the minutes of the court, the following occurred:

.. The plaintiff claims from the defendant to show cause why the defendant fails to come and effect an arrangement in respect of the defendant's farms on the plaintiff's lands, or £25 damages. This action originated in the Native Court' B ' of Asin Atandanso and by order of that Court Nana Otu IX, Omanhene of Abura State was joined as a co-defendant; I fail to see why by the order of the Land Court dated 19th October, 1954, the case was transferred to this Court for hearing.

" It now appears to me that the cause of action is one within· the jurisdiction of and properly cognizable by the Native Court in which it originated, it is not an action between two States.

" By virtue of the second proviso to section 54 (1) of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, I accordingly order that the cause be heard and determined by the Native Court ' B ' in pg 247 which the action was first instituted and that the proceedings and a copy of this order be sent to that Court."

Back in the Native Court" B " of Asin Atandanso upon this order of retransfer, the co· plaintiff, Nana Kwesi Egyie II, was struck out of the suit and on June 1, 1955, the defendant and the co-defendant's representative pleaded not liable to the claim without any objection to the jurisdiction of the court. By consent, that representative stood to contest the action on behalf of the defendant and co· defendant on the issue as to whether the Benda land on which the defendant Edubra's cocoa farm was situate was in the ownership of the plaintiff's family or of the co·defendant's stool.

On this issue the plaintiff and his witnesses and the representative of the defendant and co·defendant and their witnesses gave evidence in a trial which was before the Native Court from June 1 to September 29, 1955. The land was inspected in the presence of the parties or their representatives and judgment was delivered in the terms already stated.

The Native Court ., B " of Asin Atandanso has by virtue of sections 13 and 14 (1) of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, unlimited pecuniary jurisdiction as to land within its area. By section 3 of the Ordinance such area is defined by order of the Governor·in·Council.

The plaintiff selected as his forum the Native Court which he was advised had local jurisdiction in the area including the land in issue. His claim against the original defendant was for tribute for occupation of that land, In respect of the claim therefore the original defendant was within the same local limits as the plaintiff and the cause of action arose there. If there was for any reason uncertainty on the part of the Native Court as to the area of its jurisdiction, but it was competent as regards the nature and value of the suit, I would be inclined to hold that the trial Native Court rightly exercised jurisdiction, but here the Native Court considered, as it was entitled to do, the area where the land was situate, and found that Jakai land was within the Atandanso State; it follows that the land in dispute, which is more westerly and not so near the border with the State of Abura, is within the area of jurisdiction of the trial Native Court. In my opinion that jurisdiction is not ousted by the fact that the co-defendant, a neighbouring Omanhene, elected to intervene in a suit which had been properly instituted in the Native Court of an adjoining State. That intervention would not of itself oust the jurisdiction of the trial Native Court or disqualify the members of the panel from adjudicating in a suit of which the Native Court concerned was properly and regularly seised.

The grounds on which the first order of the Land judge was made for the transfer of the suit to the Land Court for hearing, namely that the land was in dispute between a subject of Asin Atandanso State and the Stool of Abura, and that the subject· matter affected the two rival States, were not in themselves good grounds for the order of transfer. It was not a dispute between two states nor even between pg 248

a stool of one state and the stool of another state. No bias was alleged in the Native Court, and that in my opinion distinguishes this case from Denli:yera State v. Abura State (1) which was a direct contest between two adjoining Native states.

The ground put forth for the transfer in the present case was the " desire" of the co-defendant, since the matter was alleged to affect two states-an allegation which in the event proved to be incorrect.

In my opinion the second proviso to section 54 (1) of c. 98 was expressly enacted for an instance such as this where an order for transfer had been made on untenable grounds, or where a competent Native Court had come into existence where there had not previously been one. In the present case Benson J., in my opinion rightly, exercised his discretion in ordering a transfer of the suit back to the original Native Court.

The co-defendant in making his submission has been in the dilemma that the judgment appealed from, by declaring that the land is in the ownership of the plaintiff's family, finds expressly that it is not in the Abura State and that he has no title thereto as Omanhene of Abura.

Even if there had been uncertainty as to the local limits of juris­diction, which I do not think is the case, it is set at rest by the judgment and I am satisfied that there has been no consequent failure of justice.

I would dismiss the appeal with costs for the plaintiff.

KORSAII C.J. I concur.

VElU'rY Ag.J.A. I concur.

Appeal dismissed.
