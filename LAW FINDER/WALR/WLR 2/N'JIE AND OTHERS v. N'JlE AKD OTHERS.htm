                                                 N'JIE AND OTHERS v. N'JlE AKD OTHERS

                                              High Court of the Protectorate of the Gambia


                                                                                 Miles J.

The first plaintiff was the widow of one Nodj i deceased; the second, third and fourth plaintiffs were the daughters of the deceased. The claim was for recovery of possession of land owned by the deceased, The first defendant was the ward of the deceased; the second, third and fourth defendants were grantees of the first defendant in respect of portions of the land concerned.

It appeared from the evidence that the deceased had left and never returned to the land in question, at the latest, at the beginning of 1941; that when he left he asked the first defendant to move in, which the latter did; that the first defendant had occupied the land ever since without paying rent and had exercised acts of ownership over it, including the making of grants of various portions of the land to the second, third and fourth defendants; that there had been no demand for possession made against the first defendant until early in 1956; and, that this demand, when made, took the form of an action for recovery of possession brought by the first plaintiff against the first defendant in a native tribunal

Held, (i) by native law and custom the land of a deceased intestate passes to his children to the exclusion of his widow. The first plaintiff therefore could have no interest in the land sufficient to found a claim for possession.

(ii) Even if the first plaintiff had had an interest in the land, the issue as between her and the defendants (for the second, third and fourth defendants claimed through the first defendant) was res judicata by reason of the decision of the native tribunal (which although given in favour of the first plaintiff was reversed on revision by the Com­missioner) in her action against the first defendant for recovery of possession of the land.

(iii) The first defendant had become a tenant-at-will of the deceased owner of land when the former moved into posseession early in 1941. At the expiration of twelve months from the commencement of the tenancy-at-will the twelve-year period prescribed by the Limitations Acts, 1833 and 1874 had commenced to run and had run out early in 1954 without any adverse claim being made against the first defendant. As regards the first defendant and such portion of the land as remained in his possession, the second, third and fourth plaintiffs were thereby debarred by the Acts from asserting their claim to the land.

Pato!l NJie v. Hall ( (1931) 1 W.A.C.A. 100) applied.

(iv) The second, third and fourth defendants occupied portions of the land by virtue of grants from the first defendant and thereby became tenants-at-will of the first defendant. In none of these three cases had the twelve year limitation period following a twelve months' tenancy-at­wi1l run out and therefore the second, third and fourth defendants could not avail themselves of the Limitation Acts as against the second, third and fourth plaintiffs who, to this extent, must succeed in their claim.

(1) Fatou N'Jie v. Hall (1931) 1 W.A.C.A. 100.

ACTION for recovery of possession of land. The first plaintiff was the widow of the deceased intestate owner of the land, the second, third and fourth plaintiffs were the daughters of the deceased. The first defendant was the ward of the deceased in possession of the land; the second, third and fourth defendants were grantees of the first defendant in respect of portions of the land.

Forster for the plaintiffs.

MILES J. This is a claim for recovery of possession of certain property at Basse known as Ousman N'Jie Modji's yard. The first plaintiff is the widow, and the other three plaintiffs are the daughters of OusmrLll N'Jie Modji who was the original owner of the yard. The first defendant is the ward of Ousman N'Jie Modji and the remaining three defendants are the present occupiers of different portions of the property.

The plaintiffs claim to be entitled to the property by native law and custom as the descendants of Ousman N'Jie Modji, who died on October 26, 1954. The defence is, first, that the defendant, Samba N'Jie, has a prescriptive title to the property by long possession, and, secondly, that the claim is res judicata.

I will deal first with the defence of long possession. It is common ground that Ousman N'Jie Modji ceased to reside in the yard some time ago and went to live in Bathurst, but there is a conflict of evidence as to the exact year. It is also common ground that Samba N'Jie went to live in the premises after Ousman's departure, but there is a dispute as to the circumstances under which he did so. It is not suggested that any of the plaintiffs lived in the yard after Ousman N' Jie left.

With regard to the dates, the preponderance of the evidence called on behalf of the plaintiffs is to the effect that Ousman left the property during the second year of the last war. Samba N'Jie, however, says that this took place as long as twenty-five years ago. Samba N'Jie is almost illiterate and I am not prepared to accept his statement on the question of dates particularly when it is not supported by any evidence whatsoever, and I accept the evidence that Ousman N'Jie went to Bathurst during the second year of the war.

The evidence for the plaintiffs is that when Ousman left for Bathurst he " left" his half sister Njanha N'Dow in the premises and that it was she who called Samba N'Jie to come and live there. There is no doubt that at some time Njanha N'Dow was living in the property and also that she left two or three years before the end of the war and went to Golda to see her daughter Arnie who was ill there. Njanha never returned to Basse, and she died at Golda.

Samba N'Jie says that up to the time that Ousman left he was going to the yard but sleeping at the premises of his wife's family. He says that Ousman on leaving for Bathurst told him, Samba, to bring his family to live in the yard and that he was not coming back. Samba says that Njanha was not living in the property at that time but was at Golda where her husband was residing. He says that he brought her from Colda because he had heard that in future persons living in French territory would not be allowed to leave the country. I think Samba N'Jie is telling the truth about this. After all, he was the ward of Ousman and had always been treated by him as a son, and there would be nothing surprising in Ousman making an arrangement of this kind with him.

The question is, what is the legal effect of this arrangement?

Samba N'Jie seemed to regard it as a gift of the land to himself, but Mr. Forster concedes that Samba became a tenant-at-will of Ousman. The plaintiffs' claim is based upon the footing that Samba N'Jie was a tenant-at-will and I think that is the true position. I will therefore deal with the case on this basis. In the case of a tenancy-at-will time commences to run against the landlord either at the determination of the tenancy-at-will, or at the end of one year from its commencement, whichever event first happens-Fatou N,'Jic v. Hall (1). There was no demand for possession until this year and time began to run from the beginning of the year 1942. I find therefore that Samba N'Jie has been in possession of the property or part thereof at the latest from the beginning of the year 1941, without payment of rent and has exercised rights of ownership over it. He has thus acquired a good possessory title.

The question has to be considered, however, as to how much of the original property this possessory title extends. It is clear that the property today is not in the condition in which it was when Samba N'Jie went into possession. During the war a road was constructed through the property, dividing it in half. Moreover, Samba N'Jie has disposed of various portions of the land. :Four or five years ago he allowed the defendant Mamadi Corra to occupy part of the road on the side on which he, Samba, is living. Mamadi Corra has built four houses, together with a lavatory and kitchen, and has fenced it off. On the other side of the road Samba N'Jie has allowed the defendant Kumba Kunaji to live, and she has been there some nine years. This land was also fenced off. The remaining land on this side of the road is now occupied by the defendant Lamin I(inteh and is also fenced off. Samba states that he gave it to one Taylor a long time ago and it is indicative of the extent to which he has divested himself of the property that he does not know either when or how Lamin Kinteh went into possession. The question is what is the relationship in law between Samba N'Jie and the three other defendants. In my opinion it is precisely the same as that between Samba N'Jie and Ousman, that is they are tenants-at-will. A tenancy-at-will is a mere personal relationship. The tenant does not acquire a transferable interest and cannot sublet so as to create an interest which can be enforced against a third party although he may create a tenancy by estoppel against himself. Samba N'Jie therefore could not confer an interest in the land which can prevail against the plaintiffs. These defendants have been in possession for less than twelve years. The position is therefore that Samba N'Jie has only established a possessory title to that portion of the original property whieh he now occupies himself. I strongly suspect that Samba N'Jie sold the land to these defendants although he denies it, and that is the reason why the other defendants did not give evidence.

It remains to deal with the defence of res judicata. It is the fact that in January of this year the plaintiff Fatou N'Jie instituted pro­ceedings against Samba N'Jie in the district court of Fulladu East for the recovery of this land and the defendant was ordered to quit within fourteen days. There is no right of appeal against the decision of a native tribunal under the District Tribunals Ordinance, c. 49, but the defendant applied to the Commissioner to exercise his powers of revision under section 23 of the Ordinance. The Commissioner reversed the decision of the native tribunal and ordered that Samba should remain in possession apart from a small part which was to be given to the plaintiff. The short answer to the plea of res judicata is that the proceedings before the native tribunal were not between the same parties as the present. The other three plaintiffs were not parties to the original proceedings and they cannot be said to be the privies of Fatou N'Jie since they are not claiming through her but in their own right.

 

The result of this is that the claim of the second, third and fourth plaintiffs fails so far as the portion of the property occupied by Samba N'Jie is concerned but succeeds as against the other three defendants.

I cannot see that the first plaintiff, who is the widow of Ousman, has any right to the property. According to the Seyfu the land descends by native law and custom to the children of the deceased. The claim of the first-named plaintiff is dismissed as against the last three defendants without costs.

The claims of all the plaintiffs are dismissed as against the defendant Samba N'Jie with costs. 'l'here will be an order for possession in favour of the second, third and fourth plaintiffs of the land and premises occupied by the second, third and fourth defendants with costs. Possession to be given within twenty-eight days.

Judgment accordingly.
