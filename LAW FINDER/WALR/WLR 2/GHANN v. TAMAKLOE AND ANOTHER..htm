                           May 21 and 30, 1957                              

                                               GHANN v. TAMAKLOE AND ANOTHER.  

                  Supreme Court of Ghana, Ashanti Judicial Division, Divisional Court, Kumasi

                                                                                        Smith J.

 

 

Constitutional Law - Interim Regional Assemblies - Appointment 01 chair­man not being a member 01 an Assembly-Whether appointment valid ­Nature of remedy available-Quo warranto.

By the Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, 1957, s. 63, Ghana is divided into Regions, and by section 64 a Regional Assembly is required to be established for each such Region. By section 85 (1) the Members of Parliament representing electoral districts within a Region are constituted as an Interim Regional Assembly for that Region, and by section 85 (2) the Speaker is required to convene a meeting of each Interim Assembly and to preside as Chairman " ... until the Interim Regional Assembly has made rules for regulating its own procedure and appointed its own chairman." These steps were duly taken in the Ashanti Region, and in due course the Interim Regional Assembly appointed its own chairman (the first defendant), who took up the duties of his office. The person, appointed, however, was not a Member of Parliament and was thus not a member of the Interim Regional Assembly, but was drawn from outside that body. The plaintiff, who was a member of the party in opposition in the Ashanti Interim Regional Assembly, thereupon sought an injunction or, in the alterna­tive, the issue of an information in the nature of quo warranto to restrain the first defendant from acting as chairman.

Held, (i) the office of Chairman of an Interim Regional Assembly. is an office of a public nature against which a writ of quo warranto could have issued.

R. v. St. Martin's (Guardians) (1851) 17 Q.B.D. 149 referred to.

(ii) As regards membership, the Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, 1957, s. 85 (1) and (2), provides that an Interim Regional Assembly shall consist of a fixed number of persons. The sole qualifi­cation for membership is membership of the Ghana Parliament as repre­senting an electoral district within the Region concerned. The first defendant was not such a person and was therefore not entitled to hold office as Chairman of the Interim Regional Assembly. pg 354

[EDITORIAL NOTE. In a preliminary ruling the learned judge held that an information in the nature of quo warranto (the successor to the old writ of quo warranto) exists as a remedy in Ghana by reason of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 83, which provides that; "Subject to the terms of this or any other Ordinance, the common law, the doctrines of equity, and the statutes of general application which were in force in England on the 24th day of July, 1874, shall be in force wit1;lin the jurisdiction of the Courts." The judge then pointed to the absence from the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, of any provision prescribing the procedure to be followed in applying to the courts for this remedy. In its absence the learned judge applied the residual Order 74, which provides that: "Where no provision is made by these Rules the procedure, practice and form in force for the time being in the High Court of Justice in England shall, so far as they can be conveniently applied, be in force in the Supreme Court of Ghana." The practice in England as regards quo warranto proceedings has been affected by the Administration of Justice (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 1938, which by section 9 abolished informations in the nature of quo warranto but provided that in cases where the information would have lain the remedy of injunction should henceforth be available. This Act does not apply in Ghana, and therefore, as the learned judge pointed out, it could not affect the existence of the remedy in Ghana. This being so, it would appear at first sight that the remedy of an information in the nature of quo warranto and the procedure to be followed in obtaining it should still exist in Ghana in the form in which it existed in England before 1928, for the absence of procedural provisions from the Civil Procedure Rules, 1954, cannot affect the existence of a remedy available at law. The learned judge, however, went on to hold that the Administration of Justice (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 1938, had a procedural effect only and therefore that the altered procedure applicable in England applied also to Ghana, by reason of Order 74. With deference to the learned judge, this interpretation of the 1938 Act may be open to doubt. First, had the alteration to the remedies in England been procedural only, rather than substantive, they could (and no doubt would) have been affected by Rules of Court without recourse to statute; secondly, section 9 of the Act specifically abolishes the old remedy and substitutes an entirely new one. In the words used in Poyser v. Minors (1881) 7 Q.B.D. 329 at p. 333, quoted by the judge, the Act altered the "law which gives or defines the right." It would seem arguable, therefore, that the old remedy of an information in the nature of quo warranto remains available in Ghana and the procedure to be used in connection with it should be that in use in England in 1874.

If this interpretation is correct then its effects probably go beyond quo warranto and embrace the other prerogative writs of certiorari, mandamus and prohibition. The Administration of Justice (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 1938, s. 9, abolished information's in the nature of quo warranto: section 7 provides that" the prerogative writs of mandamus, prohibition and certiorari shall no longer be issued by the High Courts." The wording of the two sections differs but their effect, particularly in view of the above remarks, is perhaps the same, i.e., the old remedy disappears. Unlike quo warranto, the other three remedies are dealt with in the Civil Procedure Rules, 1954, under Order 59. The Order is headed "Procedure for Prerogative Writs "-but the word "writ" nowhere appears in the body of the Order; "order" being used instead, and the procedure prescribed is the English post-1938 procedure. On its face, therefore, while Order 59 creates new remedies, the Rules as a whole make no procedural provisions for the old, and still extant, remedies by way of writ. pg 355

 

This situation could perhaps be corrected by the Rules Committee pro­viding, in exercise of the powers conferred upon them by the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 107, that the procedure to be followed in applying for the writs of mandamus, prohibition and certiorari, and for information's in the nature of quo warranto, shall be the procedure now in use in England for seeking orders of mandamus, prohibition and certiorari, and injunctions after the style of quo warranto.]

APPLICATION for an injunction or for an information in the nature of quo warranto to restrain the appointed Chairman of the Ashanti Interim Regional Assembly from acting in that office.

Boateng for the plaintiff.

Reindorf for the defendants.

SMlTH J. The first point for decision is whether or not the office of Chairman of the Interim Assembly comes within the scope of office to which quo warranto (now injunction) proceedings are applicable.

As was said by Tindal C.J. in Darley v. R. (1):

.. this proceeding by information in the nature of quo warranto will lie for usurping any office; whether created by Charter alone or by the Crown, with the consent of Parliament, provided the office be of a public nature, and a substantive office, not merely the function or employment of a deputy or servant held at the will and pleasure of others. "

There are three tests-the source of office, the tenure, and the duties. As regards the source, that is the Statutory Instrument-the Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, 1957. I think the tenure sufficient to satisfy the rule. In the case of R. v. Speyer (2) at p. 611, Lord Reading says:

•. I have found it difficult to understand why in principle an office held at pleasure should not equally with an office of a permanent character be the subject of this remedy, provided the office be of a public and substantive character."

As regards the duties of the office-the chairman presides over a public constituted body which has the right to advise Ministers of the National Assembly and appoint committees. It is also relevant to look at the powers, privileges and business of the Chairman in the approved standing orders of the Interim Assembly. I think these clearly comprise duties of a public nature.

As regards the point that the chairman is not the holder of a •. public office," within the definition of that office at p. 3 of the Order in Council, it seems to me clear from the authorities cited and the case pg 356 of R. v. St. Martin's (Guardians) (3), that the office against which the procedure of quo warranto is applicable need not be strictly a public office, provided that the office be one the duties of which are of a public nature.

The next question is the interpretation of section 85 (1) and (2) of the Order in Council as to whether the Interim Assembly can elect its chairman from outside its own members or not. I do not think that the fact that it is not expressly stated that the chairman must be chosen from the members is conclusive. No authorities have been cited one way or another, and I cannot find any myself. The question has been argued on the wording of the section, in conjunction with other provisions of the Order in Council. The ordinary rule for the interpretation of any statute is:

" what the Legislature intended to be done or not to be done can only be legitimately ascertained from what it has chosen to enact either in express words or by reasonable and necessary implication. "

Reading subsections 85 (1) and (2) together the Interim Regional Assembly can only consist of a fixed number of persons, and the one and only qualification for membership is membership of the National Assembly. In effect, the Interim Regional Assembly is a Select Com­mittee of members of the National Assembly and in my view if the chairman could come from outside it would have to be so expressly stated. The position here cannot be allied for example to that sort of society to which any eminent man from outside may be elected chairman.

The position of the Speaker and the National Assembly has been discussed. But there it is clear that the Speaker may be appointed from within or without the National Assembly (if the former, he vacates his seat) and the constitution is "The Speaker and 104 members. " Sections 20 and 26 of the Order in Council.

There is no provision in section 85 for a chairman and a certain number of qualified members. It is, moreover, significant that the same person to preside temporarily at meetings of the Interim Regional Assemblies is the Speaker of the National Assembly pending the election of their own. That, again, is a clear indication for the exclu­sion of anyone "else except members of the National Assembly. There is specific provision for the Speaker and no one else.

I do not think it necessary to go into the right of any chairman to have a casting vote but it seems to me that this will depend on any rules in the Standing Orders.

For these reasons I grant the motion and make an order for injunction as prayed. I am not making any order for costs.

Order for an injunction.

S. G. D.
