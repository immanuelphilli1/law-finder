                    Supreme Court of Ghana, Central Judicial Division, Land Court, Cape Coast

                                                                                          Ollennu J

. Native Law and Custom - Unlawful use of oath for commencement of judicial proceedings.

The plaintiff and the defendant had been parties to an action con­cerning land in which judgment at first instance, in a Native Court, and on appeal, in the Land Court, had been given for the plaintiff. At the time of the events which gave rise to the present action an appeal by the defendant was pending before the West African Court of Appeal. The plaintiff, as he was entitled to on his judgment, took possession of the lands in dispute, whereupon the defendant swore an oath against the plaintiff, to prevent him from taking possession, and thereby caused the initiation of further proceedings before a Native Court. The Native Court found for the defendant on the erroneous ground that the pendency of the appeal prevented the plaintiff from taking possession and that in these circumstances the use of the oath procedure for initiating proceedings was justified.                              

Held, as an appeal from a judgment in the Supreme Court does not operate as a stay of execution (without there being an order of the court to that effect), the plaintiff was entitled as of right to take posses­sion of the land in dispute and therefore the use of the oath in an attempt to prevent this happening was unlawful.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Oath swearing is a recognized procedure by native custom for initiating judicial proceedings. "It is a well-known custom ... that where an oath is sworn and broken, the bystanders should take the swearer and the swearee before the Omanhene, and ... where the bystanders do not do this, one of the parties themselves should bring the matter to the notice of the Omanhene": Smith A.C.J. in Yerenchi v. Akuffo (1905) Renner, Vol. 1, Pt. 1, 366 at p. 383. The Omanhene's powers in this regard are now exercised by the Native Courts. In Inspector-General of Police v. Asare Panyin (1931) D.C. 1929-1931, 64 it was held that the use of an oath to achieve a purpose not justified by law amounts to an unlawful use of an oath. This, in addition to creating civil liability as here, is a native customary offence punishable by Native Courts: Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, First Schedule.

The oath referred to here, as distinguished from its use in judicial pro­ceedings, "consists of a ... reference to some national or tribal disaster, and the use of these words is forbidden, because any reference to a disaster in which one of the ancestors was involved grieves their living descendants, who are thus reminded of it." See Warrington's Notes on Ashanti Custom, and for further information.]

ApPEAL from a decision of the Fante Confederacy Native Court " A," Saltpond, on July 30, 1956, giving judgment for the defendant in an action for a declaration of title to land and for damages.

Benjamin for the plaintiff. Sekyi for the defendant. pg 283

     OLLENNU J. This is an appeal from a judgment of the Fante Confederacy Native Court" A," Saltpond, delivered on July 30, 1956. The substance of the claim is for a declaration of the plaintiff's title to two pieces of land called Abiriwakur and Otsiw Edu, and for damages for interfering with the plaintiff in his ownership, possession and occupation of the said lands. The action was instituted as a result of an oath, " Amonoo Kwesida " which the defendant swore against the plaintiff and his family, restraining them from going upon the said lands. At the time of the oath the plaintiff had felled twenty-five palm-trees on the lands.

    On December 15, 1955, the Native Court made an order refusing to appoint a Receiver and Manager to tap the twenty-five palm-trees, and directed that the party who lost the action should pay to the successful party any damage which the neglect of those palm-trees would occasion.

       On February 17, 1956, the co-defendant Kwame Awotwe was joined upon his application.

       At the conclusion of the case the Native Court dismissed the plaintiff's case on two grounds. They are that the defendant as Oman-Kyeame (Town Linguist) was justified in swearing the oath upon the plaintiff's family in order to prevent a breach of the peace between the plaintiff and the defendant's uncle, one Kobina Atta; and that the pieces of land in dispute were part of the subject-matter of an appeal now pending before the West African Court of Appeal, in which the defendant's said uncle is the appellant and the plaintiff is the respon­dent, the latter having won both in the Native Court and in the Land Court, and that since the matter was pending on appeal, the plaintiff had no right to enter upon the land.

        The action of the defendant in swearing the oath is an attempt to circumvent the judgment of the Native Court and of the Land Court subsisting against his family. An appeal does not operate as stay of execution; a successful party is entitled to the occupation and enjoy­ment of land which has been declared his property by a court of competent jurisdiction. It is only by a valid order of the court before which the appeal is pending or of the court from which the appeal is taken that a successful party can, while the appeal is pending, be restrained from the entering upon land ::adjudged to be his. Having found that the two pieces of land are among those declared to be the property of the plaintiff, as against the defendant's family, the Native Court erred in holding that the defendant could by-pass the West African Court of Appeal, before which the appeal is pending, and by oath deprive the plaintiff and his family of the fruits of their judgment. Upon their own findings the Native Court should have held that the judgment of the Native Court, confirmed by the judgment of the Land Court, is of full force and effect, until it is set aside, and that as between the parties herein, the question of ownership is res judicata in favour of the plaintiff. this being the case, the interference by the pg 284 defendant, as also by the co-defendant, upon his own admission, with the plaintiff's possession and occupation is wrongful, and the Native Court should haye entered judgment for the plaintiff. For these reasons, I must allow the appeal, set aside the judgment of the Native Court and substitute therefor judgment in favour of the plaintiff.

On the question of damages, there is no doubt that the action of the defendant is a serious infringement of rights of the plaintiff which, to the knowledge of the defendant, had been determined by the courts. Such flagrant disregard for justice and such deliberate encroachment upon the liberties and rights of fellow citizens, even without proof of special damage, calls for substantial damages. As already pointed out, the Native Court ruled that the unsuccessful party should bear the loss, occasioned by the abandonment of the twenty-five palm-trees felled. The loss of so many palm-trees is heavy damage and the owner should be amply compensated. In all the circumstances I consider that £75 will be a fair sum to award as damages.

The appeal is allowed, the judgment of the Native Court" A " is ~ set aside, including the order as to costs. There will be judgment for the plaintiff for a declaration of title to the two pieces of land claimed

in the writ of summons, and for £75 damages. The plaintiff will have his costs in the Native Court, to be taxed, and his costs in this court.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
