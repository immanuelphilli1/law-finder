Practice-Appeals from Native Courts-Application for extension of time to appeal- Method of making application.

During the course of an action, MAKYO·PLAKGE J., following the rule in Evennett v. Lawrence (1) and Absi v. Mends (2), held that on an application for extension of time to appeal to the Supreme Court from a Native Court, the respondent should be given the opportunity of being heard, so as to give him the opportunity of objecting. The application must therefore be made on notice and not ex parte.

the first defendant applied to the Court and obtained a writ of fi. fa., but he did so without obtaining leave for execution to issue and without obtaining an order of substitution for the deceased Odatsia. In due course four farms, the subject of the present dispute, were sold in execution and were purchased by the second defendant, who subsequently obtained certificates of purchase from the court. Later the procedural fault of the first defendant came to light. Certain applications were then made to the court but these were withdrawn and an arbitration, involving all three parties, was held. The terms of the arbitrators' award were disputed but subsequent to it the first defendant went into possession of the farms. The plaintiff thereupon commenced an action in the Native Court for a declaration of title to the land, claiming that the sale to the second defendant was invalid. The Native Court found for the defendants, principally on the ground that the certificates of purchase had not been set aside. On appeal the defendants admitted that the sale, without leave of the court and without a substitution for the deceased OdatsA, was wrong, but claimed that the wrongful act was a mere irregularity and that as no application had been made to the court within twenty-one days (under the provisions of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Order 51, Rule 18) it was there­after too late to impeach the sale. Alternatively it was argued that the court, in ordering the sale, had acted in excess of jurisdiction, as opposed to acting without jurisdiction, and that therefore the sale was valid until such time as it should be set aside.

Held, (i) the irregularity of which complaint was made was in the events leading up to the sale; it was not an irregularity in the conduct of the sale: therefore the provisions of Order 45, Rules 31 and 21, Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, 2nd schedule (now Supreme Court (Civil Pro­cedure) Rules) 1954, Order 51, Rules 18 and 19) were not applicable.

(ii) The effect of Order 44, Rules 22 and 27 of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, 2nd schedule (which provide, inter alia, that an entitled person may apply for execution against the personal representatives where a judgment-debtor has died before execution) is that execution can only issue against the personal representative or the estate of a deceased judgment-debtor, and then only by leave of the court.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The provisions of Order 44, Rules 22 and 27 of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, 2nd schedule, are now contained in the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Order 42, Rule 22 .. This is in somewhat different language from the earlier Rules but it would appear from its construction and from the remarks of the learned judge in this case that the same interpretation will apply.]

(iii) The court, in issuing execution without leave first being obtained and, without there having been substitution, with the leave of the court, for the deceased judgment-debtor, was acting without jurisdiction. The execution, the sale under it and the certificates of purchase were therefore nullities.

In re Shephard, Atkins v. Shephard «1890) Ch.D. 131) followed.

"27. In the following cases:

(a) Where any change has taken place by death or other­wise in the parties entitled or liable to execution; ...

. the party alleging himself to be entitled to execution may apply to the Court for leave to issue execution accordingly. The Court may, if satisfied that the party so applying is entitled to issue execution, make an order to that effect, or may order that any issue or question necessary to determine the rights of the parties shall be tried in any of the ways in which any question in any action may be tried. I interpret these rules to mean that execution cannot issue against a deceased judgment-debtor; it can only issue against his legal repre­sentative or his estate, and only with leave of the court first had and obtained.

These rules are together similar to Order 42, Rule 23, of the Rules of the Supreme Court in England, which is reproduced in our Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, as Order 42, rule 22, and reads: " In the following cases viz. :-

(a) where six years have elapsed since the judgment or date of the order, or any change has taken place by death or otherwise in the parties entitled or liable to execution;

the party alleging himself to be entitled to execution may apply to the Court or a Judge for leave to issue execution accordingly. And such Court or Judge may, if satisfied that the party so applying is entitled to issue execution, make an order to that effect, or may order that any issue or question necessary to deter­mine the rights of the parties shall be tried in any of the ways in which any question in an action may be tried. And in either case such Court or Judge may impose such terms as to costs or otherwise as shall be just."

In the case of In rc Shephard, Atkins v. Shephard (3), where execution was applied for during the lifetime of judgment debtors, and was adjourned, and actually issued after the death of one of them, and against the estate of the deceased without leave to issue execution, it was held that the issue of execution was without jurisdiction and therefore void. Cotton L.J., in the course of his judgment at p. 135, said:

.. Was there any jurisdiction to make an order affecting the estate formerly belonging to Mark Shephard, when in consequence of his death the estate no longer belonged to him, and when no person interested in it was before the court? Chitty J., though he did not decide the case on this ground, expressed an opinion that there was not .... It is argued that the General Rules of Court, which have the force of an Act of Parliament, enable the court to make the order in the absence of the heir or devisee, and Order 17, Rule 1, is relied on as having that effect. The father and his son Herbert here were both liable for this debt, and judgment was recovered against both. The rule in my opinion keeps the action alive as against the son who is living but not against the deceased father. In the case of devolution by death the action is kept alive by the rule with respect to the property

only where it devolves upon a person who is before the court. The action therefore in the present case is not kept alive as against the heir-at-Iaw, who is not a party to it, and the right to make an order against what was the father's property but is now the property of the heir-at-Iaw, is gone until the heir-at-Iaw is before the court .... It is quite new to me to hear it alleged that there is anything in the Rules to enable the court to make an order against a person who is not a party to the action. It is against all principle to proceed against him until he has been brought before the court, or all proper steps to bring him before the court have been taken ineffectually. I think, therefore, that his receivership order was made per incuriam, the court having no jurisdiction to make an order against the heir in his absence."

Apart from the non-compliance \\'ith the rules, there is another matter which I consider important, It is this: the certificates of purchase declared the second defendant purchaser of the right, title and interest of Chief Kwesi Odatsia of Gomoa Ojobi.
