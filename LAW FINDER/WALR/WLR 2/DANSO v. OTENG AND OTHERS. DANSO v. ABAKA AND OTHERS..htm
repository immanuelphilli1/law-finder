November 24, 1956

                                        DANSO v. OTENG AND OTHERS. DANSO v. ABAKA AND OTHERS.

         West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session  

Coussey P., Benson, van Lare JJ.

 

.

False imprisonment-Arrest by local authority police without warrant­ Complaint by members of local authority council-Arrest and detention by police not bona fide and in pursuance of their duty .

.Malicious prosecution-Conviction quashed on appeal- Whether conviction defeats cause of action.

The plaintiff was a traditional member, appointed by a State Council, of a local authority, and in addition was a cocoa buyer. The defendants in the first suit were the chairman of the local authority, the chairman of its finance committee and two local authority police officers. The defendants in the second suit were local authority police officers. After the introduction of the Local Government Ordinance, c. 64 of 1951, some difficulties were experienced in the interpretation of section 74 of the Ordinance, which requires that the revenues of stool lands should be paid to the local authority within whose area the land lay, and guidance was given by a circular from the Ministry of Local Government declaring that revenue from stool lands, within the meaning of the section, meant any consideration received from transactions in stool land, not revenue which was merely the proceeds of sale of the produce of such land. The local authority concerned, or at least the two defendant members of it, disagreed (or appeared to disagree) with this ruling by the Ministry and demanded that the plaintiff should account to the local authority for the purchase price of cocoa he had purchased from stool land. The plaintiff replied that he had paid this money to the treasury of the appropriate State Council and he produced a receipt to this effect .. Subsequently the first defendant in the first suit laid a complaint with the local authority police to the effect that the plaintiff was suspected of misappropriating the proceeds from stool cocoa farms. As a result of this complaint the plaintiff was arrested, without warrant, by one of the defendant police officers and on each of two successive days was detained for some hours at the local authority police station. He was released on the second day only after paying over the money that had been claimed from him.

     The reaction of the plaintiff to this treatment was to commence an action (the first of the two consolidated suits) for false imprisonment. On the day on which the writ of summons was served on the defendant the plaintiff was again arrested without warrant. He was charged with attempted theft of the money he had, under pressure, eventually paid to the local authority, and was convicted before a Native Court. On appeal to a magistrate's court this conviction was quashed. The second arrest and the subsequent prosecution formed the basis of the second action, which was for false imprisonment and malicious prosecution. In the first action the first two defendants pleaded, inter alia, that they acted bona fide for the purposes of the Local Government Ordinance, c. 64 of 1951, and that they were entitled to the protection afforded by section 136 of that Ordinance. At the trial of the two suits judgment was given for the defendants in both cases. In the second suit this was partly on the grounds that the plaintiff had been convicted initially of the offence with which he stood charged and therefore an action for malicious prosecution could not lie.

     Held, (i) in the first suit, on the facts the first two defendants had merely placed a matter before the police for investigation so that, in the absence of evidence to show that they had influenced the police in the latter's course of action, and regardless of the protection, if any, afforded by the Local Government Ordinance, c. 64 of 1951, s. 136, the first two defendants could not be regarded as party to the false imprisonment.

(ii) As to the remaining defendants in the first suit, they had followed an unlawful course of action in arresting and detaining the plaintiff without a warrant. On the facts these acts were not done in good faith in pursuance of their duty, but were done with the object of obtaining money for the first two defendants. The claim against these defendants was therefore sufficiently established.

(iii) In a charge of malicious prosecution, the essential ingredients of which are malice and the absence of reasonable and probable cause, the effect of a conviction which is quashed on appeal is not to defeat the cause of action but to place a heavier onus on the plaintiff in establishing malice and the absence of reasonable and probable cause. On the facts in the second suit the defendants had acted with malice and without reasonable and probable cause.

Reynolds v. Kennedy ( (1748) 1 Wils.K.B. 232; 95 E.R. 591) doubted. R ernirnan v. Smith ((1938) A.C. 305, H.L.; [1938] 1 All E.R. 1) referred to.

   APPEAL from a decision of Korsah J. on June 15, 1955, in the Divisional Court, Eastern Judicial Division, giving judgment for the defendants in each of two consolidated suits. In the first suit the plaintiff claimed damages for false imprisonment and in the second suit for malicious prosecution and false imprisonment.

      COUSSEY P. These appeals in two actions which were consolidated for hearing arise from the dismissal of the plaintiff's claim in the first action, for damages for wrongful arrest and false imprisonment, and in the second action, for damages for false imprisonment and malicious prosecution.

In the first action, the learned trial judge held that the defendants acted in the honest belief that they were discharging their official duties; that without invoking section 136 of the Local Government Ordinance, c. 64 of 1951, which so far as it is material, provides that no matter or thing done by a member or officer or employee of a council bona fide for the purpose of the Local Government Ordinance shall subject any member, officer or employee thereof to any action, etc., there was satisfactory evidence that the defendants could not be deemed to have unlawfully caused the arrest and detention of the plaintiff.

    In the second action the learned judge apparently held, as to the claim for malicious prosecution, that the action was not maintainable as the trial court had convicted the plaintiff, although the conviction was set aside on appeal:

., because the judgment of the Court shows that in the opinion of a Judicial officer there was foundation for the~ information and prosecution. "

      Reynolds v. Kennedy (1) was cited in support of this view of the law.

     He further held that merely signing the charge sheet was not evidence of causing the plaintiff's imprisonment and that the plaintiff had failed to prove that any of the defendants had unlawfully caused his arrest and detention or had maliciously and without reasonable or probable cause, caused his arrest, prosecution and imprisonment.

   In the first action the facts are as follows. The plaintiff was a traditional member of the Kukurantumi Local Council, i.e., he was appointed to the council by the State Councilor other body established under customary law.

       The first and second defendants were, respectively, Chairman of the Council and Chairman of the Finance Committee of the Council. The third defendant was, at the relevant time, Inspector of Local Authority Police in charge of the district and the fourth defendant, a constable pg170 .

    For some years prior to his arrest the plaintiff, as cocoa buyer, had purchased cocoa from stool cocoa farms in the district, i.e., farms owned by the traditional authority, and had paid the net value of such cocoa to the stool.

      In the year 1951 Local Government Councils were established under Ordinance c. 29 of 1951, now c. 64 of 1951. Some councils interpreted the word" revenues" in section 74 of the Ordinance to apply to the proceeds of a stool farm. '1'0 clear up the misunderstanding a circular was issued by the Ministry of Local Government on the authority of the Minister which, so far as it is relevant, reads as follows:

•. The M caning of the word 'Revenues' in section 74.

4. Certain Councils have interpreted the word . revenue' in section 74 of the Ordinance to denote any monies which are derived from Stool land, for example the normal proceeds of a farm. This is incorrect: the term ' revenue' is intended to refer only to the consideration received by a stool in respect of transactions in land, a fact which is borne out by the provisions of section 74 (5), added to the Ordinance by Ordinance No. 11 of 1952."

   The events of July 30 and 31, 1953, are important. On July 30, the Akyem Abuakwa State Council wrote to the plaintiff, and copied to all Local Councils in Akyem Abuakwa (Kukurantumi Local Council is in Akyem Abuakwa), setting out a written reply given some time between July 16 and 30 by the Minister of Local Government that,

"Local Councils have been advised by me that the normal proceeds of Stool farms do not come within the meaning of the word' revenues' in section 74 of the Ordinance."

     On the same day, the Kukurantumi Local Council wrote to the plaintiff, requesting him to pay within three days all moneys received in respect of the proceeds of some stool farms. It is evident that the local council differed from the Minister in his interpretation of section 74 of the Ordinance .

'  To this letter the plaintiff replied on the following day, quoting his receipt of July 30, 1953, that he had paid the proceeds received to date to the Akyem Abuakwa Stflte Council Treflsury. There was a lull after this until August 17 when the Clerk of the Council wrote to the plaintiff at the direction of the second defendant, as Chairman of the Finance Committee, saying that the plaintiff had no right to pay the proceeds into the State Council's Treasury, pointing out that he had acted contrary to section 72 (1) of the Ordinance and requesting him within three days to pay to the Local Council's Treasurer, all moneys paid into the State Council's Treasury.

The police diary produced in evidence shows that on the afternoon of August 24, 1953, the first defendant, Oteng, complained to the police that plaintiff was suspected to have misappropriated the proceeds of stool cocoa farms. He gave the particulars of the receipt which the  pg171plaintiff had himself given, namely, Akyem Abuakwa State Council receipt No. 852245 for £2 8s. ad.

     On the following day, August 25, the fourth defendant brought in the plaintiff in custody. 'l'he receipt was produced. 'l'he plaintiff was detained without warrant at the police station from 12.10 p.m. to 7.50 p.m. on the 25th when he was released to report himself on the following morning, which he did.

Meanwhile on August 26 Inspector Abaka, under whose orders Sergeant Akuoko had acted, returned with the statement of the first defendant, Oteng. That statement is set out in the judgment appealed from. It is sufficient to say that in it there is nothing remotely suggestive of a criminal offence.

The dispute as to revenue of stool farms was of a purely civil character as the first and second defendants, Oteng and Duro, were perfectly aware, with the knowledge they must have had of the Minister's interpretation of the meaning of "revenue." It was an interpretation which, perhaps, they did not accept, but which the plaintiff was entitled to act upon in the face of a request from the State Council to pay the proceeds of the farms into their Treasury and, having regard to his practice over a period prior to July, 1953, to pay the proceeds into the Stool Treasury. Moreover, the first and second defendants were aware, on July 31, that the plaintiff had paid the proceeds in question to the State Council. There was no reasonable ground to suspect that the plaintiff had dishonestly appropriated the money or had attempted to do so. Their proper course was to apply to the State Council's Treasury for the money, if they claimed it. That would, no doubt, have resulted in controversy and probably litigation between Local Council and State Council. The easier course, these defendants considered, was to report to the police. What followed is not surprising. In the afternoon of August 26, Inspector Abaka, according to the police record, instructed that

"the suspect, the plaintiff Twum Danso, should pay back the amount of £2 8s. ad. collected and obtain a receipt from the local Treasury. After this had been done the suspect should be released to go with his documents and will be called upon when wanted by the police."

Having paid the further sum of £2 8s. ad. to the Kukurantumi Local Council, the appellant was released from detention at 4.10 p.m.

The first and second defendants plead

" that upon due investigation they were confirmed in their view that there was reasonable cause to suspect a misappropriation (not a dishonest appropriation) that the plaintiff, although afforded sufficient opportunity to dispel the suspicion of a misappropriation, did not do so, wherefore in good faith they reported to the police."

I have no doubt, on the evidence, that these defendants were not acting in good faith. Oteng says he was aware of the letters already pg172 referred to. He knew the plaintiff had paid the money to the State Council

.• but that was not the body to which the money should be paid. 1£ a person is required to pay money to a certain officer and he deliberately pays it to a wrong person it means he wishes to steal the money."

    The bald fact is that criminal process by arrest and detention was unlawfully exerted to extract a civil debt from the plaintiff.

    The first and second defendants are entitled to say, as they do say, that they only placed the matter before the police for investigation. They shield themselves behind the police. There is no conclusive evidence to show that they so influenced the third defendant, Inspector Abaka, that he acted under their direction or authorization and there­fore the first and second defendants cannot be held responsible for the arrest and detention on August 25 and 26. It follows that the appeal so far as affects them must be dismissed. The case against the third and fourth defendants does not admit of any doubt. This was an unlawful arrest. The third defendant seeks to justify it by saying, not that he was unaware of the claim of the State Council but that he did not believe the receipt produced by the plaintiff to be genuine; it was not the form of receipt he expected from the State Council, he says and, further, he believed the receipt was issued by arrangement. But he had only to telephone the State Council Treasury at Kibi as a first step in his enquiries to find out if the money had been paid and the receipt genuine. He did not do so. 1£ he had honestly investigated the matter even cursorily he would have concluded that no criminal offence had been committed. The third defendant did not act in good faith. He did not reasonably believe that the plaintiff had attempted to commit a felony. He caused the plaintiff's arrest to obtain the money for the first and second defendants. Statutory protection does not cover his act nor that of the fourth defendant, who acted under his orders and made the initial arrest without warrant. As to the third and fourth defendants the appeal is allowed, the judgment of the court below is set aside and judgment is entered for the plaintiff. The parties have not asked for a reference as to damages and in the circumstances we are prepared to assess the damages ourselves. \Ye assess the damages in the first suit at £50 against the third and fourth defendants jointly and severally.

In the second suit the plaintiff alleged a second false imprisonment and malicious prosecution against Inspector A.baka, Sergeant Akuoko and Constable Peasah in the following circumstances. The plaintiff issued the writ of summons in the first suit at Accra on September 8, 1953. Service was effected on the defendants in that suit on September 21 and 23. It will be recalled that on August 26, the plaintiff, having been arrested without warrant, was released after he had paid the £2 8s. 0d. to the Local Council Treasury. He was not taken before a  pg173 court within forty-eight hours of arrest as the law enjoins but, accord­ing to the police diary, was told he would be called upon when wanted by the police. On September 21, the plaintiff was arrested without warrant by the first defendant Inspector Abaka and put in a cell at Kukurantumi Police Station for the night and charged by the second defendant Sergeant Akuoko on the instruction of the first defendant with attempted stealing contra sections 44 (1) and 100 of the Criminal Code. On this charge the plaintiff was prosecuted in the Kukurantumi Native Court" B " on September 22, 25 and 29, 1953, when he was found guilty of the charge and sentenced by the court to one month's imprisonment with hard labour. On appeal to the Magistrate's Court, the conviction was quashed on December 28, 1953.

I think the principle in Reynolds v. Kennedy (1) supra, was over­simplified by the learned trial judge when he held that a claim for malicious prosecution is not maintainable once an appellant has been convicted in the trial court. The correct rule is that if an appellant is convicted in the first court and acquitted on appeal, a heavier onus is cast on him to prove the existence of malice and want of reasonable and probable cause. In H erniman v. Smith (2) where the conviction was quashed on appeal, the House of Lords observed in its judgment that in these circumstances it was not and could not be disputed that the proceedings had terminated in favour of the plaintiff so as to satisfy that essential element in an action for malicious prosecution. In this case the first and second defendants were aware that there was no criminal element in the plaintiff's conduct; they were aware that on August 26 at the request of the first defendant, the plaintiff had paid £2 8s. ad. to the Kukurantumi Local Council although he maintained that he had already lawfully paid that amount to the Akyem Abuakwa State Council and that the plaintiff had thereupon been released from custody.

The first defendant, prosecuting the charge before the Native Court, described the plaintiff's conduct as foul play in keeping the balance of £2 8s. ad. himself, when he was aware that the money, with no benefit to the plaintiff, had been paid by the plaintiff first to the Akyem Abuakwa State Council on July 3'0 and again under the pressure Of imprisonment to the Kukurantumi Local Council.

This court is in as good a position as the trial judge where the facts are fully recorded to determine if they constitute reasonable and probable cause-Lister v. Perryman (3). If the circumstances show that the prosecutor believed that the person whom he proceeded against as a thief took the goods under an erroneous notion that he had a lien upon them, or had a right to take and detain them, there is an absence of reasonable and probable cause. It is often a doubtful question whether a particular offence amounts to a felony; and it often depends upon the fact of the prisoner having acted with conscious dishonesty, or under a notion of right on his part. pg174

.• All the world knows there is a wide distinction between stealing and the assertion of a right; and that even supposing a man is wrong in that assertion, it is not a felony for which he can be indicted." Per Watson B. in Huntley v. Simson (4).

I have no hesitation in finding that the first and second defendants, to use the language of Hawkins J. in Hicks v. Faulkner (5), had no honest belief in the' guilt of the accused. The facts would not lead an ordinary prudent and cautious man to conclude that the plaintiff was probably guilty of attempted stealing.

 These defendants were actuated by an improper and indirect motive for otherwise, the raking up of an alleged charge which had been closed by the payment of the money involved in a civil matter, is inexplicable. They arrested and charged the plaintiff and testified against him at his tri al.

   In the case of Constable Peasah, the third defendant, it seems that he only invited the plaintiff to the police station when the first defendant, Abaka, effected his arrest. The claim is therefore not well founded against him and so far as affects him the appeal is dismissed.

     Having found that the first and second defendants acted maliciously, i.e., from an indirect or improper motive, they do not enjoy any statutory protection for their acts. The parties have not asked for a reference as to damages and, in the circumstances we are prepared to assess the damages.

     As to the first and second defendants the appeal is allowed, the judgment of the court below is set aside and judgment is entered for the plaintiff. we assess the damages in the second suit at £300 against the first and second defendants jointly and severally.

The plaintiff will have the costs of the consolidated suits, in the court below to be taxed, and in this court against the third and fourth defendants ill the first suit and against the first and second defendants in the second suit jointly and severally. For making an unfounded complaint against the appellant the first and second defendants in the first suit are deprived of costs in this court and in the court below.

In the second suit the defendant Peasah will have costs in this court and in the court below to be taxed.
