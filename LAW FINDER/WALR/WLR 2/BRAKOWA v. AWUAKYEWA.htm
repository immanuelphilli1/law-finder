November 13, 1956..

BRAKOWA v. AWUAKYEWA

West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session  

                                                               Coussey P., Korsah C.J., van Lare J.

 

 

Practice-Service-Effect of failure to serve notice of process on defendant - Native Court (Colony) Procedure Regulations, No. 10 of 1945, Regulation 49.

      Practice-Service-Service put in issue in subsequent suit-Proof of service. Practice-Parties-Application to Native Court for joinder of a defendant Principles to be followed in exercise of discretion by Native Court.

The plaintiff sought a declaration of title to land. The defendant, in an earlier action, had obtained judgment against a debtor to whom the plaintiff was a guarantor. Following upon this judgment the defen­dant had proceeded to execution whereby land, the property of the plaintiff, had been sold under an order of the court. The plaintiff, who was cited as a defendant in the earlier case, did not appear, and now contended that the writ of summons had never been served upon him. The defendant, in the present case, did not call the person alleged to have served the writ to establish that it was in fact served, nor was there sufficient certificate of service as required by the Native Courts (Colony) Procedure Regulations, No. 10 of 1945, Regulation 48.

The Native trial Court refused an application by the plaintiff to have the purchaser of the property joined as a defendant.

Held, (i) it was neither proved nor admitted that process had been served upon the plaintiff, and therefore the proceedings before the Native trial Court and subsequently were a nullity: Native Court (Colony) Procedure Regulations, No. 10 of 1945, Regulation 49.

(ii) Although the Native Court (Colony) Procedure Regulations, No. 10 of 1945, permit service to be proved by evidence on oath or by a certificate of service on the original document signed by the person affecting service, if service is put in issue in a subsequent suit, it should be established by adducing the best evidence available and not merely by producing a signed certificate of service.

(iii) As it is one of the objects of every court to avoid multiplicity of suits, it was an irregular exercise of the discretion vested in the Native trial Court to reject the application for the joining of the purchaser as a defendant.

Short for the plaintiff.

    VAN LARE J. This is an appeal by special leave from the judgment of the Land Court, Cape Coast, dismissing an appeal by the plaintiff from a decision of the North Birim Native Court" A."

The case arose out of a civil suit instituted by the defendant against one Kwame Noah, as principal debtor, and the plaintiff in the present suit as a surety, in which the claim against them jointly and severally was for a sum of money due and owing. The defendant, who was plaintiff in that suit, obtained a judgment and proceeded to execution whereby a cocoa farm, the property of the plaintiff in the present suit, was sold under a process of fl. fa. and bought by one Kwabena Gyasi. There is no dispute that the plaintiff, who was the second defendant in the case, never appeared at any stage of the proceedings. She now alleges that the writ of summons in the case had not been served on her and maintains therefore that the judgment recovered against her was a nullity, and that being so the sale of her cocoa farm in execution of a null decree against her cannot validly affect her interest in her said property. Her action, commenced in the same Native Court, is as follows:

" The plaintiff claims from the defendant a declaration of title and recovery of possession of all that piece of cocoa farm situate, lying, and being at . Pantimpa ' which bounds with the properties of Kwame Gyan on one side, on one side with Kwame Kumah's cocoa farm, on one side with Madam Fosuaah's cocoa farm and on the other side by Kwaku Sraha's cocoa farm, the property" ot the plaintiff, in that there is no existing judgment against her, which will necessitate the said cocoa farm described above being sold under the process of a writ of fl. fa."

The issue which the Native Court tried in this case is whether the writ of summons in which the present plaintiff was sued before the court was in fact served on her. It is a settled rule of law and practice that no court of law can give judgment against a party in a suit in which a copy of the writ of summons has not been served on the party concerned. Regulation 49 of the Native Court (Colony) Procedure Regulations, No. 10 of 1945 as amended by the Native Court (Colony) Procedure (Amendment) Regulations, No.8 of 1950, made under the Native Courts Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, puts it as follows:

., The Native Court shall not take any action which is depen­dent upon process having been served unless service is admitted by the person concerned or service has been proved. "

Apart from the denial by the plaintiff of service of the writ of summons on her there is other evidence, such as her absence in Kumasi and thereby from jurisdiction at the material time, in support of her contention that she had not been served. It therefore becomes pg166 incumbent on the defendant to establish satisfactorily that service of a copy of the writ of summons had been effected on the plaintiff before the case proceeded against her.

   1)roof of service is provided for by regulation 48 of the above-named Regulations as follows:

" Service effected by a bailiff or native authority policeman may be proved by evidence on oath or by a certificate of service on the original document signed by the person effecting service. Service effected by any other person shall be proved by evidence on oath."

   I am of the opinion that where proof of service is in issue in another suit it is necessary to produce the best evidence, and that is to call the person entrusted with service to give evidence on oath and to be cross-examined, unless such person is not available or cannot be pro­cured. This will afford an opportunity for the trial court to determine the authenticity or otherwise of any certificate of service that might have been endorsed on the original document.

   In answer to the case made by the plaintiff in this case the defendant did not call the bailiff who purported to have effected service in the suit. Furthermore there is no satisfactory certificate of service on the person of the plaintiff, as is required by Regulation 45 of the Regulations above-named, to be found on the original document. The only indication approximating to such a certificate" is an entry at the foot of the original document as follows:

" Served on these people of Oda on 22/12/55." "(Sgd.) ? ? ?"

   In fact there is to be found nowhere on the original document the usual form of certificate of service.

A close study of the original document discloses that the plaintiff was not even cited for service and it must therefore be presumed that no copy of the writ of summons was ever addressed to her for service. The Native Trial Court appears to have accepted hearsay evidence from a witness, Benign, who was the recorder of the proceedings in the case to the effect that the bailiff who had been entrusted with service had reported service to him. to reason appears why the bailiff himself did not give evidence.

I am of the opinion therefore that the evidence as to service of the writ of summons in the case against the plaintiff is unsatisfactory and that the Native Court misdirected itself as to proof of service. Under the circumstances the Native Court's finding of fact upon a vital issue that the writ of summons "was really served upon the plaintiff by the court bailiff" is not supported by the evidence and must be set aside.

There is one other point which must be cleared up. An application to join the purchaser of the property, the subject matter of the suit, was refused by the Native Trial Court and the learned judge of the   pg167

Land Court, Cape Coast, was of the opinion that this was matter of a discretion properly exercised. With respect I am not in agreement with this view. As the property now appears to be vested, by reason of his purchase, in the person sought to be joined, it would be right and proper and to avoid multiplicity of suits to join the purchaser, in view of the fact that any judgment obtained in the suit would affect his interest in the property.

For these reasons I would allow the appeal by setting aside both the judgment appealed from and of the Native Trial Court and remit the suit to the said Native Court for a rehearing.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

KORSAH C.J. I concur.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
