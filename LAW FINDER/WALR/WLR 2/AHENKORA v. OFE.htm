                    February 7, 1957                                 

                                      AHENKORA v. OFE.

       Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Accra

                                                                              Windsor-Aubrey J.

 

 

Certiorari-State Councils-Powers 01 Committee 01 Enquiry appointed by Governor-State Councils (Colony and Southern 'Togoland) Ordinance, c. 8 0/1952, s. 8 (1).

The Governor, acting under the powers conferred upon him by the State Councils (Colony and Southern Togoland) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, pg 234 s. 8 (1), appointed a committee of enquiry to enquire into a matter of a constitutional nature. The terms of the order appointing the com­mittee were obscure, but it appeared they required the committee to investigate charges preferred against the applicant which, if proved, might have justified his destoolment. The committee, in its findings, went beyond its terms of reference and recommended the re-enstoolment of the applicant's destooled predecessor as stool holder. This finding the Governor purported to confirm. In an application for an order of certiorari to quash the findings of the committee and their purported confirmation it was argued, against the application, that section 8 (1) of the Ordinance conferred upon the Governor the power to make any order that could be made by a state council under section 5 of the Ordinance.

Held, (i) the powers conferred upon the Governor by the State Councils (Colony and Southern Togoland) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 8 (1), are limited to intervention in a pending dispute on the occasions set out in the section. They confer no power to intervene where a state council has already been seised of a dispute and has given its decision upon it. Quansah and Another Y. Tagoe and Others (1956) 2 W.A.L.R. 90 followed.

(ii) In any event a committee of enquiry appointed under section 8 (1) of the Ordinance is bound by its terms of reference. In this case the terms of reference gave no authority to investigate and make a finding on the re-enstoolment of a des tooled chief and therefore the committee in making such a finding, and the Governor in confirming this finding, were acting ultra vires.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. In Eliza Y. Tagoe and Others, Divisional Court, Sekondi, May 1, 1957, unreported, a case in which the facts were similar to those in Q uansah and Another Y. Tagoe and Others (1) and in the present case, Acolatse J. was of opinion that certiorari would not lie against a committee of enquiry acting under section 8 (1) of the Ordinance or against a State Council exercising its powers under section 5 of the Ordinance, although prohibition might lie. The learned judge quoted Halsbury, Simonds ed., Vol. 11, p. 54, para. 112, and Vol. 11, p. 136, para. 254.

The general subject of the .use of certiorari against inferior tribunals was considered in R. v. St. Edmundsbury and Ipswich Diocese, ex parte White [1947] 2 All E.R. 170, C.A., a case concerned with ecclesiastical courts. Here it was held that prohibition would lie but certiorari would not. The funda­mental reason for holding certiorari inapplicable appeared to be that it never, throughout the many centuries of history of ecclesiastical courts, had lain against such courts. In the judgments in the Court of Appeal the nature of an inferior court and the writs that would issue against it were considered in some detail, and there was discussion as to whether certiorari would lie against a court administering a system of law foreign to the Queen's Bench Division and which the latter court would not be competent to apply if the issue were removed into it. Lord Evershed (then Evershed L.J.) said:

"I do not desire to express any concluded view on the question whether an order for certiorari to quash could lie in any case in which for any reason the King's Bench Court might itself be incompetent to try the issue between the contestants. Mr. Stranders relied on the Irish cases of In re Heaphy and Lalor v. Bland for the proposition that the pg 235 Court of King's Bench will never (allow a nullity to remain on the record,' whatever be the power or competence of the King's Bench Court as regards the question in issue. N or do I forget the general language of Holt C.J. in Viner's Abridgment, vol. IV, p. 336, that there is no court or jurisdiction which can withstand a certiorari. 'l'here is, how­ever, to my mind, a difference in principle between courts having exempt jurisdiction in the sense that by some statute exclusive jurisdiction quoad subject-matter is conferred on them (e.g., the recently established Rent Tribunal for accessing the rent of furnished lettings), and courts adminis­tering a system of law wholly distinct, both in substance and in matters of procedure, from the system of law administered in the King's Temporal courts .

There is no doubt that State Councils and Committees of Enquiry are "inferior courts" in the sense used by Wrottesley L.J. in the same judg­ment, in that they are, in certain circumstances, amenable to the supervisory

. jurisdiction of the Supreme Court exercised by means of the prerogative writs, in particular by prohibition. Thus, prohibition will issue against a State Council that assumes jurisdiction in something other than a constitu­tional matter: see Yirenkyi and Others v. State Council of Akwapem (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 237. If then the test suggested by Lord Evershed is the correct one for determining whether, of the prerogative orders, certiorari will lie, then it would appear that the jurisdiction of State Councils and of Com­mittees of Enquiry appointed under the Ordinance comes in the first category. State Councils are themselves a creation of statute and exercise only powers conferred upon them by statute. The law they administer is a part of the Ghana common law which would, were it not for the provisions of the Ordinance, be administered by the Supreme Court. It would appear, there­fore, that certiorari will lie, as it does in England against rent tribunals, where there has been an excess of jurisdiction or where there has been a breach of the rules of natural justice in the hearing and determination of the matter. On the question of excess of jurisdiction the position has been well stated by Devlin J. in R. v. Fulham, Hammersmith and Kensington Rent Tribunal, ex parte Zurek [1951] 1 All E.R. 482 at p. 488:

"When, at the inception of an inquiry by a tribunal of limited jurisdiction, a challenge is made to its jurisdiction, the tribunal has to make up its mind whether it will act or not. If its jurisdiction depends on the existence of a state of facts, it .must inform itself about them, and, if the facts are in dispute, reach some conclusion on the merits of the dispute. If it reaches a wrong conclusion, the rights of the parties against each other are not affected. For, if a tribunal wrongly assumes jurisdiction, the party who apparently obtains an order from it in reality takes nothing. The whole proceeding is, in the phrase used in the old reports, coram non judice."]

(1) Quansah and Another v. Tagoe and Others (1956) 2 W.A.L.R. 90.

APPLICATION for an order of certiorari directed to the Governor of the Gold Coast in respect of an order made by him on March 24, 1956, confirming the findings of a committee of enquiry appointed by him to enquire into a matter of a constitutional nature relating to the Stool of Adowsena. pg 236

       E. O. Asafu-Adjaye for the applicants.

    de Graft Johnson for the respondent.

Unger, Crown Counscl, for the Minister of Local Government.

WINDSOR-AURUEY J. I do not think this application for an order of certiorari presents any difficulties. By an order dated March 24, 1956, the Governor appointed a committee of enquiry to enquire into certain disputes. the order is most obscure but considered in the light of a letter dated December 12, 1\)55, from the Government Agent, Oda Birim, it would seem probable that the terms of reference are intended to mean that the committee was to enquire into certain charges preferred against the applicant which might, if proved, justify his destoolment. All counsel seem to agree that this was the purpose of the enquiry.

Crown counsel, representing the Minister of Local Government, argues that if the committee decided the charges were proved and that the applicant ought to be destooled it must inevitably conclude that the former stool-holder should be restooled. His point, as I under­stand it, is based on an alleged roving commission and an unbridled licence on the part of the Governor to make any decision under section 8 of the State Councils (Colony and Southern Togoland) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, which a State Council can make under section 5. This argument is, in my opinion, wholly untenable. A committee can only be appointed in respect of a pending matter, and cannot reverse a decision already taken by a State Council. Further­more, the committee is bound by its terms of reference and what-ever they may mean, I cannot read in them any direction to recommend the restoolment of the former holder which is what the committee recommended and what the Governor confirmed.

Counsel for the respondent has argued that as any order by the Governor under section 8 is declared to be final and conclusive, this court has no jurisdiction to enquire into this matter. So long as an order is lawfully made within the scope of the Governor's powers under section 8 the court certainly cannot intervene, but where the whole of the order is totally illegal that is precisely where the court can and should. This is the whole essence of certiorari-a power conferred on the court to quash illegal proceedings.

I have considered the powers of the court in the case of Quansah and Another v. Tagoe (1), which I decided in the Sekondi Divisional Court on August 28, 1956, and although that case is not wholly in point no arguments have been now put before me which lead me to conclude I was wrong on the general principles I therein enunciated.

The committee having recommended the restoolment of a destooled chief and the Governor having confirmed that recommendation, both have in my opinion acted without jurisdiction. In my opinion, therefore, the application is well founded and an order of certiorari pg 237 is granted requiring the respondent to surrender to this court the proceedings of the committee of enquiry and the Governor's confirma­tion thereof for the purpose of both being quashed and they are hereby quashed, a formal order to be drawn up. The applicant's counsel is awarded costs, to be paid by the Minister of Local Government and the respondents in equal shares.

Order granted.

S. G. D.
