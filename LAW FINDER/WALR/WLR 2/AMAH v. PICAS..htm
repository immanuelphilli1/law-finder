AMAH v. PICAS. 

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra

                                                                                      Wilson C . .J.

 

 

      Landlord and Tenant-Rent control-Standard rent-Contractual tenancy at 1'ent lower than standard Tent-Right of landlord to increase Tent to amount of standard 'rent-Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, .Is. 5 (3) (a) and 7 (1).

The plaintiff had let premises under a contractual tenancy for fifteen years to the defendant at a monthly rent of £20. Before the end of the tenancy period the plaintiff applied to the local Rent Assessment Com­mittee for a standard rent to be assessed for the premises. The Rent Assessment Committee assessed the standard rent at £45 a month. The plaintiff subsequently brought this action to recover from the defendant the difference between the contractual and standard rents as from the date that the standard rent was fixed.

Held, a standard rent, fixed by a Rent Assessment Committee under the provisions of the Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952 (which Ordinance exists for the protection of landlords and tenants alike), is recoverable by a landlord from his tenant, notwithstanding the currency of a lease between them which reserves a contractual rent that is lower than the standard rent, In this respect the Rent Control Ordinance differs from the English Rent Restrictions Acts.

WILSON C.J. The amended claim in this case was for £405, being the balance of the amount stated to be due by the defendant to the plaintiff as the rent of certain premises at No. D514j3, Derby Avenue, Accra, up to the end of April, 1954. No evidence was taken at the trial as the facts of the case were not in dispute and the trial resolved itself into a legal argument on a single issue. The defendant became the plaintiff's tenant of the premises under a lease dated October 27, 1944, for the term of fifteen years at an annual rental of £240 Os. 0d. In 1952 the plaintiff brought the defendant before the Accra Rent Assessment Committee on an application under section 7 (1) of the Rent Control Ordinance for reassessment of the rent and an order was made increasing the monthly rent from £20 per mensem to £45 per mensem as from October 1, 1952. The defendant did not appeal and paid the increased rent for about a year and then wrote denying his liability to pay the increased rent and alleging that he had made the increased payment since October, 1952, under mistake, presumably as to his legal rights. Subsequently the plaintiff brought this action.

The defendant does not deny that the Rent Assessment Committee had power to vary the rent of premises, held under an existing lease, by virtue of the powers given to the committee under section 5 (3) (a) of the Rent Control Ordinance, on an application under section 7 (1). But he says that the varying of the standard rent by increasing it did not make it obligatory on the tenant to pay the increased figure, since he held the premises under a lease and could rely upon the terms of the lease as regards the rent payable and refuse to pay anything more than he had contracted for in the lease. In other words he contends that the decision of the committee on the plaintiff's application to increase the rent was a purely academic proceeding which could have no effect until after the date of the expiry of the lease.

In support of his claim plaintiff's counsel cited the decision of the West African Court of Appeal in Amarteifio v. Noshie (1). In that case the Appeal Court upheld a decision of the Divisional Court.

" ... that having regard to the wide scope of the regulations as indicated by the definition of ' standard rent' in Regulation 3 of the Bent Restriction Regulations, 1943, and the plain and unambiguous language of Regulation 7 (1) the said Committee had jurisdiction to entertain the plaintiff's above-mentioned application, notwithstanding that a lease between the parties was in existence in respect of the said premises .... "

 

The defendant's counsel did not seek to question the application of this decision in the present case but argued that although the Committee, in the words of the judgment, had jurisdiction in the present case to decide as it did, that decision was not enforceable by the landlord, since the Ordinance does not lay it down that the increased rent shall be collectible when there is an existing lease in which the parties have agreed on a lower rent figure.

The authorities he cited for this proposition were (i) the case of Field Y. Gover (2), at p. 205, to show that the standard rent of premises is a maximum figure, an upward limit, but not necessarily the figure of the actual rent payable; (ii) the case of Smith v. Lloyd (3), at p. 878, and (iii) Newell v. Crayford Cottage Society (4). These two latter cases were apparently cited to support the submission that while the tenancy under the lease in this case continued to subsist the landlord could not demand an increased rent. But these two cases were concerned with the interpretation and application of section 3 (1) of the Increase of Rent and Mortgage Interest (Restrictions) Act, 1920, an entirely different piece of legislation based on different principles and employing different methods of procedure from those with which we are concerned in the Rent Control Ordinance, 1952. These cases have no application whatsoever in my opinion to the case now under consideration.

I am not attracted by the argument that the provisions of the Rent Control Ordinance, 1952, relating to the fixing or varying of standard rent are for the benefit of the tenant only. Section 7 (1) says quite clearly that

"Any landlord or tenant ... may make application to the appropriate Committee for the assessment of a standard rent, or the variation of a standard rent, for the premises of which he is the landlord or tenant .... "

It does not confine this right of application to statutory tenants under section 16 of the Ordinance or deny it to landlords under an existing lease. Under section 2 of the Ordinance its provisions are clearly applicable to a lease of the kind here in operation, as is also shown by the definition of " landlord" and" tenant" in section 3' and section 5 (3) (a) lays down in the clearest terms that the reasonable rent fixed by the Committee in accordance with its permissions

"shall be the standard rent of such premises notwithstanding any lease to the contrary."

There is nothing whatever to suggest that this is a one-way control, favouring and protecting only the tenant. The argument that the Ordinance allows a landlord under a subsisting lease the right to get a standard rent fixed, but does not allow him to demand the payment of anything more than the contractual figure in the lease seems to fly in the face of the whole tenor of the Ordinance and is one which on first principles I find myself entirely unable to accept and in the absence of any clear authority for that submission I must reject it.

I find in favour of the plaintiff in terms of the amended claim and enter judgment for her accordingly for £405 0s. 0d. with costs.

Judgment for the plaintiff.

S. G. D.
