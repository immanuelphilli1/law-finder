                          West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

Native Courts - Fraud - Jurisdiction of Native Court to entertain action alleging its judgment to have been obtained by fraud-Alleged conspiracy between Registrar of Native Court and a party-Judgment as written down and signed not in accordance with judgment as delivered orally in Native Court.

Native Courts - Practice - Order not 1'Ccorded in Record book at t-i1ne of delivery or in open court -Nat'i've Courts (Colony) Procedure Regulations, No. 10 of 1945, Part 7, Reg. 41.

Judgment was given by a Native Appeal Court, in a land dispute, in favour of the plaintiff, The order of the Native Appeal Court was not entered in the record book at the time, but several days later the Registrar of the Native Court summoned two of the panel members to the house of the President of the court with the information that the order would then be committed to writing. The two panel members discovered that the unsuccessful defendant was also present, protested against the meeting being held, and left the house. The order of the Native Appeal Court, or what purported to be the order, was then entered into the record book and signed by the President (who was blind and very aged). When the plaintiff subsequently obtained a copy of the judgment as so written, he found it differed materially, to the advantage of the defendant, from the oral judgment that had been delivered in open court. He then commenced this action, in the Native Court, .for the written judgment to be set aside on the grounds that it had been obtained by fraud and by conspiracy between the Registrar and the defendant. The Native Court found for the plaintiff and corrected the fraudulent judgment by deleting from it the passages that had been improperly introduced, On appeal it was argued for the defendant that the jurisdiction of Native Courts is limited by Ordinance and that pg 220 neither Ordinance nor native custom gives jurisdiction to a Native Court to set aside one of its own judgments on the ground that it has been obtained by fraud and conspiracy.

Held, (i) the power of a Native Court to set aside its own judgments that have been obtained by fraud is inherent in it, by its nature as a court.

Oannell v. Sewell (1858) 157 E.R. 615 referred to.

(ii) It is implied in the Native Courts (Colony) Procedure Regula­tions, No. 10 of 1945, reg. 41, that the order of the court is to be entered in the record book by the Registrar and signed by the President, forthwith upon the order being delivered, and in open court.

 

[EDITOIUAL NOTE. The judgment of the court below is reported in this volume as Gbadago v. Tsili (1955) 2 W.A.L.R. 11, and was given in favour of the plaintiff on the basis that the matter appearing in the record book was not in fact the judgment of the Native Court; that there had been a breach of the Native Courts (Colony) Procedure Regulations, 1945, by the Registrar of the Native Court j and that the plaintiff was entitled to an order from the Native Court correcting the record. APPEAL from the decision on December 31, 1955, of van Lare J., in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, dismissing the appeal by the defendant from the decision on January 24, 1950, of the Native Court" A " of Anlo giving judgment for the plaintiff in an action for the setting aside of part of an earlier judgment of the Native Court on the ground that it had been obtained by fraud.

Akufo-Addo for the plaintiff.

E. O. Asafu-Adfaye for the defendant.

KORSAH C.J. This is an appeal from the judgment of the Land Court, confirming a judgment of the Native Court •. A " of Anlo State in a suit in which the claim reads thus:

" The plaintiff claims that part of the judgment of the Keta Native Appeal Court in case Attiavishe and Others v. Chief Tsili II and Others dated the 24/1/50 be set aside on grounds of fraud and conspiracy by first defendant and the Registrar Native Appeal Court (Mr. Fred Acolatse).

" The part referred to reads as follows:

" , We hereby give to Chief Tsili II and his tribe their ancient right of hewing down palm-trees on the Atiavi land and described and bounded in the writ of summons, that Chief Tsili II and his pg 221tribe have the right to take steps against any' farmer who uproots or damages any of the palm-trees growing on these lands

. " 'The said fraud consists in the first defendant and the Registrar of the Native Appeal Court aforesaid conspiring together and inserting the said passage in the judgment, when the judgment delivered by the said Court did not contain that passage.' "

The facts briefly stated are that the plaintiff had obtained judgment against the defendant in a previous suit, with respect to a piece or parcel of land, heard originally in the Anyako Native Court, and that the parties in that suit were in the same relative positions as they are in this case, which has given rise to this appeal. The Anyako Native Court having given judgment in favour of the plaintiff, the defendant appealed to the Native Appeal Court" A " of Anlo State, which confirmed the judgment and dismissed the appeal.

In accordance with the provisions of the Native Courts (Colony) Procedure Regulations, No. 10 of 1945, the Native Appeal Court" A " delivered an oral judgment on January 24, 1950, which was to be recorded immediately by the Hegistrar. The appropriate regulation, No. 71, reads thus:

" The order of a Native Court shall be delivered in open court by the President, and shall be written in precise terms in the Record Book by the Registrar and signed by the President."

After the judgment had been delivered, the panel members di~persed. Several days later the Registrar informed two of them that they were wanted by Awoame Fia Togbi Sri II, who was the President of the court at the hearing of the appeal. When they arrived at the Fia's house they saw Chief Tsili II, the unsuccessful defendant, together with the Registrar and Awoame Fia Togbi Sri II. The Registrar informed them he was then going to reduce the oral judgment they had delivered into writing. The two men protested against the presence of Chief Tsili II, refused to take part in the meeting, and left the house. Subsequently, when the plaintiff obtained a copy of the judgment, he observed that it contained passages which did not form part of the judgment delivered on January 24, 1950, in respect of which the writ in this suit has been issued.

The plaintiff complained about the matter in a letter dated February 4, 1950, addressed to the Assistant District Commissioner, Keta, and lodged an appeal to the Land Court in which he alleged, as a ground, the inclusion in the said judgment of the portion com­plained of. He later abandoned the appeal, and instituted this action in the Native Court" A," claiming that part of the judgment of the Native Appeal Court be set aside on grounds of fraud and conspiracy by Chief Tsili II and the Registrar of the Native Appeal Court. At the conclusion of the trial the Native Court "A" of Anlo delivered pg 222 judgment in favour of the plaintiff in which, after discussing the evidence, they stated inter alia thus:-

 " Upon the above facts this court believes that that part of the judgment as contained in the claim was indeed obtained through conspiracy and fraud, and therefore enters judgment for plaintiff with costs .... The passage as referred to in the claim is hereby deleted from the judgment delivered on 24th January, 1950, Exhibit" E," by this Native Court."

On appeal to the Land Court the judgment was confirmed and it is from this decision that this appeal has been lodged. Counsel for the defendant has argued the appeal on three main grounds, viz.: (a) the Native Court" A " of Anlo had no jurisdiction to hear the suit, (b) the plaintiff having abandoned the appeal which was lodged in the Land Court against the judgment is estopped from instituting an action to set it aside, and (c) the weight of evidence.

It has been contended in support of the appeal that the Native Court has no jurisdiction to set aside a judgment alleged to have been obtained by fraud or misrepresentation, that its powers are limited by Ordinance; that there is no inherent jurisdiction by customary law which it could invoke to set aside a judgment on these grounds; and that such powers are vested only in the Supreme Court. In support of his contention, counsel referred to the following passage in Halsbury's Laws of England, Hailsham ed., Vol. 19, p. 266, para. 564:

.. A judgment, which has been obtained by fraud either in the court, or of one or more of the parties, can be impeached by means of an action which may be brought without leave and is analogous to the former Chancery suit to set aside a decree obtained by fraud. "

In my view the above passage does not support the defendant's contention that a Native Court of competent jurisdiction, having given a judgment which is alleged to have been obtained by fraud either in the court, or of one of the parties, cannot exercise jurisdiction to hear an action impeaching that judgment on ground of fraud. The fact that such an action is said to be analogous to the former Chancery suit to set aside a decree obtained by fraud reinforces the view that the power of a court to exercise such a jurisdiction is not derived from any statute but upon the principle that a court has inherent right to set aside its own order if procured by fraud. This inherent right is in no way contrary to Native customary law, it being in keeping with natural justice and good conscience. See Cannell v. Sewell (1), The Alfred Nobel; The Bjiirnstcrznc Bjornson; The Fridla11d (2).

The contention that the plaintiff is estopped from bringing the action is based on the fact that he abandoned the appeal which he had lodged against the judgment. In my view, as the allegation of fraud is a matter within the jurisdiction of the trial court, it should be pg 223 decided on evidence which must relate to matters which, prima facie, would be reasons for setting aside the judgment, if they were estab­lished by proof. There is no evidence showing that there had been previous proceedings between parties at which such evidence was adduced, or could have been adduced. I do not agree that estoppel of any kind could operate as a bar to these proceedings

With regard to submissions on weight of evidence, it is contender~ on behalf of the defendant that the passage in the judgment which the plaintiff claims should be set aside on grounds of fraud and conspiracy is part of the oral judgment which was delivered by the Appeal Court, and that it flows' from the reasoning in the judgment. This, however, is a question of fact which has been resolved by evidence on record. The Native Appeal Court which heard the case was constituted by a panel of three persons, namely, Awoame Fia Togbi Sri II, President, Amuzu Migah and Atsu Aglebe, all of whom have given evidence at the trial of this suit.

It is admitted that the President of the Appeal Court was a man well over eighty years of age at the time and had been blind some years previously. It is this old man alone who is alleged to have signed the said judgment. There is evidence that the other two members protested when they saw Chief Tsili II with the Registrar at the time the Registrar said he was going to reduce the oral judgment into writing; they left the place and never had anything more to do with it. When the judgment was recorded it was not read to them as it should have been. They had never approved of the written judgment, and they testified that the passage complained of was not part of the judgment they delivered because it purports to give to Chief Tsili II and his tribe authority to do that for which they had given judgment against him and his people. Awoame Fia Togbi could not remember how he came to sign the judgment, and in the course of his evidence he was withdrawn from the witness-box as incapable of testifying to the facts. The Registrar, the alleged culprit, could not be found at the trial but in the judgment the following reference was made about him:

" It is unfortunate that the then Registrar Fred Acolatse is out of the Gold Coast because of similar offences and the police are after him. Had it not been so this case will be referred to police for action and prosecution."

In the face of such evidence the Native Court is justified in holding that the passage had been inserted in the judgment without authority and should be deleted therefrom.

I would dismiss this appeal with costs.

Appeal dismissed.
