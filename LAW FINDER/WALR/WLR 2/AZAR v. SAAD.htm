                           February 11, 1957                                

AZAR v. SAAD.  

                                   West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                                 Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.J.A.

 

pg  242


Landlord and Tenant-Recovery of possession by landlord-Premises required for personal occupation by landlord as a dwelling-house-Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, s. 11 (1) (d).

      The plaintiff landlord obtained an order for possession of certain premises on the ground, authorized by the Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, s. 11 (1) (d), that they were required for use as a dwelling­house by members of his family. The premises concerned had been in use partly as a dwelling-house and partly as business premises and, therefore, did not come within the meaning of dwelling-house as contained in section 3 of the Ordinance. The defendant appealed on the ground that section 11 (1) (d) of the Ordinance only permitted the recovery of premises which were in use as dwelling-houses within the meaning of section 3.

     Held, the expression "any premises" in the commencing provisions of section 11 (1) of the Rent Control Ordinance means "premises" as defined by section 3 of the Ordinance. The expression "the premises" in section 11 (1) (d) refers to the" any premises" in the commencement of the section and the meaning thereof-f is in no way qualified or limited by the requirement of section 11 (1) (d) that premises recovered under it are to be used as a dwelling-house. Thus, the limitation on the words "dwelling-house" relates solely to the purpose for which the landlord acquires possession and not to the nature of the premises or the use to which they have been put by the tenant.

   APPEAL from a decision of Quashie-Idun J., on December 3, 1956, in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, awarding a plaintiff landlord recovery of possession of certain premises.

E. O. Asafu-Adjaye and Bentsi-Enchill for the plaintiff.

Akufo-Addo for the defendant.

    VERITY Ag.J.A. This is an appeal from an order for recovery of possession made under section 11 (1) (d) of the Rent Control Ordinance, 1952. The premises in respect of which the order was made were in use by the statutory tenant partly for human habitation and partly as business premises and did not therefore fall within the definition of a dwelling-house contained in section 3 of the Ordinance.

  Section 11 (1) in as far as it relates to the present issue provides that: no order against a tenant for the recovery of possession of .. any premises shall be made ... except in the following cases:- pg 243

 (d) when the premises are reasonably required by the land­lord ... for personal occupation as a dwelling-house by himself, any member of his family or any person in his whole time employment. "

The learned judge was satisfied that the premises were reasonably required by the landlord for personal occupation as a dwelling-house by members of his family and being also satisfied as required by the second proviRo to this part of the section, that no greater hardship would be caused by granting the order than by refusing it, made the order.

No exception is seriously taken or could be taken on the evidence to the findings of the learned judge, but it is contended on behalf of the tenant that this provision of the Ordinance applies only to premises held by the tenant as a dwelling-house.

It is admitted that the premises in question fall within the definition of premises contained in section 3 of the Ordinance and are therefore premises within the meaning of that word as used in section 11 (1) (d), but the submission of counsel is that this paragraph in subsection (1) does not apply to all premises but to such premises only as are for the purposes of the tenancy used for human habitation exclusively.

I am of the opinion that it is not possible to read into the enactment any such restriction upon the meaning of the word premises and I think that counsel's attempt to do so arises from the misconstruction of both the words and the intention of the provision.

The words "any premises" in the fourth line of subsection (1) clearly mean any premises the subject of the tenancy and the words " the premises" in subsection (1) (d) apply with equal clarity to any such premises in relation to which an order for recovery of possession is sought, irrespective of the nature or use of those premises. The further words of the provision relate to the grounds upon which the landlord may obtain such an order, that is to say when he reasonably, requires them for personal use as a dwelling-house. In other words, the limitation imposed by the words "dwelling-house" relate solely to the purpose for which the landlord requires possession of the premises and not to the nature of the premises or the use to which it is put by the tenant.

Had it been the intention to give this provision the effect propounded by counsel it appears to me that the words " dwelling-house" would have been used instead of the word" premises" in the first line thereof as is the case in the, analogous English enactment, and I cannot accept counsel's proposition.

Counsel further sought to raise for the first time in the appeal a question as to the necessity for a notice to quit in these circumstances. pg 244


     This point was not raised in the pleadings nor at the trial and I do not think that it can now be raised or considered.

I would therefore dismiss the appeal with costs.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

KORSAH C.J. I concur.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
