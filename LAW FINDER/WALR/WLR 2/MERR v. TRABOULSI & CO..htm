MEHR v. TRABOULSI & CO.

         Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Accra

                                                                              Windsor-Aubrey J.

 

 

Sale of Goods-Sale by sample-Cloth of different shade from that ordered­ Deviation outside" normal tolerance "-Effect on contract.

Sale of Goods-Sale by sample-Clause in contract "small difference .

must be tolerated" - Reference to independent assessor - Effect of. assessment.

The defendants ordered cloth, by sample, from the plaintiff. When the cloth arrived the defendants objected that its colour differed from that of the sample. The contract contained a clause that "small pg 249 difference in colour must be tolerated," and an independent assessor, to whom the dispute was referred (although not by way of arbitration), found that the difference was outside" normal tolerance" and considered an allowance of 5 per cent. would be appropriate. 'the plaintiffs claimed to enforce the contract: the defendants counterclaimed for damages.

Held, (i) in the case of a contract for sale by sample there is an implied condition that the bulk shall correspond with the sample in quality. For breach of the condition the buyer may reject the whole or may retain the whole, claiming damages in respect of the inferior portion.

Aitken, Campbell &: Co. Ltd. v. Boullen and Gatenby, 1908, 10 S.C. 490 applied.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The Sale of Goods Act, 1893, does not apply in Ghana but, in the words of the draftsman of the Act, " ... it endeavoured to reproduce as exactly as possible the existing law, leaving any amend­ments that might seem desirable to be introduced in committee on the authority of the legislature. So far as England is concerned, the conscious changes effected in the law have been very slight."]

(ii) The difference in shade between bulk and sample, being outside the "normal tolerance," was thereby taken outside the tolerance clause in the contract and that caused a breach of the implied condition. This was not avoided by the recommendation of the independent assessor which was no more than the suggestion of a basis for an amicable settlement.

(1) Aitken, Campbell &: Co. Ltd. v. 110ullen and Gatenby, 1908, 10 S.C. 490.

ACTION by the plaintiff for breach of a contract involving the sale of goods by sample. The defendants counterclaimed for damages.

Mitchell for the plaintiff. Enchill for the defendant.

WINDSOR-AUBREY J. There is no substantial dispute on the facts and the decision in this case largely depends on a determination of points of law. The defendants ordered yellow cloth for schoolgirls' uniform. The goods received were not identical with the goods ordered, and the defendants' sub-buyer refused to take delivery of them. With some hesitation the defendants had agreed to accept the following con­dition in their contract of purchase, namely: "Small deviations in colour must be tolerated." Eventually the plaintiffs and the defendants mutually agreed to take the opinion (without agreeing to any arbitra­tion) of the Manchester

Chamber of Commerce who submitted a report wherein the Chamber's expert stated that the difference in shade ordered compared with the goods delivered was in his opinion outside the normal tolerance and he considered an allowance of five per cent. was justified. pg 250

The plaintiffs argue that the defendants are only entitled to a five per cent. reduction of cost price. On the other hand the defendants contend that the expert having found that the goods were outside the normal tolerance they were entitled as a matter of law to repudiate the contract and counterclaim for the loss they sustained on the refusal of their sub-buyer to accept the goods. In other words the plaintiffs contend the report should be accepted as a whole while the defendants contend that the words " outside the normal tolerance" establish a breach of contract going to its roots which entitle them to repudiate it, and that therefore they are not bound to accept a reduction in price.

Some evidence has been led that if material for school uniform is not identical with that commonly in use, such children or their parents will not buy it. Certainly all schoolchildren are notoriously sensitive to discrepancies in uniform, and the fact that the defendants' sub-buyer refused to take the cloth is some indication that it was unsuitable though, of course, there may have been considerations wholly unknown to the court which induced the sub-buyer to take an opportunity of backing out of his contract. However, on such points I cannot conjecture. I am bound by the evidence on record.

One point is clear, namely that deviations in colour are so important that the overseas suppliers qualified their acceptance of the defendants' order by stipulating that small deviations in colour must be tolerated.

I now have to ask myself what is the law in such circumstances where goods received do not answer to the sample or description of the goods ordered having regard also to the fact that I am satisfied it was known by both parties that the material was required for school uniforms. As far as I can ascertain the Sale of Goods Act, 1893, has not altered the common law relating to contract as regards goods ordered by sample. The case of Aitken, Campbell J; Co. Ltd. v. Boullen &; Gatenby (1) cited in Chitty on Contracts, 20th ed., at p. 774, is in point. The facts there were that maroon twill was sold by sample, but a part of the bulk delivered was inferior to sample. It was held the buyer might reject the whole or might retain the whole and claim damages in respect of the inferior twill.

In this case now before me the expert opinion is that the deviation is outside the normal tolerance, which can only mean that the goods received are not up to the sample. In such circumstances the defen­dants are normally entitled to repudiate the contract, but I must consider the effect of the recommendation of a five per cent. reduction in price. As to that recommendation I regard it merely as a suggestion for a basis of amicable settlement.

It often happens that parties do not insist on their full legal rights, but it is matter of law for the court to decide what remedies are available to the injured party. The law seems clear that where goods are not up to sample the buyer may reject them. pg 251


     As to the counterclaim I am satisfied that the plaintiffs were aware that the defendants purchased the goods for resale. The defendants purchased the goods solely because they had a firm order for their resale. As a result they have lost the profit of £228 18s. 5d. they would have made on the resale. In my opinion the plaintiffs' claim must fail and the defendants must succeed on their counterclaim. Accordingly the plaintiffs' claim is dismissed and there will be judgment for the defendants on the counterclaim for £228 18s. 5d. The defendants are awarded costs on both the claim and counterclaim.

Judgment for the defendants on the counterclaim.

S. G. D.
