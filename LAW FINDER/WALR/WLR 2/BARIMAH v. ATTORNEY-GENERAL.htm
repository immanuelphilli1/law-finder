May 2, 1956.

BARIMAH v. ATTORNEY-GENERAL

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Ashanti Judicial Division

Benson J

 

 

                Crown Practice-Petition of right-Effect of Petitions of Right Ordinance, c. 18 of 1951, s. 4.

The plaintiff had obtained the consent of the Minister of the Interior to proceed against the Gold Coast Government by way of petition of right in a claim for damages for negligence by Government servants. At the hearing the preliminary point was made that the Petitions of Right Ordinance (which was passed in 1877) gives no right to proceed against the Crown in tort by petition of right, either with or without the consent of the Minister, save in those cases in which it was possible, in 1877, to use petition of right for tort in England. The Petitions of Right Ordinance, s. 4, reads:

" All claims against the general Government of the Gold Coast, being of the same nature as claims which may be preferred against the Crown in England by petition, manifestation, or plea of right, may, with the consent of the Minister, be preferred in the proper Divisional Court of the Supreme Court .... "

Held, the Petitions of Right Ordinance, s. 4, applies to the Gold Coast the law relating to petitions of right as it stood in England at the time the Ordinance was passed: it did not apply the English law as this might from time to time stand amended after 1877. As it was not possible to proceed against the Crown in England in 1877 for a tort of this nature, an action could not now be maintained in the Gold Coast.

ACTION by the plaintiff against the Government of the Gold Coast for damages for negligence by servants of the Government. The action was commenced by petition of right, the fiat of the Minister of the Interior having been obtained.

OWU8U for the plaintiff.

Akiwumi, Jnr., for the Attorney-General of the Gold Coast.

BENSON J. This is a claim for damages in tort arising out of the negligence of officers in the employment of the Gold Coast Government and is brought under the Petitions of Right Ordinance, the fiat of the Minister of the Interior having been obtained.


    A preliminary issue has been raised in the Statement of Defence that an action based on tort does not lie; this issue has been argued in limine and I now have to rule on it. Section 4 of the Petitions of Right Ordinance is the relevant one and reads as follows:-

" All claims against the general Government of the Gold Coast, being of the same nature as claims which may be preferred against the Crown in England by petition, manifestation, or plea of right, may, with the consent of the Minister be preferred in the proper Divisional Court of the Supreme Court .... "

It is argued on behalf of the Attorney-General that by the words " may be preferred" the legislature intended it to be understood that it referred only to claims which could have been preferred against th~ Crown in England at the time of the coming into force of the Ordinance, that is, July 23, 1877, and that the section does not mean " claims which may be preferred from time to time in England." At the time when the Ordinance was passed into law, actions against the Crown in England could have been brought by petition of right by virtue of the Petitions of Right Act of 1860 and were limited to actions in contract; actions in tort were excluded. The Crown Proceedings Act was passed in England in 1947 and materially altered the law; claims against the Crown could then be brought in contract and tort, as of right, and without the fiat of His Majesty.

It is contended by counsel for the plaintiff that in order to ascertain what claims are permissible under the Ordinance, regard must be had to the law in England as it now is and that by virtue of the Crown Proceedings Act, 1947, this present action is properly instituted.

In my view the wording of section 4 of the Ordinance limits claims to those of the same nature as claims which could, at the time of the passing of the Ordinance, have been brought against the Crown in England. I think that if the legislature had intended the section to cover claims which could be brought in England" from time to time, " it would have been clearly stated in the Ordinance.

Again it is observed that section 4 of the Ordinance applies to claims which may be brought in England by petition, manifestation, or plea of right; since the passing of the Crown Proceedings Act, 1947, claims are no longer brought in any of these ways, but as of right. It follows, therefore, that even if the 1947 Act applied in this Colony, no claim could be preferred here at all against the Government.

I rule that the preliminary plea of the defendant must succeed, that the plaintiff has no right to bring this action in tort. The action accordingly is dismissed and there will be judgment for the defendant. Counsel for defendant does not ask for costs. There will therefore be no order as to costs.

Judgment for the defendant.

S. G. D.
