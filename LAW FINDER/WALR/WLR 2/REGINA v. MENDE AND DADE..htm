Crirninal Law - Robbery - Conspiracy to rob - Ingredients of offence of robbery-Assault without intent to rob-Criminal Code, c. 9, ss. 49, 289 and 302-Court of Appeal Ordinance, c. 35 of 1957, s. 11 (2).

A group of men, instigated by one Bosompim, assaulted three com計lainants. After the assaults the complainants were taken (although not by the assaulters) to Bosompim at his house. Here they were detained and were threatened with further assault unless they paid money to Bosompim. In one of the three cases the complainant paid the money demanded and was allowed to depart: in the other two cases the complainants had not sufficient money on them and they were detained in each case until a relative arrived and paid the sum demanded. Bosompim and two of the group of attackers were jointly charged and convicted on three counts of robbery and of conspiracy to rob. From these convictions the two attackers appealed; Bosompim did not.

Held, (i) in two of the three cases of which complaint was made the money taken had not come from the complainant but from a person who had paid it on his behalf. In these cases, therefore, the complainant had neither property in, nor possession of, the money. As possession of, and some property in, the thing stolen by the complainant are essential ingredients of the offence of robbery, the convictions on these two counts could not be sustained.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The requirement that the complainant must have some property in the thing stolen is widely construed. Thus it was held to exist in a maidservant alone in her master's house as regards his goods there:

B. v. Harding (1929) 21 Cr.App.R. 166; 142 L.T. 583.]

(ii) In any event, while the appellants had assaulted the com計lainants at the instigation of Bosompim, there was no evidence that they were party to Bosompim's design to extract money from the complainants or that their use of force had been for the purpose of stealing. Their convictions for robbery (if in fact there had been any robbery) therefore could not be sustained. It followed, moreover, that although there may have been a common purpose in the appellants' acts, this was not connected with robbery, so that the convictions for con貞piracy to rob must also fail.

(iii) To support an indictment for robbery the use of force or causing harm to the person robbed must, inter alia, be established. Although the convictions for robbery could not be sustained the use of force had pg349 been established and therefore, in exercise of the powers conferred by the Court of Appeal Ordinance, c. 35 of 1957, s. 11 (2), convictions for assault would be substituted for the convictions quashed

APPEALS from convictions on January 24, 1957, at Kumasi Assize of robbery and conspiracy to rob under the Criminal Code, c. 9, ss. 289 and 302 and s. 49.

Scott, Senior Crown Counsel, for the Crown. The appellants appeared in person.

The following judgment of the court was given by VAN LARK ,J. :

The appellants are two of three persons who were tried and convicted together at the Kumasi Assize on January 24, 1957, by a judge sitting with assessors, for the offences of conspiracy and robbery. The par負iculars of the conspiracy were that they, on August 10, 1956, at Muoso village in Ashanti, acted together with certain other persons with a common purpose in committing a crime, namely robbery. There are three counts of robbery against all the three prisoners charging them together with robbing three different persons, namely Kofi Yeboah, Kwasi Anane and Kwasi Addae respectively, and in each case, of the sum of twenty-one shillings on the same date and place as in the conspiracy charge.

Against their convictions Kwame Menuh (the first accused) and Kwabena Dade (the second accused) have appealed to this court. Yaw Bosompim (the third accused), the other convicted person, has not appealed and with him we are not concerned.

The case for the prosecution was as follows. The complainant, Kofi Yeboah, one of "Jehovah's Witnesses," left his house at about 7 o'clock in the evening for Bible studies when he was suddenly sur訃ounded by a group of about seven men, including the appellants, out貞ide his house. He was interrogated by one of the men as to whether he had voted at the last general election. Upon his replying that as a Jehovah witness he did not indulge in party politics and was there; fore neither a supporter of the N.L.M. nor of the C.P.P., the second appellant slapped him in the face and took from him a " Watch Tower Pamphlet" which he tore into pieces; whereupon all the seven men pounced upon him and beat him up. After this assault he was led away by a certain man to the house of the third accused, Yaw Bosompim, who was not then at home but arrived there later. Yaw BosQmpim demanded a fee of twenty-one shillings before the com計lainant could be released and he threatened that if the complainant did not pay he might be beaten up again. In order to secure his release the complainant took Yaw Bosompim to his house and there paid him pg 350 the amount which he demanded. 'J.'he appellants and the group of other young men were not present but it would appear that they continued to commit similar offences of assault in the village the same evening. One I\wasi Anane, the other complainant, was the victim of another assault by the appellants and others who beat him up. This second incident took place in the presence of Yaw Bosompim who, after the assault, took the victim to his house and dernanded of him also the sum of twenty-one shillings before he could be set free. Apparently the complainant, who had no money on him, could not procure his release until an uncle of his, one Kwami Adu, went to the house and paid the amount demanded on the complainant's behalf. The group of the young ruffians, including the appellants, continued to roam the village that night and accosted yet a third victim, Kwasi Addae, who was on his way to a stream to have a bath. Kwasi Addae was not beaten where he was met but was led to the house of Yaw Bosompim who identified him as one of those he wanted to be beaten; thereupon upon Yaw Bosompim's instructions the young men, including the appellants, beat the complainant up in the house. Even after the beating Yaw Bosompim would not let the victim go away until his father who arrived there later paid a sum of twenty-one shillings as demanded by Yaw Bosompim.

At the trial the second appellant said that he and the first appellant together with the other young men had been invited to the village by Yaw Bosompim for the purpose of beating up some C.P.P. people in retaliation for beatings which the said Yaw Bosompim had allegedly received from the hands of the C.P.P. members. There was nothing in the prosecution case to contradict this statement of fact.

The learned trial judge was satisfied that the appellants and the others went to this village at the invitation of the third accused, Yaw Bosompim, and upon their arrival there beat up the complainants in the manner described, and he found that it was in pursuance of a previous agreement they had entered into with Yaw Bosompim. But the evidence is not clear whether the common design was to commit any offence other than one of assault and we are unable to find from the evidence as a whole that the concert was to commit the offence of robbery. 'Ve are, therefore, of the opinion that it is not possible to presume that the appellants conspired to rob but it is clear that they conspired to assault. It is hard to say that to rob a person must be deemed to be in furtherance of a common design to assault. It is not suggested that the assaults by the appellants were for the purpose of stealing moneys from the persons of the complainants, or with intent to prevent or overcome resistance to any stealing.

As there is no evidence to connect the appellants with the ulterior motive of Yaw Bosompim whose apparent design was to "exact" money from the victims, the appellants cannot be responsible for  pg 351Bosompim's acts, which were not agreed upon by the conspirators and which were acts perpetrated by him outside the assault and without the knowledge of the appellants. We hold, therefore, that the conviction of the appellants for the offence of conspiracy to rob cannot be supported having regard to the evidence.

 

We now come to deal with the alleged offences of robbery, Le., counts 2, 3 and 4, and we refer to the definition of robbery which is as follows :-(section 302 of the Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951)

.. A person who steals a thing is guilty of robbery if, in and for the purpose of stealing the thing, he uses any force or causes any harm to any person, or if he uses any threat of criminal assault or harm to any person, with intent thereby to prevent or overcome the resistance of that or of any other person to the stealing of the thing. "

We have already come to the conclusion that the appellants were in no way concerned with robbing the complainants in any of these counts. The" exacting" or the actual taking of the money was committed by Yaw Bosompim. It would have been different had the appellants been guilty of conspiracy to rob and in furtherance of that common design Yaw Bosompim had taken the money from the com計lainants against their will or had obtained their consent by duress to pass the property in the moneys to him. The motive of the crime as it relates to the appellants appears to us to be a revenge for assault and not robbery and in considering the evidence as a whole we have been unable to find satisfactory evidence of concert to rob any of the complainants. Once that conclusion is reached the fabric, not only of the conspiracy charge but also of the robbery charges, falls to the ground. Weare aware however that it is not necessary for a man to be guilty of robbery or stealing that he actually participated in the crime: if he is a confederate he is guilty, although he has no hand in the actual taking; but a man to be amenable to the law must take such part in the commission of the crime alleged as must be the result of a common design to commit the offence-R. v. Gray (1). In the present case the evidence does not in any way involve the appellants with a common design for robbery.

Furthermore, on an indictment for robbery the prosecutor must have some property in the goods, which must have been proved to have been taken either from the person of the prosecutor or in his presence-see Archbold, 33rd ed., para. 1119. With respect to the third and fourth counts in this case the prosecutors had no property in the money. It had not even been taken away from the persons of the prosecutors, but was paid to the third accused by the prosecutors' uncle and father respectively in order to effect the prosecutors' release from unlawful detention; or as the learned trial judge found, that Yaw Bosompim pg 352 ., demanded and was paid the sum of 21s. on behaH of each such victim as a consideration for refraining to beat the victims any further. ,.,

The prosecutor in the second count also parted with his money to Yaw Bosompim in the absence of the appellants in order to be released from unlawful detention. In each of the alleged robbery offences the moneys had been received after the assault and there can be no suggestion that there was an intent to prevent or to overcome the resistance of the complainant or any other person to the taking or receipt by Yaw Bosompim.

The learned trial judge found, not that the moneys were stolen from the complainants but " exacted," in other words "extorted. In his own language:

" the purpose of the assaults, the detention of the victims, and threats of further assaults was for exacting moneys from them."

In our view this can only mean that the moneys were found to be unlawfully demanded and paid under duress or obtained by means of threats. Perhaps the learned trial judge had in mind the offence of extortion contrary to section 290 (1) of the Criminal Code which is as follows:

"Whoever extorts any property from any person by means of

threats shall be liable to imprisonment for five years."

But it must be pointed out that a " threat" when used with reference to extortion does not include a threat of criminal assault or harm to the person threatened-Section 303 of the Criminal Code.

In any case the appellants would not be responsible for any other offences which Yaw Bosompim might be guilty of in respect of the moneys he had taken, received or extorted from the victims after the assault.

With regard to an offence of robbery however, assault is a minor offence which is included in the major offence. To support an indict衫ent for robbery the prosecution must prove assault and that it was coupled with an intent to rob. In this case the latter ingredient consti負uting the offence has not been proved but we are satisfied that in counts 2, 3 and 4 the prosecution had proved and the appellants had both confessed to the offences of assault in respect of which they could be lawfully convicted as provided in section 151 of the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951.

In our opinion the conviction and sentence of the appellants on counts 2, 3' and 4 cannot stand, and in exercise of our powers under section 11 (2) of the Court of Appeal Ordinance, c. 35 of 1957, we substitute for the verdict found by the trial judge a verdict of guilty of assault contrary to section 180 of the Criminal Code in respect of each of the said counts, and hereby sentence each of the appellants to pg 353 twelve months' imprisonment with hard labour in substitution' for the sentences passed at the trial. Sentences to run concurrently.

 The appeal is allowed in respect of the first count; the conviction of each of the appellants on that count is quashed and the appellants are discharged on the first count.

Convictions of assault on three counts substituted for conviction for robbery. Conviction for con貞piracy to rob quashed.

S. G. D.
