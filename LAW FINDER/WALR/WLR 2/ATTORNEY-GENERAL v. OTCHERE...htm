ATTORNEY-GENERAL v. OTCHERE..

                                   West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                                 Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.J.A.

 

 

Contract-Variation by subsequent agreement-Offer to accept discharge by alternative method.

The defendant had been in Government employ and had borrowed from them, against a deed of assignment, money to buy a car. The deed provided that the advance should be repaid by monthly installments; that if the defendant left the Government service before the advance was repaid the outstanding balance should be payable immediately; and that the Government would not seize the vehicle unless there should be, inter alia, default in repayment. The defendant left Government service while part of the advance was still outstanding. He then received a letter from the Government demanding immediate repayment of the outstanding balance or "alternatively to hand the car over to the Chief Transport Officer, Accra." The letter went on to allow ten days for one of these two courses to be followed, and stated that if neither were followed in this period the Government would invoke the assignment and seize the car. The defendant handed over the car within the time  pg 239 allowed, and it was valued by the transport officer at £425. Nine months later the Government sold the car for £150 and then demanded from the defendant the unrepaid balance of the advance less the proceeds of sale.

Held, the letter written to the defendant was to be construed as containing an offer to vary the terms of the original agreement as con­tained in the deed of assignment and, by delivering the car within the period allowed by the letter, the defendant accepted this offer and discharged his obligations under the amended contract.

VERITY Ag.J.A. In this case the Attorney-General on behalf of the Government of the Gold Coast, sought to recover first a balance due and owing under an agreement for a loan and secondly the balance due under a deed of assignment made in consideration of an advance made to the defendant for the purchase of a motor-car. The first claim having been satisfied, the sole issue before the trial court related to the second claim and the learned judge entered judgment thereon for the plaintiff .•

The facts stated briefly are that the defendant, a qualified doctor, was employed by the Government as a house surgeon for a period of .twelve months with the prospect of appointment as a medical officer at the end thereof. An advance was made by the Government to the defendant of £745 for the purchase of a motor-car and in consideration thereof the defendant executed a deed of assignment of the motor-car purchased by him, by way of security for the repayment of the advance. By the terms thereof the defendant undertook to repay the advance by monthly instalments of £15 l0s. 5d. and the deed further provided that if the defendant left the Government service before the advance was repaid in full the balance outstanding should become due and payable forthwith. It was further provided that the Government would not seize the vehicle unless (inter alia) the defendant made default in repayment of the advance.

Upon the expiration of the period of twelve months the Government informed the defendant it was not proposed to renew his appointment or offer him further employment and the defendant was called upon to repay the balance of the advance outstanding which then amounted to £543 4s. 7d.

The defendant made verbal request to be allowed to retain the use of the car and continue repayment by instalments. On January 4, 1954, the Government informed him that his request could not be pg 240 granted and the letter in which this decision was intimated to the defendant continued:

" In view of this I have to request you to pay to the District Treasury, .Accra, the outstanding balance or alternatively to hand the car over to the Chief Transport Officer, Accra. I have to request that you complete action on one of these courses within ten days from the date of this letter, failing which the Government will have no alternative but to invoke clause 6 of the Deed of Assignment entered into by you and take possession of the car."

The defendant complied with the alternative course offered him and delivered the car to the Transport Officer within the required time, i.e., on January 15, 1954. On February 17, 1954, the then Transport Officer valued the car at £425. No instructions were given him for sale until May, no action appears to have been taken to sell the car until September and it was eventually sold on October 6, 1954, for £150. During the period of nearly nine months between surrender and sale the car had been kept in an open shed in the Government Transport Yard.

On November 26 the defendant was called upon to pay the balance which had been outstanding on January 1 less the net proceeds of the sale, £145 13s. 4d. This the defendant declined to pay and this action was taken.

It was contended at the trial that the defendant had been offered alternative courses; either to pay the balance or surrender the car and that there had been no seizure in pursuance of clause 6 of the deed of assignment. The learned judge does not appear to have considered this aspect of the matter and for other reasons which I do not think now arise entered judgment for the plaintiff.

At the hearing of the appeal the principal ground argued was that by the letter of January 4, 1954, the Government offered to vary the terms of the deed of assignment by substituting as an alternative to payment of the balance outstanding within ten days, surrender of the car within that period and that by surrendering the car the defendant accepted the offer and was discharged from further obligation.

I am of the opinion that this contention is well founded. The position on January 4, 1954, under the deed of assignment was that the defendant was under obligation to repay the outstanding advance forth­with (clause 5); if he made default the Government were entitled to seize and sell the car (clause 6). By the letter of that date, however, the Government made a clear offer to the defendant of an alternative to his obligation under clause 5 and that this was intended to be in substitution for the seizure of the car under clause 6 is demonstrated by the fact that it was specifically stated that if the defendant did not comply with either alternative within ten days the Government would invoke clause 6. pg 241

This amounts, in my view, to an offer to waive the rights of the Government under clause 6 provided that the defendant either paid the amount outstanding or surrendered the car, and upon the compliance of the defendant he was discharged from all further obligations and cannot now be called upon to pay the difference between the outstanding sum and that realized by the sale nine months later.

The conduct of the Government after the car had been surrendered in delaying for so many months the sale thereof without communicating with the defendant in any way can only be regarded either as gross lack of consideration for the interests of the defendant or an acknow­ledgment that the defendant no longer had any interest therein or in the proceeds of sale, and if it were necessary for the purpose of construing the meaning and effect of the letter of January 4 I would prefer to accept the latter hypothesis.

Counsel for the respondent, who had not appeared at the trial, frankly, and I think very properly, conceded that he had been unable to find any authority in favour of the respondent's claim in these circumstances.

I think, therefore, that the learned judge erred in holding that the defendant remained under any obligation after his acceptance and com­pliance with the alternative course made open to him by the Govern­ment's offer of January 4, 1954, and that the appeal should therefore be allowed with costs, the judgment of the court below being set aside and judgment entered therein for the defendant with costs to be taxed.

By grounds 8, 9 and 10 of the grounds of appeal it was set up that the Deed of Assignment was unenforceable by reason of the provisions of the Bills of Sale Acts, and we were invited by counsel for the Crown to indicate our view on this question. Upon it being pointed out that the Bills of Sale Acts 1878-1882 are not in force in the Gold Coast and that the Bills of Sale Act, 1854, does not appear to be applicable to the present case, counsel for the appellant abandoned those grounds and the point does not therefore fall for decision in the appeal before us.
