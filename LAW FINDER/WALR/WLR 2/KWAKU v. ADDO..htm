Native Law and Custom-Defamation-Nature of action under native law and custom.

The defendant, at a public gathering, uttered remarks about the plaintiff which were alleged to be defamatory, and the plaintiff com­menced this action for damages against him. Although it appeared that, subsequent to the publication of the remarks, the plaintiff had, in settlement of a debt due to him, taken a conveyance of land that was of a smaller area than its grantors had previously undertaken to convey tQ him, this loss to the plaintiff (if indeed any loss to him was involved) was not shown to have stemmed from the publication of the remarks, nor was it relied upon by the plaintiff as special damage to support his action.

It was not clear, on the face of the action, whether its foundation lay in the English law of defamation or in defamation as it exists in native law and custom, although the implication, from the size of the sum claimed by way of damages, was that it was intended to be founded in English law. The defendant argued that if it was founded in EngJish law the words used were not actionable per se and special damage had not been proved, whereas if it was founded in native law and custom the action must fail because no custom allowing such an action to be maintainable had been proved and in fact had not even been especially pleaded, as is required by the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 19, r. 31. The plaintiff relied on the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 87 (2), and thereunder on Sarbah, Fanti Customary Law, as demonstrating that a civil action for defamation was maintainable by native law and custom and, on the proposition that a point of native custom that reflects universal rather than purely local canons of behaviour need not be specially pleaded, as is otherwise required by Order 19, rule 31.

Held, (i) if the action was founded on the English law of defamation then, as the words used did not bring the matter within one of the three permitted classes in which slander is actionable without proof of special damage, and, as no special damage was proved, the action must fail.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The three special classes are: imputation of a criminal offence punishable with imprisonment at first instance; imputation of contagious or infectious disease likely to prevent other persons from associating with the plaintiff; and, imputation of unfitness, dishonesty or incompetence in any office held by the plaintiff or in any lawful profession or trade carried on by him. The imputation of unchastity or adultery to a woman was made actionable per se by the Slander of Women Act, 1891: this does not apply in Ghana  pg 307

(ii) No cause of action lay in general native law and custom sufficient to maintain a claim, the primary purpose of which was that of recom­pensing the person defamed by means of a pecuniary award, for no such custom had, by frequent proof in the courts, become so notorious that the courts had taken judicial notice of it. Angu v. Attah «1916) Judgments of the Privy Council on Appeal from the Gold Coast, 1874-1928, p. 43) applied.

(iii) If a particular custom was relied upon to found such an action, no such custom had been pleaded, as is required by the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 19, r. 31. If such a custom existed, it was not of such universal application as to make it unnecessary for it to be specifically pleaded.

Obiter, if an action for defamation is maintainable by native law and custom, then such an action has not, as its primary purpose, the recompensing by a pecuniary award of the person defamed, but has as its primary purpose the object of obtaining a public recantation of the defamatory matter. Compensation is a secondary purpose in such an action. It will, if awarded, and depending on the circumstances of the case, be of a small amount and in the nature of a fine and will in any case lie only at the discretion of the court.

Sarbah, Fanti Customary Law, 2nd ed., pp. 113-114, quoted.

ACTION for damages for slander. The sum of £] ,000 was claimed by way of damages.

Danquah for the plaintiff.

E. O. Asafu-Adjaye for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. The plaintiff in this suit claims £1,000 damages for slander al1eged to have been published against him by the defendant at a political ral1y held at N ungua on June 3, 1956. The words com­plained of are set out in paragraph 4 of the statement of c1aim, and are to the effect that the plaintiff-Denfutse Borketey-Affoteyfio Quaye, Adjei Otwemah, Otsiame Affotey Raja and Adjoga Otu, were evil-minded citizens of Nungua, who out of malice wrote a letter to one Awudu of the Accra Municipal Council, and thereby caused some buildings in Nungua to be demolished by the Accra Municipal Council; and further, that the plaintiff was an avaricious person who had been grabbing al1 Nungua lands to deprive other Nungua citizens and their children of the lands. By paragraph 4 of the statement of claim it is pleaded that the innuendo of the said words is: pg 308 that the plaintiff is selfish and anti-social, a N ungua land grabber, and that he has maliciously reported against his fellow citizens of Nungua." Also it is pleaded in paragraph 5 of the statement of claim that the said words:  

were false and malicious and have exposed the plaintiff to ridicule and contempt both as regards his position as a person who in the past had lent money to the town of Nungua ... and as a citizen of Nungua who must seek the welfare of his fellow citizens. "

The defendant, by his statement of defence, denied publication of the said words, and contended that the said words are incapable of carrying defamatory meaning, and have not exposed the plaintiff to hatred, ridicule and contempt. Also, in paragraph 7 thereof it was pleaded that the words complained of were not actionable per se.

The following facts are either admitted or not disputed:

(a) that the plaintiff is a creditor and benefactor of the Nungua community;

(b) that the Mantse and elders of Nungua promised to give.him land in payment of the balance of the debt due to him from the town;

(c) that at the time of the alleged publication a dispute was pending in court between the plaintiff and the Nungua Mantse and Mankralo for specific performance of the agreement to convey the said land to him;

(d) that subsequent to the alleged publication the said suit was settled and a consent order was made for the conveyance to the plaintiff of a piece of land which is only one-third of what the Mantse and his elders had undertaken to convey;

(e) that the defendant is a member of the Nungua Divisional Council and the elected representative of Nungua on the Accra Municipal Council;

(f) that some houses were demolished at Nungua by the Accra Municipal Council and;

(g) that a public political rally was held at Nungua on June 3, 1950, and the defendant was one of the people who addressed the gathering.

Mr. E. O. Asafu Adjaye, for tIle defence, submitted that even if the words complained of were defamatory they are not actionable per se, and since no special damage has been alleged, and none proved, the claim should be dismissed. He cited the case of Amoah v. Djabi (1) and well-known English authorities in support of that pro­position. He further submitted that if the case is based on native custom, that custom should have been pleaded as laid down in Order 19, Rule 31, of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, pg 309 1954, and in any event that the custom should have been proved. He finally submitted that the passage in Sarbah,' Fanti Customary Law, 2nd ed., pp. 113-114, dealing with slander is merely an exposition on the subject but does not deal with the custom fully, and that it appears from it that slander in Native Custom is a criminal wrong which is punishable by a fine.

Dr. Danquah submitted that the claim is based on native custom and not on English law; that under native custom slander is both a civil and a criminal wrong actionable per se; he cited the passages in Sarbah referred to above. He submitted that the custom relied upon is one of general application and not one applicable only to a particular area, and therefore Order 19, Rule 31, does not apply, and cited the case of Adjei and Dua v. Ripley (2) in support.

Section 87 (1) of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, provides:

• (1) Nothing in this Ordinance shall deprive the courts of the right to observe and enforce the observance, or shall deprive any person of the benefit, of any native law or custom existing in the Gold Coast, such law or custom not being repugnant to natural justice, equity, and good conscience, nor incompatible either directly or by necessary implication with any ordinance for the time being in force. Such laws and customs shall be deemed applicable in causes and matters where the parties thereto are !natives, and particularly, but without derogating from their appli­cation in other cases, in causes and matters relating to the tenure and transfer of renl and personal property, and to inheritance and testamentary dispositions, and also in causes and matters between natives and non-natives where it may appear to the court that substantial injustice would be done to either party by a strict adherence to the rules of English law. No party shall be entitled to claim the benefit of any local law or custom, if it shall appear either from express contract or from the nature of the transactions out of which any suit or question may have arisen, that such party agreed that his obligations in connection with such transactions should be regulated exclusively by English law; and in cases where no express rule is applicable to any matter in controversy, the court shall be governed by the principles of justice, equity. and good conscience."

And subsection (2) reads:

" (2) In deciding question of native law and custom the court may give effect to any book or manuscript recognized in the Gold Coast as a legal authority and may call to its assistance Chiefs or other persons whom the court considers to have special knowledge of native law and custom."

It appears from subsection (1) of section 87 of the Ordinance that unless there is anything to the contrary, native customary law should pg 310 be applied in deciding this case which is between two natives. However, unless there is any book or manuscript recognized in Ghana as a legal authority or any decisions of the courts dealing specifically with the native custom of slander, evidence of persons recognized as having special knowledge of native law and custom should be led by the party who relies upon the particular native custom if he should succeed upon it. Griffith C.J. stated the principle in the case of Yerenchi v. A7cuffo (3) at p. 374, as follows:

" ... it is a serious thing either to refuse to accept a native custom, which may have existed for ages, or to put the judicial hall-mark upon a native custom, and I think that before the court holds one way or another the evidence in favour of or against any such custom should be more than merely preponderating."

And the Privy Council in the case of Angu v. Attah (4) said:

" As is the case with all customary law, it has to be proved in the first instance by calling witnesses acquainted with the native customs until the particular customs have, by frequent proof in the courts, become so notorious that the courts take judicial notice of them."

Now the passage cited in Sarbah, Fanti Customary Law, is as follows:

" Words which cause or produce any injury to the reputation of another are called defamatory, and, if false, are actionable. False defamatory words, when spoken, constitute slander. Where a person has been found guilty for using slanderous words, he is bound to retract his words publicly, in addition to paying a small fine by way of compensation to the aggrieved party . Words imputing witchcraft, adultery, immoral conduct, crime, and all words which sound to the disreputation of a person of whom they are spoken, are actionable ...

" An effective way of punishing a person guilty of slander of serious consequences, is to make him walk through the town or village carrying a heavy stone in front of an officer of the court, who, at convenient halting-places, beats a gong; the guilty slanderer is compelled to recant his base falsehoods, and to confess his disgraceful behaviour, amid the sneers and jeers of the multitude. "

In this passage emphasis is laid on such expressions as " he is bound to retract his words publicly "-" pay a small fine by way of compensation to the aggrieved party "-" make him (the slanderer) walk through the town or village carrying a heavy stone in front of an officer of the court, who at convenient halting-places, beats a gong; the guilty slanderer is compelled to recant his base falsehood and to confess his disgraceful behaviour amid the sneers and jeers of the pg 311 multitude. " These words give the impression that native customary remedy for slander is more to penalize than merely to compensate.

Dr. Danquah submitted that the remedy may be civil or criminal.

It may well be so; but where do I find that in the judicial authorities or in the evidence before me? The matter of the remedy which is provided by native customary law in case of slander is not at all free from doubt. Sarbah's book upon which reliance is placed makes the doubt apparent, it does not resolve it. I have looked up a few cases on the point including Amoah v. Djabi (1) cited above, Sarah Cheetham v. Robert Bannerman (5), Mensah v. Quamina Attopi (6), but have found no judicial authority which resolves the doubt on the subject.

Thus the native custom relied upon has neither been established by oral evidence of experts on the custom, nor shown by decided cases to be so notorious that I can take judicial notice of it. Without such proof I cannot give effect to the alleged custom and grant relief thereunder.

One point more, and that is, even if Sarbah's Fanti Customary Law were specific and unambiguous on the subject, it shows, as I have already observed, that the remedy for the wrong will be an order to recant and a small fine by way of compensation. In my opinion, therefore, a claim made under native custom must be one primarily for an order to recant or retract, and when the slander is proved the court, in addition to that remedy may, having regard to the nature· and circumstances of the case, award a small fine as compensation. The claim made in this case is not for such a remedy; it is for damages in the large sum as £1,000. Obviously that must be a claim made in accordance with English law which lays emphasis on damages in an action for defamation.

I hold that if the claim is based on native custom, it must fail because the particular native custom has not been proved. I hold further that if the claim is based on English law as appears from the nature of the writ, it must also fail as the words complained of are not actionable per se. The claim is dismissed with costs.

Judgment for the defendant.

S. G. D.
