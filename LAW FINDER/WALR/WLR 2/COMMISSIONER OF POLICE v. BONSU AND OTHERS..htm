                                  COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. BONSU AND OTHERS.                                                                       

                                         Supreme Court of Ghana, Court of Appeal                                                                         

                                              Korsah C.J., Quashie­Idun, van Lare JJ

 

 

Criminal Law-Corruption or extortion by public officer-Alternative charges 01 corruption and 01 extortion - Effect 01 dismissal 01 one charge­ Criminal Code, c. 9 01 1951, s. 413.

Criminal Law-Appeals-Amendment in charge-Effect of failure 01 court to call upon accused to answer to amended charge-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 0/1951, s. 172 (1).

The first and second accused were appea1ing from a conviction for corruption; the third accused was appea1ing from a conviction for aiding pg 335 and abetting the first two accused in the commission of that offence. The accused had originally stood charged with two offences: the second charge against the first two accused had been of extortion: that against the third accused of aiding and abetting extortion. It was clear, how­ever, that these second charges were framed as alternatives to the charges on which the accused had been convicted, since they were founded on the same set of facts. At the trial the magistrate, at the close of the case for the prosecution, dismissed the second charges on the grounds that no case of extortion had been made for the accused to answer. The defence submitted that no case had been made on either charge, but this the magistrate rejected, and he called upon the accused to enter upon their defence in respect of the charge of corruption. The accused thereupon elected to offer no evidence and closed their defences. At the hearing of the appeal it. was argued that the submission of "no case" had been wrongly rejected at the trial. It was also argued that as the charge of extortion had been dismissed, the conviction on the charge of corruption, which is contained in the same section of the Ordinance, must also fail. Further, that the prosecution had amended the second charge against the third accused, that the magistrate had not called upon the second accused to plead afresh to the amended charge as required by the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 172 (1), and that, therefore, the whole proceedings as against all the accused were a nullity.

  Held, (i) on the facts a prima facie case of corruption had been made out and the magistrate was correct in rejecting the submission that there was no case to answer.

(ii) The Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 413, creates three offences (corruption, wilful oppression and extortion) which, although brought together in the one section, are nevertheless separate and distinct offences. The dismissal of the charge of extortion was therefore quite independent of, and could have no effect upon, the conviction of the charge of corruption. R. v. Appea (1951) 13 W.A.C.A. 143 applied.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. See also R. v. Sarpong and Frimpong (1957) 2 W.A .L.R. 342 on the nature of and effect of verdicts on separate counts.]

(iii) The charge amended by the prosecution had related to the third accused only and had moreover been dismissed. The accused, all of whom were in any case aware of the nature of the charges against them, had therefore in no way been prejudiced by the failure of the magistrate to require the third accused to plead afresh to the amended charge and no failure or miscarriage of justice had resulted therefrom. The failure of the magistrate to comply with the requirements of the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 172 (1), was therefore not fatal to the convictions. R. v. Eronini (1953) 14 W.A.C.A. 366 distinguished.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. In this case the decision in R. v. Eronini (3) was contrasted with Commissioner of Police v. Williams and Another (4). The latter case ended in a conviction on the amended charge and the West African Court of Appeal refused to quash. The point taken by the defence in Commissioner of Police v. Williams and Another turned on section 162 of the Criminal Procedure Code then in force but since repealed. This section did not require the court to call on the accused to plead to the amended charge, as does section 172 of the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 pg 336 of 1951: nevertheless it would appear from the principle involved in Com­missioner of Police v. Williams and Another, and from the remarks of the court in the present case, that where there has been a failure to observe the requirements of the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 172 (1), the test applied by the court will be whether or not such defect could have resulted in a failure 01' miscarriage of justice.]

APPEALS from the decision of Sarkadee-Adoo J. in the Divisional Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, on April 5, 1957, affirming convictions by the District Magistrate, Kumasi, on December 29, 1956. The first and second appellants were convicted of corruption by a public officer under the Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, ss. 413 and 429; the third accused was convicted of aiding and abetting the commission of this offence.

Scott, Senior Crown Counsel, for the Commissioner of Police.

Koi Larbi for the appellants.

The following judgment of the court was given by VAN LARE J. :

This is a second appeal from a conviction of the learned District Magistrate sitting at Kumasi where he convicted the first and second appellants, who, as public officers, offered to permit their conduct as such officers to be influenced by a gift of the sum of £110 received by them from one Yaw Barimah, and the third appellant for aiding and abetting the other appellants in the commission of the offence of corruption preferred against them.

The learned trial Magistrate found as a fact that the money had been paid to the first two accused to influence their conduct as public officers and that the third accused did aid and abet the crime charged. There is no valid criticism of this finding on the facts which were in no way controverted at the trial because when, at the close of the case for the prosecution, counsel submitted " no case," which was over­ruled, the accused elected to offer no evidence in defence and closed their case. They must be deemed therefore to have decided, when on the horns of a dilemma to take a chance of getting a possible conviction quashed on appeal if it were found that the submissions of " no case" was wrongly overruled:

" If at the close of the case for the prosecution he (i.e., the accused) or his counsel made a submission, which was wrongly overruled and then refused to take any part in the subsequent proceedings, he will be ' quite safe,' Le., apparently certain to get a possible conviction quashed on appeal" (R. v. Aldnpelu Ajani and Others (1)). pg .337

We are, however, of the opinion that in this case the submission of " no case" at the close of the case for the prosecution was not wrongly overruled and the conviction was right. However, the~ accused have prosecuted their appeal to this court, through the Divisional Court, Kumasi, on alleged grounds of irregularity which if upheld would be to exalt mere technicalities of no merit over the claims of justice.

The accused were tried on four charges preferred against them.

It can be observed from the particulars and wording of the various charges that the first count charged the first and second accused with the offence of corruption; the second count is against the third accused only, charging him with aiding and abetting the other accused to commit the offence of corruption. The wording of the third count is different from that of the first count, but it follows the language used in explaining "extortion" (s. 437 of the Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951) while the wording in the first count follows the language used in explaining "corruption" (s. 429 of the Criminal Code). It must be assumed that the difference in language is deliberate in order to distinguish the offence contemplated in the first count (i.e., corruption) from that contemplated in the third count (i.e., extortion). The third count relates to the first two accused only, while the fourth count relates to the third accused charging him with aiding and abetting the first two prisoners to commit the offence of extortion contemplated the third count.

That counts 3 and 4 are alternatives to counts 1 and 2 respectively cannot be in doubt as the totality of the evidence in support of counts 1 and 2 is the same as for counts 3 and 4. This is particularly made clear by the learned trial Magistrate when at the close of the case for the prosecution he ruled counts 3 and 4 out and did not call for any defence in respect of those counts on the ground that there was no case of extortion to answer but that there was a case of corruption. He rightly held that

.. the same set of facts cannot establish the offences of extortion and corruption at the same time."

This is a sound proposition in spite of the fact that it is only one and the same section, that is section 413 of the Criminal Code, which creates the offences of "corruption," .. wilful oppression," or "extortion" with the result that whether it is a charge of corruption or extortion it can only be shown to be contrary to section 413. Nevertheless each is a separate and distinct offence. In any indictment where such offences appear in separate counts a verdict on one count is entirely independent of a verdict on the other. Where two counts are tried together the position is the same as if two juries were trying two indictments. Each count is a separate indictment. The case of Edmund Gotfried Appea v. The King (2) therefore disposes of the arguments of counsel for the appellants in support of his grounds: pg 338

" That the conviction is bad in law:

    (a) Because the learned Magistrate having discharged the first and second appellants on the third count ought not to have convicted them on the first count; and the learned Magis­trate having discharged the third appellant on the fourth erred in convicting him on the second count.

    (b) Because the first and second appellants were charged with corruption in counts 1 and 3 in respect of the same trans­action and not extortion. "

    There is only one other ground, which has been argued at length and is as follows: 

    " That the whole proceedings is a nullity: because the prosecu­tion having amended the charge, the learned Magistrate ought to have called upon the accused person in question to plead to the altered charge; and his failure to do so is contrary to section 172 of the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951."

   The amendment complained of affected only the fourth count and in consequence, if any person at all, the third accused only. We are unable to appreciate the purpose of the objection because the amended charge had been dismissed by the trial Magistrate at the close of the case for the prosecution and no one had been convicted on such amended charge. We wonder therefore how any of the appellants can be aggrieved or in any way prejudiced by reason of the fact that when the fourth count was amended, even before the prosecution case started, the affected accused (i.e., the third) had not been asked to plead afresh after an amendment which was only to rectify the particular charge contemplated in the third count with which the third prisoner had been charged to aid and abet.

     But the significant point is that not only was the third count dismissed but also the fourth count (i.e., the amended count). It is not suggested that any of the appellants had been unaware of the charges upon which they were convicted, and in any case it was not on the amended charge that any of them had been convicted. It is manifestly clear, therefore, that there can be no irregularity or defect in the trial resulting in a miscarriage or failure of justice.

     Enough has been said to dispose of this ground, which must fail, but we desire to refer to an interesting point arising out of the Nigerian case of Edward Umez Eronini v. The Queen (3), to which counsel for the appellants has drawn our attention. It would appear that the case is an authority for the proposition that failure to plead afresh to an amended charge will render the proceedings null and void. Now that case is distinguishable from the present case in that there had been a conviction on the amended charge on which no fresh plea had been taken; in the present case there had been no conviction on the amended charge in respect of which an appeal may lie. But assuming that there pg 339 had been a conviction on the amended charge, without a fresh plea being taken, it is clear that the Nigerian case is in conflict with another decision of the same court, for in this it would appear that the principle applied by the West African Court of Appeal was to ascertain whether the failure to plead afresh meant a failure or miscarriage of justice. The case is-Commissioner of Police v. Plange Ogycabo Williams and Another (4), where the court in delivering judgment on a similar ground of appeal held as follows:

"Notwithstanding that counsel for the accused in the trial court stated that he had no objection to the amendments in question, we think that it would have been a wise precaution, in the circumstances, if the District Magistrate had caused the amended charges to be read to the hearing of the accused, and if he had then asked their counsel if he wanted an adjournment, or any \vitnesses to be recalled. Ho\yever, he was not asked to do either of these things by counsel for the accused. We have already indicated our opinion that the amendment was lawful. We go further and say that we have detected no irregularity in the procedure adopted by the District Magistrate with regard to it, and also that we are satisfied that it occasioned no miscarriage of justice."

We are satisfied that in this case each of the appellants were properly convicted and the appeal is accordingly dismissed.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
