  November 28, 1956

ASANTE v. COMPAGNIE FRANQAISE DE L'AFRIQUE OCCIDENTALE.

                                               West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                          Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.J.A

 

 

Fraud-Alienation in fraud of creditors-Fictitious transaction-Attempt to set aside order of attachment on ground of prior sale of goods concerned.

Fraud-Alienation in fraud of creditors-Transaction not bona fide and to the knowledge of the purchaser made with intent to delay and defraud the vendor's credit01's - Statute of Fraudulent Conveyances, 1571, 13 Eliz. 1, c. 5.

The defendant company had been supplying goods on credit to one Abire. In December, 1954, they issued a writ against Abire for the balance outstanding to them and at the same time sought and obtained an order for an interim attachment of his goods. The plaintiff later came forward with an application for the order to be set aside. This failed and he thereupon commenced a substantive action against the company for an order releasing the attachment and for damages. His case was that before the order of attachment was made he had become the sole owner of Abire's business by buying out Abire and his partners. He adduced as evidence, inter alia, two purported agreements dated April and June, 1954, and a receipt by Abire, dated November, 1954, purporting to acknowledge payment of the moneys fixed by the agreement for the purchase of the business. Notwithstanding these alleged agree­ments Abire had remained on the premises on which the business was conducted and had continued to obtain goods on credit from the defendant.

Held, (i) on the facts, the documents submitted by the plaintiff in support of his case were false and the transaction they purported to pg178 evidence was fictitious and was advanced' for the purposes of defrauding the creditors of Abire.

                (ii) Even if the alleged transaction was not fictitious, it was not a bona fide transaction and to the knowledge of the plaintiff was entered into with intent to delay and defraud Abire's creditors. It was therefore void by the provisions' of the Statute of Fraudulent Conveyances, 1571, 13 Eliz. 1, c. 5.

Twyne's Case ( (1602) 3 Co. Rep. 80) referred to.

    Cases referred to:

(1) Redda/way v. Banharn [1896] A.C. 199.

(2) Twyne's Case (1602) 3 Co. Rep. 80; 76 E.R. 809.

    APPEAL from the decision of Quashie-Idun J. on October 25, 1955, in the Divisional Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, granting an order releasing certain goods from attachment and awarding damages to the plaintiff.

Lutterodt for the plaintiff.

E. O. Asafu-Adjaye for the defendants.

    COUSSEY P. The question we had to decide on this appeal is whether the transaction; evidenced by an alleged agreement between Kwabena Abire, D. K. Awuah and Yaw Manu, all of Kumasi, of the first part, and the plaintiff, KojoAsante, of the second part, whereby the plaintiff purported to buy fof £837 6s. the stock-in-trade of the parties of the first part in the Coronation Spirit and Chop Bar was made to defraud Abire's creditors, the defendants, as they plead in paragraph 5·of the statement of defence.

     The plaintiff sued the defend~nts for an order releasing from attachment the stock of goods in the Coronation Bar. These had been attached by the defendants for satisfaction of any judgment that might be awarded against Kwabena Abire in the suit of Compagnie Franr;aise de l'Afrique Occidentale v. A bire , then pending in the Divisional Court, Kumasi. He also claimed, and, was awarded by the court below, £669 damages against the defendants for loss of business consequent on the attachment of the goods in the store.

      The plaintiff, a cocoa farmer, says he entered into an agreement on April 5, 1954, with Kwabena Abire, Yaw Manu and D. K. Awuah, whereby he paid £610 15s. as capital to enter as a partner the business of the Coronation Bar then run by the three other persons named. The contribution of the other parties is not stated in the agreement, although it provides that profits should be shared between them. Kwame Nkontire, alias D. K. Awuah, says in evidence that he and Manu jointly contributed £300 while Abire contributed £300 as capital. If this is true it is strange that it is not so stated in the agreement. The plaintiff states further that he paid two sums of £500 and £110 15s. before the agreement was signed. About two months later, he claims, pg179 he bought out the other partners by an agreement made on June 1, 1954, whereby he was to pay £100 at the end of July, 1954, and £237 os. at the end of November, 1954.

      Kwabena Abire had been obtaining goods on credit, from the defen­dant company, which between February and November, 1954, amounted to over £1,800. In December, 1954, the defendant company issued a writ against Abire for £1,739 15s. 2d., the balance due for goods supplied, and obtained an order for an interim attachment of the goods in the Coronation Bar as already stated. The plaintiff, Asante, was present in the Bar when the inventory of goods was taken by the court bailiff, but he did not then advance any claim to the goods. Later, however, he applied to the court for the attachment to be set aside. This application was refused, whereupon he instituted the present action.

       Before I come to the finding of the learned trial judge there are some other features of this case that must be referred to. the Coronation Bar premises were held by Abire on a tenancy agreement with the defendant company which contained a covenant on the part of Abire not to assign or under-let the premises. Notwith­standing this covenant the plaintiff says he became a sub-tenant of Abire without the consent of or notice to the defendants.

    The two agreements were not stamped by the plaintiff until after their production in evidence in the suit. The plaintiff did not take receipts for the sums of £500 and £110 15s. which he says he paid on account. He did not take an inventory of the goods he said he bought. According to the law the spirit licence of the premises should have been taken out in the plaintiff's name on July 1, 1954, if he bought the business on June 1 as he says. This was not done.

     The second agreement provides for the final payment of £237 0s. at the end of November, 1954. The plaintiff who, apparently, had defaulted as to the installment of £100 due at the end of July, 1954, was ready to pay the whole amount of £337 6s. before the due date. He produces a document receipt signed by Kwabena Abire and dated November 5 for £837 6s. in

" full payment of the cost of all the properties belonging to me and D. K. Awuah and Yaw Manu in our Coronation Spirit and Chop Bar which we have sold transferred and delivered to him. Henceforth all the properties in the said Coronation Spirit and Chop Bar remain the absolute properties of the said Panin Kojo Asante etc."

      On November 11, D. K. Awuah and Yaw Manu sued the plaintiff in the Kumasi Municipal Court for the £100, being all their property in the Coronation Spirit and Chop Bar

" bought by the defendant and defendant promised payment on instalment basis as per agreement in hand. pg180

    But the receipt of November 5, 1954, purports to evidence that the £100 had been paid already.

     A careful examination of the documents referred to and of the receipts for rents produced by the plaintiff satisfies me, without going into further details, that these are all fictitious documents and badges of the fraud designed to shield the debtor Abire, who in fact owned the goods, and to defeat his creditors of whom the defendant company were pressing their claim in December, 1954, Abire being perfectly aware long before that date that proceedings against him were imminent.

    If the defendants had expressly pleaded the Statute of Fraudulent Conveyance, 1571, 13 Eliz. 1, c. 5, the issue in the court below might have been clearer, but I think it was sufficiently raised by the state­ment of defence. The statute, which has been held to be applicable to the Gold Coast, recites in its preamble that

.. grants, alienations, conveyances, bonds, suits, judgments and executions, have been and are devised and contrived of malice fraud, covin, collusion or guile, to the end, purpose and intent, to delay, hinder or defraud creditors and others of their just and lawful actions,and so forth. The operative part of the statute proceeds to set aside every conveyance made to or for any intent or purpose before declared and expressed." Unfortunately the learned trial judge did not consider this aspect of the case. He came to the conclusion that Whatever the conduct of Abire had been towards the defen­dants, he accepted the evidence that the plaintiff bought the business and was an innocent purchaser for value. find myself in disagreement with this finding. In failing to stamp the agreements, assuming that they were made on the dates they bear, which I do not believe, the plaintiff was keeping secret the purchase of the business. The receipt was clearly prepared and was accepted by the plaintiff in anticipation of a claim against and seizure of Abire's goods, while the action in the Kumasi Municpal Court was designed to cloak the fraud with a semblance of circumstantial truth, and was badly timed .

.. But fraud is infinite in variety, sometimes it is audacious and unblushing; sometimes it pays a sort of homage to virtue, and then it is modest and retiring; it would be honesty itself if it could only afford it. But fraud is fraud all the same; and it is the fraud not the manner of it, which calls for the interposition of the court. "

   Per Lord Macnaghten in Reddaway v. Hanham (1) at p. 221.

 I think the transaction set up by the· plaintiff in support of his claim is entirely fictitious to his knowledge and, -if it is not fictitious, that it was embarked upon with intent to delay and hinder the pg181  creditors of Abire and had that effect and that it is therefore clearly fraudulent and void under the Statute of Elizabeth and with the principle of Twyne's Case (2).

 For these reasons we allowed the appeal, set aside the judgment of the court below and entered judgment for the defendants with costs in this court and in the court below to be taxed.

KORSAH C.J. I concur.

VERITY Ag.J.A. I concur.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
