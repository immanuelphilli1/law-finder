                           February 15, 1957                                

OJUOLAPE v. BENNING.

         Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Accra

                                                                              Windsor-Aubrey J.

 

 pg 253

    Practice - Sale by order of court - Sale after postponement without the notice given-Effect of sale-Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 51, r. 15.

    A sale of immovable property of a judgment-debtor had been ordered by the court but had been postponed as a result of interpleader pro­ceedings. On the rejection of the interpleader, some ten months after the date originally fixed for the sale, the registrar of the court advised the auctioneer, by telegram, to proceed with the sale. The auctioneer then sold the property, but without giving a renewed twenty-one days' public notice of the sale. The judgment-debtor asked that the sale be set aside on the ground that there had been a material irregularity in the way in which it had been conducted.

    Held, where a sale under an order of the court is postponed indefinitely by reason of further judicial proceedings then, notwith­standing the fact that public notice of the sale has once been given in accordance with the provisions of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 51, r. 15, an order to proceed with the postponed sale operates to require a re-compliance with the rule so that the sale shall be re-advertised. Okai v. O. J. Reindorf (1914) Divisional and Full Court Judgments, 1919, p. 75, distinguished.          

    Case referred to :

       (1) Okai v. O. J. Reindorf (1914) Divisional and Full Court Judgments, 1919, p. 75.

          APPLICATION by a judgment-debtor for the setting aside of a sale under an order of the court on the ground of a material irregularity in the conduct of the sale.

   'The plaintiff judgment-creditor appeared in person.

   Doe for the defendant.

    Koi Larbi for the auctioneer and purchaser.

   WINDSOR-AUBREY J. This is an application to set aside a sale of immovable property on the grounds of material irregularity. The only material irregularity is that the sale was made without proper notice. Order 51, Rule 15, prescribes that there shall be at least twenty-one days' notice of sale. The applicant alleges that his farm is worth £1,000 and that the absence of proper notice resulted in the farm realizing only £400. He is not a reliable witness as he says his farm of 12·28 acres has roughly a million trees on it which is manifestly absurd. pg 254

What happened is that the sale was originally due to take place on January 26, 1956, but, as a result of interpleader proceedings, the sale was postponed indefinitely. Eventually the interpleader was rejected and on October 12, 1956, the Registrar sent to the auctioneer a telegram as follows:

.Reference case Ojuolape versus Benning interpleader struck out proceed with execution letter follows."

     The auctioneer took this to mean that the sale could then proceed without notice and on October 16, 1956, went to the farm and sold it, the only notice being the ringing of the auctioneer's bell on the day of the sale at Nsaba and the village of Obodam. It is obvious that in such circumstances a property is highly unlikely to be sold at anything approaching its real value and I am most surprised that an auctioneer should adopt such a procedure. Koi Larbi, counsel for the auctioneer, relies on a Government handbook on Execution at p. 44 to justify this course, but his client has totally misconstrued' the relevant passage which merely states that when a sale is' postponed from day to day, fresh notices of sale are not necessary. Clearly all interested persons will be present on the day fixed for sale and if they are then told the sale will take place next day from the common sense point of view there is no practical necessity for a fresh notice. In this sale however, after an indefinite postponement and after a lapse of months the auctioneer sells without notice.

Order 51, Rule 15, does not expressly deal with the necessity for notice when a sale is postponed because of interpleader proceedings, but it does say no sale shall take place without at least twenty-one days' notice. In my opinion this rule applies to every sale where there has been an indefinite postponement. I realize that the case of Okai v. O. J. Reindorf (1) is against the view I have propounded above, but it is founded on different rules than these in force now so I am not bound to follow it.

I hold there was a material irregularity and set aside the sale. The property will be resold on proper notice. The purchaser is, of course, entitled to his money back, but I give the judgment-creditor time to pay the purchaser until the proceeds of the subsequent sale are paid into court. If the purchaser wishes to re-bid at the subsequent sale he is at liberty to do so, the money he has already paid being treated as a deposit in account of the purchase price. The applicant is entitled to his costs, to be paid by the auctioneer.

Application granted.

S. G. D.
