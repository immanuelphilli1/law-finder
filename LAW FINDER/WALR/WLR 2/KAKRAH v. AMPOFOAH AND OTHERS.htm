                             April 12, 1957                        

                                                            KAKRAH v. AMPOFOAH AND OTHERS

                  Supreme Court of Ghana, Central Judicial Division, Land Court, Cape Coast

Adumua-Bossman J.

 

 

Native Law and Custom-Stool land-Devolution of stool land on death 'intestate of stranger holding for a determinable estate (usufructuary interest)-Obligations between stool and successors in title to stranger.

    The plaintiff stool had granted land to a stranger for an estate at will subject to the payment of an annual rent, or tribute, of £2. On the death intestate of the stranger his successor, who was duly appointed according to native law and custom, apportioned the deceased's estate, and the stool land the subject of this dispute thereon passed to the children of the deceased, the defendants in the action, as his successors in title. The stool, at a later date, approached the children for financial contribution (i.e., a fine) towards a stool debt, and this the children refused to pay. The stool thereupon claimed, in effect, that the land had reverted to the stool upon the death of the original grantee, and demanded, without apparently going so far as to assert that the children were not entitled as of right to succeed to their father's estate, that the children should enter into a new agreement by which an increased annual rent would be payable. This also the children refused to do, claiming that they were the successors in title of their father as of right, that they succeeded on the terms of the original grant, and that the stool had no right to intervene. The stool thereupon commenced this action to compel the children to enter into the new agreement pg 304

Held, (i) the stool debt, on account of which the fine was sought from the children, had not been incurred in connection with the stool land occupied by the children, nor did it appear that the deceased father would have been liable to make a contribution to it. Therefore no fine could. be levied on the children for this purpose.

(ii) The children, having been duly appointed as successors in title to their father, succeeded as of right to his estate in the stool lands granted to him, and the stool had no right to intervene.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It does not yet appear to have been decided what would have been the effect of a devise by will where the testator, as a stranger-' grantee, held a determinable estate in stool land and the advance consent of the stool had not been obtained. Whether or not the devise was to a member of the deceased's family would not appear to be relevant in this situation, for in either case the devisee would be a stranger vis-a-vis the stool.]

APPEAL from a decision of the Agona Kwanyaku Native Court" B " on November 19, 1956, dismissing the claim by the plaintiff stool for an order for the execution of an agreement dealing with stool land.

Abadoo for the plaintiff.

Dua Sekyi for the defendants.

ADUMUA-BoSSMAN J. This appeal concerns stool land cultivated by the late James Obodai, an Akyem Abuakwa person who appears to have settled with some relatives in Nyakrom and to have cultivated Nyakrom Stool forest land subject to the payment of yearly tolls of £2 to the representative of the stool.

The evidence establishes that the successor of the said James Obodai upon his death intestate was by native law and custom, a Mr. Forster, but that as a result of litigation between the children of the late James Obodai and the successor, Mr. Forster; the latter allotted or appor­tioned to the children part of the landed properties of the deceased, including:

" the land with Cocoa Farms thereon at ' Obotomfo ' in Nyakrom which is the subject-matter of this suit; with directions to the said children to discharge the' burdens' on the land," pg 305  i.e., to pay the yearly tolls payable in respect of the tenancy. Upon the representative of the stool writing to enquire: to know the present condition of our land lying and situate at Swedru K wanmu,. which your predecessor worked on at·' Obo· tomfo,' who is now working it?" the successor wrote and said " inter alia" : my children are on the land, and any case concerning the land must be referred to me .• ,

   The representative of the stool then applied to the children (the defendants in this case) for assistance in paying some stool debt-a debt unconnected with any litigation concerning the particular stool land occupied by them. They refused to assist. The stool next sought to increase from £2 to £12 per annum the yearly toll payable in respect of the land, but this also the children refused to agree to. Thereupon the representative of the stool issued the writ in this case, in effect claiming an order to compel the children to sign an agreement accepting the new rate imposed, and also claiming an order that a portion of the land farmed by two Awowin strangers called Obo and Abeh should be declared as having reverted to the stool.

  As to this latter claim, the trial court held, rightly in my view, that the two men were labourers of the late Obodai and farmed for him.

   As to the question of imposing a new rate, the trial court held, also rightly in my view, that the stool could not vary the agreement made with the late Obodai.

     The trial court however, raised the further question as to who should have been sued about the land, and they decided that in view of the correspondence between the stool's representative and the successor, the stool's representative was wrong in suing the children. With this I cannot agree. The evidence establishes that the successor allotted and gave the children a portion of their late father's estate-as he is entitled, and indeed may be liable, by custom to do-and thereby made the children owners of that portion, with all the burdens attached to the land-l hold therefore, that it is the children as owners whom the stool is entitled to sue and that they were rightly. sued in this case. I would expunge that part of the decision of the trial court. Subject to that, the appeal will be dismissed. Costs of the defendants. _

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
