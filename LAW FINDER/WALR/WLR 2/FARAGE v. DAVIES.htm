Mortgage-Illegality-Advances by moneylender-Failure to observe require­ments of Moneylenders Ordinance, c. 178, s.~. 3 and 12.

The plaintiff sought to recover £548 which he alleged to be due to him under a mortgage entered into by the defendant. The defendant claimed that the mortgage deed did not accurately reflect the transaction between her and the plaintiff. She claimed that the principal of the Joan was £400 and that to this £108 was added by way of interest and £40 for legal expenses, thus making up the £548. The plaintiff also claimed that the mortgage deed did not represent the transaction. He claimed that he had supplied goods to the value of £548 to the defendant and had taken the mortgage as security.

Held, (i) on the evidence the plaintiff had advanced money at interest, and not goods, to the defendant. As the Moneylenders Ordinance, c. 178, s. 3, provides that a person who lends money at interest shall be presumed a moneylender until the contrary is proved, and as the plaintiff had not rebutted this presumption, then he was a moneylender within the terms of the Ordinance.

(ii) The mortgage deed was not a memorandum supplied to the borrower, as is required by section 12 (1) of the Ordinance, and, even if it could be regarded as such a memorandum, it did not comply with section 12 (3) of the Ordinance in that it failed to specify the amount of the principal of the loan or the percentage rate of interest per annum or the amount of such interest. The mortgage deed was, therefore, by the provisions of section 12 (1) of the Ordinance, unenforceable.

CLAIM by the plaintiff to recover the sum of £548 due from the defendant under a deed of mortgage.

Forster for the plaintiff.

The defendant appeared in person.

MILES C.J. This is a claim for £548 alleged to be due under a mortgage made between the plaintiff and the defendant and dated October 1, 1955. By this mortgage the defendant mortgaged the leasehold premises in which she resides ',to the plaintiff and the material recital in the mortgage is in the following terms:

" AND WHEREAS the Lender has agreed with the Borrower at the request of the Borrower to lend to her the sum of £548. O. 0 (Five hundred and forty-eight pounds) Sterling of lawful money upon having the repayment thereof secured in manner hereinafter appearing. "

According to the plaintiff's own evidence this recital is not an accurate statement of the transaction at all. He says that on pg 325 September 15, 1955, the defendant came with one Badou Lowe and asked him for money. She said she had 'a property which she could mortgage as security for the loan. The plaintiff said that he told her he was unable t() supply her with money but could let her have some goods. Subsequently, according to him, the defendant agreed to this arrangement and he supplied her with bags of sugar, cases of soap, and extra· strong mints to the value of £548 on September 15. The defendant signed an invoice drawn up by the plaintiff's clerk, Mr. Williams, which contains this statement, " goods supplied to the value of £548. " The items are not detailed.

Two days later the plaintiff says that the defendant came to him and said that if she sold the goods in the town she would make a loss, and at her request he bought the goods back from her for the sum of £440 which he paid in cash. On receipt of the cash she signed the mortgage deed. He explains the difference between the sum of £440 and £548 as representing his profit on the transaction. If this is so I fail to understand why the mortgage deed was drawn up in the terms that it was and why the true facts were not stated. On the plaintiff's own showing the recital in the deed was not an accurate statement of the position. There were a number of subsequent transactions for which invoices have been produced. One of these, namely the invoice for May 11, 1956, is stated t() be for cash, but the plaintiff says that this is a mistake on the part of his clerk. I am satisfied that the defendant is telling the truth when she says that the transaction was a loan of cash. A further invoice, number 32, for £16 on May 15 is stated to be for" goods supplied £16." The defendant says also that this was a loan of money which she required for the purpose of getting a new crankshaft for her car. Her evidence receives some support from Mr. Williams, who was the former clerk of the plaintiff, and I accept it. The total amount of cash lent and goods supplied up to May 16, 1950, was £68 8s. Subsequently the defendant was sent a draft legal charge to sign as security for the sum of £68. She refused to sign this on the ground that once again the receipt was inaccurate in that it stated that this sum was wholly composed of money advanced.

The defendant's account of the circumstances in which the mortgage deed was entered into was as follows. She said that she wanted a loan from him of £400 which the plaintiff promised to let her have on September 15 in Mr. Forster's office. On the morning of that day she called at Mr. Forster's office and she says that Mr. Forster asked if' she was told of the method used by Mr. Farage in giving out loans. She replied that this had been explained to her and that it was on an interest basis. She said that the £400 was placed before her on Mr. Forster's table. She asked :Mr. Forster how much his fee would be towards the mortgage, and he said £50. After some discus­sion and on the representations of Mr. Farage, Mr. Forster agreed pg 326 to reduce his fee to £40'and this sum was given to him by Mr. Farage, and a receipt made out for it. This receipt appears to be expressed " for presentation and registration of document of mortgage" and is consistent with her story. The defendant says that thereupon the interest on £440 was calculated and amounted to £108 so that the figure of £548 was inserted in the mortgage deed. In other words the defendant's case is that the sum of £548 represents principal and interest, Upon the facts I have to decide which of the two parties is to be believed, and I have no hesitation in accepting the evidence of the defendant who impressed me as an eminently honest witness. I do not consider it likely that the defendant would have any use for £548 worth of goods which would take a considerable time to sell when she required the money urgently for the purpose of purchasing a house. It also strikes me as improbable that she would ~ave gone back a mere two days after the goods had been supplied and asked the plaintiff to buy them back. Her evidence is supported by Mr. Williams, the clerk who prepared the invoice for £548 and who says that no goods were sold to her on that occasion. He makes the point which appears to me a forcible one that the invoice does not specify the goods, and he said that this was the manner in which he was instructed to make out invoices when money was lent by Mr. Farage. Now Mr. Williams is a convicted thief, having been sentenced to imprisonment for stealing from Mr. Farage, but after carefully weighing his evidence, which seems to me to be supported by the form of the document, I have no hesitation in saying that I believe him to be speaking the truth as to this. I find as a fact that the sum of £548 represents £440 money lent as principal and £108 interest.

On this state of affairs what is the legal position? In this connection I have to consider the provision of the Moneylender's Ordinance, c. 178. Section 3 provides as follows:

" Save as excepted in paragraphs (a), (b), (0), (d) and (e) of the definition of ' moneylender' in section 2 any person who lends money at interest or who lends a sum of money in consideration of a larger sum being repaid shall be presumed to be a money­lender until the contrary is proved."

The question is whether the plaintiff has rebutted the presumption raised by this section. He has not done so to my satisfaction. According to the evidence of Mr. Williams there had been a number of occasions when the plaintiff had advanced money and on which Mr. Williams was instructed to make out an invoice for goods supplied. Evidence was given as to a transaction with a Mr. Silva, but this is somewhat different in nature from the transaction with the plaintiff and I do not think that any inference adverse to the plaintiff should be drawn from that. The Registrar-General has produced a mortgage  pg 327 dated May 20, 1956, from which it appears that the plaintiff lent the sum of £300 to one M 'Bunguma Samba on mortgage. The mortgagor has not been called but on the face of it this appears to be a loan of money. It is clear to my mind that at the material time the plaintiff was carrying on the business of a moneylender as part of his activities .

. The plaintiff having failed to rebut the presumptions of law in section 3, this transaction is governed by the provisions of the Money­lenders Ordinance. It is clear that in this case the provisions of section 12 of the Ordinance have not been complied with since first, no memorandum was applied to the defendant as required by sub­section (1) and also since the mortgage deed, even if it could be regarded as a memorandum, does not specify the amount of the principal or the rate of interest per centum per annum or the amount of such interest. The mortgage is unenforceable and there will be judgment for the defendant with costs.

No doubt from one point of view this may be considered hard on the plaintiff, who has admittedly lent £440 to the defendant which he has not been repaid, but if people will resort to subterfuges of this nature they have only themselves to blame if they come to grief.

Judgment for the defendant.

S. G. D.
