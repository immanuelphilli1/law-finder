                                                                      REGINA v. JAGNE AND N'JIE.

                                                               High Court of the Protectorate of Gambia

                                                                                         Miles C.J.

 

  Criminal Law-Theft-Charge alleging a general deficiency over a period­ Criminal Procedure Code, c. 23, s. 158.

    Criminal Law-Accessory after the fact-Charge of aiding and abetting principal- Whether possible to convict as accessory-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 23, s. 155.

The first accused was charged with theft when employed as a public servant: the second accused was charged with aiding and abetting the first accused in the commission of that offence and also, on a second count, with theft. The charge against the first accused alleged a general deficiency of a specified sum of money over a period of time. It appeared from the evidence that the first accused, with the assistance of the second accused, seized an opportunity of fabricating a situation which involved the second accused in being caught in the act of stealing a small sum of money (part of the general deficiency alleged) and which suggested an explanation for the whole deficiency. It was argued on behalf of the first accused, inter alia, that the charge was bad in that it did not allege the specific sums misappropriated and the dates on which these misappropriations occurred.

Held, (i) the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 23, s. 158, permits (in the case of a public ~servant and certain other categories of persons), on a pg116 charge of stealing, the specification of a gross sum and the dates within which the offence is alleged to have been committed without the need for specifying particular items or exact dates.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The Criminal Procedure Code, s. 158, refers to the offences created by the Criminal Code, ss. 257, 258, 259 and 260. These are all offences in which a particular relationship between the person accused and the person who suffers loss aggravates the offence of theft. Section 158 of the Criminal Procedure Code does not lie in ordinary cases of theft and therefore the following remarks of the court in H. v. Tomlin are apposite in such ca ses :

" We desire to make it plain in conclusion ... that, in the ordinary case, where it is possible to trace the individual items and to prove a conversion of individual property or money, it is undesirable to include them all in a count alleging a general deficiency. What we are not willing to do is to elevate a rule of practice, applicable to circumstances where it may be required to avoid injustice, into a rule of law applicable to circumstances in which it will defeat justice."

B. v. Tomlin is authority for saying that where it is impossible to split up an aggregate sum and to trace individual items it is proper to include the total sum in a count alleging a general deficiency.]

(ii) On the evidence the second accused was not associated with the first defendant's theft until after this theft had taken place. He was therefore in the position of an accessory after the fact (and should have been charged as such), not of one who aided and abetted the principal. Being an accessory after the fact does not, within the meaning of the' Criminal Procedure Code, s. 155, form an " integral part" of the offence with which the first accused was charged and neither section 155 nor any other section of the Criminal Procedure Code could cure the defect in the first charge against the second accused.

Cases referred to:

(1) R. v. Tomlin [1954] 2 All E.R. 272.

(2) R. v. Levy [1912] 1 K.B. 158; 7 Cr.App.R. 61; 106 L.T. 192.

The first accused is employed by the Gambia, Government and is a member of the Accountant General's Department, which he joined in 1952. He has been a government servant since January 1, 1934. Prior to 1952 he was a divisional clerk in charge of the office of the Commissioner, MacCarthy Island Division, Georgetown. He first had charge of cash on May 1, 1943.

Since 1952 he has held the post of accounting officer at Georgetown.

In that capacity it was his duty to make payments in respect of wages, salaries, government contracts etc., and to receive tax moneys and others. These included deposits from commercial firms in the division. These firms periodically made payments to Jagne for the purpose of remission to Bathurst; there being no bank at Georgetown. As accounting officer, Jagne kept a cash book in which it was his duty to enter all payments and receipts. After he had balanced the cash book for each month he was required by Financial Instructions (Appendix 4, paragraph 10) to detach the original pages of the cash book and forward them to the treasury through the sub-accountant. Jagne had a safe in which he kept the money and of which he retained the key. He also had an office of which the key was in his possession.

The sub-accountant for the division is the Commissioner who also holds a certain amount of cash in his own safe. It was the duty of the accounting officer to make payments of any cash in excess of his requirements to the Commissioner and he also drew money from the Commissioner to meet necessary payments.

The second accused, Ebrima N'Jie, at the'material time was living in Jagne's house and had been so doing for thirteen months prior to May 1, 19M. He is not related in any way to Jagne and his permanent home is at Nyorro in the Senegal. He is apparently acquainted with some of Jagne's relatives who live at Nyorro and on the strength of this J agne gave him accommodation, employed him as his farmer and provided him with food.

The deficiency first came to light on May 1, 1956. At about 3.30 a.m. on that morning the night watchman, Serima Krubally, reported to Jagne that he was sick. Jagne took him to the dispensary, where he was found to have a temperature of 103 degrees, and he was given some medicine and excused duty .. Jagne then took the watchman to the police station and explained the position to P.C. Davies, the officer on duty. The watchman, Jagne and P.C. Davies thereupon inspected the Commissioner's office and found everything in order. Jagne then returned to his house.

At 6.20 a.m. Jagne reported to F.C Davies that Ebrima N'Jie had taken some of his clothes and those of his wife and was missing from the house. These clothes included Jagne's trousers, in which were the keys of his office and safe. P.C. Davies said that on the suggestion of Jagne they went to the office in case N'Jie had broken in. The office is situated about 75 yards from the police station. On arrival pg118 at the office the door was found to be open and N'Jie was inside standing in front of the safe. He was taking money out of the safe and putting it into his pocket. P.C. Davies searched N'Jie and found the sum of £2 19s. 6d. in his pocket, in coins. The only money left in the safe was £4 in coins in a tray. Jagne was asked by P.C. Davies how much money there was in the safe and he said the amount was over £3,000. Later on Jagne calculated the amount missing more exactly and found it to be £3,230 10s. 6d. The accused N'Jie was, of course, arrested by P.C. Davies. It will be necessary to consider the events of May 1 in more detail later.

The prosecution base their case against the first accused on a number of serious irregularities on his part in the conduct of his duties which came to light upon investigation by the principal auditor and Mr. Thomas. Taken by itself I would not be disposed to rega the omission to enter these receipts on the due dates as anything mar, than evidence of slackness. pg119

 

A far more serious irregularity relied upon by the prosecution is the holding by Jagne throughout the period covered by the charge of cash balances which were substantially in excess of his requirements. Paragraph 8 of Appendix 4 (2) of Financial Instructions states:

" The maximum cash balance to be held by a cashier should be kept as low as possible and may not exceed £200 except with the approval of the Accountant-General or when the Commissioner is. out of his station."

It also appears that a number of Commis­sioners have suggested that the amount should be raised to as much as £800 or £1,000 but in fact the :Financial Instructions requires collectors of revenue to balance all cash books and to account for the cash in hand at the close of each day's business. The amount should be recorded on a daily cash statement. No daily cash statements have been made out by Jagne after the month of January, 1955. His explanation is that nobody ever asked to see them and that he was too busy to balance his cash daily.

I have most carefully considered the evidence of the two accused but their story strikes me as wholly unconvincing. N'Jie was not a satisfactory witness. His evidence contained a number of incon· sistencies in his account of his own movements on the morning of May 1. Moreover, he says that he removed the clothing from J agne's room before breakfast, whereas both J agne and his wife say it was not until after breakfast, when Jagne's wife woke up, that the clothing was found to be missing. Obviously if it had been removed before breakfast they would have noticed the fact at breakfast time. It is odd that Jagne, although he at once suspected that N'Jie had gone to the office to steal, went first to the police station, passing the office on his way when he must have noticed that the door was open. N'Jie said that he saw J agne pass the office on his way to the police station yet he made not attempt to escape. The only inference I can draw from this is that both accused were anxious that when the police arrived N' Jie should be found in the office.

I cannot accept N'Jie's statement that he made three visits to the office that morning. If what he says is true he had already taken a large sum of money in notes and two bags of coins. It is hardly likely that he would return to the office simply for the purpose of taking £6 odd that remained in view of the risk that he would run in so doing. The police station was less. than 100 yards away and, so far as he, N'Jie, knew, the night watchman was still on duty.

I do not believe the statement that he hid the notes under the government wharf. Why should he hide them in a different place pg127 from the rest of the money? If there was to be any difference in this disposition one would have expected him to hide the notes in the firewood and the bags of coin at the wharf where they would be less likely to be washed away by the tide. The notes would also be easier to conceal when he was carrying them about. There is also some evidence that there was sand on some of the coins although there was no sand in the place where they were found. This indicates that they had been in some other place previously. There· is no doubt in my mind that the reason why N'Jie said nothing about the government wharf until midnight was that he knew perfectly well that if the police went and searched the government wharf in the morning they would find nothing there. The whole occurrence bears the marks of fabrica­tion. It would be hard to conceive a more convenient time for such a theft to have occurred from Jagne's point of view. Jagne would have realized that the new Commissioner would be sure to check his cash on 1\lay 1. I see no reason to doubt the genuineness of the night watchman's illness and it is no doubt this that presented Jagne with an opportunity to stage the theft. The fact that it was not successful is possibly due to the plan being hastily concocted with the result that the two accused did not have sufficient time to agree upon their story. What the precise arrangement was between the two accused, whether N'Jie was to receive £158 8s. Od. as a reward for his part in the affair, are matters which are only known to the accused themselves. What­ever the truth with regard to this may be, I am satisfied beyond any doubt whatever that on the morning .of May 1, 1956, the only money in the safe was the sum of £6 19s. 6d. and that the missing balance had been stolen prior to May 1 by J agne. I have also no doubt that temptation to steal presented itself when Jagne found that Mr. Fyfe, the previ.ous Commissioner, did not at any time check his cash.

 

Mr. N'Jie has argued that the charge against the first accused is defective in that it charges the theft of a total deficiency; and he cited a number of authorities. I do not propose to deal with these because the position in this country, whatever may be the law in England, is governed by secti.on 158 of the Criminal Procedure Code. r would add that even in England it is now settled that, where it is not possible to trace a particular item or prove a theft on any particular date, it is permissible to charge the accused with having stolen a gross sum between certain specific dates, see R. v. Tomlin (1). It is, of course, the case here that the prosecution cannot prove that any particular sum was stolen on any particular date but that is not necessary. The evidence in this case is admittedly circumstantial, but the irregularities, to which I have referred, particularly the unneces­sarily large cash balances held. by Jagne coupled with the fact that, when he required to make payments, on a number of occasions, he had to obtain money either from the Commissioner or other sources despite the large book balances are, in my opinion, consistent only pg128 with his guilt. I am driven to the conclusion that these large book balances existed only on paper.

I find myself in some difficulty in regard to the charge so far as the second accused is concerned. On the first count the second accused has been charged with aiding and abetting the first accused in the commission of the theft of £3,230 14s. 0d. There is no evidence what­soever that the second accused aided and abetted Jagne in the actual theft of any of the money and it is not in fact the case for the prosecu· tion that he did, as Mr. Attorney conceded. What is said by the prosecution is that the second accused assisted J agne to cover up the theft by faking an office breaking. On that footing, N'Jie was not a principal in the second degree, but an accessory after the fact since the felony had already been completed. A person who gives any assistance whatever to one known to be a felon in order to prevent his being apprehended, or tried, or suffering the punishment to which he is condemned is an accessory after the fact, see R. v. Levy (2). It does not seem to me that there is any provision in the Criminal Proce· dure Code which enables the court to convict a person charged with aiding and abetting of being an accessory after the fact. Mr. Attorney has suggested that section 155 is applicable but I do not think it can be said that being an accessory after the fact is an element in the charge of theft. I accordingly find the second accused not guilty on the first count.

On the second count I am satisfied that the second accused stole the sum of £2 19s. 6d. and no more. I find him guilty of stealing that sum and convict him under section 252 of the Criminal Code.

It appears from Mr. Cook's evidence that the deficiency of £3,230 14s. 0d. was arrived at after counting the cash found in the safe which amounted to £4 Os. 0d. The deficiency presumably includes the sum of £2 19s. Bd. found on the person of N'Jie and this must be excluded from the £3,230 14s. 0d. I find the first accused guilty of stealing £3',227 14s. Bd. and convict him under sections 252 and 257 of the Criminal Code.

First accused found guilty and convicted. Second accused found guilty and convicted on second charge: first charge dismissed.
