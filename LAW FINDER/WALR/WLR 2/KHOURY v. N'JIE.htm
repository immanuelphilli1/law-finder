                                                                                   KHOURY v. N'JIE.                                    

                                                                    Supreme Court of the Colony of the Gambia  

Miles C.J

 

   pg175

      Contract-Contract under seal-When variable by subsequent agreement Consideration.

The plaintiff sold a house, by agreement under seal, to the defendant.

Of the purchase price of £18,000, £16,705 was paid on the agreement, the balance being payable some two months later, on completion. Within this period a further £1,000 was paid, leaving £295 outstanding: the plaintiff sought to recover this latter sum. The defendant pleaded an oral agreement subsequent to the one under seal whereby the plaintiff agreed to forgo £295 in consideration of receiving the £1,000 before it was strictly due and also of being allowed to remain in occupation rent-free until the date for completion.

Held, (i) a promise to allow the plaintiff to remain in possession rent free until the date of completion could not constitute valuable considera­tion to support a subsequent oral agreement since the plaintiff was, in any case, entitled to possession until completion.

(ii) Although an agreement to accept a smaller sum in satisfaction of a debt of a liquidated. sum is a nudum pactum, as unsupported by consideration, the payment of a debt, or part of it, before the due date for payment constitutes sufficient consideration to support an agreement.

(iii) Although at common law a contract under seal could not be varied by oral agreement since the Supreme Court of Judicature (Con­solidation) Act, 1925, s. 44, the rule of equity has prevailed whereby a contract under seal can be thus varied.

    Cases referred to :

(1) Steeds v. Steeds (1889) 22 Q.B.D. 537.

(2) Berry v. Berry [1929] 2 K.B. 316.

   ACTION by the plaintiff to recover £295 as the balance due under a contract under seal.

The plaintiff appeared in person. N'Jie for the defendant.

    MILES C.J. In this case the plaintiff claims from the defendant the sum of £295 being the balance due upon a promissory note dated September 29, 1953. By an agreement under seal dated Septem­ber 29 the defendant had purchased from the plaintiff the premises No. 6 Wellington Street, Bathurst, for the sum of £18,000 of which £16,705 were then paid. The deed provided that the purchase should be completed on December 31, 1953, by the payment of the balance of £1,295.

      The defence to this claim is that in the month of October, 1953, the plaintiff agreed to forgo the balance of £295 in consideration of pg176  
the defendant paying him the sum of £1,000 before it was due and also of his allowing the plaintiff to remain in occupation of No. 6 Wellington Street, Bathurst, rent free until the end of December, 1953. The sum of £1,000 was in fact paid by the defendant on October 24, 1953. The plaintiff denies that any such agreement was made.

The defendant has called three witnesses who were present when the alleged agreement was arrived at and I can find no valid reason for rejecting their evidence. One of the witnesses was in fact the plaintiff's uncle. All the probabilities seem to support the defendant's case. The plaintiff was anxious to leave in a very short time for the Lebanon where his father was ill, and I have no doubt that Mr. Salim Khoury and Alhaji Momodou Matarr N'Jie are telling the truth when they say that the plaintiff informed them that he is was not intending to return to the Gambia. The plaintiff himself was very evasive on that point. It is clear, however, that he was urgently in need of money and it is in my opinion unlikely that the defendant would have agreed to pay £1,000 before it was due unless he was getting some­thing in return. One difficulty in this case is that for some peculiar reason this agreement was not put into writing. Indeed, the plaintiff has produced what purports to be a receipt for £1,000 which states that it was paid" on account on the sum of £1,295." Mr. NJie is unable to explain how the receipt was made in these terms. On the other hand, a written memorandum was drawn up on October 27, 1953, by Mr. P. S. N'Jie, the defendant's solicitor, whereby the plaintiff gave an undertaking that he would pay no rent in the months of November and December in respect of No. 6 Wellington Street. If there has been no variation of the deed of September 29 I cannot see how this memorandum should have been necessary since under that deed completion was not due to take place until December 31, 1953, and the plaintiff would therefore not be liable to pay rent for the months of November and December in any event. This memorandum does therefore support the defendant's case.

There is the further fact that on his return from the Lebanon in December, 1954, the plaintiff took a tenancy of certain premises from the defendant at a rent of £360 a year but he paid the rent in full. If the defendant was still owing him £295 he could have deducted this from the rent, but he did not do so. indeed, he waited for over eighteen months after his return from the Lebanon before instituting proceed­ings. I find as a fact therefore that on or about October 24, 1953, the plaintiff accepted the sum of £1,000 in full satisfaction of the balance then owing. I find also that it was at the plaintiff's instigation that this money was paid at that time.

Two questions of law have to be considered upon this finding of fact. The first is whether there was any consideration for the agree­ment. Generally speaking, an agreement to accept a smaller sum in  pg177 payment of a debt of a liquidated amount is a nudum pactum, but there is an exception to this rule in the case of a payment of a part of the sum on a day before it was due. In the present case therefore there was consideration. The second question is whether an agree­ment under seal can be varied by an oral agreement. At common law a deed could not be varied by a simple contract but in equity the position was otherwise, and since the Supreme Court of Judicature Act, 1873, the rule in equity prevails. It is now well settled that an oral variation or cancellation is an answer to an action on a deed (see Steeds v. Steeds (1) and Berry v. Berry (2)). For these reasons, therefore, the plaintiff's claim fails.

  There will be judgment for the defendant with costs.

Judgment for the defendant.

S. G. D.
