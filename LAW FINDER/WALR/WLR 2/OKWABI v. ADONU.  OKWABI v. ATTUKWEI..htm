                           February 27, 1957                                

OKWABI v. ADONU. 

                                              OKWABI v. ATTUKWEI.

                                 West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                                Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.J.A.

 

 

Native Law and Custom-Succession-Devise by will-Land purchased by testator from money earned by joint efforts of testator and his son­Whether land the sole property of testator or held by him in trust for himself and his son.

The plaintiff in these two consolidated suits was the successor, duly appointed by native custom, of the late Kortey KoIL The defendant Attukwei was the son of Kortey Kofi and during his father's life had pg 269 worked for him. The money earned by father and son had been held by the father and with it he had purchased land which he devised by will upon trusts for the members of his maternal family (i.e., excluding the son). The son, who had been in possession of part of the land concerned during the lifetime of his father, claimed that the devise was ineffective in that it purported to treat the land concerned as the individually owned property of the testator, whereas it should, in fact, be treated as being held by the testator either in trust for himself and his son as joint tenants, or in trust for the son absolutely. On the refusal of the successor to accept this, the son interfered with the use of the land and ordered the other tenants thereof not to pay rent to the plaintiff. The defendant in the first action was one such tenant who paid his rent to the defendant in the second action. The plaintiff thereupon brought this action claiming damages for trespass, an injunction, and an order for ejectment, against both defendants, and asking for a declaration of title.

Held, it is a common feature of family life that a son will work with and for his father, and therefore, in the absence of strong evidence to the contrary, no presumption will be raised that property, obtained by the joint efforts of father and son and held by the father, is held by the father in trust for father and son as joint tenants or in trust for the son absolutely .

. APPEAL from the decision of Quashie-Idun J., on June 1, 1956, in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, granting the claim of the plaintiff for damages for trespass, for an injunction and for an order of ejectment, against both defendants, and for a declaration of title.

E. O. Asafu-Adjaye for the plaintiff.

Danquah for the defendants.

KORSAH C.J. This is an appeal from the judgment of the Land Court in two consolidated suits in which the plaintiff, for himself and on behalf of his family, which is described as " the maternal family of the late Numo Kortey Kofi deceased," sued each of the two defendants in respect of the same piece of land for (a) damages for trespass, (b) ejectment and (c) injunction. With regard to the suit against Nii Attnkwei the plaintiff further claimed a declaration of title to the said land. It is admitted by the defendants that they are occupying portions of the said land, but Awomi Adonu, the defendant in the first suit, contends that he was on the land by leave and licence of Nii Attnkwei, whose tenant he has been for many years. Nii Attukwei, the defendant in the second suit, contends that the land, the subject­matter of the suit, was acquired jointly by himself and his late father, Kumo Kortey Kofi, and that by the act and deed of his late father, the said land was attached to the Stool of Medie, which he now occupies.

By the will of Numo Kortey Koti, who died on April 11, 1941, the land was devised to the plaintiff

" to hold in trust and for the use of himself and all other members of my maternal family." pg 270

   The plaintiff says that since the death of the testator he has been in possession of the said land and that his agents have been collecting rents from tenants on the land, including Awomi Adonu who, during the testator's lifetime, was one of his tenants. With regard to the defendant Nii Attukwei, the plaintiff says that he, Nii Attukwei, was on the land by virtue of the fact that he was the son of the testator; that from childhood he lived with his father, who after he purchased the said land occupied it until his death with his son Nii Attukwei; that Nii Attukwei now disputes plaintiff's ownership of the said land by virtue of the will, and has been molesting the plaintiff's other licensees on the land and instigating the plaintiff's tenant, Awomi Adonu, to refuse to pay tolls or rents to plaintiff; and that this defendant entered upon portions of the land occupied by the plaintiff's other tenants and licensees and destroyed structures built by them.

The main issue therefore was whether the land was the self-acquired property of the testator or was acquired jointly by the testator and the defendant Nii Attukwei, as alleged by him. Nii Attukwei based his claim of joint ownership of the land with his father, the testator, on the fact that he had lived with his father from his childhood, and worked with his father in several places as a farmer, during which period the father kept all the proceeds of their labours as a common purse belonging to both of them, and that when he married he con­tinued to work with his father, and contributed money for the purchase of the said land. On this issue the learned trial judge stated in his judgment:

" It is clear to me from the evidence of the defendant that if he did work with his father, who was a farmer, he was doing what any child would do in this country for his father. Whatever proceeds accrued from the efforts of father and son are not the joint property of the father and the son.

"Strong evidence should be forthcoming to prove that a particular portion of the proceeds was earmarked as the property of the son and that that amount was utilised by the father in acquiring a particular property. That evidence has not been forthcoming from the defendant. I do not also accept the evidence that the defendant contributed any money towards the land in dispute. "

In my view this conclusion reached by the learned trial judge is amply supported by the evidence, and consequently disposes of the contention of Nii Attukwei that the land in question was acquired jointly by him and his father. The defendant's further contention that the land was by the act and deed of the testator attached to a stool created by testator for him, the defendant, was rightly rejected by the trial court.

It is alleged that Numo Kortey Kofi requested the Asere Manche to create Nii Attukwei Manche or chief of Medie village, and that in  pg 271

compliance with this request the Asere Manche went to Medie and created the defendant, Nii Attukwei, chief. Counsel for the defendant admitted before this court that Medie village is not within the terri­torial jurisdiction of the Ga State in which Asere Manche is a sub­chief; it is not disputed that even a Paramount Chief of one State could not go to another State and create a chief there, or even perform ceremonies appertaining to the enstoolment of a chief without the authority of the chief of that territory. The members of the testator's family denied any knowledge of such act by the testator, who by his last will and testament, made some years after the alleged incident, devised the property to his maternal family, of which defendant Nii Attukwei is not a member.                                                                                                       

The claim of Nii Attukwei having failed, the contention of Awomi Adonu, the defendant in the first suit, that he had been the tenant of Nii Attukwei, and not Kortey Kofi, during the testator's lifetime, is untenable.

This appeal should therefore be dismissed with costs.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
