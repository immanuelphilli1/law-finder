Medical practitioners-False assumption of title of physician-Unregistered person receiving payment as practising medicine-Charges under Medical Practitioners and Dentists Ordinance, c. 69, s. 20.

The appellant was charged and convicted on three counts. Two of them, drawn under the Medical Practitioners Ordinance, c. 69, s. 20, each alleged that he had falsely assumed the title of medical practitioner and had received payment for practising medicine although he was unregistered: the third, under the Criminal Code, c. 9, s. 293, alleged that he had defrauded by false pretence. The appellant, who held diplomas of various unrecognized bodies, was not registered as a medical practitioner, as is required by the Medical Practitioners and Dentists Ordinance, but had carried on practice openly as a "Doctor of Naturopathy. " pg197

Held, (i) the Medical Practitioners and Dentists Ordinance, c. 69, s. 20, creates several distinct offences. One of these arises where a person: •• wilfully and falsely takes or uses any name, title, or addition, evidently implying a qualification to practise medicine ... "; another arises where a person "not being registered or licensed under this Ordinance ... receives any payment as practising medicine ... " Each of the first two counts alleged these two offences together: they were therefore bad for duplicity.

(ii) The provision in the Medical Practitioners and Dentists Ordi­nance, c. 69, s. 20, as to taking a title implying a qualification to practise medicine requires that this must be done" wilfully and falsely" before an offence is committed. A person does not commit this offence if he honestly believed on reasonable grounds that he was within his rights in so describing himself. Whether or not a person honestly believes and has reasonable grounds for so doing is a question of fact for the trial court: in the present case the trial magistrate had not directed himself on these matters and therefore the convictions could not be sustained. For the same reason the conviction of a false pretence could not be sustained. Younghusband v. Lujtig ( (1949) 2 All E.R. 72) applied.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The presence of mens rea does not appear to be an essential ingredient of the other offences created by the Medical Practitioners and Dentists Ordinance, c. 69, s. 20.]

APPEAL from convictions on September 26, 1956, in the District Magistrate's Court at Asamankese. The appellant had been charged under the Medical Practitioners and Dentists Ordinance, c. 69, s. 2tJ, and with false pretences under the Criminal Code, c. 9, s. 293.

Boateng for the appellant.

Lassey for the Commissioner of Police.

BENSON J. In many respects this is a most unsatisfactory case and the convictions and sentences cannot be allowed to stand. The accused was charged on three counts; on the charge sheet the par­ticulars of the offences are, as to the first two counts, "practising without having been registered. Section 20 of Cap. 69 " and the third count "fraud by false pretences. Section 293, Cap. 9." The particulars of offences as to the first two counts charge the accused that not being registered or licensed under the Medical Practitioners and Dentists Ordinance did falsely assume the title of a Medical Practitioner and under such assumed character obtained the sum ... etc."

    I will refer to the third count later in this judgment. pg198 It is quite clear that the first two counts are bad for duplicity.

    Under section 20 of the Medical Practitioners and Dentists Ordinance, c. 69, several distinct offences may be charged, but that must not be done under one count. It is one offence wilfully and falsely to take or use "any name, title ... implying a qualification to practise medicine ... " and it is another distinct offence to receive any payment for practising medicine.

An accused person is entitled to know exactly what charge he has to meet. In this case the statement of offences and the particulars as to the first two counts are inconsistent. It is worthy of note also that in both these counts the prosecution has omitted the word " wilfully." In order to secure a conviction on a charge under section 20 it must be proved that an accused person acted wilfully and falsely, at any rate as regards the wrongful assumption of a title. A court must be satisfied that mens rea existed in the accused and that he did not act under a reasonable belief that he had the right to adopt any such title. The wording of section 20 follows closely the equivalent clause in the English Medical Act, 1858. In the case of Wilson v. Inyang (1) it was decided that an ingredient of the offence (which was similar to the present case) was the question of mens rea and whether the accused acted in the honest belief that he was entitled to use the title assumed, and that this was a question of fact for the trial magistrate. That case followed the decision in Younghusbarul v. Luftig (2). As a matter of fact the case of Wilson v. Inyang (1) resembled closely the present case, not only because the accused was a West African but because he styled himself a Naturopath Physician, N.D., M.R.D.F. In the present case the accused called himself a Doctor of Naturopathy. He holds diplomas of the Anglo-American Institute of Drugless Therapy and a certificate of the British Guild of Drugless Practitioners, exactly the same as the accused in the Wilson v.   Inyang (1) case in England.

The learned magistrate in this case wrote a short judgment, but completely omitted to deal with the most essential element, namely, the question of the accused's mens rea or honest belief that he was entitled to act as he did. It seems from the judgment that the magistrate never directed himself to this question: he merely found it proved that accused held himself out as a " Doctor" when in fact he was not. That is not sufficient and there was a serious non­direction by the magistrate. The case of Commissioner of Police v. Larsen (3) is not in point, as that judgment did not deal with the question of mens rea. The appellant carried out his work openly and had sign-boards outside his clinic showing the titles adopted by him and his diplomas. It was for the magistrate to find that he did not act bona fide, but he failed to do so. If he had dealt with this question of fact, it is impossible to surmise what his decision would have been, but there was plenty of evidence as to accused's bona fides. The first  pg199 and second counts against the accusei were not only bad for duplicity, but also the essential elements were not found proved by the magistrate.

As regards the third count, I agree with counsel for the Crown that the conviction was bad; the gravamen of that count was the false pretence of being a medical practitioner. For. the same reasons as in counts 1 and 2 the conviction was bad, and also for the reason that the magistrate only found part of the alleged false pretence proved.

The convictions a1ld sentences on all three counts are accordingly quashed and set aside and in lieu thereof it is ordered that accused be acquitted and discharged; the fines, if paid, to be refunded to accused.

I wish to make it clear that this judgment does not authorise the accused to continue to call himself a doctor or physician or to display the same sign-boards; he has been warned by this judgment, and if he does again so style himself he cannot be heard to say he is bona fide, or he will find himself prosecuted and punished.

Convictions quashed.

S. G. D.
