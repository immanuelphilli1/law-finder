                                        BENIN v. ABABIO.

                          West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                        Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.JA

 

 

Practice - J1trisdiction - Circumstances in which jurisdiction of Supreme Court ousted in favour of Native Court-Action between natives /01' possession 0/ land sold under order of Supreme Court-Courts Ordinance, c. 4 0/1951, s. 18 (b), and Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99 0/ 1951, s. 35.

The plaintiff had purchased land sold under an order of the Supreme Court in execution of a judgment debt and brought this action in the Land Court of the Supreme Court to recover possession. The defendant claimed that by virtue of the Stool Property Protection Ordinance, c. 101 of 1951 (now repealed by the State Councils (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 4 of 1952), the sale was of no effect. The judge in the Land Court adjudicated on the effect of the Ordinance on this transaction but then went on to hold that it was a dispute between natives concerning the possession of land; that the proper forum for such a dispute was a Native Court, and that the fact that the defence brought into question the validity of a certificate of purchase, issued out of the Supreme Court, did not oust this jurisdiction.

Held, although the jurisdiction of the Land Court was not argued upon it was considered by the court, which properly found that the dispute was within the jurisdiction of a competent Native Court. In these circumstances, by the provisions of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 18 (b), and the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99 of 1951, s. 35, the Land Court had no jurisdiction to entertain the suit and should have referred the parties to the competent Native Court without adjudicating further.

E. O. Asafu-Adjaye for the plaintiff. Danq'llah for the defendant.

COUSSEY P. This action was instituted by the plaintiff in the Land Court, Ashanti, claiming that as an auction-purchaser at a sale in execution in the suit of Opanin l{wakyiePenkoro, Ex-Bankahene v. Nana Braka Ababio, Bankahene, he was entitled to possession of the land in the writ of summons described or, alternatively, to payment of customary tribute by the defendant and all farmers cultivating the land in dispute with the leave and licence of the defendant.

The defendant contended that the land in dispute was stool property and that the sale alleged by the plaintiff was null and void in that part of the debt for which the land was attached in execution was a stool debt incurred after the enactment of, and not in compliance with, the Stool Property Protection Ordinance, Ashanti, c. 101 of 1951 (formerly c. 22 of 1940), section 4 of which provides:

"No Stool property shall be liable to be seized in execution at the suit of any person or shall be sold in execution of any pledge or mortgage unless such property has been pledged or mortgaged with the consent of the Chief Commissioner: provided that this restriction shall not apply where the debt has been incurred or the property has been pledged or mortgaged before the date of the coming into operation of this Ordinance."

The suit was tried in the Land Court at Kumasi. The learned judge,

on an issue as to the validity of the sale, expressed the opinion:

" that the fact that a portion of the judgment debt was incurred after the coming into operation of the Ordinance would not render null and void any execution levied on the defendant's property in satisfaction of a debt incurred before and after the coming into operation of the Ordinance."

The learned judge then proceeded to hold as follows in the judgment appealed from:

" The claim is for possession of land and is between parties who are natives. The certificate of purchase issued by the court does not necessarily oust the jurisdiction of the Native Court

This is not an application by the plaintiff for a writ of possession but a substantive claim for possession of land purchased by him. The defendant has appealed to this court on the grounds that the learned trial judge was (i) wrong in his interpretation of section 4 of the Stool Property Protection Ordinance, Ashanti and (ii) wrong in referring the parties to a Native Court.

Taking first the second ground of appeal, it is true that the question of jurisdiction of the Land Court to entertain the suit was not argued, but when it appears on the face of the writ, as it does in this case, that the suit is one relating to the possession of land the court is right to refer the parties to a competent Native Court.

It seems to me that it was apparent from the outset of the hearing in the court below that the jurisdiction of the Land Court was expressly ousted by the provisions of section 35 of the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99 of 1951, which provides that the Land Court shall stop the further progress of a suit before it and refer the parties to a competent Native Court whenever it shall appear that the cause or matter brought before it is one properly cognizable by a Native Court.

This being so, the learned judge erred in trying and adjudicating on the issue as to the validity of the sale upon which the plaintiff based his claim, before ordering that the parties be referred to a Native Court. As Kingdon C.J. observed in Djabartey v. Antwi Awua (1) at p. 207:

" But as I read the section and more particularly the first word . whenever,' it is the duty of the court, immediately it does , appear' that the matter is so cognizable, to stop the case, and then it has no jurisdiction to do anything but refer the parties to a competent tribunal."

In this court learned counsel for the plaintiff has agreed that the learned judge could not deliver judgment in the suit and at the same time hold that the Land Court had no jurisdiction over the suit.

In my opinion the learned judge was right in referring the parties to a competent Native Court and to that extent the appeal fails and is dismissed.

But the learned judge erred in adjudicating on the validity of the sale referred to. He should have stopped the suit and referred the parties without delivering judgment on any of the issues to be deter­mined on the merits. That part of the judgment appealed from is therefore set aside as a nullity. It is unnecessary to deal with the first ground of appeal more fully but we would add that we do not necessarily agree with the reasoning of the learned judge to the effect that the attachment and sale were valid.

The defendant was in my opinion obliged to come to this court to get rid of that part of the judgment which a Native Court might have considered of binding effect upon it. But he has failed in his other ground of appeal; in fact he did not seriously contend that the Land Court could exercise jurisdiction in the suit, in view of the combined effect to the contrary of section 18 (b) of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951 and section 35 of the Native Courts (Ash anti) Ordinance, c. 99 of 1951.

For these reasons I would order that each party bears his own costs of this appeal.

Appeal allowed in part.
