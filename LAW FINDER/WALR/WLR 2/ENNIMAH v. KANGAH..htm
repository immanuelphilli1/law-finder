                                                                                ENNIMAH v. KANGAH. 

          Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, ·Western Judicial Division, Land Court, Sekondi

Windsor-Aubrey J.)

 

.

        Native Courts-Jurisdiction-Suits relating to land- True nature of issue between parties not revealed on face of claim-Jurisdiction of Native Courts "C."

The plaintiff was the lessor of certain lands: the defendant c1aimed the right of tribute from these lands and demanded this of the lessor. The plaintiff denied this right in the defendant whereupon the latter swore an oath. The plaintiff, respondiJ1g to the oath, commenced an action in a Native Court "C" claiming damages from the defendant for attempting to restrain the plaintiff's lessees from using the land and requiring the defendant to show cause for demanding tribute from the plaintiff in respect of the land. By the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, First Schedule, a Native Court "C" has no jurisdiction in land matters. In the present case the Native Court" C " held it had no jurisdiction in the first claim by the plaintiff but it adjudicated on the second claim and found for the defendant.

Held, although the claim by the plaintiff, on its face, related to the use of the oath procedure by the defendant, the real issue between the parties was as to whether tribute was payable in respect of land. The suit was thus one relating to the ownership, possession or occupation of land. This being so, then regardless of the form in which the plaintiff's claim was made, the Native Court" C" had no jurisdiction to entertain the suit and the proceedings before it and the Native Appeal Court were a nullity.

APPEAL from a decision of the Native Court" B " of Atuabo on October 20, 1954, affirming the decision of the Native Court" C " of Esiama on July 27, H)54, giving judgment for the defendant in an action for damages arising from the use by the defendant of the oath procedure.

Blay for the plaintiff.

Bmndford-Mensah for the defendant.

WINDSOR-AUBEY J. The defendant appeals against the decision of the Native Court" B " of Atuabo reversing the decision of the trial court, the Native Court" C " of Esiama.

In my opinion the trial before the Native Court" C " was not altogether satisfactory but it will be unnecessary for this court to consider that aspect if it appears that the Native Court" C " had no jurisdiction to hear the case.

Under the Second Schedule to the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance (c. 98) a grade" C " Native Court has no jurisdiction to hear land cases. Indeed, the Native Court" C " fully appreciated that point, and for that reason held it had no jurisdiction to adjudicate upon paragraph 1 of plaintiff's claim for £25 damages, holding that this part of claim was a land case.

The Native Court" C," however, adjudicated upon paragraph 2 of the plaintiff's claim holding that this matter was a civil dispute. The plaintiff's claim was as follows:-

" Claim :-Plaintiff claims damages of £25 in default from the defendant show cause of the below accusation to wit :-To show reason why defendant should swore Oath of 'Ehanu' on him refraining plaintiff's lessees from farming plaintiff's ancestors' land which the plaintiff had granted the said lessees for that purpose.

(2) Defendant should also prove when, where, and who ordered during the arbitration at Atuabo and made the plaintiff understand that he the plaintiff should offer a drink or one third share of any rum or money which plaintiff may receive at anytime lesseers (sic) may enter into plaintiff's land upon which the defendant sworn the great Oath of' Ehanu ' on plaintiff's lesseers (sic) from farming on plaintiff's Ancesstors (sic) land with reason that the plaintiff had objected the order given at Atuabo."

 Now it is well-settled law that in determining whether an action is

a land suit, the wording of the claim is largely immaterial and that the court should determine what were the real issues before the court. The second part of the case is an " oath case" but any action either relating to land or to a civil dispute may be commenced by swearing an oath. The Native Court " C" seemed to think that an oath is automatically a civil but not a land dispute. That is not so. The defendant had sworn the great oath of Ehanu that the plaintiff should II give to the defendant one third share of any rum or money which plaintiff might receive from his lessees on the land. To this oath the plaintiff responded by this action. The court must ask itself on what ground the defendant claimed a third share of rum or money, paid by the plaintiff's lessees and tenants. It is very clear from reading the record that the defendant claimed tribute by reason of his rights

of overlordship over the land. It is, therefore, to my mind clear that to adjudicate on the part 2 of the claim the court had to determine what rights, if any, of overlordship the defendant enjoyed. Tribute can only arise from some form of overlordship and a claim to such a right is clearly a matter relating to the ownership of land.

I, therefore, hold that both claims were land causes and that Native Court " C " had no jurisdiction to hear the case. Consequently the proceedings before that court and all subsequent proceedings are a nullity and are so declared. There has been no valid trial and the proceedings before all the native trial and appellate courts are set

aside.

Order accordingly.

S. G. D.
