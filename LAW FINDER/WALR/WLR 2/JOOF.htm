          Master and Servant - Negligence - Claim by employee in respect 01 an industrial injury - Common law liability 01 employer - Previous pro­ceedings under Workmen's Compensation Ordinance, c. 93-Workmen's Compensation Ordinance, c. 93, s. 25 (1) (b).

           Negligence - Contributory negligence - Laws 01 England (Application) Ordinance, c. 1, s. 11.

The plaintiff was employed by the defendant company as a carpenter and, in the course of his employment, sustained severe injuries to his right hand as a result of its coming into contact with a circular saw. Disability was put at 42~ per cent. The defendants did not deny negli­gence but asserted contributory negligence on the part of the plaintiff and claimed that in any event the plaintiff's common law remedy had become statute-barred by the operation of the Workmen's Compensation Ordinance, c. 93, s. 25 (1) (b). This provides that a judgment under the Ordinance, whether for or against the employer, is a bar to further proceedings outside the Ordinance. In this case the defendants, evidently admitting liability, sent a cheque for £150 16s. 8d. to the Commissioner, Western Division, for payment to the plaintiff and, at the same time, advised both the plaintiff and his solicitor that they had done so. In the absence of his solicitor the plaintiff went to the Commissioner and was told that the money had been paid into court and could not be paid out until an award should be given in favour of him by the court. He was therefore advised by the Commissioner to commence proceedings against the defendants by taking out a summons, and this he did. The summons was returnable on the following day, the plaintiff attended but the defendants were not represented and the Commissioner gave a " judgment" in favour of the plaintiff, thereby releasing the money to him. It appeared from the record that the proceedings before the Commissioner. were taken against the manager of the company, not against the company itself, and that judgment was given against the manager.

Held, (i) the judgment in the proceedings before the Commissioner was given against the office of manager of the defendant company. This was not a legal entity capable of being sued either on its own account or as representing the defendant company; !lor was the manager the employer of the plaintiff. No judgment had thus been given "for or against the employer" within the Workmen's Compensation Ordinance, c. 93, s. 25 (1) (b), and therefore the proceedings before the Commissioner could !lot operate as a bar to the plaintiff's common law claim.

(ii) On the facts, general damages were to be assessed at £1,600, and contributory negligence, at 25 per cent., was established against the plaintiff. By the application of the Laws of England (Application) Ordinance, c. 1, s. 11, general damages were thus reduced to £1,200.

Semble, notwithstanding the provisions of the Workmen's Compensa­tion Ordinance, s. 25 (1) (b), a judgment obtained in proceedings under the Ordinance may not necessarily operate as a bar to an action outside pg225 the Ordinance if the requirements of the Workmen's Compensation (Rules of Court) Rules, 1941, as to the conduct of those proceedings are not complied with.

ACTION for damages brought against an employer in respect of injuries suffered by the plaintiff in the course of his employment.

N'Jie for the plaintiff. Mahoney for the defendants.

MILES C.J. The plaintiff in this case claims damages against the defendant company. The claim arises out of an accident which occurred on December 5, 1955, and resulted in the plaintiff receiving serious injuries to his right hand through its coming into contact with a circular saw. The plaintiff at the material time was employed by the defendants as a carpenter and on the day in question he was using an electrically operated circular saw. He had been required to cut a number of pieces of timber 14 feet in length by 12 inches wide and 6 inches deep to lengths of 12 feet. Having done this he was then instructed to cut the small pieces of wood so that the finished product would be 2 feet long, 12 inches wide and t inch thick. There was at one time some confusion in the evidence of the plaintiff as to precisely what he was instructed to do but eventually it was established, and it was in fact not disturbed, that the second operation Was as described above.

The saw in question is a circular saw erected vertically. Round the saw there are guides where wood can be pushed onto the saw which is rotating in the middle of the guides. Over the saw there is a guard which has been aptly described as something in the nature of a miniature car mudguard. This guard is attached to a rod on the structure of the machine. The guard is adjustable on this rod to various heights depending on the depth of the timber which is being sawn and also on the size of the blade. The guard can be removed by pushing it to the side but this should only be done for the purpose of cleaning or maintenance. The maximum clearance between the guard and the plate is Hi inches. There is no mechanical feed.

For the purpose of the second stage of the cutting operation it was necessary for the plaintiff to place the 2 feet long pieces of wood with the 12 inches side uppermost. Thus it was not possible to cut these pieces without removing the guard, since the maximum clearance was inadequate by ~ inch. The plaintiff says that the guard had in fact been removed previously when this particular work started by a Mr. Barrell under whom he had been working, and that he had not touched it since then. Mr. Barrell is now in England and has not been called, but whether it was he or the plaintiff who removed the guard does not matter since in fact the wood could not be cut to the pg226 required dimensions without removing the guard, for the reason previously stated.

It was pointed out by Mr. Hills, an employee of the defendant company, that although the guard had to be removed, there was no danger to the operator until the blade had reached near the end of the wood since the radius of the blade was 7~ inches and for most of the cutting length it would be covered by the wood. For the purpose of pushing the wood forward during the final stage the plaintiff used a piece of wood. He was not supposed to use his hand in view of the obvious danger from the unguarded saw.

The plaintiff's account of how the accident occurred is as follows.

He was holding the wood with his right hand for the purpose of guiding it and owing to the vibration of the machine the short piece of wood had fallen onto the ground. As he was picking up this piece of wood his right hand slipped and came into contact with the saw. There was no other witness to the accident.

It was, in my opinion, negligence beyond question on the part of the defendants to require the plaintiff to carry out an operation on this saw which necessitated the removal of the guard and, in fairness to Mr. Mahoney, he did not contend the contrary.

The first defence raised is that the plaintiff was guilty of contribu­tory negligence. Paragraph 7 of the defence, which alleges that the plaintiff contrary to instructions and repeated warnings removed the guard, has very properly been abandoned but it is said that he was negligent in keeping his hand on the block in view of the speed at which the machine worked. I am of opinion that there is some sub­stance in this. The plaintiff should have appreciated the danger of working this machine when the guard was removed and in my opinion he was negligent in keeping his hand on the block while bending down to pick up the piece of wood which had fallen from the blade. This is a case no doubt of familiarity breeding contempt. I would not, 'however, apportion the plaintiff's degree of negligence at more than 25 per cent. He was placed in a position in which he ought never to have been placed, in working the saw without a guard.

The second defence raised is that the plaintiff has received compensation in the sum of £150 16s. Sd. awarded by the Protectorate Court of the 'Western Division under the Workmen's Compensation Ordinance, c. 93, and that by proviso (b) to section 25 (1) of the Ordinance, that award is a bar to the present suit. The proviso is in the following terms:

" (b) a judgment in proceedings under this Ordinance whether for or against the employer shall be a bar to proceedings at the suit of any person by whom, or on whose behalf, such proceedings were taken, in respect of the same injury independently of this Ordinance . pg 227 

For the purpose of considering this it is necessary to explain the rather curious happenings in this case. On March 21, 1956, the defendants forwarded a cheque for £150 l0s. 8d. to the Commissioner, Western Division, for payment to the plaintiff. The defendants notified the plaintiff's solicitor of this by letter dated March 24 and they also informed the plaintiff himself. The plaintiff says that he was asked by some person in the employ of the defendants to go to the Commis­sioner, which he did, and that he was told by the Commissioner that before he could receive the sum which had been paid into court he would have to issue a summons against them. The plaintiff says that he wished to consult his solicitor, but he was informed that Mr. N'Jie had gone to the Protectorate. Since the plaintiff was short of money he went again to the Commissioner who instructed him to come the following day when there would be court proceedings. I must confess this struck me at the time as unlikely, but in fact a summons was issued on April 10, 1950, returnable for the following day. On April 17 the plaintiff attended the court at Brikama and, according to the certified copy of the record of the proceedings, nobody appeared on behalf of the defendants. Whether the plaintiff gave evidence or not is not clear, but he apparently made a short statement and a medical report was produced. The Magistrate gave what he described as a " judgment" in which he made an order that the plaintiff should receive the £150 los. 8d. which had been deposited in court by the defendants.

The first point to be noted about these proceedings is that the defendant is described in the proceedings as "Manager Gambia Minerals Limited" and in the summons as "Manager, Gambia Mineral, for Gambia Mineral Ltd. of Brufut." The manager is not a legal entity capable of being sued either on his own account or on behalf of the company. Moreover the correct name of the company is Gambian Minerals Limited. The judgment in this case, being against the manager, cannot be said to be a judgment " against the employer" within the meaning of the proviso. It is, of course, the fact that it was the defendant company which actually paid the money, but this is a provision which must be construed strictly, and in the circumstances it cannot avail the defendants as a bar to the present proceedings.

A further objection, although perhaps this would not necessarily be fatal, is that no attempt was made to follow the procedure pre­scribed by the Workmen's Compensation (Rules of Court) Rules, 1941. There was no " written application" in the prescribed form as required by Rule 15 (1) nor were the provisions of Rule 10 (1) with regard to the notice to be served on the respondent or the time which ought to elapse between the date of service of the notice on the respondent and the date fixed for the hearing of the application, observed. I hold, pg 228 therefore, that section 25 of the Workmen's Compensation Ordinance, c. 93, is of no avail to the defendants. No doubt the Magistrate, having received this deposit of £150 1Gs. Sd., was anxious to transfer it to the plaintiff by some legal means, but the procedure he adopted was hopelessly misconceived.

It remains to consider the question of damages. The injury necessitated the amputation of the three middle fingers of the plaintiff's right hand. Dr. Palmer said that the thumb should also be ampu­tated since it was functionally useless, but to this the plaintiff objected. Dr. Palmer said that the hand, as an instrument in the discharge of its duties, is entirely useless. He puts the disability at 42t per cent.

Taking first the special damage, it is conceded that the plaintiff is entitled to loss of earnings from December 5, 1955, to the date of issue of the writ, namely, November 13, 195G. His wage was £4 4s. per fortnight. This amounts to £130 4s. The defendants have paid the plaintiff the sum of £25 10s. on account of this, leaving a balance of £104 14s. which is the sum due. I gave leave for an amendment of the particulars of special damage accordingly but the amendment filed still contains the wrong commencing date. In order to do justice I will give leave for a further amendment of the particulars accordingly.

After his discharge from hospital the plaintiff had to attend as an out-patient for the purpose of having his hand dressed, and it is conceded that he is entitled to his traveling expenses of 4s. a day for fifteen days as claimed. This item amounts to £3.

The third item, namely, transport of the plaintiff's wife from Kololi to Bathurst for the purpose of bringing meals to the plaintiff in hospital has been abandoned. The total of the special damage therefore is £107 14s.

In addition to the above the plaintiff is entitled to general damages.

He was detained in hospital for forty-five days and, of course, sus­tained considerable pain and suffering. So far as his employment as a carpenter is concerned he must be regarded as totally and permanently incapacitated. It may be that he will be able to obtain some other employment which does not involve the use of both hands, but this must necessarily be of an unskilled nature and consequently less remunerative. He is a man in his middle forties and I suppose his expectation of working life would be about a further twelve years. Whether he would have gained any promotion or have been able to increase his earning capacity in any way in the future is a matter on which there is no evidence. Apart from the handicap in connection with his work he will, for the rest of his life, suffer the many incon­veniences, great and small, which have to be faced by any person suffering from a serious disability. Doing the best I can I assess the total general damages at £1,600. Seventy-five per cent. of this sum is £1,200, from which must be deducted the sum of £150 16s. Sd. pg 229 which the plaintiff has already received. This reduces the general damage to £1,049 3s. 4d.

    There will be judgment for the plaintiff for £1,156 17s. 4d. with costs.

Judgment for the plaintiff· S. G. D.
