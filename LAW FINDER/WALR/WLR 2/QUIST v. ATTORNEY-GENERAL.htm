Petition of Right-Public officers-Whether claim for compensation arising from act done by statutory authority barred by Public Officers' Protection Ordinance, c. 27, s. 2.

Petition of Right - Procedure for assessing compensation - Claim for com­pensation under Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance, c. 66, s. 3 (6) - Period within which claim to be presented - Application of Public Lands Ordinance, c. 134, ss. 7, 9 and 10.

The plaintiff claimed to have suffered damage as a result of certain activities, carried on under the statutory authority of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance, c. 66, by the Electricity Department of the Government. He sought compensation under section 4 (6) of the Ordinance: this section, in addition to establishing the right to com­pensation, requires that it is to be assessed and paid in accordance with the procedure set out in the Public Lands Ordinance, c. 134, ss. 9 and 10.

The activities complained of were, that employees of the department had dug a hole adjacent to a wall of the plaintiff's house, that the hole had been left open and, that with rain continually filling the hole, the foundations of his house had been affected so as to cause subsidence of the house. The department denied the allegations made against it and asserted that, in any event, the claim was statute barred under both the Public Officers' Protection Ordinance, c. 27, s. 2, and the Public Lands Ordinance, c. 134, s. 7, as being out of time. The defence as far as the Public Lands Ordinance was concerned was that in a claim for compensation under the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance, s. 3 (6), the whole of the Public Lands Ordinance is brought into operation and not merely sections 9 and 10 thereof. The defence claimed, further, that a claim for compensation was not maintainable unless negligence by the Electricity Department was established.

It was established on the evidence that the acts about which complaint was made had been done by employees of the department and that the subsidence of the building was a direct consequence of these acts.

Held, (i) the protection afforded by the Public Officers' Protection Ordinance, c. 27, s. 2, to persons executing a public duty (that an action must be commenced within three months of the commission of the act of which complaint is made) extends only to such persons in their personal capacity and gives no protection to the employing authority.

(ii) Even if the Public Officers' Protection Ordinance could be held to protect an employing authority, this protection was of no avail in the present case because the three months' period commences to run, in the case of continuing injury or damage, only from the cessation of such continuance, whereas in the present case the injury was still continuing.

(iii) The Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance, c. 66, s. 3 (6), provides that compensation is: "to be settled, awarded, and paid in pg 313  accordance (as nearly as may be) with the provisions relating to com­pensation which are contained in the Public Lands Ordinance." These "provisions relating to compensation" are contained in sections 9 and 10 of the latter Ordinance, and these therefore are the only parts of that Ordinance brought in by section 3 (6) of the former Ordinance. The provisions relating to the lodgment of claims within three months, contained in section 7 of the Public Lands Ordinance, were therefore irrelevant to the present issue.

(iv) Where statutory provision exists for the payment of compensa­tion, this is to be assessed upon a basis different from that used for assessing damages. Compensation is concerned solely with the problem of repayment, repair or reinstatement: damages may involve this, together with a sum by way of penalty for injury wrongfully inflicted.

(v) Where an ordinance makes provision for the payment of com­pensation for damage caused in the exercise of, or in consequence of, powers conferred by it, then, subject always to any statutory provision to the contrary, negligence on the part of the person or body exercising such powers is not a necessary prerequisite to a claim for compensation. Thus, the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance, c. 66 s. 3 (6), confers an entitlement to compensation in every case where there is a "person having any estate or interest in lands ... injuriously affected by the exercise of (the powers conferred by the Ordinance) or suffering any damage therefrom" and this entitlement exists irrespective of whether negligence is attributable to the Electricity Department.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Where powers are conferred by statute but no pro­vision is made for the payment of compensation in the event of damage flowing from the exercise of such powers, then a claim may lie if there has been negligence by the authority concerned (or under some other head of liability, e.g., nuisance, as in Met7'opolitan Asylum District v. Hill (1881) 6 App.Cas. 193). The duty of a public authority exercising statutory powers is to be. construed strictly, for there is a presumption in the interpretation of statutes that where the legislature confers powers upon a public body there is an intention that such powers shall be exercised so as not to interfere with private rights, unless the power is expressed in such a way as to make interference inevitable. Where a statutory power can be exercised in a manner either hurtful to an individual or in a manner innocuous to an individual, the person or body exercising the power: "will be held to be guilty of negligence if he chooses, or they choose, the former mode of exer­cising his power or their power, and not the latter, both being available to him or them": per Lord Atkinson in Lagan Navigation Co. v. Lambeg Bleaching, Dyeing and Finishing Co. [1927] A.C. 226. Moreover, even where the exercise of a statutory power necessarily involves injury, care must none the less be taken to avoid aggravating the injury by negligent execution: see Geddis v. Proprietors of Bann Beservoir (1878) 3 App.Cas. 430.

The particular difficulty that exists in Ghana is that statutory powers are usually exercised by bodies against whom, in the absence of an enactment equivalent to the Crown Proceedings Act, 1947, no action will lie. Thus, although a petition of right will lie for compensation for interference by the Crown with a subject's property (Attorney-General v. Keyser's Royal Hotel Ltd. [1920] A.C. 508, M.L.) it will not lie for damages for a tort alleged to have been committed either by the Crown or by a servant of the Crown acting by the Crown's authority: Tobin v. B. (1864) C.B.N.S. 310. And since the courts have now refused to accept the liability of a defendant nominated by pg 314 the Crown (see Adams v. Naylor [1946] A.C. 543, H.L., and Royster v. Cavey [1947] 1 K.B. 204; [1946] 2 All E.R. 642, C.A.) the injured individual may be without remedy.]

(vi) In determining whether a right to compensation will lie under the provisions of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance, c. 66, s. 3 (6), the test to be applied is whether the execution of the statutory power has caused the damage of which complaint is made or whether it merely contributed to a cause of damage lying outside the exercise of the statutory power. In the latter case no claim for compensation will lie under the Ordinance.

ACTION brought by petition of right under the Petitions of Right Ordinance, c. 18, s. 4, against the Attorney-General in respect of damage resulting from the acts of the Electricity Department. The plaintiff sought compensation under the provisions of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance, c. 86, s. 1} (6).

Unger, Cmwn Counsel, for the Attorney-General. Koi Larbi for the plaintiff.

OLI~ENNU J. This action is commenced by a petition filed by the plaintiff under section 4 of the Petitions of Right Ordinance, c. 18. By it the plaintiff claimed " £550 damages in respect of damage caused to his property by workmen of the Electricity Department. "

It is agreed by both parties that in November-December, 1954, workmen of the Electricity Department carried out some repair work in the area where the plaintiff's house is situate, and that some excavations or diggings were made in the execution of that work. The said repair work was carried out in the exercise of the powers vested in the Department by section 3 of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance, c. 66.

According to the plaintiff the excavations included a hole in the area of his terrace about 4t feet deep and 7 feet in diameter, the circum­ference of which touched the wall of the main building. The plaintiff deposed that the workmen left the hole uncovered when they com­pleted the work, waiting for a superior officer to come and examine it; that it began to rain while they were so waiting, whereupon they left the hole uncovered and did not return to fill it up until three days after, during which period it rained again with water filling the hole on each occasion. He said that about six to eight months after the pg 315 execution of the work he began to notice cracks and other damage to his building; these cracks continued to occur and he eventually consulted some experts, among whom is Mr. Frank Rogers, a structural engineer with some twenty years' experience.

The plaintiff's evidence in respect of this particular hole was corroborated by one Mr. J. S. Annan, who was a tenant of the house at the date of the execution of the work.

According to an eye-witness for the defence, namely, Mr. Amonoo, Station Engineer of the Electricity Department, who alleged he visited the work twice while it was in progress, and to Mr. Boi Quartey and Mr. Emmanuel Karbre, the two artisans who carried out the work, no hole such as was ~escribed by the plaintiff was dug in the execution of the said repairs, and Mr. Emmanuel Laud Quartey, the Senior Electrical Engineer of the Electricity Department, deposed that there would have been no necessity to dig a hole of that description having regard to the nature of the work done and the depth of the cables.

'l'he plaintiff's case is that the damage to his house was caused by a settlement resulting from the digging of the hole.

On April 1, 1957, that is after the third witness for the defence had concluded his evidence and the court had inspected the premises, Mr. Unger, counsel for the defence, raised some preliminary objections. He submitted that the claim was statute barred because:

1.    The action is against a public officer, and under section 2 of the Public Officers' Protection Ordinance, c. 27, the claim cannot be entertained since it was commenced more than three months after the execution of the work complained of.

2.    Section 3 (6) of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance, upon which the claim is brought, incorporates the Public Lands Ordi­nance, c. 134, and by section 7 of the latter Ordinance the claim cannot be entertained unless the claim was made within three months of the execution of the work.

I overruled the submissions, gave my reasons orally, and promised to set them down in this judgment. They are as follows:

As regards the first ground, in my opinion the Public Officers' Protection Ordinance affords protection to a public officer when the liability is personal and not that of the General Government or of the public au'thority employing him. In this case the claim is against the General Government of Ghana, the Attorney-General is only a statu­tory defendant representing the General Government to defend the suit by virtue of section 4 of the Petitions of Right Ordinance.

Secondly, even if the Public Officers' Protection Ordinance is applicable to the defendant in this case, the expert witnesses called on both sides are agreed that the damage complained of is continuing, in which case the statute will not begin to run until the cessation of the damage. Section 2 (1) of the Ordinance provides as follows: pg 316"The action, prosecution, or proceeding shall not lie or be instituted unless it iscommenced within three months next after the act, neglect, or default complained of, or in case of a con­tinuance of injury or damage, within three months next after the ceasing thereof: Provided that if the action, prosecution, or pro­ceeding be at the instance of any person for cause arising while such person was a convict prisoner, it may be commenced within three months after the discharge of such person from prison."

Since in the opinion of each of the experts called by both sides the damage in this case is continuous, and since the evidence shows that the damage has not ceased, the statute has not begun to run, and the claim cannot be statute barred.

As regards the second ground of objection, the words in section 3 (6) of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance, which refers to the Public Lands Ordinance, read:                                      .

" Every person having any estate or interest in lands taken by the Government for the purposes of any works, or injuriously affected by the exercise of the said powers or suffering any damage therefrom, shall be entitled to compensation, to be settled, awarded, and paid in accordance (as nearly as may be) with the provisions relating to compensation which are contained in the Public Lands Ordinance."

I interpret this to mean that compensation under the said section should be determined or assessed and paid upon the same principles and in the same manner as compensations for land acquired for public services are assessed and paid, namely, in accordance with the pro­visions contained in sections 9 and 10 of the Public Lands Ordinance. In my opinion those words do not relate to the procedural regulations for bringing the claim, and therefore section 7 of the Ordinance has no application to section 3 (6) of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance.

I therefore hold that the claim is not statute barred.

As to the facts: Mr. Unger for the defendant urged that the evidence given by the plaintiff that a hole as dug in the terrace in the execution of the work should be rejected because it contradicts the contents of letters written to the Electricity Department respectively by the plaintiff and his solicitor, in which letters it was alleged that the hole was dug under the terrace; secondly, because as stated by Mr. Quartey, the nature of the work done would not have necessitated the digging of such a hole.

In his evidence before the court the plaintiff referred to .. hole under the terrace" and" hole in the terrace" in a way which seemed to indicate that he used the two words "in" and "under" as synonymous terms. It will, therefore, be unfair to him that any of those words should be interpreted strictly against him. pg 317

 

On the second point: it· is also fair to observe that the opllllOn expressed by Mr. Quartey was based upon information given him by other people interested in the matter, most probably Messrs. Amonoo, Quartey and Karbre; therefore undue weight should not be attached to that opinion.

Mr. Unger further submitted that the evidence of the plaintiff's witness, Mr. J. S. Annan, should be discarded, because although he ~ppeared a truthful witness and gave his evidence convincingly, yet the witness is such a very busy man that it is possible that, while he did not intend deliberately to mislead the court, he might not have had time at the material moment to make careful observation of the excavations made in connection with the repair work, and his evidence might be mistaken. With Mr. J. S. Annan's evidence I shall deal presently.

As to the eye-witnesses, I would say that these witnesses for the defence impressed me as if they were on their guard all the time; Boi Quartey and Emmanuel Karbre struck me as unreliable. I do not attach any weight to their evidence.

And now to the plaintiff: Although I believe his evidence, yet since he is an interested party I would have been reluctant to act upon his evidence if it had stood alone. This takes me to the evidence of Mr. J. S. Annan. :From the submissions Mr. Unger made I am sure this witness must have struck him as much as he struck me as an honest and thoroughly reliable witness. There is no evidence before me from which I can infer that the witness is such a busy man that for lack of time his observation at the material date might be defective. And there are no circumstances to discredit him. I accept his evidence that a hole as described by him was dug in the terrace, that the same was left uncovered for about three days and that meanwhile it rained and water filled it.

The next question I have to determine is, whether the damage to the walls of the plaintiff's house was caused by a settlement, and if so whether the said settlement was the result of the hole dug. Mr. Hallam, a constructional engineer of some twelve years' standing, was called by the defence as an expert witness.

The following are some of the questions put to him and his answers:

Examination-in-chief:

Q. "If you dig a hole near a house will it affect the house?

A. "It will depend on the size of the hole and depth of the founda tions.

Q. "Could a hole 4 feet deep, 6 feet wide dug close to a wall of a house cause settlement resulting in cracks?

A. " Yes it could. pg 318

Q. "What could have been the cause of the cracks you saw in the plaintiff's house?

A. "In the first place it could be poor construction. Secondly it could be earth tremors. Thirdly and the more likely, to the poor sub-strata in Accra and weak foundations. I have in my experience in Accra condemned two buildings due to similar cracks. "

Under cross-examination he said:

" If the hole 4 feet deep and 6 feet wide dug near a wall were left uncovered and filled with water the settlement it could cause could be more serious."

In re-examination he said:

" I think the cracks in this case are due to the foundations."

Q. "How is it due to foundations?

A. "It is probably due to foundations inadequate for the conditions in Accra ... , If a settlement is caused by a hole dug in front of a house there will be cracks on all four walls.

Q. "Could the cracks you saw in the walls of the plaintiff's house be caused by a hole dug in front of the building?

A. "That is a matter of opinion. I think it is most unlikely to be due to the hole."

And in answer to the court he said:

" The cracks I saw in the plaintiff's house could be caused by settlement. "

Earlier in examination-in-chief the witness had said that when he inspected the plaintiff's house he saw cracks on all four walls and on a wall of the outhouses; and that the cracks on the front wall were about the same magnitude and the same type of cracks as those on the other walls. Later he said that if the cracks were caused by a hole dug in front of the house and the front foundations had settled without the other ones settling, the cracks between the front wall and the other wall would be more exaggerated than the other cracks.

The plaintiff on the other hand, as stated above, called another structural engineer, 1\11'. Frank Rogers. Among other things this witness said in his evidence-in-chief as follows:

"I found the front east corner of the building showed signs of settlement. The roof level had dropped down in one corner. The walls and floors were cracked. Those are sufficient indications or signs of some kind of settlement.

I had a look at the surrounding buildings to see if I could discover any obvious reasons for the settlement .... From what Mr. Quist told me I came to the conclusion that the work done by the Electricity Department was the main cause. " pg 319

Under cross-examination he said:

.. There may be other cases, but I never during my investiga­tions found out any other thing which could have caused the sort of damage I saw."

Q. ,. Do you frequently come across settlement? A. "Fairly frequently.

Q. " Here in Accra?

A ... Yes.

Q. "How are you guided in coming to conclusions as to their cause?

A. "Observation of the cracks and the knowledge that certain types of cracks are caused by certain physical forces. "

Both expert witnesses are agreed that the cracks on the petitioner's building could have been caused by a settlement resulting from a hole such as was dug in the terrace.

In my opinion the absence of evidence of any earth tremor pre­ceding the cracks eliminates earth tremor as the cause of the damage; the absence of any evidence of similar damage on other buildings in the area of the plaintiff's house, and of the nature of the construction of the building and of the foundations which have stood for twenty-five years eliminate the possibility that the damage was caused by poor construction, or sub-strata and poor foundations. Thus, of the possible causes suggested, the only one shown on the evidence to exist is the digging of the hole. Moreover, the drop in the roof level on the said building towards the side where the hole was dug, confirms the opinion that the hole is the only probable cause of the settlement which resulted in the damage.

I therefore find that the damage to the petitioner's house was caused by settlement resulting from the digging of the hole in the petitioner's terrace.

Mr. Unger submitted that no negligence was alleged and none proved on the part of the Electricity Department in the execution of their statutory duty under section 3 of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance.

Every individual who suffers damage or injury as a result of acts of another, negligently performed, is, at common law, entitled to claim damages against that other individual for the wrong done. In my opinion that is so whether the act so negligently performed was in the exercise of a common law right or a statutory right. It would be superfluous for the legislature, when passing a special Act conferring powers on a person or a body, to provide in the Act that a person injuriously affected by the negligent exercise of the statutory powers should be entitled to compensation.

On the other hand, it is a proper thing for the legislature to provide that where, in spite of the proper exercise of statutory powers, injury pg 320 is caused to the citizen, he should be compensated to the extent of the loss or damage suffered, and to lay down the bases for assessing the compensation. Where no provision is made in a special Act for payment of compensation a person injuriously affected by the exercise of the powers under the Act has no remedy except under the common law, and will fail unless he can prove negligence. See the case of Green v. Chelsea Water Works Co. (1) and Longhturst v. The Metropolitan Water Board (2).

I interpret " compensation" to mean a reward, payment of the value of loss suffered, repair of damage done or reinstatement, while' I interpret "damages" to mean a penalty for injury wrongfully inflicted plus compensation. The bases of assessment of compensation are thus different from those for assessing damages.

In my opinion claims for compensation made by virtue of statutory provisions such as section 3 (0) of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance do not require proof of negligence to succeed. See the case of Colac v. Summerfield (3). I think the essence of a claim made under section 3 (0) of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance is: that the work done under statutory powers was done properly in a workmanlike manner without negligence, and. that the injury is causally connected with the exercise of those powers.

In the case of Hornby v. Liverpool United Gas Light Co. (4), a gas company in exercise of their statutory powers broke a concrete pavement of a street within the limits of their special Act. In the course of it a workman struck the concrete with a pick and a piece of the concrete flew up some distance and broke a plate-glass window of adjoining premises. There was no question of the workman being negligent. It was held that the damage was caused in the perform­ance of the statutory power and that the owner of the premises was entitled to compensation under section 0 of the Gasworks Clauses Act, 1847. This reads:

" The Undertakers, under such Superintendence as is herein­after specified, may open and break up the Soil and Pavement of the several Streets and Bridges within the Limits of the special Act, and may open and break up any Sewers, Drains, or Tunnels within or under such Streets and Bridges, and lay down and place within the same Limits Pipes, Conduits, Service Pipes, and other Works, and from Time to Time repair, alter, or remove the same, and also make any Sewers that may be necessary for carrying off the Washings and waste Liquids which may arise in the making of the Gas, and for the Purposes aforesaid may remove and use all Earth and Materials in and under such Streets and Bridges, and they may in such Streets erect any Pillars, Lamps, and other Works, and do all other Acts which the Undertakers shall from Time to Time deem necessary for supplying Gas to the  pg 321 Inhabitants of the District included within the said Limits, doing as little Damage as may be in the Execution of the Powers hereby or by the special Act granted, and making Compensation for any Damage which may be done in the Execution of such Powers."

See also the case of The Queen v. St. Luke's Vestry (5) and the case of Harpur v. The Mayor, Aldermen and Burgesses of Swansea (6). With this latter case I shall deal more fully presently.

But in the case of Green v. Chelsea -rFater Works Co. (1), where compensation was claimed for nuisance caused by a water pipe which had been frozen in severe weather and burst afterwards, it was held that in the absence of a nuisance section in the special Act, namely, the Water Works Clauses Act, 1847, when once the company had laid the pipes without negligence no action will lie against them in respect of damage or injury caused by bursting of a water main. See also the case of Longhurst v. The Metropolitan Water Board (2) cited by Mr. Unger.

The crucial point in this case is, has the claim been properly brought under section 3 (6) of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance? In other words, has it been proved that the execution of the statutory power caused the damage, or that it merely contributed to cause it?

In my opinion if it caused the damage the case comes within section 3 (6) of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance and com­pensation is payable without negligence. If it merely contributed to the real cause, there will be no remedy unless negligence is proved.

To answer that question I must examine the section carefully. I ask what did the Ordinance authorize the Electricity Department to do? I answer: among other things the Ordinance authorized it to dig holes and trenches either during dry or wet weather and work in them. In this connection the opinion expressed by Mr. Frank Rogers in his letter dated ay 9, 1956, which the defence seemed to adopt, is important. He said:

" Such execution of the work is however almost bound to cause a settlement. Complete consolidation of material filled back into a trench is practically impossible with normal methods and then some settlement is almost bound to occur in the neighbourhood of excavations which have been back-filled. This does not con­stitute negligence by anybody, as it is the normal method of doing this sort of work."

The case of Harpur v. Swansea (6) cited above gives good guidance in the present case. In that case the Waterworks Authority opened and broke up the soil of a public road, which was within the district of and repairable by the County Council, and laid a water pipe therein, and the road was then filled in and brought to its proper condition. The whole of the work was carried out in a proper, efficient, and pg 322 Workmanlike manner under the superintendence of the surveyor of the County Council. Eight months after the completion of the work a portion of the road and a bank supporting it slipped, and the County Council repaired the damage at a cost of £87, and preferred a complaint before two justices under section 28 of the Water Clauses Act, 1847, to recover the cost of repairing the road. The said section 28 is exactly word for word with section 6 of the Gasworks Clauses Act, 1847, cited above.

It was held that the damage in that case was not done in the cause of the execution of the work as was in the case of Hornby v. Liverpool United Gas Light Co. (4) cited above, but was damage which resulted from the execution of the work, and therefore the claim did not fall within section 28 of the Act, put rather within section 6. It was held further that since by section 22 of the Land Clauses Consolidation Act, 1845, claims for compensation for any damage not exceeding £50 should be settled by two justices and by section 68 thereof claims exceeding that amount should be settled by arbitration or by jury, the justices had no jurisdiction to entertain it.

Section 6 of the said Act, which was held to apply, is similar to our section 3 (6) of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance and reads:

"Where by the special Act the Undertakers shall be em­powered, for the Purpose of constructing or supplying Waterworks, to take or use any Lands or Streams otherwise than with the Consent of the Owners and Occupiers thereof, they shall, in exercising the Power so given to them be subject to the Provisions and Restrictions contained in this Act, and if the Waterworks be situated in England or Ireland, to the Provisions and Restrictions contained in the Lands Clauses Consolidation Act, 1845, and if the Waterworks be situated in Scotland, the Provisions and Restric­tions contained in the Lands Clauses Consolidation, Scotland, Act, 1845; and shall make to the Owners and Occupiers of and all other Parties interested in any Lands or Streams taken or used for the Purposes of the special Act, or injuriously affected by the Con­struction or Maintenance of the Works thereby authorised, or otherwise by the Execution of the Powers thereby conferred, full Compensation for the Value of the Lands and Streams so taken or used, and for all Damage sustained by such Owners, Occupiers, and other Persons, by reason of the Exercise, as to such Lands and Streams, of the Powers vested in the Undertakers by this or the special Act, or any Act incorporated therewith; and except where otherwise provided by this or the special Act, the Amount of such Compensation shall be determined in the Manner provided by the said Lands Clauses Consolidation Acts respectively for determining Questions of Compensation with regard to Lands purchased or taken under the Provisions thereof, and all the Provisions of the said last-mentioned Act respectively shall be pg 323 applicable to determine the Amount of any such Compensation, and to enforce Payment or other Satisfaction thereof.

It seems to me that in this case it was the exercise of the power itself, i.e., the digging, especially during the rains, and back-filling the hole which caused the settlement. I hold therefore this claim comes within section 3 (6) of the Electricity Supply (Control) Ordinance, and that the plaintiff is entitled to compensation.

Now although the statement of claim attached to the petition called for damages, yet it is clear that it is for compensation and not for damages. I must add that if it had been a claim for damages in an action, on the case I would have said that I consider the act of the workmen in leaving the hole uncovered when they knew it was raining as acts of negligence; but I do not consider it necessary to go into the question of negligence and contributory negligence, since in my opinion they do not apply to this case.

And now to the compensation. Section 3 (6) of the Ordinance

provides that the compensation payable shall

.. be settled, awarded and paid in accordance (as nearly as may be) with the provisions relating to compensation which are contained in the Public Lands Ordinance."

Section 9 of the Public Lands Ordinance deals with determination of compensation. In assessing compensation under that section the court is not bound by any offer or any claim made by the parties. It has to assess an amount which is a reasonable cost of reinstatement.

As I have said before the expert witnesses in this case are agreed that the damage they each saw is a continuing damage, and there is evidence that more cracks have occurred since the premises were examined by the experts. While Mr. Hallam called for the defence is of the opinion that the repair suggested by Mr. Hogers in his letter is not a permanent cure, and that a permanent cure will be reinstate­ment, the cost of which will be not less than £1,500, at the same time he said that he would not condemn the building in its present state of damage. :Mr. Rogers admitted that his estimate of £550 is only a rough assessment of the cost of repair.

It appears to me from the evidence of both Mr. Rogers and Mr. Hallam that they are of the opinion that although the repair which will cost £550 will not be a complete cure to the damage, yet it is likely to arrest more serious damage occurring in the future. In the circumstances I think that the £550 will be a reasonable amount to award as compensation .

.Judgment accordingly.
