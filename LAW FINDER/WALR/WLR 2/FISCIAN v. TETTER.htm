                                                                                 FISCIAN v. TETTER

                                                       West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session          

                                                                   Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.J.A.

 

 

.

Land-Estoppel-Caim for declaration of title-Action by grantor of land­Title of grantor to his knowledge put in issue in earlier suit involving his grantee .

This was an action for a declaration of title to land. In an earlier action the defendant, then the plaintiff, had obtained a declaration of title to the land. In this earlier action the defendant, one Anna Tetteh, claimed title by virtue of purchase from a family of which the plaintiff in the present action had since become the head: the plaintiff (the present defendant) claimed his title by purchase from the successor in title of a different family. The issue in this earlier action had thus been as to which of the two families involved had been competent to grant a good title on conveyance and the judgment had gone in favour of the family from whom the present defendant derived his title.

On losing the earlier action Anna Tetteh had obtained a refund from her grantor-family of the purchase price she had paid for the land. The plaintiff Fiscian, the head of this family, then commenced the present action. He based his right to do so on the facts that, by refunding the purchase money to Anna Tetteh, his family now stood in her shoes vis-a-vis the defendant, and that his family had not been a party to, or represented in, the earlier suit between Anna Tetteh and the defen­dant. In the earlier action an application had been made for the joinder of the Fiscian's family as a co-defendant but this had been  pg193 refused by the Native Court in which the action commenced. This had been followed by an attempt by Anna Tetteh to obtain evidence from the family. This, too, had failed, partly because the then head of the family (Fiscian's predecessor and, as head, the proper person to give such evidence) was ill and partly because the family had declined to appoint another representative for the purposes of the litigation.

Held, the original estate or interest of the plaintiff's family had, to the knowledge of the family, been put in issue by their grantee of such estate or interest and, as a declaration of title had been obtained by the defendant against their grantee in respect of that estate or interest, the plaintiff's family were thereby estopped from denying the title of the defendant.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It is a feature of native law and custom that a grantor of land may be joined as a party to an action concerning the land in which his grantee is involved. In most cases the grantor is a stool or a family, so that the right to be joined exists for the protection of the rever­sionary rights, where the grant has been of a determinable estate, or perhaps for the safeguarding of adjacent land which is the property of the grantor.]

Obiter, the right to grant title to an area of the "Brazilian" lands in Accra vests in the particular family, being a constituent family of the" Brazilian" group, in occupation of such area ..

[EDITORIAL NOTE. This obiter ruling follows the decision in Oofie v. Ashong and Another (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 82. The decision in Oofie v. Aahong and Another was overruled by the West African Court of Appeal on February 11, 1957, but on different grounds.

I   In the present case the learned Chief Justice suggests that the immigrant Brazilians received grants of land by, and themselves adopted, Ga law and custom. The implication is that their estate in the land thus granted to them is a determinable one: nevertheless it appears that they are now able to alienate the land to strangers without the necessity for prior consents from the grantor stools. The general principle involved here may well become of considerable moment in the future development of land law in Ghana.]

     The plaintiff is the son of the late Isaac Cobblah Fiscian, who during his lifetime was one of the two accredited heads and repre­sentatives of the Aruna family. '1'he other was the late J. E. Maslino. There is evidence on record which proves that during the time these two men were heads and representatives of the said Aruna family, one or the other or both of them acted for and on behalf of the family in matters relating to family property and, in particular, sold, executed deeds of conveyance, instituted actions and defended suits with respect to Aruna family property-a portion of which is alleged to be the land now claimed by the plaintiff.

    There is also evidence showing that the identical piece or parcel of land the subject-Matter of this appeal was the subject-matter in the suit Joseph Tetteh v. Anna Owutokor Oracca Tetteh, which was commenced in the Ga Native Court" B." The plaintiff in that suit relied on a deed 6f conveyance dated October 11, 1937, and executed by D.S. Nelson (otherwise known as Chief Alasha), a Chief of the Brazilian community. It is admitted that the land in dispute is within the area of the large piece or parcel of land which the Ga Chiefs granted to the Brazilian com­munity, who were immigrants who arrived in Accra about 1836.

    The defendant in that suit, Anna Owutokor Oracca Tetteh, con­tended that she bought the land from Isaac Cob blah Fiscian, the immediate predecessor of the plaintiff in this suit. She attempted to serve him with a subpoena, but he was said to be too ill; in any case Isaac Cobblah Fiscian was in Accra as well as other members of the Arona family .. By the judgment dated January 20, 1947, the Ga Native Court declared Joseph Tetteh the owner in possession of the said land.

  In the course of the trial in this suit the plaintiff produced and tendered in evidence a receipt for the sum of £100 signed by Anna O. O. Tetteh, dated October 1, 1950, purporting to be money refunded by him as the successor of Isaac Cob blah Fiscian and representative of the Aruna family to Anna O. O. Tetteh in respect of the land. In this connection the plaintiff said:

" she claimed from me the amount which she alleged to have paid to my late father in respect of the land sold to. her. I produce the receipt which I obtained from her, it is dated 1st October, 1950."

     On October 18, 1950, the writ of summons in this suit was issued at the instance of the plaintiff against the defendant, who was the successful party in the suit between him and Anna O. O. Tetteh, the purchaser from the Aruna family. The judgment of the Ga Native Court was subsequently confirmed by the West African Court of Appeal; consequently a writ of possession issued and Joseph Tetteh, the defendant, was put in possession of the land.

     The Aruna family had, by a deed, transferred whatever right, interest and title they purported to have in the land to Anna O. O. Tetteh-and there has been a judgment for declaration of title and recovery of possession against her and in favour of the defendant in respect of the same land. As, in the course of this litigation, the title of the Aruna family, to their knowledge, was put in issue, the plaintiff is not competent, merely by refunding the purchase price to Anna O. O. Tetteh, to revise a title in the family in order to re-litigate the title to the land in a fresh suit at the instance of the family. In my view the defendant should have succeeded on the plea of estoppel against the Aruna family.

    The learned trial judge had unfortunately been influenced by the proposition which he propounds thus:

" It is beyond argument that those of the Brazilians who were farmers had the advantage to appropriate portions of the land to them to the extent where they cultivated and those who were not farmers did not appropriate nor had portions allotted to them. The G. A. Nelson family were not farmers but the Aruna family were. It is therefore questionable how G. A. Nelson, who was not from a farming family but was a tailor by trade, could appropriate a portion of the Brazilian community land by cultivation.' ,

The members of the community who appro­priated portions of the said land to their private use must have acquired possessory title only against the rest of the community.

It follows that the defendant, who derives title to the land by purchase from the nephew of G. A. Nelson, has a better title against all persons except the Brazilian community in whom the absolute title originally vested.
