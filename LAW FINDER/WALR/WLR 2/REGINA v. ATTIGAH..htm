                                                                  REGINA v. ATTIGAH.

                                                             West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                                Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.J.A

 

 

Criminal Law-Evidence-Admissibility of evidence as to good character.

The appellant, a police inspector, was convicted of extortion by a judge sitting with three assessors. The main evidence against him came from two men, one of whom was the victim of the alleged extortion. They asserted that an accomplice of the appellant, who was not present at the trial, had approached the victim with a story that they, the accomplice and the victim, would be prosecuted for an offence unless they provided the appellant with a certain sum of money. The assertions were supported by a receipt and a promissory note signed by the accomplice (but to neither of which the appellant was a party) and by the fact that the victim had, at one stage, been arrested. The appellant, in his defence, alleged a conspiracy against him by the two witnesses and led evidence as to his own good character. The judge held that evidence as to good character was immaterial before verdict. Of the three assessors, two were of the opinion that the appellant was guilty while the third was in doubt .•

Held, good character is a material matter for consideration where the evidence as to the commission of an offence leaves doubt: where there is no doubt as to guilt, good character provides no answer. Evidence as to good character is thus always properly admissible before verdict. In this case on the facts the learned judge should have directed himself on the possibility of conspiracy: had he done so doubts might have been introduced so as to make the evidence of good character material. As the rejection of this evidence deprived the appellant of a reasonable chance of acquittal he was entitled to have the verdict set aside.

Semble, evidence as to good character should always be submitted as material, whatever the weight of evidence against the accused.

Russell on Crimes, 7th ed., p. 2119, quoted with approval.

Cases referred to :

(1) R. v. Bliss Hill (1918) 13 Cr.App.R. 125. (2) R. v. Noble (1928) 20 Cr.App.R. 191. pg 211


 

APPEAL from a conviction for extortion by a public officer by Ollennu J. on November 11, 1956, sitting with assessors at Cape Coast Assize.

Unger, Crown Coun8el, for the Crown.

Koi Larbi for the appellant.

The following judgment of the court was given by COUSSEY P. Emmanuel Yaw Attigah was convicted at the Cape Coast Assizes on November 14, 1956, by a judge sitting with three assessors for that, on December 15, 1954, at Agona Swedru he did for the purpose of extorting the sum of £100 from Daniel Kwesi Obimpe, threaten to accuse the said Daniel K wesi Obimpe of an infamous offence namely, of intentionally and unlawfully setting fire to lorry No. W.P. 6786. He appealed to this court against his conviction.

The case for the prosecution depended mainly on the evidence of Obimpe and Robert B. Otoo, the second and third witnesses for the prosecution. The appellant claims that at the relevant time he was investigating a report concerning Obimpe made to him by one Esson, a convict on licence who had disappeared before the trial. Otoo, the appellant says, was not on speaking terms with him because the appellant, when a police sergeant at Elmina, had refused to permit Otoo to be a professional bailer of persons charged with offences. These men supported their story against the appellant with a promissory note, and a temporary receipt, to neither of which the appellant was a party but which, it is alleged, Esson took as the appellant's accomplice or agent.

Two of the assessors were of opinion that the appellant was guilty, one had a doubt and gave the appellant the benefit of that doubt. The learned trial judge in convicting the appellant as charged found as follows:

" I agree with the majority opinion of the assessors. I have no doubt at all in my mind that the defendant, acting in concert with one Esson, plotted to extract money from Obimpe; that in pursuance of that plan the defendant had Obimpe arrested at Takoradi under the pretence that an insurance company had directed that he and the said Esson should be prosecuted; that when Obimpe came to Swedru on December 13, 1954, the defen· dant had bail granted t{) him to report at Swedru Police Station at 2 p.m. on December 14, 1954; that on December 13, 1954, after Obimpe had been granted bail, Esson, pretending to Obimpe and Otoo that he, Esson, did not want to go to prison, left Obimpe and Otoo and went back alone to see the defendant and then returned to Obimpe and Otoo with a story whereby he induced Otoo and eventually Obimpe to go with him to the defendant; pg 212 that at that interview the defendant demanded the sum of £400 from Obimpe, as alleged by Obimpe and Otoo; that Obimpe agreed to pay, Esson having alleged he would be responsible for £200 of it; and that on December 15, 1954, Obimpe paid £100 to the defendant in part payment of the £400 demanded by the defendant and thereupon Obimpe was relieved from his obligation to report under the bail bond. ,.

The appellant was defended by counsel. Besides denying on oath the facts against him and accusing the principal witness of a con­spiracy to injure him by perjured evidence, the appellant put his good character in issue. He was an inspector of police who had been in the force for twenty-four years. He held the meritorious medal for long service; he had been awarded the Police medal for exceptional gallantry when on duty and he was the recipient of a letter of commendation from His Majesty's Secretary of State for the Colonies. He produced papers testifying to his general good reputation. In the following passage of his judgment the learned judge categorically held that the appellant's good character was a consideration immaterial before verdict. He said:

" Counsel for the defendant has urged with great labour that the defendant is not the type of person to commit such a crime in view of his good character and the very high commendations he has had in the police force. Those submissions are matters of sentiment which cannot be considered at this stage."

The principal ground of appeal, and upon which a strong submis­sion has been made, is that the learned judge erred in not directing the court and assessors to have regard to the appellant's proved character for rectitude and integrity as affecting the credibility of his evidence and. also not directing himself, when considering the pro­babilities of the case, to have regard to the especially ruinous consequences to him as a high police officer of knowingly making him­self party to any offence. This was the form of the argument in R. v. Herbert Bliss Hill (1), but the present case differs from that case because here the principal evidence upon which the appellant was convicted was tainted evidence given by the witnesses above referred to.

rt is true that the learned judge thought that Obimpe and Otoo gave their evidence in a straightforward manner, not shaken in cross­examination and, that they so impressed him as truthful witnesses that he was prepared to act upon their evidence even if there was no corroboration of it. But having anxiously pondered over the evidence we are unable to say that the matter was so free from doubt that the learned judge should not have considered it from the point of view of a possible conspiracy against the appellant.

On general probabilities it is unlikely that two not illiterate men pg 213 acquainted with police methods, when told by a police officer, as they allege, that he had a letter from an insurance company to charge them for burning a lorry in order falsely to claim the insurance money, would not either ask to see the letter or make discreet enquiries else­where as to the existence of such instructions before raising a loan, as they say they did, to square the police officer. The promissory note and receipt hung on tainted evidence. The police diary mayor may not have been concocted, according to the view that is take of the evidence.

 

The following passage from Russell on Crimes, 7th ed., p. 2119, is important:

., It has been usual to treat the good character of the party accused as evidence to be taken into consideration only in doubtful cases. Juries have generally been told that where the facts proved are such as to satisfy their minds of the guilt of the party, character, however excellent, is no subject for their consideration; but that when they entertain any doubt of the guilt of the party, they may properly turn their attention to the good character which he has received. It is, however, submitted with deference that the good character of the party accused, satisfactorily established by competent witnesses, is an ingredient which ought always to be submitted to the consideration of the jury, together with the other facts and circumstances of the case. The nature of the charge, and the evidence by which it is supported, will often render such ingredient of little or no avail; but the more correct course seems to be, not, in any case to withdraw it from considera­tion, but to leave the jury to form their conclusion upon the whole of the evidence, whether an individual, whose character was previously unblemished, has or has not committed the particular crime which he is called upon to answer."

Weare aware that there i,s a difference between the type of summing-up required when the case is being tried by a jury and when it is being heard by a judge sitting with assessors; in the second case it may be presumed that a judge is aware of legal principles governing .. the benefit of the doubt" but in this case it is clear from the judg­ment that the learned judge expressly excluded the evidence of good character in weighing the evidence. That there was some' doubt is clear from the fact that one of the assessors considered that the evidence was not sufficiently strong to support a conviction.

We think that if the appellant's character had been considered in relation to a potential conspiracy, the court might reasonably have come to the conclusion that the danger of the existence of such a conspiracy was sufficient to render it unsafe to convict the appellant, The question is, has the rejection of the evidence of good character in fact deprived the appellant of a reasonable chance of acquittal. If pg 214

  it has, then a sufficient injustice has resulted to entitle him to have the verdict set aside. In this case, we think it is impossible to say that with the rejected evidence before the court, the trial judge, as a reasonable judge of fact, would have convicted. 'fhe case is somewhat similar to R. v. Arthur Owen Noble (2).

For these reasons we allowed the appeal, set aside the conviction, entered a verdict of not guilty and ordered the immediate discharge of the appellant.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
