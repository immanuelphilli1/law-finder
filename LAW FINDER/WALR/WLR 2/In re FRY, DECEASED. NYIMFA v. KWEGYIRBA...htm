 January 21, 1957

                                     In re FRY, DECEASED. NYIMFA v. KWEGYIRBA..

                                                      West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                                   Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Verity Ag.JA

 

 

Wills - Extrinsic evidence - Latent equivocation - Specific devise 01 houses " the old one to my wife and children, the new one to maternal family."

The defendant was the executrix of her late husband: the plaintiff sued on behalf of the deceased husband's maternal family. The testator by his will made devises in the following terms:

" Buildings: the old one for my wife Ekua Kwegyirba and

children. The new one to maternal family."

The testator in fact left three houses. The first he built in Cape Coast; the second he bought in Kumasi before the first was completed; the third he built at Cape Coast adjoining the first, but this he did not live to complete. The plaintiff argued that the will was clear on its face and in the light of surrounding circumstances the two houses at Cape Coast were together one property and this comprised the "new one" devised to the maternal family.

Held, the wording of the will, in the light of surrounding circum­stances, disclosed a latent equivocation and therefore extrinsic evidence was admissible as to the testator's true intention. This evidence disclosed that the two houses at Cape Coast were respectively known as the old and new buildings and these therefore formed the subject-matter of the devises.

Cases referred to:

(1) Nowell v. Flannery [1936] Ch.D. 203. (2) Higgins v. Dawson [1902] A.C. 1.

ApPEAL from the decision of Acolatse J. on October 31, 1955, in the Divisional Court, Central Judicial Division, giving judgment for the defendant executrix in an action on the construction of a will.

Mercer for the plantiff.

C. F. Hayfron-Benjamin for the defendant.

COUSSEY P. On January 21, 1957, the plaintiff's appeal from the judgment of the Divisional Court, Cape Coast (Acolatse J.) rejecting pg 203 the plaintiff's construction of the will, dated September 28, 1952, of the deceased John Cassar Fry, was dismissed by this court. We now give our reasons.

The clause in the will to be construed is:-

" Buildings: the old one for my wife Ekua Kwegyirba and children. The new one to maternal family. "

The testator left a house at Kumasi known as Ebenezer House, which he bought after the building of the first house at Cape Coast had been started. After the acquisition of the Kumasi house, the testator completed his house already begun at Cape Coast and before his death he had almost completed another house, using as part of the ground floor of the second house the walls of what had been a store adjoining the first house at Cape Coast.

Counsel for the plaintiff argues that the two houses at Cape Coast comprise one property; that the Kumasi house is the one the testator intended to devise as the old building to his widow, the defendant and her children; and that the two houses together at Cape Coast pass under the will to the testator's maternal family, represented by the plaintiff, as the new building in the will mentioned. He agreed that the two buildings at Cape Coast were called the old and the- new building, but he submitted that evidence of intention is remote when the testator's words are clear and that the learned trial judge erred in admitting evidence to ascertain the testator's intention without considering the expressions and language used by the testator. Nowell v.   Flannery (1) and Higgins v. Dawson (2).

When it is admitted that the testator owned three houses at the time of his death, one at Kumasi and two at Cape Coast, and that the testator and his relatives referred to the two houses at Cape Coast as the old and the new building, to use the words of Lord Shand in Higgins v. Dawson (2), supra:

" you cannot tell exactly what is given, or to whom it is given, because of obscure and doubtful expressions of the testator's will in regard to the particular conditions of his property (and) you must have recourse to extrinsic evidence in order to ascertain his meaning. "

In this case I think the words used have no meaning without evidence as to what was known as the old building and the new build­ing and that the learned trial judge was right to take evidence as to the sense in which the testator used those expressions.

Having done so I am of opinion that the learned judge came to a right conclusion in dismissing the plaintiff's claim for the construction which he sought to set up.

The appeal was accordingly dismissed.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
