JACKSON v. COMMISSIONER OF INCOME TAX.

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Accra

Smith J

 

 

I     Income Tax - Additional assessments - Arrear.1 of salary, etc. - Whether: assessable in year in which paid or in years in respect of which paid­ Income Tax Ordinance, c. 27 of 1943, s. 53.

The appellant held a pubic office at a fixed salary, In March, 1947, there was a general salary increase but this was made retroactive to January, 1946, and as a result the appellant received a lump sum pay­ment by way of arrears of the increase. The Commissioner of Income Tax apportioned this sum between the years in respect of which it was paid and raised additional assessments for each of those years. The appellant contended that the arrears formed part of his income for the year in which the payment was made and that the Commissioner could bring the sum into the assessment for that year only.

Held, (i) by the terms of the Income Tax Ordinance, c. 27 of 1943, s. 53, an additional assessment on a taxpayer for anyone year will only lie in respect of income of the taxpayer for that year.

(ii) In the present case the payment, although in respect of an earlier period, was not made until 1947. During this earlier period the appel­lant had no entitJement to the sum that was eventually paid to him. Therefore and in the absence from the Ordinance of any provision equivalent to the Income Tax Act, 1918 (8 & 9 Geo. 5, c. 40), Schedule E, Rule 5 (which permits additional assessments for any year in respect of which additional salary, etc., is paid), the additional payment to the appellant fell to be assessed in the year in which payment was made.

The Commissioner apportioned this additional sum between the years of assessment in respect of which it was awarded and made two additional assessments on the appellant on the basis that he was liable to be assessed on the additions as if he had actually received them during the periods for which they were awarded.

The appellant has appealed against these additional assessments and argues that the whole of the arrears should be assessed in refer­ence to the year in which they were paid and not in the manner fixed by the Commissioner.

Income tax laws are notoriously difficult to construe and I have been much assisted by the arguments and authorities which the appellant and counsel for the Commissioner have put before me in this appeal.

The appellant conceded that if the matter fell to be determined under the ineome tax laws of the United Kingdom, the Commis­sioner's construction would be correct, and in this regard he has referred me to Hule 5 of Sehedule E to the Income Tax Act, 1918. He argued, however, that there is no provision in the local Ordinance corresponding to this and submitted that the case of Dracup v. Radcliffe (1) and the Irish case Af'Keown v. Roc (2) do not conclude the question in issue. Rule 1 of Schedule E makes the tax chargeable on every person having an office or employment in respect of " all salaries" for the year of assessment and Rule 5 provides that:

" If at any time either during the year of assessment or in respect thereof, a person becomes entitled to any additional salary beyond the amount for which an assessment has been made upon him ... , an additional assessment shall ... be made upon him in respect of any such additional salary,"

with a corresponding right in the taxpayer to have the assessment reduced if he has suffered any reduction in salary.

The judgments in the two cases above cited suggest that the learned judges construed Rule 1 as attaching liability for the tax to the salary in the year in which it was earned, whether it was actually paid within that year or not, and considered this construction fortified by the provisions of Rule 5, which permits the assessment to be adjusted in the light of events which may occur after the end of the year of assessment.

The Gold Coast Ordinance is quite differently worded and while the decisions in these two cases are helpful, I cannot regard them as direct authorities on the points at issue in this appeal.
