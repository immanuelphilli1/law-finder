         Divorce-A.rbitration-Adultery- Whether reference to Customary arbitration condones adultery.

Divorce_ Adultery-Condonation-Revival-Condurt not amounting to legal cruelty.

The petitioner sought a decree of divorce on the grounds of her husband's adultery. The respondent admitted the adultery but claimed that it had been condoned by the reference of the matrimonial dispute to a customary arbitration, the acceptance and observance by the parties of the arbitration award and the subsequent resumption of cohabitation. The petitioner admitted the arbitration but claimed that the respondent had failed to observe the terms of the arbitration award in that he had continued to meet the co-respondent, had failed to repay money borrowed from the petitioner and had failed to repay expenses incurred by the petitioner in an illness consequent upon childbirth. These the respondent denied but it was established in evidence that in one respect at least he had failed to observe the terms of the award.

Held, (i) condonation by the petitioner of the adultery was dependent upon the observance by the respondent of the terms of the arbitration award. The respondent had not observed the terms of the award and therefore there had been no condonation of the adultery.

(ii) Even if the reference to arbitration had operated to condone the adultery, upon the facts the respondent's subsequent behaviour towards the petitioner, if not amounting to cruelty sufficient to support a decree of divorce, was nevertheless sufficiently cruel to revive the condoned adultery.

Richardson v. Richardson ([1950] P. 16; [1949] 2 All E.R. 3;Wj 65 T.L.R. 613) foJJowed.

PETITION for a decree of divorce on the grounds of the respondent's adultery .

Amoo-Lamptey for the petitioner.

Sowah for the respondent.

DENNISON J. The petitioner was married to the respondent on August 7, 1952, at the District Registry, Accra. After the marriage the petitioner and the respondent cohabited at Koforidua. As a result of that cohabitation there was one child of the marriage who has unfortunately died. The petitioner is a school teacher employed by the Anglican School at Koforidua; at the time of the marriage the respondent was an undergraduate at the University College.

The petitioner seeks dissolution of her marriage on the grounds of the respondent's adultery with a woman named Matilda Agriba Hagan. The respondent admits this adultery but states that it has been con­doned by the petitioner. The condonation was alleged to have taken place at an arbitration held by both parties where the arbitrators made an award to pacify the petitioner. The respondent states that he followed the award made by the arbitrators; this the petitioner denies. Further, the respondent states that after the arbitration he and the petitioner resumed their normal marital relationship. Both parties were agreed that the award of the arbitrators was to the effect that the respondent should put the woman named out of his house, also that he should repay the expenses incurred by the sickness suffered by the petitioner after the birth of her child, and that he was to repay money which he had borrowed from the petitioner. The petitioner stated in evidence that the respondent had carried out none of these conditions at all; the respondent on the other hand stated that he paid off a certain amount of the debt to the petitioner's parents in respect of loans and he paid this money through the priest at Koforidua. He said he paid £10 but when he sent the third cheque the priest returned it to him. He also stated that he left Miss Hagan alone and had nothing more to do with her from the time of the arbitration; he said he had never even seen Miss Hagan since the arbitration; he also stated that after the arbitration he pacified the petitioner with four guineas.

The respondent stated that after the petitioner became sick, which illness was a mental breakdown consequent upon childbirth, he did not, on the advice of Dr. Morton, have sexual intercourse with the petitioner as the doctor stated that for at least one year after she was cured it would be injurious to her health. The petitioner stated that on medical advice she was free to have sexual intercourse but not to bear children for at least one year after her recovery. The petitioner stated that the respondent had had three children by the woman named. The respondent admits to having had twins by this woman.

With regard to the credibility of the witnesses I have no doubt at all in deciding that the petitioner is by far the more truthful of the two; she struck me as an intelligent and truthful witness. She gave her evidence without any hesitation and answered all the questions put to her without any undue delay. The respondent on the other hand was evasive and slow to answer.

The respondent stated in evidence that he had not seen Miss Hagan since the arbitration held on 1\lay 17, 1955. However, in cross­examination, a photograph was put to him which showed him and Miss Hagan together and he admitted, being faced with this photo­graph, that it was taken in August or September, 1955. That was well after the arbitration, and, of itself, is sufficient to prove that the respondent is not a witness of truth. I therefore accept in the main the evidence given by the petitioner. This being so it has to be con­sidered what the position is with regard to the condonation contained in the arbitrator's award of May 17, 1955. The award was a condi­tional award; that is if the respondent carried out the conditions imposed on him by the arbitrators the wife was ready and willing to forgive and return to cohabit and live with the respondent. It is perfectly clear from the evidence that the respondent at no time gave up his association with Miss Hagan and therefore one of the most important conditions of the award was not fulfilled. This being so I do not agree that the adultery had been condoned. Even if the adultery had been condoned, I consider that the conduct of the respondent after the condonation was such as to revive the original adultery, the petitioner was a very sick woman and the respondent deliberately carried on an association with this woman named which in my opinion can only be construed, in the words of Denning L.J., in Richardson v. Richardson (1):  that it consists of hardship or neglect of a real and substantial kind and which is such as to be likely to inflict misery on the innocent party and which has indeed led to a complete breakdown of the marriage. ,.

I do not for one moment believe that the respondent has ever been willing to return to his wife, nor to accept that he is willing to dissociate himself with Miss Hagan or with the remaining child that she has borne him. For the reasons given I order that a decree nisi issue in favour of the petitioner in respect of the said marriage.

Decree granted.

The plaintiff is an Osu subject and a member of the Anahor Quarter; the defendant, hailing from Akwapim, is not an Osu subject. She is therefore regarded by the plaintiff for the purposes of alienation of Osu lands as a "stranger." It is unnecessary to go into the question of who is or is not a stranger in the eyes of the Osu or Christiansborg custom, or into the necessity or otherwise of obtaining the consent of the Osu Manche to perfect the defendant's interest in the land derived from the head, that is, the" Shippi " of the Anahor Quarter, through a grantee of that quarter. While I must admit that the defendant's legal interest in the land may be defective, it is, never­theless, not necessary for me to go into the details as she is a defendant in possession and does not counterclaim any right of title or interest against the plaintiff. It is on him that the onus lies to establish his title in support of his claim to recovery of possession and of mesne profits. Essentially therefore, the plaintiff in effect seeks for a declaration of his title in respect of the land in dispute and he can only succeed on the strength of his own case, as the weakness of the defendant's case or any defect in the defendant's title cannot help him.

Now, what is the strength of the plaintiff's case? It is as follows;­As from December 13, 1939, the plaintiff was led to believe he had acquired title in respect of the land, a portion of which is now in dispute, under and by virtue of a customary grant by the headman of the Anahor Quarter, as evidenced by an indenture purporting to confer on him an absolute grant in fee simple. But unfortunately for him the said stool had previously, by a deed of conveyance dated September 24, 1939, divested itself of all its right, title and interest by conveying a large area of land, including the piece alleged to have been granted to the plaintiff, to the Government of the Gold Coast for the purposes of erecting thereon structures for the temporary accom­modation of people rendered homeless as a result of a recent earth­quake. The position therefore is that at the time of the alleged grant to the plaintiff the Anahor Stool had no legal title to the land which it could validly convey to the plaintiff. It is however argued on behalf of the plaintiff that by paragraph 9 of the deed of conveyance to the Government the plaintiff must be deemed to have acquired a certain equitable interest in futuro in respect of which he could maintain an action.
