                                                               West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

     Criminal Law-PerJury- Sumrnmy procedure under Criminal Proced1lre Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 149 (1) (b).

     The second appellant was plaintiff, and the first appellant one of his witnesses, in a civil action arising from the sale of a motor-car. At the close of the plaintiff's case the judge recalled the first appellant and interrogated him on his earlier evidence. Thereafter the judge charged the first appellant with perjury in the words:

     "I am satisfied that you committed wilful perjury ... "

      The judge then recited the facts on which the charge of perjury was based and called upon the first appellant to show cause why he should not be committed to prison. The first appellant replied that he had told the truth. He was then sentenced to imprisonment.

     At the close of the case for the defence, but before giving judgment, the judge called back the second appellant and, using substantially the same words as had been used with the first appellant, charged him with perjury also. The judge recited the facts on which the charge of perjury was based and called on the second appellant to show cause why he should not be committed to prison. The second appellant replied that he had been relying on his memory but believed what he had stated was true. - He, was then sentenced to imprisonment. On appeal against these convictions.

     Held, (i) as the proceedings under the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 149 (1) (b), are in the nature of summary trial a conviction under this section will be set <1 side, in the same way as any other conviction in a criminal proceeding, if it appears that the court may have prejudged the issue before it. \

          (ii) When a charge of perjury is made under this summary procedure the points of conflict of the evidence should be brought to the attention of the person charged in such manner and in such detail as to enable him to know precisely in respect of what parts of his evidence he stands charged with perjury. For this reason it is desirable that where the summary procedure is used it should follow, not precede, the giving of judgment in the case in which the perjury appears to have taken place.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. See also Commissioner of Police v. Wood (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 71 on this point.]

   APPEAL from convictions by Manyo- Plange J., in the Divisional Court, Central Judicial Division given on July 20 and 23, 1956, summarily convicting the appellants under the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 149 (1) (b) of perjury. pg130

I COUSSEY J. delivered the judgment of the court.

The question to be determined in this appeal is not whether there were circumstances in the conduct of the two appellants which might require to be dealt with but whether the course which the learned judge took was a regular and proper one in the circumstances.

The issue before the court in the suit of Nana Abutakyi, plaintiff, and Nana Tepor alias J. Quayson, defendant, out of which this appeal arises, was whether a motor-car had been sold by the plaintiff to the defendant, as the plaintiff claimed, or whether the plaintiff had sold it to the defendant's son and not to himself, as the defendant con­tended. Besides his own testimony on this issue the second appellant (who was the plaintiff) called Kofi Boye, Peter Kwamina Mensah (the first appellant), Charles Bentil Addison, Kyiriwiah Kordia Ababio, Kobina Tua, Ekow Quansah and Araba Akyiniba, as witnesses to to support his case.

Towards the close of the plaintiff's case the plaintiff (now the second appellant) was recalled by his counsel, with leave, tQ put in evidence certain letters written to him by the first appellant concerning the car in question. The court then itself interrogated the second appel­lant, not on the letters then produced in evidence, but on matters relating to the car and the licence for the car, and the court then recalled Peter Kwamina Mensah, the first appellant, and interrogated him upon his earlier evidence as to the delivery of the car to the defendant on November 15 as he alleged and not to the defendant's son.

Immediately after, upon the close of the plaintiff's case, the learned judge proceeded summarily ·under section 149 (1) (b) of the Criminal Procedure Code against the first appellant Mensah for perjury. In doing so the learned judge addressed the first appellant as follows:

" I am satisfied that you committed wilful perjury by falsely and knowingly stating on oath that on the 15th November, 1955, you took a Vanguard car to Agona; that you saw the defendant Agonahene and that you delivered the car to him; that you spoke to him asking him for £25 and he told you he did not have it and told you he was expecting money from Omanhene of Bensu. I therefore call upon you now to .show cause why I should not commit you to prison."

To this the first appellant answered:

" God will bear me out that I spoke the truth. I have nothing more to say. "

   thereupon the learned judge committed the first appellant to prison for four months at hard labour, observing that:

" in the face of evidence to the contrary you have persisted in your falsehood."  pg131

  Upon the close of the defendant's case, after witnesses had been called for the defence and after counsel had addressed the court, but before delivering judgment, the learned judge initiated summary pro­ceedings for perjury against the plaintiff, now the second appellant in the following terms:

" John Asmah, I am satisfied that on the 18th July, 1956, you were guilty of perjury in these proceedings when you stated on oath on that day that on the 14th November, 1955, the defendant in your house asked you for the licence of the car and thai you gave it to him after you had endorsed the transfer on it. That statement you know to be false. I believe the evidence that the licence was endorsed at Agona on the 13th which is confirmed by your own admission that sometime in October you handed the licence to Aggrey and heard nothing more about it. I now call upon you to show cause why I should not commit you to prison as for contempt of Court."

    The second appellant answered:

" I believe what I stated to be true. The transaction took place in November and I have been away for a long time and my mind was not so much on this matter and so I was solely relying on my memory as to what I conceive to be the truth of the matter. I therefore throw myself on the mercy of the Court."

   In committing the second appellant to two months' imprisonment with hard labour the learned judge observed that he was satisfied that the second appellant had made that statement:

., deliberately knowing it to be false just to back your story that the sale of the car was to the defendant. "

The learned judge added inter alia:

" I have made it abundantly clear in this Court that I am deter­mined to make an example of any I find guilty of perjury before me by way of deterrence to others. "

  It seems to us that in stating to each appellant that he was satisfied of his perjury instead of making a charge of perjury before calling upon him to show cause, the learned judge had prejudged the matter and, in the circumstances, had placed himself in such a position that any explanation of the appellants would be valueless and would not be accepted by the court.

  It is also" unfortunate that although the alleged false statements were put to each appellant, their attention was not directed to the conflicting evidence apart from an alleged admission of the second appellant which had so satisfied the learned judge. It was in the judgment delivered later after the committals that the conflict in the evidence for the plaintiff is dealt with.

    The learned judge's attitude to the plaintiff's case is portrayed in the following extracts from the judgment delivered after the committal of the appellants. pg132

" I have no hesitation without even considering the evidence of the defendant's two witnesses in preferring the defendant's €vidence as being more consistent with the truth ....

"I do not believe the evidence of plaintiff, his witness Tua and the driver Boye as to the payment of £75 by the defendant through Boye ....

"Needless to say the evidence of the plaintiff's witnesses Addison and Araba Akyiniba is no~hing but It tissue of lies not worthy of consideration ....

" The plaintiff's witness Mensah swore persistently that he on the 15th November personally delivered the car in the afternoon at Agona to the defendant in person. This has been proved to be deliberate perjury by :l\Iensah by the evidence of Ekow Quansah, the plaintiff's driver, who drove the car on the 15th to Agona for delivery and the evidence of N ana Kordia, both the plaintiff's wi tnesses ....

" From the letters put in by the plaintiff I am satisfied that for some reason best known to the plaintiff he chose to hold the defendant liable for the unpaid balance by his son .... It is possible that for some reason not proved the plaintiff regarded the defendant as responsible for the sale of the car to his son and therefore fabricated evidence to support his claim."

     The power to commit as for contempt is arbitrary and unlimited and for that reason should be most carefully and jealously watched and exercised with the greatest reluctance. There is no doubt that the learned judge took a strong view against the appellants on the conflict of testimony between them and two of the plaintiff's own witnesses, and was thereupon moved to adopt the unusual course of committing before judgment in the suit. He had apparently declared his intention to purge the court of perjury by extreme measures. But in view of the two letters referred to from which it is clear that some months before action the appellants regarded the contract as having been made with the defendant and not his son, it is unfortunate that the judge took the course he did against the appellants. The fact is that the second appellant had taken a mistaken view to enforce his rights.

In the circumstances, being satisfied, as the learned judge said lie was, in his charge of the perjuries, that there was contempt of the authority of his court it would have been more satisfactory in our opinion, on the conflict of evidence that had moved him, if he had adopted the other course, which, it should be observed, is the first mode of procedure under section 149 (1) of the Criminal Procedure Code, namely to commit for trial upon information for perjury when the matter would be reviewed by another tribunal free from any objection of arbitrariness. It is also difficult to appreciate why the learned judge discriminated between the appellants and the other pg133 witnesses of the plaintiff whom he considered had also committed perJury.

          In all the circumstances the course taken should not in our opinion be sustained. For these reasons we allowed the appeals and set aside each committal order. There will be no order as to the costs of the appeals.

Appeals allowed.
