October 30, 1956.

NUNOO AND OTHERS V. NYIMFA AND OTHERS.

West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session  

                                                               Coussey P., Korsah C.J., van Lare J.

 

 

Practice-Concessions-Order of court by consent on cancellation of conces­sion-Application to enforce order under Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Order 42, R~de 23, or under Concessions Ordinance, c. 136 of 1951, s. 19.

The' appellants had applied to the Land Court for a certificate of validity for a concession. The respondents opposed the application and at the enquiry reached agreement with the applicants whereby the lease, the subject-matter of the concession, was to be cancelled on certain terms, one of which was to be the preparation of a new lease containing different provisions. These terms were by consent made an order of the court. Subsequently the appellants returned to the court for an order enforcing the terms of the settlement on the grounds that the respondents had pg162  refused to make any move towards agreeing a new lease. They relied upon the provisions of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Order 42, Rule 23. Alternatively they claimed that the order of the court was an order within the meaning of the Concessions Ordinance, c. 136 of 1951, s. 19, and should thereby be enforced as a decree of the court.

Held, (i) the result of the cancellation of the first lease was that there was thereafter no concession before the court. As there could thus be no enquiry by the court, 110 interlocutory application on an enquiry could be made. If the order were to be enforced it would need to be done by a substantive action.

(ii) The Concessions Ordinance, c. 136 of 1951, s. 19, deals with the powers of a court where it has decided that a concession, or any part of a concession, is invalid. In the present case no concession had been declared invalid, and therefore the aid of section 19 could not be invoked.

   Case referred to:

(1) Pryer v. Gribble (1875) 10 Ch.App. 534.

   APPEAL from a decision of Acolatse J. on October 22, 1955, in the Land Court, Central Judicial Division, dismissing an application by claimants at a concession enquiry for the enforcement of an order of the court made on the cancellation, by the parties and at the enquiry, of the concession.

Bentsi-Enchill for the appellants.

Reindorf for the respondents.

COUSSEY P. This matter arose in the Land Division of the Supreme Court. By virtue of section 6 of the Concessions Ordinance, c. 136 of 1951 the court has power, jurisdiction and authority to enquire into a concession and certify the same as valid or invalid. Section 8 of the Ordinance provides that notice of a concession on a specified form shall be filed in the court within two months of the date thereof.

The claimants, who are now the appellants, in Concession Enquiry No. 2478 (Cape Coast) sought to have validated as a concession a lease granted to them by certain named grantors. Opposition to the grant of a certificate of validity of this concession was filed by the opposers, who are now the respondents, pursuant to section 14 of the Ordinance, on grounds stated in the notice of opposition filed, namely, inter alia. that the proper persons had not been made parties to the concession.

In the course of the enquiry that ensued, the parties, by their counsel, informed the court that a settlement had been reached the terms whereof had been reduced into writing. Those terms were by consent made an order of the court on December 2, 1954. So far as they are relevant to this appeal they were:

.• 1. The present lease, the subject-matter of this enquiry to be cancelled .

.. 2. A new lease to be drawn up and substituted therefor (naming the persons who should execute the new lease) .... pg163

"4. Terms of new lease stated (which differed from the terms of the lease, the subject of the enquiry) ....

.. 9. Form of the lease as finally drafted to be approved of by the parties before execution and grant of certificate of validity."

        On April 5, 1955, the claimants filed an application by motion in the Land Division of the court under Order 52, Rule 4 and Order 42, Rules 23, 26 and 30, of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, and in the same Concession Enquiry, for an order enforcing the terms of settlement on the grounds that although the new lease had been drafted and sent to the solicitors of the opposers, the opposers had refused to approve the same or assign any reason for such refusal or to make counter proposals for embodiment in the new lease other than a request for a mass meeting of townspeople to discuss the terms of the lease. The application to the court was resisted by the opposers.

       The learned judge refused the motion, holding that the lease the subject of the enquiry had been cancelled and that there was therefore, at the date of the application, no concession before the court which court be the subject of enquiry.

     From this refusal the claimants. have appealed and Mr. Enchill for the claimants endeavoured to convince us that since the Concessions Court is part of the Supreme Court, the Civil Procedure Rules of Court applied and therefore by virtue of Order 42, Rule 23 the Land Division had jurisdiction to enforce the settlement agreed to. He also argued that since under section 19 of the Concessions Ordinance the court upon declaring that a concession or part thereof is invalid may make such order for the settlement of any question arising with respect to any such concession as it shall deem just, the Concessions Court had power to enforce the terms of settlement as a decree of the court. The short answer to this latter point is that the court did not declare the concession or part thereof invalid nor did it declare it valid. In the relevant Concession Enquiry No. 2478 (Cape Coast) the lease was cancelled. The Enquiry thereupon terminated in no order being made, although upon the application of the parties the court, for purposes of record only, set out the terms of a new agreement between the parties. Strictly, however, it was not within the jurisdiction of the court in the enquiry to record any such consent and certainly it was not open to the parties to enforce the terms of a settlement, disputed by the opposers, by interlocutory application in the same Concession Enquiry. It is, in the words of James I..,J. in Prycr v. Gribble (1), at p. 539:

" a contract, the specific performance of which is beyond the scope of the suit, and cannot be obtained except by a suit regularly instituted for that purpose. "

When the claimants secure a new lease, they will, if they require it to be certified as a concession, be obliged to pursue afresh the procedure laid down by section 8 of the Concessions Ordinance. pg164


We consider that the learned judge was right in declining jurisdic­tion in the matter and we accordingly dismiss this appeal with costs.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
