GBADAGO v. TSILL 

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra

van Lare J.

       Native Courts-.Jurisdiction-.Judgment of Native Courts- obtained by fraud - Jurisdiction of Native Courts- to entertain an action alleging that a Judgment has been obtained by fraud.

Judgment was given by a Native Appeal Court, in a land dispute, in favour of the plaintiff. The order of the Native Court was entered in the record book not at the time of delivery in court, but privately, in the house of the President of the Native Court, several days later. When the plaintiff obtained a copy of the Judgment" so written he found it differed materially, and to the advantage of the defendant, from the judgment delivered orally in court. He then commenced an action in the Native Court" A" (which was the same body as the Native Appeal Court, but sitting in a first instance capacity), for rectification of the judgment on the ground that it had been obtained by fraud. For the defence it was argued that the Native Court was not competent to entertain such an action.

Held, (i) the essential ingredient of a claim for the setting aside of a  judgment on the grounds of fraud is that the judgment concerned must have been obtained as a result of a fraud practised upon the court (usually by way of fraudulent evidence). In the present case there was no allegation that the judgment given orally in open court had been obtained by such fraud: the fraud had arisen after judgment and therefore could not affect or influence it.

(ii) On the facts there had been a failure by the Registrar of the Native Court to comply with the duty, statutorily laid upon him by the Native Courts (Colony) Procedure Regulations, No. 10 of 1945, Part 7, Regulation 41, to enter the order of the Native Court in the record book. This constituted a violation of a legal right entitling the plaintiff to maintain an action for an order that the extraneous passage should be deleted from the record book.

(iii) Although such an action might be novel in a Native Court yet, since it was but the application of a principle already known to the law (rather than the introduction of a new principle), its novelty was no bar to it and the Native Court therefore had jurisdiction to hear the action and make the order sought.

Best v. Samuel Fux ,(; Co., Ltd. and Anuther ([1950J 2 All E.R. 798) applied.

    'Dicta (i) it is part of the inherent jurisdiction of any court to entertain a motion by which it  alleged that its own judgment has been obtained by fraud and, if such fraud shall be established, to make such order as may be necessary in the circumstances of the case.

Cases referred to:

(1) VandeRpuye v. Plange and Others (1942) 8 W.A.C.A. 170.

(2) KWaa, Kwesi v. KofI Kwaklm (1937) 3 WA.C.A. 176.

(3) Best v. Samuuel Fux & Co. Ltd. and Another [1950J 2 All E.R. 798.

APPEAL from a judgment on January 24, 1950, of the Native Court " A" of Anlo State in favour of the plaintiff in an action for the setting aside of part of a judgment given by the same court- when sitting as a Native Appeal Court.

Akufo-Addo for the plaintiff.

Ollennu for the defendant.

VAN LARE J. This appeal is within a small compass but it raises some interesting points of law which I should very much like to have considered by a higher court. The plaintiff took action against the defendant in the Native Court" A " of the Anlo State, and the claim in the writ is as follows:-

.. The plaintiff claims that part of the judgment of the Keta Native Appeal Court in case Atiavishie and others v. Chief Tsili II and others dated the 24/1/50 be set aside on ground of fraud and conspiracy by 1st defendant and the Registrar Native Appeal Court (Mr. Fred Acolatse)."

.. The part referred to reads as follows:-

. We hereby give Chief Tsili II and his tribe their ancient right of hewing down palm trees on the Atiavie land as described and bounded in the writ of summons, that Chief '1'sili II and his tribe have the right to take steps against any farmer who uproots or damages any of the palm trees growing on these lands.'

" The said fraud consists in the 1st defendant and the Registrar of the Native Appeal Court aforesaid conspiring together and inserting the said passage in the judgment, when the judgment delivered by the said Court did not contain that passage."

Prima facie this is an action to set aside a judgment on the ground of fraud, and Mr. Akufo Addo for the plaintiff argues that it is such a case. On the other hand Mr. Ollennu for the defendant says that it is in effect a claim to vary or review a judgment and that no such action is maintainable before the Native Court" A " in respect of a judgment of the Native Appeal Court of the Anlo State notwithstanding that the Native Court" A " and the Native Appeal Court are one and the same. In my view although the composition of the Native Court " .\" and the Native Appeal Court may be the same, nevertheless they are two distinct courts; the former sits as a court of first instance and the latter as an appellate court and they exercise separate juris. dictions; they are not courts with co-ordinate jurisdiction. In any event the only procedure to secure a review or variation of a judgment of the court before itself is by motion and not by an action. I do not, however, think that such procedure is available )n the Native Courts; therefore if the action is in effect to vary or review a judgment of the Native Appeal Court I "could say that it is misconceived as the Native Court" A " has no such jurisdiction. But I do not agree that the action is one to review or vary a judgment. of the Native Appeal Court because the gravamen of the claim appears to be that the portion of the judgment complained of "'as not in fact included in the judg­ment which was delivered by the Native Appeal Court.

Is the claim then essentially one to set aside a judgment obtained by fraud as contended by Mr Akufo Addo? Again I do not agree although in form it appears so to be.

In all suits before the Native Courts, it is to the issue to be tried that one must look and not the form. The Supreme Court has always been disposed to assist the Native Courts rather than be too insistent on technicalities or forms of actions which may prove obstacles in the "'ay to their carrying out of administration of justice.

Assuming that this is essentially a claim to set aside a judgment. on the ground of fraud I shall have to decide first of all whether a Native Court is competent to entertain such an action having regard to the law, i.e., Native customary la\\', which is administered in the Native Courts (see the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s, 15), The origin for an action to set aside a judgment obtained by fraud appears to be foreign to Native customary law. In this connection I refer to Halsbury, 2nd ed .. VoL 19, para. 564:

" A judgment, which has been obtained by fraud either in the court, 01' of one 01' more of the parties, can be impeached by means of an action which may be brought without leave and is analogous to the former Chancery suit to set aside a decree obtained by fraud. "

Since the right to bring an action to set aside a judgment obtained by fraud is ascertainable or arises only by reference to English law, I have a grave doubt in my mind whether a Native Court has an inherent jurisdiction to entertain such a suit. In Vanderpuye v. Plange and Others (1), it was held that a covenant for title to land has no existence in Native customary law, and that a claim for damages for breach of covenant of title is not maintainable in a Native Court. But on consideration of the case of Elcesiuzeaa v. KofI IKwakwa (2), 1 am inclined to the view that there is a necessary inference that a Native Court is competent to adjudicate in a suit to set aside a judg­ment irregularly obtained. In that case there was a claim in a Native Tribunal to set aside a previous judgment of the same tribunal on the grounds of lack of jurisdiction, irregularity, mistake and fraud. The \Vest African Court of Appeal held that a court cannot set aside a judgment regularly obtained in it. The necessary inference would appear to be that such a court is competent to set aside its own judgment irregularly obtained, such as by fraud. The court was then dealing with a case originating before a Native Tribunal. In the course of its judgment the West African Court of Appeal said as follows:

" There is ample authority for the proposition that a judgment which has been obtained by fraud either in the court, or of one or more of the parties, can be impeached by means of an action which may be brought without leave and which is analogous to the former Chancery suit to set aside a decree obtained by fraud, but we know of no authority for the proposition that a judgment given in respect of land may be subsequently impeached in another action on the ground that the land the subject-matter of the action lay in fact outside the jurisdiction of the court."

I am disposed to form the opinion that the West African Court of Appeal meant it to be understood that a Native Court could entertain a suit to impeach a judgment on the ground of fraud but I am not quite clear whether I am right in this view and I would therefore be glad to have a clarification by a higher court on this point of law, that is to say, whether a Native Court has an inherent jurisdiction to entertain an action to set aside a judgment on the ground of fraud the origin of which cause of action is based on English law and not on Native customary law. But, as I have already said that I do not agree that the instant action before the Native Court "A" is essentially and to set aside a judgment on the ground of fraud, it is unnecessary for me to decide whether or not a Native Court has an inherent jurisdiction to entertain such a suit.

It is rather my considered opinion upon a careful scrutiny of the proceedings before the trial Native Court that the real issue in this case is whether a certain portion as appearing in the written judgment of the Native Appeal Court was in fact included in the judgment delivered. In other words, the plaintiff's claim is that a certain portion complained of purporting to be a part of the recorded judgment of the Native Appeal Court was irregularly included, and that the offending portion as alleged was not contained in the precise judgment delivered.

In my view the allegation of fraud and conspiracy by the Registrar of the Native Appeal Court and the defendant in this case is immaterial; the only issue appears to be whether or not the portion of the judgment complained of and decreeing a certain right in favour of the defendant was in fact so pronounced by the Native Appeal Court which delivered the judgment. That the issue as contested cannot be deemed to be a suit to set aside a judgment on the ground of fraud is clear because, agreeing with the submissions of Mr. Ollennu, I concede that the essence of an action to set aside a judgment on the ground of fraud is constituted by the following:- that the judgment was in fact delivered or pronounced-in this case the allegation is that the offending portion was not in fact delivered nor pronounced;

(b) that the judgment sought to be set aside was delivered relying upon evidence which has been proved to have been obtained by fraud; and

(c) that but for the said fraudulent evidence the judgment would have been otherwise.

In this case there is no suggestion that there was any piece of evidence fraudulently procured to enable that portion of the judgment complained of to be obtained. It is clear therefore that this is not an action to set aside a judgment obtained by fraud but in my view an action essentially one for declaration that a certain portion of the judgment of the Native Appeal Court "'as not in fact delivered or pronounced and ought therefore to be deleted and/or declared a nullity.

Even if such a claim for declaration be considered a novelty it would nevertheless not prevent the Native Court from entertaining it. Equity will not suffer a wrong to be without a remedy. Native custo­mary law recognizes a right to make a declaration at the instance of a party entitled thereto and this action can therefore be nothing new in principle. On the authority of Best v. Samuel Fox J; Co. Ltd. and Another (3) the novelty of an action is no bar to it provided it is only a new instance of a principle known to the law and not new in principle.

In this appeal it was the duty of the Registrar of the Native Appeal Court to record in precise terms the oral judgment delivered by the court (Regulation 41 of Regulations 10 of 1945). A portion of a recorded judgment by the Registrar of the said court is impeached on the ground that it was not in fact delivered and therefore ought not to be included in the recorded judgment. In my view if a registrar of a Native Court includes in his recorded judgment a portion decreeing a right which the court did not in fact decree then surely there arises a violation of a legal right giving a cause of action to the party detrimentally affected against the party favourably affected. The case of Best v. Samuel Pox, etc. (3) is also an authority for the proposition that the violation of a legal right committed knowingly gives a cause of action.

The question for decision in this appeal is one of fact which the trial Native Court has determined. There is evidence in support of the finding of fact by the Native Court" A " that the offending passage was not a decision of the Native Appeal Court and the trial Native Court is therefore right in ordering that the said passage be deleted.

I therefore confirm the judgment appealed from; and the appeal is accordingly dismissed with costs.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
