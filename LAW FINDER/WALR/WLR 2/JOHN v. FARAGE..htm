                                                                               JOHN v. FARAGE.

         Supreme Court of the Colony of the Gambia

Miles J.

 

 

Bailment-Contract of hire-Implied warranty of fitness.

The plaintiff claimed damages for breach of a contract of hiring. He had hired a lorry from the defendant on agreed terms for a period of three months. Seven days after taking possession of the vehicle the plaintiff returned it to the defendant and determined the hiring, claiming that the lorry had broken down consistently and was not suitable for use. The defendant subsequently re-hired the lorry to a third party who used it without complaint.

Held, (i) in a contract of hire the bailor of the chattel is under an obligation to ascertain that the chattel so let out is reasonably fit and suitable for the purpose for which it is expressly let out or for which, from its character, he must be aware it is intended to be used; his delivery of it to the hirer amounts to an implied warranty that the chattel is in fact as fit and suitable for that purpose as reasonable care and skill can make it. SlItton v. Temple «1843) 12 M. & W. 50; 52 E.R. 1108) referred to, (ii) On the evidence the plaintiff had failed to establish that the vehicle was unfit for the purpose for which it was hired.

Case referred to:

(1) Sutton v. Temple (1843) 12 M. & N. 50; 152 E.R. 1108.

ACTION for damages for breach of implied warranty for fitness in a contract for hire.

FOTStCl' for the plaintiff.

The defendant appeared in person.

IHILI':S J. 'l'he plaintiff in this case elaims the sum of £81 513. advanced to the defendant on account of the hire of a motor lorry No. 0072 and there is a claim for damages for breach of contract, it being alleged that the lorry was in a defective condition. 'l'he lorry was hired by the plaintiff from the defendant for three months com­mencing on December 2, 1055, under an oral agreement. There is a conflict of evidence as to whether the rate of hire was £3' 1013. per day or £350 for the period but in view of the conclusion to which I have come it is unnecessary to go into this issue. A written agreement was prepared by the defendant but the plaintiff refused to sign it because it contained a provision that the plaintiff should be responsible for the salary of the driver, to which the plaintiff had not agreed.

'rhe lorry was delivered to the plaintiff at Jawara on December 10, HJ55. It was accompanied by a driver supplied by the defendant who has now gone to the Senegal and cannot be traced, and also a mechanic, Lamin Bah, who was called as a witness on behalf of the plaintiff. According to the plaintiff, there was trouble with the gears on December 11, 1955; on the 12th it broke down again but the cause of the trouble has not been ascertained. The plaintiff says it had cut-out trouble on the 13th. On the 14th it worked without breaking down but on the 15th the battery ran down. The plaintiff accordingly decided to return the lorry to the defendant on December 17, and he determined the hiring. The plaintiff says that he had an interview with the defendant on December 12 at Bathurst but this is denied by the defendant. The plaintiff was not a satisfactory witness and I prefer the evidence of the defendant who says that he did not see the plaintiff until after the end of the trade season.

In the case of a contract of hiring the owner of the chattel is under an obligation to ascertain that the chattel is reasonably satisfactory for the purpose for which it is hired:

"The nature of the contract is such, that an obligation is imposed on the party letting for hire to furnish that which is proper for the hirer's accommodation."

per Lord Abinger in Sutton v. Temple (1) at p. 60. The question is whether the plaintiff has proved that the condition of the lorry was such that it was not reasonably satisfactory for the purpose for which it was hired. In my opinion the plaintiff's case breaks down on the evidence which he himself has adduced. The plaintiff is not a mechanic and his evidence as to the various breakdowns is vague in the extreme and would not warrant my acting upon it alone. The only persons who would be in a position to speak on this matter are the driver, who is not now available, and the mechanic, Lamin Bah, who has given evidence. According to the mechanic the only defect was in the head lights, of which the bulbs were useless. He says that the plaintiff decided to return the lorry to Bathurst because the driver refused to work the lorry at night without lights. There would have been no difficulty in obtaining spare bulbs at Bathurst and if this was the only thing wrong it certainly did not render the lorry unsatisfactory for the purpose for which it was hired nor justify the plaintiff in determining the hiring.

The matter does not rest there, however, because after the plaintiff determined the hiring the lorry was hired to one Momodou Drammeh for the trade season and was worked for him from December 18, 1955, until April 18, 1956. Momodou Drammeh was called by the plaintiff and said that during the whole of that period the lorry never broke down on a single occasion. Mr. Forster has argued that it is unlikely that the plaintiff would have taken the drastic step of determining the hiring if all the lorry needed was a couple of head lights, but even if there were other defects they could not have been very serious, because as previously stated, the lorry worked without any trouble for the whole of the trade season. There is no evidence that any repairs were done to the lorry between the 16th and 18th December. If there were any other defects apart from the bulbs they were not sufficient to amount to a breach of warranty so as to justify the plaintiff in determining the hiring.

The plaintiff has failed to prove to my satisfaction on the facts that the lorry at the time of the hiring or during the time that it was in the possession of the plaintiff was in such a defective condition as to amount to a breach of warranty. The claim for damages accordingly fails. There was not a total failure of consideration and the plaintiff is not entitled to the return of the money which he had advanced. There will be judgment for the defendant with costs.

Judgment for the defendant.

S. G. D.
