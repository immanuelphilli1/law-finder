  November 29, 1956

     BLANKSON-MILLS v. BAKO. AND BLANKSON-MILLS.

                                                        West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session 

                                                          Coussey P., Korsah C.J., van Lare J

 

 

Practice- Appeals to Privy Council-Delay in applying for final leave to appeal-West African (Appeal to Privy Council) Order in Council, 1949, s. 20.

The applicant, in these consolidated suits, having obtained conditional leave to appeal to the Privy Council, had waited for more than six years before seeking final leave to appeal.

Held, it is implicit in the West African (Appeal to Privy Council) Order in Council, 1949, s. 20, that an applicant for final leave to appeal to the Privy Council must make his application with due diligence. If this is not done, final leave will, if it is opposed, not be granted except for cogent reasons, as for example where the delay has been due to accidental or other circumstances over which the applicant had no control and where there has been no negligence by him.

    APPLICATION for final leave to appeal to the Privy Council from a judgment of the West African Court of Appeal dated May 18, 1949. Conditional leave to appeal was granted on September 13, 1950.

Danquah for the applicant.

Bentsi-Enchill and Amoo-Lamptey for the opposers.

The ruling of the court was given by COUSSEY P.

       This application for final leave to appeal to the Judicial Committee of Her Majesty's Privy Council is made after a lapse of more than six years from the date of the grant of conditional leave to appeal, namely, September 13, 1950. pg182


   One of the conditions imposed by this court when granting leave to appeal was that the appellant should within three months give notice of appeal to the respondents. This condition is not provided for by section 6 of the Order in Council regulating appeals. The applicant says he duly perfected the other conditions of appeal but the notice of appeal directed was not given as it is not necessary nor provided for and as notice of the intended application for conditional leave to appeal had already been given to the respondents.

    In his affidavit in support of this application the applicant then says:

" Having perfected and completed what was required of me, I waited on counsel for the appeal to be completed and transmitted to the Privy Council. That in or about April, 1956, after a continuous number of visits to my counsel, my brief was returned to me " ;

   whereupon other counsel who was instructed advised that no applica­tion for final leave to appeal had been filed.

   It is true, as pointed out by counsel for the applicant, that the respondents did not, in the intervening six years, apply to the court to rescind the order granting conditional leave to appeal as provided for by section 20 of the Order in Council, but we do not think that failure on the respondent's part can avail the applicant. It is implicit in section 20 that the appellant must apply to the court for final leave with due diligence.

The applicant had the carriage of his order for leave to appeal.

      Even after he became aware of the situation on April 24, 1956, he waited until July 18, 1956, before filing this application. But no cogent reasons have been shown in support of this application, such as some accidental circumstance or otherwise over which the applicant had no control or that there has been no neglect in pursuing the appeal.

      We consider that the applicant is asking for an indulgence from the court. Having regard to the delay that he has allowed to take place and his neglect to apply with due diligence for final leave to appeal, we do not think this is It case in which the final leave to appeal now sought should be granted in an appeal which could only involve a simple issue of fact upon which two courts have come to concurrent findings adverse to the applicant.

      The application is accon!il1g1y dismissed and the applicant will pay the respondent's costs.

Application dismissed.

S. G. D.
