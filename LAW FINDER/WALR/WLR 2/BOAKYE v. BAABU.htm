     BOAKYE v. BAABU

                                                        West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session 

                                                          Coussey P., Korsah C.J., van Lare J

Practice--Native Coutts-Visit of court to "locus in quo "-Duty of Native Appeal Court towards findings of fact by t1'ial Native C01l1't.

This case is reported for the directions given by the court to Native Courts, and particularly to Native Appeal Courts, on the subject of visits by such courts to a " locus in quo."

The plaintiff claimed a declaration of title to certain land: the defence was a prior grant of the same land. The plaintiff argued that if there had been an offer of a grant to the defendant, she had refused it. The issue was thus one of fact: there was no dispute as to the identity of the land. The trial Native Court heard the evidence, preferred that of the defendant, and found for her. On appeal the Native Appeal Court paid a visit to the " locus in quo" and then reversed the decision of the trial Native Court on the ground that it was "erroneous and must be reversed. "

Held, although a visit by a Native Court to a "locus in quo" is sometimes useful and may indeed be essential, the members of a Native Court must guard against allowing their impressions, gained on such a visit, to replace the conclusions they should properly draw from the evidence given in court and the demeanour of the witnesses who give it. This applies to a Native Appeal Court with particular force, for such courts, which do not have the advantage of seeing and hearing the witnesses, must not use their own impressions, gained on such a visit, as a substitute for the reliance they should place on the conclusions drawn by the trial Native Court, which has seen and heard the witnesses.

[   EDITORIAL NOTE. See further on this point Ketekll and Others v. Dzogbenuku alld Others (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 134 and Dza Y. Komla (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 145.]

   VERITY Ag.J.A. This is an appeal from a judgment of the Land Court, Kumnsi, whereby on an appeal from a Native Court of Appeal the original judgment of a Native Court of first instance was restored. The plaintiff had claimed a declaration of title to certain land which he alleged had been granted to him by the present occupier of the Bare Stool. The defendant averred that the same land had been pg184 granted to her at an earlier date by the previous Barehene and the court of first instance found in her favour and dismissed the plaintiff's claim.

    The issue appears to have been a simple one of fact, for it was conceded before us by counsel for the plaintiff that if the ex-Barehene had in fact previously granted the land to the defendant a subsequent grant to the plaintiff by the present occupier of the Stool was of no effect. The plaintiff contends, however, that no such grant was made to the defendant but that when an offer of the land in dispute was made to her in addition to certain adjoining land she had rejected it on the ground that the land was swampy and unsuitable for cultivation. The defendant's case is that she at no time rejected the area in dispute but that when it was offered to her she in common with others who had been offered similar land in that area reserved the right to cultivate it after they had finished cultivating the higher ground adjoining.

    The court of first instance in plain terms rejected the evidence of the plaintiff and his witnesses and accepted that of the defendant and her witnesses on the ground that the former was conflicting and the latter " very corroborative." The Appeal Court, however, after visit­ing the locus in quo (a course not pursued by the trial court) allowed the appeal on the ground that the judgment of the trial court was .. erroneous and must be reversed," two members of the Appeal Court expressing the view that the judgment of the court below was not based on the evidence. With this view the learned judge of the Land Court did not agree.

     The Native Court of Appeal appears to have based its judgment largely on what its members saw and heard on the visit to the land, but I am at a loss to understand why they found such a visit necessary, for neither the location nor the area of the land nor, indeed, its boundaries were in doubt, and it is difficult to see how in the present case any observation of its physical feature could have assisted them in coming to a conclusion in relation to the conflict of evidence in the court below. Such visits are in many cases useful: at times they may be essential, but they lie open to the danger that the members of the court may find themselves in the position of substitu­ting their own observations for the sworn testimony in matters which cannot be determined thereby. This is more especially the case in regard to an appeal court which has not seen or heard the witnesses under examination by the more orthodox methods of a trial court. In the present case the Appeal Court while acknowledging the established rule as to the functions of such a court in dealing with the findings of the trial court appears to have allowed itself to be led astray by the ,events of the visit, and in this I think they fell into error.

   The issue was no more than to decide what happened six years earlier when the defendant and nine other persons were offered the pg185 swamp land in that area. There was a conflict of evidence which the trial court was bound to resolve and which it did resolve by its judgment of the credibility of the witnesses who testified before it. Having accepted as the more reliable the testimony of the defendant and her witnesses, they could do no other than enter judgment in her favour. I agree, therefore, with the learned judge of the Land Court that in the circumstances of the present case

.• the Appeal Court was not entitled to reverse the findings as to the veracity of the witnesses merely as a result of visiting the land. "

I would therefore dismiss this appeal with costs .

COUSSEY P. I concur.

KORSAH C.J. I concur.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
