    Native Law and Custom-Land-Long possession-Effect of laches by owner.

In 1893 and 1895, the defendant's family, acting through its head, conveyed its absolute estate in certain land to one Russell, a European. There were subsequent transfers of the land by conveyances which, although lost, were established by evidence to have existed, and as a result the land, in 1916, became vested in one Renner. In 1932 Renner made the plaintiff caretaker of the land and in 1936 made him an absolute gift of it. The vesting in the plaintiff was not evidenced by a formal deed of conveyance but evidence existed that it had taken place and the plaintiff remained in possession until dispossessed by the defen­dant's family in 1950. In an action by the plaintiff for a declaration of his title to the land, the defendant's family, sued in the person of their head, relied on the absence of a conveyance as between Russell and Renner, and claimed that the death or abandonment of the land by Russell, by native custom, caused it to revert to the family as the original grantors. At first instance, before the Native Court (but after are-trial had been ordered by the West African Court of Appeal) judgment was given for the defendant, apparently on the ground that the plaintiff had failed to establish a title to the land and that, although he had been in possession for a long time, in the absence of prescription in native custom, he could not succeed against the defendant's family, as the original grantors of the land.

Held, (i) land alienated absolutely will escheat to grantor in the event of it becoming bona vacantia either from voluntary abandonment or through the death of the grantee without a successor. In the present case, whatever the position of the original grantee (and there was no evidence that he had died) the land had been effectively conveyed from the grantee and had never been abandoned.

Sasrakn v. Okille alld Others «1930) 1 W.A.C.A. 49) referred to. (ii) If it were assumed that the land had not been duly vested in the plaintiff or in his grantor, yet the defendant family could not succeed as against the plaintiff in possession, for although a prescriptive title is unknown by native custom the possession of the plaintiff could not be defeated save by a superior title in the defendants, and they had divested themselves of this absolutely by their conveyance to the original grantee. The right, if any, to an escheat does not amount to a title sufficient to defea t possession.

(iii) If it were further assumed that the defendants had never divested themselves of their superior title, yet they could still not succeed against the plaintiff in possession, by reason of their laches.

   APPEAL by the plaintiff from a decision of the Cape Coast Municipal Court on August 9, 1956, giving judgment for the defendant in an action for a declaration of title to land, and injunction and damages for trespass.

Aunoonor- Williams for the plaintiff.

Hayfron-Benjamin for the defendant.

     OLLENNU J. By his writ of summons issued in the Native Court " B " of Oguaa State, Cape Coast, on December 17, 1951, the plaintiff claimed declaration of title to a piece of land situate at Jukwa Road, Cape Coast, commonly known as Renner's Russell Farm, £50 damages for trespass and an injunction. To these claims the defendant pleaded not liable. At the close of the plaintiff's case, the Native Court gave judgment for the defendant without calling upon him for his defence, holding that the plaintiff had made no case. The plaintiff appealed from that judgment to this court, which dismissed the appeal, but substituted a non-suit for the judgment of the Native Court dismissing the plaintiff's claim. From that judgment the plaintiff appealed to the West African Court of Appeal which, in its judgment delivered on March 16, 1955, allowed the appeal, holding that the two letters dated September 8 and October 2, 1937, written by P. A. Renner to the plaintiff and tended by him were wrongly rejected, and that the plaintiff established a prima facie case which the trial Native Court should have called upon the defendant to answer. The court thereupon remitted the case to the Native Court "B" of Oguaa State for hearing by a different panel de novo. The fresh hearing by the Native Court commenced on October 7, 1955. It appears that after May 8, 1956, the name of the court was changed, because the record shows that at the next sitting of the court, which was on May 15, 1956, the court had come to be known as the Municipal Court of Cape Coast. That court concluded the hearing and delivered its judgment on August 9, 1956.

   The plaintiff's case briefly stated is as follows. The land in dispute originally belonged to the defendant's family. By two deeds of con­veyance in English form, dated respectively November 11, 1893 and September 10, 1895, and admitted and marked as exhibits in the case, the defendant's family, acting by their head, Amba Kuma, conveyed their title therein absolutely to one H. B. W. Russell. H. B. W. Russell transferred it to his company, H. B. W. Russell & Co. Ltd., which in 1916 sold it to the late P. A. Renner. Renner entered into possession immediately upon the purchase, and he, his agents, servants, grantees, and licencees, have continued in possession ever since, undis­turbed and exercising full and open acts of ownership. In 1932, Renner made the plaintiff caretaker of his estate in Cape Coast and Sekondi, which estate included the land in dispute, and in 1936 made a gift  pg157 of this part of the land to him, demarcated it and placed him, the plaintiff, in possession. He says the conveyance from H. B. W. Russell or H. B. W. Russell & Co. Ltd. to Renner could not be traced, but he tendered in evidence the two letters dated September 8 and October 2. 1937, from Renner to him. In the letters reference is made to the two deeds: these are the letters referred to in the judgment of the West African Court of Appeal remitting the case back to the Native Court for hearing de novo. In 1950, the defendant entered upon the land claiming it to b~ his family property.

    The defendant in his defence admitted that his family divested themselves of all their right title and interest in the land to H. B. W. Russell. He also admitted the long undisturbed possession of Renner and his agents, including the plaintiff. At first, he said this was only up to 1932, but he appeared later to concede in the Municipal Court, and definitely conceded in this court, that such possession continued up to the date when the cause of action arose. He pleaded however that the land reverted to his family upon the death of Hussell, because Russell was a stranger, and, according to native custom, land sold to a stranger reverts to the vendor upon the death intestate of the purchaser without an heir or successor.

In their judgment delivered on August g, 1956, the Municipal Court found the following facts.

(i) By the conveyance of November, 1893, and September, 1895, the defendant's family conveyed the whole of their right title and interest in the land to the said H. B. W. Russell.

(ii) The long possession of Renner and those claiming through him as alleged by the plaintiff was not disputed.

(iii) " In 1950, the defendant having realized that the plaintiff was occupying the land in dispute without title deed entered upon the land" and that act necessitated this action.

They held that" in the absence of any title deed between H. B. W.

Russell and P. A. Renner, from whose interest the plaintiff derives his prescriptive title, ... the plaintiff has failed to discharge the onus of purchase and therefore cannot succeed on the question of long possession." I interpret this passage to mean that because of the plaintiff's inability to produce a deed of conveyance of the land from H. B. W. Russell to P. A. Renner, his case must fail in spite of all other evidence on the record. To me this is a very strange proposition. I cannot accept it. Are there not many people who have acquired property by deed in one form or another, and having lost the deed through fire, theft or some other means, and not having registered it, cannot produce a deed to support their title in a law suit? Are they to be deprived of their property, however valuable, simply because of the absence of the document, if there is other convincing evidence of their ownership of the property?  pg158    
 

    The Municipal Court .further held, relying upon a passage in the judgment of Deane C.J. delivered in the Divisional Court and printed with the judgment oi the West African Court of Appeal in Sasra];u Y. Okine and Others (1), that since Russell had died without any heir the defendant was entitled to take possession of the land in dispute as pleaded by him. I am unable to accept this finding also, for reasons which I shall give later.

As regards the first finding, in addition to the plaintiff's oral evidence that there was a conveyance to Renner which could not be traced, the first letter from Renner to the plaintiff made two significant statements. 'these are: "Look in the paper box and on my desk if you can find a draft deed made by me in your name in relation to my land at Russell :Farm and send it to me immediately." . " "I hope to prepare your document soon, meantime put your pillars on the points shown you in company of Willie and Halm," and: "I have got the original relative deeds between Amba Euma, H. n. W. Russell & Co. Ltd., and myself which I passed on among other Aboom Deeds to the Catholics for perusal. "

The second of these statements satisfies me that there were in existence, as far back as 1937, original relative deeds between Amba Kuma, H. B. W. Russell and Renner upon which Renner based his ownership of the property in dispute. This fact taken together with the forty years of undisturbed occupation and exercise of ownership, with the full knowledge of the defendant's family, leads me to the irresistible conclusion that Renner's possession and occupation amounts to ownership derived from Amba Euma through Russell. Also, the first of the statements I have referred to in the first letter together with the second letter and other evidence on the record satisfy me that Renner did in or about 1937 convey the land in dispute, or a portion thereof, to the plaintiff, and placed him in possession of it--as distinct from the plaintiff's position as agent of Renner.

The Municipal Court, although they had the judgment of the \Vest Africnn Court of Appeal before them, completely disregarded the hro letters (in my view very important pieces of evidence) in their con­sideration of the case. This, in my opinion, has led them to a miscarriage of justice by their judgment.

I am entitled, sitting as a Court of Appeal, to consider these letters along with all the other evidence in the case and the judgment of the Municipal Court, and to come to my own conclusions. Having done so, I am compelled to hold that the plaintiff discharged the onus which was upon him to establish his title, and judgment should have been entered for him. As I understand the native custom taken together with the principles of equity which apply to this country, long undis­turbed possession such as was proved in this case can only be defeated by an owner who has never divested himself of his ownership of the land. The basis of a claim against long possession is the continuity pg159 of title over the years without a break. Once a break occurs, the former owner is in no better position than any other stranger to the land. Here the defendant admitted, and the Municipal Court found as a fact, that b,y the conveyances made in 1893 and 1895 the family, the former owners of the land, completely divested themselves of the whole of their right title and interest in the land. There was no continuity of their ownership; there was a break in it, and they became strangers as far as the land is concerned. Granted then, for the sake of argument, that the plagiarist that possessory title? None whatsoever. Again, even if they had never specifically divested themselves of their title, their laches in this case, in my opinion, could amount to fraud and they would be estopped by their conduct in standing by while Renner, his agents, grantees and licensees occupied the land for such a long time, reasonably regarding it as their own and spending money to maintain it. Equity will not permit them to benefit by that fraud. In my opinion, upon such a strong case as \\'as made by him, the plaintiff should have been the one to succeed if the defendant had chosen not to lead any evidence at all, or if the evidence he has led was not such as could in law or fact rebut the plaintiff's case.

That takes me to the second finding made by the Municipal Court, namely, that since Russell, a stranger purchaser, had died without an heir, the property by native custom reverted to the defendant's family.

The passage in the judgment of Deane C.J. in the case of Sasra7m v. Qh'inc and Others (1), which the Uunicipal Court relied upon as authority for that finding, reads:

" It is quite true that a chief who sues for stool lands sues as representing not only himself but all the members of his tribe which have an interest in the stool or communal lands, but when his predecessor has once parted with the lands by sale to a stranger neither he nor the members of his tribe can have any further right in the land except on the rcmote possibility that the lands may revert to the stool if the purchaser dies intestate and wthou t heirs." (The italics are mine.)

That remote possibility can only arise where the land is left vacant, unoccupied, and no person claims title to it either by sale or otherwise from the stranger purchaser. It certainly is not the case here where the plaintiff claims title through Russell, the stranger purchaser.

For the defendant to succeed on this plea, granted the land were vacant, he must, in my opinion, prove each of the following things: that the purchaser "'as a stranger; that he is dead; that he died intes­tate; and that he has no heir or successor. If he fails to prove anyone of these he must fail. Asked by the court whether there was sufficient evidence on the record in proof of those essentials of the plea, Mr. Hayfron-Benjamin, who originally argued the case for the defendant. pg160 referred the court to the following passages in the record as evidence in proof thereof. The passages he referred to appear at pp. 21 and 25 in the evidence given by the defendant. These are:Page 21: "My predecessor Kobina Moore informed me that Russell, a European, bought a portion of the land, an area near the Fosu Lagoon, but later Mr. Russell left the Coast before 1914 and never sold nor bequeathed this portion of the land to anybody, and as a foreigner without any successor the land devolved upon the original owner (i.e., my family)."

Page 25: "The land in dispute had devolved upon my family since Mr. Russell, the foreigner \d1O bought the land, had since died. "

    Counsel had to concede that although those passages constitute some evidence he could not urge that they were enough to discharge the onus. Under those circumstances he said he could not proceed with his argument in support of the judgment.

    At that stage Mr. Hayfron-Benjamin applied for leave to retire from the case as his client thought he could argue the appeal better . I myself noticed the way in which the defendant was behaving in court towards his counsel, who was doing his very best, and I felt obliged to grant counsel's application. I accordingly granted leave, and Mr. Hayfron-Benjamin retired from the case.    

   When he took up the argument for himself the defendant referred the court to the very passages Mr. Hayfron-Benjamin had previously referred to, and to two other passages in his evidence, which appear at pp. 28 and 29. The passage at p. 28 reads as follows:

"When the building on the property fell and Russell did not return to the land, we then decided that the land should devolve on the original owners as Russell was not in need of the property."

The passage on p. 29 reads:

" Russell died 20 years before the death of late Amba Kuma."

It is of interest to note the sentence which immediately follows that passage at p. 29. It says: " Amba Kuma took no steps about the land after the lapse of 20 years when Russell died." The sentence which follows the passage referred to at p. 28 also reads: "During the life· time of late Amba Kuma she did not challenge Renner's occupation on the said land. "

Asked by the court whether he thought that the passages he had referred to were sufficient to prove the four elements of his plea, he replied: "I admit there is no clear evidence on the record in proof of those facts," and " I submit however that I refuted part of the prima facie case made by the plaintiff." He concluded his submissions in these words: "In any event I do not accept the judgment or opinion of the West African Court of Appeal ... that the plaintiff made a prima facie case. " pg161


    If I am right in my opinion as to the essential elements which must be proved before the plea put up by the defendant can be sustained, then I say that like both Mr. Hayfron-Benjamin and the defendant I can find nothing on the record which satisfies me that each of the essentials of that plea has been proved. That being the case, I hold that the defendant failed to discharge the onus which was on him, and that he was unable to meet the very strong case made by the plaintiff. It follows that I must allow this appeal. The appeal is allowed and the judgment of the Municipal Court is set aside including the order as to costs. In substitution therefor judgment is hereby entered for the plaintiff for a declaration of his title to the land situate at Jukwa Boad, Cape Coast, commonly known as Renner's Bussell Farm, and described in the writ of summons, and £50 damages for trespass. Since it appears from section 40 of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance that the jurisdiction of the Native Court to grant an injunction is limited k> an interim injunction, I cannot, sitting as a Court of Appeal on It judgment of a court constituted under that Ordinance, grant It relief which that court is not empowered to grant. I therefore say nothing about the injunction.

The plaintiff must have his costs in this appeal. He will also have his costs in the first trial in the Native Court and the fresh trial in the Native Court and Municipal Court to be taxed.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
