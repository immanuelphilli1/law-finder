April 28, 1956

ANKWANDAH v. GWIRA STATE COUNCIL.

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Western Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Sekondi

Windsor-Aubrey J.

 

.

       State Councils-Jurisdiction---Purported conviction by State Council for an offence under the State Councils (Colony and Southern Togoland) Ordinance-Certiorari-State Councils (Colony and Southern Togoland) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, ss. 5 (3), 9 and 26.

The applicant had been summoned before the Gwira State Council in connexion with destoolment proceedings but he had failed to attend. The State Council thereupon purported to convict him of an offence under the State Councils (Colony and Southern Togoland) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 26, and fined him £20. By this section of the Ordinance the obstruction of proceedings before a State Council is made an offence punishable by fine or imprisonment. The applicant thereupon sought an order of certiorari to quash the conviction. For the State Council it was argued that failure to respond to a summons to attend before a State Council was an offence by native law and custom in addition to being an offence under section 26 of the Ordinance and that, as by section 5 (3) of the Ordinance a State Council is empowered to conduct its pro­ceedings according to native customary law as well as having the powers of a magistrate's court to compel the attendance of witnesses, the State Council had jurisdiction in convicting the applicant. Alternatively it was argued that the failure of the applicant to respond to the summons was a constitutional matter and that the sum which the applicant was required to pay was a customary award authorised, in matters of a constitutional nature, by section 9 of the Ordinance.

Held, (i) the conviction of the applicant purported to have been made under the State Councils (Colony and Southern Togoland) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 26. Contravention of this section is not a matter of a constitutional nature but is a criminal offence and, as the Ordinance gives no jurisdiction to State Councils in criminal matters the conviction must therefore be quashed.

Obiter, even if the failure of the applicant to attend before the State Council could be considered to be a matter of a constitutional nature, yet the penalty imposed on the applicant was a fine, not a customary award, and by the terms of the Ordinance (and in particular section 9 thereof) a State Council is expressly debarred from imposing fines.

(ii) Although section 5 (3) of the Ordinance empowers a State Council to conduct its proceedings in accordance with native customary law, neither this nor any other section of the Ordinance gives jurisdiction to State Councils in matters of native customary law.

APPLICATION for an order of certiorari to quash a purported con­viction under the State Councils (Colony and Southern Togoland) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 26, made by the Gwira State Council on January 11, 1956.

Brandford-M cnsah for the applicant. Mercer for the Gwira State Council.

WINDSOR-AUBREY J. This is an application for an order for certiorari to quash the proceedings before the State Council on the grounds that the State Council, being only authorised to try consti­tutional matters, has acted without jurisdiction in convicting the applicant of an offence contra section 26 of the State Councils Ordinance, 1952, and in imposing a fine of £20.

It appears that the applicant failed to appear at destoolment proceedings. Destoolment proceedings are, of course, a constitutional matter, but although those proceedings could have been carried on without him, the act of the applicant is, in my opinion, clearly an offence under the Ordinance committed during those proceedings. The applicant's misconduct was in no sense whatsoever a constitutional matter. It is, however, also argued for the respondent that as the State

Council in the charge framed alleged in the alternative that the appli­cant had committed a wrong according to Native Customary law the Council had jurisdiction because section 5 (3) of the Ordinance empowers it to conduct its proceedings according to Native Customary law.

In my opinion that argument is untenable. In the context the reference to Native Customary law clearly applies only to the form of procedure to be adopted at proceedings and gives no power to the Council to try offences contrary to Native Customary law. For the above reasons this application must succeed and it is unnecessary to determine whether the punishment was a fine or a customary award of a civil nature within the meaning of section 9, because, having held the Council was trying a criminal offence, it had no jurisdiction to hear the case. I would, however, express the opinion that the order requiring the applicant to pay £20 was the infliction of a fine and not a customary award.

An order of certiorari is granted and it is ordered that the proceed­ings be brought into this court to be quashed and they are hereby quashed, a formal order to be drawn up. The applicant is awarded his costs.

Order granted.

S. G. D.
