    OFUMAN :STOOL v. NCHIRAA AND BRANAJvI STOOLS.  

                                  West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                            Coussey P., I\orsah C.J., Verity Ag.J.A

Native Courts-Practice-Traditional evidence-Limitation on parties competent to give traditional evidence.

Practice - Appeals - Land Court - Power of Land Court as an appellate Court  to direct retrial by a Native Court-Manner 0/ exercise 0/ power­ Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99 0/ 1951, s. 28 (1) (and similarly, the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 0/ 1951, s. 50)

In this action between three stools and concerning land in Ashanti, each stool was represented before the Native Court by a person holding a power of attorney and purporting to appear on behalf of his stool under the provisions of the Native Court (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99 of 1951, s. 20. After hearings before the Native Court and Native Appeal Court the action reached the Land Court, on appeal, where it was held that the powers of attorney were defective in that they had not been validated by the elders and other appropriate stool officials and that in any case the persons appointed were neither competent and qualified to act as representatives nor qualified to give evidence as to tradition. The traditional evidence that had been given was thus inadmissible as evidence and, as its striking out resulted in leaving insufficient evidence upon which the Native Court could have arrived at a fair decision in one direction or another, the Land Court exercised its statutory power of ordering a new trial. From this decision one defendant appealed, arguing, inter alia, that under the provisions of the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, s. 20, it was open to the appropriate stool authori­ties to appoint any person they saw fit as a representative, and that by virtue of such appointment the person concerned was held out by the stool as competent to give traditional evidence.

Held, (i) the class of persons who may be appointed by a stool for the purposes of litigation is strictly limited. It includes the linguist, elders and office holders of the stool (who are its natural representatives) but the class wiII not be extended beyond these save in the case of a particular stool where a local custom, proved to the satisfaction of the court, establishes that some other person or persons are entitled to represent the stool concerned. In the present case, regardless of the pg 230 effect of the powers of attorney, the persons appointed were not members of the permitted class and therefore, as no particular local customs had been established to vary the rule, they were not competent to represent their stools.

(ii) The power of determining whether or not a person is competent and qualified to give traditional evidence (such evidence forming an exception to the rule forbidding the admission of hearsay evidence) is vested in the court, which must satisfy itself that a person held out as competent and qualified to give such evidence is so competent and qualified. The class of persons so competent and qualified is strictly limited and will not be extended save in particular cases where a local custom extending the class is established: the fact that a person is appointed as a representative to give traditional evidence does not of itself establish that he is competent and qualified to give such evidence.

 

[EDITORIAL NOTE. See also Nyarko v. Abunyawah (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 137.]

(iii) The power vested in an appellate court by the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99 of 1951, s. 28 (1) (and the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s. 50), to order a rehearing is a discretion which must be exercised judicially. If it is so exercised, this exercise will not be interfered with.

ApPEAL from a decision of Benson J., on May 11, 1956, in the Land Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, ordering a retrial on an appeal from a decision of the Asantehene's Court A.2 on June 13, 1953, reversing the decision of the Asantehene's Divisional Court" B " on August 5, 1952, in favour of the second defendant in an action between three stools for a declaration of title to land.

E. O. Asafu-Adjaye and Prempeh for the plaintiff stool. C. F. Hayfron-Benjamin for the first defendant stool. Akufo-Addo and OWUSll for the second defendant stool.

VERITY Ag.J.A. This is a case in which three stool holders of Ashanti each claimed by way of the Great Oath certain land as the property of his stool. In the action which followed each appointed a person to represent him in the proceedings throughout which one of the parties is described as the plaintiff and the others as the first and second defendants respectively.

The hearing at first instance in the appropriate Grade" B " Native Court resulted in the success of the second defendant, whose claim was held to have been established.

The plaintiff and the first defendant thereupon appealed to the Grade" A " Native Court, this appeal resulting in the reversal of the judgment of the trial court as far as the second defendant is concerned, judgment being entered for the plaintiff and the dismissed of the first defendant's claim being upheld. pg 231

Both defendants then appealed to the Land Court and the first defendant who, up to this stage, had been uniformly unsuccessful, raised formally for the first time the objection that the proceedings were irregular throughout in that the parties thereto were improperly represented.

This ground of appeal the learned judge in the Land Court dealt with as a preliminary ground, his ruling upon which might render the consideration of further grounds unnecessary. In a considered ruling he held that the representation of the parties was irregular, there being in his view nothing on record to show that either of the repre­sentatives of the plaintiff or of the first defendant was competent to represent his stool nor had it been shown that the second defendant's representative was a person qualified to represent his stool. It appeared to him also that there was no authority from" the elders of the stools and others" to validate the powers of attorney by virtue of which the stool holders had purported to appoint their representatives.

Proceeding to deal with the result of this irregular representation the learned judge observed that each of the " representatives" had given extensive evidence of tradition and of facts not within his per­sonal knowledge, put in documents and cross-examined the other parties' witnesses. This he held to be counter to the law of evidence and the practice long established in the Gold Coast, and further held that this evidence should have been ruled to be inadmissible and disallowed by the trial court. Being of the opinion that if this inadmissible evidence were disregarded there would not remain sufficient admissible evidence on which either the trial court or the Native Court of Appeal could have come to any fair decision or that he could do so, he remitted the case to the court of first instance for rehearing.

Against this decision the second defendant only has appealed, both plaintiff and the first defendant conceding that their representation in the proceedings was wrong.

It was contended before us on behalf of the appellant (the second defendant) that it is competent for a stool holder to appoint any person to represent the stool without any such limits as are imposed by section 20 of the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99 of 1951, or otherwise and that any defect in the power of attorney by which such appointment is made is cured by the action of the representative taken publicly and without objection by the elders, such power being not void but voidable only at the instance of the elders. It has been further contended that by virtue of such appointment the representative is held out by the stool to be and must therefore be presumed to be a person qualified to give evidence of tradition and the question of the extent of knowledge of such tradition is one of credibility and cogency only. pg 232

While it may be possible that counsel's contention in regard to the validity of the power of attorney is a contention of some substance, even though as in the present case the powers are on their face incompetent, I am unable to accept in the first place the proposition that the stool may appoint any person irrespective of his status to represent the stool for the purpose of conducting litigation on its behalf.

It would, I think, be right to say that in the appointment of a representative for such purposes the stool is bound to act not arbitrarily but constitutionally, that is to say in accordance with Native customary law, for the discharge by the representative of his duties in the course of litigation, involving as it may power of compromise or settlement, concerns the interest of not only the stool holder or the elders but the entire body of their people.

The question posed in the direct terms appearing in the present case may not have been before this the subject of a decision of this court but it is apparent that the courts of the Gold Coast have held the view that a stool may be properly represented by a linguist or elder or office holder of the stool, these being the persons who are, to borrow the phrase used by counsel for the appellant, the "natural repre­sentatives " of the stool. I should not be prepared to extend further the classes of person who may represent the stool unless I were impelled thereto by the direct authority of decided cases or reliable evidence of customary law in support of such an extension. In the present case neither such authority nor such evidence is forthcoming. As in the present case it appears that the representatives of the parties in no instance fall within the classes of person who may be properly appointed to act as representatives I am of the opinion that the learned judge rightly held that the representation of the parties was irregular no matter whether or not the form of their appointment rendered them void.

I am equally unable to uphold the appellant's contention that by his appointment as representative of the stool any person must be presumed to be qualified to give evidence of tradition. There can be no doubt that the courts have long admitted evidence of tradition' as an exception to the rule against hearsay when testified by witnesses such as a linguist or other person who may be presumed to have knowledge of the traditions of his people, but the competence of such a witness, as with the competence of any witness, is a matter for the court which cannot abdicate its authority to decide such a question in favour of the party who calls the witness, whether the party be a private individual or a stool holder and his elders. In the present case the learned judge was right, in my view, in holding that the so-called representatives of the parties were competent neither as representatives nor as witnesses of tradition, for In the latter capacity as in the former they do not, in my opinion, fall within any pg 233 class of persons customarily accepted by the courts of the Gold Coast as competent.

Finally, counsel for the appellant sought to show that even if it is held that the evidence of the improperly appointed representatives is inadmissible the learned judge erred in holding that there was insufficient admissible evidence to enable the court to come to a proper decision on the issues before it.

It is provided by statute that the Land Court in the exercise of its appellate jurisdiction may (inter alia) order the cause or matter to be reheard before the court of first instance. The discretion thus con­ferred upon the Land Court must of course be exercised judicially, but if it is so exercised the court will not interfere therewith.

In this case, in which the parties were irregularly represented at the trial, in which a very considerable body of inadmissible evidence touching the nature and basis of the conflicting claims was accepted and relied upon by both the trial court and the Native Court of Appeal, I find it quite impossible to hold that the learned judge exercised his discretion other than judicially when he decided that the proceedings in the court below were such that justice would best be served by remitting the case to the court of first instance for rehearing in order that the parties may be properly represented and be enabled to put forward their respective claims, supported by such admissible evidence as would enable the court in its turn fairly and properly to determine the issues before it.

I would therefore dismiss the appeal with costs.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

KORSAH C.J. I concur.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
