July 23, 1956.

MANSOUR v. COMPAGNIE F'RANQAISE DE L'AFRIQUE OCCIDENTALE.

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Western Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Sekondi

                                                                                    Windsor-Aubrey J.

 

       

      Landlord and Tenant-Bent control-Standard rent-Contractual tenancy at rent lower than standard rent-Bight of landlord to increase Tent to amount of standard rent-Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, s. 5 (3) (a).

The plaintiff had let premises, under a contractual tenancy for two years, to the defendants at a monthly rent of £40. Before the end of the tenancy period the plaintiff applied to the local Rent Assessment Com­mittee for a standard rent to be assessed for the premises. The Rent Assessment Committee assessed the standard rent at £65 a month. The plaintiff then brought this action to recover from the defendant the difference between the contractual and standard rents as from the date that the standard rent was fixed.

Held, the Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, confers no powers on a landlord, who is contractually bound to a tenant at a rent lower than the standard rent for the premises, to demand the standard rent from his tenant during the pendency of the contractual period.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. This decision would appear to be difficult to reconcile with the earlier decision of Wilson C.J. in Amah v. Picas (Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra, unreported, May 8, 1954), which was not referred to in this case. Amah v. Picas is reported as the next following case in this volume.

The decision of Windsor-Aubrey J. in the present case seems completely in accord with English practice under the Rent Restrictions Acts-see the decision of the Court of Appeal in R. v. Paddington and St. Marylebone Bent Tribunal, ex parte Bedrock Investments Ltd. [1948] 2 All E.R. 528, in which Romer J. said:

"One criticism by the rent tribunal (of a decision of the Divisional Court) is that it was founded on the view that a statutory right to the standard rent is conferred on a lessor by (the Rent Restrictions) Acts. Although passages are to be found which might suggest that the court did hold that view, we do not think that the learned judges were in reality intending to convey anything of the kind .... In a loose sense it is not incorrect to say that a lessor is authorized by the Rent Restrictions Acts to receive the standard rent, because those Acts impliedly recognize and leave undisturbed his right to exact that rent by contract while eliminating his common law right to contract effectively for anything more. Strictly, however, the lessor's right to receive, and, if necessary, enforce, payment of the standard rent is in no way conferred by those Acts. It arises, and arises only, out of the contractual relations into which he enters with his tenant."

See also the remarks of the Court of Appeal in Dean v. Bruce [1951] 2 All E.R. 926.

The decision of Wilson C.J. in Amah v. Picas, however, is based on the view that the Rent Control Ordinance, c 2 of 1952, is a substantially different piece of legislation from the English Rent Restrictions Acts, and that cases decided thereunder are of no asstance in dealing with matters arising under the Ordinance. By this view, apparently, the !lent Control Ordinance is not so much concerned with restraining inflation of rentals during a period of shortage of accommodation, as with applying a common standard of rent to property generally. If this view were correct it might be reasonable to expect the Ordinance to create penalties for any variation from the standard rent, rather than only (by section 12) for attempts to extract a rent in excess of the standard.]

WINDSOTI-AGBREY J. The plaintiff CLaims £\)35 for rent due in respect of premises No. 90/3 Prince of Wales Road, Takoradi, which were leased to the defendants under an agreement for a lease dated September 12, ] 958. The facts are not in dispute and the sole question to decide is a point of law. The lease operates as from June 1, 1953, for a period of two years at the monthly rental of £40 and thereafter at a rent to be agreed between the parties. JI1 March, 1954, the plaintiff applied to the Rent Assessment Committee to fix the standard rent, which it assessed at £65 per month. The plaintiff therefore contends that as from March, 1054, be is entitled to rent at this rate and relies principally on section 5 (3) (a) of the Rent Control Ordinance, which provides that where the committee has fixed the standard rent such rent shall be the standard rent of such premises notwithstanding any lease to the contrary. The defendants, however, assert that the eon tract rent is payable so long as the eon tract remains in force if it does not exceed the standard rent. I understand counsel for the defendants now admits that as form June 1, 1955, the defendants must pay the standard rent of £65 per month.

At first sight section 5 (3) (a) appears to support the plaintiff's argument but counsel for the defendants has cited a number of English authorities based on the Rent Restrictions Acts in force in England to establish that where the contract rent is less than the standard rent only the contract rent is chargeable. The defendants' counsel argues that section 5 (3) (a) of the Ordinance only operates to protect a tenant from being charged more than the standard rent. On these points he has referred me to the 8th edition of Mcgarry on the Bent Acts, pp. 385 and 386 and 389, and to the following cases-Smith v. Lloyd (1) and Phillips v. Copping (2). On considering those authorities in the light of the English legislation as compared with the Gold Coast Ordinance I am satisfied that the arguments of the defendants' counsel are well founded. Accordingly rent from .June 1, 1953-May 31, 1955, is charge­able at only £40 per month and thereafter at £65 per month.

 

Judgment for the defendants.
