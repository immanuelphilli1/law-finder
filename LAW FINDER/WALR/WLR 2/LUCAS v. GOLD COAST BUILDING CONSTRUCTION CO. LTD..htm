                                                    LUCAS v. GOLD COAST BUILDING CONSTRUCTION CO. LTD.

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Accra

Dennison J.

 

      pg 105

        Master and Servant-Contract 01 service-Determination by employer-Dis­obedience to lawful and reasonable order.

The plaintiff had been employed by the defendant company and was dismissed by them as a result of his failure to attend a meeting of directors of the company, after he had been requested so to do, and his failure to obey an instruction to hand over his work to another and to go on leave. The plaintiff sought damages for wrongful dismissal, claim­ing that in neither instance had he been given sufficient time to comply with his instruction and alleging that, as a result, these instructions were so unreasonable as to be unlawful.

Held, even if the instructions of the defendants could be said to be unreasonable, they were only unreasonable from the point of view of the defendants, in that it would have been they and not the plaintiff who suffered thereby. In these circumstances therefore the conduct of the plaintiff amounted to wilful disobedience of the lawful and reasonable orders of the defendants, who were thereby justified in dismissing the plaintiff.

Cases referred to:

(1) Bouzourou v. Ottoman Bank [1930] A.C. 271, P.C.

(2) Turner v. Mason (1845) 14 M. & W. 112; 153 E.R. 411.

ACTION for damages for wrongful dismissal of a servant.

Apaloo and Sowah for the plaintiff. Franklin for the defendants.

DE1\NISON J. The plaintiff claims damages for wrongful dismissal from his contract of employment; the terms of the contract are set out in an agreement. For the defendants, it is alleged that the dismissal was justified on the grounds of misconduct; the statement of defence did not contain any particulars regarding the details of the misconduct. Fuller and better particulars were not asked for by the plaintiff, this is due, no doubt, to the asked-for particulars being given to his solicitors in a letter.

From the evidence it appears that the misconduct for which the plaintiff was dismissed was his disobedience to the order to hand over work to Mr. Jaffka. He was ordered to do so in two letters written by Dr. Busby, a director of the defendant company. The firm has no managing director, but Dr. Busby stated that at the various meetings of the company he was always elected as chairman of the directors, in any event I am satisfied that he was in a position to issue lawful orders on behalf of the company to its servants. pg106

The plaintiff raised the point that he was a " proxy" for another director, Dr. Lucas, who is in England; whether he was or not is of no importance because as he sues on his agreement he is bound by it and particularly by paragraph 15 thereof.

There is no evidence to show whether or not the plaintiff ever had the overseas leave due to him under the agreement. By reason of his application for leave dated March 3, 1956, it would appear not, so that contrary to his evidence he was due for leave last March.

As stated, the letters sent to him by Dr. Busby contain the order to the plaintiff to hand over his duties to another; further, the letter sent to his solicitors ordered him to attend a meeting of the directors and this he also failed to do. For both these failures to obey orders the plaintiff endeavoured to excuse himself on the grounds that in each case the notices were too short. I consider that it was not for him to argue about the shortage of notice. If his handing over was incomplete, or if the company lost stores through his being absent from the site, that was their own responsibility in the circumstances.

It is the case for the plaintiff that he was not bound to obey these orders in that they were so unreasonable as to be unlawful. I have no doubt at all that when the plaintiff received Dr. Busby's second letter he knew perfectly well the capacity in which Dr. Busby was acting, and also that he knew full well he was expected to take his leave to Trinidad.

In Bouzoulou v. The Ottoman Bank (1) there appears at pp. 275­276 a reference to the case of Turner v. Mason (2), at the said p. 276 of the report Lord Thankerton, in delivering the judgment of their Lordships, referred to the said case of Turner v. Mason and quoted these words of Rolfe B. :

"In truth the cases suggested by my brother Alderson are cases in which there is not legally any disobedience, because they are cases not of lawful orders. It is an unlawful order to direct a servant to continue where she is in danger of violence to her person or of infectious disease. "

In this case the failure to obey the orders could not have caused any danger or harm to the plaintiff, and, as stated, if loss were occasioned to the company by reason of these orders the plaintiff could not have been held responsible for any such loss.

The law implies certain duties of the parties to a contract and one is the right of the employer to terminate the contract without notice for substantial breaches of these duties; in other words for conduct inconsistent with the relationship of master and servant, and the plaintiff's refusal to obey orders I find to be inconsistent with his substantive appointment as a servant to the defendant Company.    pg 107

     

For the reasons I have given I find that the plaintiff, without any lawful excuse, refused to obey the reasonable and lawful order to hand over his duties to Mr. Jaffka; this claim must therefore fail with costs to the defendant company.

 Judgment for the defendants.

S. G. D.
