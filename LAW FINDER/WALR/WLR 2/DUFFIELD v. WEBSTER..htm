July 18, 1956.

                                                                               DUFFIELD v. WEBSTER.

         Supreme Court of the Colony of the Gambia

Miles J.

 

 

      Practice-Abuse of process-Action which cannot succeed-A item pt to obtain evidence to support libel action by use of process of court.

This was an application by the defendant in a libel action for a stay of proceedings on the grounds that the action was frivolous, vexatious and an abuse of the process of the court. The plaintiff sought to found his action on the contents of a letter which was not in his possession and which he had not seen. The defendant, in an uncontested affidavit, swore that he did not write a letter on the date alleged and that the letter he wrote on another day differed materially in the words used from those alleged by the plaintiff.

Held, (i) although a litigant may file a statement of claim which on the face of it discloses a valid cause of action it is open to the defendant to show by extraneous evidence that the action is an abuse of the process of the court; the Supreme Court has an inherent jurisdiction to stay or dismiss proceedings which are an abuse of its process.

Bemmington v. Scoles ([1897J 2 Ch. 1) and Critchell v. London and South Western Haillcay CO. ([1907J 1 K.B. 860) applied.

(ii) A plaintiff may not bring a libel action on a letter he has not seen and the contents of which he is unaware. The court will require

·     him to give particulars so as to ensure he has a proper case to put before the court and is not merely fishing for one. If he cannot give the particulars he will not be allowed to go on with the charge.

Collins v. Jones ([1955J 2 W .L.R. 813) followed.

(iii) In the present case the plaintiff, on the facts, could not hope to succeed, first because no letter was written on the date he alleged and second because he did not know the contents of the letter that was written and could not prove the words he alleged had been used. The plaintiff's action could therefore only amount to an attempt to fish for information and this was an abuse of the process of the court.

MILES J. This is an application by the defendant for a stay 0 proceedings on the ground that the action is frivolous and vexatiou, and an abuse of the process of the court.

The action is one of libel, the libel being contained in a letter, or should say an alleged letter, of February 8, 1956, written to on F. Carter of Gambia Minerals Ltd. It is alleged in paragraph 2 of the statement of claim that the words ~complained of are as follows:-

" send me a more responsible person other than Mr. Duffield' discuss matters with me as I would require to drive a pile parallel to the Minerals Wharf for the Admiralty Jetty."

That the Court has an inherent jurisdiction to prevent an abuse o~ its own process cannot be doubted. Moreover, the exercise of the jurisdiction is not confined to cases in which the statement of claim would be demurrable. The court is not confined to the pleadings, se Remmington v. Scoles (1) and Critchell v. London and SMith Westen Railway (2). The effect of these decisions is that, although a litigant' may file a statement of claim which on the face of it discloses a va Ii cause of action, it is open to the defendant to show by extraneous! evidence that the action is an abuse of the process of the court.

In support of the application there is an uncontradicted affidavit by the defendant in which he says that he wrote no letter 0 February 8, 195(), referring to the plaintiff, but that he did on a date other than February 8 write a letter to Mr. Carter relating to him.

Paragraph 6 of the affidavit is in the following terms:

"The said letter does not contain or embody or use to incorporate anyone of the following words, that is to say-' send , more,' 'person,' 'matters,' 'would,' 'require,' 'parallel.'" , Wharf,' 'Admiralty,' and not any of the said words appear anywhere in or on the said letter or in any part or context thereof.was pointed out by Lord Coleridge C.J. in Hauis v. Wane (3) ., in libel and slander it is the very words complained of which are the facts on which the action is grounded. It is not the fact of the defendant having used defamatory expressions but the fae of his having used those defamatory expressions alleged, which i the fact on which the case depends. "

In Collins v. Jones (4), Denning L.J. said:

" a plaintiff is not entitled to bring a libel action on a letter which he has never seen and of whose contents he is unaware, He must in his pleading set out the words with reasonable certainty; and to do this he must have the letter before him or a least have sufficient material from which to state the actual word' in it. A suspicion that it is defamatory is not sufficient. H cannot overcome this objection by guessing at the words putting them in his pleading. The court will require him to give particulars so as to ensure that he has a proper case to put before the court and is not merely fishing for one."

Can it be said in the present case that the plaintiff is " fishing" for a case to put before the court? The statement of claim contains a paragraph, namely paragraph 3, which is, to say the least, unusual in a pleading. It sets out a conversation between the plaintiff and Mr. Carter, in which Mr. Carter is alleged to have informed him of the contents of the letter. As to this, it is significant that on February 14, 1956, the plaintiff wrote to Mr. Carter a letter in which he sets out what he believed to be the words contained in the letter from the defendant. These were "to appoint a responsible person other than Mr. Duffield." In the statement of claim, however, the word "more" has been inserted before the word "responsible." I have no doubt that this was done because it must be obvious to anyone that the words "to appoint a responsible person other than Mr. Duffield" could not be said to be capable of a defamatory meaning.

The defendant in his affidavit has sworn that he wrote no letter on February 8, 1956. In this connection it may be pointed out that the conversation referred to in paragraph 3 of the statement of claim is alleged to have taken place on February 8, 1956. Assuming that the letter had been sent by post, it is difficult to see how Mr. Carter could have received it on the same day. This is an indication on the pleading itself that there could have been no such letter written on February 8.

The history of the litigation between these parties is of some relevance. This is not the first action brought by the plaintiff against the defendant in respect of a letter written by him to Mr. Carter. In the writ in the first action the plaintiff did not identify the letter nor did he set out the words complained of. Moreover the writ contained a claim in the alternative for damages for slander. The inference to be drawn from the manner in which that writ was framed is that the plaintiff did not know whether he had been libelled or slandered. It is significant that although the case was set down for mention only, Mr. Carter was subpoenaed to produce the letter in question.

It is undoubtedly a drastic step to bar a plaintiff from bringing his action before the court.

" It is a jurisdiction which ought to be very sparingly exercised, and only in very exceptional cases. I do not think its exercise would be justified merely because the story told was highly improbable, and one which it was difficult to believe could be proved. "

Per Lord Herschell in Lawrance v. Norreys (5), at p. 219. In the present case the position is not that the plaintiff's story is one which it is difficult to believe could be proved but that he cannot possibly hope to succeed: first because there was no letter written by the defendant on February 8, and secondly, because he will be unable to prove that the defendant used the words of which he complains.

 

It is apparent, not only from the defendant's affidavit but from the statement of claim, that the plaintiff has not seen the letter. It is also apparent fro the defendant's affidavit that the plaintiff is unaware of its contents. Why, then, is he bringing this action? I agree with the learned Attorney-General that the only inference to be drawn from all the circumstances is that the plaintiff has launched the action in the hope that by discovery, or subpoena, or some other means he will get hold of this letter, and that the loss of the present action is a price that he is prepared to pay for the attainment of that end. This is precisely the evil to which Denning L.J. referred in Collins v. Jones (4) and is an abuse of the process of the court.

The application succeeds and there will be an order for a stay of the proceedings with costs against the plaintiff\

Application granted. .

S. G. D.
