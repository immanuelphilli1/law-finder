               Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra               

                                              OKAIKOR v OPARE AND MENSAH

                                                                       Quashie-Idun J.

 

Native law and custom-Severance of family ties-Custom of "tako mli mfo "-Effect 1Lpon inheritance-Customary method of re-uniting ties.

     The deceased father of the co-defendant was a brother of the plaintiff, and with her had occupied a house owned by them jointly. A dispute arose between them and led to a customary arbitration by which the house was partitioned. Later, amity not having been restored, the plaintiff severed her family ties with her brother by performing the (Ga) custo­mary rite of "taka mli mfo." One of the rooms in the brother's portion of the partitioned house was occupied by the defendant, and he continued in occupation after the death of the brother, paying rent to the brother's son, the co-defendant. The plaintiff brought this action for possession of the room occupied by the defendant, apparently on the grounds that no partition had ever taken place, or whether it had or had not, that on the death of her brother the entire premises vested in her alone. pg 276

Held, (i) there was ample evidence to support the existence of an arbitration at which a partition of the premises had taken place. The effect of this partition was to replace the existing joint ownership of the premises with sole ownership by each of the parties of their respective portions.

(ii) As the custom of the Tema area, from which the deceased brother originated, provides that the individually owned estate of a deceased man shall pass to his children, the plaintiff could have no claim by way of succession to her deceased's brother's portion of the premises.

(iii) As no reconciliation had ever taken place, either with the deceased while he was still alive, or with a close relative of his vicariously for him after his death, the plaintiff could in any case establish no claim to the room, by succession or by any other way.


  [EDITORIAL NOTE. For a Fanti case on severance, see Arnoabimaa and Another v. Bad" and Others (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 227. Although the custo­mary rite to be performed may differ in the two areas it is unlikely that there is any difference in the effects of severance. In the Fanti case it appeared that the consent of both parties was necessary to complete the severance although at first sight consent does not appear to be an essential ingredient in Ga areas. This is accountable for by the fact that in the former case a division of jointly held property was involved, whereas in the latter the division of jointly held property had already taken place at an earlier arbitration. It seems clear that consent is not required for the actual severance but is essential to any division of jointly held property involved in the severance. In practice the consent will probably be to an arbitration over partition rather than to specific proposals for partition put forward by either of the parties.

Leaving aside the problems created in connection with jointly owned property, a major aspect of severance of the family ties is that it constitutes a revocable disinheritance by the severer of a member or members of his family group, coupled with a renunciation on his part of any rights of succession he may have to them. It appears that the severance can either relate to the family group as a whole or, by implication from Saekey v. Okantah (1), to individual members of it, according to the circumstances and to the intention of the person severing. There do not appear to be any cases on record where severance, in the absence of formal performance of the customary rite, has been implied from conduct.

The judgment contains a statement on the requirements for reconciliation, by Ga custom.]

QUASHIE-IDUN J. The plaintiff and her brother the late C. A.

Mensah had quarrelled over a house known as No. D219/3 Hansen pg 277 Road, Accra, during the lifetime of the late C. A. Mensah. The dis­pute was settled by an arbitration and the property was partitioned between the plaintiff and C. A. Mensah. The plaintiff occupied her portion of the premises.

The quarrel between the plaintiff and C. A. :Mensah did not end with the arbitration and partitioning of the premises. Thirty years before C. A. Mensah died the plaintiff performed a custom known in Ga as "'Tako mli mfo," which is interpreted to mean" severance of family connections" between the parties. There is ample evidence

·   that this ceremony was performed by Okaikor. The evidence is that in performing the custom the plaintiff stated that from that date she would have nothing to do with her brother and that if she died her brother should not attend her funeral, and that if the brother died she would not attend his burial. 'rhe deceased C. A. Mensah was ill for some time before he died. The plaintiff did not visit him during his illness. When C. A. Mensah died the plaintiff did not attend the funeral. Before C. A. Mensah died he let out some of the rooms in his portion of the premises and went to live at I\aneshie.

The plaintiff claims an order for possession of the rooms in the premises, one of which she states she permitted the deceased C. A. Mensah to occupy in his lifetime, and which it is alleged C. A. Mensah allowed the defendant Opare to occupy. The plaintiff alleges that since the death of C. A. Mensah his son, the co-defendant, has been collecting rents from the defendant in respect of the room in defiance of the plaintiff's prohibitions. The room in dispute, according to the evidence, is a portion of the premises \vhich went to C. A. Mensah after the partition.

It is contended on behalf of the defendants that as the plaintiff performed the custom of "'Tako mli mfo " and severed family con­nection with her brother C. A. Mensah, the plaintiff is not entitled to claim any interest in any property left by the deceased C. A. Mensah.

The defendants' statement in their statement of defence that the late C. A. Mensah was a Tema man and that his children are entitled by Tema custom to succeed to his estate has not been denied by the plaintiff. Evidence has been given by Komey 'l'etteh, a linguist of the Gbese Manche, as to the consequences following the performance of the custom of " Tako mli mfo." The persons who perform it sever their family connections and have nothing to do with each other's estate. 'They do not attend the funeral of each other. If, however, one of the parties dies and the survivor wishes to attend the funeral, the survivor should ask the members of the family to go into the dispute between himself and the deceased, a brother or a close relative of the deceased being made to represent the deceased for the purpose of settling the dispute. The guilty party is ordered to provide a live sheep, 32s. cash and two bottles of rum. After this the survivor pg 278 becomes entitled to a share of any property left by the deceased. If the reconciliation is not made, the survivor has no right to succeed to the property of the deceased. This custom of " Tako mli mfo " was pleaded in 1916 before Sir Philip Crampton Smyly C.J. in Sackey v. Okantah (1).

On the evidence before me, I am satisfied that the plaintiff performed the custom of " Tako mli mfo," and I hold that she is not entitled to any interest in the property of her brother the deceased C. A. Mensah. Her claim therefore fails, and judgment is entered for the defendant and co-defendant with costs.

Judgment for the defendant.

S. G. D.
