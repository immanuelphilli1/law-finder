The plaintiff, a police constable, was knocked down in the road by a taxi driven by the defendant and he received injuries. It was establishe& that the plaintiff was crossing the road, evidently without taking due care for his own safety, and had taken two or three steps when th? defendant's oncoming vehicle, which was travelling fast, struck him. On a claim by the plaintiff for damages for negligence, the defendant set up contributory negligence as a defence.

Held, a pedestrian in stepping into the roadway, assumes a duty towards traffic approaching him with risk of collision to exercise due care. The plaintiff here had not exercised such due care and thus was guilty of contributory negligence: this contributory negligence was the decisive cause of the accident.

Radley v. London and North Westem Railway Company (1876) 1 App.

Cas. 754 referred to.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The Law Reform (Contributory Negligence) Act does not apply in the Gold Coast. The principle in Radley v. London and North Western Railway Company referred to in this case is contained in the judgment of Lord Penzance at p. 759:-

" The first proposition is a general one, to this effect, that the plaintiff in an action for negligence cannot succeed if it is found by the jury that he has himself been guilty of negligence or want of ordinary care which contributed to cause the action.

" But there is another proposition equally well established, and it is a qualification on the first, namely, that though the plaintiff may have been guilty of negligence, and although that negligence may, in fact, have contributed to the accident, yet if the defendant could in the result, by the exercise of ordinary care and diligence, have avoided the mischief which happened, the plaintiff's negligence will not excuse him."

This was an affirmation of the law expressed in Davies v. j\fann (1842) 10 M. & W. 546. Of the two cases, and of the rule of "last opportunity" Lord Simon said, in Admimlty Commissioners v. North of Scotland and Orkney and Shetland Steam Navigation Co. Ltd. [1947] 2 All E.R. 350:-

" The principle in Davies v. jl,Iann has been approved and applied by this House in Radley v. Mann has often been explained as amounting to a rule that when both parties are careless, the party which has the last opportunity of avoiding the results of the other's carelessness is alone liable. The suggested test of • last opportunity' seems to me ineptly phrased and likely in some cases to lead to error, as the Law Revision Committee said in their report (Cond. 6032, p. 16): 'In truth there is no such rule the question, as in all questions of liability for a tortuous act, is, not who had the last opportunity of avoiding the mischief, but whose act caused the wrong.' " The Lord Ordinary's view was that: ' ... the effect of any negligence of the second defenders was broken by the later negligence of the first defenders.' This reasoning seems to me to be akin to that which has led to frequent and determined attempts to establish the so-called 'rule of last opportunity' of which less will be heard since the decision of Your Lordships' House in Admiralty Commissioners v. There is abundant authority for the proposition that the mere fact that a subsequent act of negligence has been the immediate cause of disaster does not exonerate the original offender."

It is clear from these and other cases that Lord Penzance's remarks in Radley Y. London and North Western Railu;ay Company can no longer be taken as an accurate exposition of the law, for a plaintiff may succeed ,,-hen he him­self has been guilty of contributory negligence if this contributory negligence is not the decisive cause of the damage. Lord Penzance's second proposition, on which this case turns, confirms this if it means, as it presumably does, that failure by the defendant to exercise ordinary care and diligence, when exercising it would have avoided the damage, will make his failure the decisive cause. Seen in this light Lord Penzance's propositions do not support the" last opportunity" theory.

That this brushing aside of the "last opportunity" rule by recent cases is not a result of the passage of the Law Reform (Contributory Negligence) Act is clear from the decision of Denning L.J. in Davies v. Su:an Motor Co. Ltd. [1949] 1 All E.R. 620:-

"This case raises the important question, how far the doctrine of 'last opportunity' has survived the Law Reform (Contributory Negli­gence) Act, 1945. It was well and fully argued before us, but the answer to my mind is that, as a doctrine of law, it was dead before the Act, though it remained in use by some as a practical test. Since the Act, it is no longer a practical test, and should disappear from the books. I ,,,ill give my reasons.

" The doctrine of contributory negligence was based on causation. If the plaintiff's negligence was one of the causes of his own damage, he could not recover anything: see Caswell v. Pou;ell DllftTyn Assoe'iated Coli'ieries ([1939] 3 All E.R. 730) per Lord Atkin. To resolve the question of causation the courts used to apply the so-called doctrine of 'last oppor­tunity.' That was not a principle of law, but a test of causation. It was a fallacious test, because the efficiency of the causes do not depend on their proximity in point of time, but it held sway for many years because it enabled the courts to mitigate the harshness of the doctrine of contributory negligence. After the decisions of the House of Lords in The Volute and SlI;adling v. Cooper the doctrine of • last opportunity' fell into disrepute and was superseded by the simple test: what was the cause, or what were the causes, of the damage?: see the notes to Davies v. JIann, in Smith's Leading Cases, 13th ed., vol. 2, pp. 579-589. Since the recent speeches of Lord Simon in The Hoy A ndTew ([1927] 2 All E.R. 353) and of Lord du Parcq in Grallt'v. Sun Shipping Co. ([1948] 2 All E.R. 245) the doctrine has no place even as a test of causation."

It seems clear therefore that the question to be asked in cases where contributory negligence is established is simply" what was the decisive cause of the damage?" If the decisive fault was the plaintiff's (or if his fault was so closely implicated with that of the defendant as to make it impossible pg 212 to discover whose was the decisive fault) then he cannot recover. In England, since the Law Reform (Contributory Negligence) Act, 1945, Denning L.J. suggests there are two questions: What faults were there which caused the damage? What are the proportions in which the damages should be appor­tioned having regard to the respective responsibilities of those in fault?

In this case the court held that the decisive factor in the accident was the plaintiff's negligent way of crossing the road; his contributory negligence was the decisive cause. Nance v. British Oolum.bia Electric Railway 00. [1951] 2 All E.R. 448 deals directly with this point. Here, Lord Simon said:

"When a man steps from the kerb into the roadway, he owes a duty to traffic which is approaching him with risk of collision to exercise due care.'j; This duty may need to be viewed (inter alia) against the speed at which traffic is approaching him, thus, Clerk and Lindsell, 11th ed., p. 369:- 1)

" The speed at which a vehicle should be driven must be reasonable in the circumstances. The general rule is that the vehicle should be driven at a speed which enables the driver to stop within the limits of his vision and failure to do this will almost always result in the driver being held, in whole or part, responsible for the collision.]

As to Contributory Negligence as a Defence to a Claim for Injuries, see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 23, pp. 679-680, paras. 963, 964 and Supplement.

ACTION for damages for negligence in a running down accident against a defendant taxi driver by a plaintiff pedestrian. The defence was that of contributory negligence.

Mrs. Forster for the plaintiff. Apa.loo for the defendant.

SJ\IITII Ag.J. Reluctantly, I have come to the conclusion that the plaintiff has not established his case in this action. It is unfortunate, I think, that there is not some legislation in force in the Gold Coast corresponding to the Law Reform (Contributory Negligence) Act, 1945. This case has to be dealt with by consideration of what was the proximate cause of the accident and the consideration of the doctrine formerly referred to as " last opportunity."

I do not pay much attention to the fact that after the accident the plaintiff was smelling of alcohol; that by' itself is not proof of drunkenness, but, nonetheless, I am not accepting the suggestion that when he was knocked unconscious by the taxi he was given alcohol as a restorative.

The difficulty of the plaintiff's case is the conflict of evidence.

Take the following differences. The plaintiff says he saw the taxi coming towards him when he was twenty-five yards off; that he was standing on the untarred portion of the road (at the side) just beside a hawker's table and that. he jumped further back when the taxi got pg 213

to within a foot of him. It is there that he says he was dropped by the vehicle. Furthermore, he denies he was ever on the tar­macadamized part of the road. Mabel Asante, who was sitting on the opposite side of the road, says, on the other hand, that the plaintiff had taken two or three steps across the road towards the Regal Cinema when he was knocked down, and she showed this place to the investi­gating police constable, Ayim. But constable Ayim's evidence makes the plaintiff's case worse. According to him the plaintiff was standing jmmediately behind him at the hawker's table and on the untarred portion at the side of the road. Ayim never heard the vehicle approach '\lor saw its lights and yet, if his evidence is to be believed, the defendant's car, which was certainly being driven fast, just picked the plaintiff off from within a foot of the constable without so much as disturbing him, although it must have passed within inches of him.

What I believe happened was that the plaintiff had just turned from the hawker's table and begun to cross the road without looking. He was certainly two or three yards across the road and the plaintiff's evidence that he was not cannot be accepted.

Following the principle in Radley v. London and North Western Railway Company (1) and if one looks at the cases in Gibb' s Collisions on Land, 5th ed., pp. 34 and 35, the contributory negligence of the plaintiff in circumstances such as these defeat his claim. Negligent though the defendant was, the proximate and direct cause of the accident was the plaintiff's act in suddenly crossing the road, without looking. I much regret that the Law Reform Act is not in operation in this country; it would have obviated hardship to the plaintiff in this case. There will, however, have to be judgment for the defendant with costs.

Judgment fat the defendant.

S. G. D.
