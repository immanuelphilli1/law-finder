                                                                       October 8, 1955          

                                                WEST AFRICAN COURT OF APPEAL ACCRA,  

                                             Cor. Korsah Ag.C.J., Windsor-Aubrey and van Lare JJ.       

                                                           REGINA  v  ABISA GRUNSHIE

                                                   

Appeal from the Supreme Court, Gold Coast.

Criminal Law-Misdirection-Burden of proof-Defence of accident to charge of murder-Defence which might reasonably be true-Rules to be followed on giving directions as to the weight attaching to the defence.

Criminal Law-Vol1tntary statements made to police-Desirability of presence of independent witness when statement taken.

Held, (i) it is the duty of a judge in giving his directions to a jury (or in directing himself, as the case may be) particularly in cases where the account advanced by the prosecution differs from the defence's account by only a narrow issue of fact, to make clear to the jury the effect of the three views which may be induced in them on weighing the evidence:

(a) if the explanation of the accused is accepted they must acquit;

(b) if the explanation of the accused falls short of acceptance but nevertheless creates a doubt th~y must acquit;

(c) quite apart from the explanation of the accused, they must on a consideration of the whole evidence, be satisfied of the guilt of the accused before they may convict.

R. v. Murtagh and Kennedy, 39 Cr.App.R. 72; [1955] Crim.L.R. 506, C.C.A. followed.

(ii) Where police take a voluntary statement from a prisoner it is desirable for them, particularly for a confession statement in a murder case, to require the presence of an independent civilian witness who understands what is being said and who is present throughout the time the statement is being taken. The civilian witness should sign the statement, as a witness, on its completion.

Conviction quashed.

Unger, Crown Counsel, for the Crown. Ollennu for the appellant.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by VAN LARE J. :

The appellant was convicted of the offence of murder by Quashie-. Idun J. sitting with assessors at Tamale. The Crown's case is that pg 38 misdirected himself on the evidential value of that testimony. We have therefore the evidence of Kavogo Grunshie only in support of the Crown's case. She was walking in front and the incident took place behind her. She did not suggest that any particular incident attracted her attention to make her turn to see what was happening behind; she only turned to see if the appellant was still following them when she saw the appellant take the axe from over the deceased's shoulder while he stood in front of the deceased and hit him, and the deceased fell to the ground. According to her, this incident occurred as soon as the deceased requested her not to stop; and that the deceased did nothing while the appellant was removing the axe from him, nor while he was going to be hit. It is not clear whether the appellant after removing the axe went behind the deceased again before hitting him on the top of the neck. After the incidenb, she was walking away home to cook when she saw Womogwiba Grunshie, a relative of the deceased, arrive, but she did not tell him what had happened; Whether such a witness can safely be relied on in the circumstances and upon a capital charge is a matter of opinion. But this is the' nature of the evidence left in support of the conviction.

The appellant denied hitting the deceased as alleged but explained, however, how the deceased fell to the ground. He said that, contrary to the wish of the deceased, he called out a second time to the woman, Kavogo, to stop, whereupon the deceased turned round and raised his axe to hit him; that in self-defence he bent down, went underneath the deceased and threw him down; the deceased "cried" as he fell to the ground. The appellant's case was clearly that the deceased died as a result of a fall and not a result of a blow. As the cause of death is consistent with the defence it was the duty of the judge to direct himself and the assessors to take a view most favourable to the appellant. The learned trial judge omitted to direct himself adequately on the question of death resulting from a fall to the ground; the only way he dealt with the defence was that he did not believe the appellant's evidence.

It has been contended on behalf of the appellant that the learned trial judge could not have directed the assessors nor himself properly on the evidence of the first and second witnesses for the prosecution; and also in the light of the medical evidence there was no clear or specific direction on the burden of proof. With these submissions we agree as we are unable to discover that the defence was adequately considered, and there was no direction to acquit if the defence raised a reasonable doubt. It is not safe to dismiss the defence of an accused person, especially on a charge of murder, simply by finding his case as not being true if, on the whole of the evidence, his case might reasonably be true. In such a case the prosecution would have failed to discharge the onus of proving the case beyond reasonable doubt. pg 39

Taking the whole of the evidence into consideration, and acting on the principle that it was for the court to adopt a view of the evidence most favourable to the accused, we consider that there was some substance in the case put forward by the appellant, and between the case put forward by the prosecution and that put forward by the appellant there was a narrow issue which could only be resolved either in favour of or against the appellant on the balance of probability in contradistinction with the principle of the burden of proof in criminal matters.

Following the recent decision in R. v. Murtagh &; Kennedy, 39 Cr.App.R. 72, we are of the opinion that, having regard to the evidence, it was pre-eminently essential for the judge to make clear in directing the assessors and himself, bearing in mind throughout that it was not for the accused to establish his innocence, not only whether the explanation of the appellant was true before they must acquit, but also, short of the explanation being true, if it left them in doubt they must acquit; and also, thirdly, on consideration of the whole of the evidence they must be satisfied of the guilt of the appellant of the crime alleged against him before they could convict. We are unable to say that if these directions had been given the verdict would have been the same; we are therefore of the opinion that there had been a misdirection on the burden of proof in this case.

There is one last point to be dealt with. The evidence for the prosecution at the trial included that of two policemen, who said that the appellant had made a certain statement which, if true, amounted to a confession that the appellant hit the deceased on the head. The appellant is illiterate. The policeman, called to corroborate another policeman who took down the statement, did not sign as a witness when the statement was alleged to have been made. The appellant denied making it. It has been pointed out ad nauseam by the courts in this country that the proper procedure in taking voluntary state­ments from prisoners, especially confession statements in murder cases by the police, is to require the presence of an independent civilian witness who should sign the statement at the relevant time. This procedure was not observed in this case. Furthermore the statement purports to be made in the Twi language; but the appellant is a Grunshie man and the statement was obtained at Navrongo in the Northern Territories. One would expect a Grunshie interpreter and/or a Grunshie witness. As Lord Hewart C.J. said in R. v. Dwyer If; Ferguson .. [1925] 2 K.B. at p. 803, •. It is the duty of the police to behave with exemplary fairness." This does not appear to have been the case in this matter. There is no doctrine of police infallibility., It was therefore necessary for the trial court to treat such evidence with grave suspicion and to be most cautious in relying on it. No warning appears to have been given concerning this piece of evidence pg 40 which must have influenced the assessors and the learned trial judge in arriving at the verdict.

 For the above reasons we· allow the appeal and set aside the conviction; the appellant is acquitted and discharged.
