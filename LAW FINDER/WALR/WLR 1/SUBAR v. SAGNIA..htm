                                        February 7, 1956

                       Supreme Court of the Colony of the Gambia

                                                                                        Miles J.

                                                                                SUBAR v. SAGNIA.                                                                                                                   

 

Mohammedan Law-Divorce (Repudiation)-Cruelty by husband as grounds for repudiation by wife.

A husband, who had had intercourse four times with his wife during the course of one night, demanded intercourse for a fifth time. The wife refused, whereupon the husband beat her violently.

Held, an act of cruelty on the part of a husband constitutes grounds for repudiation of the marriage, even though no continued course of cruelty has been established.

As to divorce generally in Mohammedan law, see Russell, Moham­medan Law of Marriage, and Anderson, Islamic Law in Africa (with particular reference to the Colony of the Gambia at p. 232). For a case on cruelty as a ground for repudiation by a wife, see the Calcutta High Court decision, Kadir v. Koleman Bibi (1935) 62 Cal. 1088. pg 98

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Moslems form a much larger proportion of the popula­tion of Gambia than they do of the Gold Coast. This is recognized in the Colony of the Gambia by the existence of the Mohammedan Law Recognition Ordinance, 1905 (Laws of the Gambia, c. 76), which gives to a Mohammedan court at Bathurst jurisdiction: "in all causes and matters, contentious, between or exclusively affecting Mohammedan natives, relating to civil status, marriage, succession, donations, testaments and guardianship." Appeal lies from the Mohammedan Court to the Supreme Court of the Colony. The Gold Coast does not accord such a special position to Mohammedan law. Save for the provisions relating to registration of marriages and divorces contained in the Marriage of Mohammedans Ordinance, c. 129 (Laws of the Gold Coast (1951)), Mohammedan law in the Gold Coast is treated as native law and custom and is administered as such. See the remarks of .Miles J. in N'Jie v. N'Jie (1956) W.A.L.R. 157. In the Protectorate of the Gambia those branches of Mohammedan law covered in the Colony by

the Mohammedan Law Recognition Ordinance are administered by the established native tribunals, but it is unlikely that, in practice, these tribunals draw any sharp distinctions between native customary law and Mohammedan law.]

ApPEAL from the decision of the Cadi of I{anifing on December 19, 1955, granting a divorce to the respondent.

The parties appeared in person.

MILES J. This is an appeal from the decision of the Cadi of Kanifing granting a divorce to the respondent on the ground of her husband's cruelty.

The evidence of the respondent, which the Cadi accepted, was that after she had had intercourse with him four times during the night she refused to have intercourse a fifth time, whereupon the appellant beat her until she bled and discharged excreta on her" pagne."

Two witnesses were called who testified to seeing the appellant and the "pagne" in this condition. We have also seen the "pagne." The appellant both before the Cadi and, before us admitted beating his wife because she refused intercourse on the first occasion. He admitted before us that she bled. The appellant alleges that the Cadi had fabricated the evidence of the witnesses. We cannot entertain a suggestion of that kind.

Russell's Mohammedan Law of Marriage (p. llO), states that a woman cruelly treated by her husband may repudiate the marriage herself even though there be no proof of repeated cruelty.

I would add that even on the appellant's own admission there would be ground for a divorce. The learned assessor and I are agreed that there was evidence to justify the decision of the Cadi and that he had not misdirected himself in law.

The appeal is therefore dismissed with costs.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
