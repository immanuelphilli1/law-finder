                                                       ANKRAH AND OTHERS v. DABRA AND OLAGA

The plaintiffs sought to recover possession of land that had been attached and sold to the co-defendant's prederessor, one Olaga, in execution of a judgment debt. The plaintiffs alleged that Olaga had agreed orally, before he purchased the land, that he would retain it for three years and then allow the plaintiffs to repurchase (or redeem) it at the price he paid for it, £117. When the time came Olaga refused to allow the repurchase and eventually the plaintiffs commenced an action with the object of enforcing the alleged oral agreement. The dispute, by the consent of the parties, was referred to two independent persons, and the main issue in the Lease  vms wn.etner this constituted a -reference to customary arbitration the award of which would bind both. parties, as the plaintiffs claimed it was, or whether it was a customary attempt to reach a negotiated settlement, in which case an award or settlement would not be binding on the parties until formally accepted by them. From the evidence it appeared that the "arbitrators" attempted to bring the parties together, and in fact went so far as to try to persuade Olaga to accept £235 instead of the original £117 j Olaga, however, pg 90 consistently declined to agree to the proposals of the plaintiffs and the " arbitrators."

A further point taken by the plaintiffs was that the land in dispute lay within the area covered by the Administration (Togoland) Ordinance, c. 112 of 1951, s. 4 (2); that Olaga was not a native of Southern Togoland (these two facts were admitted), and that the original sale to Olaga was therefore void as being against the express provisions of the Ordinance.

Held, (i) the subsection of the Ordinance makes illegal, without the consent of the Governor, any alienation of land by a native to a non­native of Southern Togoland. An alienation under an order of the court is not an alienation by a native and is therefore not caught by the Ordinance ..

Efefiurn Eyarnba and Others v. Kouri «1937) 3 W.A.C.A. 186) followed.

(ii) The three characteristics of a customary arbitration, as opposed to negotiations for a settlement, are:

(a) a voluntary submission of a dispute by the parties to arbitrators for the purpose of having the dispute decided informally, but on its merits;

(b) a prior agreement by both parties to accept the award of the arbitrators, and

(c) publication of the award.

On the facts of the present case there - had been no prior agreement to accept the award and therefore there had been no customary arbitration.

  [EDITORIAL NOTE. Native law and custom recognizes (although perhaps it does not clearly differentiate between) two types of customary extra-judicial procedure for the determination of disputes. These are references to a third person or persons with the intention, ab initio, that such persons shall adjudicate between the contesting parties on the merits of the dispute and issue an award binding upon them, and references to mediators whose task it is to seek a compromise arrangement, not necessarily on the merits of the dispute, but that is acceptable to the contending parties. In both types the award or compromise is binding once it has been accepted by the parties: Kwasi v. Larbi (1). In both cases also the first of the three tests of a binding customary arbitration must be present, for there clearly can be no mediation unless the parties agree to hear the mediators. Probably the third requirement, as to publication, is also present in both cases. This leaves only the presence or absence of prior agreement to accept the award as the distinguishing feature between the two types. Gyesiwa v. Mensah (1947) W.A.C.A. Cyclostyled Reports, November-December, 1947, p. 45, and Samuel v. Okyi (1947) W.A.C.A. Cyclostyled Reports, November-December, 1947, p. 36. The manner in which the "arbitrators" proceed to their task may provide evidence, although it would be dangerous to overrate its value as such, as to the intention of the parties to the dispute, and indeed, this is referred to in Kwasi v. Larbi (1) ..

   The difficulty facing the courts is thus that of ensuring that injustice is done neither by allowing a disputant to escape from an unpalatable award where he has agreed in advance to accept the award, nor by holding an innocent disputant to an award when his only intention has been to enter into negotiations for a compromise. This difficulty may - resolve itself into two specific problems:-

        (a) what evidence will the courts require to determine the existence of a prior intention to be bound in the parties, and pg 91

   (b) in what circumstances, if any, will a disputant who has agreed to submit to a binding arbitration be permitted to resile, before the award is given?

As to (a), Verity C.J. said, in Gyesiwa v. Mensah (1947) W.A.C.A.

Cyclostyled Reports, November-December, 1947, p. 49, that the payment of a fee was not necessarily evidence of a binding arbitration since it could be equally consistent with the giving of a present, according to custom, to those who attempt a reconciliation.

As to (b), the Privy Council in Kwasi v. Larbi (1), although they did not commit themselves, appeared to lean in the direction of refusing any right to resile between agreement and award. This is also the view taken by van Lare J., obiter, in Oyere and Ntirn v. Edurnawu and Aduo (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 278. On the other hand Wilson C.J. expressed the view, obiter, in the present case in the court below that such right to resile exists up to the time of the award. It may also not be without significance that English experience, as shown by the common law, would not permit revocation of an agreement to refer, but allowed the same end to be reached by permitting either party to revoke the authority of the arbitrator, provided that the agreement to refer was not a general one in which the arbitrator were not named. ]

ApPEAL from a decision of Wilson C.J., given in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, on March 31, 1954, dismissing an appeal from a decision of the Buem Native Court" B " given on November 18, 1953, in favour of the defendants in an action for the recovery of land.

Akycampong for the plaintiffs.

Bossman and Puplampu for the defendants.

AMES Ag.J.A. This is an appeal against a decjsion of the Land Court dismissing the appellants' appeal to that court from a decision of the Buem Native Court" B." In the Native Court the appellant was the unsuccessful plaintiff and he had sued for the recovery of possession of land at Apesokubi.

Several grounds of appeal were filed: but they all hinge on the same issue, which is whether or not the learned Chief Justice, who heard the appeal in the Land Court, was correct to find that a certain reference or submission to "arbitrators" as they. have been called, which had occurred when the dispute was before a magistrate's court (which was afterwards found not to have had jurisdiction) was or was not an arbitration under native customary law, the decision in which bound both sides.

We allowed another ground of appeal to be added, to which I will refer later.

Mr. Akyeampong's argument for the appellant on the former issue was, in effect, that the learned Chief Justice has made a new ruling pg 92 as to what is a submission to arbitration under native law and custom, which conflicts with decisions of the courts, including the decision of the Privy Council in the case of Asong J(wasi v. Larbi (1). I think this line of argument may have been suggested by the learned Chief Justice's dicta about the possibility of the courts having allowed English law to influence their decisions about what is an arbitration under native customary law.

The learned Chief Justice set out the three characteristics which the courts have held in various cases, some of which he mentioned, to be the essential characteristics of an arbitration under native customary law. His decision did not (and of course could not) turn on whether those decisions were right or wrong, but on whether the submission of the dispute in this case to what was called, and called by laymen and not lawyers, "arbitration" was a submission to that sort of proceeding having those essential characteristics. Whether it was or not was a question of fact to be decided on the evidence ..

After a careful examination of the evidence, which had been given in the Native Court, the learned Chief Justice came to the conclusion that it was not, that there was no previous agreement that the arbitrators, to use the same word, should adjudicate upon the matter and that their "decision, whatever it might be, should be binding upon the parties, but that the submission was to arbitrators who were to inquire into the dispute and attempt to effect a settlement of it on terms which would be acceptable to both sides, and that this was a proceeding recognized under native customary law.

It is not possible to examine the evidence and say that these findings were not justified.

The question in dispute was whether or not the late Daniel Olaga, who had bought the appellant's property at a sale thereof by the Sheriff under a writ of fi. fa., issued in enforcement of a judgment of the Supreme Court given against the appellant, had agreed, three years later and so after three years' enjoyment of the profits thereof, to the redemption of the lan"d by the appellant upon payment of the price for which Daniel Olaga had bought it, which had been £117 IDs.

This is a recognized transaction under native customary law, and where there is such an agreement, the redeemer (usually of course the former voluntary vendor; here the Sheriff had sold) has to repay the purchase price, and no more, because the other party has had the use and profit of the land in the meantime. In this particular case the arbitrators had said (in the record it is " ordered ") that Daniel Olaga's successor should pay £235. To my mind this alone is very strong evidence that the arbitrators were not appointed to adjudicate but to see if a settlement could be effected. The added inducement of double the amount might well have drawn the respondent to agree, and if he had agreed, he would have been bound by the settlement, alike by native customary law and English la,,,. But he was under pg 93

no obligation to do so, and he refused to do so for whatever may have been his reason.

The other ground of appeal depends upon the interpretation of subsection (2) of section 4 of the Administration (Togoland) Ordinance, c. 112, which reads:

" (2) It shall not be lawful without the previous consent of the Governor for any native to alienate any estate, right, or interest in, or with respect to, any land lying within the Southern Section to a person who is not a native of the Southern Section. Any conveyance, gra_nt, mortgage, transfer of possession, lease, bequest, or other instrument or transaction (whether in writing or not) which purports to effect an alienation in contravention of this section shall be void of effect. "

It is admitted that the appellant, who was the judgment debtor, is a native of Togoland within the meaning of the subsection and that Daniel Olaga was not a native of the Southern Section and that the land is within the Southern Section, and that the previous consent of the Governor was not obtained.

The appellant argues that the sale was therefore" void of effect." The section postulates a general right possessed by the peopie of Togoland to alienate any estate, right, etc., in their land. This sub­section creates a particular limitation of that right. And of course there may be other particular limitations of it, whether statutory or under native customary law.

The Supreme Court possesses a general statutory power to order the attachment and sale of lands of judgment debtors by way of execution and enforcement of the decrees and orders of the court. This general statutory power would be subject to any particular statutory limitation thereof.

Had this subsection said that "any alienation," etc .• "shall be unlawful without," etc., it would have been arguable that it included alienation under process of the Supreme Court (as happened here) and that it was a particular limitation of the court's general power. But it is otherwise. It specifically refers only to alienation by "any native ...

In the absence of any preamble or indication in the long title or in the Ordinance as a whole to suggest that it is otherwise, in my opinion the subsection does not include a sale such as happened here.

This interpretation of the section is in keeping with a decision of this court in the case of Efefiom Eyamba and others v. Joseph Kouri (2), where a similar interpretation was given to section 3 of the Nigerian Native Lands Acquisition Ordinance. That section prohibits the acquisition by an alien of any interest or right in land from a native except under an instrument \which has received the approval of the Governor, and makes null and void any instrument which has not received such approval. This court held that the purchase by an alien pg 94 at a sale by the Sheriff under a writ of ii. fa. of a native's interest in land did not come within the prohibition of the section, because it was not an acquisition from a native

. In my opinion the appeal should be dismissed.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

KORSAH J.A. I concur.

Appeal dismissed.
