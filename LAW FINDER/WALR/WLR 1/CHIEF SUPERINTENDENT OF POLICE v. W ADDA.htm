                                                                        May 7, 1956.           

                                             High Court of the Protectorate of the Gambia               

                                    CHIEF SUPERINTENDENT OF POLICE v. W ADDA 

                                                                               Miles J

 

 

Criminal law-Appeals-Inadequacy of trial record-Omission of material evidence-Acceptance of statement by counsel present at trial.

The accused was convicted by a magistrate of theft by a clerk or servant. The basis of his defence was that he was some miles away from the place at which the theft occurred at the time and day when the charge against him, as it was originally framed, alleged it to have taken place. This defence was supported by two witnesses called for the prosecution who confirmed that the accused had been in their company at the material hour, but the fact that their evidence thus confirmed the accused's defence was' not shown in the magistrate's notes of the trial. The magistrate's notes comprised the only record of the trial, shorthand writers not being employed in the courts in the Gambia, and it appeared that these particular notes were also deficient in other respects. On appeal from the conviction,

Held, (i) that a criminal appeal court is not exclusively bound by the record of the trial court but may in exceptional circumstances go beyond it for further information as to the proceedings of the trial. An example of such exceptional circumstances is where the record is deficient in respect of material particulars. pg 258  R. v. Rimes ( (1912) 7 Cr.App.R. 240) followed.

              (ii) In such circumstances the appellate court may supplement the record before it with statements from persons present at the trial.


 

[EDITORIAL NOTE. There seems to be abundant authority for the state­ment that the appellate court will accept the recollections of counsel present at the trial, e.g., R. v. Moran (1910) 5 Cr.App.R. 219 and R. v. Strickson (1936) 25 Cr.App.R. 206, in which Lord Hewart C.J. said: "Mr. Crew's (counsel) statement as to what took place in the first court is of course conclusive." R. v. Martin (1927) 20 Cr.App.R. 103 is probably authority for saying that the record is conclusive as far as .it goes, in the absence of ') clear mistake.]

As to Procedure on Appeal in Criminal Cases, see Halsbury, Simonds ed., Vol. 10, pp. 524-535, paras. 963-984.

Cases referred to:

(1) R. v. Rimes (1912) 7 Cr.App.R. 240. (2) R. v. Elliott (1909) 2 Cr.App.R. 172.

APPEAL from a decision of the Acting Colonial Magistrate at the Protectorate Court of the Central Division of the Gambia on April 7, 1956, convicting the accused of theft by a clerk or servant contrary to the Criminal Code; c. 24 of 1942, ss. 251 and 257.

Lloyd-Evans for the Chief Superintendent of Police. N'Jie for the appellant.

MILES J. This appellant, Francis Omar Wadda, was convicted by the Acting Colonial Magistrate sitting at the Protectorate Court of the Central Division of theft by a clerk or servant of twenty-seven bags of ground-nuts, the property of his employers, contrary to sections 251 and 257 of the Criminal Code. He was sentenced to twelve months' imprisonment. He now appeals against both conviction and sentence.

'rhe appellant was employed as a Secco Supervisor by the Gambia Oilseeds Marketing Board and at the material time was in charge of the secco at J awara.'l'he case for the prosecution was that on a date, as to which there appears to be some uncertainty both in the charge and in the evidence, the appellant had an interview with one Bouba N'Jie, the agent of Maurel Freres, at which he arranged to sell him ground-nuts which would be placed o1}tside the secco later on. Evidence was given by one Tomaring Singhateh, a badge messenger, to the effect that on Wednesday, January 18, 1956, in the morning, he saw the accused come to the secco with labourers and open it. The labourers entered the secco and the appellant went into his compound. The labourers took some bags out of the secco and placed them near the screen outside. The head labourer, one Peter Jammeh, then locked the secco and went into the appellant's compound. This aroused the suspicions of Singhateh, who accordingly kept watch on pg 250 the nuts for the whole day and at sunset saw Bouba N'Jie's lorry come to the secco where the groundnuts were loaded into the lorry. He reported the matter and in consequence, Bouba N'Jie having had a suspicion that the ground-nuts were stolen, or else having heard that the theft had been discovered, returned them to the secco on the following day. On Friday the 20th, the groundnuts were screened and a conversation took place between the Alkali and the appellant at which. the appellant said that he thought that the groundnuts were

·  being delivered as repayment for seed nuts. It is not necessary to g~ into detail as to the further developments because the material

·  question in this case is as to the evidence relating to the events of January 18.

The appellant's defence was that the evidence of the prosecution witnesses amounted to a conspiracy against him, and he set up an alibi to the effect that on Wednesday, January 18, he went on trek with his field officer, Mr. Edward Mendy, and a labourer, Babou N'Yassi, both of whom gave evidence for the prosecution. He says that they left Jawara at 8 a.m. and went on ground nuts collecting to Salikene, Balingho and Kanikunda, and returned to J awara at 8.30 p.m. He called a witness, one Louis Edward Mendy, his assistant secco supervisor, who confirmed that he saw the appellant leave the ~eld officer's quarters between 7.20 and 8 a.m. on January 18, 1956, going on trek with the field officer.

A number of grounds of appeal have been raised in this case, but there is only one which, in my opinion, is of any real substance and that is that the record of the evidence omits material evidence in favour of the appellant which 'supports the appellant's alibi, and that this evidence was not considered by the magistrate. It was stated by Mr. N'Jie and agreed by Mr. Lloyd-Evans, who appeared for the Crown in this appeal and was present in the court below, that both Badou N'Yassi and Edward :Mendy gave evidence to the effect that the appellant accompanied them on trek on the morning of January 18. This was plainly a most material omission from the record and the question arises as to the extent to which an appellate court is entitled to go beyond the magistrate's record. There is nothing in the Criminal Procedure Code to suggest that the record is to be regarded as final. In England it is clear that in certain circumstances the Court of Criminal Appeal will go beyond the shorthand notes which were taken at the trial although, as stated in n. v. Rimes (1), the court will not do so unless there are " grave reasons for departing from the usual practice." It is significant that in that case the court permitted counsel for the Crown to give his recollection as to whether certain evidence was given or not, and also consulted the Deputy Chairman who presided at the trial. In R. v. Elliott (2), Channell J. said:-

" As far as the shorthand note is concerned, apparently the official shorthand writer had not a clear idea of his duties; but it pg 260 is necessary to point out what the real results of the decisions given by the court are. They amount to this, that the provision of statute with regard to there being a shorthand note taken of the proceedings of the trial is directory only. The absence or insufficiency of a shorthand note is not of itself a ground upon which a prisoner can succeed upon appeal, nor the existence of a proper note a condition precedent to a good trial. \Vhere, however, there is reason to suspect that there is something wrong in con­nection with the hearing of a case, the absence or insufficiency of , a proper shorthand note may be material. "

If the Court of Criminal Appeal will not regard as final a shorthand note which purports to be a complete record of every word that is uttered during the course of the trial and the transcript of which has to be verified by a statutory declaratjon, it seems to me to follow a fortiori that the Supreme Court in this country should not regard the record as final and conclusive in all circumstances since it does not purport to be a complete record of everything that has taken place at the trial. Normally, of course, in the event of there being a disagreement between counsel who were present at the trial as to what took place or as to the evidence which was given, the court would then regard the record as conclusive. But if, as has happened here, it is agreed by both counsel for the appellant and the Public Prosecutor that the record is incomplete and inaccurate in material respects, to hold that the record was final and conclusive would occasion a flagrant miscarriage of justice. It may be mentioned that both the prosecutor and learned counsel for the appellant have complaints to make against the accuracy of the record ..

The position is, therefore, that it is admitted, although it does not appear in the record, that the appellant's alibi is supported by two witnesses for the prosecution. The trial magistrate, in a very short judgment, based his finding on the evidence of the badge messenger, Tomaring Singhateh, and the Alkali. He does not allude to the evidence of Badou N'Y assi or the field officer or in fact to any of the evidence given with regard to the alibi, and I can only conclude that he must have failed to appreciate its significance, particularly since it apparently made so little impression on his mind that he failed to record the evidence of two prosecution witnesses on the point.

In the case of an alibi dates are obviously of importance, and it is material to note that the charge was amended by giving the date of the offence as "on or about January 18, 1956," the words "on or about" not having been included in the charge as originally framed. The reason for this appears to be that neither Bouba N'Jie nor Ousainou Pani, who gave evidence that the appellant came to Maurel Freres' shop looking for N'Jie, was able to say with any precision on what date this took place. They both gave the date as " on or about January 18." Singhateh is the only witness who is definite about pg 261 dates at all, and he says that the removal of the nuts took place on 'Wednesday, January 18, but he does not state the time at which he saw the appellant unlock the secco. I understand from Mr. Lloyd­Evans that there was a half-suggestion at the tria.! that the arrange­ment between the appellant and N'Jie was arrived at on Tuesday the 17th and that the removal of the nuts took place on the following day. This could not have been so, however, because N'Jie says that he collected the groundnuts on the same evening. He does not say at that time this interview with the appellant at the latter's house took place, but Ousainou Pani gave the time the appellant called at Maurel Freres' shop looking for N'Jie as between 9 and 10 a.m. If, however, the evidence of the appellant, the field officer and Badou N'Yassi is correct, the appellant was miles away at the time. It is true that the field officer says that he returned from trek at 2 p.m. whereas the appellant says that they did not get back until 8.30 p.m. This, however, is immaterial because whatever took place happened during the morning.

Mr. Lloyd-Evans, while admitting that the appellant went on trek on January 18, argues that it would have been possible for the appellant to have opened the secco and given instructions to the labourer before he left. If this was in fact the case for the prosecution at the trial, it is unfortunate that Singhateh was not questioned more closely about times. He does not say whether it was early or late in the morning that the appellant came to the secco. Badou N'Yassi states that on the instructions of the appellant he placed some bags at the entrance to the secco and that the appellant was then working in the office at the lunch hour, closing time. This cannot be reconciled either with Singhateh's evidence or that of the field officer.

Another witness, Momodou Saho, says that after lunch on a day which he does not specify the appellant instructed him to take care of some groundnuts outside the secco. If the witness was referring to January 18, this evidence also conflicts with that of Singhateh, who does not mention the appellant returning to the secco later in the day, although he, Singhateh, was keeping watch throughout the day. I would add that this is not a case where the alibi has been kept up the sleeve of the appellant until the trial. As soon as he was charged by the police the appellant made a long statement giving a full account of his movements on January 18. The charge as then framed, by the police, alleged that the theft took place on January 18. It is extra­ordinary that the judgment makes no reference whatsoever to this most material conflict of evidence in the case for the prosecution, and I can only conclude that it must have escaped the magistrate's notice. There is a reference in the judgment to ,. certain apparent incon­sistencies " but the magistrate does not state what they are and, in any case, the inconsistencies are not apparent but real. pg 262


       The case for the prosecution so far as dates are concerned is far from clear, but where an alibi is set up the dates and times become of vital importance and any discrepancies or obscurities must react to the benefit of the accused and not to the prosecution. In this case the question of dates and times was never fully investigated and no attempt was made in the judgment to analyse the evidence.

There are other matters which are conceded to have been omitted from the record. There is no mention of the charge having been amended, nor of the fact that there was a submission of no case by defending counsel, nor that certain evidence consisting of plans was tendered on behalf of the defence and rejected. All these matters ( should have been recorded, but I do not regard their omission: as being of themselves grounds for quashing the conviction. 'l'here are undoubtedly elements of suspicion, so far as the appellant was con­cerned, but the evidence could not be said by any means to have established his guilt beyond reasonable doubt and the trial was, for the reasons mentioned above, unsatisfactory. The appeal is allowed, the conviction quashed, and the sentence of imprisonment is set aside.

The appellant has already been released on bail, and his recog­nizance and those of the sureties will be discharged.

Conviction quashed.

S. G. D.
