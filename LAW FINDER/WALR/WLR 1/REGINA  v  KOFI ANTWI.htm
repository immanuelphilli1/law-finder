                                 [WEST AFRICAN COURT OF APPEAL]

                                            Accra, October 3, 1955. .

                                            Cor. Korsah Ag.C.J., Coussey J.A., Windsor-Aubrey J.

                                                                         REGINA .                                          RESPONDENT;

                                                           v.

                                                                   KOFI ANTWI                                           APPELLANT

 

 Appeal from the Supreme Court, Gold Coast.

Murder-Abetting a riot or unlawful assembly-Knowledge of abettor that unlawful violence intended or likely to be used-Death caused by person unknown during course of riot-Application of Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 47 (2).

Held, if a participant in a riot or unlawful assembly commits murder in executing the purposes thereof then any other person who has abetted the riot or unlawful assembly with knowledge that unlawful violence is intended or is likely to be used himself is guilty of abetting the murder.

Appeal dismissed.

Unger, Crown Counsel, for the Crown.

 Prempeh for the appellant.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by COUSSEY J.A. :

The appellant and thirteen other persons were indicted for that they purposely encouraged the commission of a crime by a person or persons pg 30 unknown, namely, the murder of one Kwabena Nsiah. At their trial at the Ashanti Assizes by a judge and three assessors thirteen of the accused were acquitted while the appellant was convicted of murder.·

Section 47 (2) of the Criminal Code provides:

.. 1£ a person abets a riot or unlawful assembly with the knowledge that unlawful violence is intended or is likely to be. used, he is guilty of abetting violence of any kind or degree which· is committed by any other in executing the purposes of the riot or assembly, although he did not expressly intend to abet violence of that kind or degree. "

Applying this section to the facts established, there is no doubt, notwithstanding the alibi set up by the appellant, which was rejected, that he was one of several persons who assembled at the premises' of the Krontihene and who had resolved to prevent with force, if. necessary, the removal of the Krontihene's stool by a destooling group who have been termed" the malcontents."

The appellant is an elder of the Stool of Nkoranza which he serves.

His official duty appears to be to look after the household of the Omanhene of Nkoranza during his absence. The Omanhene was one of those tried and acquitted of the charge. A number of shots were fired at the Krontihene's premises. Several of the malcontents were wounded. Many witnesses for the prosecution testified to seeing the appellant fire at the group of malcontents. They also testified that the Omanhene himself also fired at them; that the appellant was armed with a shotgun while the thirteen other accused jointly charged with him were armed with guns and cutlasses. Kwabena Nsiah was killed by a shot fired by an unknown person at the unlawful assembly.

There is the evidence of Boateng, a police constable, that shortly after the firing he saw the appellant emerge, holding a gun, from the main entrance of the Krontihene's house and that he said he had wanted to shoot Yaw Mensah but had missed him. The appellant is an old man of striking appearance, and if Boateng is to be believed, he could have made no mistake as to identification. Further, while he would not know the Yaw Mensah mentioned, the appellant himself says one Yaw Mensah had attempted to bribe him to hand over a black stool.

Two of the assessors were clearly of the opinion that the appellant was guilty. The learned trial judge, in convicting the appellant and acquitting the other accused, held that the case against the appellant unlike the case against the others was supported by Boateng's evidence, which he accepted. The court ,vas satisfied that the appel­lant went to the Krontihene's house with others to oppose by violence the taking away of stool property. As to the other accused the learned judge found it unsafe in all the circumstances to act on their evidence.

The brevity of the judgment and the reasons given for acquitting the other accused while convicting the appellant have given the appeal pg 31 an appearance of some substance. Mr. Prempeh has argued that if it was unsafe to convict the thirteen persons with whom the appellant was jointly charged because the court disbelieved the witnesses for the prosecution, it was unsafe to convict the appellant on the same evidence.

But the trial judge's finding, which was one of fact, was a proper one. Considering that the body of the prosecution witnesses were partisan witnesses opposed to the accused, he looked for independent corroborative evidence. It was present in the case of the appellant in the testimony of constable Boateng.

Having examined that evidence carefully we think the trial judge was right in acting upon it. The fact that the particular shot fired by the appellant did not take the life of the dead man does not detract from his guilt. He was an active armed member of the attacking party and he had both intention and a common object with other members of the party. In our opinion the legitimate effect of the evidence against him would not require a different verdict. He was properly convicted of murder according to law, and his appeal therefore fans.
