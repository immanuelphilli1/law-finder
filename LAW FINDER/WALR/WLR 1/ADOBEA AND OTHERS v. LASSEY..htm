                                                                          March 3, 1956            

                         Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra  

                                                         ADOBEA AND OTHERS v. LASSEY.

                                                                                   Lingley J.

 

 

Native Law and Custom-Customary pledge of land-Customary right of pledgor to redeem at any time-Effect of court order upon right to redeem.

The plaintiffs were sisters whose father had died when they were small children leaving them in the care of his successor. The plaintiffs' father had, during his lifetime, built a house on land for which he paid an annual sum by way of rent, and this house was to pass to the plaintiffs, although while they were still of tender years it was managed on their behalf by the successor. During this period of management the successor pledged the house to the defendant to raise two sums of money-£35 and £40 15s. Od. The defendant took possession of the house and later bought out the interest of the landlord. The plaintiffs sought to recover possession of the house: in so doing they alleged that the pledge by the successor had been fraudulent in that he used the money raised by it for his own purposes, and that the defendant owed some sort of fiduciary duty to them which he had not fulfilled.

Held, (i) there is no presumption of fraud, so that in the absence of evidence demonstrating its existence it was irregular (a) to assume that the money raised by the successor by the pledge was utilized in' any way other than for the plaintiffs or (b) that the pledge itself was improperly made. pg 182

(ii) The defendant owed no duty to the plaintiffs either in connection with the pledge and his occupation of the house, or in his later purchase of the landlord's interest

(iii) Since by native custom a pledgor or his successor may redeem a pledge at any time, the plaintiffs were entitled to redeem the pledge and regain possession of the house. The defendant, when this had been done, would then occupy the position of landlord towards the plaintiffs on the same terms as the original landlord had towards the plaintiffs' father.

(iv) Where an order of the court is sought, and obtained, for the enforcement of a customary right to redeem a pledge, the customary right becomes merged in the court order so that if the pledge is not redeemed within the period allowed for redemption by the order, the right to redeem it is lost.

APPEAL from a decision of Akyem Abuakwa Native Appeal Court given on June 30, 1955, affirming the decision of the Suhum Native Court" B " given on May 2, 1955, awarding judgment to the plaintiffs in an action for the recovery of possession of land.

Larbi for the plaintiffs.

Lassey for the defendant.

LINGLEY J. stated the facts as set out above and continued:

The plaintiffs allege that this pledge was fraudulent and that Asamoah used the money for his own purposes. Both courts seem to have accepted that allegation without question. I do not consider it can be taken as proved that this money was not spent on the children; there can be no presumption of fraud. The children were maintained and have to admit that there is no evidence that Asamoah used the money " for his own debt"; their witness admits that Asamoah used to give the children whatever they demanded. The plaintiffs have delayed bringing proceedings for some years: I think this confirms the idea that they knew the money was used for their benefit.

While in possession of the house the defendant purchased the interest of Atta, the landlord. Both courts below consider this was in some way a breach of duty owed to the plaintiffs. I see no reason why he should not buy in the landlord's interest. I see no way in which he can be regarded as, for example, trustee for the plaintiffs. The defendant acted dishonestly by trying to dispose of houses and land, but I do not consider this affects his position now.

The plaintiffs are clearly entitled to redeem: the courts below differed in working out an equitable order as to redemption. I consider that it must be on the basis that the pledge and subsequent purchase of the landlord's interest were both lawful. The appeal is allowed and the judgments of the two lower courts are set aside, and judgment must be entered in the following terms :-

(1) The plaintiffs are entitled to redeem on payment of the two sums of £35 and £40 15s. pg 183

(2) I see no reason why the defendant should plaintiffs were entitled to the use of one room: right go by default they cannot now complain.

(3) The defendant is the owner of the land and the plaintiffs are liable to pay £3 a year as rent from the date they go into possession. I do not increase this amount because of his conduct: ordinarily I might adjust the amount to present economic conditions.

(4) The plaintiffs are to have three months in which to redeem by

paying the defendant £75 15s. Once their rights have been merged in this judgment I do not consider they should be able to rely on their customary right to redeem at any future date.

(5) The defendant is to have two weeks to quit the premises in dispute from the date the £75 15s. is paid.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
