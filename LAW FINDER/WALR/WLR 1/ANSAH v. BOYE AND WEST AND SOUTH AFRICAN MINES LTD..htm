                                             West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session                 

                                  ANSAH v. BOYE AND WEST AND SOUTH AFRICAN MINES LTD.

                                                 Coussey P.,Korsah J.A., Baker Ag.J.A

 

 

Master and servant-Liability of master to third persons for acts of servant­Acts within scope of authority and course of employment-Collision involving vehicle driven by servant-When servant "on a traffic of his own. "

The first defendant was a servant of the second defendants and was employed by the latter as the driver of one of their vehicles. On a day in November, 1953, the first defendant was delivering manganese at Takoradi harbour. He delivered his final load at the harbour at about 6 p.m. and was then told to garage his vehicle for the night. The garage was, on the direct route, some fifteen minutes' drive from the harbour. At approximately 8 p.m. a collision occurred at a place a substantial distance off the direct harbour-garage route, between the vehicle of the first defendant, which he was driving, and a stationary vehicle belonging to the plaintiff. The plaintiff's vehicle was damaged beyond repair and he himself sustained injuries. What the first defendant had been doing, and where he had been, between 6 p.m. and 8 p.m. was not established. The first defendant's vehicle appeared to be travelling in the direction of the garage at the time of the collision, but if the driver had come direct from the harbour the route he was following to reach the garage was unwarranted and unnecessarily circuitous. It should not in any case have taken more than fifty-five minutes to travel from harbour to garage by this circuitous route. It was not denied that the first defendant drove his vehicle carelessly and thus caused the accident, and the point at issue pg 220 was whether or not liability attached to the second defendants, as employers of the first defendant.

Held, where a servant, employed to drive his master's vehicle, has made an unauthorized detour from his appropriate route then, in deciding whether his act was outside, or, notwithstanding that it was unauthorized or forbidden, that it was within his employment, deviation in time as well as deviation from the appropriate route in distance is significant.

Goh Choon Seng v. Lee Kim Soo ([1925] A.C. 550) applied. Storey v. Ashton ( (1869) L.R. 4 Q.B. 476) followed.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Lord Phillimore's classification in Goh Choon Seng v.Lee Kim Soo [1925] A.C. 550, at p. 554, reads:                            ~

"As regards all the cases which were brought to their Lordships' notice in the course of argument this observation may be made. They fall under one of three heads: (1) The servant was using his master's time, or his master's horses, vehicles, machinery or tools for his own purposes: then the master is not liable. Cases which fall under this head are easy to distinguish on analysis. There is more difficulty in separating cases under heads (2) and (3). Under head (2) are to be ranged the cases where the servant is employed only to do a particular work or a particular class of work and he does something" outside the scope of his employment. Again the master is not responsible for any mischief which he may do to a third party. Under head (3) come cases like the present, where the servant is doing some work which he is appointed to do, but does it in a way which his master has not authorized and would not have authorized, had he known of it. In those cases the master is, nevertheless, responsible. "]

As to the Master's Liability for Wrongful Acts of his Servant in the Course of Employment, see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 22, pp. 225-230, paras. 403-409.

APPEAL from a decision of Dennison .J. given at the Divisional Court, Sekondi, on October 9, 1954, whereby judgment was given for the plaintiff as against the first defendant only in an action for negligence, and whereby the second defendants, the employers of the first defendant, were held not liable for this neglignce.

Gwira for the plaintiff.

The defendants were not represented and did not appear.

BAKER Ag.J.A. The original action was brought by the plaintiff against the defendants (jointly and severally) for damages "in that on or about November 11, 1953, the first defendant, being in the pg  221 employ of the second defendants, while driving the second defendants' lorry on John Sarbah Road, Takoradi, in the course of his employment negligently drove the said lorry into the plaintiff's lorry" causing the damage more particularly set out in the plaintiff's statement of claim.

At the hearing of the case before the Divisional Court, Sekondi, the plaintiff deposed, inter alia, "that on the 18th November, 1953, at the hour of 8 p.m. he was sitting in his lorry, which was stationary 'on its correct side of John Sarbah Road with parking lights on, when .the first defendant driving a lorry crashed into him damaging his, the platntiff's lorry, beyond repair and causing injuries to him."

• The first defendant gave evidence and deposed "that I was responsible for the accident; that when the accident happened I was teaching a man to drive at the request of the manager, Mr. McDernatt. I don't know the name of the man I was teaching to drive. I finished unloading my manganese at about 8 p.m. The time-keeping clerk called Hayford was not on duty at the harbour that night. I was told to garage the lorry at the West African Industrial Garage. I was instructed to train a driver that evening. I was instructed so to do early that morning. The direct way from the harbour to the market is by way of the Prince of Wales Road but I had to drop this other man. I left the harbour and went on the Sekondi Road and turned left at the John Sarbah Road. I was not working against instructions when the accident happened, there were two of us on the test."

C. H. McDernatt gave evidence and deposed, inter alia, " that he was the manager of the second defendants' company; that the first defendant was employed by his company, and that on the said November 18, 1953, he had been employed in transporting manganese from Yako to Takoradi Harbour. On that day he made two trips the second ending between 5.30 and 5.45 p.m. Hayford was the time­keeper at the harbour that day. That day I sent the first defendant out with a heavy duty driver, a man who had been driving for ten years, in order to show the latter the road to Yako. I did not instruct the first defendant to take out a learner driver, that is completely untrue. We instruct all drivers when their day is finished to garage their cars for the night in the West African Industrial Garage. The staff are not allowed to be taken by our lorries from the harbour to their homes and the drivers were all told they were not to carry staff on their lorries. The first defendant had his definite instruction. The time from the harbour to the West African Industrial Garage by lorry is about fifteen minutes."

Isaac Kojo Hayford deposed" that he was the chief clerk to the second defendants' company and that on the said November 18, 1953, he was Wharf Superintendent and his duties were to supervise the off-loading of manganese from Yako; that on the said date the first defendant brought two loads of manganese to the harbour, the last load being about 5.35 p.m., and he finished the unloading at 6 p.m. pg 222

and then was instructed to take the lorry straight back to the West African Industrial Garage and leave it there for the night. That evening at 8.30 he went to John Sarbah Road and saw that one of their lorries had been involved in an accident. He saw the first defendant in the hospital where he, the first defendant, admitted to the doctor there that he had taken a small amount of alcohoL"

Under crosg-examination, he said it takes fifteen minutes to unload a lorry load of manganese; that as soon as the first defendant parked his lorry at the garage his work was finished, and that he was on duty; on the said November 18 until after 6 p.m.

The learned judge in his judgment found that the accident was solely due to the negligent driving of the first defendant who was in the employ of the second defendants; that the plaintiff's lorry was thereby damaged beyond repair; that the plaintiff suffered injuries necessitating hospital treatment, and that the first defendant was clearly liable in damages to the plaintiff. He then addressed himself to the question of whether or not the second defendants were liable for the tortious acts of the first defendant. He says that the first defen­dant was not an impressive witness, being hesitant in his answers and self-contradictory regarding his instructions in garaging the lorry after the day's work and that he was much more impressed by the evidence given on behalf of the second defendants and accepted it as a true version of the affair. He concluded therefore that the first defendant, on November 18, 1953, after delivering a load of manganese to the harbour, left with the lorry unladen at 6 p.m. with instructions to go to the West African Industrial Garage and there garage the lorry for the night. Instead of doing so the first defendant decided to convey the other driver to the latter's destination and in the course of doing so the accident occurred, the same taking place between 8 and 9 p.m.

The trial judge then went on to find that " the whole purpose of the first defendant's journey to Sekondi was his own business and not that of his employer; that it was not a case of mere deviation but a journey undertaken against the express orders of the second defendants, and that it falls within the principle laid down in the case of Mitchell v. Crassweller (1)." Accordingly he gave judgment for damages against the first defendant and dismissed the suit against the second defendants.

Against this judgment the plaintiff appeals on the following grounds:

1. That the judgment is erroneous and bad in law in that there was ample evidence to prove that the first defendant was acting within the scope of his authority and in the course of his employ­ment with the second defendants when the damage occurred and that the second defendants are also liable in damages to the plaintiff.

2. That the judgment was against the weight of the evidence. pg223

3. That the amount of £86 15s. Bd., being two months' loss of earnings, awarded is inadequate.

The trial judge does not in actual words state in his judgment that he does not believe the first defendant's account that he spent the time after he finished unloading until the accident happened in: •. teaching a person whose name was unknown to the first defendant to drive." What he does find, which is supported by the evidence in the case, is " that after leaving the harbour at 6 p.m. instead of ioing to the garage as instructed he decided to convey the other dri,,~r to the latter's destination and in the course of doing so the !iccident occurred between the hours of 8 and 9 p.m.

There is no evidence to show \vhere the other driver's destination was and the trial judge makes no finding thereon, and we are left therefore in the dark as to what the defendant was actually doing during the two hours which elapsed between the time he left the harbour and the time of the accident. We have had the benefit of inspecting a map or plan of Takoradi and it is obvious from the said plan that John Sarbah Road, the scene of the accident, deviates considerably from the direct and normal route from the harbour to the garage. This route, the court was told, could normally be covered in a period of fifteen minutes.

The general principle of the law in cases of this nature is that a master is not responsible for a wrongful act done by his servant unless it is done in the course of his employment, and it is deemed to be so done if it is either (a) a wrongful act authorized by the master or (b) a wrongful and unauthorized mode of doing some act authorized by the master.

Taking all the circumstances of our present case I am of the opinion that the case quoted by the learned trial judge in his judgment, Mitchell v. Crassweller (1), cannot be held to be on all fours with our present case, for there a carman, after having finished his day's work and before shutting up his master's horse and cart for the night, started off with it without the master's knowledge on business of his own and in the course of his journey negligently drove over the plaintiff. The master there was not held liable. Clerk & Lindsell on Torts, 11th ed., p. 132, after referring to this case, proceeds as follows :-

" But if the carman whilst engaged on his master's business, improperly and for his own purpose made a slight detour the master might be liable; in such case ' it is a question of degree as to how far the deviation could be a separate journey.' If the carman • had been merely going a roundabout way home the master could have been liable but he had started on an entirely new journey on his own or his fellow servant's account and could not in any way be said to be carrying out his master's employment.' " pg 224

In Joel v. Morison (2), Parke B. said:-

"If the servants being on their master's business, took a detour to call upon a friend, the master will be responsible ... If the servant was going out of his way against his master's implied commands, when driving on his master's business, he will make his master liable, but if he was going on a frolic of his own, without being at all on his master's business the master will not be liable."

In Storey v. Ashton (3), Cockburn C.J. said:-                               ..,

" I am very far from saying, if the servant, when going 01. his master's business, took a somewhat longer road, that owing to thiJ deviation he would cease to be in the employment of his master, so as to divest the latter of all liability; in such cases it is a question of degree as to how far the deviation could be considered a separate journey. "

It is unfortunate that there is no reliable evidence to prove what the first defendant did to occupy the time between leaving the wharf and the time of the accident (some two hours). The whole time obviously could not have been occupied in depositing his fellow driver at his place of abode, and in my opinion under all the circumstances the first defendant, in departing from the usual route to the garage and not returning for some two hours, was not acting as a servant in the course of his employment. His act cannot be considered as so con­nected with the act he was authorized to do as to be a mode of doing it, but was an independent act.

Accordingly I am of opinion that the appeal should be dismissed without costs, the defendants not appearing.

COUSSEY P. A certain aspect of this case has raised a matter of difficulty, as the learned trial judge held that this was not a case of mere deviation by a servant from his direct route, but a journey undertaken by him against the express orders of the second defendants.

The court has had no assistance from the defendants as they have not appeared on the appeal.

The evidence and defence of the first defendant were directed, naturally, to establish that he was acting in the course of his employ­ment at the time of the negligent act charged and that his employers were consequently liable to a third party, the plaintiff, for the conse­quences of his negligence. The plaintiff also seeks to fix the master with liability for, as is usual in such cases, the servant, a driver, is probably a man of straw against whom a judgment in damages is worthless.

The first defendant did not impress the learned judge at the trial.

He found as facts that on November 18, 1953, the first defendant delivered his load of manganese at the harbour at Takoradi and left with his lorry unladen at 6 p.m. ; that his instructions were to go direct to the second defendants' garage for the night. Instead of doing so, the first defendant decided to convey another driver to the latter's destination, and in the course of doing so this accident occurred. The learned judge accepted the plaintiff's evidence that the collision took place between 8 and 9 p.m. In a later passage of his judgment he held that the whole purpose of the first defendant's journey into Sekondi was his business and not that of his employers. The difficulty is that there is no evidence that the first defendant actually made a journey from Takoradi into Sekondi or by inference that he decided to convey the other driver to the latter's destination in Sekondi; that the collision ..occurred on the return Journey.

 The evidence of the first defendant on this point which, I think, must be accepted although it may be unsatisfactory is, "the direct way from the harbour to the market (where the garage is) is by way of the Prince of Wales Road but I had to drop this other man. I left the harbour and went on to the Sekondi Road and turned left into the John Sarbah Road." The plan of Takoradi, which we have examined, shows the route from the harbour up Prince of Wales Road to the market as on one side of a triangle; the base is roughly the Sekondi Road and the other side of the triangle is the John Sarbah Road which runs from the market at the apex to a roundabout at which John Sarbah Road converges with the Sekondi Road. The route, Sekondi Road-John Sarbah Road, is a detour which, admittedly, was taken by the first defendant. There is evidence that the direct route by lorry from the harbour along Prince of Wales Road to the garage occupies about fifteen minutes. The detour described should not, by estimate, judging by the plan, take more than three-quarters of an hour, making every allowance for traffic halts and putting down the other man. The first defendant should therefore have been back at the garage certainly by 7 p.m.

On this question of deviation Salmond on Torts, 11th ed., p. 116, says:-

" Where a driver deviates for his own purpose from the direct line which he ought to have followed in the execution of his master's business, and an accident happens while the deviation continues, it is a question of degree whether the deviation is so great that the servant can no longer be said to be driving on his master's business but to be on a journey of his own. In Joel v. Morison (1834) 6 C. & P. 501, Parke B. said: 'If the servant being on his master's business took a detour to call upon a friend the master will be responsible; but if he was going on a frolic of his own, without at all being on his master's business the master will not be liable.' "

It is possible to discern the principle of the degree of deviation in the cases of Joel v. Morison (2), Sleath v. Wilson (4), which was not pg 226 followed in the later case of Storey v. Ashton (3), Mitchell v. Crass­weller (1), and Rayner v. Mitchell (5). In my opinion deviation as to time is as material as is that of distance in deciding the issue.

This question, to which I have given anxious consideration, is not complicated in this case by any question of contract with a third party. A master must not lightly be exonerated from liability. He selects his lorry driver and places him in charge of mechanism which can become the instrument of destruction in the control of an unskilled or negligent servant engaged on the master's business; but the servant must be aib the time on the master's business. '

In Mitchell v. Crassweller (1), which was cited by the learned judge" in support of his judgment, the carman, contrary to his duty, after obtaining the key of the stable from the shop of his master, drove a fellow workman to Euston instead of putting up the horse and cart as it was his duty to do, and on the way back ran over and injured the plaintiff and his wife. This was held to be a new journey not on the master's business. In the case under consideration the servant, the first defendant, deviated completely. At least one hour of the time, namely, between 7 p.m. and 8 p.m., after which the accident occurred, is unaccounted for. The collision with the plaintiff's vehicle happened after the end of a period of at least one hour when the lorry was not wanted for the purposes of business.

Without the misdirection that the first defendant had gone to Sekondi, as to which there is no evidence, I arrive at the same conclusion as the learned judge but for a different reason. The second defendants' express orders do not enter into it. The period of time after normal business hours in conjunction with the detour, leads me to hold that the first defendant was out for his own purposes after 7 p.m. and not on the second defendants' business; in other words the first defendant was not, at the time, doing an authorized act at all; the second defendants' lorry was not being driven upon its lawful occasions.

In my opinion the case falls within the first head of Lord Phillimore's classification in Goh Choon Seng v. Lee Kim Soo (6) at p. 554, which is referred to in Canadian Pacific Railway Co. v. Lock­hart (7) at p. 599, and the second defendants are not liable to the plaintiff. I am therefore of the opinion that this appeal fails and should be dismissed.

KORSAH J.A. I concur.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
