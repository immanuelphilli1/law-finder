                        BATANHENE AND MBIR v. KRAMPAH AND AGGREY.

                                                           Coussey P., Korsah J.A., Jackson Ag.J.A.

     Malicious Prosecution-Prosecution for native customary offence-Absence of reasonable grounds for assuming guilt-Presence of improper motive for pursuing prosecution-Discretionary acts by police office1'-Provision of support for prosecutor.

This action was founded on the activities of a paramount chief, the first defendant, who instituted proceedings in a Native Court against" the plaintiffs and a number of other chiefs in his state for the native customary offence of offering an insult to a chief. It appeared that the plaintiffs were two of a group of chiefs and sub-chiefs who were dissatis­fied with the conduct of the first defendant and who complained of him to the head of the stool family. In this complaint they asked the head of the stool family to undertake a customary arbitration in the matter and they named a day and place for it. The head of the stool family declined to do this and recommended the discontented chiefs to take their complaint to the State Council (which, by virtue of the State Council Ordinances, c. 4, 5 and 8 of 1952, have judicial powers in political and constitutional relations under customary law between chiefs and may order the deposition of a chief): this the discontented chiefs did. The State Council confirmed the date previously suggested for the cust<Jmary arbitration but changed the venue. The first defendant, to \vhom copies of all letters had been sent, claimed that he had not learnt of the change of venue and that he had gone to the place originally put forward for the arbitration. He had then taken the absence of everyone else to be the insult against his position as paramount chief. The second defendant, who at the time these incidents occurred was a sergeant in the local authority police, had been active in pursuing the charges made against the plaintiffs, had arrested them and detained them at the police station, and had opposed applications made on their behalf for bail. The plaintiffs, when eventually the charge against them was heard by the Native Court, were acquitted.

Held, (i) if indeed the first defendant had not known that the place of the State Council meeting had been changed, his failure to make the inquiries about it that an ordinary, prudent man would have done negatived any grounds that might otherwise have existed for treating the absence of the plaintiffs as an insult. This, together with an absence of honest belief in the guilt of the plaintiffs, indicated an absence of reason­able and probable cause for instituting a prosecution.

Hicks v. Faulkner (1881) 8 Q.B.D. 167 applied.

(ii) Further, the knowledge in the first defendant that it was in his interests to stifle or hinder an inquiry that could lead to his deposition as a chief was, coupled with the absence of reasonable and probable cause, sufficient grounds for attributing to him an improper and wrongful motive such as would constitute malice.

Brown v. Hawkes [1891] 2 Q.B. 722 applied.

(iii) The acts of the second defendant in supporting and forwarding the design of the first defendant, and in failing to ascertain for himself whether grounds existed for attributing to the plaintiffs the commission of the offence, indicated on his part the exercise of a discretion rather pg 189 than a purely ministerial act, and an absence of reasonable and probable case and the presence of malice.

Cases referred to:

(1) Hicks v. Faulkner (1881) 8 Q.E.D. 167.

(2) Lister v. Perryman the Y olinger (1870) L.R. 4 H.L. 52!.

(3) Brown v. Hawkes [1891] 2 Q.B. 722.

APPEAL from the decision of Acolatse J., given in the Divisional Court at Cape Coast on April 30, 1054, by which judgment was given for the defendants in an action by which the plaintiffs claimed £5,000 damages for malicious prosecution.

Larbi for the plaintiffs.

The defendants did not appear and were not represented.

KORSAH J.A. The appellants, the plaintiffs in the action, sued the defendants claiming £5,000 damages for malicious prosecution. At the trial before Acolatse J., a verdict was returned in favour of the defendants.

The circumstances out of which the proceedings arose may be briefly stated as follows. The plaintiffs, together with sixteen other Divisional Chiefs and sub-chiefs of the Gomoa Ajumako State, preferred charges in writing dated November 17, 1952, against the first defen­dant, the Omanhene of the said state: the second defendant is an Inspector of the Local Authority Police. The charges were duly served on first defendant and copies forwarded to the Government Agent, and the Ebusuapayin-head of the stool family-before whom first defen­dant was required to answer the said charges or to give his explanation to the Divisional Chiefs of the State.

The preamble to the charges reads:-

" We the undersigned Divisional Chiefs and Odikros of Gomoa Ajumako State request Nuna Nyanful Rrampah IX to explain to the Divisional Chiefs of the State, before you, at Gomoa Ewam­krone on Tuesday, the 2nd December, 1952, at 9.30 a.m. the following (twenty) charges."

It was addressed to Ebusuapayin Robina Gura, whose right to preside over such an inquiry is not disputed. He, however, declined to hold the inquiry but wrote a letter dated November 23, 1952, to the complainants in which he said, inter alia:-

" I am unable to settle the case in arbitration. I advise you to take the matter to a State Council Meeting."

He also forwarded a copy of this letter to the first defendant, who received it about four days before the date fixed for the inquiry.

Nana Okadum Mensah V, Nifahene, one of the signatories to the charges, acting on behalf of himself and the other complainants, wrote a letter dated November 26, 1952, to Benkumhene of Gomoa State. It reads:- pg 190


.. We beg to transfer herewith charges preferred against Nana Nyanful Rrampah IX Omanhene of Gomoa Ajumako State by the Divisional Chiefs and Odikros whose names appear under the Charge Sheet for your good Nana's hearing and determination on Tuesday, the 2nd December, 1952. Attached are the charges returned by Ebusuapayin Robina Gura for your action please. "

Copies were forwarded to the first defendant, the Government Agent and the Chief Regional Officer.

The said Benkumhene agreed to preside over the inquiry and changed the venue to Pomadzi instead of Ekwamkrone: he caused notices of the change to be given to the first defendant and the com'! plainants. The first defendant says he was at the time away from his headquarters, having left about four days previously, to attend a meeting at Cape Coast, but he admits that Nana Kwa Prah II, the Chief he left in charge of his headquarters, received the notice and replied: .• He told me he replied that I was at Cape Coast." The first defendant admits Konina Gura, the Ebusuapayin, informed him that he (the Ebusuapayin) was unable to settle the case in arbitration, and that he had referred the matter to the State Council. .. Later I heard that the State Council would hear the charges against me under the Presidency of the Benkumhene but no place of meeting was fixed. "

On December 2, 1952, the date fixed for the inquiry, the Benkumhene and the complainants assembled at Pomadzi; but the first defendant says he went to Ekwamkrome because he was not aware of the change of the place of meeting. On the next day the first defen­dant, without making inquiries, lodged a complaint with the Native Authority Police. In his evidence during the trial by the Native Court, the first defendant said ;-

.• Because the accused failed to meet me at the appointed place (Ekwamkrone) I realised it amounts to disrespectful conduct to a Paramount Chief and so I lodged in a complaint for the arrest of the accused persons, but because fourteen members have with­dra\vn from the offence committed ordered police to set them free. "

In cross-examination, the first defendant said; .. I ordered Police , to cause arrest of four persons, you are included."

The plaintiffs were arrested on December 3, 1952, and kept in custody until December 4, 1952, when they were taken to the Native Court, which refused bail; they were later that day granted bail upon the intervention of the Government Agent, to whom representations had been made on behalf of the plaintiffs. The criminal case against

, the plaintiffs was eventually heard in June, 1953, and the plaintiffs herein were acquitted. The Native Court made the following order:­.. Accused acquitted. Charge frivolous."

There are four essential elements which constitute the tort of malicious prosecution; the plaintiff in an action of malicious prosecution  pg191 is required to prove first that he was prosecuted by the defendant, that is to say, that the law was set in motion against him on a criminal charge; secondly, that the prosecution was determined in his favour; thirdly, that it was without reasonable and probable cause; fourthly, that it was malicious.

In this case the first defendant has testified that not only did he lodge the complaint for the arrest of the plaintiffs, but he ordered the police to cause their arrest because he considered their failure to attend a State Council Meeting at Ekwamkrome constituted disrespectful conduct towards him as an Omanhene and therefore an offence for '~hich they should be prosecuted; he maintained that attitude until the plaintiffs were acquitted. In my view these circumstances clearly prove that the first defendant took advantage of his position as an Omanhene and set the law in motion against the plaintiffs on a criminal charge, which has resulted in their acquittal.

'rhe next question is whether the prosecution was instituted without reasonable and probable cause. Hawkins J., in Hie7,s v. Faulkner (1), defines reasonable and probable cause:-

•. to be an honest belief in the guilt of the accused, based upon a full conviction, founded upon reasonable grounds of the existence of a state of circumstances, which assuming them to be true would reasonably lead any ordinary prudent and cautious man, placed in the position of the accuser, to the conclusion that the person charged was probably guilty of the crime imputed."

In this matter, there is evidence on record which shows that the first defendant could have known, if he had made inquiries, that the State Council was being held at Pomadzi and not Ekamkrome. It is improbable that a meeting of the State Council specially convened for the trial of charges preferred by seventeen or more Divisional Chiefs and sub-chiefs against the Omanhene of the State would not be widely known by responsible members of that community. There is no need to speculate on this question, because the first defendant says he was not concerned to find out where the meeting was being held; that he did not inquire either from the Ebusuapayin or the Benkumhene who to his knowledge was to preside at the meeting. The average prudent person would have ascertained from the President why he and other State Council members did not go to Ekwamkrome. The place and date of meeting were no longer in the control of the complainants, as the first defendant must have known.

In my view the first defendant did not take reasonable and proper care to satisfy himself of the guilt of the plaintiffs before he set the law in motion against them. It is a rule of law that the jury must find the facts on which the question of reasonable and probable cause depends, but that the judge must then determine whether the facts found constitute reasonable and probable cause: Lister v. Perryman the Younger (2). The facts have been fully stated on record, and we pg 192 are in as good a position as the learned trial judge to determine whether they do constitute reasonable and probable cause. In my view they do not.

 It is clear from the evidence that the charges preferred by the plaintiffs and fifteen other chiefs as well as the inquiry which was fixed for December 2, 1952, related to an intended destoolment of the first defendant; it was in his interest to stop the inquiry if possible; not only did he persuade some of the complainants to recant, but he caused the arrest of those who refused to withdraw their support to the charges. Malice means the presence of some improper and wrongful motive, an intent to use the legal process in question for somethir7g other than its legally appointed and appropriate purpose. This can be proved as in this case where the circumstances are such that the prosecution can only be accounted for by imputing some wrong or indirect motive to the prosecutor: Brown v. Hawkes (3).

With regard to the second defendant; there is evidence that he was at the material time a sergeant of the local authority police; that he was aware of the circumstances and the facts which prompted the first defendant to set the law in motion, but that he made no investigation to satisfy himself that there was reasonable and probable cause for the arrest and prosecution. He detained the plaintiffs, who are chiefs, in the cells and opposed bail the next day when they appeared in court, until the Government Agent intervened and ordered that bail be granted. Having regard to the circumstances, the second defendant's conduct shows that he was aware of the improper and wrongful motive which actuated the first defendant and that he agreed to aid the first defendant to use the legal process in order to coerce the plaintiffs and to prevent the fair trial of the destoolment charges against the first defendant. He is therefore equally liable.

For these reasons I would allow this appeal, set aside the judgment of the court below, enter judgment for the plaintiffs and remit the case to the court below for inquiry as to the damages claimed.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

JACKSON Ag.J.A. I concur.

Appeal allowed.
