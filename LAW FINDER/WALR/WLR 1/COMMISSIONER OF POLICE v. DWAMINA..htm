The appellant was convicted before a magistrate of obtaining money by false pretences. He had approached one Amegbe with a request for a loan of £600, telling him that he (the appellant) was a building con­tractor; that he was short of ready money with which to buy materials, and that he wished to use £600 to purchase diamonds which he would then resell at a profit. To substantiate this story he introduced Amegbe to a man whom he claimed to be a diamond dealer willing· to sell diamonds to him. Amegbe on the strength of these representations loaned £300 to the appellant. The defence was that the loan was a straightforward one, there being no evidence of deception and thereby no purpose to defraud, and that if Amegbe's evidence appeared to indicate deception he ought to be treated as an accomplice because he knew that the man to whom he had been introduced was not a licensed diamond dealer and that the proposed transaction was therefore illegal.

Held, (i) the appellant made statements of fact to Amegbe which he knew to be untrue, and he made them with the intention of inducing Amegbe to advance money which the latter would not have advanced if he had not believed the truth of the statements. He had deceived Amegbe and the intention to repay the money, whether or not it existed, was immaterial to this deception.

B. v. Carpenter (22 Cox 618) applied.

(ii) Amegbe was not a particeps criminis to the offence with which the appellant was charged, and therefore could not be regarded as an accomplice. pg 56

Davies v. Director of Public Prosecutions ([1954] 1 All E.R. 507; (1954) 38 Cr.App.Rep. 11; [1954] A.C. 378) applied .

EDITORIAL NOTE. Lord Simonds, in Davies v. Director of Public Prosecutions (2), amplified the definition of accomplice given in his judgment by dealing with the method by which a witness is or is not to be regarded as a "particeps criminis." In most cases it is determined by the witness himself confessing to participation, pleading guilty to it, or being convicted of it. In other cases, as where there is no confession or the witness has not himself been put on trial, the judge may rule that there is no evidence that the witness was a participant. If there is evidence on which a reasonable jury could find that the witness was a participant, then the issue of " accomplice vel non" is for the decision of the jury. The judge should, in such cases, direct them that it is dangerous for them to act on the evidence of such a witness although they may do so, if they think fit.]

As to definition of defrauding by false pretences, see Criminal Code, c. 9, ss. 39 and 40 (Laws of the Gold Coast (1951), Vol. 1, pp. 325 and 326).

As to Corroboration of Evidence by Accomplices, see Halsbury, Simonds ed., Vol. 10, p. 459, para. 844.

Cases referred to:

(1) R. v. Carpenter, 22 Cox 618.

(2) Davies v. Director of Public Prosecutions [1954] 1 All E.R. 507; (1954) 38 Cr.App.Rep. 11 j [1954] A.C. 378.

APPEAL from a conviction on November 3, 1955, before the Acting Senior Magistrate, Accra, on a charge of obtaining money by false pretences, contrary to section 293, Criminal Code, c. 9 (Laws of the Gold Coast (1951), Vol. 1, p. 430).

Crabbe for the Commissioner of Police. The appellant was not represented.

SMITH Ag.J. The substance of the charge is that the appellant represented that he was a contractor and that he wanted £600 to buy diamonds to carryon his business. According to the evidence of the witness Amegbe, the appellant said that when he got the diamonds he would resell them to buy his contracting materials. The appellant obviously had no contracting business as he implied, and his pretence that he wanted to buy diamonds from .a friend he had brought from Akim - Oda was false. In his statement to the police the appellant describes himself as a tailor and he denies wholly the receipt of any money from the complainant, and in fact denies the latter's version of the transaction.

There is no substance in the submission that if the money was pg 57 borrowed there was no fraud by false pretences; see R. v. Carpenter (1), at p. 622:

" It is no defence to the charge of obtaining money by false€ pretences to say that the money was obtained on loan, that it was borrowed and was to be repaid. ,.

See also p. 624:-

" If the defendant made statements of fact which he knew to be untrue, and made them for the purpose of inducing persons .to deposit with him money which he knew they would not deposit but for their belief in the truth of his statements, and if he was intending to use the money so obtained for purposes different from those for which he knew the depositors understood from his state­ments that he intended to use it, then, gentlemen, we have the intent to defraud, although he may have honestly intended to repay the money if he could, and although he may have honestly believed, and may even have had good reason to believe that he would be able to repay it. You see it is the fraud in the mode of getting the money, because you may by fraud get hold of money even if you mean to repay it, and thoroughly believe that you can repay it--you are still defrauding the depositor. You are not defrauding him of the money if you eventually do repay it, but you are defrauding the man because you are giving him something altogether different from what he thinks he is getting, and you are getting his money by your false statement. In such a 'case as that the false statement would not be honestly made, and this question as to the intent to defraud substantially comes to this: whether or not the statements were honestly made."

The whole conduct of the appellant after the receipt of the £300 was as fraudulent as could be.

On the question of whether the witness Amegbe is an accomplice, I should have held that he was not. The headnote of Davies v. Director of Public Prosecutions (2), reads:-

Carpenter (1), at p. 622:-

" It is no defence to the charge of obtaining money by fals@ pretences to say that the money was obtained on loan, that it was borrowed and was to be repaid. "

See also p. 624:-

" If the defendant made statements of fact which he knew to

be untrue, and made them for the purpose of inducing persons.to deposit with him money which he knew they would not deposit but for their belief in the truth of his statements, and if he was intending to use the money so obtained for purposes different from those for which he knew the depositors understood from his state­ments that he intended to use it, then, gentlemen, we have the intent to defraud, although he may have honestly intended to repay the money if he could, and although he may have honestly believed, and may even have had good reason to believe that he _ would be able to repay it. In such a case as that the false statement would not be honestly made, and this question as to the intent to defraud substantially comes to this: whether or not the statements were honestly made."

The whole conduct of the appellant after the receipt of the £300 was as fraudulent as could be.

On the question of whether the witness Amegbe is an accomplice, I should have held that he was not. The headnote of Davies v. Director of Public Prosecutions (2), reads:-

" The term accomplice includes (i) persons who are' participes criminis' in respect of the actual crime charged, whether as principals or accessories before or after the fact (in felonies) or persons committing, procuring or aiding and abetting (in cases of misdemeanours); (ii) on a trial for larceny, receivers as regards the thieves from whom they receive the goods; (iii) where a person is charged with a specific offence on a particular occasion and evidence is admissible and has been admitted of his having com­mitted crimes of the identical type of other occasions, as proving system of intent or negativing accident, parties to such other pg 58 offences. No further extension of the term' accomplice' should be accepted.

" In a case where it is not clearly established that the witness was a participant in the crime, but there is evidence on which a reasonable jury could find that he was, the issue of ' accomplice vel non' is for the jury; and the judge should direct that, if they consider on the evidence that the witness was an accomplice, it is dangerous for them to act on his evidence unless corroborated. "

I should certainly have treated the evidence of the witness Amegbe with care and should have said so but that, I think, would have been all that I should have considered necessary. That the magistrate went further and said, even if he were an accomplice he had duly warned himself of the dangers of convicting on the evidence of an accomplice, is not in the circumstances any misdirection.

I think this appeal should be dismissed.

Appeal dismissed.
