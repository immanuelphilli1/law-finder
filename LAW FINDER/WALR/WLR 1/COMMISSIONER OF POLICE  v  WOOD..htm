Criminal Law - Perjury - Summary procedure under Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 149.

In July, 1955, one Addy, a police constable, was convicted by the Senior Magistrate at Sekondi of careless driving and unlicensed driving. The appellant, who was also a police constable, was a passenger in the vehicle at the time the offences were committed and gave evidence on oath at the trial to the effect that Addy was not the driver of the vehicle. After sentencing Addy the magistrate called the appellant before the court and summarily sentenced him for perjury under the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 149 (1) (b). Although the record of the trial stated that the appellant, when called before the court and told of the intention of the magistrate to deal summarily with him, said: "I am telling the truth," it was not apparent from the record that the appellant was specifically informed of the gist of the accusation against him or 'that he was asked to show cause why he should not be sentenced for perjury.

Held, in cases where a court takes summary action under the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 149, for perjury, the substance of the accusation should be made sufficiently clear to the person concerned to enable him to know what the charge is against him, and he should be given the opportunity of answering the charge. The fact that these two requirements have been met should appear in the record of proceedings. The powers conferred by section 149 should in any case be used sparingly.

Chang Hang Kiu v. Piggot and Another [1909] A.C. 312 applied. Be Sam'Uel Nunoo, B. v. Olujomoye (1936) 3 W.A.C.A. 74 distinguished. pg 72

 [EDITORIAL NOTE. The decision does not appear to close the door on cases where a person charged is not specifically informed of the particular accusa­tion against him. Thus if a witness had been asked and had answered one question only it would presumably be permissible without more to ask him to show cause why he should not be sentenced for perjury. It would however be safer in all cases to follow the procedure set out in Gesellschaft v. Attorney­General for Nigeria (2) :-

"The proper procedure in such cases is for the court to call up the witness and address him to the following effect: 'It appears to this court that you have been guilty of perjury in that you have falsely sworn so and so (giving the substance of the allegations of perjury). What have you got to say why you should not be fined or committed to prison as for a contempt of court?' If he does not answer or succeed in showing cause why he should .not be dealt with summarily, the court can then proceed to pass sentence ....

"The fact that the witness has been so addressed, and his answer, if any, should be recorded in the notes of the presiding judge."

It may be noted, in connection with the second point in Chang Hang Kiu and Others v. Piggot and Another (4), that, unlike Malaya, under section 149 of the Criminal Procedure Code, an accused has no right to elect to be tried formally for perjury.]

For the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10, s. 149, see Laws of the Gold Coast, (1951) Vol 1, p. 557.

APPEAL from the decision of Windsor-Aubrey J. in the Divisional Court, Sekondi, given on October 8, 1955, rejecting an appeal from the decision of the Senior Magistrate, Sekondi, given on July 16, 1955, summarily convicting the appellant under the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 149, of perjury.

Ollennu for the appellant.

Scott, Crown Counsel, for the respondent.

JIBOWU Ag.J.A. delivered the judgment of the court. The appellant, a police constable, was a witness for the accused in the case of the Commissioner of Police v. Addy, which went before the Senior Magistrate, Sekondi, on July 16, 1955. In the course of his judgment the learned magistrate wrote: "In addition to convicting the accused I propose to deal summarily with Constable Wood for perjury under section 149 of the Criminal Procedure Code." The record of the appeal shows the Senior Magistrate to have recorded, after sen­tencing the accused in the case: "Constable Wood called before the court: states: 'I am telling the truth.''' Whereupon the Senior pg 73Magistrate fined him £12 or awarded him two months' imprisonment with hard labour. The appellant appealed against his conviction to the Divisional Court, Sekondi, on the grounds that, inter alia, he was not. informed of the substance of the allegations of perjury with which he was charged and convicted, but his appeal was dismissed on October 8, 1955, by Windsor-Aubrey J., who heard the appeal. At the hearing of the appeal counsel for the appellant relied on R. v. Otubu (1) in which was reviewed the case of Gcsellschaft v. Attorney-Gcncral for Nigeria (2). 1\1r. Scott, Crown Counsel, who represented the respon­dent, relied in support of the judgment of the learned Senior Magistrate on the decision of this court in Re Samucl Nunoo, R. v. Olujomoye (3). The learned judge's reasons for dismissing the appeal are to be found in his judgment, the relevant part of which is reproduced as follows:

" It is correct to say that the Senior Magistrate did not follow the procedure laid down in the case of Gcscllschaft v. Attorney­Gcneral for Nigcria (2) which is set out in the case of R. v. Otubu (1).

"Appellant's counsel argues that this failure is fatal to the conviction while counsel for the Crown relies on the case of Be Samucl Nunoo (3) and asserts that in any event R. v. Otubu (1) never laid down any general proposition that failure to comply with the proper procedure is necessarily fatal. Crown counsel argues that each case depends on its particular circumstances .

.. In my opinion the contention of Crown counsel is correct.

Where it is obvious from the face of the record what is the nature of the perjury alleged, it is not essential, though certainly strongly advisable, to set out a specific assignment of perjury. It is abundantly clear from the record and judgment that the only sub­stantial issue was whether Addy or Acquah was driving the vehicle. The appellant was convicted of perjury for stating on oath that Acquah was driving and he must have well known the nature of the charge against him. As to being given an opportunity to show cause before conviction appellant said: 'I am telling the truth.' These words satisfy me that the appellant must have been invited to show cause."

From this judgment the appellant appealed to this court. The points taken up by :Mr. Ollennu, counsel for the appellant, are (a) that the record does not show that the appellant was called upon to show cause and (b) that the record does not show that the appellant was told for what falsehood he was to be punished. He submitted that the proper procedure was not followed in that the learned Senior Magistrate did not give· the appellant the gist of the evidence complained of. He further submitted that as the appellant in the course of his evidence made several statements, he did not know for which of them he was charged with perjury and that it was wrong of the learned judge to have assumed that the appellant must have known he was being. pg 74 charged with perjury in respect of a particular issue. Mr. Scott sub­mitted that the main issue being tried was who was driving the car, the accused Addy, or Acquah, and that the appellant must have known what the perjury was.

 There can be no doubt that the record of the proceedings for perjury as for a contempt is very scanty. As contended on behalf of the appellant it does not show that the learned Senior Magistrate told the appellant in what respect he had been guilty of perjury nor does it show that the appellant was asked to show cause why he should not be dealt with summarily for perjury. This court cannot speculate as to what took place on the occasion and can only be guided by the record of the court concerned. It is a fundamental principle of the administration of criminal justice that an accused person must be told what the charge against him is to enable him to put up his defence. This fundamental principle has, without doubt, been violated according to the record before the court in this case ..

It is not disputed that the provisions of section 149 of the Criminal Procedure Code enable the courts to deal summarily as for a contempt of court with persons considered to have committed perjury in pro­ceedings before them, but this is a power to be exercised with great care and in accordance with the fundamental principles of criminal justice.

The Privy Council has laid down in Ohang Hang Kiu v. Piggot and Another (4):

(a) that it is not necessary on summary proceedings for perjury to formulate charges as in the case of an ordinary trial, but that the gist or pith of the accusation, sufficiently clear to enable the accused person to know what the charge against him is, must be given; and,

(b) That the opportunity of being heard against being tried sum­marily should be given to the accused.

The case of In re Nunoo (3), quoted by counsel for the respondent, can be distinguished from the present case. In that case, there was a proper record of what transpired in the assize court. In this case, there is no proper record. In Nunoo's case (3), the learned judge recorded that Samuel Nunoo was brought before him to show cause why he should not be committed for contempt in that he committed wilful and corrupt perjury in the box. It is therefore clear that the witness was informed of the gist or pith of the accusation against him. In the present case, the record only shows that Constable Wood was called before the court. It is silent as to why he was called before the court; silent as to the gist or pith of the charge or accusation against him and also as to whether he was asked to show cause. In In re Nunoo (3), both requirements laid down by the Privy Council in Ohang Hang Kiu's case (4) were complied with, whereas both are disregarded and not complied with in this case. In Ohang Hang Kiu's pg 75 case (4), the conviction was quashed because the accused were not given the opportunity of showing cause why they should not be dealt with summarily, whereas in Otubu's case (1), the conviction was quashed because the accused was not informed of the gist or pith of the charge against him. It must be pointed out that it is the duty of the court to inform the accused of the gist or pith of the charge against him and that it is wrong for the court to assume that he must have known what the charge is.

In the circumstances, the conviction of the appellant cannot be allowed to stand and is accordingly set aside and the appellant is discharged. Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
