                                West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session                                                               

                                           Coussey P., Korsah J.A., and Jibowu Ag.J.A..

    F. & M. KHOURY v. JOJO.

                                                                             
 

Trust and Trustees-Duty of trustee-Liability for care of trust property­ Trustee as a gratuitous bailee-Disability of trustee from purchasing trust property.

    Letters of administration in the estate of S. who died intestate in Accra, were granted to his sister's husband, one Elias Chahin. He, finding it unlikely that the estate would be solvent, and by arrangement with the creditors, conveyed all the trust property to three trustees, of whom he was one, upon trusts for the sale and conversion of the estate into cash and for payments to be made therefrom according to a stated order of priorities. The plaintiffs were creditors of the estate; the defendant one of the trustees, appointed as "treasurer" by the other  pg103 two. Among the assets of the estate were certain personal effects of value and, so it appeared, a house. The personal effects were delivered to the defendant and subsequently' were found to be missing. The explanation of their loss given by the defendant was that they had been stolen from a safe in his store during the period of rioting and civil commotion in Accra. It appeared that the defendant had anticipated loss and damage to property before the rioting occurred, for he had amended an insurance policy on his own goods to cover loss caused by civil commotion. He had not, however, done anything to insure the personal effects, nor had he removed them to a place of greater safety. As regards the house, this was purchased by the defendant at a public auction in September, 1948, for the figure of £2,500, which sum appeared as an asset of the estate. Prior to the auction the defendant had, from the estate, redeemed the property from a mortgage with which the deceased had incumbered it. The sum that had been secured by the mortgage was greater than that realized by the unincumbered auction of the property. The defendant claimed that he had arranged the auction at the request of, and had purchased the property from a sister of the deceased, one Salime Chahin, to whom it had been conveyed by the deceased in June, 1946. The defendant did not produce the conveyance.

Held, (i) the defendant as trustee had been negligent in failing to take reasonable care of the personal effects of the estate and therefore must account for their value;

(ii) there had been no conveyance of the property to the sister of the deceased as alleged by the defendant; that the attempt to set up such conveyance was a fraud on the part of the defendant; and, it not being permissible for a trustee to purchase trust property, the property should be reconveyed to the estate.

As to the Duties of Trustees, Possession and Preservation of Trust Property, see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 33, pp. 216-218, para. 399; as to Dealings with Beneficiaries and Their Interests, Purchases by Trustees, see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 33, pp. 282-287, paras. 498-501.

APPEAL from the decision of Elyan Ag.J., given in the Divisional Court, Accra, on January 19, 1954. The plaintiff claimed an account of the assets of the estate that had come into the hands of the defen­dant and an order for distribution of such amounts as should be shown by the account to be properly distributable. The Divisional Court ordered the account and as a result gave judgment for the plaintiffs for £4,130 Os. 6d. The court held, however, that the defendant had not been demonstrated to be negligent as regards the personal effects and was therefore not accountable for their loss, and that as the conduct of the auction sale had not been challenged during the time allowed by law it was not possible subsequently to interfere with it. Against that part of the judgment dealing with the personal effects and the house the plaintiff appealed. pg 104

KORSAH J.A. This is an appeal from the judgment of the Divisional Court in a suit by creditors of an estate. The writ reads as follows:-

"The plaintiffs claim an account of the assets which have come into the hands of the said A. L. J ojo, and of the payments made out by him on the instructions and authority of the defen­dant Elias Chahin, and an 'Order for distribution of the amount that may be found out, after taking of such accounts, to be properly available in the hands of the said defendants for distribution. "

'fhe plaintiffs are creditors of the estate of the late Sassine Elias Sassine who died in Accra in 1947, intestate. One Elias Chahin, a brother-in-law of the deceased, was granted letters of administration to administer the estate. As it appeared to the administrator that the assets in his hands might be insufficient to enable him to settle the claims of all the creditors in full, he and the creditors" conveyed and assigned unto three trustees all the effects and assets of the said estate unto the trustees upon the trusts and conditions hereinafter declared and contained" with powers of sale, and with directions that they should pay and apply the net proceeds of such sale and conversion in the following manner: ( a) payment of funeral and testamentary expenses of the estate; (b) payment of all the creditors whose debts were proved; and (c) payment 6f the surplus, if any, to the administrator. The defendant, who is one of the trustees, was appointed" treasurer" by his co-trustees to act on their behalf.

There were originally two defendants, Elias Chahin and Jojo, the " treasurer," but Chahin was struck out before the Divisional Court made the following order:-

"Defendant to file accounts within 14 surcharge 7 days. Accounts to be taken on registrar. "

Pursuant to this order, the defendant filed a statement of accounts which omitted to account for or produce trinkets belonging to the estate which had come to his possession as tr)1stee, but which showed the sum of £2,500 as the value of a house, belonging to the said estate, which the defendant says he bought at a public auction. The plaintiffs challenged the accounts on these matters and on others.

The Referee investigated the accounts and filed his report in court. the learned judge, having heard the parties, gave judgment in favour of the plaintiffs for the sum of £4,130 Os. 6d., but held, with respect to the trinkets that it had not been proved that the defendant has been " grossly negligent," and with respect to the house that, although it was put up for sale without a reserved price, equal to at least the amount spent in redeeming it, the defendant was declared the highest pg 105 bidder and the house sold to him for £2,500, a sum which is less than what the estate paid through the defendant to the bank to redeem it shortly before the sale, the declaration by the auctioneer not having been challenged during the period allowed by law, it was not for him to make a finding on the conduct of the sale.

     The plaintiffs have appealed against the decision in respect of the two items, viz. (a) the trinkets and (b) the house.

The grounds of appeal briefly summarized are that the defendant's story that he kept the trinkets in his safe at a time when to his own knowledge there was such imminent threat of civil commotion and looting, on account of which he had taken the precaution to amend his insurance policy for goods in the store to cover that risk, cannot be believed, and that the rule in Coggs v. Bernard (1) which the court relied on, did not apply to the degree of negligence which has been established by evidence in the case. As regards the house, that it is unnecessary for the principal on whose behalf an agent or trustee is acting to file a bill to set aside a wrongful or illegal purchase made by such agent or trustee before claiming accounts or any other relief, and that it is wrong for the court to declare as a principle of law that the alleged purchase by the defendant is only voidable and not absolutely prohibited.

The defendant admits that the trinkets had been in his possession as trustee, but says they were contained in a box which he kept in a safe in his store, and during the Accra riots persons unknown broke into the stores and stole them together with other goods in the store. He further admits that twenty days before the riots he had reason to believe that there would be riots and looting and consequently he amended an insurance policy for the goods to cover civil commotion and looting. However, he neither insured the trinkets nor deposited them in the bank because he did not anticipate that if rioters broke into the store they would open the safe and steal its contents.

As regards the house purchased by the defendant he contends that he bought it at a public auction and is only liable to account for the price he paid for it. In Hatch v. Hatch (2) the Lord Chancellor (Eldon) said:-

"This case proves the wisdom of the court in saying, it is almost impossible in the course of the connection of guardian and ward, attorney and client (Gibson v. Jeyes, 6 Ve. 267), trustee and cestui que trust, that a transaction shall stand, purporting to be bounty for the execution of antecedent duty. (See Coles v. Trecothick, 9 Yes. 234, and the references in the note, p. 240.) There may not be a more moral act, one that would do more credit to a young man beginning the world, or afford a better omen for the future, than if, a trustee having done his duty, the cestui que trust, taking it into his fair, serious, and well-informed, pg106 consideration, were to do an act of bounty like this. But the court cannot permit it. "

There are numerous authorities which support the view that this principle applies, however innocent the purchaser may be in a given case. The cestui que trust is not bound to prove, nor is the court bound to decide, that the trustee has made a bargain advantageous to himself. The fact may be so and yet the party not have it in his power distinctly and clearly to show it. It is to guard against such uncertainty and hazard of abuse and to remove the trustee from temptation that the rule does and will permit the cestui que trust to come at his option, and without showing essential injury, to insist on another sale or to compel the trustee to reconvey the property to the estate on repayment of the purchase money. It was held in Ex parte Lacey (3) that:

" As to a purchase by a trustee of the trust property the rule is that it shall not prevail under any circumstances unless the connection appears satisfactorily to have dissolved a transaction to be viewed with great jealousy from the opportunity of acquiring knowledge as trustee, or by universal consent."

In this case there is undisputed evidence on record which proves that the defendant acting on behalf of himself and of his co-trustees and the administrator paid from the estate the sum of £2,630 16s. 2d. and' other sums to Barclays Bank to redeem the property which the deceased in his lifetime had mortgaged to the bank. Shortly after this, and while the trust had not been dissolved, the defendant purports to have bought the same property for £2,500 at a public auction alleged to be at the instance of the sister of the deceased, who is the wife of the administrator. In the notice filed the property is described thus:-

" All the estate right title and interest of Mrs. Salime Chahin in all that piece or parcel of land, together. with the storey building and out-houses thereon situate lying and being at Adabraka etc. known and described as the property of Salime Chahin under a Deed of Conveyance dated 27th day of June 1946."

It will be observed that the same property which the defendant as trustee redeemed with trust money of the sum of not less than £2,500, he purports to have bought at a public auction at the instance of the sister of the deceased in September 1948, she holding it under an alleged deed of conveyance dated June 27, 1946, i.e., more than one year before the death of the late Sassine Elias Sassine of whose estate defendant is a trustee. The said document has not been produced in evidence, nor is there any proof on record showing how the deceased's sister became the owner of the property. Although it was sold as alleged by the sister, the purchase-money, a sum of £2,466 alleged to be subject to deduction of £33 19s. by the auctioneer, is shown in the accounts as money realised from the sale of trust pg 107 property. In these circumstances it is difficult for the defendant to contend that he has acted honestly in the transaction, and indeed whether any right, title and interest of the estate has passed to defendant or anyone else.

Shortly after the alleged sale, the defendant leased the property to a tenant for £300 per annum, now increased to £500. In my view the whole transaction is tainted with fraud such as would make the defendant liable not only to reconvey the property to the estate, if indeed it was ever conveyed by anyone to the defendants, but also to account for the rents and other profits since September, 1948.

In regard to the trinkets, there is no other evidence on record apart from the statement of the defendant that they were in fact kept in his safe in the store, or, even if they were kept in the safe at any time, that he left them in that safe when to his own knowledge there was an imminent threat of civil commotion and looting, on account of which he had taken the precaution to amend his insurance policy to cover' the same risk with respect to his own goods in the same store. In these circumstances, assuming the trinkets were in the safe as alleged there could hardly be any more glaring evidence of gross negligence on the part of a trustee, as to make him liable for the value of the trinkets, if indeed they have been stolen.

The learned judge in the above circumstances held that there was no gross negligence: with this view I am unable to agree. This court is in as good a position to assess the weight of the evidence on record as the trial judge had with respect to gross negligence, and it is my view that the evidence clearly proved gross negligence.

In Dorman v. Jenkins (4), the defendant, a coffee-house keeper, having custody of money without reward, lost it, and made the following statement: that he had unfortunately put it with a larger sum of money of his own into his cash box, which was kept in his taproom; that the taproom had a bar in it and was open on a Sunday, but the rest of the house, which was inhabited, was not open on Sunday, and that the cash-box with his own money and the plaintiff's had been stolen on that day. The judge left it to the jury whether the defendant was guilty of gross negligence; and he told them that the loss of the defendant's own money did not necessarily prove reasonable care. The jury having found for the plaintiff, he held, first, that the question of gross negligence was properly left to them, secondly that there was evidence upon which they might find for the p lain tiff.

For these reasons I would allow this appeal.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

JIBOWU Ag.J.A. I concur.

Appeal allowed.
