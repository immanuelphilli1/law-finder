                                West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session                                                               

                                           Coussey P., Korsah J.A., and Ames Ag.J.A.

                               KOFI GARH AND OTHERS v. AKA VIDEY AND OTHERS.

                                                                             

pg 99

Practice-Appeal from Native Court-Reference back to Native Court with directions-Further appeal from Native Court-Whether or not appeal part-heard-Procedure to be followed by trial court-Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98, s. 50.

An appeal in this case came before Chalmers Smith J. from the decision of a Native Court. It appeared on the hearing of the appeal that the appellants' main complaint against the judgment of the trial court was that the land in respect of which judgment was given was not adequately described. Chalmers Smith J., in exercise of his powers under the Native Courts Ordinance, c. 98, s. 50, referred the case back to the trial N a ti ve Court with a direction "to define the boundaries of the land which they found belonged to the defendants and incorporate these in their judgment." Chalmers Smith J. then adjourned the hearing pending: "result of this clarification." The appeal subse­quently came before Jackson J. (Chalmers Smith J. having left the country), who amplified the order of Chalmers Smith J., and re-referred the case to the trial Native Court. The appeal next came before van Lare J., who ordered the appeal to be heard de novo, apparently by himself. Eventually it came before Manyo Plange J., whose attention was called to the various directions and orders made by the previous judges and who he1d, in effect, that the appeal had been part-heard, at any rate by Chalmers Smith J. and Jackson J., and that he could not continue from where the previous judges had left off, and decided to hear the appeal de novo. From this decision the defendants appealed.

Held, (i) that Manyo Plange J. was right in ordering the appeal to be heard de novo by him ..

(ii) That by so ordering Manyo Plange J. had not set aside the previous order by Chalmers ~mith J.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The real issue in this appeal appears to be whether one judge can continue the hearing of an appeal which has been part-heard by another judge. Put as simply as this the problem does not require much searching for an answer, which is that just as one judge cannot continue the trial of a suit begun by another judge, an appeal judge cannot continue the hearing of an appeal which is part-heard by another judge. But the problem which has often been presented to judges of the Land Court sitting in their appellate jurisdiction is: "what constitutes a part-hearing of an appeal from a Native Court." By section 50 of the Native Courts Ordinance, c. 98, a Land Judge in his appellate jurisdiction has certain powers enumerated in that section, which reads as follows:-

"A Native Appeal Court or, when sitting in its appellate capacity, the Magistrate's Court or the Land Court, may in an appeal as herein­before provided re-hear the cause in whole or in part and may admit such further evidence as it sees fit and may reverse, vary or confirm the decision of the court from which the appeal is taken and may make such other order as it shall consider the justice of the case requires, and it may refer the case back to the Court or Native Court or Native Appeal Court from which the appeal is taken for re-hearing de novo or for the purpose of taking further evidence." pg 100

When a judge has heard argument on the appeal and has made an order for the hearing of the cause in whole or in part or for the admission of further evidence, does this step make the appeal part-heard so as to make it improper for another judge to continue from where the former judge left off? Judges of the Land Court are divided in their views on this point, particularly as regards an order to re-hear the case in whole or in part. There is no decision of the West African Court of Appeal on this point, but it is submitted that the decision in this appeal covers the point.

The ratio decidendi of this judgment is that a reference such as was made in this appeal to the trial Native Court makes the appeal part-heard: it seems likely that an Order to re-hear the cause in whole or in part falls in the same category. The necessary inference to be drawn from this decision would appear to be that an order made by one judge in the hearing of an appeal is not binding on another judge before whom the appeal may later come if the hearing had not been completed before the former judge.

The determination of the question whether or not the order of Manyo Plange J. amounted to setting aside the order of Chalmers Smith J. does not appear to be necessary for this decision, and was considered only because one of the appellants' main grounds of appeal was based on this question.]

APPEAL from the decision of Manyo Plange J., given in the Land Court, Accra, Eastern Judicial Division, on November 4, 1954, on appeal from the decision of the Battor-:Mepe Native Court" B," given on December 4, 1947, in favour of the defendants.

Ollennu for the plaintiffs.

Bossman for the defendants.

RORSAH J.A. The plaintiffs sued for a declaration of title to a piece or' parcel of land of which the Native Court, by its judgment delivered on December 4, 1947, declared plaintiffs' ancestors to be the principal owners, but that as they had granted portions thereof to t~e defen­dants' ancestors as "farming portions" it would be " irregular and uncustomary" for the plaintiffs to claim the portions so granted from the defendants, who are successors of the original grantees. 'rhe Native Court concluded as follows:-

"The court in weighing very carefully statements and evidences adduced by both parties, gives judgment for defendants against the plaintiff with costs to be taxed. To avoid any future trouble between the parties, defendants are hereby strictly warned not to encroach upon any other portion outside the land or farm­ing portion given their grandfathers by plaintiffs' ancestors from time immemorial and the boundaries of which had been inspected by the court."

The plaintiffs filed an appeal against the judgment on three grounds: -

" (1) Having found that the plaintiffs' ancestors are the principal owners of the land, the Native Court was wrong in making a declaration of this title in favour of the defendants. pg 101

(2) The trial was irregular in that no record was taken of the Viewers' report, and the other parties were not given opportunity to examine the said report.

(3) The judgment is against the weight of evidence."

The record of the proceedings before Chalmers Smith J., sitting in the Land Court in exercise of its jurisdiction as an Appellate Court, shows that counsel for the plaintiffs argued only one ground of appeal -Ground 2, and contended that the Native Court did not define the boundaries of the land which the court held had been granted to defendants; and further stated" Don't really argue judgment is wrong, but it is not clear enough."

Counsel for the defendants contended that the judgment was clear and that the land in dispute is accurately defined in plaintiffs' claim. Counsel for the plaintiffs replied: "I only ask that boundaries be stated in judgment." Whereupon the court made the following note: -

" If that is the only complaint against the judgment it was quite unnecessary for the appellant to appeal. He could have asked the Native Court to clarify its judgment and state the boundaries of the land which they found belonged to the defendants.

" In this respect I agree that the judgment of the Native Court is not fully satisfactory and I refer to the Native Court to define the boundaries of the land which they found belonged to the defendants and incorporate these in their judgment.

" Appeal stands adjourned to November 1, pending result of this clarification."

This order was not carried out until after Chalmers Smith J. left the country, and when eventually a report was received, it was agreed that the order had not been carried out, so Jackson J., who was then presiding in the Land Court, amplified the order giving more specific directions, and remitted the case back to the Native Court. It would appear that in the meantime the Native Court became defunct. When eventually the case was called before van Lare J., he ordered that the appeal be heard de novo in whole by the Land Court. It was again adjourned and was later called before Manyo Plang'e J., who, after discussing the various orders and directions, held that the appeal was still pending, but since he could not continue from where it was left off, it should be heard de novo.

From this order plaintiffs now appeal to this court on the grounds briefly summarized thus: (a) the order of Chalmers Smith J. was a final order finally determining the rights of the parties, the reference to Native Court to define the boundaries was an interlocutory order to work out the rights of the parties. The said orders are in full force and effect as there had been no appeal from them; (b) the plaintiffs having virtually abandoned the appeal and obtained an order merely pg 102 for the incorporation of the boundaries in the judgment, it was not open to them to reopen and argue the whole appeal; ( c) the order of Manyo Plange J. in effect amounts to a review of the order of Chalmers Smith J.; (d) the court has misinterpreted the true intent and effect of Chalmers Smith's order of September 17, 1948 .

These grounds have been argued before us, but in my view counsel for appellants has failed to satisfy this court that the order of Manyo Plange J. is wrong. It might be that counsel for appellants misunderstood this order because of the following passage in it:-

" And since I. cannot continue from where it was left off, I order the appeal to be heard de novo; since there is nothing in the Ordinance to empower a judge to continue an appeal the hearing of which has been commenced by another judge."

In my view the learned judge could not proceed to hear the case without making the order on record that he was hearing the appeal de novo; and the contention that he had intended to set aside any previous order is pure conjecture which cannot support this appeal. It may be that at the hearing de novo, the appellant in the Land Court, in view of the statement of his counsel that he was not appealing from the judgment of the Native Court on the merits but required only a definition of the area or areas which were found to belong to the defendant, will be confined to that issue, but that will be a matter for the court below to determine.

I would accordingly dismiss it.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

     AMES Ag.J.A. I concur.
 

Appeal dismissed.

E. A.-A.
