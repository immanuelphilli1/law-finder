                                        February 7, 1956

                          Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session

                                                              Coussey P., Korsah J.A., Ames Ag.J.A.

                                                                   NTRAMA v. ATTA AND FORFIE.                                                                                                                   

 

Native law and custom-Sale of land-Ingredients of effective transfer of fitle-Evidence required to establish change of ownership.

This was an action for the recovery of possession of a plot of land.

Some years before the action a deceased brother of the plaintiff had sold the land to the first defendant for the sum of £90 and the first defendant had installed the second defendant upon it as a caretaker. The action was concerned with the nature of an agreement between the plaintiff and the first defendant by which the plaintiff paid £20 to the first defendant and promised to pay a further £70 after a short lapse of time. The plaintiff claimed that the agreement was one for the purchase by him of the plot of land and, in effect, that either the land had formally been transferred to him, in which case he asked for possession, or that if the land had not been formally transferred to him by the due observance of the customs concerned with publicizing transfer of land, that the agree­ment was effective to confer ownership upon him and entitle him to specific performance of it, in which case he again sought possession. The first defendant claimed that the agreement, although concerned with the land, was not an immediate agreement for sale, but one by which he promised to sell the land to the plaintiff as soon as the balance of £70 was paid; that shortly after the £20 had been paid the plaintiff asked for the transfer of possession and ownership, which he refused to grant; that the £70 had not been paid; and that the inchoate arrangements as they stood did not confer ownership on the plaintiff. It was established at the hearing that the agreement was evidenced in writing by a receipt given for the £20, but that this receipt contained nothing that would identify the plot of land as the subject of a sale; that if there had been any customary demarcation of the boundaries, this had not been witnessed; and that after waiting five months for the balance of £70 to be paid the first defendant had sold the land to the second defendant for £105.

Held, (i) as there was no evidence that there had been the public demarcation of boundaries required on a customary sale of land, no formal transfer of title to the plaintiff had taken place. pg 95

(ii) There was insufficient evidence to demonstrate that the agreement between the plaintiff and the defendant was one for a sale of land by which ownership passed from vendor to purchaser.

Basel Mission Factory v. Bruce «1899) Fanti Law Report 99) distinguished.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. In this case it was clear that Native custom, rather than English law, was intended to regulate the transaction, and therefore the provisions of English law neither as to the requirement of an agreement in, or evidenced by, writing, nor as to the condition in which specific performance will be ordered, wholly apply to the agreement. It is true nevertheless that the decision of a court as to the nature of the agreement will largely be governed by the wording used in any written document evidencing the transaction. At the same time it is worth recalling that the relevant rules of English law developed in England to meet exactly the same sort of situation that this case shows to be arising in the Gold Coast, and.· there is therefore no reason to suppose that in the interests of justice as between the parties a court would not, in circumstances somewhat different from those of the present case, have pointed to the distinction between ownership and the right to possession and ordered specific performance of the agreement. ]

As to the Principles Governing a Sale by Native Custom, see Sarbah, Fanti Customary Law, 2nd ed., pp. 86 and 87.

APPEAL from the decision of Jackson J., given in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, on January 29, 1951, reversing the decision of the New Juabeng Native Court" A " given on July 3, 1950, making an order for possession in favour of the plaintiff.

Akyeampong for the plaintiff.

Bossman for the defendants.

COUSSEY P. The plaintiff was successful in the Native Court" A " of New Juabeng in an action for recovery of possession of two parcels of land with cocoa farms thereon called respectively Pandakyere and Abamu which, according to his Writ of Summons, "were transferred to the first defendant by my late brother Topoa but first defendant had refunded same to plaintiff and had taken the sum of £20 from plaintiff and thereby (then) sold same to the second defendant." On the appeal of the two defendants the judgment of the Native Court was set aside by the Land Court. The plaintiff appeals from that reversal on the ground that the learned judge on appeal erred in hold­ing that ownership in the properties had not vested in the plaintiff because the sale alleged by him was conducted without the formalities necessary by native custom to constitute a valid transaction in that there were no witnesses present thereat and the lands were not inspected and marked out in the presence of neighbouring owners. pg 96

It is not disputed that immediately before the alleged sale the second defendant was in possession of the farms as the first defen­dant's caretaker. The properties had been sold some years earlier to the first defendant by the plaintiff's late brother Kwaku Topoa or Tuapoa. The plaintiff and the first defendant agreed that the farms would be sold to the plaintiff for the sum of £90, the amount the first defendant had paid Kwaku Topoa for them. The divergence between the parties was that the plaintiff, on the one hand, alleged that the first defendant had agreed to sell the properties on credit against a payment of £20 on account, the balance of £70 to be paid later, but that ownership passed immediately to the plaintiff, whilst the first defendant contended that there was no question of ownership passing to the plaintiff before full payment, but that on acceptance of £20 on account the plaintiff promised to pay the balance of £70 within one week. When he failed to do so the plaintiff asked the first defendant to transfer possession and ownership to him of the properties on credit, which the first defendant refused to do. After waiting for five months the first defendant sold the properties to the second defendant for £105.

The judgment of the Native Court is contained in a short passage as follows:

"The court after inspection formed an opinion that according to the facts gathered from both sides it was clear that the first defendant transferred the disputed farms to the plaintiff on terms of credit and should have right therefore to sue him (plaintiff) for the recovery of the £70 balance due by him and not to make another transfer to the second defendant about the same farms."

In my opinion, and this is a question of law, there was no evidence on which the Native Court could arrive at its finding that there had been a transfer of the disputed farms on credit, importing a vesting of ownership in and a right of possession by the plaintiff.

The plaintiff's evidence and that of his witnesses fall far short of establishing that he was ever put in possession. It is not without significance that the second defendant, the caretaker to whom the properties were later sold, was not asked a single question by the plaintiff to suggest that after October 25, 1949, the date of the alleged sale on credit, the second defendant went out of possession in favour of the plaintiff. Moreover, the first defendant denies that this happened.

It is to be observed further that the tenor of the first defendant's receipt does not evidence a completed sale of land. No description whatever of the property is given and there are no witnesses to evidence that the ceremonies denoting a sale and transfer of property by native custom had taken place.

Mr. Akyeampong has argued that the principles governing a sale by native custom as enunciateu in Sarbah's Fanti Customary Law, pg 972nd ed., at p. 86, do not cover the case of a sale where part payment is made. But in my opinion the evidence and the proper inference to be drawn from the evidence does not support the sale on credit set up by the plaintiff. Nor does the case of the Basel Mission Factory v. Bruce (1) assist Mr. Akyeampong's argument. In that case, in answer to questions put to him by the court which are set out at p. 103 of Sarbah's Fanti Law Reports, Captain Kojo, an expert on native law, said:-

Q.-By native custom how is land transferred by sale?

A.-Anybody who wants to buy comes, and you sell it to him.

Q.-When does it become his property?

A.-When they bargain and payment is made.

From the foregoing I am of the opinion that the plaintiff not only failed to prove an express agreement for sale on credit, but that the properties were never transferred to him on sale and he was never put in possession. The Native Court therefore erred in granting an order for recovery of possession and the learned judge of the Land Court was right in setting aside that judgment on appeal. The appeal must therefore be dismissed with costs.

KORSAH J.A. I concur.

AMES Ag.J.A. I concur.

Appeal dismissed.
