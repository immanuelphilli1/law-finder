 APAPAM STOOL v. ATTAA.

                                                                             

pg117

Native Law and Custom-C1lstomary tenancy of land-Obligation to render tribute (or courtesy, or hornage)-A ttempt by tenant to set up repetitive title-Plea of estoppel against landlord.

This was an action for a declaration of title to land. Permission to cultivate the land had, in the past, been given to a predecessor (in the line of succession) of the defendant, by the plaintiff stool. The pre­decessor at some stage had attempted to alienate the land, whereupon the stool had intervened and had substituted a grant of a customary occupa­tional tenancy for the existing informal licence. It was an incident of the customary tenancy that the tenant should provide a sheep annually to the stool, but this tribute had never'been either demanded by the stool or provided by the tenant. The defendant had occupied, farmed and improved the land for over twenty years without ever acknowledging the title of the stool, and against this action by the stool for a declaration of title to the land she sought to set up the defence that by refraining from enforcing its rights for more than twenty years the plaintiff stool was estopped from denying that she, the defendant, was the owner of the land in dispute.

Held, the provision of homage, or tribute, under a customary tenancy is not a rent as this is understood in English law. Failure to demand such tribute could not operate to vest some other kind of tenancy in the occupant of the land: much less could it operate to give the occupant a prescriptive title of ownership.

APPEAL from the decision of Manyo-Plange J., given in the Lands Division of the Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, on October 7, 1954, reversing the decision of the Akyem Abuakwa Native Court" A " on March 30, 1953, giving a declaration of title to the plaintiff stool.

Akycampong for the plaintiff.

Bossman for the defendant.

AMES Ag.J.A. This case started in August, 1951, in the Akyem Abuakwa Native Court "A," where the present appellant was the pg118 plaintiff. He claimed a declaration of title to land and recovery of its possession and mesne profits against one Attaa of Asuboi (as customary successor to Agya Mfodjo, deceased). Afterwards one Odartey Lanquaye was joined and called the co-defendant. The Native Court gave judgment for the plaintiff for a declaration of title and possession but added an order in the following terms: -

" Since the plaintiff has been declared the rightful owner of the land and farm in dispute we make an order that he compensates defendant (successor of the late Mfodjo who cultivated it) with one half portion thereof. To be divided by the Court for them if they wish."

There was an appeal against that decision to the Land Court, which ended in October, 1954. the Land Court set aside the decision of the Native Court and the order for the division of the land and substituted an order dismissing the plaintiff's claim. Against that judgment the plaintiff has made this present appeal.

The first thing to decide is who appealed to the Land Court, because this point is the basis of a ground of appeal, which is that the co-defendant did not appeal and that the Land Court therefore was wrong to reverse the Native Court's judgment in favour of the defen­dant and co-defendant. Mr. Bossman, who has appeared to oppose the appeal to us, started by saying that he represented the " respon­dents" (in the plural) and he had purported to represent both in the appeal to the Land Court. It seems that the appeal in that court was argued on the assumption that both defendants had in fact appealed. But when one looks at the documents one finds that, although an application for an extension of time purported to have been made by both of them and both of them entered into a bond and so on, only the defendant gave notice of appeal. There was no notice of appeal by the co-defendant as there ought to have been if he had wished to appeal.

In the case of Muffat, Dsasetse of Sempe, ete., and Others v. Kpeshie II, Mantsc of Sempe and Another (1), this court held that the filing of a notice of appeal within the prescribed time was an indis­pensable condition to the existence of an appeal. In my opinion, what is before us is an appeal by the defendant only and there has been no appeal by the co-defendant.

Of the other grounds of appeal, one is based on misdirection as to the burden of proof, another on misdirection as to estoppel, and the others can be summed up as being that Oil the evidence the Native Court's findings of facts should have been accepted and that their decision should not have been reversed.

It is not necessary to set out again in detail what was the case for each party. This has been done in the judgments of the Native Court and of the Land Court. It will be sufficient to say that the plaintiff is the Apapam Stool holder; that the land was (and in the result still pg 119 is) Apapam Stool land; that a predecessor of the defendant's, a stranger, had been allowed to cultivate the land (but did not); that he (or the next predecessor) purported to sell the land; that the matter came to the notice of the then stool holder as the result of a complaint made to him, and that the defendant's predecessor was "found guilty. "

This is common ground and it is important, I think, that the defendant's case admits that up to this time the land had not been alienated by the stool in any way recognized by native customary law. This admission, even if not thus stated by her in so many words, is postulated by her case, which was founded on an alleged sale by the then stool holder as a consequence of the complaint. In these circumstances it was for her to prove the sale. She attempted to do so, but the Native Court found against her on the probabilities.

This made the learned judge to say in his judgment:

The judgment appears therefore to have been founded on the weakness of the defendant's case instead of the strength of respondent's case, overlooking the principle which is now almost commonplace that in an action for a declaration of title, the plaintiff can only succeed by the strength of his case and not by the weakness of the defendant's case."

With all respect, I think this is a misdirection. The plaintiff had a case, which was sufficient unless rebutted, proved by evidence and by admission that up to the point at which the defendant's case diverged from the common ground the land was stool land. He succeeded by the strength of that.

This disposes of the ground of appeal about misdirection as to the onus. I will leave the ground about estoppel for the moment and consider the others, which concern the facts.

The learned judge said:-

" I am aware that the issue in the case is one of fact and that an appellate court should be loath to interfere with findings of fact by courts of first instance."

This, of course, is perfectly correct and is especially applicable to an appeal from a Native Court in a land case as was pointed out by the Privy Council in the well-known observation of Lord Atkin in the case of Abakah Nthah v. Anguah Bennich (2).

The learned judge followed the above passage with this:-

" but this is only true where the facts found are supported by the evidence; but where the findings are not so supported there is a duty on the appellate court to set aside such findings."

With the greatest respect, I am not able to agree that the Native Court's findings were not supported by the evidence. The Native Court "carefully examined the facts in this case," as they put it. There were one or two details which they might have gone into (for example, the witness who wrote the £10 receipt might with advantage pg 120 have been asked to explain why the date written on it is in the month . before the date of the £50 receipt). Nevertheless one must agree that they went into the matter with care, and there was also an inspection of the land

Their conclusion was:-

" In our judgment there has not been any lawful or customary sale of this land by the Apapam Stool to defendant's predecessor to enable the latter to dispose of it to co-defendant. Inasmuch as the late Mfodjo was allowed to till this land on condition that he would observe some courtesy to the Apapam Stool, his successor's could not claim title to it as a bona-fide property without the approval of the occupant of the said stool. Any negotiations made between Mfodjo's successors and co-defendant is uncustomary and unlawful and must be declared void."

In my opinion this conclusion was supported by the evidence and was the correct decision apart from any question of estoppel, to which I now come ..

This question had to be considered by the learned judge because one of the grounds of the appeal to the Land Court had been that even assuming the Native Court were right in finding that there had been no outright sale of the land, " the conduct of the stool in allowing the defendant's predecessor a stranger to occupy and cultivate and generally improve the land for over twenty years should legally operate to preclude or estop the stool from claiming possession."

The plaintiff's case was that the consequence of the complaint (which consequence the defendant alleged to have been a sale to her predecessor) was the grant of a customary tenancy for occupation and use of the land upon annual homage (or " courtesy," as the Natire Court called it) to the Apapam Stool, which was to be the provision of a sheep. The plaintiff admitted that a. sheep was never provided. The learned judge's view of this was:-

" The court in accepting the plaintiff's case completely failed to give consideration to the unexplained conduct of the Apapam Stool in allowing the defendant and her predecessors to cultivate the land, plant cocoa, make a village on the land and remain in possession of the land for over twenty years without acknowledging any overlordship over the land in the Apapam Stool."

and later his conclusion was:-

" Therefore on respondent's own evidence, I hold him estopped from asserting any title to the land."

In my opinion and with respect, I do not think that the circum­stances created an estoppel.

The appeal to the Land Court on this ground was founded on occupation, cultivation and general improvement of the land for over twenty years. But each of these is no more than what every occupier of farm land under customary tenure may be expected to do, in the pg 121 normal practice of good farming and husbandry, including where necessary the erection of buildings.

In the case of Kwasi Bruku, etc. v. Stephen Oeansey (3), it was said by this court (per Coussey J.A. as he then was)


      "One of the elements which must be present to establish acquiescence is that the possessor of the legal right must have encouraged the plaintiff" (the party who had pleaded estoppel in that case) "in his expenditure of money or in the other acts which he has done, either directly or by abstaining from asserting his legal right."

And a statement of Fry J. in the English case of Willmott v. Barber (4), at p. 105, was approved:-

., It has been said that the acquiescence which will deprive a man of his legal rights must amount to fraud and in my view that is an abbreviated statement of a very true proposition. A man is not to be deprived of his legal rights unless he has acted in such a way as would make it fraudulent for him to set up those rights."

The provision of a sheep by way of homage or courtesy was not a rent, in the sense of the word in English law, or in any sense. The defendant and her predecessors were there under a customary tenancy, and their continued occupation was referable to that. Failure to ask for the provision of the sheep was not an implied representation to them that they were in occupation on any other sort of tenancy, much less as owners of the land in succession to the stool, so as to induce them to alter their circumstances or situation as between themselves and the stool. (Whether or not an estoppel could arise as between the defendant and the co-defendant is another question which does not arise on the plaintiff's claim.)

As regards the order of the Native Court about the division of the land, an unusual sort of order, it was a qualification of the judgment for the plaintiff. When the defendants appealed to the Land Court there was no cross appeal by the plaintiff against it.

In my opinion this appeal should be allowed and the judgment of the Land Court set aside and the judgment of the Native Court restored.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

KORsAH J.A. I concur.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D
