           SACKEY v. ASHONG

                                                                             

 pg 108

Landlord and Tenant-Agreement for a lease-Part performance-Effect of covenant to repair- " Usual" covenants-Absence of proviso for re-entry for breach of covenant.

    Early in 1949 the plaintiff leased a house in Accra to a trading concern with which the defendant was closely connected. The trading company became moribund, so it appeared, and failed to observe the terms of the lease, whereupon the defendant came forward and offered to take a new lease himself. In September, 1950, the plaintiff's solicitor sent the following letter to the defendant:

              "P.O. Box 113,

Christiansborg, Accra, Gold Coast.

September 19, 1950.

Dear Sir,

Proposed Lease from Elizabeth N ah Adjeley Sackey to Samuel Kpakpoe Ashong, of House situate in Lutterodt Street, Accra ..

The following are the principal terms and conditions on which my client Elizabeth N. A. Sackey proposes to grant you a new lease of her premises situate at Lutterodt Street, Accra:

1. Rent at £300 (Three hundred pounds) per annum payable monthly.

2.  Period of Lease-Ten (10) years certain.

3. Lessor to be responsible for present house and water rates.

Any future increase to be borne by Lessee.

4. One (1) Store at present occupied by Metropolitan Chemists on ground floor and two (2) living rooms on first floor of premises to be excluded from Lease.

5. Lessee to be responsible for all structural and interior repairs and decorations during period of Lease.

6. Cost of initial repairs (to be agreed between parties before execution of deed) to be charged against rent by Lessee at the rate of £20 (Twenty pounds) per month.

7.  Lease to operate as from January 1, 1951.

8.  Usual covenants.

I am,

Dear Sir,

Yours faithfully,

(Signed) J. Quist-Therson, Solicitor for Elizabeth N. A. Sackey.

S. K. Ashong, Esq., Accra. "

The correspondence of which this letter formed part did not amount to a concluded agreement, but the defendant, who. was already in possession as a result of his connection with the trading company, undertook suo· stantial repairs to the property. The cost of these repairs was not agreed pg 109 between the parties in advance and the plaintiff complained that some of them were of a quality and were executed in a manner to which she would not have given her agreement. The plaintiff sought possession of the property on the grounds that no lease or agreement for a lease had ever been entered into, or, if one had, either that she had granted it on the strength of fraudulent misrepresentations made by the defendant (i.e., that in 1949 he had represented that the lease was required by a trading concern, when in fact he was merely clearing the ground so that he could take a lease himself), or that she was entitled to re-enter as a result of the failure of the defendant to observe the "terms and conditions" of the agreement. 'l'he trial court found for the plaintiff and granted an order for possession on the grounds that although an agreement was in existence between the parties: (a) it was reached as a result of equitable fraud by the defendant, (b) the proviso concerning initial repairs had not been satisfied by the defendant, and that as this constituted a condition precedent to the grant of the lease or agreement for a lease that the parties had in contemplation, the plaintiff was -entitled to terminate the tenancy and recover, and (c) the defendant occupied under a monthly tenancy at will in which there was an implied covenant to repair, the breach of which by the defendant entitled the plaintiff to recover possession. Held, (i) there was no evidence on which to base a finding that the defendant was guilty of equitable fraud: the plaintiff by her conduct was estopped from denying that she had the consent of the trading concern to the granting by her of a lease to the defendant.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. A proviso for re-entry on non-payment of rent is one of the incidents of the "usual" covenants-see Hodgkinson v. Crowe (2). If the "usual" covenants were embodied in the original lease or agreement between the plaintiff and the trading concern, and if (as appeared to have been the case) the trading concern had not paid rent, then the new agreement between the plaintiff and the defendant would have operated automatically to cause forfeiture of the original lease. The covenants and provisos which may be regarded as usual in all cases are:-

"covenants by the lessee to pay rent; to pay taxes, except such as are ultimately charged by statute on the landlord; to keep and deliver up the premises in repair; and to allow the landlord to enter and view the state of repair; the usual qualified covenant by the landlord for quiet enjoy­ment; and a proviso for re-entry on non-payment of rent."

In addition other covenants may be implied in particular circumstances, e.g. where they are the custom of the neighbourhood or district. No specifically Gold Coast covenants appear to have emerged.]

(ii) The letter of September 19, 1950, may have made the execution of repairs a condition precedent to the granting of a lease, but the defendant had executed repairs and therefore the condition, if it existed, did not operate against him. The express proviso in the letter was for agreement as to the cost of repairs: this might operate as a covenant, giving the plaintiff the right to seek an inj unction or specific perform­ance or a claim for damages if she had suffered loss in connection with the repairs, but it was not a condition precedent to entry into an agreement.

(iii) In the absence of express provision otherwise the breach of a covenant to repair does not give a right of re-entry to a landlord.

Hodgkinson v. Crowe (1875) 10 Ch.App. 622 and Be Anderton and Milner's Contract (1890) 45 Ch.D. 476 followed. pg 110

(iv) The letters between the parties would have constituted sufficient memorandum for the purposes of the Statute of Frauds but as they indicated that agreement had not been reached on the proposals put forward by the plaintiff they could not, on their own, constitute an agreement for a lease. On the other hand the plaintiff had allowed the defendant to take possession, accepting rent from him on an annual basis. Moreover the defendant had executed repairs, and these, coupled with possession, constituted sufficient acts of part performance to establish an enforceable agreement.

Richardson v. Gifford (1834) 1 Ad. & El. 52 referred to.

ApPEAL from the decision of van Lare J. given in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, on January 12, 1955, awarding an order for recovery of possession to the plaintiff.

Bentsi-Enchill for the plaintiff. Ollennu for the defendant.

COUSSEY P. This is an appeal from a decree of the Supreme Court, Accra, ordering recovery of possession of premises described as House D.684j2, Lutterodt Street, Accra.

By the statement of claim, the plaintiff claimed as follows:-

.. (1) A declaration that the said proposals for a lease made on behalf of the plaintiff were induced by false and fraudulent misrepresentations by the defendant. Alternatively,

(2) That no lease or agreement for a lease has ever been con­cluded between the plaintiff and the defendant. Alternatively,

(3) That the defendant having failed to carry out the essential terms and conditions stated in the said proposals the plaintiff is entitled to recovery of possession of the said property and that order for such recovery of possession be made against the defendant.

(4) An account of all moneys proceeds and profits that have been collected by the defendant or have accrued to his benefit since February 1, 1951.

(5) A detailed and full statement of account of any moneys spent by the defendant out of his own pocket in respect of repairs carried out in or about the said House No. D.684j2 Accra.

(6) A detailed and full account of money advances received by the defendant from tenants in the said House No. D.684j2 or credited to such tenants for repairs or improvements carried out by them.

(7) Payment of all rents due not only under the terms and conditions of the said proposals for a lease but also (as from April 1, 1953) the rents due under the assessment made by the Rent Assessment Committee, Accra, amounting to Fifty­seven pounds fifteen shillings (£57 15s.) per month."

           Looking at the writ of summons it will be observed that claims 5 and 6 above are an extension of theclaims in the writ which, however, was not amended. Those two claims relate to accounts which would more properly be required for the purpose of executing a formal lease between the parties than upon a landlord's claim for recovery of possession.

            The court found for the plaintiff on the first three claims but not on the fourth and sixth claims for an account of rents collected by the defendant since January 1, 1951, or for moneys advanced by tenants towards· repairs. As to claims 5 and 7 the court took an account of the defendant's expenditure for repairs to the property, and found that it amounted to £1,112 15s. 3d. It deducted therefrom £960 as for rent payable by the defendant from January 1, 1951, to January 1, 1955, and ordered the plaintiff to pay a balance of £153 15s. 3d. (this should be £152 15s. 3d.) to the defendant by monthly payments of £20.

   The defendant appeals from this judgment on several grounds. The case has been complicated by the introduction of matter which must be referred to in this judgment, as it led to one of the findings in the court below, but which is irrelevant to a decision of this appeal, relating to earlier dealings between the plaintiff, on the one hand, and a company called the Afro Pioneer Traders Ltd. and the defendant in connexion with the premises.

The property consists of a large house in a busy street near one of the public markets in Accra. It was in a very dilapidated condition when negotiations for a lease took place between the parties. Both the plaintiff and the defendant were actuated by motives of interest, the one to let the property in order to get it repaired without any initial monetary outlay on her part, but while obtaining a fair rental; and on the part of the defendant to invest, as he was entitled to, in leasehold premises in a rapidly developing city. pg 112

The learned trial judge has recorded that, from the circumstances surrounding the transaction and more particularly from watching the defendant's demeanour when he gave evidence, he formed the impression that the defendant was untrustworthy and unreliable. The learned judge then held that the defendant induced the plaintiff to let him into possession of the premises by equitable fraud, the fraud being that when she executed a lease with the Afro Pioneer Co. Ltd., if she had known the true facts and appreciated that she was not, in fact, dealing with a corporate body she would not have agreed to let the defendant into possession and, later, would not have acceded to the defendant's request for a new lease to himself personally.

This finding, which is the subject of the second ground of appeal,

is in conflict with the plaintiff's own evidence. She said:-

" the defendant represented to me that the company, that is Afro Pioneers, were no longer interested with the lease and that he was personally interested and would take on the lease from me. He promised he himself would repair the house. I was anxious to have the house repaired and agreed to let the house to him personally. "

Later, after an adjournment, the plaintiff said, continuing her evidence: -

" I say if the defendant had approached me in the first instance for a lease of the premises I would not have given the house to him. I told him I would rather deal with a trading company. Defendant must have known that I would not have given him the lease so he used a company's name. Later he told me the company was no more interested, and he asked me for a lease for himself. He was in the house and I could not do otherwise."

Towards the close of her examination, the plaintiff. summed up her position as follows:-

" Apart from the sum of £30 I have not received any other sum by way of rent from the defendant in respect of my premises. I wish to recover possession and to put a stop to speculations by defendant in respect of my premises. Defendant is trading with my house to my detriment. I am getting nothing from it: defen­dant is taking advantage of me. He had not done what he promised to do."

It appears from a statement of account of the Afro Pioneer Co., that the company made advances to, or payments on the plaintiff's account under its lease, as from January 1, 1949, and amounting to £104 odd. The company had failed to pay at £40, the monthly rental reserved by the lease, for about eighteen months before the plaintiff, by her solicitor, wrote proposals to the defendant for a new lease ofl the premises.

There is evidence that the Afro Pioneers Trading Co. had ceased t, function but had not been officially wound up. pg 113

Having regard to the evidence, in my opmlOn the plaintiff is estopped from disputing that when she let the defendant into posses­sion under terms for a new lease, she had not the assent of the company. She certainly did an act inconsistent with the continuance of the tenancy of the company and she could not allege that the first lease was still continuing. The company has not intervened to claim any interest in the premises.

I cannot find that the defendant's conduct was fraudulent or that he suppressed any fact about himself which induced the plaintiff to make a new lease with him.

One of the principal issues in this case is the interpretation to be placed on the proposals for a lease already referred to and its effect on the rights of the parties. The proposals are contained in a letter to the defendant dated September 19, 1950, which reads:-

The correspondence following this letter does not amount to a concluded contract, but the defendant was let into possession and he proceeded ~o do substantial repairs without objection by the plaintiff, the extent and cost of which is set out in a report of the Deputy Town Engineer.

The learned judge held that owing to the failure of the defendant to observe the initial conditions of the proposals for a lease the plaintiff was entitled to give notice terminating the defendant's tenancy. It was a condition, he held, within the contemplation of the parties that the repairs that the defendant undertook to do would make the premises decently habitable before a formal lease would be executed. The learned judge went on to hold that the plaintiff was entitled to give notice terminating the defendant's tenancy owing to his failure to observe the initial conditions of the proposals for a lease. Later the learned judge held that as the plaintiff's letter containing the proposals for a lease provided that the cost of initial repairs was to be agreed between the parties before execution of the formal lease, and no such statement or agreement had been settled by the parties, there was no final agreement but only a contract to make a contract.

In another passage of his judgment the learned judge held that in using roofing material of inferior quality, and in not effectively repairing the electrical system in the house, the defendant committed a breach of an implied covenant to repair which gave the plaintiff a right to terminate the tenancy and, as the defendant had collected substantial sums as rents from his sub-tenants during his tenure of the premises no injustice or hardship was occasioned him by granting the plaintiff an order for recovery of possession.

The learned judge also held that the defendant did not enter into possession under an enforceable agreement for a lease and that his tenancy had been valid only as from month to month.

With these conclusions of the learned judge, I find myself, with respect, unable to agree. In my opinion this was not a case for an order for recovery of possession. Unfortunately there appears to have been some confusion of thought between a condition precedent to the grant of a lease and a covenant to execute repairs and again as to the existence or non-existence of an agreement for a lease. It has been established, in the event, that the defendant did execute initial structural and other repairs to the extent of £1,112 15s. 3d. It is true that there was delay on his part in submitting his statement of account, but I fail to see how on that ground only his right to a lease could be defeated upon his account for repairs being agreed upon. It seems to me that if the completion of initial repairs and agreement as to their cost had been a condition precedent to an agreement for a lease the letter would have so stated. The expression "initial repairs" is obviously used to distinguish repairs that would be charged against the pg115 rent reserved, from the ordinary repairs which the lessee would be liable to pay for during the term demised.

Mr. Enchill for the plaintiff has submitted in support of the judgment that the defendant's possession was similar to that on a building lease, the defendant having a mere licence or a tenancy at will until the initial repairs were completed in accordance with the agreement and a lease executed. But the difference is that such an agreement for a building lease expressly excludes the creation of a tenancy from year to year.

As to the nature of the defendant's interest, clearly the plaintiff must be considered to have let the defendant into possession to do the repairs. Those repairs could only be referred to an agreement for a lease, for otherwise the defendant would have had nothing to do with the property. The agreement was not in itself a lease; being for more than three years and not under seal it was void as a lease. But, as from January 1, 1951, rent, on the basis of its being part of an annual rental of £300, was received by or credited to the plaintiff. In my opinion she was thereby estopped from denying the existence of a tenancy at least from year to year (not from month to month) upon such terms of the agreement contained in the letter as are applicable to such a hiring-Richard8on v. Gifford (1).

There were acts of part performance, referable only to the agree­ment for a lease, on the part of each party. On the plaintiff's part in letting the defendant into possession and on the defendant's part in doing repairs and paying rent as from January 1, 1951. In these circumstances the plaintiff cannot run away with the benefit to her property done by the defendant. Equity looks upon that as done which ought to be done, for here on a promise of a lease the defendant had laid out his money on repairs to the extent stated already. He cannot be treated as a trespasser. If the plaintiff, who was acting by a solicitor, was dissatisfied with the work done she had the matter in her own hands. She could have insisted at the time the roof was repaired upon a particular quality of roofing material, if it was a term of the agreement, and in default of her demand being complied with as to any particular work she could have instituted. at that time if she were in a position to establish it, an action for injunction or specific performance. She chose to rip off part of the roofing done by the defendant and to re-roof at her own cost.

If the plaintiff has suffered from any breach on the defendant's part as to the agreement for repairs her remedy may be in damages, but it is not for recovery of possession after she had acquiesced in the defendant's outlay on her property.

On this aspect of the case, in my opinion Mr. Ollennu's submission on behalf of the defendant must be sustained, namely: that under an agreement for a lease to contain usual covenants (see the letter of pg 116 September 19) a landlord is not entitled to claim a right to re-enter on breach of any of the covenants by the tenant save for non-payment of rent.

The learned judge has held that breach of an implied covenant to repair, by the use of inferior material, gave the plaintiff a right to recover possession. But it has been held in such cases as Hodgkinson v. Crowe (2), and Re Anderton and Milner's Contract (3), that such a covenant is not a usual one and a proviso for re-entry in such circumstances would only be by express agreement, as to which the letter of September 19, is silent. As observed by James L.J. in Hodgkinson v. Crowe (2):-

" A case in which I was counsel many years ago produced a strong effect upon my miml-a case where a forfeiture was enforced in which there was no legal defence, and no equitable relief could be obtained. Extensive copper works were forfeited by reason of a breach of covenant in not keeping up a fence which had become perfectly useless, and the not keeping it up did not do one shillingsworth of damage to anybody. Cases of that kind show how oppressively such a power may be used, and there is no necessity for giving it in the absence of express stipulation."

The rule applies to leases of special property as well as to ordinary leases.

Sufficient has been stated, I think, to decide that the judgment appealed from cannot be sustained. As there may be further litigation if the parties are unable or unwilling to compose their differences, I refrain from extending this judgment to a discussion of the accounts which is raised by Ground 14.

I would allow the appeal, set aside the judgment appealed from and enter judgment for the defendant with costs in this court and costs in the court below to be taxed.

Appeal allowed.
