                                                                        April 27, 1956.           

                                             West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session              

                               COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. YANKUM AND TANDOH

                                                 Coussey P., Korsah C.J., Baker Ag.J.A.

 

 

Criminal law-Appeals-Power of appellate Court to alter finding of trial writ-Circumstances in which power may be exercised-Power of court to convict for a minor offence other than the offence charged-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10, ss. 333 (1) (a) (ii) and 151 (2).

The two accused persons were concerned in an incident in which the bailiff of a Native Court was obstructed and hindered in the performance of his duties. The first accused was convicted by the magistrate of the offence, under section 372 of the Criminal Code, c. 9, of hindering and obstructing a public officer by using violence. The second accused was convicted of the offence, under section 170 (1) of the Code, of assaulting a public officer. On appeal to the Supreme Court their convictions were set aside and for them were substituted, by the exercise of the power contained in section 333 (1) (a) (ii), convictions for the lesser offence of pg  255 assaulting a Native Court bailiff, under section 21 (3) of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98. From these convictions the accused persons appealed on the grounds that the Supreme Court had no power under section 333 (1) (a) (ii) of the Criminal Procedure Code to alter the findings of the magistrate

Held, the power of an appellate court under section 333 (1) (a) (ii) of the Criminal Procedure Code to alter a finding, in conjunction with the power of a court, under section 151 (2) of the Criminal Procedure Code, to convict for a different, but lesser, offence than that charged, should only be exercised in circumstances where the accused person has,

·      by his defence to the original charge, at the same time offered his defence to the substituted charge. A finding may thus only be altered to one of an offence cognate to the offence that formed the basis of the original charge; it may not be an alteration to a finding of an offence that should have formed the subject of a separate and distinct charge. It may not be an alteration of such nature as to convict an accused person of an offence more serious than that with which he was first charged; nor may it result in a conviction for an offence which the original court was not competent to try.

Indian Criminal Procedure Code, s. 423, referred to; Sohoni, Code of Criminal Procedure, 13th ed., p. 903, quoted with approval.

APPEAL from the decision of Dennison J., given in the Divisional Court, Sekondi, on June 28, 1955, altering the convictions of the accused by the Acting Senior Magistrate at Sekondi to convictions for cognate but lesser offences than those with which the accused were first charged.

Scott, Crown Counsel, for the Commissioner of Police. M ereer for the appellants.

The following judgment of the court was given by COUSSEY P. The appellant J. A. Yankum was charged and convicted by the Acting Senior Magistrate, Sekondi, of the offence of obstructing a public officer, a bailiff of the Shama Local Council, acting in the execution of his duty at Aboadzie, contrary to section 372 of the Criminal Code; and with a similar offence in respect of the same bailiff, contrary to the same section of the Criminal Code, at Shama, and sentenced on each charge to twelve months' imprisonment with hard labour, the sentences to be concurrent.

The appellant Robina Tandoh "'as charged and convicted at the same time by the same magistrate of the offence of assault on the same bailiff acting in the execution of his duty at Aboadzie, contrary to section 170 (1) of the Criminal Code, and sentenced to three months' imprisonment with hard labour.

They appealed to the Divisional Court, Sekondi, from their convictions, and one of their grounds of appeal was that they were charged under sections of the Code which were not applicable to the circumstances of the case, as the legislature had specifically made pg 256 provision as to assaulting or obstructing a Native Court bailiff by section 21 (3) of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98, which thereby excluded Native Court bailiffs from the protection of sections 170 (1) and 372 of the Criminal Code.

After hearing argument the learned appeal judge determined, under the powers conferred by section 333 (1) (a) (ii) of the Criminal Procedure Code, to alter the findings on the three charges set out to convictions under section 21 (3) of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance. He did so and altered the sentences of Yankum to six weeks three days' imprisonment with hard labour on each charge;, concealment, and the sentence of Tandoh to six weeks three days and, in addition, ordered each appellant to enter into a bond to keep the peace for a period of eighteen months.

The appellants have now appealed to this court, the ground of appeal being:-

" That the judge had no power under section 333 (1) (a) (ii) of the Criminal Procedure Code to alter the findings of the magistrate to convictions under section 21 (3) of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance."

Section 21 (3) provides:-

" Any person who assaults, obstructs, molests 01' resists of aids or incites any other person to assault, obstruct, molest 01' resist any bailiff of a Native Court acting 01' proceeding to act in the execution of his duties shall be liable on summary conviction or on conviction by a Native Court, to a fine of £10 or imprisonment for a period of two months."

We do not propose to set out in detail the argument of Mr .. Mercer for the appellants, as they are sufficiently dealt with and answered in our reasons for dismissing this appeal.

Section 333 of the Criminal Procedure Code is identical with section 423 of the Indian Criminal Procedure Code. The practice under the latter section is set out in Sohoni's Code of Criminal Procedure, 13th ed., p. 903, and Ratanlal's Criminal Code, 7th ed., p.342.

To alter the finding means to alter the conviction under a certain section to one under another section and for that purpose the appellate court may avail itself of the provisions of section 151 of the Criminal Procedure, 13th ed., p. 903, and Hatanlal's Criminal Code, 7th ed., section 151 provides that when a person is charged with an offence and facts are proved which reduce it to a minor offence, he may be convicted, as in this case, of the minor offence although he was not charged with it.

It is improper to alter the finding so as to convict the accused of an offence of an entirely different character 01' of a more serious offence. '}'he alteration of the finding must be of some cognate offence and not of an offence which should have formed the subject of a new and separate charge; and it must be an offence which the original court is competent to try.

In this case section 21 (3) of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance provides for summary conviction, thereby clearly giving jurisdiction to the Magistrate's Court.

'fo sum up, as set out at p. 903 of Sohoni:-

"If the prosecution establishes certain acts constituting an offence and the court misapplies the law by charging and convict­ing an accused person for an offence other than that for which he

" should have been properly charged and if, notwithstanding such error, the accused has by his defence endeavoured to meet the accusation of the commission of those acts, then the appellate court may alter the charge or finding and convict him of an offence which those acts properly constitute, provided the accused be not prejudiced by the alteration in the finding. Such an error is one of form rather than substance."

We are satisfied that neither of the accused was prejudiced in this case.

The appeals are accordingly dismissed.

Appcals dismisscd.

S. G. D.
