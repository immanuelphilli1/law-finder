                                 [WEST AFRICAN COURT OF APPEAL]

                                           Accra, October 8, 1955. 

                                  Cor. Korsah Ag.C.J., Windsor-Aubrey and  Lingley  JJ.

                                                                         REGINA .                                              RESPONDENT

                                                            v.

                                                              KWABENA KWANSAH                                 APPELLANT

 

pg 35   Appeal from the Supreme Court, Gold Coast.

      Criminal Law-Detention of felon-Justification for killing person detained -Question as to whether a felony had been committed to be decided by jury-Criminal Code, c. 9, ss. 61 (1) and 310.

     Held, on a question of whether a killing was justified by section 61 (1) of the Criminal Code (which concerns arrest and detention where a felony has been committed) it is for the jury to decide if, on the evidence, a felony had been committed. By expressing the view that no evidence existed that a particular felony had been committed a judge in effect withdrew this issue from the jury: a consequent conviction for man­slaughter could not, in these circumstances, be upheld.

   Conviction quashed.

        Unger, Crown Counsel, for the Crown.

        Koi-Larbi for the appellant.

      The following judgment of the court was delivered by LINGLEY J. :

    In this case the accused was found guilty of manslaughter by the Jury.

     The accused shot the deceased with a shotgun, and in his final words to the jury the learned judge correctly directed the jury that if they thought the killing was justified they should return a verdict of not guilty.

     Earlier in his summing up he dealt with the possible justification for shooting the deceased; he told the jury that stealing a fowl was of course a felony: but expressed the view that there was no evidence that this felony had been committed. The accused, however, in his account of the events leading up to the shooting, the only evidence there was as to those events, described how he found the deceased in his home armed with a cutlass: and how the deceased came towards him with his cutlass raised, compelling the accused to retreat to his room. It was for the jury to consider this alleged conduct of the deceased: conduct that would amount to a felony under section 310, c. 9. In effect the summing up withdrew this aspect of the matter from the jury when dealing with c. 9, section 61 (1): we feel, however, this matter was essentially one for the jury. pg 36

We are unable to say that if this matter had been so left, the jury might not have come to a different conclusion. Accordingly we think the conviction cannot stand, and we allow the appeal.
