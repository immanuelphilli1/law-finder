                               West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session                                                               

                                       Coussey P., Korsah J.A., and Ames Ag.J.A.

                               ABABIO AND OTHERS v. DARKW A AND OTHERS. .

 

 

Native Law and Custom-Sale of stool land-Ingredients of effective transfer of title-Evidence required to establish change of ownership.

The plaintiffs claimed a declaration of title to an area of forest which, they asserted, had been purchased by their predecessor in J from the Akanteng Stool. They were unable to adduce any evidence show that there had been the public demarcation of boundaries regulated by custom to establish a sale of land, but they put forward a deed conveyance, dated in 1927, which purported to convey to their sucessor all the "right, title and interest" of the vendor. The deed of conveyance was executed by the Odikro of Akanteng, as vendor, by the Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa as the consenting party. defendants, who were in occupation and possession of the land in dh as tenants and licensees of the Asamankese Stool, claimed that the. belonged and always had belonged to the stool, that the Akanteng had no power to convey and the Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa no  rity to confirm a conveyance of Asamankese Stool land, and that had been no valid conveyance of the land to the plaintiffs' predec It was not disputed, however, that Akanteng was a subject stc Asamankese and that the Asamankese Stool owed allegiance to A Abuakwa as the paramount stool. The plaintiffs claimed that up t( the forest land in dispute had been unoccupied and that even thought pg 125 might lie within the area controlled by the Asamankese Stool, nevertheless the act of selling it constituted a sufficient assertion of ownership by the Akanteng Stool to enable them to convey a good title to a purchaser. They claimed further that in any case the consent of the Akyem Abuakwa Stool was sufficient to validate the conveyance to the plaintiffs' predecessor.

Held, (i) although it is possible to establish limited rights over virgin land by bringing it into effective occupation and cultivation it is not possible to acquire rights sufficient to support alienation by merely asserting ownership without more.

(ii) The land concerned was in any case clearly demonstrated to be Asamankese Stool land even though it might not have been in occupation at the material time, and as such, title on conveyance could only be made by the Asamankese Stool. The consent of the paramount stool (Akyem Abuakwa) is not required to alienations of Asamankese land.

Asamankese Arbitration (1929) Divisional Court 1926-1929, 220 followed.

(iii) It was a requirement of custom in 1913 that a conveyance of forest land should be evidenced by the performance of the customary rites concerned with publicizing such sale. In the absence of such supporting evidence and on the particular facts of the case the conveyance of 1927 would not operate to confer a valid title on the plaintiffs, even if the Akanteng Stool had been in a position to grant such a title.

(iv) If, in an action for a declaration of title, the plaintiff fails to establish his case, the correct order (provided the defendant has not counterclaimed for a declaration and established his case), is judgment for the defendant.

Kodilinye v. Odu (1935) 2 W.A.C.A. 336 followed.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. See also Abinabina Stool v. Nkasawura Stool (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 247, on this point.]

Cases referred to:

(1) Asamankese Arbitration (1929) Divisional Court 1926-1929, 220. (2) Kodilinye v. Odu (1935) 2 W.A.C.A. 336.

APPEAL from the decision of Jackson J. given in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division on December 21, 1951, after the suit had been transferred to the Land Court from the Native Court " B ,. of Akyem Abuakwa. The Land Court dismissed the plaintiffs' claim for a declaration of title.

Ahyeampong for the plaintiffs.

Ollennu for all the defendants save the thirty-seventh. Sawah for the thirty-seventh defendant.

KORSAH J.A. This is a claim for declaration of title to, and recovery of possession of a large area of forest land alleged to have been purchased by plaintiffs' predecessor, Kwame Kwakwa, from the Odikro of Akanteng about the year 1913 when it was a virgin forest. The Deed of Conveyance purporting to convey the right, title and pg126 interest of the vendor to the purchaser is dated May 25, 1927, and executed by Kwabena Birikorang, Odikro of Akanteng, as vendor and Nana Ofori Atta, Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa, as the consenting party, in consideration of the sum of £400 " now paid by the purchaser to the vendor."

Apart from this document, no evidence of the sale was adduced as required by native customary law, such as the ceremony of .. Cutting of Guaha," without which no sale of forest land can be deemed to vest title in the purchaser. This ceremony is performed in the presence bf witnesses specially invited by the parties to the transaction, in whose presence the boundaries are cut and the dimensions given, after which the necessary fee according to custom is paid to the witnesses. The object of this custom is obviously to give as much publicity as possible to the transaction, and to prevent secret dealings purporting to transfer title to land, especially in the forest areas, described by the plaintiffs as " Virgin Forest."

The defendants are tenants and licensees of the Ohene of Asamankese who were put in possession of the areas they occupy by representatives of the Ohene of Asamankese. It is admitted that the area in dispute is generally known as " Owuram land" and that the village of Owuram is situated at the edge of it. It is also admitted that Owuram is an ancient village and that there were people living there in 1913 when the sale was alleged to have taken place. The Odikro of Owuram is a relative of the Ohene of Asamankese and has testified that he holds his position as Odikro of Owuram under the Ohene of Asamankese and that the land in question is part of the stool lands of Asamankese of which he is caretaker.

The suit was originally instituted in the Native Court "B " of Akyem Abuakwa, but was subsequently transferred to the Land Court. while it was pending in the Native Court the Ohene of Asamankese applied to be made a co-defendant, but his application was refused. The Odikro of Owuram and other representatives and agents are, however, defendants, and have together with the other defendants except the thirty-seventh defendant, contested the case as licensees and tenants of the Asamankese Stool.

In the course of the proceedings frequent references were made to a dispute between the Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa and the Ohene of Asamankese, and to what is known as the Asamankcsc Arbitration Award (1); this shows that from 1921 or even before that year there was a dispute between the Omanhene and the Ohene of Asamankese with respect to the rights and interest which the Akyem Abuakwa Stool claimed to have in lands attached to the Asamankese Stool. The Odikro of Akanteng, who is llnder the Ohene of Asamankese, and is reputed to be a relative of the Ohene of Asaman­kese, supported the Omanhene against his immediate superior chief. pg 127

Eventually the dispute was submitted to arbitration, and the award was published in 1929.

During the pendency of the dispute the deed of conveyance upon which the plaintiffs rely to prove the purchase was executed by the Odikro of Akanteng with the concurrence of the Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa. Counsel for. the plaintiffs has made capital of this fact, contending that as the sale was with. the concurrence of the Omanhene, it must be deemed to be valid. It would appear that this was one of the issues submitted to the arbitration, and in respect of which the award declared:-

.. That the assent of the Paramount Stool is not, according to custom, necessary for the valid alienation of lands held by the Stools of Asamankese and Akwatia."

In regard to this finding, the learned judge in his judgment stated: -

"Quite clearly in the face of that finding the Omimhene of Akyem Abuakwa had no control over the alienation of Asamankese lands and . a fortiori'· could not possibly alienate them without the concurrence of the Asamankese Stool and there has been no such concurrence."

No satisfactory explanation has been given why the conveyance for the sale, which plaintiffs allege took place in 1913, was not executed until May, 1927, when the dispute touching the question of the Omanhene's concurrence to alienatio~ of Asamankese Stool land was ragmg.

There is evidence· on record which supports the contention of the representatives of the Asamankese Stool in regard to the relations between the Akanteng Odikro and the Asamankese Stool. I refer to the evidence of the Omanhene, the plaintiffs' witness:-

"Q.-Ohene of Asamankese is in charge of all the lands at Akanteng?

A.~It is not he only who looks after them, both he and Akanteng look after them and at Owuram where they meet: Yes, Asamankese and Akanteng are the same people.

Q.-As a matter of fact you cannot say to which of the stools this land belonged?

A.-As I've said before both people are the same and each farm up to a point, thus they meet and each one says it is his. 1£ a man sells he does not keep the money it is for the State. "

It is contended on behalf of the plaintiffs that the Odikro of Akanteng acquired title to the land in question by the sale to the plaintiffs' predecessor. The argument is that the land being up to that time unoccupied, subjects of the Asamankese Stool and those of Akanteng could reduce it into ownership by the simple act of selling it to strangers. It is conceded that subjects of a stool may acquire pg 128 farming rights over portions of stool land which they farm. But it is a strange proposition, which is not supported by any evidence on record, that a Chief or Odikro whose subjects had not previously occupied land can acquire title to forest land within the limits of a State by the mere act of selling it to strangers and contend that he has thereby reduced the land into his ownership sufficient to enable him to transfer his title to a purchaser.

It is further contended that the learned judge was wrong in

dismissing the plaintiffs' claim and entering judgment for the defendants, and that if in the opinion of the court the plaintiffs failed to prove title, the court could only non-suit the plaintiffs. In my view there is ample evidence on record to support the learned judge's finding of fact that there is no evidence which he could accept that any portion of the land described in the claim is Akanteng Stool land; also that in his judgment the rights of the plaintiffs to occupy the areas edged "green "-the plaintiffs' claim-could only be derived

. from either the Asamankese Stool or from those enjoying title under it. In a claim for declaration of title if the plaintiff fails the proper order is judgment for defendant. Kodilinye v. Mbanefo Odu (2). In my opinion this appeal should therefore be dismissed.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

AMES Ag.J.A. I concur.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
