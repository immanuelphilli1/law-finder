                                 [WEST AFRICAN COURT OF APPEAL]

                                            Accra, August 29, 1955.

                                            Cor. Foster-Sutton P., Korsah Ag.C.J., Coussey J.A.

                                                                       OSEl YAW                            APPELLANT

                                                           v.

                                                                    OPEl KUMl                               RESPONDENT.

 

 

pg 24   Appeal from the Supreme Court, Gold Coast

Documents-Agreement for sale of house-Agreement expressing intention of parties-Extrinsic evidence not admissible-Question whether purchase price as expressed included or excluded a sum already due from the vendor to the purchaser by way of mortgage on the property ..

Held, the agreement for sale was unambiguous on its face and expressed the intention of the parties that the purchase price specified in the agreement was the price at which the property was to .change hands. Extrinsic evidence was therefore not admissible to vary this expressed intention.

Appeal allowed.

Bossman (Prempeh with him) for the appellant.

Ollennu for the respondent.

The following judgment was delivered by KORSAH Ag.C.J.:

The plaintiff sued the defendant for specific performance of an agreement made on October 14, 1952, between the plaintiff and the defendant whereby the defendant agreed in writing to sell his house on Plot No. BA.1l3, Kumasi, to plaintiff for the sum of £3,800. In the alternative he sued for the return of the sum of £3,852 10s.

The defendant denied that he was liable and contended in effect that there was an ambiguity in respect of the said agreement of October 14, 1952, and therefore adduced evidence to support his con­tention as to what he alleged was the true intention of the parties at the time the agreement was executed. The defendant further counterclaimed the sum of £1,000.

With respect to the said agreement, the learned trial judge said: •. I am satisfied that it expressed the intention of the parties." In the face of this finding of fact, the only question to be considered is whether there is an apparent ambiguity on the face of the agreement to warrant further evidence to explain the purport and intention of the parties, as has been contended on behalf of the defendant.

The facts briefly stated are that on May 16, 1951, the defendant mortgaged the said building to the plaintiff for £1,000. Subsequently the said house was attached in execution of a judgment debt at the pg 25 instance of one Amadu Watala for recovery of the sum of £2,560 12s. and it was in consequence of this debt that the agreement was made by the parties thereto. It reads:

" That it is arranged that I sell my House on Plot No. BA.113 Kumasi through the Land Court, Kumasi to Osei Yaw of Kumasi for the sum of £3,800 (Three thousand eight hundred pounds) which amount he would pay into the court to liquidate the judgment debt and costs against me amounting to £2,556 and the balance be drawn by me.

" That in the event of his inability to comply with full payment of the cost of the sale, I empower or authorise him to pay the amount to cover the judgment debt and cost due to him by me, and the balance of the sale paid a month after."

The plaintiff paid the sum of £2,560 12s. representing the judgment debt and costs to the court. He also paid the sum of £239 8s. to the defendant for which the defendant gave a receipt, leaving a balance of £1,000 which the plaintiff appropriated in settlement of the mort­gage debt due by the defendant to him. This mortgage, dated May 16, 1951, and made between the defendant as mortgagor and plaintiff as mortgagee, provided that the said house was duly conveyed to the plaintiff as security for a loan of £910 with interest of £90, with necessary covenants enabling the plaintiff as mortgagee to sell the said property to recover the said mortgage debt and interest if the said mortgage debt and interest was not paid within one year from the date of the execution of the said mortgage.

The mortgage debt and interest, amounting to the sum of £1,000 had not been paid on October 14, 1952, when the agreement was made between the plaintiff and the defendant. It will be observed that no reference was made to the mortgage debt or the sum of £1,000 in this agreement. The defendant, however, contends that by it the parties intended the purchase price to be £4,800 including the mortgage debt of £1,000, and not £3',800 as stated therein. The plaintiff contends that there is no ambiguity on the face of the agreement; that by it the defendant agreed to sell the house for £3,800; and that it had no reference to the mortgage debt of £1,000 which the defendant was liable in any event to pay to the plaintiff.

The learned trial judge having found as a fact that the agreement expressed the intention of the parties, it was hardly open to the court to accept the defendant's contention that it was intended that the purchase price should be £4,800, or that it was intended to refer to the mortgage transaction. In my view to import such an inter­pretation would do violence to the terms of the agreement, which undoubtedly expressed the intention of the parties without any reference whatsoever to the mortgage transaction.

The learned trial judge was obviously influenced by the evidence that the house cost £4,800; also by the evidence of the defendant and pg 26 his witnesses who gave evidence of matters calculated to explain the purport and interpretation of the agreement. With due respect I am clearly of the opinion that he was wrong in permitting himself to be influenced by such extrinsic evidence in a claim based on a written agreement, which, he has found as a fact, expressed the intention of the parties.

 

In the case of North Eastern Railway v. Hastings ([1900J A.C. 263) the Earl of Halsbury L.C. said:

" So far as I am aware, no principle has ever been more universally or rigorously insisted upon than that written instru­ments, if they are plain and unambiguous, must be construed according to the plain and unambiguous language of the instrument itself. "

For these reasons I am clearly of the opinion that judgment should have been entered for the plaintiff on his claim against the defendant; and the defendant's counterclaim should have been dismissed. In my view this appeal should be allowed and the judgment of the court below set aside.

FOSTER-SUTTON P. I concur.

COUSSEY J.A. I concur.
