                                         Accra, April 13, 1955.

                                      Cor. Foster-Sutton P., Coussey and Hearne JJ.A.

                                                                       TEMING-AMOAKO                                          APPELLANT.

                                                                                     v.

                                          W. BARTHOLOMEW & CO. (TIMBER) LTD.                         RESPONDENTS.

 

Appeal from the Supreme Court, Gold Coast.

Contract-Frustration--Agreement to supply logs-Agreement impossible of fulfillment-Insufficiency of supply to meet 1'equirements-Liability of supplier to deliver to the full extent of his supplies.

Held, although the defendants were excused compliance with the terms of their agreement to the extent to which it was impossible for them to comply with it they remained bound by it to the full extent to which it was possible for them to comply.

Appeal allowed.

Blay· for the appellant. Christian for the respondents.

The following judgment was delivered by FOSTER-SUTTON P.:

This was a claim for damages against the defendant company for breach of an agreement under which they, in return for certain felling rights in the Numia Forest Reserve granted by the plaintiff, undertook, inter alia, to supply the plaintiff with forty-eight exportable logs every month out of those felled by them in the Reserve, to consist equally of mahogany and secondary species, for a period of six months.

The defendants admitted obtaining sixty-five mahogany logs and 143 of other species from the Numia Reserve out of which they supplied the plaintiff with thirty-one and forty-eight, respectively. They maintained that it was not possible under the permit issued by the Forestry Department to supply the plaintiff with the number of logs required by the agreement, and the present proceedings were instituted by reason of their failure to do so.

The learned trial judge found as a fact that it was impossible for the defendants to supply the full number of logs required by the agree­ment because there were not a sufficient number of trees of the required species in the Forest Reserve in question to produce the number of logs called for. The evidence was that one tree would produce, on an average, three logs.

There can, I think, be no question that there was evidence upon which the learned trial judge could properly reach the conclusion he did regarding the impossibility of the defendants being able to supply the full number of logs required by the agreement, and I am of the opinion that they were excused from compliance to the extent that it was physically impossible so to do: Howell v. Coupland (1876) 1 Q.B.D. 258.

The manager of the defendants' company, Mr. Barber, gave evidence that it was impossible to supply the number of logs required under the agreement, and the Forestry Officer, Mr. Yearsley, who was in charge of the Numia Forest Reserve during the relevant period testified that: •• the defendant company worked this reserve as efficiently as possible."

I am, however, unable to agree with the final conclusion reached by the learned trial judge, that on the facts before him the plaintiff was only entitled to recover a nominal sum by way of damages, which he fixed at £100.

Clause 9 of the agreement enabled the defendants to terminate the arrangement by giving one month's notice. They did not avail them­selves of this escape clause, but chose to go on working the reserve, presumably in an effort to get as much out of it as possible.

Having elected to do this I am quite unable to agree that they could make a further election by arbitrarily fixing the number of logs, out of the total won, which the plaintiff was entitled to receive. The defendants' case was that they had won all the exportable timber available in the forest reserve, and they having elected to go on with the agreement, I am of the opinion that the plaintiff was entitled to all the logs won up to a total of twenty-four mahogany and twenty­four other species for each of the six months during which the agreement subsisted.

It follows that, in my view, the plaintiff is entitled to judgment for the value of all the logs obtained from the Numia Forest Reserve, since they are less than the total the defendants contracted to supply under the agreement. I would, therefore, amend the judgment of the court below by setting aside the award of £100 damages to the plaintiff, and substituting therefor an award of £2,949 15s., being the amount of the agreed value of thirty-four mahogany logs, and ninety-five of other species, kept by the defendants for their own use, less 15s. per ton haulage allowed under the agreement.

I would also set aside the order for costs made in the court below, and award the plaintiff his costs to be taxed. The plaintiff is also entitled to his costs on this appeal.

COUSSEY J.A. I concur.

HEARNE J.A. I concur.
