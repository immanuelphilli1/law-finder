                                                         Supreme Court of the Colony of the Gambia

In re N'JIE (DECEASED). N'JIE AND OTHERS v. N'JIE AND ANOTHER.

                                                                                      Miles J

 

 

Probate-Claim for revocation of letters of administration-Unproved will­Production of will forty-five years after death of testator and after death of last surviving beneficiary-Presumption "omnia praesumunhlr rite esse acta," without evidence alhinde.

M., who died in 1910, left a will dated 1903 by which he bequeathed property to S. F. and D. as joint tenants. F., the last surviving joint tenant, died testate in 1953 and it was not until her death that M.'s will of 1903 came to light, it having apparently been in F.'s possession throughout. In 1955 letters of administration to the estate of M. had been granted to the first defendant. pg158

In an action by the beneficiaries under the will of F. for revocation of the grant of letters of administration to the estate of M. :-

Held, any suspicion there might have been as a result of the belated production of the will could only have arisen from events subsequent to the date of the will and the death of the testator and therefore, there being no evidence aliunde, the maxim "omnia praesumuntur rite esse acta" must apply to the will.

Davis v. Mayhew [1927J P. 264 applied.,

Semble, the English rules as to the devolution of property on death will be followed to the exclusion of any rules of inheritance contained in Mohammedan law, where a Mohammedan has duly executed a will drawn in English form. On this point see the remarks of Lord Radcliffe in Leong and Another v. Lim Beng Chye [1955J P. 648 j [1955J 2 All E.R. 903; [1955J 3 W.L.R. 303.

As to Presumption of Due Execution, see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 34, p. 65, para. 83 j for Cases, see Digest, VoL 44, pp. 280-285, Nos. 1122-1171.

As to the application of Mohammedan law in the Gambia, see Anderson, Islamic Law in Africa, pp. 225 et seq., and the Editorial Note to Surah v. Sagnia (1956) W.A.L.R. 97.

ACTION by the plaintiffs for revocation of a grant of letters of administration made to the first defendant. The defendants challenged the validity of the will put forward by the plaintiffs. The facts appear in the judgment.

N'Jie for the plaintiffs. Defendants appeared in person.

MILES J. The plaintiffs claim the revocation of a grant of letters of administration of the estate of Machimban N 'Jie, deceased, granted to the first defendant on October 26, 1955. The deceased, Machimban N'Jie, or Machim M'Ban N'Jie, died in the year 1910. He left five children; namely, Fatou, who died on August 5, 1953, N'Dumbay, who predeceased FatDu, Sainabou, who is the second defendant, Sassoon, and Momor, also now deceased. Momor was the father of the first defendant. Machimban had apparently four wives; Soseh, the mother of Fatou and N'Dumbay, Marrietta Taal, the mother of Sainabou, Marie Cham, the mother of Sassoon, and Bicay, the mother of Momor. The other plaintiffs are the children of the first plaintiff.

After the death of FatDu in 1953 there came to light a will purporting to be made by Machimban and dated July 17, 1903. By this will the testator devised No. 33, Leman Street, Bathurst, to his wife Soseh and to his daughters Fatou and N'Dumbay as joint tenants. Fatou, being the survivor, acquired the whole interest in the property. After Fatou's death there was found a will made by her on July' 13, 1945. By this will the testatrix divided the property at No. 33, Leman pg 159 Street, between the plaintiffs and the children of Momor N'Jie including the first defendant. Both these wills were found in Fatou's box by the plaintiff, Rodu N'Jie, who was residing in the premises at the time of Fatou's death and has in fact lived there most of her life. Soseh N'Jie and Rodu's father, Laitey N'Jie, were children of ~the same father but different mothers. Rodu is, therefore, a cousin of Fatou N'Jie. No action was taken by the various executors or bene­ficiaries at any time to prove either of the two wills. The defendants challenge both these wills. The only issue in the case, however, is the validity of the will of Machimban N'Jie in respect of whose estate the letters of administration were granted. This is purely a question of law.

The wiII having been made as long ago as 1903, it is not surprising that both the attesting witnesses are dead. A witness, Mr. J. F. Gomez, has, however, been called by the plaintiffs and he has identified the signatures of both attesting witnesses. It may be mentioned that the wiII contains an attestation clause in the usual form. The testator appears to have been iIIiterate and to have made his mark. The Clerk of Courts, Mr. Allen, who was also called by the plaintiff, stated that the handwriting in which the body of the wiII is written resembled that of the late Mr. Charles Thomas, a licensed but unqualified advocate, who practised in this country. Mr. Allen is unable to put it any higher than this and he is not positive on the matter.

Mr. N'Jie argues, first, that this wiII, being over thirty years old, is an ancient document and therefore that having been produced from proper custody no proof of proper execution is required. Secondly, he says that this is a case where the maxim, " omnia praesumuntur rite esse acta" applies.

In my opinion both these arguments are sound. There can be no question but that the will was produced from proper custody, namely, from the box of Fa tou N' Jie, one of the beneficiaries named in the wiII. Mr. N'Jie's second point is supported by two very strong authorities. The first of these is Davis v. Mayhew (1). The headnote of that case, in which the facts are very similar to those in the present case, reads as follows:-

" By a document dated in 1890 L. M. purported to appoint her cousin E. M. D. her executrix, and to bequeath the whole of her property to A. D., the daughter of E. M. D., absolutely. L. M. died in 1905. The executrix took no steps to prove the document as a wiII, and died in 1924. In 1925 the document was found by the plaintiff in a desk which had been in the possession of the executrix up to the time of her death. Both the attesting wit­nesses were dead. The plaintiff, as personal representative of his late wife A. D., propounded as the wiII of L. M. this apparently duly executed document, which was a printed form of wiII drawn and filled up in a handwriting that was not proved, but contained pg160 signatures of L. M. and two attesting witnesses, of whom the executrix was one, the signatures of the attesting witnesses being opposite a formal attestation clause. All the signatures were proved to be in the handwriting of the respective persons:

" Held, by Hill J., that suspicion was excited in his mind that there was something about the document which prevented the executrix from putting it forward as the genuine will of L. M.; that the onus lay on the plaintiff to remove that suspicion and that, in the absence of evidence aliunde, the maxim 'omnia praesumuntur rite esse acta' could not be relied upon as sufficient for that purpose:

" Held, by the Court of Appeal (reversing the decision of Hill J.), that there was sufficient internal evidence to show that L. M. knew and approved of the contents of the will at the time of its execution; that the suspicion arose at a later time and did not relate to the date of execution; and, therefore, that the presump­tion ' omnia praesumuntur rite esse acta' must prevail, and that in such circumstances the court would, without evidence aliunde, presume that the document was duly executed and that L. M. knew and approved of its contents."

Lord Hanworth l\f.R., at p. 276, said:

" 'l'he suspicion that the judge refers to is a suspicion as to the conduct of Emma :Mercy Dunmall and her forbearance to put the will forward. But if the will was once a good will, it cannot be disposed of except in one of the authorised ways of revocation: see sections 18, 19 and 20 of the Wills Act.

" When I attempt to analyse the suspicion aroused it seems to me that it arises from a disinclination to accept the will, upon a survey of the circumstances of Lavinia at the time of her death, or of later facts.

" If it was once a will, and as such was handed over to the custody of the executrix-and no more effective act could be done by a testatrix in support of it-it remains her will, and no mere conduct of the executrix subsequently will stop its effect. The judge was satisfied that Lavinia had entirely forgotten the document. Forgetfulness-even if surprising-may explain the inaction of Emma Mercy Dunmall. To import more into this inaction appears to me to be acting upon a presumption arising from conjecture alone. "

These observations are of great relevance here, since the first defendant has made great play with the fact that neither :Fatou N'Jie nor the executor made any attempt to prove the will. It is true that in the present case the will was apparently not handed to the executors, but to Fatou N'Jie, but I do not think this affects the application of the principle laid down by the learned Master of the Rolls. The explanation of Fatou N'Jie's inactivity may be forgetfulness, or, more pg 161 probably, that she regarded the will as a sufficient document of title to 33, Len:an Street, and was unaware of the necessity for probate.

An even stronger authority in favour of the plaintiffs is In the Goods of Francis Peverett (2). There the court extended the presump­tion of law " omnia praesumuntur rite esse acta" to the case of an informal holograph document containing no attestation clause what­soever, and in regard to which there was no evidence to prove the hand',:riting of one of the persons (both of whom were dead) whose names appeared near the signature of the testatrix at the foot or end of the document. This case was described by Lord Hanworth in Davis v. Mayhew (1) at p. 279 as a "striking, indeed a startling, decision." In the present case, as I have pointed out, there is both an attestation clause and evidence of identification of the signatures of the witnesses.

The first defendant objected to the will on the further ground that the deceased was a Mohammedan and that the will did not comply with Mohammedan law. There is no evidence before me as to what is the Mohammedan law with regard to wills, and it is therefore unnecessary to consider whether the court can take cognizance of Mohammedan law in view of the Mohammedan Law Recognition Ordinance, 1905 (Laws of the Gambia, c. 76). This Ordinance appears to confine the recognition of Mohammedan law to the Mohammedan court. In other West African territories Mohammedan law is applied as .• native law and custom," but this would seem not to be the case in this country. However, it is unnecessary in this case to express a definite opinion on this point. There is in any event, so far as I can see, nothing to prevent a Mohammedan from disposing of his property according to English law if he so chooses. The present will has clearly been framed so as to do this, and must be construed according to English law.

No suggestion is made that the testator was of unsound mind or lacked testamentary capacity in any other respect, and I accordingly hold the will of July 17, 1903, to be a valid will.

There will be an order that the letters of administration of the estate of Machimban N'Jie granted to the defendant Mustapha N'Jie on October 26, 1955, be revoked.

Application granted.

S. G. D.
