                                                     ABINABINA STOOL v. NKASAWURA STOOL                

                                                      Coussey P., Baker Ag.J.A., van Lare J.

 

•

Evidence-Claim for declaration of title to land-Absence of traditional evidence as to first occupation-Admissibility of sworn evidence given in other proceedings-Evidentiary value of possession-Evidentiary value of payment of tribute.

The plaintiff stool claimed a declaration of title to land, which admittedly had long been in the possession of the defendant stool, on the grounds that the predecessors of the plaintiff stool had first occupied the land and had granted possession to the predecessors of the defendant stool subject to the payment of a tribute. There was a paucity of reliable traditional evidence on the subject of migratory movements and conflict between the parties on this score: the plaintiff stool sought largely to rely upon the judgment and proceedings in another case to which they (but not the defendant stool) were parties, but which, they claimed, concerned the same land. It was established that some of the occupants of the land in dispute paid tribute to the plaintiff stool.

Held, (i) in actions concerned with the ownership of land the payment of tribute by the occupants of the land is not conclusive evidence of ownership of the land by the persons receiving the tribute, nor is it conclusive evidence of the existence of a customary tenancy, or of a licence, as between the payers and the receivers of the tribute.

(ii) In actions for a declaration of title the plaintiff must rely upon the strength of his own case and not upon the weakness of his opponent's. If the plaintiff does not discharge the onus thus upon him judgment must be given for the defendant, and this wiII operate as a bar to further proceedings between the parties on the same issue. The defendant wiII pg 248 not obtain a declaration of title in his own favour unless (a) he has counterclaimed for one, and (b) establishes his right to a declaration by the strength of his own case. Kodilinye v. Odn «1935) 2 W.A.C.A. 336) applied.

As to Judicial Proceedings, Admissibility of Judgments, Pleadings, etc., see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 13, pp. 684-693, paras. 753-763.

(The dictum of Sir Arthur Channell in Kobina Angu v. Oudjoe Attah (4), at p. 43, seems difficult to reconcile with Halsbury, and indeed with the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 37, r. 21, on this point.)

ApPEAL from the decision of Acolatse J. given in the Land Court, Central Judicial Division, on November 29, 1954, giving judgment for the defendant stool in an action by the plaintiff stool for a declaration of title to land and for damages for trespass.

Bing Q.C. and Ollennu for the plaintiff. Hayfron-Benjamin for the defendant.

COUSSEY P. This action was, by order dated January 12, 1953, remitted by the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council to the Land Court, Cape Coast, for a new trial.

In making this order the Judicial Committee set aside the judgment of Jackson J., the trial judge, on the grounds (1) that the opinion of Jackson J. that frequent and positive numerous acts (of ownership) within living memory are essential to justify the inference of exclusive ownership of land, thereby holding by inference that such title could not be established by traditional evidence alone, and (2) that the said trial judge erred in finding that the plaintiff was lying on the ground of discrepancies between his evidence given (a) by him at an abortive trial of the same action in September, 1948, and (b) by his predecessor in title in a Concession Enquiry known as the Bokitsi Conccssion Enquiry (1), heard in 1902 by Brandford Griffith C.J.

The new trial commenced in the Land Court on November 18, 1954, and concluded on ~November 29, 1954, when Acolatse J. dis­missed the plaintiff's claim for a declaration of title to the land, which is known as Nkasawura and is edged in pink on the plan in evidence, and for damages for trespass.

In considering the evidence the learned trial judge put to himself the following questions: (1) whether the plaintiff's predecessor granted the land in dispute to the defendant's predecessor on payment of a certain sum of money and on terms of payment of tribute, as averred pg  249 in paragraph 3 of the statement of claim; (2) whether, in fact, tribute was paid by the defendant to the plaintiff's stool annually in recogni­tion of the plaintiff's ownership of the land as averred in the first part of paragraph 4 of the statement of claim; and (3) whether the plaintiff's case was sufficiently established by the evidence, traditional 01' other­wise (this being my interpretation of the expression traditional and factual used in the judgment) to support a declaration of title to the land.

The plaintiff put in as evidence the proceedings and judgment in

·     t.e case of Chicf Arhin Dorman v. Chief Acquassie Esscamin, heard

·     in 1879. Dealing with this the learned judge observed in effect, in a somewhat confused passage, that the parties to the case were the present plaintiff's Stool of Abinabina (then the defendant), and the Stool of Ayanfuri; that the proceedings and judgment did not operate by way of estoppel as the present defendant's predecessors were not parties to that suit, and that its admission in evidence was rightly objected to by counsel for the defendant,-" but it was admitted solely for what it was worth."

The learned judge was, however, inclined to believe-and here, as I shall later explain, I disagree with him-that the land Oweawosoo, the subject-matter of the 1870 action, is the land round the village of Wioso. This is sho\yn slightly north-west of centre of the area of land verged green as representing the defendant's claim, and which is the land in isssue: the defendant does not set up any claim to the land outside the limits verged green.

Reviewing the evidence the learned judge declined to place any value, as supporting the plaintiff's tradition of a grant on terms to the defendant's predecessor, on the evidence of one Kojo Yamoa, a Denkyera man "'ho had died before the llew trial but who had been a witness for the plaintiff in the former trial before Jackson J. The judge declined on the ground that a certain difference I\ojo Yamoa had had with his own people, the Oman of Denkyera, affected his credi­bility. In addition, the evidence of the plaintiff's witness, John Sampson, apparently left a sorry impression on the trial judge as one who was trying to build up a case for a litigant whose side he supported.

The learned judge found that the defendant's predecessors were the first to clear the virgin forest of the Nkasawura land and to settle thereon; that even if the plaintiff's predecessors in their migration from Akwamu "'ere the first settlers at Abinabina and the surrounding area, they did not make any settlement at Nkasawra, and that the plaintiff's predecessors were not living on Nkasawura land before (" until" is clearly a recording error) the defendants had settle(1 there.

The learned judge, after considering the defendant's evidence of his predecessor's origin and occupation of the disputed land, then answered the questions he had earlier put to himself:- pg 250

.. it would be impossible for me to say with a degree of certainty that the defendant's predecessors came on the land and got permission from the plaintiff's ancestors to live on the land with the conditions attached to the grant ... showing when and how the plaintiff's ancestors reached the land in question so as to be able to decide which of the two tribes, the Wassaws or the defendant's predecessors, first arrived on the land in dispute."

" The evidence," the learned judge held, " clearly proved that they (the defendants) had been in undisputed possession of the land and had never acknowledged the plaintiff's stool as their ,overlord. "

The concluding portion of the judgment consists of a finding, expressed in different forms, that the plaintiff had failed to discharge the onus upon him of showing that the land in issue was so attached to his stool as to entitle him to a declaration of title and to damages for trespass. The plaintiff's claims were accordingly dismissed and judgment entered for the defendant.

The plaintiff appealed from this judgment on ten grounds. At the outset of the argument before us Mr. Benjamin objected to, and Mr. Bing for the appellant abandoned, grounds 3, 4, 5, 6 and 8 as they did not comply with rule 12 (4).

With the leave of the court, ground 9 was amended to read: "The closing remarks of the judge that the judgment is not intended to give the defendant the right to oust the plaintiff's people who are in occupation of any portion of the land in dispute are not consistent with the rest of his judgment."

Mr. Bing argued, as he said he would, the whole appeal on ground 1, namely, that the judgment is against the weight of the evidence.

He submitted that the learned judge misconceived the case; that there is ample corroboration of the plaintiff's tradition of a grant to the defendant's predecessors and there are facts that are inexplicable on the defendant's part and which can only be explained if the plaintiff's traditional story is true. He argued that the 1879 proceed­ings are some evidence of an act of ownership, not binding on the defendants, but of some persuasive value, as the Oweawosoo land therein referred to is the Wioso already referred to as being within and near the centre of the area green now in dispute, and that the defendants are therefore estopped by conduct from alleging ownership of the land in dispute because they stood by and allowed the Ayanfuris to litigate with the plaintiff's predecessor over the title to land which they now claim to be their own.

In my opinion the 1879 proceedings do not operate as an estoppel.

These were not so pleaded by the plaintiff and no evidence was led to suggest that the defendant's predecessors had knowledge of the pendency of that suit. In those days of limited communications it is pg 251 highly improbable that they would have knowledge of proceedings ill a court at Cape Coast, more than eighty miles away. Further, a reference to the judgment of Brandford Griffith C.J. in the Bokitsi Concession Enquiry (1), satisfies me that the land in dispute in that matter was considerably to the south of a line drawn from Abinabina to Ayanfuri, which villages are both shown on the plan put in evidence. The following passages occur in the judgment at p. 152:-

" ... in the 1879 case Essiemi's representative himself states that the land is called Abinabina and that the gold mines are at

·Owiosu. It is stated to be near Abosso (Aboasu), which the evidence in this case shows to be between Princisu and Abinabina And the evidence in the present case shows conclusively that in 1879 Kofi Ahin Ampong was working near Princisu at a place called Owiosu."

Now if we refer to the plan put in evidence in the present case we find, at its bottom, the village of Princisu, below the words " Abrabra Concession." At p. 153 of the 1902 case, the paragraph ends: "that there was a village of Owiosu about a quarter of a mile from the present Princisu." The next two lines at p. 153 of the judgment read:-

., In my opinion it is clear that the 1879 case referred to land round about Owiosu village."

Sufficient has been set out to show that the Wioso in the present suit, which is several miles as the crow flies north of Princisu is not the Oweawosoo (Owiosu) land the subject-matter of the 1879 case.

In my opinion the 1879 case is not relevant tD the present con­troversy, either as to estoppel or as to proof of the plaintiff's tradition relating to the land known as Nkasawura, and it should not have been imported into this case by the plaintiff.

In the course of a skilful argument Mr. Bing examined all the evidence, submitting that the learned trial judge misunderstood the evidence of the plaintiff's witnesses Kobina Teng, Sampson and Kojo Yamoa, while he ignored the fact that the defendant's second witness. Kojo Opoku, supported the plaintiff's tradition.

Having carefully considered that evidence I am unable to find that the trial judge failed to pay the propel' weight or paid undue weight to the evidence before him or that his observations on certain witnesses were unfounded.

Fobenso is a border village. It is not surprising that some of its occupants adhere to the plaintiff's and some to the defendant's stools. Payment of tribute there is not, in my opinion, a deciding factor one way 01' the other. When the 1879 case is disposed of there remains the fact that the learned judge in his discretion was not prepared to grant the plaintiff a declaration of title on his evidence both as to tradition and payment of tribute. 'the claim on trespass depended on proof of title. pg 252

Although there are certain passages in the judgment which it is difficult to follow, I am not prepared to say that this fundamental finding of fact by the trial judge is enoneous, indeed, in my opinion, he arrived at a right conclusion on the evidence.

The plaintiff's burden \vas undoubtedly a heavy one. \\' e are dealing with a sweep of over 250 years. In that space of time the defendant's people on the land have progressed from the occupation of hunting to settled agricultural communities. Generation after generation has come and gone, hunters' camps have grown into' villages, some have crumbled away and fresh ones have taken their place. It is not without significance that the plaintiff in his statement • of claim merely alleges that the land in dispute is attached to his stool. How it became attached and "'hat the origin of title is, was not averred.

This disposes of the appeal and I do not consider it necessary to deal with grounds 0 and 10, which were briefly argued, but which do not affect the main findings of fact against the plaintiff's case.

VAX LARE J. The parties are rival chiefs, the plaintiff represents the Stool of Abinabina and the defendant the Stool of Nkasawura. My Lord the president has already refened to the defendant's people dealing with the area in dispute for over two and a half centuries, during which time generation after generation has come and gone; villages have grown up and agricultural communities have settled on the area. There can be only a little doubt that the area in dispute has with the passage of time become stamped with the ownership of the defendant stool. I am of the opinion that the plaintiff stool also shares in this knowledge of reputed ownership in the defendant stool for when, in the year 1030, the defendant stool granted the Nkasawura Concession, affecting a portion of the disputed area, to the Gold Coast Selection Trust Ltd., the plaintiff stool in consideration of the sum of £100 received agreed not to oppose the application for a grant of Certificate of Validity.

The present controversy arose because subsequent to the grant of the said Nkasawura Concession the defendant stool, in continuing to deal with the land as owner, permitted one R. T. Briscoe to fell mahogany and other timber on the land. The plaintiff, claiming interest, therefore commenced this action to establish title to the area. The pleadings show that the claim of title is in the sense of. " usufructuary right" as there is no denial that the defendant's people are in possession of the area in dispute, but the allegation is that such possession had been with leave and licence of the plaintiff stool and payment of tribute, etc. The defendant flatly denies any leave or licence as alleged and puts up an adverse title of absolute ownership. As the titles are more or less self-contradictory, possession would seem pg 253 the only safe guide (Peter Awoonor Renner v. The Fanti Consolidated Mines Ltd. and Others (2) )to succeed the plaintiff must therefore make a really strong case. I

n my view it was necessary not only to establish positively that the defendant's predecessor obtained a grant of the disputed area upon which his people have settled for the last two and a half centuries, but also origin or root of title evidencing absolute ownership in the plaintiff stool. The record of the trial does not disclose any research·into the early history of how and when the plaintiff's predecessors, the Akwamus, migrated to settle in the neighbourhood so as to decide the  earliest or first settlers in the area. 'the plaintiff did not go further than the bare evidence in proof of his root of title than that his ancestors migrated from Akwamu and settled on the land in dispute; that they cleared the virgin forest and settled thereon. The defen­dant's case is that his ancestors also migrated from Ashanti and were the aborigines living on the south side of the Offin River, and that after the Ashanti invasion the parent body also migrated and joined the pioneer aborigines on the area now in dispute.

It would appeal' that the evidence of the origin of possession or hOI\' the disputed land came to be attached to either stool is conflicting, if not somewhat confused; there is little to choose between the rival traditional stories. Under the circumstances the learned trial judge was justified in dismissing the claim in accordance with the principle laid down by this court in Kodilinye v. Zvlbanefo Odu, etc. (3).

I am unable to find satisfactory corroborative evidence of the plaintiff's case, which I consider to be weak. Mr. Bing, for the plaintiff, has done his best, but without success, to convince this court that the proceedings in the 1879 case afford valuable corroboration of the plaintiff's case. While agreeing with the learned President that the 1879 action is not relevant for the reasons given, I would venture to go further in saying in plainer language that those proceedings were wrongly admitted by the learned trial. judge. By their reception in evidence the door was opened for Mr. Bing, who has tried to use the evidence of some witnesses in the ancient case to support the plaintiff's case in the present action. But:

When a witness has sworn to certain matters in one action, the record of what he has so sworn cannot be used in another action as evidence of the facts which he has sworn to. This is so even when the second action is between the same parties as the first and a fortiori where it is not. The use of evidence in a former action may of course be the subject of agreement between the parties to another action to sa \'e expense or the like" (Kobina Angu v. Cudjoe Attah (4), at p. 47).

There is no suggestion of any such agreement in the present case, and in the absence of such an agreement such evidence cannot be used. pg 254


   I share the opinion that the village "Oweawosoo," the subject­matter of the 1879 action, is not· the same village of "Wioso" appearing on the plan in these proceedings. Although the spelling of the name may be immaterial, it is nevertheless well acknowledged in this country that more than one village or town, even in the same part of the country, may have similar names. A reference to the map of the Gold Coast unmistakably shows that" Oweawosoo" (Wioso) in the 1879 case is several miles distant from the Wioso (Oweawosoo) appearing in the admitted plan.

The plaintiff's case becomes substantially denuded, and what remains of it is a mere asseveration by the plaintiff's own evidence but unsupported by any satisfactory or convincing proof, and the learned trial judge had no alternative but to dismiss the claim. Mr. Bing, attacking the judgment appealed from, has argued that the learned trial judge failed to take account of certain minor circum­stances and that he misdirected himself on them; he attaches great importance to the proceedings in the 1879 case which have now been ruled out. To succeed on the main ground which he has argued before us the appellant must satisfy this court that on a full consideration of the fundamental facts of the case the conclusion arrived at by the learned trial judge is wrong. In my humble opinion this has not been done, and I would dismiss the appeal.

BAKER Ag.J.A. I concur.

Appeal dismissed.
