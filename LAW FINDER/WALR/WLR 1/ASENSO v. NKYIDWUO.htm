                                             West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session 

                                                                   ASENSO v. NKYIDWUO                

                                                      Coussey P.,Korsah J.A. Ames Ag.J.A.

 

    

Native law and custom-Customary tenancy of land-Absence of agreement between parties-Forfeiture of tenancy by abandonment.

Native Courts-Evidcncc-Absencc of strict  rules in Customary procedure ­Admissibility of documentary evidence that materially throws light on the dispute.

The defendant, some years before this action was brought, entered into an agreement with a stool to which he was a stranger whereby he ' was allowed to cultivate a piece of stool land. It appeared that by local custom the question of the tribute payable by a grantee is settled at the pg 244 time of the grant where the land is to be cultivated for the production of foodstuffs, but that where cocoa is to be produced the question of tribute may be left until such time as the trees planted by the grantee produce a crop. In this case the defendant was asked by the stool to enter into a tribute agreement at the time he received his grant, but he declined to do so on the grounds that he was planting cocoa trees. After a lapse of some years, during which it did not appear that any tribute was paid, the stool regranted the land to the plaintiff, who was a neighbouring farmer, apparently on the grounds that the defendant, by failing to enter into a tribute agreement and also by neglecting to cultivate the land, had abandoned any rights he may have had. The:> defendant thereupon complained to the local authority which, acting presumably in furtherance of the powers conferred upon local authorities :> by the Local Government Ordinance, c. 64, instructed him to retake possession of the land. This he did for a period, dispossessing the plaintiff, and it was in respect of this dispossession that the plaintiff brought the present action, alleging that the original grantors had been justified in regarding the conduct of the defendant as abandonment amounting to forfeiture, and that his, the plaintiff's, customary tenancy of the land was the only valid subsisting one.

 Held, (i) it is not unreasonable for a stool to require that the terms of an agreement between itself and a stranger grantee of stool lands shall be settled before the grantee is a1Iowed to take possession.

(ii) The refusal to enter into an agreement for tribute, coupled with the neglect of the land concerned over 1t considerable period of years, constituted abandonment sufficient to allow the grantors to treat the original grant as forfeitable at their option.

Sarbah, Fanti Customary Law, 1st ed., p. 56, quoted with approval. (iii) The Native customary rules of procedure, applicable in Native Courts, permit the admission of documentary evidence where this will materially shed light upon the subject in dispute, and even though such evidence would be inadmissible in the Supreme Court.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. See also Amuabimaa and Another v. Badu and Others (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 227, on this point.]

APPEAL from the decision of Lingley J. in the Land Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, given on June 4, 1954, reversing the judgment of the Asantehene's Court" A " given on March 16, 1954, and restoring the judgment of Juaben Native Court" 13 " given on September 18, 1953, allowing the plaintiff £75 damages in his action arising from his dispossession of land by the defendant.

Prempeh for the plaintiff.

Danquah for the respondent.

COUSSEY P. In this action, the defendant appeals from the judgment of the Land Court, Kumasi, which restored the judgment of the Native Court " 13 " of Juaben which had awarded the plaintiff £75 damages for being" deprived by the defendant" of the possession on his cocoa farm at Sradiebedwumgya. pg 245

It is not disputed that the farm had, in the first instance, been made on Juaben Stool land by the defendant. The defendant said in evidence at the hearing:-

" I introduced myself to the Omanhene of Juaben that I am cultivating in farm or land in his Stool land ... I went away. When I was in my town he sent a bearer to call me. He told me I must come and enter into agreement with Juaben Stool and myself. I told him that I am now planting cocoa trees, also it is not good to enter into agreement by now. He said he is not

·        talking about cocoa but foodstuffs. I went to inform my chief Adontenhene. Adontenhene deputed bearer to come and see Omanhene about the agreement. Omanhene told the bearer that he is thinking of it. Because there was some misunderstanding between myself and Omanhene I was not caring the farm.

" About seven years after the farm had been taken away by late Nana Akuamoah Boaten for failing to enter into agreement with the Juaben Stool my nephew Kwaku Wusu came to tell me that the farm had been given to I\wame Antwi. I lodged a complaint against Kwame Antwi before the Local Council who ordered that I must take my farm."

In answer to the court the defendant admitted that he then entered into agreement with the Juaben local council.

Sufficient has been set out from the defendant's evidence and it is only necessary to add that the plaintiff's claim was that after the farm had been taken by the Omanhene from the defendant it was granted to the plaintiff as an adjoining O\mer and subject of the Stool of Juaben to cultivate, which he did, but that for a period of six months he was deprived of possession, as above set out, by the Juaben local council's order in the defendant's favour.

The issue is whether the plaintiff is entitled to ownership of the farm by virtue of the later grant to him or whether the defendant is right in contending that he was the original grantee and that he could not be deprived of his interest in the farm by reason only of his failure to enter into an agreement with the Juaben Stool when required to do so.

The trial Native Court held that the defendant refused to enter into the agreement and waived his right to possession to the Juabenhene, who thereupon granted the land to the plaintiff.

Dr. Danquah invites this court to hold that there was no waiver by the defendant, but that the stool improperly forfeited the defen­dant's right to occupy the land. In support of his contention, Dr. Danquah relied on the pronouncement of the Appellate Native Court, the Asantehene's Court" Al " at Kumasi, that the customary practice throughout Ashanti is that until a stranger-farmer's cocoa trees have matured, the landlord has no right to conclude any agree­ment with the farmer as to tribute and even if the tenant declines to pg 246enter into an agreement, the landlord's remedy is to institute pro­ceedings against the tenant, but that he has no right of his own free will and accord to dispossess him of his cultivation for the benefit of another.

Dr. Danquah submitted that this pronouncement, coming as it does from such an expert authority, could not be impeached and that the judgment of the Native Court of Appeal reversing the decision of the Native Court of first instance should not therefore have been set aside by the Land Court and should be restored on this appeal.

In my view the above statement of the alleged Native customary  law is too sweeping to be given approval by this court without further I> authority, which Dr. Danquah, when asked, was unable to supply to the court. However that may be, it must be borne in mind that the decision of the Native Court is based not on forfeiture but on the act

of waiver or abandonment of the farm by the defendant.

In my opinion it was reasonable for the Omanhene to request the defendant to enter into terms of tenancy, not only as to simple tribute, but also in the general interest of the stool including its timber and mineral rights.

The defendant's refusal to enter into any form of agreement and his failure to take any care whatever of the farm or clearance he had made on the said land for over ten years, constitute sufficient evidence of abandonment entitling the Omanhene to reallocate the area to the plaintiff. In the light of the defendant's admission I fail to see how the Native Appeal Court could hold that" there is no corroboration on record proving that the defendant willingly waived his possession of the farm in favour of stooL" It was not abandonment by express declaration it is true, but it was abandonment by conduct over a number of years. It seems to be founded on common sense and reason that a stranger or a subject of a stool who declines to observe a lawful and reasonable demand of the stool holder affecting his tenancy should not expect his rights to land to be preserved, in this case for some thirteen years. This is supported by Sarbah, who says at p. 56 of the Fanti Customary La,,", ]897 edition:-

" But where a person in possession of the public land abandons it or his family have abandoned it for more than ten years at least the village headman and elders can allow another person to occupy the same."

The second point raised by Dr. Danquah is that a letter, from the Juabenhene to the Assistant Government Agent, should not have been admitted in evidence as it was res inter alios acta the defendant. That letter contains the passage:-

" Before the stranger could start plantation of cocoa he was called to enter into agreement with the Juaben Stool, he refusing to agree to my request risked his title of possession to me and left a way. " pg 247


        That letter would not, perhaps, have been admissible in evidence in the Supreme Court, but there are no strict rules of evidence in Native customary procedure, the tendency being to admit all that materially throws light on the controversy, as this letter undoubtedly did.

Dr. Danquah argues that if this letter is disregarded there is no evidence of waiver. As to this it is only necessary to observe that the evidence of abandonment of the farm is supplied by the defendant himself as has already been pointed out.

I would therefore dismiss this appeal with costs .

KORSAH J.A. I concur .

BAKER Ag.J.A. I concur.

Appeal dismissed.
