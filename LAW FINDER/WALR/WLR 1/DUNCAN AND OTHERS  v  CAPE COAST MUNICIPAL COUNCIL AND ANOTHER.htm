                     Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court, Cape Coast

                                                                              Manyo-Plange J

                                                                           DUNCAN AND OTHERS

                                                                                           v                                                                                      

                                        CAPE COAST MUNICIPAL COUNCIL AND ANOTHER

 

 

Local Government-Duty of municipal council to levy rates-Requirements as to publicity-Municipal Councils Ordinance, c. 9 of 1953 and Muni­cipal Councils (Rates) Reg111ations, 1953, L.N. 432.

The duty to levy rates is imposed upon municipal councils by the Municipal Councils Ordinance, c. 9 of 1953, ss. 93, 94 and 95. By pg 66 section 97 every council, after passing the resolution necessary to deter­mine a rate, is required to publicize the rate so determined in accordance with regulations made under the Ordinance. The power to make such regulations is conferred upon the Minister responsible by section 109. This section permits the Minister, inter alia, to prescribe the notice to be given by a council of its intention to levy a rate, as well as to prescribe the manner in which a rate, duly resolved upon by a council, shall be publicized.

Regulations have been made by the Minister of Local Government and Housing under section 109 and are contained in the Municipal Councils (Rates) Regulations, 1953, L.N. 432. They do not prescribe the notice to be given by a council of its intention to levy a rate, but they make provision for the subsequent publication required by section 97 of the Ordinance. The Schedule to the Regulations sets out the form in which the notice of a rate is to be cast, section 2 (1) requires this to be inserted in the Gazette and section 2 (2) requires that the council imposing a rate shall: "cause such notice to be published in at least one daily newspaper circulating in the area of its authority ... "

The Cape Coast Municipal Council had passed a resolution levying a rate of eight shillings per capita for 1954-1955, and this rate, as is required by section 95 (1) of the Ordinance, had received the approval of the Minister. A notice in the approved form had been inserted in the Gazette but the council had failed to make the necessary insertion in a daily newspaper. In an action for a declaration, brought by ratepayers in Cape Coast against the municipal council and the Minister,

Held, the Cape Coast Municipal Council had not complied with the requirements of section 97 of the Ordinance as to publicity and the ratepayers as a result were entitled to a declaration that the rate was invalid.

Per curiam: the council had also failed to publish in advance notice of their intention to levy a rate, but as the only mandatory requirement laid upon them by the Ordinance was that contained in section 97, relating to subsequent publicity, this failure would not have sufficed to invalidate the rate.

For the Municipal Councils Ordinance, c. 9 of 1953, see Laws of the Gold Coast, Supplement 1952-1954, p. 1. For the Municipal Councils (Rates) Regulations, 1953, L.N. 432, see Laws of the Gold Coast, Subsidiary Legislation 1953, p. 609.

ACTION for a declaration, an injunction and an account. The facts are fully stated in the judgment.

Awoonor- Williams, Blay and Benjamin, Jnr., for the plaintiffs. Ademola for the defendants.

MANYO-PLANGE J. The plaintiffs in this case are ratepayers of the Municipality of Cape Coast, and by their writ are claiming against the Cape Coast Municipal Council and Mr. Asafo-Adjaye, the Minister of Local Government and Housing;-

(i) a declaration of the invalidity of a basic rate for 1954-1955 made by a resolution of the Cape Coast Municipal Council, and amounting to eight shillings (reduced to four shillings in the case of women) for all persons of or above the age of pg 67 eighteen years residing within or owning immovable property within the municipal area,

(ii) an order setting aside the said rate and an injunction restrain­ing the council from proceeding against persons defaulting in payment of it, and

(iii) an account of rates already collected by the council.

The plaintiffs' writ was specially endorsed and the defendants respectively filed their defences. Both defendants maintained that the rates were validly imposed. The first defendants by paragraph 4 of their defence pleaded that paragraph 7 of the plaintiffs' particulars of claim disclosed no cause of action and so should be struck out, while by paragraph 6 they pleaded that the whole action was misconceived and should be dismissed. By this they presumably meant that the action was not maintainable by the plaintiffs. As the matters in paragraphs 4 and 6 are matters of law, these were set down for argument and were argued.

On December 19, 1955, I ruled that the claim for a declaration was maintainable by the plaintiffs but that the claim for an account was not, and I accordingly dismissed that claim. This judgment therefore only concerns the claim for a declaration ..

The rate, the validity of which is challenged, was approved by the Minister of Local Government and Housing, the second defendant, on November 3, 1954, and notice of it was published in the Gold Coast Gazette, No. 76 of November 13, 1954, at p. 1422.

The power of the municipal council to impose and levy an annual rate is given by sections 93, 94 and 95 (1) of the Municipal Councils Ordinance, 1953 (hereinafter referred to as " the Ordinance "). The plaintiff's case is that the rate imposed by virtue of the sections quoted is invalid because the municipal council failed to give the notices of this rate that are required by law. 'l'hey say that only one notice was given, this being the one published in the Gazette. This was admitted to be true by the defendants. Section 97 of the Ordinance provides that the imposition of rates is to be by a resolution of the council; that after passing such a resolution, notice of the rate shall be given by the council, and that the rate shall not be valid unless the notice is given in the manner prescribed by regulations which are authorized to be made under section 109 of the Ordinance.

Section 97 of the Ordinance reads as follows:

" Notice of every rate shall be given by the Council after the passing of a resolution making such a rate, and the rate shall not be valid unless notice thereof is duly given in the manner for the time being prescribed by Regulations made under the provisions of section 109 of this Ordinance."

Section 109 empowers the Minister with the prior approval of the Governor to make regulations prescribing, inter alia, the notice to be given by a council of its intention to make and levy a rate and the pg 68 manner in which notice of a rate shall be published. "Minister" is defined as meaning: "the Minister for the time being charged with responsibility for the exercise of the powers and the performance of the duties vested in the Minister by this Ordinance." There is no evidence before me that the Minister of Local Government and Housing is the Minister charged with responsibility for the exercise of these powers and for the performance of these duties. Regulations have, however, been made by the Minister of Local Government and Housing under section 109 of the Ordinance and, since his power to make these regulations has not been challenged, he must be presumed to be the Minister charged with the exercise of the powers and the performance of the duties vested in a Minister under the Ordinance. The Regulations made by the Minister are the Municipal Councils (Rates) Regulations, 1953, L.N. 432 (hereafter referred to as "the Regulations ").

Section 109 (a) in my view impliedly enjoins the giving by the council of a notice of its intention to make and levy a rate in such manner as may be prescribed by the Minister. There is nothing in sections 93, 94 or 95 of the Ordinance about the giving of such notice; therefore unless section 109 (a) is considered as I have considered it, it will be meaningless. The Minister in the Regulations under section 109 has omitted to prescribe the notice to be given of an intention to make and levy a rate. The Regulations only prescribe the notice to be given of a rate and the manner in which such notice is to be given.

In view of section 97, the omission to give notice of an intention to make and levy a rate does not, in my opinion, invalidate a rate duly made. The section expressly states what would inva1idate a rate duly made by a resolution of the council, and that is either an omission to give notice of a rate, or the giving of a notice in the wrong manner. By Regulation 2 (1) the notice is to be in the form of the Schedule to the Regulations. This is to be published in the Gazette. In addition, by Regulation 2 (2):

" ... the Council imposing a rate shall cause such notice to be published in at least one daily newspaper circulating in the area of its authority and may cause such notice to be published in such other place and in such other manner as may appear to it to be desirable."

Now, the mandatory provision of the first part of Regulation .2 (2) is understandable; it is to ensure that the notice comes to the attention of as many of the ratepayers as possible.

As has been conceded by the defendants, the only notice' given of the rate challenged was the notice published in the Gazette. The council therefore failed to give notice of the rate: "in the manner for the time being prescribed by regulations under the provisions of section 109 of this Ordinance." Therefore the rate made by resolution pg 69 of the Cape Coast Municipal Council for the financial year 1954-1955 was invalid, by virtue of section 97 of the Ordinance, for failing to comply with the provisions of Regulation 2(2) of the Municipal Councils (Rates) Regulations, 1953, and I accordingly declare the said rates invalid and set them aside. I therefore give judgment for the plaintiffs. Both plaintiffs and defendants agree that the said rates have already been collected. The granting of the injunction claimed would thus serve no purpose and so I do not grant it

. Declaration granted.
