                                 January 23, 1956

                  Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Ashanti Judicial Division, Divisional Court, Kumasi

                                                                        Quashie-Idun J.

                                                                              NSIAH  

                                                                                    v   

                                                                   ANANE AND OTHERS.                                                                                  

                                  

 

Negligence - Highway - Collision of motor-vehicles - Injury to passenger­ Amputation of right arm-Quantum of damages.

The plaintiff was travelling as a passenger in a vehicle belonging to the third defendant and being driven at the time by the latter's servant, the fourth defendant. The vehicle came into collision with a second vehicle, travelling in the opposite direction, belonging to the first defendant and being driven by the second defendant. The plaintiff received injuries of such severity that his arm had subsequently to be amputated. '1'he defence claimed that although the injuries received by the plaintiff were caused by the collision, the severity of those injuries was largely contributed to by the position in which the plaintiff was sitting and that this position constituted either contributory negligence on his part, or a voluntary acceptance of the danger to which he was exposed. The plaintiff, who was a tailor earning about £1 per day and also owning some cocoa farms, in normal circumstances would have been able to look forward to many years during which he would have earned an unimpaired income.

Held, on the facts, both vehicles were being driven negligently at the time of the accident; there was no evidence that the position in which the plaintiff was sitting indicated a lack of reasonable care for his own safety such as would sustain a defence of contributory negligence; and there was no evidence to support" volenti non fit injuria." In the circumstances of the plaintiff and bearing in mind his age, which was about forty years, and the incapacitating effect of his injury, which by the agreed medical report was about 60 per cent., the appropriate award of general damages was £1,500.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Actions for negligence in West Africa (a predomi­nantly rural and non-industrialized area) are, relative to their frequency pg 76 in England, rare. The law of negligence in the Gold Coast is basically the same as English law but, unless by express statement or necessary impli­cation it is clearly otherwise, it does not include English statutes made after the Gold Coast acquired its own Legislative Assembly. This happened in 1874. Thus the Law Reform (Contributory Negligence) Act, 1945 (8 & 9 Geo. 6, c. 28), does not apply and therefore, as in England before June, 1945, in an action for injuries arising from negligence it is a good defence for the defendant to prove that the plaintiff, by some negligence on his own part, directly contributed to the injury in the sense that it formed a material part of the effective cause thereof (see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 23, pp. 679-689, paras. 963-973). The Law Reform (Married Women and Tortfeasors) Act, 1935 (25 & 26 Geo. 5, c. 30), does not apply, so that as in England before August 2, 1935, there can be only one action and only one judgment for the amount of damages to which a plaintiff is entitled; a recovery of judgment against one of joint tortfeasors is a bar to any further action against the others, even though it remains unsatisfied, while a release granted to one discharges the others; and any damages awarded cannot be apportioned between the various defendants. Where a negligent act has caused a death the position is not so clear, because the relevant English law includes statutes passed before 1874. Such statutes as these apply only if they are interpreted as being "of general application" (in the Gold Coast this provision is contained in the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 83). It is not clearly settled whether the Fatal Accidents Acts, 1846 and 1864 (9 & 10 Viet. c. 93, and 27 and 28 Viet. c. 95) apply in the Gold Coast, but the better opinion appears to be that they do. The Fatal Accidents Act, 1908 (8 Edw. 7, c. 7), does not apply; nor does the Law Reform (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 1934 (24 & 25 Geo. 5, c. 41).

It appears to be general practice in the Gold Coast to plead general damages especially although it is not necessary to do so: see the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 20, r. 5.]

ACTION for damages brought by the plaintiff against all four defen­dants as joint tortfeasors for personal injuries sustained as a result of their negligence. The first and second defendants (those concerned with the vehicle that collided with the one in which the plaintiff was a passenger) did not enter an appearance and interlocutory judgment was given against them. The third and fourth defendants denied negligence on their part; alternatively they alleged either contributory negligence or " volens" on the part of the plaintiff. No evidence was offered as to contributory negligence.

Prempeh for the plaintiff.

Jones for the second and third defendants.

QUASHIE-IDUN J. In this case the plaintiff has sued the four defendants for £3,250 damages, of which £250 is special damage, for personal injuries sustained by the plaintiff by the negligence of the defendants.

The first defendant is the owner of lorry No. AS 486, which was driven by the second defendant at the material time as the servant of first defendant. The third defendant is the owner of the lorry No. pg 77 AS 1499 and which at the material time was driven by the fourth defendant as the servant of the third defendant.

It is alleged by the plaintiff that on November 23, 1954, while he was travelling as a passenger on the lorry No. AS 1499 driven by the fourth defendant the lorries Nos. AS 486 and AS 1499 were so negli­gently driven by the second and fourth defendants respectively, that the two vehicles violently grazed against each other and in consequence the plaintiff was violently hit and suffered severe personal injuries including a fracture of the right arm. The plaintiff's arm has since been amputated from the shoulder.

The incident was reported to the police by the second and fourth defendants, who took a police constable Sanniez to the scene. As a result of what he saw the two drivers were charged with the offence of careless driving at the Magistrate's Court, Kumasi. They both pleaded guilty to the charge and were each convicted and sentenced to a fine of £15 or to three months' imprisonment with hard labour.

The first and second defendants have not entered appearance and, on the application by counsel for the plaintiff, interlocutory judgment has been entered against them.

The third and fourth defendants have denied that the injury or damage sustained by the plaintiff was caused by them and have con­tended that it was caused or contributed to by the negligence of the plaintiff. They allege that the plaintiff was negligent, in that he sat with his arm or other part of his body overhanging the side of the lorry. Alternatively, the third and fourth defendants contend that the injuries suffered by the plaintiff were caused by the negligence of the first and second defendants. As another alternative the third and fourth defendants have alleged in their statement of defence the following:-

" In the further alternative the plaintiff knew or ought to have known that any attempt to do anything which necessitated standing or leaving his seat whilst the lorry was in motion involved the risk of injury and the plaintiff acting as he did impliedly consented to running the said risk."

As to whether or not the plaintiff was guilty of contributory negligence, the third and fourth defendants have not attempted to establish this and I find no evidence whatsoever that the plaintiff was guilty of it. Nor do I find any evidence that would support the defence of " volenti non fit injuria."

As to the allegation that the injury suffered by the plaintiff was caused by the negligence of the first and second defendants, or either of them, I have to consider the evidence. A part from the fact that the two drivers pleaded guilty to the charge of careless driving and were convicted, there is the evidence of the constable who said that he was taken to the spot on the road and was shown the point of impact and that it was in the middle of the road. pg 78

There is evidence that the lorry driven by the second defendant was loaded with bags of cocoa which were protruding. The fourth defen­dant said he saw the lorry coming from the opposite direction and that it was travelling in the middle of the road. He said he swerved his lorry to the left side and nearly ran into the bush, but that the two vehicles nevertheless grazed against each other.

His evidence was supported by the plaintiff's wife, Addai, but in view of the evidence of the police constable that the two drivers pointed out the point of impact of the two vehicles as being in the middle of the road, I do not accept this evidence of the fourth defendant. In any case, the fourth defendant says that he saw the other vehicle loaded with protruding cocoa bags, and that the vehicle was travelling in the middle of the road. If it is a fact that he swerved to the left, I am certain that this grazing of the two lorries would not have taken place, in view of the width of the road, which was twenty feet. The suggestion of the fourth defendant is that the grazing took place on the left-hand side of the road. This cannot be true. The position then is as follows: the fourth defendant having seen the other lorry loaded with cocoa bags protruding at the sides, drove his lorry on the road in such a manner as should have been clear to any reasonable person that if the other vehicle continued to travel in the middle of the road, as alleged by the fourth defendant, there would be a collision between the two lorries. This clearly is negligence.

There is evidence that after the incident, the third and fourth defendants approached the Odikro of Atwedie and asked him to beg the plaintiff to withdraw his action against them so that they might pay all the expenses the plaintiff had incurred. The plaintiff did not appear to accede to the request and refused to have the matter settled.

It is submitted on behalf of the third and fourth defendants that at the time the request was made by them to the Odikro they were not aware of the cause of the accident.

It is my view that, although this is not conclusive evidence of negligence on the part of the third and fourth defendants, a fact that cannot be ignored, especially having regard to the admission of the fourth defendant, is that he was found guilty of careless driving. This also negatives the allegation contained in the defendant's state­ment of defence that the plaintiff was guilty of contributory negligence.

On the evidence before me, I find as a fact that the fourth defendant drove the third defendant's lorry negligently and that he cannot be exonerated from civil liability although the second defendant was also guilty of negligence.

The plaintiff has stated that he is a tailor. I accept the evidence that he is a tailor and also has some farms, but in considering the question of damages, I do not think the consideration as to whether he is a farmer or tailor should weigh very much. The question is pg 79 whether by the loss of his right arm from the shoulder he can carry on his duties efficiently, whether as a farmer or tailor. The answer definitely must be that he cannot. Apart from the incapability in which he has been placed in relation to his work, whether as a farmer or tailor, there is the great handicap which has been occasioned by the complete loss of his right arm. The plaintiff is not an old man and his chances of improving his standard of living have been considerably minimised.

 

Plaintiff claims £250 special damages. I allow him his claim of £8 for the hire of lorry, £10 medical charges, and loss of earnings at the rate of 10s. per day, amounting to £126 10s. I also allow the plaintiff an amount of £1,500 as general damages against all the defendants.

Judgment for the plaintiff· S. G. D.
