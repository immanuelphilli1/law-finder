                                                                             Smith Ag.J.

Negligence-Burden of proof-Necessity for party alleging negligence to satisfy court of its existence-Vehicle running off ferry pontoon into river.

          '"            The plaintiff shipped a vehicle across a river on the defendants' ferry,

which consisted of a pontoon propelled by a launch. The disembarkation

·          point was a ramp at the river's edge by which vehicles left (and reached) the pontoon. On this occasion the launch brought the pontoon against the ramp with a jolt and thereupon the plaintiff's lorry ran backwards through the gates of the pontoon into the river and became a total loss. The plaintiff claimed that the impact between pontoon and ramp was unnecessarily violent and that it was this impact that caused his vehicle to run off the pontoon. He claimed against the defendants for negligence.

Held, an impact such as that established by the evidence, and relied upon by the plaintiff would tend, particularly if the vehicle were insecurely braked, to jerk it forwards instead of backwards. In these circumstances, and in the absence of any other evidence from the plaintiff to show to what cause the loss should be attributed, the plaintiff had failed to establish any negligence or default on the part of the defendants.

ACTION by the plaintiff for damages for negligence on the part of the defendants resulting in the total loss by the plaintiff on June 15, 1955, of a laden vehicle while it was being ferried across a river by the defendants.

Apaloo for the plaintiff.

Stammers (Giles Hunt) for the defendants.

SMITH Ag.J. Certain facts are not in dispute. The plaintiff's lorry AD 2429 was transhipped by the defendants' ferry across the Volta from west to east. The ferry consisted of a pontoon attached to a launch which propelled the pontoon across the river. The lorry, the only vehicle being transhipped, was set in the centre of the pontoon, according to the evidence. '1'he weather was wet on June 15, 1955, the day in question, and no doubt ·the wooden surface of the contoon was slippery; it is left to the driver of any lorry to put chowks under the wheels of his lorry, if he so wants. But whether that is done to ensure that vehicles do not slip at all, or that on the dis­embarkation they do not fall back in the case of any tilting of the pontoon, is not clear. pg 218

I am prepared to find, and I do so find, that on this occasion the pontoon hit the opposite bank with a jolt, not a very serious or forceful impact. There is no evidence that any passengers, either on the pontoon or the launch were thrown off their balance nor of any damage to the pontoon in front or to the ramp on the east bank of the river. The plaintiff's lorry, however, with all its load went backwards, crashed through the gate at the back of the pontoon and disappeared into the river. It cannot be salvaged.

The plaintiff's case is that through the impact against the other side of the bank, the lorry went backwards. That is not possible. Apart from the engineer's evidence for the defendants which is referred to as the evidence of an expert but with which all the plaintiff's witnesses agreed, the vehicle should have been thrown forwards and the greater the jolt, the greater is the impetus forwards. I am not therefore relying on the evidence of "expert" witnesses-all the witnesses are agreed on the matter. One can experiment oneself and the result is always the same.

I cannot accept that it is possible to get a forward jerk, followed by a backward one in such circumstances; I cannot possibly accept that the jolt against the opposite bank caused the plaintiff's lorry to go backwards and that the jolt was the cause of the accident. There is no evidence that the pontoon was tilted up on the bank, which would have made the issue an easy one; on the contrary, the evidence in the main was that the pontoon remained on an even keel. If there had been a tilting up of the pontoon, the lorry might well have gone backwards, but it would have required a very considerable tilt for the lorry to roll off the pontoon backwards and even then the gear system and the brakes of the vehicle would have had to have been singularly ineffective.

As I have said, the jolt cannot possibly have been the cause of the plaintiff's lorry going backwards. That is the plaintiff's charge of . negligence. It is clear that on any impact the lorry would have been thrown forwards-the defendants' and plaintiffs' witnesses admit this. The plaintiff does not himself say that his lorry was given a jerk forwards, followed by a jerk backwards. The plaintiff has made a particular charge of negligence and in the circumstances in this case I do not think that any principle of res ipsa loquitur applies.

It would be pure surmise for me to express any opinion how this accident occurred. Something must have happened to cause the lorry to go over backwards. But I am satisfied that this could not happen as a result of the pontoon striking the other bank. I should have thought that the only way in which the accident could have happened was that the pontoon was tilted up on the bank but that, according to the evidence, did not happen. I have no other evidence as to the effect of the weight of the lorry on the smaller pontoon, what the balance was, or any other matter which may indicate negligence on  pg 219 the part of the defendants. I cannot imagine why the plaintiff's gear and brakes (hand-brake as well as foot-brake) could not have helped him. The position is, I think, as is set out in Charlesworth, Law of Negligence, 2nd ed., p. 23, quoting from Lord Wensleydale in Morgan v. Sim (1);-

" The party seeking to recover compensation for damage must make out that the party against whom he complains was in the wrong. The burden of proof is clearly upon him, and he must show that the loss is to be attributed to the negligence of the opposite party. If at the end he leaves the case in even scales and does not satisfy the court that it was occasioned by the negligence or default of the other party he cannot succeed."

In my opinion, on the evidence before me, the plaintiff's claim fails and there will be judgment for the defendants with costs.

Judgment for the defendants.

S. G. D.
