    Supreme Oourt of the Gold Coast, Oentral Judicial Division, Divisional Court, Cape Coast                  

                                                                  ALAGBADE v. AKYEAMPONG

                                                                           Manyo-Plange J

The plaintiff, a licensed moneylender, on two occasions advanced money to the defendant against the security of his cocoa farms, and an agreement was entered into covering each advance. By the first agree­ment the defendant promised to repay to the plaintiff the sum of £500 plus £50 by way of interest; by the second the defendant was to repay £184 plus £10 interest. It was a term of each agreement that the plaintiff should instal labourers on the farms and that he should harvest and sell the cocoa. The proceeds of the sale of cocoa were to be applied as to one-third in payment of the plaintiff's labourers and as to two­thirds in reduction of the capital and interest of the advances. These parts of the agreements had not been acted upon and the defendant had remained in occupation of the farms. The plaintiff had received £137 19s. 9d. from the defendant and sought to recover the balance of £606 Os. 3d. provided for by the agreements. The defendant contended that the two signed agreements did reflect the real transactions between himself and the plaintiff.

Held, (i) to the knowledge of the plaintiff the two agreements taken by him did not truly state the principal sums due to him, and as the agreements constituted contracts for the repayment of money lent, within the terms of section 26 (1) of the Moneylenders Ordinance, they were made void and unenforceable by the provisions of section 26 (2) of the Ordinance.

(ii) The plaintiff did not, as is required by section 12 (1) of the Moneylenders Ordinance, deliver a copy of the second agreement t{) the defendant within seven days of its being entered into, and therefore by the provisions of that subsection the second agreement was made unenforceable.

(iii) In any event, the contrary not having been proved, the interest charge was excessive and under the provisions of section 13 (1) of the Moneylenders Ordinance and section 3 (1) or the Loans Recovery Ordinance should be reduced to the level permitted by section 13 (1).

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It is probable that agreements of the type declared void in this action are not uncommon in the cocoa-producing areas of the pg 206 Gold Coast. While it is true that they show a flagrant disregard for the purposes of the Moneylenders Ordinance and give the appearance of attempt­ing to secure for the lender an exorbitant return on his capital outlay, in practice the actual interest yield is often not as great as at first sight appears. The arrangement that debtor and creditor often have in mind is that the creditor shall stand in the shoes of the debtor as regards the cocoa yield of the land until such time as the principal of the loan, plus a flat increase by way of interest, is fully discharged. It is the produce of thE! land, not the land itself, that is regarded as the security for the loan, and the repayment date given in the agreement is not regarded as the date by which full repayment must have been made. Seen in this light the arrang@. ment becomes a highly speculative one on the part of the creditor. His actual interest yield, expressed as a rate per annum, depends upon the speed .a which the cocoa produce pays off the loan. His speculation thus turns upon his estimates of the cocoa yield of a farm over a period of years, and of the market price of cocoa in the same period. Thus, a loan of £100 with apparent interest of a further £100, if repaid by three equal annual instalments, gives an interest rate of 66 per cent. per annum.

In the present case the learned judge may have overlooked the fact that the. rate of interest could not have been 100 per cent., although this particular point is not material to the judgment. Schedule" A " of the Moneylenders Ordinance prescribes the method by which the interest rate per annum is to be calculated in cases where the interest payable is not expressed as a rate.]

As to Void and Illegal Contracts, Contracts affected by Statute, see Halsbury, Simonds ed., Vol. 8, pp. 140-146, paras. 243--252.

As to the Moneylenders Ordinance, c. 176, see Laws of the Gold Coast (1951), Vol. IV, p. 181; and as to the Loans Recovery Ordinance, c. 175, see Laws of the Gold Coast (1951), Vol. IV, p. 179. Section 3 (1) of the Loans Recovery Ordinance provides that, in an action for recovery of money lent, the court may at its discretion lower the rate of interest payable if it feels such rate to be excessive. By section 13 (1) of the Moneylenders Ordinance the court is required, unless the contrary be proved, to presume excessive for the purpose of the Loans Recovery Ordinance any interest rates greater than those provided by the subsection. This presumption is raised without prejudice to the powers of the court to regard an interest rate as excessive, for the purpose of the Loans Recovery Ordinance, although it may be lower than those specified by section 13 (1) of the Moneylenders Ordinance.

ACTION by which the plaintiff moneylender sought to recover £G06 Os. 3d., the balance appearing due to him under two money­lending agreements. The plaintiff's writ was specially indorsed; the defendant entered appearance and was granted leave to defend on the defence he filed.

Alakija for the plaintiff.

Nyinah for the defendant.

MANYO-PLANGE J. The plaintiff's claim is for £606 Os. 3d. being the balance of two sums lent to the defendant with interest. The plaintiff's writ was specially indorsed. The defendant entered appear­ance and was granted leave to defend on the defence filed by him. pg 207

The plaintiff is a licensed moneylender and his claim is based on two memoranda or agreements dated respectively February 2, 1954, and April 20, 1954. The memorandum of February 2 is exhibit" A " and that of April 20 is exhibit" B." According to exhibit" A " the amount lent was £500 with £50 interest, to be repaid on November 30, 1955. By exhibit" B " the amount lent was £184 with £10 interest also to be repaid on November 30, 1955. Each loan was secured with a cocoa farm and the lender was to put his own labourers on the farm to pluck the cocoa for him. The cocoa plucked was to be valued; one-third of the value was to be paid to the labourers by the lender, and the remaining two-thirds was to be retained by the lender towards liquidation of the two loans and interest.

According to the plaintiff the value of all the cocoa he has from the two farms is £137 19s. 9d. for which he has given the defendant credit thereby reducing the two loans and interest to £606 Os. 3d., which is what he now claims. According to the defendant, exhibits" A " and .. B " do not disclose the true nature of the transactions. He says the first loan was to be £300 with £300 interest but that after begging the interest was reduced to £250, making the loan and interest £550, and that the second loan was £100 with interest to be £100 but that because of the first loan this was reduced to £94, making the loan and interest £194. He says each of the two documents was read and interpreted to him; he says that when he objected to the way the transaction was represented the plaintiff told him that that was how he made his documents, and so he had either to sign it and have the money or leave it and go without the money, and as he needed the money he had to sign. He says he was given a copy of exhibit" A " but not of exhibit " B." He says the documents were prepared by the plaintiff's clerk. The defendant says he was introduced to the plaintiff by one Kofi Adoko, a friend of the plaintiff. The defendant called Kofi Adoko as a witness; he is also a witness to exhibits" A " and" B." Kofi Adoko corroborated the defendant's version of the two transactions; he says the actual amounts lent were £300 and £100 and that the interest on each loan was at first 100 per cent. but was reduced as stated by the defendant. He also confirms the defendant's evidence that he was not given a copy of the document on the transac­tion. Both the defendant and Adoko gave their evidence in a straight­forward manner and neither was shaken in cross-examination. Although the defendant did not say so, Adoko said in the first instance he had wanted a loan of £400 but that the plaintiff could only give him £300, hence his having to go again for a further loan of £100.

The plaintiff gave evidence to the effect that exhibits " A" and ,. B " represent the true transactions between him and the defendants, that is, that the amounts lent were £500 and £184. He said of the first loan that the defendant wanted the money to purchase a lorry; of the second that the defendant said he wanted the money to redeem  pg 208 another farm which he had pledged to someone else and which he wanted to give to him, the plaintiff, because he preferred to have both his farms in the hands of one person. The plaintiff has called as his witness one Bello, his clerk, who prepared exhibits" A " and" B." This witness confirmed the plaintiff's version of the transactions as contained in the exhibits.

There are therefore two issues I have to decide in this case, and they are (a) were the amounts lent £300 and £100 or £500 and £184, and (b) whether or not the defendant was given a certificated copy of exhibit "B." Both are issues of fact. The decision depends on whether I believe the plaintiff and his clerk or the defendant and his witness Adoko.

Both the plaintiff and his witness gave me a very poor impression of themselves as persons of truth. Neither gave his evidence in a straightforward manner. They both prevaricated badly under cross-examination. The plaintiff had the brazen effrontery to state in the witness-box that he has never told a lie in his life. By both exhibits " A " and" B " the plaintiff was given the right to pluck cocoa by his own labourers and to pay himself, yet he wants me to believe that because he did not want any argument, and in fairness to the defen­dant, he allowed the defendant to pluck the cocoa by his own labourers and sell it to him. I have yet to see a moneylender who is prepared to show such generosity to his debtor. He had the documents pre­pared; if he did not intend to exercise his right why did he have it put into the document?

Again, the amount of the second loan is itself suspicious. If one needed £184 and wanted to borrow money you would expect him to raise a loan of £190, or even £200, a round figure rather than an odd amount such as £184. The plaintiff says the defendant said he wanted the money to redeem another farm, not because he was being pressed by the lender, but to pledge it to him, the plaintiff, because he, the defendant, preferred to have both his farms in the hands of one person. In other words, just to satisfy this whim the defendant wanted to incur additional interest, or increase his liability by another £10.

The plaintiff's clerk had a different story to tell about the plucking of the cocoa. According to him the plaintiff tried to exercise his right by sending his labourers to pluck the cocoa, but the defendant drove them away and threatened to shoot them if they stepped into the farm.

The plrrintiff did not know the defendant before the day he was introduced by Adoko for the loan. On each of the loans the plaintiff is by law allowed to charge up to 15 per cent. interest; in this case he fell in love at sight with the defendant, so much so that he only charged 10 per cent. interest. That, at least, is what the plaintiff expects me to believe.

These are only some of the matters which, together with the demeanour of the plaintiff and his clerk in the witness-box, make their pg 209 version of the transaction unacceptable to me. Another very significant matter which, in my view, tells strongly against the plaintiff is this­the first transaction was witnessed by four persons and the second transaction by five. Of these persons no less than four are, by their names, Yorubas, like the plaintiff himself. The plaintiff knew the defence of the defendant yet he did not see fit to call any of these persons, and he gives no explanation for his failure to call any of them. He contents himself with his hireling, the man in his pay. The ,assumption I make from his failure to call any of these persons without any satisfactory explanation is that if they were called they would not support his case.

For 'these reasons I accept the evidence of the defendant and his witness Adoko, who is admitted by the plaintiff to be his friend. Adoko himself sees nothing wrong with the plaintiff's rate of interest because he has himself obtained a loan from the plaintiff at the same rate which he has repaid, and he has introduced others besides the defendant to the plaintiff for loans.

I am, on the evidence, satisfied that on each of the two loans the plaintiff charged interest at the rate of 100 per cent., contrary to section 13 of the Moneylenders Ordinance. This section refers such loans to section 3 of the Loans Recovery Ordinance, and for the purposes of this latter section the interest charged by the plaintiff must be presumed excessive and the two transactions harsh and unconscionable.

I am also satisfied that on the second transaction the plaintiff never gave the defendant a copy of exhibit " B." That agreement is there­fore unenforceable by virtue of section 12 (1) of the Moneylenders Ordinance, and so I disallow the plaintiff's claim for £194. On the first transaction, by virtue of section 3 (1) of the Loans Recovery Ordinance, I grant the defendant relief by reducing the interest to 15 per cept. which makes loan and interest £378 15s. This amount, less £137 19s. 9d. which the plaintiff has already received, is all that the plaintiff would have been entitled to recover; but in exhibit" A," which is a contract for the repayment of a loan, the plaintiff, who is a licensed moneylender, has not truly stated the principal amount lent and the interest charged. The agreement therefore is by virtue of section 26 (2) of the Moneylenders Ordinance, void and unenforceable. The plaintiff cannot therefore recover and I accordingly give judgment for the defendant with costs. I direct that exhibits" A " and" B " and a copy of these proceedings be sent to the Senior Superintendent of Police, Cape Coast, for any action he may see fit to take.

Judgment for the defendant.

S. G. D.
