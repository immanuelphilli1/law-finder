                                                                NYARKO v. ABUNYAWAH. 

                                                                               Benson J.

Practice-Native Courts-Representation of parties-Duty of representative and his position as 7'egards giving evidence on behalf of his principal -Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99 of 1951, s. 20.

Practice-Order for a rehearing made by appellate court-Effect of failure of order to set aside judgments of courts below.

The plaintiff had commenced an action in the Native Court of Offinsu for an injunction restraining the defendant from entering upon land claimed by the plaintiff, and for damages for trespass. The case went· on appeal to the Asantehene's Court" A " and thence to the Land Court of the Ashanti Judicial Division. The latter court made an order on February 5, 1955, which required the Native Court to rehear the case but which did not at the Same time give any instructions as to the setting aside or otherwise of the judgments of the courts below. No appeal was made from this order, and in due course the Offinsu Native Court reheard the case, giving a majonty decision in favour of the defendant. The plaintiff appealed to the Asantehene's Court "A" which reversed the judgment of the court of first instance and found for the plaintiff. From this decision the defendant appealed to the Land Court. Three grounds of appeal were put forward; the first that the matter was" res judicata" as a result of an old decision of the Asantehene's Court given on March 5, 1951, concerning the ownership of the land in dispute; the second that the matter was "res judicata" as a result of an arbitration between the parties; and the third that the Native Court of first instance had improperly allowed hearsay evidence to be given by a person representing the plaintiff before that court.

Held, (i) the implied effects of the order of the Land Court of February 5, 1955, must be that the judgments of the courts below were set aside.

(ii) The duty of the court of first instance was, and is in all cases where a rehearing is ordered, to hear all the available evidence and decide any objections of law or otherwise. The court of first instance therefore misinterpreted the order of the Land Court of February 5, 1955, by limiting itself only to a consideration of evidence on the point specifically mentioned by the order.

(iii) The decision of the Asantehene's Court given on appeal on March 5, 1951, was that the judgment of the Native Court below it was a nullity for want of jurisdiction, and it set it aside for this reason. As the judgment on appeal was not given on the merits of the case it could not support a plea of " res judicata."

(iv) The Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99 of 1951, s. 20, which prohibits a legal practitioner from appearing before a Native Court but which allows a Native Court to authorize representation of a party to an action by one of a class of persons set out in that section, does not permit such an authorized person to give evidence as if he were a principal rather than a representative. An authorized representative occupies substantially the same position in relation to the party he represents as does a legal practitioner in relation to his client before a higher court. If an authorized representative gives evidence he must pg 138 conform to the rules of evidence (in so far a~ these apply to Native Courts) ; he must refrain from giving hearsay evidence, and may speak only of facts within his own knowledge, or, if he is a person recognized by the court as having special knowledge of native custom, of matters of an historical or traditional nature.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The language of section 20 of the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance is repeated in section 20 of the Native Courts (Northern Territories) Ordinance, c. 104 of 1951. The equivalent provisions for the Gold Coast Colony and for the Southern Section of Togoland are contained in the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s. 22, and in the Native Courts (Southern Section of Togoland) Ordinance, c. 106 of 1951, s. 22. These two sections use identical language but although their provisions are different in some respects from those of the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, s. 20, there does not appear to be any' difference in the matter of the powers and duties of representatives.]

ApPEAL from the decision of the Asantehene's Court" A " given on July 8, 1955, reversing the decision of February 25, 1955, given by the Offinsu Native Court in favour of the defendant. In substance the plaintiff was claiming a declaration of title, an injunction and damages for trespass.

Owusu for the plaintiff.

Prempeh for the defendant.

BENSON J. This is an appeal from the decision of the Asantehene's Court" A " which reserved the decision of the Native Court of first· instance and gave judgment for the plaintiff, now the respondent. The case had already been heard in a Native Court and gone on appeal to the Asantehene's Court and the Land Court: the last-named court made an Order dated February 5, 1955, and it is that Order that has led to further proceedings which have again reached the Land Court on appeal. At the hearing of this appeal I drew attention to the Order, which was made by Quashie-Idun J., and invited argument as to its effect, because by it the judgments of the courts below were not set aside. The relevant part of the order reads as follows :-

" I order the case to go back to the Offinsu Native Court for the court to rehear the case, take evidence on the question as to whether or not it was in respect of this same piece of land that the parties litigated right up to the Native Appeal Court resulting in the defendant obtaining judgment .... "

I note that in that Order or judgment of the Land Court there is no mention that the appeal is allowed or that the judgments of either court below are set aside. I have therefore to consider the effect of such an Order, from which there was no appeal. It is argued by counsel for the appellant that this court must assume that the Land Court, by its order, intended to set aside the judgments of the courts below and intended the case to be reheard by the court of first instance. pg 139  I have given careful consideration to this point and have come to the conclusion that the Native Court of first instance had to carry out the Order of this court, as there was no appeal from it. In other words it has to rehear the case and give judgment accordingly. I have no power to vary any Order made by this court and must consider this appeal on its merits.

Only three grounds of appeal have been put forward and argued.

The first I will consider is whether the matter is " res judicata" on account of a judgment of the Asantehene's Court" A " which was delivered on March 5, 1951. Assuming that the parties and the subject-matter were the same as in the present case, the plea of " res judicata" must fail as the Asantehene's Court did not give a judgment on the merits of the case but allowed the appeal on the ground that the proceedings in the court below it were a nullity for want of jurisdiction: that is quite a different matter from declaring the appellant to be the owner of the farm.

The next ground is again that of "res judicata," this time by virtue of a decision on arbitration between the parties on the same matter by the Krontihene and Elders of Offinsu. The court of first instance refused to entertain this plea on account of their feeling bound by the Order of the Land Court referred to earlier: they record their reasons in the record. In this they were clearly wrong because at a retrial a court must hear all available evidence and decide any objections in law or otherwise. As a result of this ruling very little was said about the arbitration and no opportunity was given to the plaintiff or his witnesses to say what they knew about it. Some evidence was given on this subject by the Krontihene for the defendant, but it was inconclusive. Neither of the courts below considered the question in their judgments: there was clearly no evidence that the matter was "res judicata" as a result of an arbitration bu~ more evidence might have been available if the court had not ruled that the matter was inadmissible.

The last ground to consider is the question of the representation of the plaintiff by Kwabena Kyei and the reception of a lot of inadmissible hearsay evidence given by him. This is a matter which is repeatedly arising in cases in the Native Courts and I will repeat what I have just written in another judgment:-

" This question of a party being represented in court is one which does not appear to be understood properly by some Native Courts. I will explain the law as I understand it. According to section 20 of the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance a Native Court may permit a husband, wife, guardian, etc., to appear for a plaintiff or defendant .. The beginning of the section lays down that no legal practitioner may appear or act for any party before a Native Court. In my opinion the proviso that a person may be allowed to appear for a party was intended by the Legislature to pg140 assist litigants who, either from age, lack of intelligence, illness, or other good cause, could not conduct their cases themselves: this is a very fair provision for courts where legal practitioners cannot appear. The section does not, however, give the right to any such representative of a party to give evidence as if he were the party whom he represents; he must still conform to the rules of evidence and refrain from giving hearsay evidence; he can speak of facts within his own knowledge as any other witness, but he cannot testify to facts imparted to him by the party he appears for: such evidence is inadmissible and if allowed to be given should be ignored. The above remarks do not, of course, apply to evidence' of a traditional or historical nature which can properly be given by persons who are supposed by native custom to be cognizant of such matters."

In the court of first instance Kwabena Kyei gave evidence which was clearly hearsay throughout, and spoke as if he were the plaintiff herself. That evidence was inadmissible from beginning to end.

It remains to be seen whether there was any other sufficient evidence for the plaintiff, against whom the judgment was given in the court of first instance, but which was reversed on the facts by the Asantehene's Court" A." If the evidence of the representative of the plaintiff is disregarded there remains virtually none for the plaintiff and certainly not enough to allow a finding in her favour, especially bearing in mind that the onus of proving her case rested on her and that there should be a preponderance of evidence on her side. In my view the plaintiff should have been non-suited. The case was unsatis­factory for two reasons, first because the Native Court wrongly refused to entertain the plea of "res judicata" by the arbitration of the Krontihene, and second because hearsay evidence was admitted. It was proper to allow Kwabena Kyei to appear for the plaintiff, but not correct to let him give evidence of matters not within his personal knowledge.

I accordingly allow this appeal and set aside the judgment of the Asantehene's Court. I vary the judgment of the court of first instance and in lieu of judgment given for the defendant I substitute a judgment that the plaintiff be non-suited with liberty to bring a fresh action. Costs to the defendant in the court of first instance as it does not appear that he was awarded any costs in that court as he should have been. The defendant is also entitled to his costs in the Asantehene's Court and to the costs of this appeal.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
