                                                                         February 14, 1956.

                       Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra

                                  KETEKU AND OTHERS V. DZOGBENUKU AND OTHERS.

                                                                               Lingley J

 

 

Native Courts-Practice-Visit of members of court to "locus in quo Procedure to be followed in reporting to court.

This case is reported for the directions given by the court to Native Courts as to the procedure to be followed when a Native Court, or members of it, visit a "locus in quo" to make an inspection. The plaintiffs' case was, in effect, for a declaration of title to land. During the course of the hearing before the Native Court members of the court made a visit of inspection to the land in dispute. On their return they were sworn as if they were witnesses, gave a report of their visit as if it were evidence and were cross-examined upon it by the parties.

Held, (i) If. a Native Court desires itself to make an inspection of a "locus in quo" the parties to the dispute must be present throughout the inspection. pg135

(ii) It is desirable, although not essential, that such an inspection should be made by the full panel of thj3 court and not merely by members of it.

[EDITqRIAL NOTE. Edusei v. Denkye (1947) W.A.C.A. Cyclostyled Reports, February 10, 1947, p. 28 (which distinguished Tawiah v. Ewudzi (1936) 3 W.A.C.A. 52), is authority for saying that it is permissible by native custom for a member, of the Native Court to join in giving judgment although he was not present at an inspection made of the" locus in quo" by the court, and that this practice is not repugnant to natural justice, equity and good conscience.]

(iii) If the panel, or members of it, make an inspection they 'must give a report on the finding to the court on resumption of the hearing. This report may be reduced to writing and attached to the record of proceedings: if this is done it should be accompanied by a rough sketch of the" locus in quo" that will show the points regarded by the viewers as being of importance.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Ampong v. Badoo (1949) W.A.C.A. Cyclostyled Reports, February 28, 1949, p. 64, is authority for saying that it is not essential for a written report of the inspection to be included in the proceedings.]       ,

(iv) The report should be delivered from the bench: it should not be given on oath nor should it be made the subject of cross-examination by the parties to the dispute.

APPEAL by the defendants from the decision of the Northern East Dain Native Court" B " given on May 9, 1955, awarding a declaration of title to the plaintiffs.

Mrs. Forster for the plaintiffs.

Akyeampong for the defendants.

LINGLEY J. The first ground on which the judgment of the court below is attacked by the appellants might be termed procedural. There was a claim and a counterclaim filed in the court below. The trial court said it "has no alternative than to enter judgment for the plaintiffs for possession and ownership of the land in dispute." It made an order: "The first defendant to retain his cocoa farm alleged to have been made by his father and not to make any new farm or cocoa farm."

- It is now argued that there should have been specific findings on both claim and counterclaim.

I think, however, that the judgment of the trial court dealt with the issues between the parties in a way that was quite clear and quite adequate.

Secondly, it is argued that the plaintiffs' evidence is purely traditional. I cannot consider it is a weakness of the plaintiffs' case that they base their case on their tradition. As argued by the pg136 respondents, there is no evidence of occupation, etc., to support the traditional history.

A point is made of the distinction between Hove and Meku land.

I do not consider the report of the viewers reflects any failure of the plaintiffs to point out the distinction between these lands.

It is also argued that the making of a cocoa farm by the first defendant's father, Dzogbenuku, in the middle of the plaintiffs' land confirms the defendants' case. I consider this point was explained by the plaintiffs and that explanation is reflected in the .order quoted above. I consider that the plaintiffs also explained Nawodu's presence on the land: an explanation it was open to the trial court to accept. Similarly, I think, the weight to be attached to the defendants' witnesses, Biakum Mensa and Ankudse, was a matter for the trial court.

There is one matter of procedure on which I think it desirable to make some comments of general application.

I notice that in this case, and others, the practice was followed of one or more members of the court viewing the land and then returning to the court house and being sworn before giving a report of the view of the land and then being cross-examined by the parties.

I consider that such a practice is undesirable and should not be followed.

If the viewer is [1 member of the court it is sufficient if he makes a report that can be attached to the proceedings in writing. It is unnecessary for the report to be given on oath and most undesirable that the parties should be allowed to cross-examine a member of the court. There should be nothing in the practice to give rise to any appearance of a conflict of evidence or testimony between a member of the court and either of the parties. Members of the court should not descend into the arena of battle in this way.

While it is not essential it is desirable that all the panel members should inspect the land; the parties, of course, being present.

One might add that the roughest kind of sketch plan is a great assistance particularly if it incorporates points regarded as important by the viewers.

In these circumstances I think the appeal should be dismissed

with costs .

.Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
