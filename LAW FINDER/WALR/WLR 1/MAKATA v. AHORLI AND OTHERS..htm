The plaintiff worked for his uncle, Havim, until the death of the latter, which occurred more than fifty years before the date of the action. Thereupon the plaintiff, at the request~ of Havim's family, paid part of the customary funeral expenses of the deceased and assumed possession of the family land which had been in the occupation of the deceased. Some thirty years later the plaintiff left the vicinity leaving the land in the charge of a caretaker. On his return, after five years, he found that the fourth defendant, Amekufe Siameh, who was a son of a brother of Havim, had dispossessed the caretaker and put his, Siameh's, three maternal nephews into possession of the land. The three nephews were the first, second and third defendants in the action.

On the first trial of the action before the Agave Native Court" B" the President of the Native Court dissented from the majority opinion of the two other members of the court and neither signed the majority judgment nor, as is required by Regulation 41 of Part 7 of the Native Courts (Colony) Procedure Regulations, No. 10 of 1945, signed the resultant order of the court.

On appeal to the Lands Division of the Supreme Court, Eastern Judicial Division, only the first and second defendants signed the notice of appeal.

Held, (i) there are no rigid rules of intestate succession in Gold Coast native custom but the elders of a family of which a deceased was a member may appoint a successor at their discretion, to stand in the shoes of the deceased with respect to the family property that had been in the possession of the deceased. The nature of the interest thus created for the successor by the elders of the family is limited, unless provision is made otherwise at the time by the elders, to an inalienable life interest. On the death of the successor the property is once again at the disposal of the family.

Observations as to the meaning of "tribe" and "family."

(ii) The effects of section 7 (1) of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 (which provides that in the event of the members of a Native Court disagreeing, the opinion of the majority shall be taken to be the judgment of the court) is such that, notwithstanding Regulation 41 of the Native Courts (Colony) Procedure Regulations, No. 10 of 1945 (which requires a President to sign any order made by a court), the pg 170 failure of a dissenting President to sign the majority order of the court will not invalidate its judgment or render its proceedings irregular.

      (iii) A notice of appeal should be signed by all parties to the appeal.

Parties who do not do so are not taken as having appealed, and the Native Courts (Colony) Procedure Regulations, 1945, Regulation 115 (1) gives no power to the court to join them to the appeal once they are out of time.

Muflat v. Kpeshie «1952) W.A.C.A. Cyclostyled Reports, December, 1951, to July, 1952, p. 38), followed.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. A distinction is drawn between family property and personally acquired property in native custom, but a presumption in favour of the former exists until it is rebutted. Family property vests in the heau of the family automatically upon the death of the possessor and remains there until a new appointment of a possessor is made by the elders of the family. This rule holds good throughout the Gold Coast but there are local customary variations as to who constitutes the family and who is its head (the family may be a patrilineal or matrilineal group or both). There are also variations in particular instances and localities as to which members of the family group constitute the family for determining questions of succession. Thus, personally acquired property which is not disposed of by testamentary disposition becomes family property on the death of its owner, but the family group for this purpose may be a smaller unit than the family group which has power of disposal over property that always has been family property.

Where a life interest is created by appointment to family property, then unless contrary provision is made at the time of appointment, an attempted alienation inter vivos by the life tenant will destroy his limited interest.]

ApPEAL by the plaintiff from a decision of Korsah J. given in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, on February 25, 1956, reversing an order of the Agave Native Court" B II given on August 21, 1953, which awarded a declaration of title to the plaintiff.

Ollennu for the plaintiff.

The defendants did not appeal and were not represented.

COUSSEY 1). The first question raised by Mr. Ollennu in presenting this appeal is the form of the judgment in the Native Court.

The President of the Native Court "B II of Agave, Eastern Province, delivered his decision on August 5, 1953, in favour of dismissing the suit. The registrar of the court appended a note, at the foot of this written decision, that" the other two panel members disagree with this decision and propose to submit their judgment on August 13, 1953. II On August 21, the Native Court, similarly con­stituted, again sat and the decision of. the two other members of the court, who are marksmen, was delivered in favour of allowing the plaintiff's claim. It was reduced into written English beforehand by pg 171 a letter writer, not in the service of the Native Court, but endorsed by the Registrar of the Native Court on the same day and entered as a record of the court.

Regulation 41 of Part 7 of the Native Courts Regulations made under section 70 (2) of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98, provides for an order on judgment to be signed by the President of the court. No order was signed by the President and Mr. Ollennu argued that as no order was so drawn up and signed, there was no judgment and the proceedings are therefore irregular. We ruled at the time, and . it is now placed on record, that by section 7 (1) of the principal

Ordinance (c. 98), the opinion of the majority members of the panel of a Native Court shall in the event of the members disagreeing, be deemed and taken to be the judgment of the court. The decision of the two panel members delivered on August 21 therefore became the judgment of the Native Court. The circumstance that the President, who gave a minority opinion, did not countersign the majority decision (he could hardly be expected to do so, but it was so suggested in argument) nor sign an order under Regulation 41 does not in our opinion detract from the validity of the judgment nor render the proceedings in the Native Court irregular.

On the appeal of the first and second defendants (the third and fourth did not sign the notice of appeal and therefore did not appeal ­ Mnffatt v. Kpeshie (1)) the learned judge of the Land Court held that if the plaintiff's case were accepted that he paid the funeral debt of his late uncle Havim, that would simply raise a debt payable to him by the family" but it could not constitute him successor of Havim's estate. In order to bolster up this story, the plaintiff added the further allegation that on payment of the sum of £7 5s. the tribe retired for

,consultation and, on their return, declared that the plaintiff having paid the money should succeed to Havim's property. This is also inconsistent with Native customary law by which only the head and members of the family of the deceased, and not the tribe, could appoint

a successor to the deceased from among the members of the family after the property had vested in the head of the family for and on behalf of the rest of the family." The learned judge accepted the I dissenting opinion of the President of the Native Court and held that the judgment of the majority of the panel was inconsistent, evasive and contrary to the Native customary law of the Agave tribe. He allowed t?e appeal and set aside the judgment of the Native Court. From this judgment of the Land Court, the plaintiff comes to this court on appeal. The defendants have not appeared.

This is a difficult case because with two principal exceptions, which will be referred to, nearly every fact alleged by one party is flatly contradicted by the other.

There is no difficulty about the Native customary law of inheritance among the Agave (Addah) people. pg 172

" In the case of family property the eldest surviving brother acts as administrator and divides the property among the children of the deceased, the eldest one receiving the greater portion. Failing children of the deceased, the eldest brother succeeds or his children, if the eldest brother has predeceased the deceased."

See p. 15, Government Report upon Oustoms relating to Tenure of Land on the Gold Ooast, 1895. But there is no codified or rigid law of inheritance anywhere in the Gold Ooast although it is spoken of as the Native customary'law. The elders, on due considera­tion where circumstances so warrant, may depart from the usual practice and often do so. "Native custom generally consists of th~ performance of the reasonable in the special circumstances of the case": per Griffith O.J. in Yerenchi v. Akuffo (2).

The two facts that emerge from the conflicting evidence recorded are (1) that over half a century ago the plaintiff did live with and serve his uncle Havim until his death, and (2) that after Havim's. death, the plaintiff was for some years actually in possession of his late uncle's property working portions of the farms and fishing places. which Havim had inherited from Boshua, the original holder.

The plaintiff, who must now be a very old man, testifies that he was requested by the tribe to pay the balance of the funeral custom expenses of his late uncle; that he paid £7 5s. which was then outstanding, and which, at that time, would represent a far greater sum of money than what it would today at current values; that he was thereupon put into possession of the three farms described in his writ of summons and that he was in possession until twenty years before the trial of this action, when he went away from the district to seek a livelihood leaving a caretaker in charge, but that when he returned after five years he found that the fourth defendant, Amekufe Siameh, had dispossessed the caretaker and placed his maternal nephews', the first, second and third defendants, in possession of the farms.

Since then the plaintiff has been consistently litigating with the defendants regarding the farms. Each side alleges that each case or settlement has been in his favour. No record has been produced by either side of those earlier cases, but the Ohief of Agave (the second witness) has testified for the plaintiff that he presided to endeavour to settle the dispute and that the plaintiff then claimed that one Siameh Adeti (who was the fourth defendant's father, and Havim's brother) was among the several elders who placed him in possession of the farms. The names of the other elders present are corroborated in a great degree by the plaintiff's fourth witness, Awuku Badjie, who also says Siameh was present, and by the plaintiff's first witness, Kuwornu Ametepey, who says it was a meeting of the family, not of the tribe, and· that it was Siameh Boshua, brother of Havim, and therefore the member principally concerned who took the decision that the plaintiff pg 173 'Should pay the £7 5s. and take possession of the farm and fishing fences until his death.

Havim had no issue. witness, Dagba Yevu, is " head troubles."

In my opinion the learned judge erred in setting aside the judgment -of the Native Court on the grounds above set out.

I have stated earlier that there is no rigid law of inheritance in Native custom. 1£ the family decided, as the evidence indicates they did, in justice to the plaintiff who had helped the deceased and ~robably worked the farms with him, that a more distant relative was more deserving and should inherit from him, there is nothing strange <or improper about that-it was within the decision of the family council, of which, on the evidence, the deceased's brother, Siameh Boshua, was a member.

With respect to the learned judge, I think he mistakenly thought that there was a meeting of the tribe, who could not take a decision for the family, but it is clear, I think, that the witnesses for the parties speak of family and tribe without distinction. In particular, it ,vill be noticed that, as to the so-called Deed of Gift made by the fourth defendant purporting to transfer farms and fishing enclosures to the first, second and third defendants, the defendants' second witness 'States " Semawhle Adzedu and myself as elders of Agudahu family witnessed to the Deed. The whole tribe confirmed the Deed in -question." Again, in cross-examination by the plaintiff, he was asked :and answered:

Q.-When Havim died, who was responsible for his burial? A.-Siameh Adete, alias Boshua, was responsible.

Q.- Was the family responsible for the burial or the tribe? A.-The tribe was responsible.

Q.-Had the late Havim no family?

A.-He had tribe and family.

The Siameh Adete alias Boshua here mentioned is the brother of Havim and has already been referred to. The fourth defendant did not himself give evidence that he had ever been appointed to inherit  Havim's properties.     ,

The Native Court, whatever is said in criticism of its judgment, held that' the plaintiff had "established his case in that he was in possession for several years undisturbed when (before) the defendants 'Set in." That is, in my view, a proper finding on the evidence. That possession is in itself the foundation of a right to possess, and the plaintiff's evidence that that right was given by the family is, in my <opinion, of more value than the bare denial of the defendants. It is unnecessary to enter into the issue as to payment of tolls to the Gameo tribe. pg 174

I would alJow the appeal and restore the judgment of the Native Court but I would qualify the declaration of title claimed by the plaintiff by the limitation that he is entitled to possession of the farms deseribed, during his lifetime only, as the plaintiff's first witness, Kuwornu Ametepey, states. Upon his death the plaintiff's sons or family will have no inherent right to inherit. It will be for the council of the family of Boshua to decide who next inherits the properties.

 

AMES Ag.J.A. I concur.

Appeal allowed.
