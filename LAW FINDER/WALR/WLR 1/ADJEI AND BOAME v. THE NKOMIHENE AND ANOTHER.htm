                                            West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session               

                                    ADJEI AND BOAME v. THE NKOMIHENE AND ANOTHER:

                                               Coussey P. Korsah C.J., Baker Ag.J.A

The plaintiffs were chiefs of villages in the Volta River Federation, and for some years had been in dispute with the State Council of the Federation as to their position vis-a-vis the chiefs on the Council and, in particular, vis-a-vis the Nkomihene. In pursuance of this dispute the plaintiffs submitted a memorandum to the Minister of Local Government setting out their views and asking for an inquiry to be held to determine their status within the Federation. Subsequently the plaintiffs were invited to a meeting of the State Council to discuss the dispute. At this meeting they produced a copy of their memorandum and it was read to the Council. The second defendant, who was the President of the State Council, and the Nkomihene, who was present, took offence at the attitude of the plaintiffs, summoned a police sergeant, and directed their arrest. The plaintiffs were detained by the police for three days and were then pg 263 brought before the local Native Court, where they were charged with insulting behaviour towards a chief, convicted, and fined £25 each. The second defendant sat as President of the court. On appeal to the magistrate the convictions were quashed.

Held, the defendants, by their conduct in instituting prosecutions for the native customary offence of offering insulting behaviour towards 11 chief and by their subsequent pursuance of the prosecutions, were acting without reasonable and probable cause and with malice.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. In the Gold Coast questions of both fact and law fall \0 be decided by the judge: in England, where the trial is before a jury, it is for the jury to decide the two questions of fact:-

(a) "Did the defendant honestly believe in the plaintiff's guilt?" or "Did the defendant honestly believe in the charges he was preferring?" See Tempest v. Snowden [1952] 1 All E.R. 1.

(b) "Was the defendant actuated by malice?"

From the answers given to (a) the judge will decide, as a matter of law, if the defendant acted without reasonable and probable cause. In the words of Hawkins J. in Hicks v. Faulkner (1881) 8 Q.E.D. 167:-

" Now I should define reasonable and probable cause to be an honest belief in the guilt of the accused based upon a full conviction, founded upon reasonable grounds, of the existence of a state of circumstances, which, assuming them to be true, would reasonably lead any ordinarily prudent and cautious man, placed in the position of the accuser, to the conclusion that the person charged was probably guilty of the crime imputed. "

In this case the defendants may well have honestly believed in the guilt of the accused: it is clear that an ordinarily prudent and cautious-and impartial­ man would not have done so.

Strong evidence of malice is afforded by: "any lack of good faith in (the defendant's) proceedings, any indication of a desire to concoct evidence or procure a conviction at any cost": Clerk and Lindsell, 11th ed., p. 870. There is clear evidence of this in the present case.

"Knowingly using disrespectful or insulting language or offering any insult whether by word or conduct to a chief" constitutes a native customary offence punishable by a Native Court.]

As to Reasonable and Probable Causes, see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., VoL 22, pp. 14-16, paras. 18-21.

CONSOLIDATED ACTIONS by the plaintiffs, each of whom sought damages of £5,078 against the defendants, jointly and severally, for malicious prosecution. The actions were heard in the Divisional Court, Kumasi, by Lingley J., who gave judgment for the defendants on May 4, 1954. Against this decision the plaintiffs appealed.

Kessi for the plaintiffs. Siriboe for the defendants.

KORSAH C.J. This is an appeal from the judgment of Lingley J. in two suits which by consent were consolidated and heard in the Divisional Court at Kumasi. The plaintiff in each suit instituted an pg 264 action claiming the sum of £5,078 damages from the two defendants for malicious prosecution. The facts briefly summarised are: the plaintiffs are chiefs and live in villages within the area of the Volta River Federation: the defendants are members of the State Council of the Volta River Federation. On October 31, 1951, the plaintiffs,' at the request of the defendants, attended a meeting of the State Council, and in the course of the meeting produced to the Council a copy of a document described: "Memorandum of the peoples of the three Towns or Villages of Odeefour, Burae and Abukonor in the Volta River Federation, Ashanti," and which was addressed to the l\linistEa' of Local Government, praying that a special inquiry be instituted to determine the status of each of them in the said declaration.

The plaintiffs contended, inter alia, in the memorandum that they were members of the Royal family of the E:rachi Paramount Stool. but that in consequence of recent readjustments of territorial boundaries their towns had been grouped together with other towns to constitute the Volta River Federation. This, they claimed, was a Federation of chiefs of equal status, entitled to elect anyone of themselves as President for a stated period, but so that such a President was not thereby created a paramount chief over them so as to entitle him to demand that the plaintiffs should swear an oath of allegiance to him as their overlord.

It is not disputed that for a period of more than six years prior to the date of the incidents on which their action was founded the plaintiffs had resisted the attempts on the part of the first defendant to assert authority over them as his subjects, and to compel them to swear oaths of allegiance to him. In consequence of these acts of the first defendant the plaintiffs requested the Government to institute an inquiry to determine their status and relationship with the first defendant and with other chiefs in the Federation. It is admitted that the plaintiffs were invited to the State Council meeting on October 31, 1951, with the object of settling this matter; but there is no evidence on record showing what transpired, apart from the facts that the plaintiffs produced a copy of their memorandum which was read at the meeting, and that the second defendant, who presided at the meeting, together with the first defendant, regarded the contents of the memorandum as insulting and disrespectful because the plaintiffs claimed in it to be the equals of the chiefs who were there assembled. for' this reason the first and second defendants handed the plaintiffs over to a police sergeant who immediately put them both in a cell, where they were kept in custody until the third day, when they were taken to a court, of which the second defendant was President, and tried. The record of the proceedings shows that the second defen­dant's linguist as well as the first defendant gave evidence for the prosecution. The offence which the plaintiffs were alleged to have committed was stated as:- pg 265

" For that you in the month of November, 1951, at Nsuanua and within the jurisdiction of this court did present a petition to the Volta River Federation Council, which amounts to a gross defamation to the rank and title of chieftaincy of the Volta River Federation and insult to the Nkomihene in particular and the Volta River Federation chiefs in general by stating that there is no paramountcy in this area, and that you rather deserve or reserve the rights to demand allegiance from the Nkomihene vide (paragraphs 10, 12, 13 and 14 of your petition refers)."

The judgment of the court, signed by the second defendant, con­victed the plaintiffs and imposed fines of £25 on each of them; but on appeal to a magistrate the convictions were quashed. It is obvious that a conviction by a court at which one of the accusers presided and also gave evidence by his representative, the linguist, is such a travesty of justice as would amount in law to no conviction by a court of competent jurisdiction. Consequently the decision of the magistrate which quashed the alleged conviction and acquitted the plaintiffs is in law the only trial by a court properly constituted which decided whether the plaintiffs were guilty or not of the offences charged.

The plaintiffs then commenced their actions, and at the trial the learned judge delivered a short judgment which reads:-

" In this country at the present time new institutions are being created: their interrelation and their relationship with existing institutions has not received much attention from the legislature and has not been worked out yet in the courts.

" One would not readily agree with the proposition that people like the plaintiffs were debarred from sending a petition to the :Minister of Local Government unless they refrained from men­tioning matters offensive to the defendants.

"Here, however, the plaintiffs adopted the common but dangerous procedure of scattering copies of their petition broadcast over the country. In these circumstances they must take the consequences that may follow.

" I find no useful reason for their presenting a copy of their petition to the State Council. I think it was done as an insulting manoeuvre in their dispute with the Chiefs. I think the latter properly regarded the terms of the petition, thrown as it were in their faces, as an insult according to customary law.

"In these circumstances I think the arrest and prosecution was lawful and bona fide: the prosecution was actuated from no improper motive that could constitute malice. I consider one must assume that the defendants were responsible for both; I cannot feel the native police had any real discretion in the matter. While it was proper for the convictions of the plaintiffs to be set aside, I do not consider the fact that the second defendant sat on pg 266 the panel gives rise to any action: he had jurisdiction and exer­cised it bona fide. Judgment for the defendants with costs."

With respect, I am unable to agree with the conclusion reached by the learned judge; it appears to me to be inconsistent with the first two paragraphs of his judgment. It is agreed that the Volta River Federation is a new institution; also that the court would not agree

to the proposition that the plaintiffs were debarred from sending a petition to the Minister of Local Government. rrhere is evidence that, apart from the copy which was produced to the chiefs at the State " Council meeting, the plaintiffs had forwarded only one other copy of the memorandum to the Government and this would, in the absence of I) malice, be a privileged communication. I therefore fail to see what evidence there is to support the judge's third paragraph that the plaintiffs" scattered copies of the petition broadcast over the country,"

or that its production could be held to be an insulting manoeuvre. In my view the best method for the plaintiffs to present their case at the State Council was not to conceal the fact that they had forwarded a memorandum which contains their case, 'but rather to produce the copy

at the first opportunity so that they would not be misunderstood. In fact, this is a common practice adopted in the Native Courts, particularly in appeals. I would venture to say, that even in the Supreme Court, if the dispute between the plaintiffs and the first defendant were an issue before the court, the production of the memo­randum in the circumstances would not be regarded as wrongful or inadmissible.

I cannot therefore agree that the arrest and prosecution was lawful and bona fide. 'l'here is the clearest possible evidence to show not only that the proceedings were instituted by the defendants, as found by the learned judge, but also that they acted without reasonable and probable cause and acted maliciously.

I would allow this appeal, set aside the judgment of the court below, and enter judgment for each of the plaintiffs with costs.

As the learned trial judge did not assess what damages the plaintiffs would have been entitled to if he had found in their favour, the action must be remitted to the court below to ascertain and award damages.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

BAKER, Ag.J.A. I concur.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
