                                                                             March 21, 1956            

                                                  Supreme Court of the Colony of the Gambia

                                               DUFFIELD v. WEBSTER.

                                                                                         Miles J.

 

 

      Defamation-Libel and slander-Practice-Allegation of libel in letter and of slander-Contents of letter not known and not shown-Details of slander nut provided-Speculative writ-B.S.C. (Gambia) 1928, Ord. 5, r. 1.

     The plaintiff sought to commence an action against the defendant for defamation and indorsed his writ to the effect that the defendant had (a) published a libel against the plaintiff in a letter written on an pg 202

  unspecified day in February, 1956, and (b) on an unspecified date or dates had slandered the plaintiff by imputing to him irresponsibility in his conduct of his trade or profession. The indorsement contained no further details of the alleged defamation. On a motion by the Attorney General for the Gambia for the striking out of the writ on· the grounds that it contained insufficient information to advise the defendant of the case he had to meet.

Held, a writ in an action for defamation must contain sufficient particulars to enable the defendant to know the case he has to meet. Where a writ for defamation fails to provide such particulars, these are appropriate circumstances for the judge, under R.S.C. (Gambia, 1928, Ord. 5, r. 1, to refuse to sign the writ . Collins v. Jones [1955] 2 W.L.R. 813 applied.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It is clear from the English decisions that in cases of defamation particular importance attaches to a clear statement on the part of the plaintiff, of the charges the defendant is called upon to answer. R.S.C., Ord. 3, r. 9, provides: "In actions for libel the indorsement on the writ shall state sufficient particulars to identify the publications in respect of which the action is brought." Moreover the courts will not go out of their way to assist a plaintiff to erect a case where he cannot demonstrate that one exists. Thus, Gatley on Libel and Slander, 4th ed., p. 460: "The court will not grant discovery to enable the plaintiff to set out the libel in his statement of claim. If he is unable to set out the very words of the libel owing to the fact that the holder of the defamatory letter or other document refuses to hand it over to him, or to give him a copy, or to reveal its contents, his only course is to draft his statement of claim as best he can and serve such holder with a subpoena duces tecum. At the trial he can apply to the judge for leave to amend if there is a variance between the words alleged in the statement of claim and the words proved." In the Gambia pleadings are ordered only in special circumstances and it is therefore necessary to have all the material particulars shown on the indorsement on the writ.]

Cases referred to:

(1) Philipps v. Philipps (1878) 4 Q.E.D. 134.

(2) Collins v. Jones [1955] 2 W.L.R. 813.

(3) Guest v. Iraq Petroleum Co. Ltd.-unreported May 7, 1954 (C.A.). (4) Harris v. Warn (1819) 4 C.P.D. 125.

APPLICATION by the Attorney-General for the Gambia for the striking out of a writ for defamation on the grounds (a) that it made claims in the alternative, and (b) that it did not state the plaintiff's claim with sufficient particularity to inform the defendant what case he had to answer.

N'Jie for the plaintiff.

Weston, Attorney-General for the Gambia, for the defendant.

MILES J. On this case coming before the court on first mention the learned Attorney-General has applied that the action be struck' out on the ground that the indorsement on the writ is bad in law.

pg 203

 
  The indorsement is in the following terms:-

    The plaintiff's claim is for damages for libel contained in a letter written in February, 1956, published by the defendant to F. Carter, a Director of Gambia Minerals Limited; alternatively, the plaintiff claims damages for slander in imputing to the plaintiff irresponsibility as an engineer and contractor of engineering works particulars of claim annexed hereto. "

     It is objected first, that claims should not be made in the alternative Ji'nd, secondly, that the writ does not state the claim with such particularity as to inform the defendant what case he has to meet.

  I do not think there is any objection to claims in the alternative, per se, although of course in certain circumstances they may be embarrassing. There is nothing to prevent a plaintiff from setting out two or more inconsistent sets of material facts and claiming relief thereunder in the alternative (see note to Ord. 20, r. 7, p. 373 of the 1956 Annual Practice and Philipps v. Philipps (1)). 1£ this were all that could be said against the present writ the objection would fail.

    In support of his second contention the Attorney-General has cited Collins v. Jones (2). This was a libel action based on a number of letters in which the defendant applied for particulars of each of the letters specifying the date, time and publication of each, identifying the person or persons to whom each was alleged to have been pub­lished and specifying which of the words complained of were alleged to have been published or contained in each of the letters. It was held that the defendant was entitled to these particulars.

   Denning L.J. said:-

        "Assuming that these letters did contain some statements defamatory of the plaintiff, that is not sufficient to ground a libel action. She must show what the actual words were. A plaintiff is not entitled to bring a libel action on a letter which he has never seen and of the contents of which he is unaware. He must in his pleading set out the words with reasonable certainty; and to do this he must have the letter before him, or at least have sufficient material from which to state the actual words in it. A suspicion that it is defamatory is not sufficient. He cannot overcome this objection by guessing at the words and putting them in his pleading. '1'he court will require him to give particulars so as to ensure that he has a proper case to put before the court and is not merely fishing for one. 1£ he cannot give the particulars, he will not be allowed to go on with the charge. That is what was done in the recent case in this yourt of Guest v. Iraq Petroleum Co. Ltd. (3) and I think we should make a similar order here. " pg 204

     In libel and slander everything may turn on the form of words, and in olden days plaintiffs constantly failed from small and even unimportant variance between the words of the libel or slander set out in the declaration and proof of them ... , In libel and slander the very words complained of are the facts on which the action is grounded. It is not the fact of the defendant having used defamatory expressions, but the fact of his having used those defamatory expressions alleged, which is the fact on which the case depends."       

      Of course it is obvious that at some stage in the present case, and) before the defendant can be called to file a defence, the plaintiff will have to supply further particulars than the writ contains. The question is, at what stage the rules of the Supreme Court in this country require him to give them? The relevant provision is rule 1 of Order 5 of the Second Schedule. This rule leaves it to the discretion of the judge to require the plaintiff to deliver such particulars as shall give the defendant reasonably sufficient information as to the details of his claim. The writ in the present case certainly does not disclose to the defendant what case he has to meet. The claim for damages for libel does not identify the document with sufficient particularity: it does not specify the date. Here it may be observed that Ord. 3, r. 9, of the English rules which require as a rule an indorsement in the most general terms, states that" in actions for libel the indorsement on the writ shall state sufficient particulars to identify the publications in respect of which the action is brought." The present writ does not, of course, contain any of the particulars which were ordered in Collins v. Jones (2). In my view a writ in an action of libel or slander should contain sufficient particulars to enable the defendant to know what case he has to meet. This particularly applies to a claim such as the present case which the learned Attorney-General, with some justifica­tion in my opinion, has described as "fishing." The plaintiff has plainly not seen the letter and has not the faintest idea what it contains. This is evidenced by the fact that he has served the defen­dant with notice to produce the letter although it is obviously not in his possession and has also subpoenaed a witness to bring the letter although the case was listed only for mention.

     So far as the claim in respect of slander is concerned, the writ specifies neither the date nor the persons to whom it was published nor the words complained of.

      It is no answer to say that pleadings are commonly ordered in cases of this kind. It does not by any means follow that they always will be. Ord. 23, r. 1, in fact provides that" suits shall ordinarily be heard in a summary manner without pleadings. " It is for this reason, no doubt, that Ord. 5, r. 1, requires rather more particularity in a writ than is ordinarily required in England.pg 205

In these circumstances it cannot be said that the writ gives the defendant reasonably sufficient information as to the details of the claim within the meaning of Ord. 5, r. 1, and the writ is struck out with costs.

Application granted.

S. G. D .
