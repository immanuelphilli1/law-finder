Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Western Judicial Division, Divisional Court,  Sekondi (Windsor-Aubrey J.), December 19, 1955.)

                                                          COMPTROLLER OF CUSTOMS AND EXCISE

                                                                                                       v.

                                                                                      AKAM AND OTHERS.

 

pg 51

Customs-Interpretation of Ordinance-Evasion of import duty-Forfeiture of vehicle used for conveyance of uncustomed goods-Customs Ordinance, c. 167 of 1951, ss. 203 and 232.

Certiorari-Mandamus-When order granted-C1Lstoms Ordinance, c. 167 of 1951-Whether refusal of magistrate to order forfeiture was an act done without jurisdiction or a refusal to exercise jurisdiction.

An action was brought before the District Magistrate, Axim, by the Comptroller of Customs and Excise in June, 1955, against four pers9ns for offences under the Customs Ordinance. The offences sprang from an attempted evasion of import duty on a large quantity of cigarettes smuggled into the country by the second defendant. The first defendant was a Customs officer who had agreed, so it was proved, not to seize the smuggled goods. Judgment was given against these two defendants under section 197 (e) of the Customs Ordinance. The fourth defendant, who with the third defendant had judgment given in his favour, was the driver of the lorry in which the smuggled goods were being conveyed at the time of their seizure. The lorry was the subject of a hire-purchase agreement between its owner, Staveley & Co. Ltd., and one Nwigwe, the employer of the fourth defendant. Neither Staveley & Co. Ltd., nor Nwigwe were concerned in the action, nor was there any suggestion that they had any knowledge of the use to which the vehicle was being put.

By section 203 of the Customs Ordinance: "all ... carriages ... together with ... things made use of in the importation, attempted importation, landing, removal, conveyance ... of any uncustomed, pro­hibited or restricted goods ... shall be forfeited ... " By section 232 : "a court ... on proof that articles are liable to forfeiture under the Customs laws may condemn t~e same." The magistrate, in view of the innocence of the parties directly concerned with the vehicle, held it would be inequitable to order forfeiture of it and he refused to do so. The Comptroller of Customs and Excise did not appeal against this refusal: instead he applied to the Divisional Court for an order of certiorari to pg 52 quash the decision of the magistrate on this point, or in the alternative for an order of mandamus to compel the magistrate to order forfeiture.

Held, (i) on reading sections 203 and 232 of the Customs Ordinance together, "may" in section 232 must be interpreted as "must," and therefore that the magistrate had no discretion to refuse to order torfeiture of the vehicle.

De Keyser v. British Railway Traffic and Electric Co. Ltd. ([1936] 1 K.B. 224) followed. This case, in which the facts were substantially the same as the present case, turned on the interpretation of the Customs Consolidation Act, 1876 (39 & 40 Vict. c. 36), ss. 202 and 226. These two sections are somewhat similar to sections 203 and 232 of the Customs Ordinance, c. 167, including the use of the discretionary word "may" in section 226.

(ii) The magistrate had not refused to exercise jurisdiction in declining to order forfeiture, and therefore mandamus was inappropriate.

(iii) Although on some occasions the prerogative orders provide an alternative procedure to appeal, which in this case would have been the appropriate remedy, certiorari would not lie because the court had neither exceeded nor abused its jurisdiction. What it had done was incidentally to misconstrue an ordinance whilst exercising its jurisdiction.

Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 9, p. 888, para. 1493, quoted with approval.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It may perhaps be doubted if mandamus was not the more appropriate remedy, since it lies to enforce the performance of a duty imposed by law. It is clear from De Keyser v. British Railway Traffic and Electric Co. Ltd. (1) that the Customs Ordinance conferred no discretion on the magistrate. If it had done, then save to ensure that the discretion itself was exercised, mandamus would not have lain. The decision would have been the same, however, if mandamus had been held to be a more appropriate remedy than certiorari, because: "The court will, as a general rule, and in the exercise' of its discretion, refuse a writ of mandamus when there is an alternative specific remedy at law which is not less convenient, beneficial or effective. Alternative remedies which thus exclude the remedy by writ of mandamus are ... appeal to quarter sessions and other appellate tribunals.

See Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 9, p. 773, para. 1309.

The Administration of Justice (Miscellaneous Provisions) Acts of 1933 and 1938 do not apply in the Gold Coast: the procedure for prerogative writs is contained in the Gold Coast Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Order 59.]

For the Customs Ordinance, c. 167, see Laws of the Gold Coast (1951), Vol. IV, p. 2.

APPLICATION for an order of certiorari or in the alternative for an order of mandamus directed to the District Magistrate, Axim, 'in respect of a judgment given by him on June 27, 1955, whereby he pg 53refused to order forfeiture, under the Customs Ordinance, c. 167 of 1951, s. 203', of a vehicle seized while conveying uncustomed goods

Christian for the applicant.

Blay for the first defendant.

Abbensetts for the second, third and fourth defendants.

Mercer for Staveley & Co. Ltd.

WINDSOR-AUBREY J. The applicant has applied in the alternative for an order of certiorari or mandamus directed to the District Magis­trate, Axim, in respect of a judgment given by him on June 27, 1955, in the case of Comptroller of Customs and Excise v. Akam and Others wherein he refused to order the forfeiture of a lorry used for the conveyance of goods that had been illegally imported.

These applications have entailed much research on my part to determine whether in the circumstances of this case the magistrate's order was wrong and, secondly, whether certiorari or mandamus lies ai all. I propose in the first case to consider the correctness or otherwise of the magistrate's decision, and I have been fortunate enough to find an English case directly in point. The lorry in question was the property of Staveley & Co. Ltd., and was hired by them to one Nicolas Nwigwe, the employer of the fourth defendant, under a hire­purchase agreement dated March 11, 1955. The magistrate acquitted the fourth defendant, thereby holding he was not implicated in the Customs offence. Needless to say, the owners Staveley & Co. Ltd. and the hirer were completely ignorant and innocent of the use to which the vehicle was being put. Section 203 of the Customs Ordi­nance provides that any carriages (a term which covers a motor vehicle) made use of in the megal importation of goods shall be forfeited. The magistrate, however, presumably relying on the dis­cretion which he not unnaturally thought was conferred on him by section 232 of the said Ordinance, the marginal note of which reads .. court may condemn articles liable to forfeiture," held it would be inequitable to forfeit the lorry. If the magistrate has a discretion no one can possibly say that he exercised it improperly, but the applicant says he has none. I find that our Customs Ordinance is based on the English Customs Law Consolidation Act, 1876. SeCtion 202 of the English Act closely resembles section 203 of our Ordinance while section 226 of the said Act is somewhat similar to our section 232, and the important words " may condemn" are common to the said sections 226 and 232.

The case which I have already mentioned, namely De Heyser v. British Railway Traffic and Electric Co. Ltd. (1) is directly in point pg 54 and seems to me to place the matter beyond dispute. In that case a motor tank wagon belonging to the defendants was seized by the Customs on the grounds that it had been made use of in connection with a customs offence. It was not in dispute that the defendants were completely innocent and ignorant of the knowledge that th~ vehicle had been illegally made use of. The defendant, relying on the words" may condemn" in section 226 of the English Act, argued that the trial court had discretion, but the superior court held that reading sections 202 and 226 of the English Act together the word " may" meant " must" and therefore the trial court had no discretion and the vehicle must be forfeited. In the light of that decision I have no doubt the magistrate was wrong in not forfeiting the lorry.

   I now come to consider whether certiorari or mandamus lies in the circumstances of this case. At the outset I would remark that in form the proceedings before the magistrate were a civil action and provision is made in the Customs Ordinance for an appeal to the Supreme Court in like manner as in all other civil actions. I am at 3: loss, therefore, to understand why the Customs Department did not proceed by way of appeal instead of invoking this highly complicated and involved prerogative procedure. On examining the authorities, I do' find, however, that in exceptional cases proceedings by way of certiorari or mandamus can be used instead of the method of appeal.

   Turning now as to whether certiorari or mandamus was the most apt. remedy, this is also a matter of considerable complexity, but I now incline to the view that certiorari was more appropriate than mandamus. Broadly speaking mandamus is used to compel an inferior court to exercise jurisdiction where it has declined to do so, and certiorari is employed to quash proceedings where the inferior court has acted without jurisdiction or in an excess of jurisdiction. I therefore think that mandamus was not appropriate, but it is still necessary to consider whether certiorari lies. In Halsbury's Laws of England, 2nd ed., Vol. 9, p. 888, para. 1493, the following passage occurs :-

" Where the proceedings are regular upon their face and the magistrates had jurisdiction, the superior court will not grant the. writ of certiorari on the ground that the court below has miscon­ceived a point of law. Where the court below has jurisdiction to decide a matter, it cannot be deemed to exceed or abuse its jurisdiction merely because it incidentally misconstrues a statute ... "

Here the court below had jurisdiction to determine the issue of forfeiture. What has happened is that it misconceived a point of law and misconstrued a statute. pg 55 In view of the above it seems to me that certiorari does not lie.

Under these circumstances, although the magistrate was wrong, I am not empowered to correct his error as no appeal has been filed and the time for appeal has elapsed. An order for either certiorari or mandamus is refused and I order the release of the lorry from the custody of the plaintiffs.

Application refused.
