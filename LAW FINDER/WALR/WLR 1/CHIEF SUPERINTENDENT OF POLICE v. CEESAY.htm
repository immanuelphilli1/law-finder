                                                                        May 21, 1956.           

                                            Supreme Court of the Colony of the Gambia              

                                   CHIEF SUPERINTENDENT OF POLICE v. CEESAY

                                                                        Miles J.

 

Criminal law-Procedure-Appeal from conviction-Fail1tre of magistrate to allow accused to present his defence-Whether or not trial a nullity.

The accused was convicted before a magistrate of stealing by a clerk or servant. The proceedings at the trial were irregular in that the magistrate, at the close of the case for the prosecution, had asked the accused if he had anything to say before sentence was passed, and had then proceeded to convict the accused without giving him opportunity to present his defence.

Held, that the irregularity, although of such gravity as must cause the conviction to be quashed, would not have been evident from the record of the trial if this were prepared as in England, and therefore, as the accused had been in jeopardy, it did not render the trial a nullity.

R. v. Neal (33 Cr.App.R. 189) applied.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The distinction drawn between England and the Gambia is that in England the formal record of the trial does not include the evidence, whereas in the Gambia it does.]

As to Appeals in Criminal Cases, A ppeals against Conviction, see Halsbury, Simonds ed., Vol. 10, pp. 535-541, paras. 985-993.

Case referred to:

(1) R. v. Neal [1946] 2 K.B. 590; 33 Cr.App.R. 189; 65 T.L.R. 557.

APPEAL from the decision of the magistrate at the Protectorate Court of the Central Division of the Gambia on May 1, 1956, convicting the accused of theft by a clerk or servant contrary to the Criminal Code, c. 24 of 1942, ss. 251 and 257.

Weston, Attorney-General for the Gambia, for the Chief Super­intendent of Police.

N'Jie for the appellant.

MILES J. This appellant was convicted by the magistrate of the Protectorate Court of the Central Division of the offence of stealing pg 274 by a clerk or servant contrary to sections 251 and 257 of the Criminal Code, and was sentenced to six months' imprisonment with hard labour. He now appeals against both conviction and sentence.

It is not necessary to go into the facts because, unfortunately, the trial magistrate made an error in procedure, the effect of which is that the conviction cannot be sustained, and the learned Attorney-General, who appeared for the Crown, has intimated that he is unable to support it.

After hearing the case for the prosecution the magistrate, according to the record, asked the appellant if he wished to say anything" before sentence was passed. this indicates that he did not give the appel­lant the opportunity of giving evidence on oath or making an unsworn statement or of calling witnesses as enjoined by section 169 (1) of the Criminal Procedure Code. The magistrate appears to have decided to convict the accused without giving him an opportunity to make a defence to the charge.

The only difficulty which now arises is as to whether this omission renders the trial a nullity or not, since if it is not a nullity no new trial can be ordered. It is contended by . N'Jie, on behalf of the appellant, that this was not a case where the magistrate acted without jurisdiction, and that the appellant was throughout in jeopardy. It is argued on the other hand by the learned Attorney-General that the magistrate had no jurisdiction to convict the accused without giving him an opportunity of putting forward a defence.

It is not every irregularity which occurs in the course of a trial that has the effect of rendering the proceedings null and void. There are three cases in which the trial has been invariably held to be a nullity. The first is where the charge is one which crn1 only be tried by a High Court, the second is where the accused has not been given the right in the appropriate case to be tried by a jury, and the third is where the plea of the accused has been wrongly entered as a plea of guilty. In all these cases it can be said that the court has acted without jurisdiction and the accused was never in jeopardy.

Neither Mr.  N'Jie nor the learned Attorney-General has been able to cite any case exactly in point so far as the facts are concerned, but in R. v. Neal (1), the Court of Criminal Appeal had occasion to consider the circumstances in which an irregularity in procedure would amount to a mistrial so as to render the proceedings a nullity. Lord Goddard C.J. said at p. 198:-

" We think a test of what would amount to a mistrial sufficient to render the trial abortive and void would be whether the irregularity would be patent on the record if it were drawn up. If, for instance, the record in Cranc's case had been drawn up, it would have appeared that there were two indictments and not a joint indictment. Both indictments would have had to be set out, and yet one verdict was returned and one judgment pg 275 delivered. So, too, if an indictment were preferred at quarter sessions which quarter sessions had no jurisdiction to try, there would be a patent defect at once apparent on the record; the prisoner would never have been in peril of a lawful sentence being passed upon him, and there would in fact have been no trial. Or again, it might appear that only eleven jurors were empanelled, for when the record was drawn up in former days the names of all the jurors sworn appeared thereon."

•• Applying this test to the present case I think it is clear that the inegularity was not such as to amount to a mistrial so as to render the trial abortive and void. The irregularity, if this were a case in

England, would not be patent on the record if it were drawn up since this does not contain a note of the evidence. Down to the end of the case for the prosecution everything was perfectly regular and in order. It is conceded by the learned Attorney-General that the appellant was in jeopardy and he therefore could establish the plea of autrefois convict.

The irregularity, although not amounting to a mistrial, is, of course, fatal to the validity of the conviction, but since the trial was not a nullity a new trial cannot be ordered. The appeal is therefore allowed, the conviction is quashed and the sentence of imprisonment set aside. The appellant is to be released forthwith.

Conviction quashed.

S. G. D.
