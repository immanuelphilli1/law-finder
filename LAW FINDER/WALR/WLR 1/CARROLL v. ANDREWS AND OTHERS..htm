                                                      Supreme Court of the Colony of the Gambia 

                                                         CARROLL v. ANDREWS AND OTHERS.

                                                                                   Miles J.

 

 

Landlord and Tenant-Monthly tenancy-Tenancy for two years at monthly rent-Holding over at monthly rent-Rebuttal of presumption of yearly tenancy-Notice to quit on basis of monthly tenancy.

Clubs-Unincorpomted members' club-Officials sued in personal capacities -Effect of R.S.C. (Gambia), Ord. 3, r. 3.

An unincorporated members' club entered into an agreement with their landlord for a tenancy for two years of certain premises at a rent of £10 a month. At the end of the two-year period they held over at the same rent, and later were given notice to quit on the basis that they held a monthly tenancy. pg 177

In an action by the landlord for recovery of the premises the three defendants were named in the writ and described respectively as secretary, president and treasurer of the club.

Held, (i) it was essential to the presumption of a yearly tenancy on the determination of a letting for a term of years that the, rent should be expressed as annual sum; if the rent was expressed in terms of periods of less than a year, such as a month (as distinguished from an annual rent payable in monthly instalments), the presumption was rebutted.

Adler v. Blackman [1952] 2 All E.R. 945 (C.A.) applied.

(ii) An unincorporated members' club is not a legal entity and an officer or officers of it cannot be sued in its name, even if its rules purport to authorize this. The proper course is for an application to the court

" for an order underR.S.C. (Gambia), Ord. 3, r. 3, authorizing selected representative defendants to be sued on behalf of themselves and all other members of the club. In this respect the requirement of R.S.C. (Gambia), Ord. 3, r. 3, is the same as the English R.S.C., Ord. 16, r. 9.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. If the interest in the premises had vested in the executive committee of the club, to be held by them on the club's behalf, the proper persons to have been sued would have been either the members of the . committee or such members representative of the committee as might be authorized by tHe court under rule 3.]

As to Presumption of a Yearly Tenancy, see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 20, p. 124, para. 137; as to Actions by and against Clubs, see Halsbury, Simonds ed., Vol. 5, p. 275, para. 648.

ACTION by which the plaintiff landlord sought possession of certain premises occupied by the Bathurst Reform Club. The premises had been let to the Freemason Lodge of St. John, Bathurst, for a period of two years from September 1, 1948, at a rent of £100 per annum payable by equal monthly instalments. The agreement to let con­tained a clause prohibiting the tenants from assigning, subletting or otherwise parting with possession of the premises without the consent of the landlord. Soon after this agreement had been entered into the Lodge, with the oral consent of the landlord, sublet part of the premises to the Bathurst Reform Club. In June, 1949, the Lodge vacated the premises entirely and they were completely taken over by the club, which thereupon entered into an oral arrangement with the landlord. This oral arrangement provided for an increased rent of £10 per month. On October 24, 1955, the landlord gave notice to the honorary secretary of the club terminating the tenancy on November 30, 1955.

The defendants, who were the secretary, president and treasurer of the club, and who were sued as such, claimed that the club occupied the premises on a yearly tenancy, so that the purported notice to quit was invalid, and that in any case they were not the proper persons to be sued. pg 178

N'Jie for the plaintiff.

Forster for the defendants.

MILES J. In this action the plaintiff claims recovery of the premises No. 77, Hagan Street, Bathurst, which are also known as the Richmond Memorial Hall. The plaintiff brings this action under a power of attorney for his father, Mr. H. D. Carroll, who is the owner of the premises and is described hereafter in this judgment as the landlord. The three defendants are respectively the secretary, president and treasurer of the Bathurst Reform Club.

By an agreement dated August 1, 1948, the premises were let by the landlord to the Freemason Lodge of St. John, Bathurst, for the period of two years from September 1, 1948. The rent under this agreement was £100 per annum payable in equal monthly instalments on the first day of each month in advance. The only other proviso to which I need refer is clause 4 (d) by which the tenants agreed" not to assign, underlet or part with the possession of the premises or any part thereof without the consent of the landlord."

Shortly after the commencement of this agreement the Freemason Lodge of St. John sublet the ground floor of the premises to the Bathurst Club under an oral agreement. The club had previously been tenants of the premises from Hr20 to 1946, when it got into financial difficulties and was evicted. In June, 1949, the Freemason Lodge, having obtained other accommodation, vacated the premises, which were then taken over by the Reform Club. The nature of the agree­ment between the landlord and the Reform Club is one of the issues in this action. The arrangement, whatever its nature, was oral. The negotiations were apparently conducted by Mr. John Hill and the late E. B. W. Carroll, who were members of the Freemason Lodge, on the one side, and certain members of the Reform Club on the other. The landlord did not himself directly take part in these negotiations.

The landlord, Mr. Carroll, gave evidence and said that the Reform Club entered into a monthly tenancy at an increased rent of £10 per month. He further said that he did not agree that the club should take over the agreement of August 1, HJ48, in other words that there was no assignment of the agreement to the club. He gave as his reason the previous trouble with the club in HJ46 which I have mentioned.

Mr. S. J. Forster, who is now the president of the Reform Club, was one of the members who negotiated this agreement, and his account of the arrangement is very definite. He said, " the club took over as soon as the Masons vacated and continued to fulfil the terms and conditions of the lease, which were subject to this one proviso that the rent of the premises would be increased at the expiration of the lease and that the rent be paid monthly, but the period was to be a like period as the lease provided." It is common ground that the pg179increased rent was £10 a month. The landlord has apparently suffered ill-health recently and his recollection did not seem to me very clear. In this conflict of evidence I think I must accept that of Mr. Forster. Mr. :Forster's evidence does receive some support from Mr. Hill, who was called by the plaintiff and who said, .. when we left the premises, as far as I remember, this particular agreement went to the Reform Club." Later on he said, .. as far as I remember I handed over the agreement to the Reform Club and said ' this is your agreement.' I left it to them to make any arrangement with Mr. Carroll. I always assumed it went on the lines of this agreement."

 

' .• Mr. N'Jie has pointed out that according to the evidence no written consent was obtained under clause 4 (d) to the assignment, but it is clear that the landlord knew perfectly well what was going on; he was on very friendly terms with Mr. Hill and the late E. B. W. Carroll and there is no doubt that he waived this particular stipulation.

I find as a fact that the agreement between the landlord and the Bathurst Reform Club was that the lease was assigned to them and that after its expiration they were granted a further tenancy of two years at the rate of £10 a month.

Towards the end of the year 1955 disputes arose over some repairs to the building and on October 24 the plaintiff gave notice to the honorary secretary of the club terminating the tepancy on November 30, 1955. The dispute in this case is as to the sufficiency of this notice. The extended term expired on August 31, 1952, and the question, therefore, arises as to the nature of the tenancy under which the Reform Club are at present occupying the premises. It is contended on behalf of the defendants that it is a tenancy from year to year.

Normally where the tenant for a fixed term under an agreement by which he pays rent by reference to a yearly rent holds over after its expiration, the inference is that a yearly tenancy has been created, and had the Reform Club simply held over after the expiration of the lease of 1948 without entering into any further agreement I would have been prepared to hold that they ~ould be yearly tenants. It is plain, however, from the evidence that the rent during the second period was not increased to £120 a year but to £10 a month. It is essential to the presumption of a yearly tenancy on the termination of a letting for a term of years that the rent should be expressed as an annual sum, see Adler v. Blackman (1), and also Ladies' Hosiery and Under­wear Ltd. v. Parker (2). In the present case, as I have said, there is no suggestion that the rent payable after September, 1950, was expressed to be £120 a year. I hold therefore that the Reform Club held the premises from and after September 1, 1952, as monthly tenants. The notice to quit is accordingly valid.

This does not, unfortunately, dispose of the case since the technical point has been raised that the defendants are not the proper persons pg 180 to be sued in this case. In this connection it is necessary to trace the history of this action. It first came before the court on December 21, 1955, when the plaintiff was not legally represented. The writ merely described the defendant as "the Secretary, Bathurst Reform Club." Ames J., before whom the matter came, raised the question as to whether the writ had been properly framed and the plaintiff was given an adjournment to obtain legal advice. The action came again b~fore the' court on January 19, 1956, when the plaintiff was represented by Mr. N'Jie, and leave was given to him to amend the writ by citing the three present defendants and describing them respectively as the secretary, president and treasurer of the Reform Club. Paragraph 8 of the defence reads: "The plaintiff is not entitled to sue the defen­dants who are not the executive of the said Club." It may be mentioned here that Rule 25 of the club rules provides that the management of the club shall be carried on by a committee of not more than four members and that in addition thereto the officers of the club shall be ex officio members of the committee. With all respect to Mr. Forster it seems to me that this rule is irrelevant. The Bathurst Reform Club is an unincorporated members' club and is therefore not a legal entity. The question arises therefore whether the action can properly be brought against the present defendants. It is quite clear that under English law an officer of an unincorporated members' club cannot be sued on behalf of the club even if the rules purport to invest him with power to that effect. Mr. N'Jie has relied upon Order 3, r. 1, of the Second Sehedule to the Rules of the Supreme Court. This rule is in the following terms:-

"If the plaintiff sues, or any defendant is sued in any representative capacity, it shall be expressed on the writ. The court may order any of the persons represented to be made parties either in lieu of, or in addition to, the existing parties."

I do not think that this rule is of any assistance to Mr. N'Jie. ~s'll it means is that the plaintiff must specify in his writ whether he cuing, or the defendant is being sued, in a personal or representative

Mr. N'Jie argues that it is for the club to take action under this Rule to have representatives appointed. It is clear that" authorized" must mean" authorized by the court" (see the note to Ord. 1o, r. 9, of the English Rules of the Supreme Court on p. 248 of the Annual Practice, 1955). This is the corresponding English rule. In my opinion Mr. N'Jie's contention is unsound. It is for the plaintiff to see that he sues the right parties and not for the defendant to put the plaintiff's tackle in order for him. I must, therefore, hold Mr. Forster's contention that the action is not properly brought against the present defendants to be sound although my ruling is based upon a "different reason from that pleaded. If the action were to go forward in its present form there would be obvious difficulties in the way of executing the judgment.

It follows that the claim must fail, but in view of the unsatisfactory nature of the pleading which -necessitated an amendment at the outset of paragraphs 1 and 2, I do not think it would be right to give the defendants the costs of the action.

There will be judgment for the defendants but no order as to costs.

 

Judgment for the defendants.

S. G. D.
