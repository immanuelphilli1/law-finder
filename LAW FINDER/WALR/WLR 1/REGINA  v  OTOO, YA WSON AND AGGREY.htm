 Appeal from the Supreme Court, Gold Coast.

Criminal Law-Extortion by a public officer-Gold Coast Criminal Code, c. 9. ss. 413 and 437-Distinction from Nigerian law relating to extortion Demand not lawfully authorized.

Criminal Law-Corruption by a public officer-Gold Coast Criminal Code, c. 9, ss. 413 and 429.

Held, (i) it is not an essential ingredient of the offence of extortion by a public officer, under the Gold Coast Criminal Code, c. 9, that a public officer who has made an unlawful demand shall have conveyed the . impression to his victim that he had, by virtue of his office, authority to. make such a demand.

Commissioner of Police v. Koji, W.A.C.A., February 25, 1952, and Commissioner of Police v. Hero Leurand Sawyerr and Others, W.A.C.A., June 5, 1953, followed.

Commissioner of Police v. Mataya, W.A.C.A., November 11, 1950, distinguished. (This case was concerned with extortion under the

     Nigerian Criminal Code, c. 42 of the Laws of Nigeria, 1948 ed.) . pg 32

(ii) Although it may assist, in offences concerning public officers, ,to distinguish extortion from corruption by equating the former with a demand, express or implied, moving from the officer, and by equating the latter with the reception of an offer, express or implied, moving towards the officer, yet the offence of corruption in respect of the duties of his office will have been committed by a public officer who directly or indirectly agrees to permit his conduct as such officer to be influenced by a gift, promise or prospect of valuable consideration whether or not the initiative in the matter has moved from him or towards him.

Conviction upheld.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by KORSAH Ag.C.J. :

The appellants were tried by a judge, sitting with assessors, and convicted on two counts:

(1) Conspiracy to extort money contrary to sections 49 (1) and 394 (now section 413), of the Criminal Code.

(2) Corruption by public officer contrary to section 394 (now section 413), of the Criminal Code.

The prosecution adduced evidence to prove that the appellants demanded a sum of money subsequently reduced to £100 from the complainant by threats that if he did not pay the said sum they would report irregularities alleged to have been committed by one Owusu for the benefit of the complainant, and thereby cause the dismissal of Owusu, and also cause criminal action to be taken against the complainant; that the sum of £100 was paid to the first appellant in the presence of second and third appellants.

The appellants denied that they demanded £100 or any other sum· of money from complainant, but said that they discovered certain irregularities which they threatened to report, and that the complain­ant offered to bribe them with a sum of money, the amount of which they did not know because they rejected the offer, consequently they did not.count the money.

The learned trial judge accepted the evidence adduced by the prosecution and convicted each of the appellants on both counts. In his judgment the learned judge said:

" I am satisfied without any doubt that the three defendants demanded money from Wiredu and that Wiredu handed £100 to the first defendant. in the presence of the other two defendants.

" I am satisfied also that they stated that if the money was not paid to them they would report the irregularities alleged to

have been committed by Owusu and cause his dismissal and that they would also take action against Wiredu. Whether the defen­dants could have achieved those objects if the money had not been paid, is in my view immaterial"

In this court two supplementary grounds have been argued, both of which raise questions of law which were not taken at the trial. They are:

" (1) The learned judge misdirected himself in holding the charge of conspiracy to extort proved, when his findings of fact do not constitute the offence of extortion as defined in section 394 (now section 413), and section 437 of cap. g.


       ,. (2) The learned judge misdirected himself in holding the offence. of corruption by a public officer proved, when his findings do not constitute the essential elements of the said offence as defined in section 3g4 (now section 413), and section 42g of cap. 9."

Counsel contends that the expression" under colour of his office •• contained in the definition of extortion, section 437, as interpreted in the judgment of the West African Court of Appeal in the case of Commissioner of Police v. G. A. Mataya, November 11, 1950, which is based on the Nigerian law relating to extortion is applicable to the relevant sections of the Gold Coast law relating to extortion, and that the appellants could not be convicted of extortion unless it was proved that it was their duty to collect or demand money. It is further contended that the true meaning of the expression is that the demand must have been made in exercise of some authority by virtue of their office, that if they owed no duty to demand payment of any kind then they could not be said to have demanded " under colour of their office. "

This court, however, has already expressed the opinion in the unreported case of Commissioner of Police v. G. M. D. Koft, on February 25, 1952, that section 404 (1) (a) of cap. 42, Nigerian Laws, on the offence of extortion, differs from our definition of extortion, section 418 (now section 437) of cap. 9, Gold Coast Laws containing the words ., not lawfully authorized" which do not appear in the Nigerian section.
