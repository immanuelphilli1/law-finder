                                                                        April 3, 1956.           

                                             West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session 

                                                     DENKYERA STATE v. ABURA STATE               

                                                 Coussey P.,Korsah J.A. Baker Ag.J.A

 

 

Native Courts-Land cause-Jurisdiction-Concurrent original jurisdiction of Land Court-Meaning of "competent" in Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 24 (4) (a)-Effect of Courts Ordinance, c. 4, s. 18, in conjunction with Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s. 58 (and with the other Native Courts Ordinances).

This action was brought, on behalf of their respective states, by one paramount chief against another for a declaration as to the boundary pg 236 between the two states; for damages for trespass to land; and for an injunction. The action was brought on for trial before the Land Court of the Supreme Court, where the preliminary point was taken by the defendants that as the parties to the action were persons of African descent, and as the dispute was a "cause or matter relating to the ownership, occupation or possession of land," then by virtue of the provisions of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, ss. 10 and 14 (1), the action was properly triable by a Native Court and therefore that by section 58 of the said Ordinance the Land Court should remit the action to the competent Native Court. The Land Court held that there was no Native Court competent to hear the action and than tit (the Land Court) could do so by virtue of the original jurisdiction conferred upon it by the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 24 (4) (a).>1 From this ruling the defendants appealed.

Held, (i) Native Courts and the areas over which they are to exercise jurisdiction are determined by order made under the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s. 3. Such courts had been duly created for the states of both the plaintiff and the defendant, but the area of their jurisdiction, in land causes, was in each case limited to their respective states. As this dispute turned upon whether certain lands were within the boundaries of one state or the other it was beyond the authority of either court to hear it, for it involved the preliminary issue of jurisdiction. The Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance conferred no authority upon Native Courts to determine their own jurisdiction: such questions, involving the interpretation of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, were matters for the Supreme Court.

 

[EDITOlNAL NOTE. The following passage from the judgment of Lord Esher N.R. in H. v. Income Tax Special Purposes  commers (1888) 21 Q.B.D. 313, at p. 319, illustrates this point:

"\Vhen an inferior court or tribunal or body, which has to exercise the power of deciding facts, is first established by Act of Parliament, the legislature has to consider what powers it will give that tribunal or body. It may in effect say that, if a certain set of facts exists and is shown to such tribunal or body before it proceeds - to do certain things, it shall have jurisdiction to do such things, but not otherwise. There it is not for them conclusively to decide whether that state of facts exists, and, if they exercise the jurisdiction without its existence, what they do may be questioned, and it will be held that they have acted without jurisdiction. But there is another state of things which may exist. The legislature may intrust the tribunal or body with a jurisdiction, which includes the juris­diction to determine whether the preliminary state of facts exists as well as the jurisdiction, on finding that it does exist, to proceed further or do something more."]

(ii) In the absence of a Native Court with authority to hear the dispute the Land Court was, by the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 24 (4) (a), the appropriate court to determine the dispute.

(iii) Even if it were established that one or other of the Native Courts had jurisdiction it was still beyond the competence of such court to hear the dispute. The Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 24 (4) (a) confers original jurisdiction in land causes upon the Land Court in all cases where no Native Court is competent to try the cause or matter. Reading this subsection in conj unction with the Native Courts (Colony) Ordin­ance, s. 58 (or with the equivalent sections in the other Native Courts pg 237 Ordinances) "competent" means not merely having jurisdiction but also the ability to adjudicate without bias or interest. On the principle "nemo judex causa sua" and bearing in mind that justice must not only be done but should manifestly and undoubtedly be seen to be done, it would be irregular for a Native Court of a state to adjudicate in a matter which might involve its members in a conflict between their regard for the due and proper administration of justice and their loyalty to the state or stool to which they belonged. Such a matter would not be " properly cognizable" by the Native Court, as is required by the Courts Ordinance, s. 18.

'[EDITORIAL NOTE. This principle is probably not limited to issues which

.involve the land of a state (or stool) and which are therefore regulated by the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 24 (4) (a). By section 18 of the same Ordinance the Supreme Court has no jurisdiction in "any civil cause or matter which the court considers to be properly cognizable by.a Native Court in accordance with the provisions of section 58 of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance" (or with the equivalent sections in the other Courts Ordinances). It would appear that any cause or matter in which such conflict of loyalties might be involved would be not "properly cognizable" by the Native Court concerned and would therefore, unless some other Native Court had jurisdiction under section 14 (2) or (3) of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, be triable by the Supreme Court.]

As to Control of Inferior Jurisdiction, Collateral facts and the juris­diction of inferior tribunals, see Halsbury, Simonds ed., Vol. 11, pp. 59-60, para. 116. As to Control of Inferior Jurisdiction, Bias by Interest, see Halsbury, Simonds ed., Vol. 11, pp. 67-70, para. 123.

APXPEAL from the decision of Acolatse J., given in the Land Court at Cape Coast on January 20, 1955, dismissing the claim by the defendant, in a suit concerning state boundaries, that the suit was not properly before the Land Court and should be remitted by the Land Court to a Native Court with competent jurisdiction.

Awoonor-Williams for the plaintiffs. Hayfron-Benjamin for the defendants.

COUSSEY P. In this case the plaintiffs, a sub-chief and the Paramount Chief 01' Omanhene of Denkyera State, instituted an action in the Land Court of the Central Judicial Division at Cape Coast against the Paramount Chief 01' Omanhene of Abura State averring what they described to be the ancient boundary between the plaintiffs' stool and the defendant's stool, that the defendant's subjects, agents and licensees had trespassed over such boundary; and claiming a declaration that the boundary was as alleged by the plaintiffs and damages for trespass on the plaintiffs' land called Ajentonghu land and an injunction to restrain further trespass by the defendant over the said boundary and on the plaintiffs' land.

By the statement of defence delivered, the defendant described a different boundary as the correct boundary between the contending pg 238 stools and denied the trespass alleged and the plaintiffs' right to the injunction claimed.

When the action came before the Land Court, counsel for the defendant raised the question that as the parties were persons of African descent and the suit a land cause as defined by section 2 of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, c. 98, by virtue of the operation of sections 10 and 14 of that Ordinance the suit should be tried and determined by a Native Court having jurisdiction over the area in which the land in dispute is situated. It was submitted that the» action should have been instituted in the Native Court of the Denkyi:Jra State, being a competent Native Court, and that having been» erroneously brought in the Land Court, the latter court, in obedience to the mandatory direction of section 58 of the Ordinance, should stop the further progress of the suit in the Land Court and refer the parties to a competent Native Court.

The learned judge of the Land Court ruled that formerly under the Native Administration Ordinance, c. 76, the only Native tribunal having jurisdiction to hear a suit of this nature between two states would be the Provincial Council, but that such jurisdiction was now vested in the Land Court under section 24 (4) of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4. The learned judge came to the conclusion that there is no Native Court competent to hear and determine the suit and that the Land Court has exclusive original jurisdiction over the suit by virtue of section 24 (4) of c. 4, and he ordered that the hearing do proceed in the Land Court.

From this ruling the plaintiffs appeal to this court. In support of their appeal, in addition to the arguments advanced in the court below, it is submitted that the learned judge wrongly invoked subsection (c) (i) of section 24 (4) of the Courts Ordinance in support of his ruling as the suit was commenced in the year 1951 and therefore was not pending in a Provincial Council under the Native Administration Ordinance as that subsection provides when the Land Court was established in the year 1944.

If this were clearly the ratio decidendi of the ruling appealed from, we might have to consider this submission, but as stated above, the learned judge concluded as follows:

" I hold therefore that ... there is no Native Court competent to try this cause or matter and that the Land Court has exclusive original jurisdiction to hear and determine the matter."

This part of the ruling appealed from is clearly based on section 24 (4) (a) of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4, which says:-

" ... a Land Court shall have exclusive original jurisdiction to hear and determine any cause or matter relating to the ownership, possession or occupation of land-

(a) where there is no Native Court competent to try the cause or matter: " pg 239

It is clear that this IS a land cause as the title to and ownership of the land between the rival boundaries set up by the parties is in dispute. A suit in which a decree is asked for operating directly on the land is a land suit.

It is submitted for the plaintiffs that they were right in instituting the action in the Land Court as a court of first instance because where two states each with Native Courts established under the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance are contesting title to land, each claiming such land to be within the territorial limits of its state, the Native Courts of ~ach state are not competent to determine the issue and, further, -that the onus is on the party who asserts the fact to show that a competent Native Court exists so as to oust the jurisdiction of the Land Court.

In my opinion the submissions of the plaintiffs are sufficient to sustain the direction of the court below that the hearing of the suit should proceed in the Land Court. Section 24 of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4, confers on the Supreme Court and therefore on the Land Court, which is a superior court, a jurisdiction concurrent with that of a Native Court over suits in which a Native Court has jurisdiction.

Section 24 (4) (a) of c. 4 gives the Land Court exclusive original jurisdiction in land causes where there is no Native Court competent to try the cause or matter. Ordinarily, competency means jurisdiction, but in the context of section 24 (4) (a) in my opinion it means also the ability to adjudicate without that bias or interest that would vitiate a judgment pronounced. That this is so becomes clear when section 18 (a) of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4, is considered. That section provides:

" that the Supreme Court shall not exercise jurisdiction:

(a) in the Colony in any civil cause or matter which the court considers to be properly cognizable by a Native Court in accordance with the provisions of section 58 of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance."

Section 58 of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance referred to in subsection 18 (a) is to this effect:

" Whenever it shall appear to any court (meaning the Supreme Court) that any civil cause or matter brought before it is one properly cognizable by a Native Court, the court shall stop the further progress of such civil cause or matter before it and refer the parties to a competent Native Court."

It is to be observed that this 'obligation to stop the proceedings arises when it appears to the court in its discretion that the cause or matter is properly cognizable by a Native Court.

I am unable to hold that a land cause in which the members of a Native Court, being subjects of the stool, have necessarily, by reason of local conditions, the same interest as the stool and state in which the Native Court is established, is properly cognizable by that Native pg 240 Court. That interest, in my opinion, is sufficient to create a real bias such as to render undesirable the institution of the suit in the Native Court having jurisdiction over the area.

The principle nemo debet esse judex in propria causa is one of general application apart from legislative enactment, and justice would not be served by referring the parties, in the circumstances of this case, to a Native Court nor in insisting, as the appellants invite us to hold, that the suit be instituted in the first instance in a Native Court having jurisdiction, only to put one of the parties to the expense of appl~in5 to the Land Court for an order of tmnsfer to the court on the ground of interest.

It will further be observed that under section 3 of the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, 1944, now section 3 of c. 08, the Governor-in­Council may by order provide for the constitution of Native Courts which shall exercise jurisdiction ... within such area as may be defined in the order. When this action was instituted orders had been made by the Governor-in-Council providing for the constitution of Native Courts in the Denkyera and Abura States whose jurisdiction was confined within the territorial limits of each of the said states. Obviously the courts in neither state could exercise jurisdiction over land outside its territorial boundaries. But as each party, the plaintiff state and the defendant state, contends that the area in dispute is within its territory, there is a conflict as to jurisdiction, neither party being willing naturally to yield exclusive jurisdiction over the land in dispute to the courts of the opposing state. In these circumstances it is not surprising that the learned judge did not consider the cause to be properly cognizable by a Native Court ..

To put the proposition in another way, if after this action had been instituted in the Land Court, the defendant had instituted an action in a Native Court having concurrent jurisdiction, for the same cause of action, the same subject-matter and the same relief, one of the parallel suits would be stayed. I venture to suggest that it would be the action in the Native Court on the principle that the court could not adjudicate in its own cause.

The defendant may have been able to show that a competent Native Court exists in one of the contending states, but he has failed to discharge the onus which rests upon him to show that an impartial Native Court 01' one having exclusive jurisdiction exists in either state before which the dispute would be properly cognizable. The ruling appealed from rested in the discretion of the learned judge, and this court will not interfere with it.

I would therefore dismiss this appeal. The hearing of the suit will proceed in the Land Court, Cape Coast, in accordance with the ruling appealed from. pg 241

KORSAH J.A. I concur.

BAKER Ag.J.A. I concur.
