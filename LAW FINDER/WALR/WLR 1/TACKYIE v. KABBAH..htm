                                                                        March 29, 1956           

             Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Divi­sional Court, Accra                  

                                                                   TACKYIE v. KABBAH..

                                                                             Smith Ag.J.

 

 

Defamation-Libel-Qualified privilege-Statements made in discharge of a public or private duty - When privilege exceeded - Express malice defeating privilege.

The plaintiff had performed the duties of assistant to his uncle who, until his death, held office as an acting head chief. On the death of the uncle the plaintiff continued to' discharge certain official duties as a Regent, and drew an allowance from the local authority in this capacity. In due course it became apparent that the people of the plaintiff's pg 214 division were not unanimous on the question of his holding office as Regent, and thereupon the local authority ceased paying the allowance, although it did not reclaim what it had already paid out. In addition to discharging duties as a Regent the plaintiff, for a time, held office as the Chairman of the Finance Committee of the local Native Authority. During the currency of this office the defendant wrote several letters to the police in which he alleged the plaintiff to be fraudulently diverting Native Authority money to his own use. The police satisfied themselves that these charges were without foundation and so informed the defen­dant. Later the defendant wrote another letter and sent it to the local government agent with copies to the Minister of Local Government, th~) Chief Regional Officer at Ho, and to the local native court. In it he renewed his charge that the plaintiff had defrauded the Native Authority) and in addition implied that the drawing of a Regent's allowance had also been fraudulent. In an action for libel the defendant attempted to set up the defence of qualified privilege.

Held, (i) the fact that the defendant persisted in uttering defamatory remarks about the plaintiff after the police inquiries, of which the defen­dant was made aware, had established that the remarks were without foundation, was sufficient to establish express malice on the part of the defendant, thereby defeating the privilege.

(ii) In any event the extent of publicity given to the defamatory matter went beyond what would have been appropriate in the circum­stances, thus exceeding any privilege that might have existed.

As to Defence to Libel and Slander, Qualified Privilege, see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 20, pp. 468-486, paras. 569-592.

Case referred to:

(1) Adam v. Ward [1917] A.C. 309; (1917) 117 L.T. 34; (1917) 33 T.L.R. 277.

ACTION for damages for defamation by publishing a libel on the plaintiff in letters. The defence was one of qualified privilege in that the defendant sent the letters in pursuance of a public duty.

Lassey for the plaintiff.

Larbi for the defendant.

SMITH Ag.J. Santrokofi Division is made up of three villages­namely, Benua, Abume and Gbodome. There has been no paramount chief since 1927. One Tete Kalai was acting chief of Gbodome and until his death on May 7, 1954, he was the acting head chief for Santrokofi Division. His nephew (the plaintiff) acted as assistant to Tete Kalai, in view of the latter's extreme infirmity, until his death and afterwards he carried on in the capacity described as " Regent." He has never been officially recognized by the Government as Regent or in any official capacity, although if the Government had any matters with Santrokofi the Government Agent said he got in touch with the plaintiff. The plaintiff had never at any time claimed to be a Mankrado.

Between 1951-1953 the plaintiff was Chairman of the Finance Committee of the Ayonkodo Native Authority. He had nothing to do pg 215 with the handling of cash: vouchers and such documents he signed at the end of each month after they had been prepared by the Treasurer.

After Tete KaIai's death the plaintiff drew" Regent's" allowances from Biakoye Nkonya Local Council for some months. These allow­ances were stopped by the Government Agent after a letter from one Otitiaku of Abume dated September 1, 1954, was received stating that the plaintiff had not been appointed Regent and it was ascertained that

.• the Division was not unanimous in wanting him as Regent. If there had been no opposition, allowances would have continued to be paid •• to him. Allowances so paid to him were not claimed back by the Government.

The defamatory letter complained of is a letter set out in the particulars of the plaintiff's claim and dated September 20, 1954. It was addressed to the Government Agent, J asikan, and published to the following parties:-

1.    Chief Regional Officer, Ro.

2.    Minister of Local Government.

3. Secretary, Biakoye Local Council Law Court, Mempeasem.

I should have thought that the purport of this letter of September 20, 1954, was as regards the acceptance of Regent's allowances by the plaintiff when he was not established as Regent by the unanimous vote of the Santrokofi Division. The defendant, however, himself insists that what he was referring to was not only these allowances but claims for labourers' allowances which the defendant says the plaintiff, when Chairman of the Ayonkodo Native Authority Council had inserted fraudulently in payment vouchers for fictitious labourers and had appropriated the money to himself.

I am unable to hold that the defendant's particulars of justification as set out in his statement of defence have been established. The defendant was referring in his letter of June 20, 1954, to matters which had taken place in 1951 and 1952. On his own admission the defen­dant wrote several letters to the police, complaining that the plaintiff was making false payment vouchers in his capacity as Chairman of Ayonkodo Native Authority, showing payments to non-existing labourers and that he was stealing such moneys. The police made sufficiently exhaustive investigations but nothing was proved against the plaintiff, and the police closed the investigation and marked their file" Found False." The defendant was then notified.

So far as the allegations concern the drawing of allowances as Regent after the death of Tete Kalai, I again point out that the plaintiff was his chief assistant during the latter part of his life, and , he considered he was entitled to 'carry on as a sort of Regent. In a letter to the Government Agent, Jasikan, from the office of the Para­mount Stool Father, the Government was informed that the plaintiff pg  216 had been elected Regent of Santrokofi Division pending the appoint­ment of a Paramount Chief. It was not until September 24, 1954, that the Government Agent ordered the stoppage of payment of the allowances to the plaintiff in view of the fact that a number of letters had been received, some confirming the plaintiff's appointment as Regent and some opposing. I do not consider that there is any justification for calling him a thief so far as the drawing of these Regent's allowances are concerned. He was treated as a Regent.

The remaining point is whether there was qualified privilege: in Gatley on Libel and Slander, 4th ed., p. 198, there is the following passage :-

" The principle upon which the law of qualified privileges rests is this: that where words are published which are both false and defamatory the law presumes malice on the part of the person who publishes them. The publication may, however, take place under circumstances which create a qualified privilege. If so, the pre­sumption of malice is rebutted by the privilege, and ... the plaintiff has to prove express malice on the part of the person responsible for the publication. The effect of proving express malice is sometimes spoken of as defeating the privilege .... "

See also Adam v. Ward (1), at p. 334, where Lord Atkinson said:-

"It was not disputed in this case on either side, that a privileged occasion is, in reference to qualified privilege-and that is all we are concerned with-an occasion where the person who makes a communication has an interest or a duty, legal, social or moral, to make it to the person to whom it is made, and the person to whom it is so made has a corresponding interest or duty to receive it. 'rhis reciprocity is essential."

Modern cases show that the common interest or duty must exist in the case of all parties if the occasion is to be privileged, and the privilege may be exceeded quite apart from the question of malice. I think the dispatch of so many copies of the defamatory letter of September 20, 1954, to others apart from the addressee exceeds the privilege.

As regards the question of malice if I am wrong in my views on privilege the defendant made a series of persistent attacks against the plaintiff in 1952-1953. There were several police investigations-the police docket was formally closed and the defendant was notified that his complaint was found to be false. Yet he persisted, and I think there is in all these circumstances, in addition to publishing the letter of September 20, sufficient evidence from which malice can be inferred.

I give judgment for the plaintiff and .assess the damages at £200 with costs.

Judgment for the plaintiff.

S. G. D.
