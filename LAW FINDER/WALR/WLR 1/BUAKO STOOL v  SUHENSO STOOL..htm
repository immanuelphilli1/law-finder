                                 January 25, 1956

                          Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Western Judicial Division, Land Court, Sekondi

                                                                        Windsor-Aubrey J.

                                                                          BUAKO STOOL

                                                                                    v   

                                                                    SUHENSO STOOL.                                                                                 

                                  

 

Certiorari-Excess of jurisdiction-Judicial powers of State Council-State Councils (Colony and Southern Togoland) Ordinance, c. 8.of 1952, s. 5­Court to which application for order should be made-Procedure 1lnder Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules), 1954, Ord. 59, r. 2 (2).

The applicant is the chief for the time being occupying the Stool of Buako: the opposer is the Chief for the time being occupying the Stool of Suhenso. A dispute between the two stools over the boundaries of certain of their stool lands was carried by the opposer to the State Council of Sefwi Wiawso; the State Council, notwithstanding the arguments of the applicant that they had no jurisdiction, purported to make an order giving a declaration of title in favour of the opposer. The applicant sought leave to apply for an order of certiorari directed to the State Council to quash their purported order on the grounds that their juris­diction was limited by the State Councils (Colony and Southern Togo­land) Ordinance to matters of a constitutional nature (as these are defined in the Ordinance) arising within the area of their authority, and that a dispute over the boundaries of stool lands, not being a matter of a constitutional nature, was beyond their jurisdiction. It was not disputed on behalf of the opposer that the State Council had no jurisdiction to hear the dispute, or to make the order it did: the opposer claimed that the application should have been made to the Divisional Court rather than to the Land Court, and that it should be rejected in any case as failing to comply with the requirement of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 59, r. 2 (2), that each application shall clearly set out the grounds on which relief is sought. pg 80

Held, (i) the Sefwi Wiawso State Council had acted without juris­diction and therefore certiorari properly lay to quash their proceedings.

(ii) The original dispute between the two stools was a "matter relating to the ownership, possession or occupation of land "-see the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 24 (l)-and therefore the application was properly brought before the Land Court.

(iii) Although the ex parte motion was defective in form in that it did not clearly set out the ground's on which relief was sought, the accompanying affidavit did so fully, and this was sufficient to cure the defect as the motion and affidavit go together to make up the application.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The powers of the State Councils are contained in the State Councils (Colony and Southern Togoland) Ordinances, c. 8 of 1952, c. 29 of 1953, and c. 37 of 1955; the State Councils (Ashanti) Ordinances, c. 4 of 1952, c. 41 of 1952, c. 2 of 1953, and c. 38 of 1955; and the State Councils (Northern Territories) Ordinance, c. 5 of 1952. So far as their judicial activities are concerned their powers are substantially similar under each Ordinance. The extent of their judicial activity is to "inquire into and determine any matter of a constitutional nature, arising within the area of authority of such State Council, in which the parties are members of or subject to the authority of such State Council under the provisions of this Ordinance or according to customary law." This provision is contained in c. 8 of 1952; s. 5 (1) ; c. 4 of 1952, s. 7 (1) ; and c. 5 of 1952, s. 5 (1). What comprises a matter of a constitutional nature is set out in section 2 of each principal Ordinance. Appeals from decisions of State Councils on matters over which they have jurisdiction lie not to the courts but to the Governor (see c. 8 of 1952, s. 11; and c. 5 of 1952, s. 10), or to the Asanteman Council and thence to the . Governor (see c. 4 of 1952, ss. 13-15). The common law powers of the Supreme Court, however, are unimpaired by the State Councils Ordinances and it would thus appear that although a matter cannot reach the Supreme Court by way of appeal in the ordinary way the Supreme Court will exercise jurisdiction over the State Councils in the following circumstances: -

(i) Where a State Council has acted in excess of, or without juris­diction (as in the present case), by the use of certiorari and prohibition or by a declaration. This control by the Supreme Court will only exist if a State Council is acting or purporting to act in a judicial capacity (it being open to the Supreme Court to decide when a State Council is so acting), and then only if the Supreme Court does not decide that a State Council judicially constituted is not an "inferior court." As to the latter point it seems unlikely that the Supreme Court would look upon a State Council as being of equal status with itself. The power of the Supreme Court to decide if an act is done without or in excess of jurisdiction clearly includes the power to determine, by interpretation of the Ordinances, whether or not an issue before a State Council comes within the Ordinances' definition of a "matter of a constitutional nature": it does not lie within the competence of a State Council to interpret the ordinance which gives it its powers.

(ii) Where a State Council has a duty to perform, whether this duty is of a judicial, administrative, legislative or quasi-judicial nature, or where it has a discretion to exercise, to compel the performance of such duty or the exercise of such discretion. An example would be that of compelling a State Council to discharge its duty under c. 8 of 1952, s. 7, -;-the duty to report any decision of a constitutional nature to the pg 81 Governor within one month of reaching it. This exercise of jurisdiction by the Supreme Court will be by the use of mandamus or by a declaration.

It may be that the point as to the court from which the order of certiorari should issue was not a valid one. The issue before the court appears to have been not whether the original dispute was a matter relating to the ownership, etc., of land, but whether or not the State Council was acting in excess of its jurisdiction. At first sight it would appear that this makes the issue of an order of certiorari a matter for the Divisional Court. In fact, as the Land Court is a Division of the Supreme Court (see the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 24 (1», and as all the judges of the Supreme Court have equal power, authority and jurisdiction (see the Courts Ordinance, s. 7 (1) and (2», it would appear that such issues can be decided in either court. This view is reinforced by the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954. The Procedure for Prerogative Writs is contained in Order 59, which in Rule 2 (2) requires the application to be made ex parte to "the court." By Order 1 "court" is defined as including the Supreme Court and the Chief Justice and puisne judges of the Supreme Court sitting together or separately.]

For the State Councils (Colony and Southern Togoland) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, see Laws of the Gold Coast, Supplement 1952-1954, Vol. 1, p. 267. For the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Order 59, see Civil Procedure Rules, 1954, p. 399.

APPLICATION for leave to move for certiorari to quash a decision of the State Council of Sefwi Wiawso on April 29, 1955, making a declaration of title to certain lands in favour of the Stool of Suhenso. The applicant is the chief for the time being occupying the Stool of Buako; the opposer is the chief for the time being occupying the Stool of Suhenso.

WINDSOR-AUBREY J. This is an application for a writ of certiorari to bring the judgment of the State Council of Sefwi Wiawso before this court for an order quashing the proceedings. Counsel for the opposer admits that the State Council had no jurisdiction to hear the proceedings or to make the order it did. He, however, contests the application on the grounds that the ex parte motion on which the proceedings for certiorari was commenced is defective in form. Counsel further argues that the application should have been made in the Divisional Court and not in the Land Court.

Dealing with the second point first, Order 59 is silent as to the court in which such an application should be brought, and as the judgment clearly affected the ownership, possession or occupation of land it was in my opinion, rightly brought in this court.

As to the alleged defect in procedure I agree that the ex parte motion read by itself is defective in form but it is accompanied by an affidavit which sets out fully the grounds for the application, and paragraph 10 correctly states the relief sought. In my opinion the motion and the affidavit should be read together as both form the pg 82 application. I am satisfied that the defect is not fatal. I am fortified in my view by the fact that where the parties are Africans undue regard should not be had to technicalities

.

Application granted.

S. G. D.
