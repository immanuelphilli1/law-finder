                                    CHIEF SUPERINTENDENT OF POLICE v. N'JIE AND GAYE

                                                                        Miles J.

 

  pg 267

Criminal law-Demanding money by threats-Reasonable and probable ca1lse for demand-Criminal Code, c. 24 of 1942, s. 275.

This prosecution arose from a letter addressed by the second appellant, apparently at the instigation of the first appellant, to the chief clerk in the office of the Public Trustee of the Gambia. the first appellant had received from the Public Trustee a sum of £130 to be spent on certain purposes and for which he was obliged to account. It appeared that he had lost or misapplied some of this money and the letter sent to the chief clerk was an attempt to covel' this loss. The letter implied that after the first appellant had received the £130, the chief clerk had asked for and obtained from him £80 by way of " commission." The letter, on threat of exposure, demanded the £80 from the chief clerk, or a receipt for this sum. When the case was tried by the magistrate the first appellant stood by the contents of the letter: the second appellant claimed that he had merely written what he was told by the first appellant. The magistrate found that the appellants had jointly uttered the letter with intent to extort £80 and that they had done so without reasonable or probable cause. On appeal from their convictions the first appellant argued that the magistrate misdirected himself in law in saying the test of reasonable and probable cause was whether or not the chief clerk had taken the money. The second appellant argued from the same grounds, claiming that in his case the test was the presence or absence of honest belief that the chief clerk had taken the money.

Held, (i) the test in cases of demanding money by threats is not " is there reasonable and probable cause for alleging a set of facts or a state of affairs," but" is there reasonable and probable cause for making the demand for money," for although the facts may be as alleged they may still not give rise to a justifiable demand for money. But in cases, such as the present, where the existence of the alleged facts would give rise to a justifiable claim, the test is satisfied by determining if there is reasonable and probable cause for making the allegation. In the case of the first appellant, since he had no reasonable and probable cause for alleging the facts, it followed that he could have no reasonable and probable cause for demanding the money.

(ii) A genuine belief in the existence of facts constituting reasonable and probable cause is a sufficient defence even though it may be ilJ­founded. Although there could, on the facts, have been no such belief on the part of the first appellant there could not have been on the part of the second appellant, and as this had not been explored at the trial the second appellant was entitled to an acquittal.

Thorne v. Motor Trade Association ([1937] A.C. 808) followed.

As to Taking Property, Extortion by Threats, see Halsbury, Simonds ed., Vo1. 10, pp. 797-801, paras. 1543-1549.

'APPEAL from the decision of the Acting Colonial Magistrate on May 2, 1956, convicting the appellants of demanding money by written' threats contrary to the Criminal Code, c. 24 of 1942, s. 275.

Weston, Attorney-General, for the Chief Superintendent of Police. Forster for the first appellant.

Mahoney for the second appellant.

MILES J. The two appellants, Prince N'Jie and :Momadou Samba Gaye, were convicted by the Acting Colonial Magistrate of demanding~ f money by written threats contrary to section 275 of the Criminal Code, [) and sentenced to two years' imprisonment with hard labour. They now appeal against conviction and sentence.

The two appellants are cousins. The first appellant is the nephew of Bourika N'Jie, an old lady and a widow. She had a son, who died two or three years ago. On his death Mrs. N'Jie became entitled to a pension of 75 dollars per month and also to the sum of £2,000 paid under a life insurance policy. By an order of the Supreme Court made at Bathurst on October 6, 1955, the £2,000 life insurance money was paid to the Public Trustee, in view of the age and infirmity of Mrs. :N'Jie. On January 17, 1956, an order was made by the judge in chambers on the application of the Public 'rrustee for the disposal of some of the money. The Public Trustee had applied for the sum of £200 for expenditure on charitable purposes. there were present at the application, Mr. Bridges (the Public 'rrustee), the first appel­lant, Mrs. N'Jie, Musa Jobe, who is chief clerk in the Public Trustee's Office, and Landing Sanyang, the court interpreter. After some dis­cussion, the amount allowed was reduced to £130. The judge ordered that the amount was to be accounted for by Mrs. N'Jie with the assistance of the first appellant. On the following day, January 18, a cheque was made out by the Postmaster-General for £130 payable to the Public Trustee. this was endorsed to the first appellant, who cashed the cheque and gave a receipt for the £130.

The case for the prosecution was that Musa Jobe early in February received a letter from the second appellant, dated February 10, 1956, in the following terms:-

Dear Sir,

I have been informed by my cousin, Mr. Prince N'Jie, that the sum of £80 (Eighty pounds) was paid to you as what may be called "Curator's Commission" without any receipt issued. This is an indication of great ignorance on his part.

" As he and his mother intend to consult a solicitor, I would, in my capacity as a relative, and in their own interest, request that the sum or receipt of the amount be sent forthwith .

.. I am much inclined to believe that if the £80 (eighty pounds) were paid to you, which is surely the case, then the procedure pg  269 adopted would be reprehensible to Government system of mone­tary transaction and in fact a charge most unprecendented in British Estate Administration. Furthermore, the money entrusted to the being Public Trustee of which you may be one, must be well protected against misappropriation etc.

" If the amount is denied to have been paid to you, I shall be obliged to 'make an inquiry through your departmental head and will proceed with any step that the law may provide .

" I hope you will avoid any unpleasant event by yielding to my request.

Yours sincerely,

M. S. Gaye."

It is this letter which forms the subject of the charge. On receipt of the letter, Musa Jobe sent for the second appellant and denied taking the money. According to the evidence of M usa J obe the second :appellant was satisfied with this explanation. Jobe, however, wished to see the Public Trustee. The second appellant objected to this and wished to tear up the letter, but Jobe insisted on a written with­drawal of the allegation. Jobe and the second appellant then went to the office of Maurel Freres, where the duplicate of the letter was produced. J obe tore off from the duplication of this letter a piece of . paper giving his explanation of the matter to which the appellant added a note which he signed. The appellant thereupon tore up both the original and duplicate of the letter but later, on Jobe's request, handed the pieces to him and they were starched together. Jobe then reported the inatter to Mr. Bridges, who sent for the first appellant. On March 1 the first appellant came to M1'. Bridges's office. On arrival he said that he wished to consult the second appellant before saying anything. Both appellants later had an interview with Mr. Bridges. After some conversation the second appellant asked for time to think the matter over. On March 5 the second appellant returned and said that after consideration he stood by what he had said in the letter.

Both appellants gave evidence at the trial. The first appellant, who is a carpenter employed by the Public Works Department, said that on cashing the cheque for £130 he returned to Jobe's office, on 30be's instructions, whereupon Jobe extracted £80, explaining that this was due as Curator's Commission, and promised to let him have a receipt for the amount later. As no receipt was forthcoming, the appellant said that he instructed the second appellant to write the letter in question, demanding the receipt or the return of the £80. In other words, at the trial he stood by the allegation made in the letter. pg 270 the defence of the second appellant was that he wrote the letter on the instructions of the first appellant, and that he merely wrote what he was told.

The magistrate rejected both these defences. He found that the two appellants jointly uttered the letter and that they intended thereby to extort £80, or a valuable thing, namely a receipt with threats of detriment to Musa Jobe, and that there was no reasonable or probable cause for their action. He consequently convicted both appellants.

rfhe first ground of appeal argued by Mr. Forster on behalf of th~ first appellant was that the letter in question contained no threats of any injury or detriment of any kind to be caused to Musa Jobe. The letter contains an allegation that J obe had misappropriated the sum of £80 and the writer threatens to report the matter to the head of his deparment in the event of Jobe denying payment. The letter concludes "I hope you will avoid any unpleasant event by yielding to my request." This phrase is characteristic of letters demanding money by threats. There is no substance in this contention.

Mr. Forster's next point is that the magistrate has misdirected himself in his judgment when he said: ., to justify the letter it is required that there should be reasonable or probable cause for making the demand contained in it. The reasonable or probable cause in this case would be present if Jobe had taken the money. It is a fact for decision therefore whether he took the money."

I do not think that there is much difficulty as to the law which is applicable to such a case as this. The words, ., without reasonable or probable cause" apply to the money demanded and not to the accusation, the truth or falsity of which may be immaterial. To take an example, if A writes to B accusing B of having committed an unnatural offence against him and demanding a sum of money as a condition of A refraining from reporting the matter, it is immaterial whether the allegation is true or false since even if it were true A would not be entitled to demand money. There may, however, be cases, of which the present is one, where the justification for the demand of money does depend upon the truth of the allegation. If J obe had in fact taken the £80, as alleged, the first appellant would have been justified in demanding the money or a receipt, and there would have been reasonable or probable cause for the demand. The magistrate therefore did not misdirect himself in law in stating that the fact for decision was whether J obe took the money, at any rate so far as the first appellant was concerned.

The passage in the judgment is perhaps a little unfortunate in its wording since it might appear at first sight that the magistrate was placing the onus on the defence to justify the letter. Looking at the judgment as a whole, however, it is clear that the magistrate realized that the onus of proof of absence of reasonable cause rested upon the prosecution, and he states, after dealing with the evidence: "I am satisfied that Jobe did not receive the money or any sum from Prince N 'Jie and that this allegation is false."

Mr. Forster further contends that the finding is unreasonable and cannot be supported having regard to the evidence. The question whether Jobe took the money was a pure question of fact. The magistrate analysed the evidence very fully and gave reasons for his finding in his judgment which it is unnecessary to repeat and which are unassailable. There was ample evidence to support his finding .

 It would, indeed, have been surprising if, upon this evidence, the magistrate had come to any other conclusion.

Mr. Forster finally contends that the charge is defective in that the particulars do not contain the words "without reasonable or probable cause." It is, of course, true that the absence of reasonable or probable cause is an essential ingredient in the offence, but the charge has been framed in accordance with the precedent contained in the second schedule to the Criminal Procedure Code, which also omits the words" without reasonable or probable cause." No objection was taken to the charge by :Mr. Forster at any time during the trial. The point was only mentioned by Mr. Mahoney in his final speech on behalf of the second appellant. Even if the charge were technically deficient it could not possibly be said that any failure of justice has occurred. It was clearly appreciated by counsel on both sides that the main issue in this case was as to the absence of reasonable or probable cause.

The appeal of the first appellant against conviction fails and is dismissed. He appeals also against the sentence. The appellant is stated to be twenty-six years of age and of good character, and a sentence of two years' imprisonment with hard labour is undoubtedly severe. This, however, was a calculated and cold-blooded attempt to ruin a senior civil servant. There was no suggestion of any previous animosity between J obe and the first appellant and the object of making this demand was to cover up a loss, to use a neutral term which mayor may not be appropriate, of £80 for which the appellant was under an obligation to account. The sentence in the circum­stances was richly deserved and there are no grounds upon which I would be justified in interfering with it. The appeal against sentence is accordingly dismissed.

Mr. Mahoney's main argument on behalf of the second appellant is based upon the passage quoted above from the judgment of the trial magistrate, and in particular the last sentence, in which the magistrate states that the question for decision is whether Jobe took the money. Mr. Mahoney argues that the magistrate was wrong in law in applying the same test to both appellants since the second, unlike the first appellant, was not present at the time when Jobe was alleged to have taken the money. Mr. Mahoney contends that the question which pg 272 the magistrate should have considered was whether the second appel­lant honestly believed that this allegation was true. He cites in support the observations of Lord Atkin in Thome v. Motor Trade Association (1), in which he says:-

" I only wish to add a word as to the case of Rex v. Dymond referred to in the argument which may have a bearing on cases to be tried under this section. In that case the prisoner was charged under this subsection. Her defence was that she had been indecently assaulted by the prosecutor and that she thought; she had a right to demand money from him in compensation. The learned judge at assizes, Darling J., had refused to allow evidence to be called either as to the fact or as to her belief. The argument for the Crown was that an indecent assault differed from a common assault, and that even if the accused woman had been assaulted it would not justify a claim for compensation and that her belief that it would was irrelevant. The court agreed with this contention: and I express no opinion upon it. But language was used in the judgment which seemed to indicate that even if the mistake were as to a fact which would have constituted a reasonable cause such a mistake would be irrelevant; in other words there must be in fact a cause, not merely a genuine belief in a cause. 'l'his seems to me incautiously expressed: and I do not think that doubt should exist upon a well established proposi­tion in criminal law that normally a genuine belief in the existence of facts as apart from law, which if they existed would constitute a defence, is itself a sufficient defence. "

The learned Attorney-General, in reply, argued that it was never suggested by the second appellant at the trial that he honestly believed Jobe took the money, but that his defence throughout was that he acted as a mere letter-writer. It is true that the appellant did not say in express terms in evidence that he believed that Jobe took the money, but I think it was implicit in his defence. In the letter itself he says: " I am much inclined to believe that the £80 (eighty pounds) were paid to you, which is surely the case." Furthermore, l\1 r. Mahoney cited the case of Thorne v. Motor 'bade Association (1). to the magistrate in his closing speech at the trial, so that the point was raised.

I am of opinion that Mr. Mahoney's argument is sound. The magistrate, having found, and there was ample evidence to support his finding, that the second appellant was no mere letter-writer, should have gone on to consider the question whether he honestly believed his allegation to be true. In his judgment, however, the magistrate has said in effect: "I find (a) that Gaye was not a mere letter-writer, (b) that Jobe did not in fact take the money, therefore there was no reasonable or probable cause." This, in the light of Lord Atkin's    pg 273 remarks, must be held to be a misdirection in law. The appeal of the second appellant is allowed. The conviction is quashed and .the sentence of imprisonment set aside.

 

Appeal of first accused dismissed.

Appeal of second accusedallowed.
