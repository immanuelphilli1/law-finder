Mortgage-Mortgagee's power of sale-Whether power properly exercised if sale by private t1'eaty where this is not authorized by the mortgage deed.

In August, 1951, a mortgagor conveyed by way of mortgage his full interest in certain property to one Kwesi Krah, a licensed moneylender. The mortgage deed was drawn in' English form and conferred an unrestricted power of sale upon the mortgagee. In July, 1953, the mortgagor purported to convey the property, on sale, to the first defen­dant. In November, 1953, Kwesi Krah exercised his power of sale and the property was purchased by the plaintiff. There was dispute between the parties as to the actual day on which this sale took place and as to whether it was by public auction or by private treaty. On the question 'whether a mortgagee exercising a power of sale is required to sell by public auction where no specific power to sell by private treaty is given,

Held, a mortgagee selling under a power of sale conferred by a mortgage deed which contains no provisions as to the method of sale may sell either by public auction or by private treaty.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. There appears to be no decided case directly on this point, although Davey v. Dumnt (1857) 1 De G. & J. 335 is authority for pg 59 saying that where the mortgage deed permits either method of sale, a sale by private treaty even if done without advertisement, is good, provided it is bona fides and for a fair price. There was, however, little doubt but that a private treaty sale is permitted, for although a mortgagee is a trustee for the holder of the equity of redemption he is not a trustee of the power of sale, which is given to him for his own benefit, and if he exercises it bona fide without corruption or collusion with the purchaser, the court will not interfere even though the sale be very disadvantageous, unless indeed the price is so low as to be evidence of fraud in itself: see Nash v. Eads (1880) 25 S.J. 95, and see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 23, p. 436, para. 642: "A mortgagee is entitled to sell upon such conditions as he thinks suitable for securing a sale of the property" ; and see also Coote on Mortgages, 9th ed., p.928.

It is probable though that the point is answered more directly by the Trustees and Mortgagees Act, 1860 (23 & 24 Vict. c. 145) (Lord Cranworth'g Act). This appears to be an act of "general application" and it therefore applies in the Gold Coast, for the relevant parts in it were not repealed in England until 1881, by the Conveyancing Act of that year. By section 11, Lord Cranworth's Act provides that after one year from the date of the mortgage, or after interest upon it has been in arrear for six months, a mortgagee: "shall have the following powers to the same extent (but no more) as if they had been in terms conferred by the person creating the charge, namely, a power to sell or concur with any other person in selling the whole or any part of the property by public auction or private treaty, subject to any reasonable conditions as he may think fit to make ... " By section 13 a mortgagee proposing to exercise this power of sale is required to give six months' notice in writing to the mortgagor, but: "when a sale has been effected in professed exercise of the powers hereby conferred, the title of the purchaser shall not be liable to be impeached on the ground that no such notice as aforesaid had been given; but any person damnified by any such unauthorized exercise of such power shall have his remedy in damages lIgainst the person selling."

This part of Lord Cranworth's Act came in for sharp criticism before it was repealed, because it is not clear from the wording of section 11 what the quantum of the estate is that the mortgagee can convey on sale; thus, does "selling the whole or any part of the property" enable a mortgagee to Qverreach a,ny interest retained in some other person? This point could have arisen in this case for although the mortgage deed of August, 1951, spoke of the mortgagor's interest as being a fee simple unencumbered he was in fact paying a' 'ground rent, and his actual position appeared to be that of a sub-lessee.

The difference should be noted' between the effect of a sale by a mortgagee' selling under a power contained in an English deed and a sale by a pledgee under a customary pledge. In the former case the sale extinguishes the equity of redemption remaining in the mortgagor; in the latter case the pledgor's right to redeem may continue.]

As for Mode of Exercise of Power of Sale, see Halsbury, Hailsham ed., Vol. 23, pp. 435-439, paras. 640-647.

ACTION by which the plaintiff sought as against the first defendant a declaration of title to a plot of land at l)restea; as against both defendants an account for mesne profits, and as against the second defendants an order for possession. The facts appear in the judgment. pg 60

Asafu-Adjaye for the plaintiff.

Brandford-Mensah for the first defendant.

Blay for the second defendants.

 

WINDSOR-AUBREY J. In this action the plaintiff claims the following relief:

•. Wherefore the plaintiff claims :-

(a) A Declaration by the Court that upon the Interpretation of the Deed of Mortgage and Deed of Conveyance K wesi Krah was the proper person to sell and convey the Compound House situate at Prestea and that the Legal estate in the said Compound House has rightly passed to the plaintiff.

(b) An Order of the Court setting aside the alleged sale of this very Compound House by the said William Minow Grant des Bordes to the first defendant.

(c) An Order of ejectment against the second defendants.

(d) The plaintiff further claims from the defendants the sum of One hundred and twenty-nine pounds ten shillings (£129 10s.) being rent in arrear for the period of 15th November, 1953, to 31st May, 1955, inclusive at the rate of Seven pounds (£7) per month.

(e) The plaintiff also claims from the defendants mesne profits for the use of the said premises from 1st June, 1955, to the date of Judgment at the rate of Seven pounds (£7) at the rental of which the said premises was let. Annual rental is Eighty-four pounds (£84)."

So far as the second defendant is concerned he is the tenant of the premises in dispute, and is largely a nominal defendant, being willing to pay his rent to the plaintiff or the first defendant, whichever is found to be the owner.

The facts are simple and not seriously in dispute except as to whether the plaintiff purchased the property by public auction or by private treaty. This type of case is regrettably not uncommon in this court. The position being that the original owner has fraudu­lently sold it to the first defendant when it was heavily mortgaged and the issue is whether the plaintiff or the first defendant is to suffer from this fraud. I find the following facts proved. The original owner, William Minow de Bordes, mortgaged the property to K wesi Krah as security for a loan of £500 by a deed of mortgage dated August 17, 1951. Kwesi Krah exercised his power of sale, and on November 11, 1953, the plaintiff purchased the property which the mortgagee conveyed to him by a conveyance dated February 4, 1955. In the interim, William de Bordes sold the property to the first defendant on July 27, 1953, for the sum of £330. This sale, therefore, took place before the mortgagee's sale to the plaintiff, but after the mortgage to Kwesi Krah. The mortgagee arranged to sell the property pg 61 by public auction through an auctioneer, Mr. J an tuah, and the sale was fixed to take place on November 4, 1953. The first defendant, an illiterate, hearing of the proposed sale, arranged for his brother, a literate, to attend the sale and protest and stop it. The brother did attend the sale and undoubtedly protested and, according to him, by his protest stopped the auction. If he did stop the auction sale, which the plaintiff denies, the plaintiff must have bought the property subsequently by private treaty.

Under the mortgage deed, there was no restriction on the mortgagee as to the mode by which he exercised his power of sale; merely a provision that if he did sell by public auction he had the power to buy in and resell. It seems to be settled law that any mortgagee can sell by private treaty or by any means he thinks fit, though, of course, if his method of sale is so hole-in-the-corner as not to result in a fair price, then the mortgagor may have a claim against him, but that does not affect the validity of the sale as between the mortgagee and the purchaser for value. Consequently, therefore, for the purposes of this decision it is not strictly necessary to decide whether or not there was a public auction but I propose, nevertheless, to make a finding on this point, in case there should be any appeal from this judgment.

The plaintiff's evidence about the auction sale was far from convincing and he was evasive and contradictory about the bidding at the auction. It is true there is documentary evidence in support, namely the Certificate of Purchase and the receipt, both prepared by the auctioneer, but he has not been called as a witness. There is also considerable conflict in the evidence as to the date on which the auction was held and the notice advertising the sale has not been produced. Other documentary evidence suggests that the auction was fixed for November 4 and the first defendant's brother, who was unshaken in the. witness-box, gives that as the date when he stopped the auction sale. On the other hand, the documentary evidence to which I have already referred, states that the auction was held on November 11, 1955. The whole evidence for the plaintiff about the auction is unsatis­factory and I accept the evidence of the first defendant's brother that he successfully stopped the sale on November 4, and I find that the plaintiff bought the property subsequently on November 11 and in the absence of any evidence as to a second auction I find that the plaintiff bought the property by private treaty.

Turning now to the law, the first defendant's counsel argues that at the date of the sale to the plaintiff, the first defendant had already purchased the property, and, in effect, purchased the equity of redemp­tion. That may be so, but the sale by the mortgage was a valid sale and that sale extinguished the equity of redemption. I hold, therefore, that the first defendant has no title to the property in dispute and make a declaration of title that the plaintiff is the owner of the property. The sale to the first defendant is declared to be null and pg 62 void. As regards the second defendants they have always been ready and willing to pay the rent, and as far as I can gather, have been and possibly still are paying their monthly rent of £7 to the first defendant. The second defendants are entitled to remain in possession, and now become statutory tenants of the plaintiff. I order that the second defendants do pay all arrears of rent not already paid to the first defendant to the plaintiff and all future rent to the plaintiff. I order that the first defendant do pay to the plaintiff as mesne profits the sum of £7 per month as from November 13, 1953, up to the date on which the second defendants have ceased to pay rent to the said first defendant.

 

Judgment for the plaintiff.

S. G. D.
