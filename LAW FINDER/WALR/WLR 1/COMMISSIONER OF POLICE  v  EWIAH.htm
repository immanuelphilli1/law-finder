                                  January 11, 1956.

                Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Western  Judicial Division, Divisional Court, Sekondi

                                                                              Windsor-Aubrey J.

                                                               COMMISSIONER OF POLICE 

                                                                                           v   

                                                                                   EWIAH                                                                                  

                                  

 

Family Provision - Duty of father under Ordinance to provide for his children-Responsibility of mother for children under native custom­Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, ss. 72 and 73--Detention (Fit Person and Supervision) Regulations, No.1 of 1947, as amended by No. 23 of 1947.

The Courts Ordinance, by s. 72 (1) (c), permits any police officer to bring before a court any juvenile whom he has reasonable grounds for believing has a parent or guardian who does not exercise proper guardianship p. By section 72 (2) (b) of the same Ordinance, the court may make an order committing the juvenile to the care of any fit person, whether a relative or not, who is willing to take care of him. By section 73 the Governor in Council is empowered to make regulations for the purpose of giving effect to the provisions of the Ordinance concerning juveniles; those regulations that have been made are contained in the Detention (Fit Person and Supervision) Regulations, No. 1 of 1947, as amended by No. 23 of 1947. Regulation 9 (1) permits the court, at its discretion, to make an order against the parent or guardian requiring payment of a sum not exceeding £3 a month in respect of each child concerned. Any sum so ordered is paid through the court to the duly appointed fit person for the maintenance of the child. Regulation 9 (3) forbids the making of any order for payment under Regulation 9 (1) until the parent or guardian has had a chance to appear and show cause why an order should not be made.

Early in 1955 the two children of one Alex Ewiah were brought before the Juvenile Court at Sekondi as having a parent or guardian who did not exercise proper guardianship. Some time before the Juvenile Court made its order the children were removed by their mother from their father's house to their aunt, one Amda Techiwah, and it appeared from the report of the Probation Officer that this was done because of the father's failure to observe his parental responsibilities. In due course the Juvenile Court made a "fit person" order under the Courts pg 70  Ordinance, s. 72 (2) (b), in favour of Amda Techiwah, and a payment order against Alex Ewiah, under Regulation 9 (1), of £2 a month in respect of each child. Against this payment order Ewiah appealed. It was argued on his behalf that by native custom the custody of the children of a marriage belongs to the mother, not the father, and if the mother chooses to remove them from the father his responsibility for them thereupon ceases. It was further argued that in any case the order of the Juvenile Court was ill-founded because at the time it was made satisfactory arrangements had already been entered into for the welfare of the children.

Held, the obligation placed on a parent or guardian is absolute and is not to be displaced by the voluntary or charitable acts of other persons.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Although by native custom a father is required to maintain his child (see Sarbah, Fanti Customary Laws, 2nd ed., p. 39), it remained the case that the custody of a child and the responsibility for its welfare were obligations primarily upon the mother. It would appear that as the expression" parent or guardian" can clearly refer to both father and mother, the effect of sections 72 and 73 of the Courts Ordinance is to increase the measure of responsibility borne by the father, and thereby, to a certain extent to relieve the burden on the mother.] For the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, see Laws of Gold Coast (1951), Vol 1, p. 25. For the Detention (Fit Person and Supervision) Regulations, Nos. 1 and 23 of 1947, see Laws of the Gold Coast--1947 Legislation, pp. 217 and £65 respectively.

APPEAL from an order of the Juvenile Court at Sekondi made on March 10, 1955, and affirmed on a motion for variation on June 30, 1955, by which the father of two juveniles for whom a " fit person" order had been made under the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 72 (2) (b), was required to pay £2 a month in respect of each juvenile, by .way of maintenance.

Blay for the appellant.

Alfiwumi, Jnr., for the Commissioner of Police.

WINDSOR-AUBREY J. This is an appeal from the decision of the Juvenile Court at Sekondi in respect of an order requiring Alex Ewiah to contribute £2 a month in respect of each of his two children, Gift and Alex Ewiah. The appellant apparently admits he is the father of the two children. The broad outlines of the appeal are that at the time the proceedings were instituted the two children were already in the care 6f Madam Techiwah, their aunt, and that as the court made a " fit person" order placing the two children in her care the whole proceedings were ill founded, as the children were being properly looked after prior to and at the time the proceedings took place. It is alleged, therefore, that no grounds existed for the institution of proceedings under section 72 (1) (c) of the Courts Ordinance or for orders under sections 72 (2) (b) and 73 (4) of the said Ordinance and the regulations made under the last-mentioned section. Jurisdiction, pg 71 however, arises under section 72 (1) (c) wherever a parent or guardian does not exercise proper guardianship. In a careful, well-considered judgment the Acting Chairman of the Juvenile Court exhaustively considered the arguments of counsel for the appellant and, in my opinion, correctly came to the conclusion that the charitable acts of the aunt did not oust the court's jurisdiction or in any way detract from the responsibility of the parent. As the Chairman remarked, it is manifestly contrary to the spirit of the legislation that a parent can relieve himself of his parental responsibilities because a relative is good-natured enough to shoulder them. The mere fact that such an event has occurred is, in itself, evidence that the parent has not exercised proper guardianship.

I have really nothing to add to the judgment of the court below, and would only say I am in entire agreement with it.

 

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
