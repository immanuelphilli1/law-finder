Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Ashanti Judicial Division, Land . Court, Kumasi

                                          KOJO v. ANANE AND ANOTHER.

                                                                 Benson J.

 

 

 Native law and custom-Succession to stool property-Inventory of stool property.

Native law and custom-Gifts made by customary procedure-Necessity for publicity where immovable property concerned.

Evidence-Practice in Native Courts-Status of witnesses-Need to establish status for record-Insufficiency of taking judicial notice of status.

For some fourteen years a farm had been managed by the first defendant who had been installed as a caretaker and who paid the proceeds of the farm to the occupant of the local stool. The stool occupant in turn paid the proceeds, or part of them, to the plaintiff. A year or so prior to the date of the action the stool occupant had relinquished· the stool and his successor, to whom the caretaker then paid the money, refused to pay this over to the plaintiff. The plaintiff claimed that he had inherited the farm from one Kwame Nuro deceased and that he had arranged for the installation of a caretaker to tend it during his absence. The first stool occupant was a member of the family of which the plaintiff was the head and it was for this reason that the farm proceeds had been paid through him. The succeeding stool occupant, the second defendant in the action, claimed that the farm was stool property, it having been given to the stool during the occupancy of his predecessor by the late Kwame Nuro out of gratitude for the kindness shown to him by the then stool occupant in meeting his medical expenses. The second defendant also claimed that the caretaker had been installed by the preceding stool occupant who in this respect was acting in his own right and not merely on behalf of the plaintiff. The plaintiff denied that the farm had been a gift to the stool and claimed that the second defendant ought, in support ,of his case, to have produced an inventory of the stool property made at the time of his enstoolment. pg 132

Held, (i) it was not an invariable custom that a written inventory of stool property should be taken on an enstoolment and no assumption could ,be drawn from the fact that an inventory of stool properties was not tendered as evidence.

(ii) One of the essential elements of a valid gift of immovable property in native custom is publicity: in the case of gifts to a stool this publicity should be established by evidence from the elders of the stool.

Sarbah, Fanti Customary Laws, 1st ed., pp. 69 and 70, quoted with approval.

(iii) A Native Court must ensure that the identification of witnesses 'and the capacity in which they give evidence are treated as matters for the record and not as matters of which judicial notice may be taken.

APPEAL from the decision of the Asantehene's Court" A " given on September 5, 1955, reversing the decision of July 5, 1955, given by the Offinsu Native Court in favour of the defendants. The plaintiff claimed a declaration of title and mesne profits.

OW1l8U for the plaintiff. Prernpeh for the defendants.

BEXSON J. This 'case originated in the Native Court of Offinsu, which found in favour of the defendants, now the appellants. The judgment of the court of first instance was reversed by a unanimous decision of the Asantehene's Court " A." The case for the plaintiff is that the farm in dispute belongs to 'him by right of succession from one Kwame Nuro deceased. The case for the defendants is that the farm was given by the late Kwame Nuro in his lifetime to the Ksenia Stool. The second defendant is the present Nseniahene: the first defendant occupies the farm by his authority, as caretaker.

The court of first instance held that there was a valid gift according to native custom to the Nsenia Stool. The Asantehene's Court on appeal reversed this finding apparently for two reasons: that the weight of eviden~e was in favour of the plaintiff and that the evidence of the s~cond defendant should have included the production of the inventory of stool properties handed over to the stool at the time he was enstooled. A point which does not seem to have engaged the attention of the Asantehene's Court is the necessity, or otherwise, of calling persons as witnesses, such as Elders of the Stool, to testify to the gift of the farm. Much has been made of this point by counsel for the plaintiff and it deserves full consideration; before doing so I will dispose of the question of the inventory.

There is nothing on the record to show whether or not a written inventory was drawn up: from my experience of such matters in other cases it would seem that they are not always written, especially in the pg 133 case of small stools. I know of no customary law which makes it obligatory to produce an inventory in evidence if a dispute arises as to ownership; it seems to' me that that is a matter to be considered by the trial court when deciding on the sufficiency of the evidence. I do not consider that the absence of the evidence of the inventory would alone be enough to justify an appeal court in reversing the findings of a court of first instance.

I will now deal with the question of witnesses or evidence required to prove' a valid gift according to native custom. From the record 9f the proceedings there is nothing to show that any Elders of the Nsenia Stool gave evidence to prove the gift, but I see in the judgment of the court of first instance it is stated that the second defendant called two witnesses who are stool occupants and Elders of the Stool of Nsenia. The two witnesses referred to are presumably Yaw Agyei and Yaw, Abudofuor: the record does not disclose who Yaw Agyei is, bl)t Yaw Abudofuor is the Odikro of Mpobi. If the Native Court found them to be Elders of the N senia Stool it would seem they must have known this fact themselves and taken judicial notice of it. I do not consider that they should have done so but that they should have elicited the information from the witnesses and placed it on record. I notice that the second defendant stated he was calling elders as witnesses and that the only witnesses he called were the two I have referred to.

In order that a gift of immovable property be valid, according to native law and custom, it is necessary that certain formalities be complied with. In Sarbah's Fanti Customary Law, 1st ed., at pp. 69 and 70, it is stated:-

"The acceptance of a gift of immovable property must invariably be made public. Acceptance is made (1) by rendering thanks with a thanks-offering, etc., (2) corporeal acceptance, (3) or using or enjoying the gift, (4) or exercising rights of ownership over the gift. "

The Native Court of first instance must be presumed to know what is required by native custom to constitute a valid gift as it is one of those matters essentially within the knowledge of such court. There was evidence, which, if believed, a~ it undoubtedly was by the court of first instance, was sufficient for that court to find that there had been a valid gift. It has been argued by counsel for the plaintiff that the evidence of the second defendant was all hearsay on material questions; undoubtedly some of it was, but there was also other evidence to go on. It has also been argued that both the ex-Nseniahene and the second defendant were interested parties, as the first could succeed to the plaintiff and the latter has an interest as the occupant of the stool.

These are facts which are obvious, but which were no doubt present in the minds of the members of the court of first instance and taken into consideration by them. pg 134 

   In my view the whole matter depends on the credibility of witnesses and this is essentially a matter for the court of first instance which saw and heard them. I am asked by the plaintiff to accept the view taken by the Asantehene's Appeal Court as to the evidence. I will refer to Nthah v. Bennieh (1), at p. 3, where it was said:

"It appears to their Lordships that decisions of the native tribunal on such matters which are peculiarly within their know­ledge, arrived at after a fair hearing on relevant evidence, should not be disturbed without very clear proof that they are wrong."

Again, in the case of Kisiedu v. Dompreh (2), at p. 286, it was said in the Privy Council::-

" Nevertheless before an Appellate Court can .rroperly reverse a finding of fact by a trial judge, who has seen and heard the witnesses, and can best judge not merely of their intention and desire to speak the truth, but of their accuracy in fact, it must come to an affirmative conclusion that the finding is wrong. There is a presumption in favour of its correctness which must be displaced. "

The co.urt of first instance in the present case made findings of fact which, in my opinion, were quite justified by the evidence. They were entitled to believe certain witnesses and disbelieve others. The Asantehene's Court" A " was wrong in disturbing the findings of the court of first instance. I accordingly reverse the decision of the Asantehene's Court "A" and restore the judgment of the Native Court of first instance.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
