Practice-Appeal Courts-Power of an Appeal Court without jurisdiction to remit to an Appeal Court with jurisdiction-Native Courts (Ashanti) Procedure Rules, No. 32 of 1949, Rule 129 (1).

A civil dispute concerning land was taken on appeal from Ahafo Local Authority Court (a Native Court, grade" C "), the court of first instance, to Kumasi Division Native Court (a Native Court, grade " B "). From this court it was taken on further appeal to the Asan­tehene's Court and from there to the Land Court. The Kumasi Division Native Court appeared to have been constituted as a court with appellate jurisdiction in land matters by an Order (Native Courts (Ashanti) (Establishment) (No.4) Order, No. 22 of 1951) made by the Chief Regional Officer, Ashanti (at that time Chief Commissioner, Ashanti) acting under powers delegated to him by the Governor and originating in the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99 of 1951. In 1953 a second Order (the Native Courts (Ashanti) (Establishment) (No.6) Order, L.N. 405 of 1953), made by the Chief Regional Officer revoked the Order No. 22 of 1951 as from December 1, 1953, and created new Native Courts within the Kumasi Division. Later in the same year 11 further Order (Native Courts (Ashanti) (Variation) (No. 14) Order, L.N. 451 of 1953), purported to remove any ambiguities that may have existed in the second order by affirming that none of the Native Courts mentioned in that order should exercise appellate jurisdiction in land matters, and by conferring on the Kumasi Municipal Court jurisdiction to continue and conclude any matters commenced in the Kumasi Division Native Court before December 1, 1953.

Held, (i) the court constituted to hear appeals from Native Courts in civil causes or matters relating to the ownership, possession or occupation of land in the Confederacy of Ashanti is, by section 25 (2) of the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99 of 1951, the Asantehene's Court. The Native Court (Ashanti) (Establishment) (No.4) Order, No. 22 of 1951, was therefore invalid in so far as it purported to create the Kumasi Division Native Court (a grade" B" Native Court) as a Court of Appeal in land matters, and the Chief Regional Officer, Ashanti (the Chief Commissioner for Ashanti as he was at that time) who made the order under powers apparently conferred upon him by the Governor acting under sections 3 and 24 of the principal Ordinance, was acting ultra vires in so doing.

(ii) The Native Courts (Ashanti) (Establishment) (No.6) Order, L.N. 405 of 1953, which revoked the Order No. 22 of 1951, and the Native Courts (Ashanti) (Variation) (No. 14) Order, L.N. 451 of 1953, which amended the Order L.N. 405 of 1953, by conferring, in para. 3 thereof, jurisdiction upon the Kumasi Municipal Court to continue and conclude pg 142any causes commenced in the Kumasi Division Native Court before December 1, 1953, are invalid in so far as they purport to give the Kumasi Municipal Court jurisdiction to hear appeals in land matters commenced in the Kumasi Division Native Court.

(iii) In the case before the court the proceedings before the Kumasi Division Native Court, to which the case first went on appeal from the Native Court of first instance, and before the Asantehene's Court, to which the case went on appeal from the Kumasi Division Native Court, were a nullity.

(iv) The Land Court, as the appropriate Court of Appeal from the Asantehene's Court in land matters, had power to hear the appeal and therefore the court had no power under the second proviso to Rule 129 (1) of the Native Courts (Ashanti) Procedure Rules, 1949, to remit the appeal elsewhere.

Amchi1·i v. Kwafo «1953) W.A.C.A. Cyclostyled Judgments 1952­1953, p. 13) applied.

As to the Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99, see Laws of the Gold Coast (1951), Vol. III, p. 196; as to the Native Courts (Ashanti) (Establishment) (No.4) Order, No. 22 of 1951, see Laws of the Gold Coast-1951 Legislation, p. 518; as to the Native Courts (Ashanti) (Establishment) (No.6) Order, L.N. 405 of 1953, see Laws of the Gold Coast-1953 Legislation, p. 505; as to the Native Courts (Ashanti) (Variation) (No. 14) Order, L.N. 451 of 1953, see Laws of the Gold Coast -1953 Legislation, p. 638.

As to the Native Courts (Ashanti) Procedure Rules, No. 32 of 1949, see Laws of the Gold Coast-1949 Legislation, p. 242. Rule 129 (1) provides that:-

"An appeal court shall of its own motion as soon as may be convenient after receipt of Notice of Intent to Appeal upon being satisfied that the appellant is not entitled to appeal order the appeal to be struck out."

There then follows a provision:-

"If it appears to the Appeal Court that it has no jurisdiction to entertain the appeal but some other court has it shall not strike out the appeal but shall remit it to such court."

Amchi1·v. Klcafo (1) establishes that the proviso gives no powers of remitting where the court has jurisdiction to hear a case.

BESON.J. This is an appeal from the decision of the Asantehene's Court " A," and this court is the fourth court to which the case has come since it started. Before the appeal was to be argued on its pg143 merits I drew the attention' of counsel for both parties to a matter I considered was of first importance in this appeal and invited their arguments. It occurred to me that the case had been heard in too many courts before it reached the Land Court and that some proceedings must have been a nullity.  

The history of this case is as follows: it started in the Ahafo Local Authority Court at Goase. This is a grade" C " Court. It was taken on appeal to the Kumasi Division Native Court, a grade" B " Court, then to the Asantehene's Court" A," and now to the Land Court. In my opinion there is no authority in law for this ladder of appeals. The anomaly has occurred on account of an Order of the Chief Commissioner of Ashanti which purported to create the Kumasi Division Native Court as an appellate court in all suits relating to the ownership, possession or occupation, of land within the area of its jurisdiction; the Order I referred to is No. 22 of 1951, made by the Chief Commissioner of Ashanti on November 27, 1951. In my opinion section 6 (2) of that Order, insofar as it purports to give the Kumasi Division Native Court appellate jurisdiction in land causes, is ultra vires and of no effect, and the courts should not apply it at all.

In giving this judgment I propose to refer to sections of the Ordinances as now revised, although the Order of 1951 was passed before the Revised Laws came into force.

I do not think it is disputed that the land which is the subject matter of this action is situated within the Confederacy. The Native Courts (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 99, s. 25 (2), provides that an appeal from a Native Court of first instance in a case relating to the owner­ship, etc., of land shall lie to the Asantehene's Court, or direct to the Land Court if the Asantehene's Court shall not have been constituted a Court of Appeal, as in fact it has. Section 3 of the Ordinance autho­rizes the Governor to establish Native Courts by Order: section 24 authorizes the Governor to appoint any Native Court to be a Court of Appeal within the area of its jurisdiction for the purposes of section 25. The powers of the Governor were delegated to the Chief Commissioner of Ashanti, now the Chief Regional Officer.

Where powers are given to legislate by Orders, Rules, Regulations or otherwise, it is clear law that such powers can only be exercised if they are not in conflict with any Ordinance or other law in force in the Gold Coast. If the powers are exceeded, any Order, etc., is ultra vires and of no legal effect and the courts will not give effect to it. By section 25 (2) of c. 99 the Legislature has provided for appeals in land causes to the Asantehene's Court from a Native Court, and no Order of the Governor or the Chief Commissioner can alter that: the section was made law by the Legislature and can only be repealed or modified by it.

,The effect of the Chief Commissioner's Order, No. 22 of 1951, if valid, is to create a court, the Kumasi Division "B " Court, as a pg144 Court of Appeal in land causes from grades" C " and" D " Native Courts, thus apparently ousting the jurisdiction of the Asantehene's Court in such cases; even if the jurisdiction of the Asantehene's Court is not ousted, the Kumasi Division " B " Court would at least be given concurrent jurisdiction with the Asantehene's Court.

One can well imagine what might happen if such an Order was legally valid; for instance, if both parties were aggrieved by a decision of a grade" C " or " D " Native Court in a land cause (if any such court had jurisdiction in land causes) one party might appeal to the Asantehene's Court and the other to the Kumasi Division" B " Court. If this happened it would certainly tax the ingenuity o£ the panel members of each court to decide what should be done.

Another anomaly would also arise. If the appeal were heard in the Kumasi Division "B " Court, to which court would a further appeal lie? C. 99 does not provide for an appeal from a Native Appeal Court in a land cause to any court: it is only from the Asantehene's Court that such an appeal lies to the Land Court, except, of course, in cases concerning land outside the Confederacy.

It would seem therefore that the Chief Commissioner's Order, No. 22 of 1951, purports not only to vary section 25 (2) of c. 99 but also to prohibit, by obvious inference, any further appeal after one to the Kumasi Division" B " Court.

It is interesting to see what has occurred in the present case, no doubt owing to the Order of 1951. The case went on appeal first to the Kumasi Division "B" Court, then on further appeal to the Asantehene's Court and finally to the Land Court. Even assuming that the Chief Commissioner's Order, No. 22 of 1951, is intra vires, there is no provision for an appeal from the Kumasi Division" B " Court to the As:mtehene's Court.

I am quite convinced that the Legislature never intended that the number of rungs in the ladder of appeal in land causes should be increased: I think that they would prefer to see them reduced, and at all events never intended the appeallndder to be extended by Orders made under c. 99.

I rule accordingly that the Order, No. 22 of 1951, made by the Chief Commissioner, insofar as section 6 (2) is concerned with the Kumasi Division " B " Court as an appellate court in land causes, is ultra vires and of no legal effect.

I see that Order No. 22 of 1951 was revoked by Order L.N. 405, -the Native Courts (Ash anti) (Establishment) (No.6) Order, 1953­which contained a transitional provision in section 7 (1). Moreover a further Order, L.N. 451-the Native Courts (Ashanti) (Variation) (No. 14) Order-was made amending the one just mentioned in the same year. In section 3 of the last-mentioned Order there is a proviso affecting proceedings instituted in the Kumasi Division Native Court before December 1, 1953. But whatever may be the aim of that pg145 proviso or of any transitional provisions, the fact remains that the original Order, or part of it, was ultra vires, and this cannot be remedied in any subsequent Orders.

I will now go back to the present appeal in the light of my ruling given above. I hold that no appeal lay from the decision of the Native Court of first instance to the Kumasi Division" B " Court, and that the proceedings in that court were therefore a nullity. It follows as a matter of course that no appeal lay to the Asantehene's Court" A " from the Kumasi Division "B" Court, so the proceedings in the Asantehene's Court were also a nullity.

The Land Court is the correct court to which to appeal in a land cause from the Asantehene's Court, and therefore I have no power to remit this appeal to any other court: Regulation 129 of the Native Courts (Ashanti) Procedure Rules and the second proviso thereto does not apply to this case, vide the decision of Anwhiri v. Kwafo (1).

The only effective judgment is that of the Native Court of first instance, which was in favour of the defendant, now the appellant. I have a great deal of sympathy for the parties to these proceedings, as this is the fourth court where this has been dealt with.

I accordingly declare the proceedings in both the Asantehene's Court" A " and the Kumasi Division" B " Court to be nullities and set them aside and restore the judgment of the Native Court of first instance. Whether or not the plaintiff can now appeal out of time is not a matter for me to decide, but will be considered if and when an application is made. The appeal is dismissed.

Judgment of the Native Court of first instance restored.

S. G. D.
