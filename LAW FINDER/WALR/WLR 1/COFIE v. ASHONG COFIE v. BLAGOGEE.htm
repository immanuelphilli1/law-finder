                                                    January 31, 19

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra     

                                                                                               van Lare J.

                                                                                       COFIE v. BLAGOGEE. 

                                         COFIE v. ASHONG AND ANOTHER

                                                                           

 

Land-Claim and counterclaim for declaration of title to land-" Brazilian" family lands in Accra-Who can give good title on transfer.

Native law and custom-Nature of title gained by long occupation-Power to protect such title by action.

Estoppel-Land-Effect of non-intervention by party claiming title to land in previous litigation concerning the land.

These were actions brought by the plaintiff against the defendants for a declaration of ownership, damages for trespass and for recovery of possession of a piece of land in the " Brazilian" quarter of Accra. The plaintiff's case was that his father, one Cofie Congo, was granted the piece of land by the Ga Mantse through an elder of Atupai, a Gbese man; that his father occupied the land and cultivated it for many years without disturbance and that his occupation ripened into ownership. The plaintiff sues for himself and on behalf of his brothers and sisters as the persons entitled to the land as devisees under their father's will. The defendants had entered the land, which they claimed to. have purchased from Nii Azuma III, the second defendant in the first of the 'two consolidated cases and Head of the "Brazilian" community of Accra, and had constructed buildings thereon. The defendants attempted to set up a plea of estoppel by the conduct of the plaintiff in failing to intervene'during previous litigation concerning the same land.

Held, (i) the ownership of "Brazilian" lands in Accra was not vested in the Head of the "Brazilian" community, but in the con­stituent families who severally owned portions of the lands, and that therefore the Head of the "Brazilian" community could not transfer a valid title to "Brazilian" lands to a purchaser.

Nii Azuma III v. Lariki Aruna (Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, January 13, 1950, unreported) followed.

(ii) Although Native law and custom does not admit of prescriptive titles, a grant by an original owner followed by continuous occupation and effective possession without payment of rent or dues for several years and without let or hindrance gives a valid title, and although this may only be of a usufructuary nature it entitles its possessor to maintain an action against an intruder.

(iii) Estoppel such as to deprive a man of his legal rights can only spring from conduct on his part of such a nature as would make it fraudulent for him to seek to set up those rights. Mere intervention in pg 83 litigation cannot amount to encouragement of his opponent of such a nature as would amount to fraud in this sense. Willmott v. Barber ( (1880) 15 Ch.D. 96) followed.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. This case is one of a long series of cases relating to lands owned by the •• Brazilian" community of Accra. As found by van Lare J., it has been established by this series of cases, that the" Brazilian" lands are owned by individual •• Brazilian" families and that the Head of the •• Brazilian" community as such has no control over these lands except over such portion thereof as is in the ownership of his immediate family­see also Amui v. Sabih, per Coussey J. as he then was.

It is a well-known fact of history that the •• Brazilians" migrated to this country about the year 1838 and that they were given a large tract of land in Accra by the Ga Mantsemei to settle on, and many decisions in the law courts have established that the various families comprising that community appropriated portions of this land to themselves by effective occupation and cultivation. The claim in this case is based on similar appropriation by grant and effective possession.

The plaintiff's father, through whom the plaintiff claims, was, however, not a •• Brazilian" and the ratio decidendi in this case was based on the fact, as found by van Lare J., that the Ga Mantse made a grant of the land to the plaintiff's father for purposes of cultivation; that the plaintiff's father had effectively occupied the land in question and that the plaintiff could therefore not be ousted by an intruder.]

CONSOLIDATED ACTION by the plaintiff against the defendants and their grantor, the co-defendant, for a declaration of title, recovery of possession, and damages for trespass in respect of certain land in Accra. The actions were transferred to the Land Court from the Ga Native Court" B " on September 15, 1952.

Ollcnnu for the plaintiff.

Bcntsi-Enchill for the defendants.

VANLARE J. These consolidated cases were transferred for hearing and determination before this court from the Ga Native Court" B " where the actions commenced. One of the parties, Awumee Gbedemah, who joined as a co-defendant in the second suit before the Native Court, has been struck out, because there has been no appearance by him before this court and there is nothing on record showing the interest he claimed in the subject-matter; and also because he is believed to have since died and there has been no substitution. The plaintiff claiming to be the present owner of a certain tract of land in the Asylum area, north-west of the Mental Hospital, Accra, discovered some preparatory building operations of two different sites or plots within the area which he claime(l. Those responsible for dealing with   pg 84 the land as aforesaid turned out to be the defendants K. A. C. Ashong and E. D. Baker Blagogee respectively. The plaintiff and his mother drew the attention of each of these gentlemen to their claim of ownership of the piece of land on which each was preparing to build, and each defendant was duly warned and requested to stop his building operations as found. The plaintiff's family offered to sell the respective sites or plots to the defendants who at first appeared interested in the proposition but did not in fact treat with the plaintiff's family; they nevertheless continued their respective dealings with the land and each has now completed a block house on the land despite the knowledge and the warning of adverse title.

The plaintiff was obliged therefore to institute these proceedings against the defendants each of whom claim to derive title by purchase from the co-defendant Nii Azuma III, as Chief of the Brazilian Community, Accra. It would appear that the defendants preferred Nii Azuma Ill's claim of title to the lands to that of the plaintiff, and treated the plaintiff's adverse claim and warning with complete disregard to such an extent that even when the suits were instituted before the Native Court and an interim injunction was granted to prevent them from dealing with their respective plots they treated the Native Court's Order of Injunction with contempt and continued to build. It cannot be denied, under the circumstance, that the defen­dants continued their respective building operations' at peril; and I venture to think that it was a bold and courageous conduct; an act done with eyes wide open as to the risk in case the plaintiff succeeded in his claim.

As to whether the co-defendant Nii Asuma III claiming the disputed areas as portions of Brazilian Community land had any title which he could validly convey to each of the defendants regarding their respective plots is easy to determine. On the authorities the co-defendant, qua Chief, purporting to convey the plots to the defen­dants could not validly do so even if the areas concerned were found to be Brazilian Community land because he, as such Chief, had no portion of the Brazilian Community land vested in him. Furthermore the evidence on behalf of the defendants supports the view that there is no portion of the Brazilian Community land which has not been appropriated or allotted to one or other Brazilian family. I find that the co-defendant Nii Azuma III has been wrongly holding himself out as clothed with authority to alienate portions of Brazilian Community land in Accra. He has been told by the courts in this country that the Brazilian Community lands are not vested in him, and the earlier he realizes the true position the better for all concerned:-

"The weight of the evidence and the earlier cases affecting the so-called Brazilian lands in Accra to which I have been referred are against the idea that the land in question is so vested in the Head of the Community that he could alienate the land in pg 85 .question; certainly he could not do so with the concurrence of his own immediate relative only; for it is not his family land": per Coussey J. in Nii Azuma III v. Lariki Arona and another (1).

I find this dictum applicable on all fours with the present case before me. The co-defendant has admitted that the people who witnessed his signature in Abloh's conveyance, referred to in the Lariki case, were the same people who witnessed his signature in the conveyances to the defendants in the present case. In other words the co-defendant has again as Head of the Brazilian Community purported to convey portions of what he claimed to be Brazilian Community land with the concurrence of certain members of his own and immediate relatives only to each of the defendants in the present case. I declare therefore that the title as pleaded by each of the defendants is ineffective and cannot be relied upon.

But the plaintiff, however, claiming as he does for a declaration of title cannot take advantage of the weakness of the defendants' case; he must rely on the strength of his own case. The onus lies on him to satisfy the court that he is entitled on the evidence adduced by him to' the declaration he seeks as the weakness of the defendants' case will not help him. He has, however, in my opinion, satisfactorily discharged that onus on him. He is a son of the late Cofie Congo who died in Accra in the year 1940, and it will appear that he sues on behalf of himself and his brothers and sisters, children of his said late father. The disputed pieces of the land are clearly shown on the plan admitted in evidence and form a portion of a larger tract of land . which is also shown on that plan. The plaintiff claims title through his father, the late Cofie Congo, to the larger area hatched blue on the plan. He claims a declaration of title "against the co-defendant Nii Azuma III in respect of the whole area but as against each of the defendants a declaration of title only in respect of the respective plots upon which he has built.

The fact that the late Cofie Congo had been in effective possession of a tract of land which he cultivated in the Asylum area for many years before his death is not in dispute, and there is evidence which r accept that he planted neem trees to mark the extent of the area cultivated by him and which in due course became stamped as his property. I am satisfied that this area is the one hatched blue as shown on the admitted plan by the surveyor, Mr. Simpson, for the purposes of this case. Lariki Aruna, a witness called by the defence, corroborates the evidence on behalf of the plaintiff that the late Cofie Congo planted neem trees to mark the extent of the area cultivated by him. Lariki Aruna is an adjoining boundary owner, and I find on the evidence as a whole that the pieces of land which the defendants claimed through the co-defendant are included in the area which the late Cofie Congo acquired by cultivation and by virtue of long occupa­tion. The land forms a boundary with land occupied by certain   Brazilian" families in that neighbourhood. Although it is true that there is no such thing in native customary law as a prescriptive title and this has been accepted by the courts to mean that mere use and occupation for any length of time cannot oust an original title, it is nevertheless good law that a grant by an original owner followed by a continuous occupation and effective possession without payment of rent or dues coupled with reputed ownership for several years without let or hindrance give a valid title. This is how the "Brazilian" families in that neighbourhood have come to own their respective lands and it is in exactly the same manner that the late Cofie Congo also came to own the land the subject-matter of the suit. A very usual form of native title is that of usufructuary right or possessory title in respect of which the owner can maintain actions against intruders and trespassers. In my view, it is this kind of native title which the plaintiff family has acquired and which he can protect by action.

Now, to the origin of possession, I accept the evidence that the late Cofie Congo acquired his original possession by grant from the Ga Mantse through the late Nii Tetteh J\wamine, an elder of Atukpai, a Gbese man. The late Cofie Congo and his wife and children farmed the area and openly exercised undisturbed acts of ownership over it for about thirty years before his death, and after him his said wife and children have continued in possession ever since. The defence suggests that the area is a portion of " Brazilian" community land and that the late Cofie Congo's original possession was by leave and licence of the members of the ,. Brazilian" community or of one or other •• Brazilian" family. The evidence in support of the suggestion is unsatisfactory and unimpressive and I am not disposed to accept it.

The late Cofie Congo by will devised his said acquired property near the" Lunatic Asylum" to his children, including the plaintiff. I am satisfied from the evidence that the defendants Ashong and Blagogee have wrongly entered upon portions of the plaintiff's said land and have built thereon ..

I have now to deal with the legal point of estoppel which has been pleaded by the defence and that is: whether the plaintiff is estopped (1) by conduct of himself and his predecessor in title in standing by while others litigated concerning this land; (2) by the judgment of the Native Court in suit Nii Tettey Gbeke II v. Nii Azuma III and by the judgment of Jackson J. in the Kokomlemlc Consolidated Cases delivered on May 31, 1951.

With respect to the plea of estoppel by conduct it is settled law that the conduct of the party complained about must amount to acquiescence. In this connection I refer to what is the equitable doctrine of acquiescence in the dictum of Fry J. in Willmott v. Barber (2), as follows:-

" It has been said that the acquiescence which will deprive a man of his legal rights must amount to fraud, and in my view pg 87 that is an abbreviated statement of a very true proposition. A man is not to be deprived of his legal rights unless he has acted in such a way as would make it fraudulent for him to set up those rights. What then are the elements or requisites necessary to constitute fraud of that description? In the first place the plaintiff must have made a mistake as to his legal rights. Secondly, the plaintiff must have expended some money or must have done some act (not necessarily upon the defendant's land) on the faith of his mistaken belief. Thirdly, the defendant, the possessor of the legal rights, must know of the existence of his own right which is inconsistent with the right claimed by the plaintiff. If he does not know of it he is in the same position as the plaintiff, and the doctrine of acquiescence is founded upon conduct with a knowledge of your legal rights. Fourthly, the defendant, the possessor of the legal right, must know of the plaintiff's mistaken belief of his rights. If he does not, there is nothing which calls upon him to assert his own rights. Lastly, the defendant, the possessor of the legal right, must have encouraged the plaintiff in his expenditure of money or in the other acts which he has done, either directly or by abstaining from asserting his legal rights. Where all these elements exist, there is fraud of such a nature as will entitle the court to restrain the possessor of the legal right from exercising it, but in my judgment nothing short of this will do.

 In my view those elements do not exist in this case, and the plea therefore fails. It is my opinion also that the second plea of estoppel by record does not avail because the plaintiff is not a party, nor is he a privy of any of the parties to the suits mentioned; in other words, I am unable to discover that plaintiff's title has been adjudicated upon in any of the judgments pleaded.

In the result the plaintiff is entitled to judgment against each defendant (a) for declaration of title to the land he has trespassed and built upon; and (b) for injunction restraining each defendant, his servants or agents from any further entry upon the land for any purposes whatsoever; and (c) as against the co-defendant Nii Azuma III for a declaration of title in respect of the larger tract of land hatched blue on the plan in this case.

As I consider that the plaintiff has rather substantially gained, by reason of the buildings on the trespassed area, than lost I would not award the pecuniary damages claimed.

The Assessor, Mr. Sai Annan, agrees with this judgment.

Judgment for the plaintiff.
