                                         Accra, April 13, 1955.

                                      Cor. Foster-Sutton P., Coussey and Hearne JJ.A.

                                                                                               AKILL                                         APPELLANT;

                                                                                                v.

                                          SYHIAN THADING ASSOCIATION, PER ASCHKAR           RESPONDENTS.

 

Appeal from the Supreme Court, Gold Coast.

Landlord and Tenant-Dispute as to rent payable-Action by landlord for arrears of rent and recovery of possession-A ward of possession by trial judge-No evidence to support case as presented by plaintiff for recovery of possession-Absence of reason to justify order for possession on other grounds.

Held, the plaintiffs sought possession on the ground that the defendant had given notice, but as no evidence was adduced in support it was not open to the judge to make an order for possession on this ground. Further, if the judge had been minded to exercise his discretion and grant possession on the ground that rent was in arrear, it would have been improper for him to do so unless the defendant had had proper opportunity to present a defence-to this ground.

Appeal allowed in part.

Akyeampong for the appellant.

 Heward-Mills for the respondents.

The following judgment was delivered by HEARNE J.A.:

The plaintiffs-respondents sued the defendant-appellant for arrears of rent which they alleged were due by the latter in respect of two stores which had been let to him. The respondents also claimed " recovery of possession (of the stores) on the grounds that the defen­dant has given notice to terminate his tenancy-agreement with the plaintiffs - .. " Significantly, the date of the alleged notice is not mentioned. pg 2

The question of whether the rent payable by the appellant was in arrears turned upon the further question of whether the appellant had agreed to pay £60 or £30 per month for the two stores. Exhibits A and B on the face of them suggest that the agreed rent, as the appellant asserted, was £30. On the other hand, Exhibits C1, C2 and C3, on the face of them, suggest that the. agreed rent, as· the respondents asserted, was £60. It was recognised by both counsel at the hearing of the appeal that Exhibits A and B, in the absence of

- any explanation, were inconsistent with the respondents' claim that the. agreed rent was £60, and also that Exhibits C1, C2 and C3, in the absence of any explanation, were inconsistent with the appellant's defence that the agreed rent was £30. Explanations were, however, given in evidence by both parties before the learned trial judge who found in favour of the respondents. I am unable to say that he was wrong in accepting, as he did, the evidence of the respondents, and the appeal from that portion of his judgment which ·awarded the respondents a total of £597 5s. as arrears of rent up to the end of October, 1953, -in my opinion, must be dismissed.

It is understood that the whole of the amount has been paid and the main concern of the appellant is to obtain relief from this court by the setting aside of the order made by the trial judge that "the plaintiffs shall recover possession of the said stores on November 15, 1953."

The respondents, as I have said, asked for recovery of possession of the stores on the ground that the appellant had given notice to terminate his tenancy agreement with the respondents. But there was no evidence that the appellant had given notice and it was not even suggested to him in cross-examination that he had.

It was, however, argued by counsel for the respondents that as the judge had found that the rent payable by the appellant was in arrears, he must be taken to have made the order for possession which he did on that footing. I find myself quite unable to. make this assumption. It is possible that the judge overlooked the absence of evidence that the appellant had himself " given notice." But even if it is assumed that he did make the order for possession on the ground of arrears of rent being due, in my opinion, his order cannot be upheld.

A plaintiff must give a defendant " notice of those facts which will put the latter on his guard and tell him what he will have to meet when the case comes on for trial" Particulars of claim can only be amended, by leave, on it appearing that the defendant will not be prejudiced by the amendment (see the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, Second Schedule, Ord. 5, r. 4). The respondents asked for possession on the ground that the appellant had given notice. No. leave to amend was applied for, and the judge could not properly have ordered possession on the ground of arrears of rent being due (if he did) as it was not the ground on which recovery of possession was sought. pg 3


    It is not merely a matter of pleading that is involved in the view I have taken, but also one of principle. Counsel for the respondents admitted that before the judge could have made an order for possession on the ground that arrears of rent were due, he would have had to exercise his discretion as to whether such an order was justified. If the respondents had applied for leave to amend their pleadings so as to ask for possession on the ground that arrears of rent were due, the appellant, if leave had been granted, could have abandoned his defence that the sum of £30 only per month was payable by him as rent, have paid the full claim of the respondents into court and have invited the judge to take what he had done into consideration in deciding whether the respondents' claim for recovery of possession should be granted. Again, if the appellant was aware that the judge had it in mind to make an order for possession, not on the ground on which the respon­dents had asked that it should be made, but on the ground (as it is assumed for the purpose of argument) that he adopted for making the order, his counsel would undoubtedly have asked him, in the exercise of his discretion, to take into account that the appellant had been required to pay £60 per month for two stores the rental of which had been fixed under section 5 of .the Rent Control Ordinance, 1952, at £16 15s. with effect from April 1, 1953, about two months after the suit had been filed (Exhibit 2). It is this, I have little doubt, that led the respondents consciously and advisedly not to ask for possession on the ground that what appears to be an exorbitant rent had not been paid in full: for such a prayer based on that ground, as it must have been foreseen, would have afforded scope for the exercise of judicial discretion in favour of the appellant.

I have dealt with the submissions of counsel for the respondents on the basis of the assumption which he made. But, as I have said elsewhere, it is an assumption I am unable to accept. From a perusal of the whole record I am only prepared to assume that the judge made the order he did on the ground on which he was invited to make the order, and there is not an iota of evidence to justify the order on that ground.

For these reasons I would set aside the judge's order which reads: .. I further declare that the plaintiffs shall recover possession of the said stores at November 15, 1953."

I would allow the respondents to retain the benefit of the costs decreed in their favour in the court below, and I would award the appellant, who was partially successful on appeal, only the costs of obtaining a copy of the record.

FOSTER-SUTTON P. I concur.

COUSSEY J.A. I concur.
