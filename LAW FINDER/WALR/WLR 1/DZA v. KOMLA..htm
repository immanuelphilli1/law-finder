                                                                       February 22, 1956

                     Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra 

                                                                       DZA v. KOMLA.  

                                                                           Lingley J.

 

 

Native Courts-Practice-Duty of Native Court to assess oral evidence.

 This case is reported for the directions given by the court to Native Courts on the matter of reception and assessment of oral evidence given by witnesses on oath.

   Held, it is the duty of the panel members of a Native Court, when the court is receiving oral evidence, to form their own conclusions as to the value to be attached to such evidence. The judgment given by the court should indicate what parts of the oral evidence the court accepts and what parts it rejects, the reasons for such acceptance or rejection, and pg146 the weight the court attaches to it. Although, in land cases, a view by the court of the land concerned is often a desirable thing, it is undesirable that too much weight should be attached to the impressions gained by the court on viewing the land, to the detriment of the weight the court should attach to the demeanour of witnesses when giving evidence on oath, and to the evidence they give.

APPEAl, from the decision of the Asogli Native Court .. B" given on June 7, 1955, in favour of the defendants.

Akyearnpong for the plaintiff.

Bossman for the defendant.

LINGLEY J. I consider this case should be sent back to the trial court for a trial de novo by a different panel.

(1) A proper plan should be prepared.

(2) The court in giving judgment should indicate the oral evidence given on oath which it accepts and that which it rejects. It should indicate its reasons for the weight it attaches to the oral evidence.

I make this order granting a new trial because I feel that the time has come when Native Courts should be capable of hearing oral evidence and deciding on the weight to be given to such evidence.

It is all too common for a trial court to set out at some considerable length the respective cases of the parties and then state that they viewed the land and therefore believe the party for whom they give judgment.

It is the duty of a trial court to assess the value of the oral evidence it hears: it ought not to shirk making a decision one way or the other. If the court is genuinely unable to decide which side to believe, it follows that the plaintiff has failed to prove his case and the defendant is entitled to judgment.

The trial court should make the effort to decide which party is giving truthful evidence during the hearing of the oral evidence. In this case and many others far too much reliance is placed on the view of the land to resolve conflicts of evidence. It must be apparent that false allegations by one side in court may be supported by that side confidently pointing out boundary marks in support of its claim.

The capacity to point out boundary marks may corroborate a party's claim but such conduct ought not to be the sole test of a party's veracity.

The judgment of the court below is set aside: the case is sent back for trial de novo by a different panel: I make no order as to costs in this court: the costs of the first trial to abide the result of the new trial. Appeal allowed. Order for new trial.

S. G. D.
