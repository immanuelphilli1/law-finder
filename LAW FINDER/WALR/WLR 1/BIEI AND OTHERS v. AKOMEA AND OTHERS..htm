                                                                             February 28, 1956.

                          Supreme Co urt of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra

                                                         BIEI AND OTHERS v. AKOMEA AND OTHERS.

                                                                                    Lingley J.

 

 

Native Law and Custom-Stool land-Action for declaration of ownership of stool land sold to a "purchasing company" (huza)-Plea that sale not in accordance with custom and made without concurrence of superior stool -Plea that plaintiffs estopped by previous judgment against one member of the" purchasing company."

This was an action brought in a Native Court in Akyem Abuakwa by a "company" of land purchasers against the Stool of Nsuta, the vendor stool and a subordinate stool in the Akyem Abuakwa state, to resist an attempt by the vendor stool to deprive them of the ownership of the land in question. The" company" purchased the land in 1920, entered into possession, and cultivated the land. At the time of this action the members of the" company" were still in possession. It would appear that some time after the purchase of the land the Nsuta Stool brought an action in the Omanhene's Tribunal against one Anobi, a brother of Kwaku Ansa, who was the leader of the" company" and a member of it in respect of his portion of the land allotted to him, and the Omanhene's Tribunal declared the sale void. The defendants' case was that the judgment in the Anobi case' amounted to a declaration that the sale to the "company" was void and that therefore the plaintiffs (being members of the "company") were estopped by that judgment from making their claim. The defendants also alleged that the sale was not in accordance with Native custom. The Trial Native Court found in favour of the defendants on two grounds, namely, '(a) that if the strict requirements of the custom of sale are not performed a sale is null and void, and (b) that the Okyenhene (i.e., the Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa) has the inherent right to annul any sale of stool land not properly sold in accordance with custom.

The latter ground clearly refers to the judgment in the A nobi case.

On appeal to the Land Court, Accra. pg 175

Held, (i) that Anobi was not sued in a representative capacity, and while the judgment against him might be binding on him and his successors if it referred to a specific portion of the land allotted to him, it could not bind the" company" as a whole.

(ii) That the proposition contained in the two grounds on which the judgment of the Native Appeal Court was based cannot be applied with­out qualification and cannot be made applicable to the circumstances of this case. Local customs cannot be allowed to override general principles of law.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The principle of Native law and custom governing the sale of land by a subordinate stool is that such a sale is void if it is not fake with the consent and concurrence of the superior stool (see Sintirn v. Apeatu (1934) 2 W.A.C.A. 197). In this case the sale was obviously without the consent of the Okyenhene and the sale was therefore void, but the decision of Lingley J., reversing the decision of the Trial Native Court, would appear to be based on the fact that the "company" had been in possession since 1920 and had made extensive farms on the land and it would be inequitable to deprive them of possession.]

APPEAL from the decision of the Native Court "A" of Akyem Abuakwa given in favour of the defendants.

Larbi for the plaintiffs.

Danquah and Dua Sekyi for the defendants.

LINGLEY J. This is an appeal from a decision of Akyem Abuakwa Court "A." The decision was that of the majority of the court. The third member left "during the delivery with annoyance." On reading the record one can hardly be surprised.

The action is one by a ,. purchasing company" brought to resist the attempts of the local stool to drive them off land sold to them by the Odikro on the stool in 1920.

The trial court was compelled to accept the fact~ relied on by the plaintiffs but arrived at a perverse judgment in their determination to find for the local stool. I am not sure that the matter should not be sent back for a new trial with a recommendation that the case be transferred to an impartial court. However as I have said, the trial court were compelled to accept the main facts on which the plaintiffs rely.

These facts are that the plaintiffs under Kwame Ansah (now deceased) were sold land in 1920 by the Odikro of Nsutam; they paid the purchase price of £373 which was paid by instalments. They entered into possession and their labourers made farms. That posses­sion was not challenged for many years. (It is sufficient for the plaintiffs' purpose if they show some of the money was paid.) The defence alleged that the plaintiffs were bound by a finding of the tribunal of Omanhene who found for the stool against one Anobi; a brother of the deceased Kwame Ansah. This might be binding on  pg176 Anobi or his successors if it is shown to refer to a specific portion of the land demarcated and apportioned to Anobi, but it is relied on to estop all the plaintiffs in respect of the whole land. He was, however, not sued in a representative capacity nor was his standing such as to bind the" company" as a whole.

Some years have been taken up by the attempts of the plaintiffs to get the matter determined in the Supreme Court. Events have justified the lack of confidence in the local courts.

As I say, the court was compelled to accept the facts outlined above but found for the defendant by applying two propositions of customary law to these facts; as follows:-  

           (1) That if the strict requirements of the custom of sale are not performed the sale is null and void.

       (2) That the Okyenhene has the inherent right to annul any sale of stool land not properly sold in accordance with custom.

I do not agree that either of these propositions can be applied without qualification. They will certainly not be enforced in this court without regard to all the circumstances of the case. This court cannot allow local customs to override general principles and practice in these days of changing conditions.

In these circumstances I allow the appeal. The judgment of the trial court is set aside and judgment is entered for the plaintiffs, who are granted an injunction restraining the defendant, his agents, servants or labourers from interfering with the plaintiffs' property. The appellants to have costs in this court and taxed costs in the trial court.

Appeal allowed.

E. A.-A.
