In an action brought in a Native Court the defendants, before the case came on for hearing, were allowed, on application, a ruling that the matter was res judicata by reason of a previous decision of the same court. An appeal by the plaintiffs to a Native Court of Appeal was allowed on the grounds that the court of first instance had not adhered to the procedural regulations governing the taking of pleas of res judicata. 'l'he case then returned to the Native Court of first instance but came on for hearing on a day when the plaintiffs, although they had been advised, were not present. The Native Court, this time following the correct procedure, heard the defendants' plea of res judicata and again upheld it. The plaintiffs did not appeal from this decision but at a later date issued another summons; when the case came on for hearing the plea of res judicata was again upheld. From this decision the plaintiffs appealed on' the ground that the day when the previous case was heard in the Native Court, on its return from the appellate court, was a " return day" within the meaning of Regulation 10 of the Native Courts (Southern Section of Togoland under British Mandate) Procedure Regu­lations, 1949, and that as the plaintiffs were not present at the hearing the cause should have been stn.ck out, under the provisions of Regulation 10.

Held, in a Native Court a suit has only one" return day "-the day. on which the defendants, having been summoned to the court, make$ known whether or not he intends to defend. All other dates are hearing: dates, either fixed by the court in the presence of the parties or notified, to them.

AMES Ag.J.A. This appeal raises a question of interpretation 0 Rule 10 of the Native Courts (Southern Section of Togoland unde British Mandate) Procedure Regulations, 1949, which reads:-

" 10. If the plaintiff or complainant does not appear when th cause is called on the return day, the Native Court, unless the~ is some good reason for keeping it on the list (and in that pg123 reason shall be recorded and the hearing day fixed), shall strike the cause out."

    The question is this. A suit is brought by summons in a Native Court and an application is filed before the return day of the summons by the defendant by motion on notice for a ruling that the matter is res judicata by reason of a previous decision of the same court. Both the suit and the application come before the court after the return date of the summons (presumably there were adjournments) and the court hears the defendant's application and upholds the submission of res judicata whereupon the plaintiff appeals to the Native Court of Appeal. This appellate court allows the appeal and sends the suit back to the lower court for the suit to be heard and determined according to pro­cedure set out in Regulations 15, 17 and 18. The suit consequently comes again before the lower court, and a day is appointed for the hearing. In these circumstances is this last-mentioned day a " return day" within the meaning of Rule 1O?

    That was the course of events which had happened here. There were two plaintiffs and five defendants; the plaintiffs sued for them­selves and on behalf of their family, the Magadagbe family, which had appointed them their representatives for the purpose. The Native Court of Appeal seems to have thought (and perhaps correctly) that the lower court should not have heard, as they did, the application by motion made by two of the defendants, leaving the suit on the shelf, so to speak, but should have followed strictly the procedure in Rules 15, 17 and 18, by calling the suit and by asking the defendants their answer and then hearing evidence about the plea of res judicata.

   On the second occasion they did do so, but not immediately. The parties were notified to appear on May 12, when the first plaintiff appeared but not the second, and then again on August 4, when the second plaintiff appeared but not the first, and then again on August 17, when neither of them appeared. The court then heard the merits of the defendants' plea of res judicata and upheld it again. (The plea was founded on a 1937 decision of the same court, which was reversed on appeal to the Native Court of Appeal and restored on a further appeal to the Court of the Provincial Commissioner.)

The plaintiffs did not appeal against this second ruling, given on August 17, 1950, but on September 1, 1950, they issued another summons in the Native Court, which was transferred to the Land Court by an order dated September 5, 1951. When the suit came on for hearing in the ,Land Court, the same plea of res judicata was made and upheld in a decision given on June 27, 1952, against which the plaintiffs have made this appeal.

   The only argument that Mr. Bossman has been able to make on behalf of the plaintiffs is that after the case went back to the Native Court (after the appeal) the date fixed for hearing was a " return day" within the meaning of Rule 10, and that as neither plaintiff was pg 125 might lie within the area controlled by the Asamankese Stool, nevertheless the act of selling it constituted a sufficient assertion of ownership by the Akanteng Stool to enable them to convey a good title to a purchaser. They claimed further that in any case the consent of the Akyem Abuakwa Stool was sufficient to validate the conveyance to the plaintiffs' predecessor.

Held, (i) although it is possible to establish limited rights over virgin land by bringing it into effective occupation and cultivation it is not possible to acquire rights sufficient to support alienation by merely asserting ownership without more.

(ii) The land concerned was in any case clearly demonstrated to be Asamankese Stool land even though it might not have been in occupation at the material time, and as such, title on conveyance could only be made by the Asamankese Stool. The consent of the paramount stool (Akyem Abuakwa) is not required to alienations of Asamankese land.

Asamankese Arbitration (1929) Divisional Court 1926-1929, 220 followed.

(iii) It was a requirement of 'custom in 1913 that a conveyance of forest land should be evidenced by the performance of the customary rites concerned with publicizing such sale. In the absence of such supporting evidence and on the particular facts of the case the conveyance of 1927 would not operate to confer a valid title on the plaintiffs, even if the Akanteng Stool had been in a position to grant such a title.

(iv) If, in an action for a declaration of title, the plaintiff fails to establish his case, the correct order (provided the defendant has not counterclaimed for a declaration and established his case), is judgment for the defendant.

Kodilinye v. Odu (1935) 2 W.A.C.A. 336 followed.
