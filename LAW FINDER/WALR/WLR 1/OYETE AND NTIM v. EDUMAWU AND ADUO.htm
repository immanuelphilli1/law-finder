                                           West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session              

                                             ABABIO AND OTHERS, v. DARKWA AND OTHERS.

                                                     Coussey Korsah J.A., Ames Ag.J.A.

 

Native Law and Custom-Sale of stool land-Ingredients of effective transfer of title-E1Jidence req1/i7'ed to establish change of ownership.

The plaintiffs claimed a declaration of title to an area of forest land which, they asserted, had been purchased by their predecessor in 1913 from the Akanteng Stool. They were unable to adduce any evidence to show that there had been the public demarcation of boundaries required by custom to establish a sale of land, but they put forward a deed of conveyance, dated in 1927, which purported to convey to their prede­cessor all the "right, title and interest" of the vendor. The deed of conveyance was executed by the Odikro of Akanteng, as vendor, and by the Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa as the consenting party. The defendants, who were in occupation and possession of the land in dispute as tenants and licensees of the Asamankese Stool, claimed that the land belonged and always had belonged to the stool, that the Akanteng Stool had no power to convey and the Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa no autho­rity to confirm a conveyance of Asamankese Stool land, and that there had been no valid conveyance of the land to the plaintiffs' predecessor. It was not disputed, however, that Akanteng was a subject stool of Asamankese and that the Asamankese Stool owed allegiance to Akye Abuakwa as the paramount stool. The plaintiffs claimed that up to 191 the forest land in dispute had been unoccupied and that even though present at August 17, the court should have struck out the suit, as in Rule 10, instead of proceeding with the hearing of the defendants' plea.

In my opinion this is not a sound argument. In my opinion the date fixed for the hearing after its return from the Court of Appeal was not a " return day." In my opinion each suit has one return day, namely that day on which the plaintiff summons the defendant, by 'writ of summons issued by the court, to appeal' and answer the claim

and on which it becomes known if the plaintiff intends to prosecute his suit and if the defendant intends to defend it. All other dates are either hearing dates to which the matter is adjourned in the presence of the parties or which are fixed by the court and of which the parties are notified by hearing notice served upon them.

In my opinion this appeal should be dismissed.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

KORSAH J.A. I concur .

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D .
