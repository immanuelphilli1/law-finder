Practice-Magistrates' courts-Sale under order of court-Dispute over ownership of property sold-Objection heard after sale completed-Juris­diction of magistrate to hear objection-Courts Ordinance, c. 4, Sched. 2, .Ord. 45, rr. 25 and 26 .

In October, 1952, a vehicle and trailer were sold, at the instance of the defendants, by public auction in execution of a decree. Two days before the sale took place the plaintiffs, who claimed an interest in the vehicle and objected to the sale, filed interpleader proceedings before a District :Magistrate. The two interpleader suits were consolidated but were not heard until after the sale had taken place, when the magistrate held that the sale should not be impeached. The procedure (which was correctly followed by the plaintiffs) for objecting to a proposed sale, is contained in the Courts Ordinance, c. 4, Sched. 2, Ord. 45, rr. 25 and 26. The rules require the court to investigate such an objection as if the objectors had been made a party to the original suit: they make no provision as to investigation of an objection after a sale has taken place. On appeal,

Held, an inferior court, such as a magistrate's court, has no juris­diction other than that which is expressly conferred upon it. As the rules confer upon a magistrate jurisdiction over claims or objections to a proposed sale in execution of a decree only up to the time the sale takes place, it followed that the magistrate was acting in excess of his jurisdiction in hearing an objection after the sale had taken place.

Falkingham v. Victorian naillcay Commissioners ([1900] A.C. 452, P.C.) applied.

Woodroffe and Ameer Ali's Civil Procedure in British India, p. 970, quoted with approval.

Our law on the subject is contained in Ord. 45, rr. 25 and 26, of Schedule 2 to the Courts Ordinance. The material parts of which for the purposes of this appeal read as follows:-

Rule 25: "In the event of any claim being preferred to, or objection offered against, the sale of lands, or any other immove­able or moveable property which may have been attached in execution of a decree, or under any order for attachment made before judgment, as not liable to be sold in execution of a decree against the judgment debtor, the court shall, subject to the proviso contained in the next succeeding rule, proceed to investigate the same with the like powers as if the claimant had been originally made a party to the suit, and if it shall appear to the satisfaction of the court that the land or other immovable or movable property was not in the possession of the party against whom execution is sought, or of some person in trust for him, ... the court shall make an order for releasing the said property from attachment .... "

" The claim or objection shall be made at the earliest oppor­tunity to the court; and if the property to which the claim or objection applies shall have been advertised for sale, the same may (if it appears necessary) be postponed for the purpose of making the investigation mentioned in the last preceding rule:

Provided that no such investigation shall be made if it appear that the making of the claim or objection was designedly and unnecessarily delayed, with a view to obstruct the ends of justice, and in such case the claimant shall be left to prosecute his claim by a regular suit."

Order 21, rr. 58-61, of the Indian Code of Civil Procedure is a similar enactment to our Order 45, rr. 25 and 26, and it is of con­siderable interest to find that the courts in India have decided:­" that the order of the court after the claim of the objector has been investigated must be made before the sale has taken place, • for upon the sale the application terminates ipso facto. " ...

tp. 970, Woodroffe and Ameer Ali's Civil Procedure in British India.

I accordingly find that the magistrate had no jurisdiction to try the issue of ownership after the sale had taken place.

In the consolidated actions, the subject of this appeal from the judgment of the Divisional Court dated July 23, 1954, the plaintiffs are pressing their claims for damages in respect of the lorry and trailer by means of a regular suit. It follows from the fact that the magis­trate had no jurisdiction in the circumstances stated to adjudicate as to the ownership of the lorry and trailer that the plaintiffs are not estopped by virtue of the magistrate's decision from prosecuting their claims, and that the judge erred in dismissing the plaintiffs' action on the plea of res judicata raised by the defendants.

I would therefore allow the appeal, set aside the judgment of the court below and remit the action to the Divisional Court, Cape Coast, for trial on the merits.

The plaintiffs will have the costs of this appeal and the costs in the Divisional Court on the issue of res judicata, to be taxed.
