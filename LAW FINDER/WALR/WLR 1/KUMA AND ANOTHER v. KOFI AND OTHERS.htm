                                                  February 14, 1956

Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra

                     KUMA AND ANOTHER v. KOFI AND OTHERS

                                                           Lingley J.

 

.

Native law and custom-CustOm4TY pledge of land-Customary right of pledgor or his family to redeem at any time.

The plaintiffs brought this action in a Native Court claiming an order for the redemption of land alleged by them to have been pledged to the defendants some years before the institution of the action. The defendants resisted the claim on the ground that the land was sold to them and not pledged. The Native Trial Court found in favour of the plaintiffs but the Native Appeal Court reversed the decision and entered judgment for the defendants. The plaintiffs appealed from that decision to the Land Court, Accra.

Held, (i) that the judgment of the Native Trial Court in favour of the plaintiffs was based on findings of fact and that the Native Appeal Court was not justified in reversing that decision. pg 129

(ii) By Native law and custom a pledge can be redeemed at any time, and lapse of time is no bar.

(iii) The performance by the original pledgor and his successors of. the custom of keeping alive the pledge by introducing themselves to the pledgee's successors must be held to amount to evidence of the continued validity of the pledge, but failure to perform that custom cannot be allowed to invalidate the existence of a pledge, if its existence can be proved by some other evidence.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The principle of Native law and custom that lapse of time is no bar to the right of a pledgor to redeem his pledged property is stated in Agbo Kofi v. Addo Kofi (1933) 1 W.A.C.A. 284.]

ApPEAL from a decision of the Asogli Native Appeal Court given on August 4, 1955, reversing a decision of the Adaklu Native Court" B " given on April 7, 1955, in favour of the plaintiffs.

Ollennu for the plaintiffs. Apaloo for the defendants.

LNWLEY J. In this case the plaintiffs were claiming to redeem land in a dispute concerning whether the first plaintiff's predecessor had pledged to a predecessor of the first defendant. The defence was that the first plaintiff's predecessor had sold the land outright. The first plaintiff gave traditional evidence of the pledge and also evidence of an arbitration at which the first defendant's father appeared. It is not suggested that this arbitration was binding. The plaintiffs, however, rely on admissions by the father of the first defendant that the dispute was about the amount for which the land was pledged: that it was for a full keg of gunpowder and not for a half keg of gunpowder. 'fhe first plaintiff's evidence is that at this arbitration it was never suggested by the defendants' side that there had been a sale.

The first defendant in evidence endeavoured to prove that there was an outright sale, a sale performed with the proper customary incidents. The Native Trial Court accepted the first plaintiff's evidence and were not prepared to accept two criticisms of the plaintiffs' case put forward by the defendants. The first of these was that a predecessor of the first plaintiff had a dispute with a predecessor of the first defendant, a dispute that resulted in the first defendant's predecessor being liable for costs; that the latter did not pay these costs, and that therefore the first plaintiff's predecessor issued a fi. fa. to sell this land. This was conduct that, if it were true, was alleged to be quite inconsistent with the story of the plaintiffs' side that this was land that had been pledged by tbem. Tbe second point of criticism was tbat tbere bad been proceedings in the District Commissioner's Court which the plaintiffs had lost. The trial court dealt with both these points; accepted the plaintiffs' evidence and rejected the defendants' evidence as to the performance of the custom surrounding a sale. The trial court gave judgment for the plaintiff. pg130

The Native Appeal Court upset this decision. First they felt the criticisms as· to the performance of the custom of sale were not justified; secondly they relied on a ·custom whereby a pledgor and his line must notify the pledgees' family whenever a member of it in possession of the land dies; a notification to keep the pledge alive. For these reasousthey allowed the appeal.

It is established law that a pledgor can redeem his land after any lapse of· time~ . Ho\v8ver much one may feel that, with changing coilditions, there ought to be some limitation of this doctrine it is a well established principle and it is not open to this court to depart from that principle.

One may certainly be tempted to regard this customary require­ment of notification as a convenient way of limiting claims for redemption after very long periods.

In a case before van Lare J., in which he gave judgment on . January 31, 1953, he heard the evidence of two linguists of the Asogli State. One linguist gave evidence in these terms:-

"He said it is true that according to custom whenever a pledgee in possession dies the pledgor or his successor sends a rum or customary notification to the successor of the pledgee to remind him of the pledge lest it is forgotten. This is a means of reviving the pledge between respective survivors. "

One has therefore to decide whether that custom can operate to defeat the right of redemption of a pledgor who proves his case in other ways: in this case, by the admissions of the nrst defendant's predecessor that there was no dispute about the fact of the pledge but the only dispute was as to the amount of gunpowder to be paid by way of redemption.

In my opinion a custom of this sort peculiar to this one state cannot reasonably be held to defeat the rights of the pledgor to redeem.

I do not think that the custom referred to is anything more than the usual attempt of systems of law that had no writing to endeavour

I do not think it can be regarded as affecting the continuing validity of a pledge.

 I do not think it will be equitable to allow a pledgee to rely on the breach of this custom to defeat the rights of redemption.

  In looking at the evidence of these linguists, one notes that when they are dealing with the custom as to sale they place great reliance on the fact that the consent of the chief is necessary. In the case at present before the court, the defendant was unable to produce such evidence. "

  I appreciate that the onus was not, of course, on the defendant, but I think it is material to note this defect in his case when one of the grounds on which the Native Appeal Court acted ·was a criticism of the trial court's conclusions as to the evidence put forward by the defendant in an endeavour to prove that the transaction was one of pg 131 sale. As one has indicated, one is compelled to feel some reluctance in allowing redemption after such a length of time but' there was evidence on which the trial court could find tHat this was a pledge. I therefore feel that the appeal should be allowed. I may say that I find it difficult to understand why the second plaintiff was a party and why the co-defendant was joined. However, the trial court dealt ,with the matter as a straight issue between the first plaintiff and the defendant.

Appeal allowed: judgment of the Native Appeal Court set aside: judgment of the trial court restored; the plaintiff to have taxed costs in the Native Appeal Court and costs in this court.

Appeal allowed.

E. A.-A.
