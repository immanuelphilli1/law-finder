                                             West African Court of Appeal, Gold Coast Session 

                                                   NKAWIE STOOL v. KWADWO AND OTHERS                

                                                      Coussey P.,Korsah J.A. Baker Ag.J.A

 

 

  Practice-Judgment non-suiting plaintiff with leave to bring fresh action­ Appeal from judgment-Whether .judgment interlocutory or nor Whether or not special leave to appeal required.

     This action concerning land reached the Land Court, Kumasi, on appeal from a judgment given in favour of the defendants. The Land Court varied the judgment of the court of first instance by non-suiting the plaintiff with liberty to bring a fresh action. From this judgment the defendants appealed to the Court of Appeal. Here the preliminary point was taken by the plaintiff that the judgment of the Land Court did not finally dispose of the dispute between the parties, and that it was therefore an interlocutory order only. As special leave to appeal is required in the case of interlocutory orders (by the West African Court of Appeal Ordinance, c. 5, s. 3 (3», and as this special leave had not been obtained, the appeal should, so argued the plaintiff, be struck out.

    Held, an order which dismisses the plaintiff's action but without prejudice to his right to bring another action does not finally dispose of the rights of the parties and therefore is an interlocutory and not a final order. As such, special leave is necessary before it can be appealed against to the Court of Appeal.

      Isaacs & Sons v. Salbstein ([1916] 2 K.B. 139, C.A.) applied. Samodua and Another v. Konadi and Another (W.A.C.A., March 29, 1954, unreported) distinguished.

Altrincham Urban Council [1903] 1 K.B. 547, C.A., as: "Does the judgment or order, as made, finally dispose of the rights of the parties?" APPEAL from the decision of Benson J., given in the Land Court at I\:umasi on November 22, 1£155, setting aside the judgment of the Asantehene's Court given on October 26, 1£154, and varying the judg­ment of the Kumasi Municipal Court" B " given on September 2,,) 1954, by ordering that the plaintiff be non-suited with liberty to bring a fresh action.

Hay/ron-Benjamin for the plaintiff. Ollennu and Prempeh for the defendants.

BAKER Ag.J.A. At the outset of this appeal, Mr. Benjamin raised the following preliminary objection (due notice having been served):

"Because of the judgment being interlocutory, the appeal herein should have come by way of special leave, which has not been obtained, consequently the appeal herein is not competent before the court."

Mr. Benjamin referred us to the relevant part of the judgment, i.e., paragraph 15, which is as follows:-

"I accordingly set aside the judgment of the Asantehene's Court and vary the judgment of the }{umasi Municipal Court " B " by substituting for the order giving judgment for the defen­dants an order that the plaintiff be non-suited with costs for the defendants and co-defendant, with liberty to plaintiff to bring a fresh action."

Mr. Benjamin submitted that the said judgment did not finally determine the rights of the parties and therefore was interlocutory. He referred to Salaman v. Warner (1), and to the ruling of Lord Esher in Standard Discount Company v. La Grange (2), at p. 71, which, on the whole, Lord Esher thought to be the best rule for determining these questions, and which is as follows:-

" No order, judgment, or other proceeding can be final which does not at once affect the status of the parties, for whichever side the decision may be given; so that if it is given for the plaintiff it is conclusive against the defendant and if it is given for the defendant it is conclusive against the plaintiff."

Mr. Ollennu for the appellant argued at some length that the judgment determined the rights of the parties and was final as to the claim there adjudicated upon, and he cited the case of Nana Yaw Samodua Sampahene and Another v. Nana Hwabena Honadi VI Sumahene and Another (3). pg 243

In that case the judge of the Land Court had set aside the judgment and ordered a new trial, and a preliminary objection was taken on the appeal that it was an interlocutory judgment and that special leave ought to have been obtained.

This court ruled that as the Land Court has set aside the decision of the Native Court without considering the merits of the appeal and ordered a new trial, the case fell within section 4 (2) of c. 5 and therefore special leave to appeal under the joint operation of sections

.26, c. 4, and 3 (3) of c. 5, was not necessary.

• The distinction between that case and the present one is that then

·   the appeal was from a judgment setting aside the decision of the Native Court, whilst in the present matter, the appeal, as Mr. Ollennu agreed, is from that part of the judgment which gives liberty to the plaintiff to bring a fresh action, that being the only part of the judgment to which the appellant takes objection as in other respects it is in his favour.

On consideration of the cases already referred to and also of Isaacs d'; Sons and Sal/Jstcin and Anothcr (4), where it was held that an order for a new trial is an interlocutory and not a final order, I have no doubt that in this case the order giving liberty to bring a fresh action is interlocutory, and special leave to appeal was necessary.

This not having been obtained the appeal is not properly before the court. I would therefore strike out the appeal with costs.

COUSSEY P. I concur.

KORSAH C.J. I concur.

Appeal struck out.

S. G. D.
