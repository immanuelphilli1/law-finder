                                                                         February 22, 1956.

                     Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra

                                         KATAI AND ANOTHER v. KATAI AND OTHERS AND ABODIN.   

                                                                              van Lare J.

 

 

( (), February 22, 1956.)

Native Law and Custom-Succession on death-Claim for accounts by "six­cloth" children of deceased intestate-Deceased a native of Gar-No share of deceased's estate allotted to children.

This was a claim by some of the children of a Ga man who died intestate against the other children of the deceased for an account of rents which had accrued from a house which formed part of the deceased's estate. The children were" six-cloth" children of the deceased intestate and as such were entitled by Ga native customary law to a share of their deceased father'!,! estate. The children without having had any share of the estate allotted to them had assumed possession of the house in question and had arranged among themselves that some of the children should collect the rents accruing from the said house for and on behalf of all the children. Those of the children who had been authorized by the other children to manage the house and collect the rent therefrom did not render any account of the rents so collected and this action was brought by the aggrieved children against their other brothers and sisters for an account. The head of the family on her application was joined as co-defendant. The issue then was whether the children, although "six-cloth" children, were in the circumstances entitled to the house as their share of their father's estate and therefore to an account of the rents that had accrued therefrom.

Held, (i) that as "six-cloth" children the plaintiffs and the original defendants were entitled to a share of their deceased father's estate.

(ii) That the share to which such children (i.e. "six-cloth" children) were entitled was to be determined by the head of the family and that a portion of the estate representing that share should be allotted to the children by the head of the family. pg 152

     (iii) That in this case no such determination or allotment had been made and that the children could not therefore maintain the action.

Nelson v. Nelson (1932) 1 W.A.C.A. 215 distinguished.

Obiter: that, following the decision in Vanderpuiye v. Botchway (W.A.C.A. unreported, .March 8, 1951), the interest to which "six­cloth" children are entitled amounts to " a means of support" and that this does not entitle them to appropriate to themselves a specific portion of the estate such as would entitle them to call for a partition of the esta te.

(iv) The head of a family, acting on behalf of the family, alone can sue or be sued in respect of family property.

Laryea v. U. T. C. Ltd., Selected Judgments, Divisional Court, 1929­1931, 122, applied.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It is an established principle by Ga native customary law that the children of "six-cloth" marriage, as distinct from the children of other forms of marriage among the Gas, occupy a different position as far as inheritance on the death intestate of their father is concerned. By the Native customary laws of the Gold Coast the self-acquired property of a deceased Gold Coast African dying intestate becomes family property. What the constituents of the "family" are differ among the various tribes of the Gold Coast. Among the Gas as among the Akans the "family" in this context is traced through the maternal branch of the deceased's family. While children of other forms of marriage among the Gas have no interest in their deceased father's estate beyond the right to live in their father's house quamdiu se bene gesserint (i.e., during good behaviour), the children of a "six-cloth" marriage have a share in their deceased father's estate.

There is however no rule of Ga native customary law which provides what such share is or should be; it is a matter for the head of the family to decide. The law on this subject is exhaustively dealt with in Solomon and Vanderpuye v. Botchway (1943) 9 W.A.C.A. 127) the locus classicus on the subject. Since this case, however, the West African Court of Appeal has placed a novel interpretation on what constitutes the interest to which these children are entitled. This arose in a case concerned with the same estate as Solomon and Vanderpuye v. Botchway(1943) 9 W.A.C.A. 127. The later case was Vanderpuye v. Botchway, W.A.C.A .. March 8, 1951, unreported, and the interpretation contained in it has been reported in TV ellington v. Quartey Papafio and A nothe?', W.A.C.A. April 4, 1952, unreported, to which van Lare J. refers in his judgment. In Vanderpuye v. Botchway, W.A.C.A. March 8, 1951, the West African Court of Appeal held that the interest of " six-cloth" children in the share allotted to them "has been held" to amount to a means of support only and does not entitle the children to call for a partition of the estate, as family property is "indivisible." No authority was however cited by the West African Court of Appeal in support of its dictum "it has been held" and the judgment of the court has since been set aside on appeal to the Privy Council. The judgment of the Privy Council (dated April 23, 1956) was not based on a consideration of the merits of the statement of the principle of Native customary law contained in the judgment of the West African Court of Appeal but it is doubtful whether that principle, as so stated, can still be considered binding. The further issue regarding the liability of the head of a family to account to members of the family in respect of family property in his or her possession does not appear to be material to the decision in this case. That the head of a family is not accountable to members of the family is, of course, an accepted principle of ,Native customary law. But" six-cloth" children, who do not belong to pg153 their father's family, have been treated differently. They have been held to be entitled to an account from the head of the family in respect of their share of their father's estate, and the decision in this case regarding the non-accountability of the head of the family can only be justified on the ground that no share had at the date of the issue of the writ been allotted to the children. It seems clear that when such a share is allotted to the children they will be entitled to an account from the head of the family in respect of that share as from the date of their father's death.]

ACTION by the plaintiffs for an account, commenced in the Ga Native Court" B " and transferred to the Land Court on May 11, 1954.

Bossman for the plaintiffs.

Bentsi-Enchill for the defendants and co-defendant.

VAN LARE J. This suit originated in the Ga Native Court" B " by summons, which is as follows:-

" The plaintiffs are two of the children of the late Atta Tettey Boateng alias Katai of Kokompe, Accra, whose House No. D160/4 was allotted and given by his sister and successor to all the children of the deceased including both the plaintiffs and the defendants.

" By arrangement between all the children, the three defen­dants were appointed caretakers to administer the property by collecting the rents accruing from same and defraying rates and other expenses and Reserving the balance of such rents collected for distribution among the children.

"The defendants since about 1945 when they entered upon the administration have not paid any portion of the rents to the plaintiffs and refuse to account to the plaintiffs.

" The plaintiffs therefore claim:

(1) An Order for accounts to be submitted by the defendants of their administration of the property and all rents collected therefrom from 1945 up to date.

(2) An Order for payment to the plaintiffs of their share of whatever amount may be found to be in the hands of the defendants for distribution among the children of the deceased."

The case found its way through the Magistrate's Court constituted by the Government Agent, Accra, to the Divisional Court, Accra, and pg 154 from there to this court. The co-defendant applied and was joined at the earliest stages of the proceedings before the Native Court. I find and accept her as. the present head of the family of the late Atta 'rettey Boateng alias Katai, and the person who holds the property, the subject-matter of the suit, in trust for herself and other members of the family including the plaintiffs and the original defendants ..

I am satisfied that the house, the subject-matter of the suit, was a self-acquired property of the late Atta Tettey Boateng, alias Katai, on whose death intestate it became family property. He was suc­ceeded by his sister Oyebah who became head of his family; and OJ?- her death the co-defendant, a daughter of Oyebah and the surviving niece of the owner of the property, succeeded to the headship of the family.

The plaintiffs are lawful children of their late father begotten by their mother, Attiah, who was married according to the Ga native custom known as " Buer Kpa "(Six-Cloth). The heads of the family had at all times respectively acknowledged the interest of all the children however born in this their late father's property, and the plaintiffs and their other brothers and sisters have rooms allotted to them in the premises. It appears that there are some tenants paying rents totaling about £6 a month and the claim in this action relates to such rent accruing.

The basis for the plaintiffs' claim as it appears from paragraph 4 of their Statement of Claim is as follows:-

"4. Some time after the premises became family property, the deceased's sole surviving sister and successor according to Native Customary Law convened a family meeting at which she declared that she acknowledged the interest of all the children, including the plaintiffs and the original defendants, in the premises, and that having regard to her condition she wished to transfer the management to such of the children as can be approved by the rest for them to take charge of, manage the same and collect the rents to maintain the premises and distribute the net proceeds from time to time amongst all the children. ,.

In the first place let us examine the interest which the plaintiffs and the original defendants can have in the property according to Ga Native customary law. In this connection I cannot do better than rely on what Coussey J .A. said in delivering the judgment of the West African Court of Appeal on April 4, 1952, in the case E. D .• J. Wellington, etc. v. E. Quartey Papafio and Another (1), as follows:-

" That aspect of Ga customary law which deals with the rights of children has frequently been before that court. It was con­sidered and the case thereon reviewed in the recent decision of this court in Mary Vanderpuye and Others v. Mary Akua Botchway (2). From that decision, we deduce the following principles: - pg 155

(1) In the case of intestacy the self-acquired property of the deceased vests immediately on his death in the family as family property.

(2) The native customary law is that only the children of a Ga six-cloth marriage are entitled under Ga custom to a share of the self-acquired property of their deceased father. (3) That share has been termed an interest but it amounts only to a right of support out of the estate.

(4) The exact share or interest which the child gets is not laid down. It depends upon the decision of the head of the family assisted by other members of the family. If he thinks he has been unjustly treated, a child has the right to say so.

(5) The members of a family are traced through the maternal ancestor, and the family is the unit for the purpose of ownership of property. All the members have a joint interest in the family property which is indivisible.

(!)) Being indivisible, there can be no apportionment in the sense of the alienation of a particular part of the estate to the children which would have the effect of severing the family property, unless the parties agree to that course."

I have no evidence that this house was ever allotted and given to all the children of the late Katai as alleged in the writ of summons, nor is there any satisfactory evidence in support of the plaintiffs' allegation as contained in paragraph 4 of their statement of claim. The evidence of the first plaintiff, and that of a man called Robert Ayiku, himself not a member of the family, on this allegation are in conflict and must be rejected as unsatisfactory and unimpressive. What has emerged on the evidence as a whole is that after the death of Oyebah, and during the headship of the present head, the co-defendant, the children of the late Katai, labouring under the erroneous impression of the Ga Native customary law of succession, assumed exclusive ownership of their late father's property. They arranged between themselves and assumed the management and collection of the rents accruing over the head of the co-defendant. I find that this agree­ment was without the knowledge and consent of the co-defendant, the head of the family. Some of the children were appointed caretakers and an account opened at the Post Office. I am not satisfied that the defendants or any of them were or was lawfully appointed caretaker in respect of this property. Such adverse claim, together with the attempted dealing with the family property by the plaintiffs and their other brothers and sisters to the exclusion of other members of their late father's family, constitutes a transgression according to native customary law; in my view the plaintiffs and the other children concerned render themselves liable to forfeit their interest in the family property. pg 156

The parties, that is to say the plaintiffs and the original defendants, are in pari delicto, and as the cause of action appears to arise ex turpi causa or from a transgression of the law, the plaintiffs cannot be assisted by the court. Furthermore the plaintiffs as children are not competent to institute this action in view of the well-established and recognized principle that the head of the family alone can sue and be sued as the representative of the family respecting claims on the family possessions. It has been many times laid down by the courts that the head of the family in whom the property vests in trust for the rest of the family is the proper person to bring actions with respect to the family property. (Laryea v. U. T. C. Ltd. (3) at p. 125.)

Even if, as it does appear on the evidence, some members of the family are collecting rents from the family property without accounting for the same, it is for the co-defendant, as head of the family, to take an action for account, and not the plaintiffs, as in this matter. This case is distinguishable from Nelson v. Nelson (4), where a deceased person before his death appointed, by the death-bed disposition (Simansiw), one of his sons to look after the interest of his brothers and sisters, the appointment not constituting appointment as head of the family but merely as a "caretaker" of the family property, and as such the son was held liable to account to his brothers and sisters for all the proceeds of the family property coming into his hands. It is significant that in that case the property was in fact granted or given by the deceased father to his children; but in this case the property still remains vested in the head of the family and was not proved to have been transferred or allotted to the children.

I am disposed to think that upon the head of the family being joined as a co;defendant in the case the plaintiffs should have dropped their action; but they did not. Their statement of claim was filed as against the co-defendant as well claiming accounts and for an Order against her to pay to the plaintiffs what may be found due to them. On the authorities the head of a family is not liable to account; that being so the plaintiffs' action, which has been fought to this bitter end, is in my view misconceived. In J. A. Pappoe v. Tetteh Kweku (5), the law has been laid down as follows:-

" The appellant does not attempt to assert that Ga law imposes on the head of the family an obligation to account which is unknown to Fanti law, but he argues that English law applies. In my opinion the learned Chief Justice was justified in finding that native law applies, and that it is a general principle of native law that the personal property of a deceased head of a family passes to his successor (subject to certain obligations to support the other members of the family), and that an action of accounting is unknown to native law. It may be asked what remedy a member of a family has if the head refuses to give him anything. The answer would appear to be that although, in the pg 157 words of Sarbah, he cannot ' claim an account from the head of the family or call for an appropriation to himself of any special portion of the family estate, or income therefrom arising,' yet he has a certain claim upon the head of the family for support-the extent of support varying with the family means-and I assume that he could enforce this by action.

In my view the rents accruing from this property become personal property and ought to pass to the co-defendant, the present successor -of the original owner and the head of the family, who is not account­able. In the result I would dismiss the action in favour of the defendants and co-defendant.

I must, however, record that the assessor, Mr. S. D. Codjoe, dissents from this judgment. In his view the action properly lies .against the co-defendant as head of the family to account to the plaintiffs because "the co-defendant has utterly failed in her duties according to Native Custom and as she can be removed from office for lack of management she is accountable in the circumstance to the plaintiffs and must therefore give account of her stewardship." I am unable to accept the opinion expressed by the assessor. The courts should not by any subtlety of reasoning or by any sense of morality attempt to weaken the well-established doctrines of the law which have long been acted on by the public and their advisers.

There will therefore be judgment for the defendants and co-defen­dant. The defendants are to bear their own costs; but the co-defendant shall have her costs.

Judgment for the defendants.

E. A.-A.
