                                                                    February 17, 1956

                     Supreme Court of the Gold Coast, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, Accra 

                                                      YIADOM AND ANOTHER v. DAKWA. 

                                                                           van Lare J.

 

pg147

Land-Claim for declaration of title-Land in joint possession of plaintiff and defendant-Binding effect of customary arbitration-Inactivity of defendant at Forest Reserve Inquiry-Plea of estoppel by acquiescence.

This was an action brought in the Native Court" A" of the Kwahu State sitting at Abene for a declaration of ownership to a piece of land lying between the stool land of the plaintiffs and that of the defendant. The case of the plaintiffs was that the land in dispute was within the area of land belonging to their stool and that at an arbitration held by certain chiefs of the state some years before a boundary was demarcated between the two stool lands and that the land in dispute fell within the area adjudged to be the property of the plaintiffs' stool.

In or about 1935 a portion of the land in dispute was reserved under the Forest Reserve Ordinance, but this area had been released by the Forest Reserve Authorities and rendered free for cultivation. The plain­tiffs further contended in support of their claim that the area of the forest reserve had, prior to its being reserved, been sold to one Asafoatse Nimo and that at the Inquiry held by the Forest Reserve Settlement Commissioner under the Forest Reserve Ordinance the said Asafoatse Nimo was an alienation claimant, but the defendant's stool which was a party to the Inquiry did not challenge the claim made by the said

      Asafoatse Nimo.                                                                     '

Upon the release of the area for cultivation the defendant's subjects entered on the land and started to make farms thereon. This action was brought to eject the defendant's subjects from the land.

The trial Native Court entered judgment for the defendant declaring that the land in dispute was the common property of the two stools. From this judgment the plaintiff appealed to the Land Court, Accra, and raised for the first time the plea of estoppel referred to above.

Held, (i) that there was sufficient evidence before the trial Native Court to justify its finding that the land was the common property of the two stools.

(ii) That the plea of estoppel by acquiescence failed.

Case referred to:

(1) Willmott v. Barber (1880) 15 Ch.D. 96.

Bossman for the plaintiffs. Alwfo-Addo for the defendant.

VAN LARE J. This is an appeal from the decision of the Kwahu Native Court" A " (Abene), sitting at Nkwatia, giving judgment for the defendant in a suit in which the claim is as follows:-

" Plaintiffs' claim is for: the declaration of the right, title, interest and ownership of:

(a) All that piece or parcel of land with forest land thereon and bounded on one side by Bepong Stool land by River Eyekyenflmon, thence to Worobong  River; one side by pg 148 Akyekyesua stream forming boundary with Kwahu Tafo Stool land; and the other side by Asensefi-Kyekyenamon foot-path; i.e. going from Asensefi to Kyekyenamon River the left for plaintiffs' stool land and the right for defendant's stool land;

(b) One hundred pounds (£100) damages from the defendant to show cause why defendant should let or lease part of claim (a) Le. Forest Reserve area acquired by Government and marked B.P.20 and released to plaintiffs upon their application to farmers for cultivation without the know­ledge and consent of the plaintiffs as the owners thereof.

( c) Perpetual Injunction restraining defendant, his agents. servants, tenants and/or lessees from entering into the land in dispute until final hearing and determination of this dispute."

It is conceded that the contest is between the Asakraka Stool represented by the plaintiffs, and the Bepong Stool represented by the defendant. The Asakraka Stool claims the land under dispute as its exclusive property; but the Bepong Stool says it is a joint property of their two stools. Both stools are neighbours and " brothers"; their subjects occupy adjoining lands on the left and right respectively of the area under dispute. The finding by the trial Native Court that " historically, it is noted on the record that both Bepong and Asakraka Stools are brothers who live on a piece or parcel of Kwahu State land within the Nifa Division" has not been challenged. I should point out that a ground of appeal that this view is wrong has not been argued but rather abandoned by Mr. Bossman for the appellants.

There appears to be some disagreement between Mr. Bossman and Mr. Akufo-Addo, for the defendant, as to what in their respective opinions is the area claimed and/or the disputed land in the suit. It is true a plan would have made such an issue unnecessary. However it does appear to me on the evidence that the disputed land lies between what may be supposed to be Asakraka Stool lands on the left and Bepong Stool lands on the right. As I understand the case, the Asakraka Stool in effect attempts to seek a declaration of the court that there is an ancient and recognized boundary between the two stool lands, and that the Asensefi-Kyekyenamon foot-path, i.e. from Asensefi to Kyekyenamon river, leading to Akim Abuakwa State but ending in the Kyekyenamon stream "is the correct boundary between" the Asakraka and Bepong Stool lands. It is the case for the plaintiffs that all the lands to the left of this foot-path are Asakraka Stool lands, and that all the lands to the right of the foot-path are Bepong Stool lands. The defence denies the existence of such a boundary or any other between the adjoining lands of their two stools. My impression is that an issue became joined as to whether or not there is or had been any known boundary between the adjoining stool pg 149 lands; and if so, whether it is the foot-path as alleged by the plaintiff stool.

There is no doubt as to what is the trespass area as alleged, and this is the forest land recently released from the Worobong Forest Heserve portion of which the Bepong Stool has also permitted his subjects to till for cultivation following similar permission by the Asakraka Stool to its subjects.

This forest land is on the left of the alleged boundary, i. e., the footpath, and is included in the lands claimed by the Asakraka Stool. But the Bepong Stool says, as it were, that there is a no man's land between their adjoining stool lands, and that this no man's land containing the trespass area is the joint property of their two stools.

Mr. Bossman for the plaintiffs has argued with characteristic force that the land claimed is only confined to the trespass area, that is to say the forest land recently released from the \Vorobong Forest Heserve.

The Asakraka Stool instituted this action because the Bepong Stool claims a joint ownership in respect of the trespass area which is included in the lands as claimed by the Asakraka Stool as its exclusive property. It is my view that the plaintiffs must have to prove exclu­sive ownership of all the lands claimed by them, and I uphold Mr. Akufo Addo's contention that the subject-matter of the suit is not confined to the trespass area. It must be pointed out that the area claimed appears to be well defined in the writ of summons; and paragraph "b" thereof clearly states that the trespass area is only " part" of the area claimed. I do not think that the trial Native Court had any doubt as to the land the subject-matter of the suit. The trial court visited the locus and in the absence of anything to the contrary it must be presumed that there has been no uncertainty as to the subject-matter of the suit.

The simple questions are: (1) has the Asakraka Stool proved its exclusive ownership of the area claimed? (2) has it established any boundary to exist between their adjoining stool lands? (3) does the evidence justify the finding that the disputed land is joint property of both Asakraka and Bepong Stools and also that no boundary ever existed or had been fixed between the two adjoining lands?

In my view the Asakraka Stool has failed to establish its claim; on the balance of probabilities the version of the Bepong Stool as to the result of an arbitration, affecting the material questions in the present case before the late Nana Kwasi Nyarko, Nifahene of Kwahu, and his subordinate wing Chiefs, was rightly accepted by the trial Native Court. There is ample evidence in support of the findings of the trial Native Court on the material facts of the arbitration. The present case appears to have been fought and decided upon what was the result of the Nyarko Arbitration between the parties some time in year 1928-1929. pg150

On the facts Mr. Bossman has argued that the trial Native Court should have accepted the plaintiffs' version. I am not sitting as a trial court; as a Court of Appeal I cannot set aside a finding of fact except for clear and convincing reasons. I am aware that I am nevertheless not debarred from coming to my own conclusions on the facts, and that I am entitled to make my own mind on the printed evidence; but after a full consideration of the facts I am unable to come to the conclusion that the judgment appealed from is wrong. There is, for instance, the question of credibility or reliability of the witnesses who have not given their evidence before me; and the question is not one as to what is the proper inference to be drawn from the proved facts with the result that I am not in any better position to evaluate the evidence which appears in the record. I have not discovered any misdirection or unsatisfactory reasons for the decision of the trial Native Court, and I would dismiss the ground that the judgment appealed from is against the weight of evidence.

I now come to deal with Mr. Bossman's additional ground of appeal which is as follows:-

" That the defendant is estopped by the finding of Mr. Beeton etc., from disputing the plaintiffs' claim of title in respect of the area in dispute; or alternatively by his conduct in relation to this land during the course of the Worobong Forest Reserve Enquiry. The estoppel is by record or alternatively by acquiescence."

There cannot be much force in this ground of appeal, as I. have held that the disputed land is not confined to the forest land released from the Worobong Forest Reserve. I have allowed and received further evidence at the hearing of the appeal. There is evidence which I accept that the forest land, i.e., the trespass area, which is only a portion of the area claimed, had been previously sold to one Asafoatse Nimo by the Asakraka Stool and this was prior to the Worobong :Forest Enquiry. At the Enquiry the Bepong Stool did not put forward its claim of joint ownership to the land concerned; the alienation claimant was put forward by the Asakraka Stool only. that should be so because it was the Asakraka Stool from which Asafoatse Nimo obtained his land. Relying on the claim of joint ownership the Bepong Stool might well have conceded the right of the Asakraka Stool to deal with their joint property in that manner. In my opinion this evidence might add some weight to the case of the Asakraka Stool but it cannot operate as estoppel by acquiescence which will deprive a man of his legal rights. Such acquiescence, it has been held, must amount to fraud. I am not disposed to hold that such conduct of the Bepong Stool during the Worobong Forest Reserve Enquiry was fraudulent. The elements or requisites necessary to constitute fraud of the descrip­tion amounting to acquiescence to deprive a man of his legal rights can be found in Willmott v. Barber (1), at p. 105, and those elements are not present in this case. pg 151

As to estoppel by record I have no special arguments addressed to me how the judgment of the Enquiry estops the Bepong Stool from asserting a claim of joint ownership. There has been no adjudication against the Bepong Stool in favour of the Asakraka Stool in that judgment. In my view the additional ground also fails, and this disposes of the appeal.

It is unnecessary to decide the point raised by Mr. Akufo-Addo as to the effect of another alleged arbitration before the Obomenghene and two others on the matter in controversy between the parties, the award of which arbitration the Asakraka Stool appears to resile before instituting the present action.

In the result I confirm the decision of the trial Native Court; the appeal is accordingly dismissed with, costs.

Appeal dismissed.

E. A.-A.
