                                                  Supreme Court of the Colony of the Gambia  

                                  N'DOW v. SAGNIA AND OTHERS.

                                                                                  Miles J.

 

 

Sale by Order of Court-Failure to comply with the order of the court­ Conveyancing Act, 1881, s. 70.

The plaintiff and the first defendant were tenants in common of a certain property and in 1954, the plaintiff commenced an action for partition. The Supreme Court found partition to be impracticable and ordered a sale, prescribing that each tenant was to have an option to purchase, and that in the event of neither tenant having exercised the option within three months, the property should be sold by public auction within two months thereafter. The order was made on February 16, 1955, and as neither tenant exercised the option before May 16, 1955, the property was in due course put up for sale by public auction. The auction was held on August 20, 1955, more than one month after the final date prescribed by the order, and the property was purchased by the fourth defendant. The second and third defendants were respectively the solicitor acting for the first defendant and the auctioneer who sold the property. The plaintiff sought to have the sale set aside on the grounds that the order of the court had not been complied with. The fourth defendant counterclaimed for the execution of a conveyance to him of the property.

 

Held, the title of a purchaser, buying at a sale under an order of the court, is not to be attacked on the grounds of non-compliance with technicalities of the order on the part of the vendor.

Singh v. Singh (1863) 9 L.T. 129 applied.

Conveyancing Act, 1881 (44 & 45 Vict. c. 41), s. 70 explained.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The headnote to Colclough v. Sterum (2) reads:­"A person purchasing lands under a decree is bound to see that the directions of the decree are observed."

The point of law decided in Singh v. Singh (1), appears to have been made statutory by the Conveyancing Act, 1881 (44 & 45 Vict. c. 41), s. 70, and has been repeated in the Law of Property Act, 1925 (15 & 16 Geo. 5, c. 20), s. 204 (1). The Conveyancing Act, 1881, applies in the Gambia and section 70 reads: -

" An order of the court under any statutory or other jurisdiction shall not, as against a purchaser, be invalidated on the grounds of want of jurisdiction or want of any concurrence, consent, notice or service, whether the purchaser has notice of any such want or not."

The headnote to Singh v. Singh (1) reads:-

"After an estate has been sold by auction under a decree of court, the purchaser's title is not to be attacked merely on the ground that the holder of the decree had not complied with all the strict formalities for the conduct of such sale; as, for example, the want of due notice of sale; and the burden of proof of such compliance is not on the  purchaser."

The beneficial effect of this section is somewhat limited by Jones v. Burnett [1900] 1 Ch. 370, which holds that it was intended only to cure irregularities in procedure. As a result it may be that Colclough v. Sterum (2) remains good law, for it will apply where a vendor, purporting to sell with the authority of the court, in fact has no such authority to sell: such a sale can hardly be regarded as an irregularity in procedure. If this interpretation is correct then the remarks in Halsbury, Hailsham ed., VoL 29, p. 271 may be misleading.

In the present case the court treated the failure of the defendants to return for an extension of time after an order for sale had once been made as an irregularity of procedure within section 70 of the Conveyancing Act.]

Section 14 (2) provides that: "no sale by auction of any land shall take place until seven days after notice thereof shall have been published once in the' Gazette' ... " Section 28 (1) provides that the provisions of the Ordinance shall, inter alia, in no way apply to sales by order of the Supreme Court.

ACTION by which the plaintiff, who had been a tenant in common with the first defendant of No. 10, Hill Street, Bathurst, sought to set aside a sale of the property made under an order of the court, on the grounds that the terms of the order and of the Sales by Auction pg 185 Ordinance, 1901, relating to the publication of notice of sales of property, had not been complied with. The plaintiff also sought to recover damages against the first, third and fourth defendants for wrongful sale of the property; against the second defendant (solicitor to the first defendant) for negligence, and against the fourth defendant (the purchaser) for trespass. The facts appear in the judgment.

N'Jie for the plaintiff.

Forster for the defendants.

• MILES J. This is a further chapter in a most dismal story of family friction. The plaintiff is the half-sister of the first defendant, Francis Sagnia; the defendant Samuel John Forster is a barrister-at-Iaw and solicitor practising at Bathurst; the defendant Albert Akigbogun is an auctioneer, and the fourth defendant, Hassan Farage, the unfor­tunate innocent victim of this family dispute, is the purchaser of the property, No. 10, Hill Street, Bathurst, in a sale by public auction.

The plaintiff and the defendant Sagnia were tenants in common of No. 10, Hill Street, their shares being two-thirds and one-third respectively. Until recently they were residing in different houses in the premises but there was constant friction between them and in 1954 the plaintiff brought an action for partition of the property. The action was tried by me on February 16, 1955. On viewing the premises I came to the conclusion that partition in any form was impracticable, and I made an order that the property be sold; each party having the option to purchase the interest of the other and in the event of such option not being exercised, that the property be sold by public auction. It is this order which has given rise to the present suit. It was in the following terms:-

" I order that the property be sold, either party to have the option to purchase the interest of the other, such option to be exercised within three months of today. In the event of such option not being exercised, the property to be sold by public auction within two months thereafter."

Negotiations between the parties broke down and on April 29, Mr. Forster wrote to the plaintiff stating that he was instructed to put the property in the hands of an auctioneer for sale by public auction. The plaintiff replied on May 12, pointing out that the property could not be sold according to the court order until May 16. Nothing further happened until July 11, when Mr. Forster stated that he had contacted the defendant Akigbogun with a view to advertising the sale. The plaintiff did not reply to this letter, and on August 5, Mr. Forster again wrote stating that he had instructed the defendant Akigbogun to sell the property. The plaintiff replied on August 11, objecting to the appointment of Mr. Akigbogun and insisting that one, Mr. Francis Senega], now deceased, be appointed as a joint auctioneer with pg 186 Mr. Akigbogun. Mr. Ii'orster replied on the same day, pointing out that it was unusual to have more than one auctioneer but that the plaintiff was at liberty to have another auctioneer present at the sale to watch her interest

. In the meantime notice of the intended sale was published in the Gambia" Gazette" on August 15, the date of the sale being stated to be August 20. On August 16, the plaintiff wrote to Mr. Forster pointing out that according to the court order the sale by auction should have been effected not later than July 16. She also objected that seven days' notice of the sale must be given. This was, no doubt, a reference to section 14 (2) of the Sales by Auction Ordinance, c. 127~

Notwithstanding this warning the property was put up for sale at an auction on August 20, 1955, and was purchased by the defendant Farage for the sum of £950. The plaintiff now brings this action claiming (i) that the sale be set aside, (ii) damages against the defen­dants Sagnia, Akigbogun and Farage for wrongful sale of the property, (iii) damages against Farage for trespass and (iv) damages against the defendant Forster for negligence. There is a claim by the fourth defendant for the execution of a conveyance.

So far as the sale is concerned it is clear the court order was not strictly complied with since the sale was not effected within the time laid down. At one time this might have been fatal to the defendants' case, but it is clearly not fatal now. Section 70 of the Conveyancing Act, 1881, provides that" an order of the court under any statutory or other jurisdiction shall not, as against a purehaser, be invalidated on the grounds of want of jurisdiction, or want of any concurrence, consent, notice or service, whether the purchaser has notice of any such want or not." By parity of reasoning if an order of the court cannot be invalidated for want of jurisdiction it seems to me plain that non­compliance with some unimportant terms of the court order cannot invalidate the sale. It is obvious that if an application had been made to the court for an extension of time to carry out the sale it would have been granted as a matter of course. The defendant was quite ignorant of the provision of the court order as to time.

The tendency of the courts nowadays to protect innocent purehasers from failure to comply with technicalities of this kind is well exemplified by Singh v. Singh (1), where it was held that after an estate has been sold by auction under a decree of the court the purchaser's title is not to be attacked merely on the ground that the holder of the decree has not complied with all the statutory formalities for the conduct of the sale, as for example, the \vant of notice of such· sale. The case of Colclough v. Sterum (2), cited by Mr. N'Jie, which decided that it was the duty of the purchaser to see that the directions of the order had been observed, can no longer be regarded as good law, see Halsbury's Laws of England, Hailsham ed., Vol. 29, p. 271 (referring to the provisions of the Conveyancing Act, 1881, s. 70, and pg187 their incorporation into the Law of Property Act, 1925, s. 204 (1)) .. 'rhis disposes of Mr. N'Jie's argument based on section 14 (2) of the Sales by Auction Ordinance, 1901, for the sale was nonetheless a sale by order of the court even though it was out of time. In any case I should have held that the sale was covered by section 28 (1) (a) of the Ordinance, rather than by section 14 (2).

I now turn to the plaintiff's fourth and last claim. This is against the fourth defendant and is for damages in respect of damage caused to the premises and of the removal therefrom of certain of the plaintiff's property. There is no evidence as to who was responsible for the flemoval, and certainly no evidence that it was done by any person for whom the fourth defendant is vicariously liable. The fourth defendant admits that he put a lorry in the compound, but of course he was entitled to do so.

It follows that the plaintiff's claim fails against all the defendants.

I may say that I am extremely glad to be able to arrive at this decision since it would be difficult to conceive any claim more lacking in merit than the present. The plaintiff herself had no objection to the sale at one time provided that her own auctioneer conducted it. Moreover the parties cannot be restored to their original position since the premises have been let to tenants. Even had the claims for damages succeeded against the defendants Sagnia, Akigbogun and Forster, I would have only awarded nominal damages since the plaintiff has suffered no actual damage whatever as a result of the sale. The property would have to be sold some time whether she consented or not, and she herself admits that it was sold for a fair price. The suggested apportionment of the price between the parties as made by Mr. Akigbogun may be open to question but that does not arise in this case.

There will be judgment for the defendants with costs on the claim.

The fourth defendant's claim succeeds and there will be an order for the execution of a conveyance by the plaintiff as a joint vendor of the premises, No. 10, Hill Street, Bathurst, with costs on that claim.

   Judgment for the defendants, order for the execution of a conveyance.

S. G. N.
