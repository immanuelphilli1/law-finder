                                            November 30, 1957

                              In re KANJARGA, DECEASED. KANJARGA v. DETER.

                  High Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, Divisional Court                           

                                                     Murphy J.

 

 

Practice-Native law and custom-Reference to native court of questions­ Whether answers supplied by native court to be treated as findings of fact-Courts Ordinance, c. 4, s. 89 ..

Succession-Slavery-Probate-Intestacy-Appointment of successor to ex-slave -Position where deceased has himself succeeded to property in enslaving family.

The plaintiff sought a grant of letters of administration to the estate of the deceased on the ground that he was the eldest surviving full brother of the deceased and was therefore, by custom, the proper person to be appointed as the deceased's successor. The defendant denied the plaintiff's claim and himself sought a grant, his ground being that the deceased had been purchased (as a slave) by a member of his (the defendant's) family. The court, acting under the Courts Ordinance, s. 89, referred a number of questions as to native custom to a Native Court. The Native Court heard evidence and reported their findings to the court. Among other things they found, as a fact, that the deceased had succeeded to the defendant's grandfather and had thus inherited property of the defendant's family.

Held, (i) where, under the Courts Ordinance, s. 89, reference is made to a Native Court for the answers to stated questions on native custom, the findings of the Native Court after hearing evidence are to be regarded by the court making the reference in the manner in which an appellate court regards findings of fact by a trial court, that is to say the findings will be accepted unless they are unreasonable and unsupported by the evidence.

(ii) Although a claim to succeed to a deceased person will be rejected as repugnant to justice, equity and good conscience where it appears that the claim is based on the deceased's position as a slave, this principle will be displaced if the deceased had himself been appointed as successor to a member of the enslaving family and had thus inherit€d property in that family.

In re Demptey, deceased. Kodieh v. Affram (1930) 1 W.A.C.A. 12 distinguished.

Case referred to :

In re Kweku Demptey, Kodieh v. Affram (1930) 1 W.A.C.A. 12. [EDITORIAL NOTE. See also Yeboah v. Bonko (1956) 2 W.A.L.R. 107.]

APPLICATION for a grant of letters of administration. The defendant entered a caveat and himself claimed a grant.

Lutterodt for the plaintiff.

Siriboe, with him Adade, for the defendant.

MURPHY J. This is a dispute as to who is entitled to a grant of letters of administration in the estate of one Appieba Kanjarga alias Yaw Brenyah. In his statement of claim the plaintiff claims to be the eldest surviving full brother of the deceased and therefore the proper person to adminster the estate by the custom of the Kanjarga tribe. The defendant in his defence says that the deceased became an adopted member of his (the defendant's) family and remained so until the time of his death. In an earlier affidavit the defendant says that ~he deceased was bought many years ago by the defendant's great-grand­uncle.

Under the Courts Ordinance, s. 89, this court referred the case to a Native Court to determine the following issues:

(a) Whether or not the plaintiff being the next-of-kin and the person acknowledged by the people of the village of Ayomso as the personal representative of the deceased is the rightful person to be granted letters of administration.

(b) Whether the defendant, claiming the estate of the deceased through slavery, has a better right to administer the estate of the deceased than the plaintiff who is the next-of-kin of the deceased.

(c) Whether or not there was an adoption of the deceased by the defendant's family in accordance with native customary law.

The Native Court heard evidence and delivered an opinion in favour of the defendant. I have now heard arguments on the question of whether I should adopt or reject this finding.

The Native Court, it would appear, went into the case thoroughly and heard a number of witnesses. I agree with counsel for the defen­dant that I should at this stage apply the principles appertaining to the hearing of an appeal and should not reject the Native Court's findings of fact unless they are manifestly unreasonable and un­supported by the evidence. This still leaves open the question of whether it would be repugnant to natural justice, equity and good conscience for this court to adopt the Native Court's decision, a point to which I shall come later.

So far as findings of fact are concerned it appears to me that the only obvious criticism of the Native Court's decision is in regard to its finding in the third issue referred to it by this court. The relevant part of the decision reads:

" There was an adoption of the deceased by the defendant's family in that acceptance of " aseda " at the time of deceased's succession to Dantano and Danyansa made Buanyina (Appieba) a member of the defendant's family."

This indicates that the question of whether the deceased became an adopted member of the defendant's family depended upon the accept­ance of " aseda," but unfortunately there is no mention in the record of evidence of any of the witnesses of this acceptance of "aseda" at the time of either of the two successions referred to. It has been submitted by counsel for the defendant that .the Native Court was entitled to infer that there had been an acceptance of " aseda " from the evidence as to the successions. However that may be, I do not consider that the point is of great importance because in paragraphs (a) and (b) of their decision the Native Court had already given what appear to be adequate reasons for their view that the defendant and not the plaintiff had the better right to administer the estate, the main reason being that the deceased had succeeded the defendant's grand­father and had inherited family property.

I consider therefore that I should adopt the finding that under native customary law the defendant rather than the plaintiff is the proper person to administer the estate. It has, however, been sub­mitted by the counsel for the plaintiff that on the authority of In re Kweku Demptey, Kodieh v. Affram (1) this court should not recognise the defendant's claim since it is based on a native custom which is repugnant to justice, equity and good conscience, namely, slavery. It is true that, according to the defendant's own evidence, the deceased was originally bought as a slave. But it appears to me that the present case is distinguishable from In re Kwelw Demptey (1) since the defendant's claim does not depend upon the slavery but, according to the Native Court's finding, on the deceased having become the succes­sor to the defendant's grandfather and having inherited family property.

In deciding against the plaintiff I am also influenced by the fact that his evidence before the Native Court as to his relationship to the deceased is inconsistent with his statement of claim. In his state­ment of claim he claims to be a full brother of the deceased, whereas in his evidence he says that the deceased was his maternal uncle. His counsel has submitted that persons in this country are often vague in their description of such relationships. This may be true, but in the first three paragraphs of the statement of claim the relationship of the plaintiff to the deceased is described with considerable precision and this description is, so far as I can see, wholly inconsistent with the deceased having been the maternal uncle of the plaintiff.

For these reasons I give judgment for the defendant for the grant of letters of administration. No order as to costs, since although I have decided that the defendant has a better claim, it does not appear that the plaintiff's claim was wholly without merit.

Grant of letters of administration to the defendant.

S. G. D.
