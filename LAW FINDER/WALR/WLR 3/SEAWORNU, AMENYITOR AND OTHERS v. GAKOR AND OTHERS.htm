                                          May 29, 1957.

                                     SEAWORNU, AMENYITOR AND OTHERS v. GAKOR AND OTHERS.

                         High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court                                               

                                                                                 Ollennu J

                     

Practice-Native Courts-Refusal of Native Court to discharge order­ Whether an interlocutory matter-Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s. 51.

Practice-Native Courts-Native Appeal Court-Order for trial de novo­ Whether order an interlocutory matter-Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s. 52.

The plaintiffs commenced an action in a Native Court which, the defendants having failed to appear, heard the plaintiffs' evidence and gave judgment for them. The defendants applied to the court, under the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 51, for the decision to be discharged. On refusal they appealed to the appropriate Native Appeal Court and this court set aside the decision of the lower court and ordered trial de novo. From this decision of the Native Appeal Court the plain­tiffs appealed to the Land Court. Before the appeal the defendants took the preliminary point that the decision of the Native Appeal Court was a decision in an interlocutory matter, from which, by the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 52 (2) (a), appeal lies only to the Magistrate's Court, and, moreover, that, by section 52 (3), the leave of the Magistrate's Court must be obtained before the appeal could move from the Native Appeal Court, which leave had not been obtained. It was suggested further, that the defendants' appeal to the Native Court was an appeal not to consider the merits of the case but, simply, against the refusal of the trial Native Court to discharge its decision and, that this was thus an appeal in an interlocutory matter for which the leave of the Native Appeal Court was needed, also under sections 52 (2) and 52 (3), which leave had not been obtained.

Held, (i) an appeal under the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordi­nance, s. 51, against a refusal of a trial court is an appeal, within the ambit of sections 46, 47 and 48 of the Ordinance, that calls the judgment of the trial Native Court into question, on questions of law or fact or law and fact. It is thus not an interlocutory matter and leave to appeal, under section 52 (3), is not required.

(ii) Although the order of the Native Appeal Court was for trial de novo and thus did not dispose of the issue between the parties, never­theless it finally disposed of the matter before the Native Appeal Court and was therefore a final and not an interlocutory decision. Appeal, therefore, properly lay to the High Court.

Cases referred to:

(1) Ex parte Moore, in re Faithful (1885) 14 Q.E.D. 627.

(2) Turkson v. Amoah Ababio and Another (1950) 13 W.A.C.A. 35.

APPEAL from a decision of 'the Anlo Native Appeal Court on Novem­ber 6, 1956, ordering trial de novo on an appeal from a decision of the Anloga Native Court" B " given on July 12, 1956.

Apaloo for the plaintiffs.

E. O. Asafu-Adjaye for the defendants.

OLLENNU J. Mr. E. O. Asafu-Adjaye for the respondent, Kwabla Gakor, submitted as a preliminary objection that the order of the Anlo Native Appeal Court against which this appeal is brought is inter­locutory and therefore this court has no jurisdiction to entertain the appeal on the following grounds: (1) That under the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, s. 52 (2) (a), appeals from an interlocutory order or decision of a Native Appeal Court lies only to the Magistrate's Court and (2) that in any event special leave to appeal has not been obtained as provided under section 52 (3) of the Ordinance. I have therefore to determine first of all whether the order of the Anlo Native Appeal Court dated November 6, 1956, is interlocutory.

The history of the case is as follows: The suit was commenced in the Anloga Native Court" B " by a writ of summons issued on June 25, 1954, returnable on July 12, 1956. The defendants failed to appear and the Native Court, acting under regulation 11 of Regulations No. 10 of 1945, heard the cause and gave judgment for the plaintiffs upon their evidence.

The respondent, who is one of the defendants in ~he said suit, applied to the Native Court under the Native Courts (Colony) Ordinance, s. 51, to discharge that judgment on the grounds that: (1) he was not served with the writ of summons in the case, and (2) the three persons who constituted the court were not authorised by law to do so.

The Native Court refused the application, whereupon the respondent appealed to the Anlo Native Appeal Court, which allowed the appeal, declared the judgment of the Native Court" B " of July 12, 1954, a nullity and ordered trial de novo. It is from that order of the Native Appeal Court that the appeal to this court has been brought.

I take Mr. Asafu-Adjaye's submissions to mean that the right con­ferred by section 51 of the Ordinance to appeal upon the refusal by the Native Court to reverse, vary, or discharge, a judgment given in default of attendance by a defendant or respondent is only with respect to the order of refusal and not with respect to the judgment on the merits given in default of appearance. He submits that in its very nature the order of refusal is interlocutory.

If that were so, section 52 (2) of the Ordinance would apply to such an appeal and it could only be taken after special leave to appeal had been obtained. In that event the Anlo Native Appeal Court would have had no jurisdiction to entertain the appeal; special leave not having been granted, and their order would have been a nullity.

In my opinion an application under section 52 of the Ordinance to vary or discharge a judgment given in default calls that judgment in question either on law or fact or both law and fact, that is to say it may challenge the judgment upon its merit.

In my opinion the words " an appeal shall lie in like manner as an appeal from any other decision of a Native Court," appearing at the end of section 51, operate to restore to the defendant or respondent the right to appeal under sections 46, 47 and 48 of the Ordinance against the judgment given on the merits, of which right he had been deprived by the first part of the said section 52, by reason of his default of appearance. That appeal is from a final order and not an interlocutory order.

Mr. Asafu-Adjaye further submitted that the order of the Anlo Native Appeal Court is interlocutory because it does not finally deter­mine the issue between the parties-namely, ownership of the land raised upon the writ of summons-because it ordered retrial, keeping the writ alive.

 It is true that the order of the Anlo Native Appeal Court directs hearing or the case de novo by the Native Court" B " and therefore did not finally determine the main issue between the parties. But in my opinion that order finally determined, as far as the Native Appeal Court is concerned, the issue as to whether or not the judgment of the Native Court" B " should stand. As far as the Anlo Native Appeal Court is concerned, there is nothing more to be done by it in respect of that particular issue.

The position as I see it is exactly 'the same as a judgment given by this court or of a higher court exercising appellate jurisdiction, setting aside judgment of a lower court on the merits and ordering retrial. Such an order in my opinion is final; it does not leave anything more to be done by the court which made it in spite of the fact that the issue on the writ of summons itself remains to be determined by another court.

The finality or otherwise of a decision must be considered in relation to the court which gave it. If the decision completely determines the particular issue which the parties brought to 'that court, that decision will be final, otherwise it will be interlocutory.

In the case of Ex parte Moore, in re Faithful (1) Brett M.R. said:

" If 'the court ordered the result of inquiries to be reported to itself before the judgment was given, it would not be a final judg­ment. But if the court orders something to be done according to the answer 'to the inquiries, without any further reference to itself, the judgment is final. "

This passage was cited with approval by the West African Court of Appeal in its decision given in Turbon v. Amoah Ababio and Another (2).

In that case the 'Vest African Court of Appeal allowed an appeal brought by the plaintiff, and remitted the case to the Divisional Court to take accounts. Upon application for conditional leave to appeal against that judgment to the Privy Council it was contended that the judgment did not finally determine the matters in dispute between the parties and therefore no appeal laid as of right. The West African Court of Appeal held that there was nothing more to be done by it--­namely, the West African Court of Appeal-in the matter which came before it on appeal, and therefore their order was final, notwithstanding that there remained something more to be done by the lower court to finalise the matter in dispute between the parties.

Guided by these principles laid down in the cases I have referred to above. I have come to the conclusion 'that the order of the Anlo Native Appeal Court is final, and that the appeal is properly before the court. The objection to jurisdiction therefore fails.

S. G.
