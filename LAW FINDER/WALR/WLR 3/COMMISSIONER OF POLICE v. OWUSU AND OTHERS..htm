                                                       March 11, 1958

                       COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. OWUSU AND OTHERS.

                         High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                                Ollennu J.

 

 

Criminal Law-Evidence-Accomplice-Circumstances in which evidence of an accomplice affords corroboration.

Criminal Law-Evidence-Confessions-Statement made by accused to police­Right of cross-examination of prosecution witnesses as to circumstances in which statement was made-Whether statement evidence against other accused.

Criminal Law-Evidence-Refreshing memory from documents-Limitation on rights of defence to examine documents used by prosecution witnesses to refresh memory.

Criminal Law-Evidence-Character-Accused wrongly cross-examined as to character-Effect of admission of such evidence.

Criminal Law-Practice-Offences alleged against several persons-Power to convict although offences proved against some only of those persons.

Criminal Law - Evidence - Hostile witnesses - Circumstances in which a witness may be treated as hostile-Evidentiary value of hostile witness.

Fifteen persons, jointly tried, had been convicted by a Magistrate variously of assault and unlawful detention. On the appeal a number of objections were taken to certain proceedings at the trial. The points argued were:

(1) the wrongful admission as evidence against all the accused of a statement made to the police by the fourteenth accused upon his being charged with the offences;

(2) a wrongful refusal to allow counsel for the fourteenth accused to cross-examine as to the circumstances in which he made that statement to the police;

(3) a denial to defence counsel of the right to see documents which prosecution witnesses had used to refresh their memory;

(4) the wrongful treatment of some prosecution witnesses as hostile j (5) the wrongful cross-examination of some of the accused as to character;

(6) that the charges were bad by reason of the inclusion in the parti­culars thereof of the names of some persons as complainants who gave evidence that they had not been assaulted or detained.

Held, (i) the rule that a court should not convict upon the uncorrobo­rated evidence of an accomplice, applies only to evidence given by an accomplice called as a witness for the prosecution. It does not apply to evidence given by an accomplice in his own defence, or as a witness for his co-accused.

(ii) Where inadmissible evidence has been wrongly admitted, the court

Will quash the conviction, unless it holds that the evidence so admitted cannot reasonably be said to have affected the mind of the court (or jury) in arriving at the verdict.

(iii) Though a statement made by an accused to the police may have been properly admitted in evidence, the accused has a legal right to cross-examine after its admission, with a view to eliciting facts which will diminish its weight and value against him. Denial of that right deprives him of the opportunity of putting his full defence before the court, and in the circumstances of this case the conviction of the four­teenth accused could not stand.

(iv) Though the defence in criminal cases should be allowed very wide latitude, it would be dangerous to lay down a general principle that they must be shown any document in the hands of the prosecution, simply because a prosecution witness has used it to refresh his memory on any point, however trivial.

(v) Even when an accused has been wrongly cross-€examined as to his character, an appellate court will not on that ground allow his appeal if it considers that a reasonable jury, properly directed on the admissible evidence, would certainly have convicted. Though the case against the twelfth appellant was very strong, yet it could not be said with certainty that the Magistrate was not influenced by evidence of his previous con­viction. His appeal must therefore be allowed.

(vi) If there is clear evidence that an accused has committed the offence named in the charge (e.g., assault) against some only of the persons named therein, he can properly be convicted of the offence.

(vii) The effect of treating a witness as hostile is to render his evidence nugatory.

Cases referred to:

(1) R. v. Adebowale and Others (1941) 7 W.A.C.A. 142.

(2) R. v. Murray [1951] 1 K.B. 391; 34 Cr.App.R. 203; [1950] 2 All E.R. 925; 66 T.L.R.(Pt. 2) 1007.

(3) R. v. Harris (1927) 20 Cr.App.R. 144.

(4) Stirland v. D.P.P. [1944] A.C. 315; 113 L.J.K.B. 394; 171 L.T. 78; 60 T.L.R. 461; [1944] 2 All E.R. 13; 30 Cr.App.R. 40.

APPEAL from decisions of the District Magistrate, Accra, on Novem­ber 5, 1957, convicting the appellants variously of assault and/or un­lawful detention.

N.arayan for the appellants.

Battcock, Solicitor-General, for the Crown.

OLLENNU J. summarised the seven submissions made by counsel for the appellants, and proceeded: I shall deal briefly with the sub­missions of learned counsel both for the appellants and for the respon­dent on each of these points.

On the first point, Mr. Narayan for ~he appellants referred the court to a passage in the judgment which reads as follows:

" The fourteenth accused did not give evidence, but made a statement to the police on the 28th August, 1957. This statement agrees with the story of Henry Thompson and his three friends even down to details; the fourteenth accused apparently made a clean breast of everything. Although he is an accomplice, his statement is amply corroborated, and I accept it."

Counsel submitted that that passage from the judgment shows that the learned District Magistrate misdirected herself on the statement, and wrongly accepted it as evidence against all the other accused. Mr. Battcock, on the other hand, submitted first that the passage has reference only to the fourteenth accused, and not to any other accused; and secondly, that in any event the passage makes no sense, and has not been shown to have affected the conviction of any of the accused other than the fourteenth.

A statement made by an accused to the police in the absence of other accused persons who are later charged jointly with him, is evi­dence only against himself; it is not evidence against any of the other persons charged jointly with him. Moreover, the rule of practice (which has become almost a rule of law) that a court should not con­vict upon the uncorroborated evidence of an accomplice applies only to evidence given by an accomplice called as a witness for the prosecu­tion. It does not apply to evidence which one defendant gives in his own defence, or as a witness for other defendants, and which incriminates other accomplices charged jointly with him or with the person on whose behalf he gives the evidence (R. v. Adebowale and Others (1) ).

The observation in the judgment of the trial Magistrate that the statement was amply corroborated, and that it was accepted by the Magistrate, can mean only one thing-that the Magistrate regarded the statement made by the fourteenth accused to the police as evidence against all the other defendants. In so doing, the learned Magistrate erred. Where inadmissible evidence has been wrongly admitted, the court will quash the conviction, unless it holds that the evidence so admitted cannot reasonably be said to have affected the mind of the court or jury in arriving at the verdict (see Archbold, 33rd ed., p. 343, para. 569, and the cases there cited). I have therefore ,to exclude that statement from consideration of the case against the other accused, when I come to deal with them.

On his second point, learned counsel referred the court to the pas­sages in the proceedings which show that after the statement of the fourteenth accused had been admitted without objection, defence counsel sought to cross-examine prosecution witnesses on the circum­stances in which the statement was made, and that objection was taken to it by counsel for the prosecution, the objection being upheld by the learned Magistrate. Crown counsel conceded that counsel who conducted the prosecution in the court below was wrong in taking the objection, and that the court erred in upholding the objection.

There is no doubt that the expression used by defence counsel at the trial, that he would cross-examine to show that the statement was obtained from the fourteenth accused "under duress," misled both prosecuting counsel and the court into the erroneous idea that once a statement has been properly admitted it should be taken as conclu­sive. Admissibility of evidence and value of evidence are two dif­ferent things. An accused may not be able to show that a statement made by him to the police upon his being charged is inadmissible, but he may bring out facts in cross-examination, and in his own evidence­in-chief, which may considerably diminish the effect of a statement or alleged confession which has been properly admitted according to all the rules of evidence. It is one thing to admit a statement, it is another thing to decide what weight or value to attach to such a state­ment or confession. It is by cross-examination that the value and weight of evidence, both oral and documentary, is tested. It is there­fore a serious denial of justice to refuse a defendant his legal right to cross-examine generally, or on a statement he makes to the police (R. v. Murray (2)).

The Magistrate's refusal to allow the fourteenth accused's counsel to cross-examine on the statement, denied to that accused the opportu­nity of trying to convince the court, if he could, that the said state­ment should not carry much weight. He could not be said, .therefore, to have had the opportunity of putting his full defence before the court. That is so even though he refused to give evidence on his own behalf. Sitting here as a court of appeal, I am unable to say what weight the learned trial Magistrate might have attached to that alleged confession of the fourteenth accused if defence counsel had cross-examined upon it. I am therefore bound to exclude that statement from consideration of the case against the fourteenth accused also.

In evaluating the evidence left on the record against the fourteenth accused, it must observed that even with the alleged confession (con­sidered together with that other evidence) the learned Magistrate did not feel sanguine enough to convict the fourteenth accused of any of the offences with which he stood charged. She convicted him only of aiding and abetting assault. I cannot say that she would necessarily have convicted him even of aiding and abetting assault, if his counsel had by cross-examination been able to show that the alleged confession was not of much weight as a piece of evidence in the case. In those circumstances the conviction of the fourteenth accused cannot stand.

As to his third point, learned counsel for the appellants submitted that he applied through the court to be shown documents which wit­nesses for the prosecution had used to refresh their memories, but his application was refused. He submitted that he was entitled to see those documents, and he referred the court to the following passages at pages 524 and 525 of the 33rd edition of Archbold:

•• If a witness refreshes his memory from entries in a book, counsel may cross-examine on those entries-without making them his evidence, and the jury may see them if they think fit, but if counsel cross-examines as to other entries in the same book he makes them his evidence .... If the witness has not referred to any book in his evidence for the purpose of refreshing his memory, and has merely admitted in cross-examination that he did make a note, he cannot be compelled to produce the book."

Counsel submitted that upon seeing the documents in question he might perhaps have been able to find something in them which he could have successfully used for the benefit of the accused persons, or any of them. Counsel submitted, therefore, that the refusal to show him those documents has resulted in a miscarriage of justice.

In reply to this submission learned Crown counsel contended that while it would have been proper to show some documents to defence counsel, it is not a general rule that defence counsel is entitled to be given any document which is shown to a prosecution witness to refresh his memory. Crown counsel submitted that in this particular case there has been no miscarriage of justice.

There are two instances (shown in the proceedings in the court below) which counsel for the accused complained of in this regard. The first is in respect of the fourth prosecution witness, who gave his evidence-in-chief without refreshing his memory. He was cross-examined in a way suggesting that it was in his evidence at the trial that he for the first time stated certain facts, and that he had said some other things at another trial. When the witness denied the suggestion made in cross-examination, he was shown in re-examination a statement which he made to the police shortly after the occurrence of the inci­dents he deposed to, and he was referred to passages in it. The second instance is in respect of a written statement which the twelfth prose­cution witness had used to refresh his memory as to time.

Prosecuting counsel refused to show the first document to defence counsel when the latter called for it, but offered to give the second one to him. Defence counsel, however, refused to have the second one unless prosecuting counsel gave an undertaking that he would give him the former document also. Defence counsel did not cross-examine the fourth prosecution witness upon the passage read out to him from the documents which he had used to refresh his memory.

While the defence in criminal cases should be allowed very wide latitude, I think it would be dangerous to lay down a general principle that they must be given any document in the hands of the prosecution simply because a prosecution witness uses the document to refresh his memory on any point, however irrelevant or trivial. In this case, the passage which the fourth prosecution witness used to refresh his memory in re-examination was read out in open court. Defence counsel could have cross-examined upon that passage without neces­sarily seeing the document; but as already pointed out, he did not do so. In any event the second document was offered to counsel, but he would not have it. In those circumstances his complaint on this point is, in my opinion, not a fair one. That refusal has not occasioned any miscarriage of justice, and therefore cannot affect the decision in this case.

The submission of learned counsel for the appellants that the twenty-first and twenty-third prosecution witnesses were wrongly treated as hostile witnesses was conceded by the Crown counsel. The latter submitted, however, that no miscarriage of justice resulted from the mistake of the learned Magistrate in granting leave to prosecuting counsel to treat those witnesses as hostile.

It is difficult to see on what grounds the witnesses were allowed to be treated as hostile. It is strange, too, that statements which such witnesses agreed they had made to the police were admitted in evidence though the witnesses had not denied the truth of the contents. More­over, the contents of those statements do not in any way contradict the evidence the witnesses gave in court. I cannot see upon what principle of law those witnesses were allowed to be treated as hostile, or upon which their previous statements were admitted in evidence. The effect of treating a witness as hostile is to render the evidence given by that witness nugatory (R. v. Harris (3)). The witnesses who were wrongly treated as hostile were witnesses called in advance to rebut the defence of alibi, of which some accused had given notice. Therefore, had the evidence given by any of those witnesses materially supported the defence of alibi of the particular accused in respect of whom they gave their evidence, I would, without the slightest hesitation, have allowed the appeal of those appellants. But upon careful study of the evidence given by those witnesses I am satisfied that that is not the case.

I pass now to the fifth point made by counsel for the appellants, that some of the accused were wrongly cross-examined as to their character and history, and that the whole proceedings were thereby vitiated. Counsel for the appellants referred to the particular accused whom he alleged were so cross-examined. Crown counsel submitted that the cross-examination complained of cannot be regarded as being of a nature calculated to bring out bad character, or previous criminal history. He further submitted that evidence given by the twelfth accused in cross-examination as to his previous conviction might have been volunteered by the accused; that suggestion, he submitted, is supported by the observations of the learned Magistrate that "this witness gave the impression that he was enjoying himself in the witness box, and did not expect anyone to take him seriously; he spoke with his tongue in his cheek."

Learned Crown counsel submitted that even if the accused had been cross-examined as to their character, it does not appear on the record that the trial Magistrate was in any way influenced by that evidence against the particular appellants so cross-examined.

I must say that, with the exception of the cross-examination of the twelfth accused, I can find nothing in the cross-examination of any of the accused which shows, or from which it can be inferred, that the cross-examination was aimed at the character of the accused. But even when an accused has been wrongly cross-examined as to his character, an appellate court will not on that ground allow his appeal if it considers that no substantial miscarriage of justice has actually occurred, and that a reasonable jury, if properly directed on the evi­dence properly admissible, would, without doubt, convict (Stirland v. D.P.P. (4)). With the exception of the cross-examination of the twelfth accused, I am satisfied that, when properly directed upon the evidence properly admissible, a reasonable jury would without doubt convict.

In the case of the twelfth accused, however, I hold a different view.

It appears to me that prosecuting counsel deliberately cross-examined him to bring out his previous criminal history. If the twelfth accused himself had volunteered the evidence about his prevous conviction, or had given that evidence accidentally, prosecuting counsel would not (or should not) thereupon have elicited answers such as .the following: " I learnt to sew in the prisons. I did not have time to learn to cut before I left." This piece of evidence creates the impression that prosecuting counsel planned his cross-examination deliberately to bring out the defendant's criminal history. As soon as this evidence of previous conviction came out, the learned Magistrate should, in my opinion, have stopped the case as against the twelfth accused, for his case to be tried separately by another magistrate.

I must say that on the facts the evidence against the twelfth accused is very strong. None of the other accused has evidence against him which can be said to be stronger than that against the twelfth, yet I cannot say with certainty that in spite of that very strong evi­dence the mind of the learned Magistrate was not influenced by the evidence of his previous conviction. With the evidence of his previous conviction on the record, I cannot say that, by convicting him, justice has been made manifestly to appear to have been done. Thus, in spite of the very strong evidence against him, I feel that there has in the circumstances been a miscarriage of justice by the conviction of the twelfth accused.

 

I shall deal now with the point made by learned counsel for the appellants that since the name of Attoh Quarshie, the fifth prosecu­tion witness, appears in each of the two charges as having been assaulted and detained, and he himself gave evidence that he was neither assaulted nor detained, the charges should have been dismissed against the appellants. I cannot accept such a proposition. If there is clear evidence that the appellants (or some of them) assaulted and detained some only of the persons named in the charge, they can properly be convicted of the offences.

Finally, learned counsel for the appellants submitted that the con­victions were unreasonable, and that there are discrepancies in the evidence led on behalf of the prosecution. In so doing he made pre­cisely the same points, as appears from .the record, that he had raised in the court below at the close of the case. On this last ground learned counsel for the defence himself frankly conceded that quite a few of the appellants could, upon the evidence, properly have been convicted upon the charges.

I have gone through ~he record of proceedings with meticulous care as I am bound to do. As a result of my study of the record and of the arguments advanced on behalf of the appellants and of the respondent, I have come to the conclusion that there is sufficient evi­dence on the record, which, if believed, would amply justify the convictions. This evidence the trial Magistrate believed. It is not open to me to interfere with the convictions in those circumstances.

Before concluding I cannot help observing that many of the criti­cisms which counsel for the appellants levelled against the proceedings were well founded. It is difficult to believe that both the prosecuting counsel and the presiding Magistrate did not realise ~hat they were glaringly giving cause to the defence for complaint against the pro­ceedings. But, of course, it must also be remembered, as was observed by the Court of Appeal recently, that even the most able of judges may err in material respects. I wish to repeat that in our administra­tion of justice, no effort should be spared both by the Bench and Bar, to make it manifestly appear that we hold the scales blind-foldedly even between parties.

For the reasons stated above, I allow the appeal of the twelfth and fourteenth accused, quash their convictions and sentences, and enter a verdict of not guilty in respect of each of them. Each is acquitted and discharged. I dismiss the appeals of each of the other appellants.

Appeals of twelfth and fourteenth appellants allowed.

Remaining appeals dismissed
