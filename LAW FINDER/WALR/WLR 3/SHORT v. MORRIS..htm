                                                         January 31, 1958.

                                                       SHORT v. MORRIS.

 High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

Adumua­Bossman J.

                                                 

.

Contract-Specific perf01·ma.nce-Sale of land-Note or memorandum required by Statute of Frauds-Agreement complete save as to price-Statute of Frauds, 1677, s. 4.

Practice-Jurisdiction-Law to be applied-Meaning of "native" in Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 87 (1).

The plaintiff, who was a native of Sierra Leone, sought specific performance of an agreement relating to the sale of land. He adduced in evidence a document signed, inter alia, by the defendant and in the following terms:

"We the undersigned, G. B. Arthur-Johnson and Winnefred Arthur-Johnson alias Mrs. W. Morris, hereby acknowledge receipt of the sum of £97 by way of advance on the purchase price of our joint property at J. P. Brown Street which we have agreed to sell to him after agreement on the purchase price which Mr. J. B. Short has put at £850 as against our demand for the sum of £1,000."

It appeared in evidence that the plaintiff subsequently offered, orally, to pay the £1,000 demanded by the defendant but this was refused by the defendant.

Held, (i) a native of Sierra Leone is not a native within the contem­plation of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4, s. 87 (1), so that the transaction fell to be regulated by the requirements of English law, as opposed to native law and custom.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. In Kaney v. U.T.G., Ltd. (1934) 2 W.A.C.A. 188, referred to by the learned judge, the question of the applicability or otherwise of English law was dealt with by Graham Paul J. at p. 194:

" Where the parties to a cause or matter are natives the onus is upon the party who opposes the application of 'such native customary law' to satisfy the court that it should not be applied. In causes and matters between natives and non-natives the onus is upon the party seeking to apply 'such native customary law' to satisfy the court' that substantial injustice would be done to any party by a strict adherence to the rules of any law or laws other than native customary law.' "

In practice difficulty seldom arises for it is usually apparent from the form of the transaction what law was intended by the parties to apply.]

(ii) On the evidence, including that of the memorandum adduced by the plaintiff, no complete agreement was ever reached between plaintiff and defendant. There was thus nothing that could be enforced by the court.

(iii) Even if agreement had ultimately been reached between plaintiff and defendant as to price, this was not disclosed by the memorandum, which, to satisfy the requirements of the Statute of Frauds, s. 4, must disclose all the material terms of the agreement.

Cases referred to:

(1) Kaney v. U.T.G., Ltd. (1934) 2 W.A.C.A. 188.

(2) George v. Afari and Another (1929) Div.Ct. 1926-1929, 195.

(3) Lever v. Koffler (1901) 1 Ch. 543; 70 L.J.Ch. 395; 84 L.T. 584.

ACTION for specific performance of an agreement to sell land. The defence was that no concluded agreement had ever been reached and, that if it had, it was not evidenced by a note or memorandum in writing as required by the Statute of Frauds, 1677, s. 4.

The plaintiff appeared in person.

Benjamin J. for the defendant.

ADUMUA-BOSSMAN J. The plaintiff by his writ claims specific per­formance of a contract to sell to him land in Cape Coast, which contract he alleges the defendant has refused .to complete. The plaintiff being a non-native, a Sierra-Leonean, and the defendant a native, it is clear that the law properly applicable between the parties or which regulates the obligations, if any, arising out of their alleged transaction, is English law. See the Courts Ordinance, s. 87 (1), and Kaney v. U.T.C.

(1).

In his statement of claim the plaintiff averred in paragraphs 5 and 6 as follows:

 The defendant on the 12th March, 1955, called on the plaintiff with her brother and two witnesses, one Alice Deheer and one Kwesi Assam. The defendant and her brother agreed to sell their joint property to the plaintiff for a price between £850 and £1,000, if the plaintiff would redeem the said property due to be sold the same day. "

To succeed to his claim, by the provisions of English law which the plaintiff himself concedes to be applicable to the case, he had to prove not only that there was a concluded agreement, i.e., one in respect of ~vhich the parties were ad idem on all the material terms, but more important still, to establish the existence of the agreement or contract, by a note or memorandum in writing signed by the party to be charged, as required by the Statute of Frauds, s. 4, which, it is settled, is one of the English Acts of general application in force in this country, see George v. Afari (2), and which provides ~hat the contract shall be un­enforceable" unless the agreement on which the action is brought or some note or memorandum thereof shall be in writing and signed by the party to be charged. "

The plaintiff, a practitioner of the court, accepting that position, gave oral evidence and called one witness as to the circumstances leading to the signing of the written agreement on which he founds his claim. The terms of this all-important memorandum are as hereunder:

" We the undersigned, G. B. Arthur-Johnson and Winnefred Arthur-J ohnson alias Mrs. Morris hereby acknowledge receipt of the sum of £\)7 (ninety-seven pounds) by way of advance on the purchase-price of our joint property at J. P. Brown Street which we have agreed to sell to him after agreement on the purchase price which Mr. J. B. Short has put at £850 as against our demand for the sum of £1,000."

The important difference between the terms of this memorandum which clearly and unambiguously state " we have agreed to sell ... after agreement on the purchase price" and the averment in paragraph 6 of the statement of claim that " the defendant and her brother then agreed to sell for a price between £850 and £1,000 " should be noticed. In my opinion the memorandum does not evidence a concluded agree­ment to sell even at a price to be ascertained. It states clearly and unequivocally that a price was to be agreed on, as the offer of the defendant and her brother at £1,000 had not been accepted but on the contrary a counter-offer of £850 had been made which in turn had not been accepted. However it is the plaintiff's main contention that the offer of £1,000 was in effect kept open indefinitely for him to accept, and that in fact he communicated his acceptance to the defendant the following Saturday after the signing of the memorandum. He has referred me to, and relied upon, the case of Lever v. Koffier (3) where it was held that verbal acceptance of one of two alternatives in a written and signed offer is sufficient to constitute an enforceable contract. It is to be observed, however, that the memorandum relied on in that case, contained the following important words: "The owner agrees to the following proposal which is firm and open for your consideration up to Thursday next at 12 noon." By comparison, the plaintiff's memoran­dum contains no words indicating that the rejected offer of £1,000 was kept open for consideration or acceptance within a reasonable time. On the contrary, it is specific that the parties could not agree on price, and that agreement should be reached thereafter. On the plaintiff's own showing when he next saw the defendant and then told her he was prepared to buy at the £1,000 originally asked for by her, she told him she had had a dream and ,the property was not to be sold.

I am satisfied therefore that there was never any concluded con­tract to sell, i.e., one on which the parties had agreed and were ad idem as to the price on March 12, 1955, or at any time thereafter; that even if there was, there has not been produced a memorandum thereof sufficient to satisfy the Statute of Frauds; that in so far as the plaintiff relied on the memorandum he was, as indicated by me at the commencement of the hearing and upon examination of the copy of that memorandum attached to the statement of claim, bound to fail in his action. I accordingly dismiss the plaintiff's claim and enter judgment for the defendant.

Judgment for the defendant.

S. G. D.
