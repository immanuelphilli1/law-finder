                                                     October 17, 1958

                                                REGINA v. ABALEY AND ANOTHER.

                                                           Court of Appeal

                           Korsah C.J., van Lare and Granville Sharp JJ.A

 

 

Criminal Lau,'-Practice-Trial upon information-information preferred after withdrawal of charge at reopened hearing-Subsequent proceedings a nullity-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10, ss. 202 and 203.

Criminal Law-Practice-Appeals-Power of Court of Appeal to order trial by court of competent jurisdiction-Court of Appeal Ordinance, c. 35 of 1957, s. 11 (5).

The appellants had been convicted at assize for offences in connection with an unlawful trial by ordeal. They had originally been committed for trial after preliminary investigation before a Magistrate and, as required by the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 199, an authenticated copy of the depositions, statement and answer had been transmitted to the Attorney-General. The Attorney-General, being of opinion that no prima facie case was disclosed, had returned the depositions to the Magis­trate, who thereupon, and in accordance with section 202 of the Code, had reopened the case as if there had been no committal for trial. At the fresh hearing the prosecution had sought leave to withdraw the charges. This had been granted and the appellants discharged. Subse­quently it appeared that the Attorney-General revised his first view, deciding that the depositions disclosed a prima facie case, and presum­ably purporting to act on the provisions of section 203 of the Code, pre­sented an information based on the depositions. It was upon this information that the appellants were arraigned, tried and convicted at assize. On the appeal it was accepted by the Crown that the proceedings on assize were a nullity for want of jurisdiction but it was argued that the Court of Appeal Ordinance, 1957, s. 11 (5), empowered the court to order that the appellants should be tried by a court of competent jurisdiction.

Held, where proceedings are a nullity through" want of jurisdiction or otherwise" the Court of Appeal Ordinance, 1957, s. 11 (5), allows an order for a trial by a court of competent jurisdiction to be made. This contemplates a defect, in the course of proceedings that have been properly instituted, through want of jurisdiction of the court before whom they are brought, or otherwise. Where no proper proceedings have effectively been commenced, there is no court of competent jurisdiction to which a reference can be made, and this therefore is a situation to which section 11 (5) has no application.

Cases referred to:

(1) Motayo v. Commissioner of Police (1950) 13 W.A.C.A. 4.

(2) R. v. Zenvinula and Others (1946) 12 W.A.C.A. 68.

ApPEAL from convictions by Simpson, J. on April 2, 1958, at Accra Assizes convicting the accused of offences under the Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 465 (2).

Crabbe, Senior Crown counscl, for the Crown. Koranteng Addo for the appellants.

The following judgment of the court was given by Korsah C.J.

The appellants were convicted at the Criminal Assizes held at Accra on Wednesday, April 2, 1958. The first appellant was convicted on a count stating that he directed an unlawful trial by ordeal, contrary to the Criminal Code, s. 465 (2), and the second appellant was convicted on a count stating that he and others took part in an unlawful trial by ordeal contrary to the same section. The others who were charged together with the appellants were also convicted, but did not appeal. The first appellant was sentenced to six months' imprisonment with hard labour, and the second to three months' imprisonment with hard labour .

Each of the appellants applied for leave to appeal against his conviction on several grounds, but at the hearing counsel wisely abandoned them because they were not such grounds as could have induced the court to grant the leave sought. Counsel, however, obtained leave to argue a supplementary ground of appeal, contending that the Assize Court which convicted the appellants had no jurisdiction to try the appellants; consequently the judgment against which the appeal is lodged should be declared a nullity, and accordingly set aside.

The short history of the case is that the appellants and others were duly charged with the offence under section 465 (2) as already stated, before a District Magistrate, who after the preliminary investiga­tion committed all the accused persons, including the appellants, on November 8, 1957, for trial to the Supreme Court as required by the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 186. It would appear that the depositions and other relevant documents were duly transmitted to the court before which the trial was to be held, and an authenticated copy transmitted to the Attorney-General as required by the Criminal Procedure Code, s.199.

By a letter from the Attorney-General's office dated November 8, 1957, and addressed to the District Registrar, Magistrate's Court, Accra, the writer states inter alia:

"In view of the report of the chemist and the evidence disclosed in the depositions, I am of the opinion that there is no sufficient evidence to warrant the committal of this case.

"I therefore return the depositions in this case and request the court to deal with the case in accordance with the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 202."

The section reads:

" If the Attorney-General shall be of opinion that there is not sufficient evidence to warrant a committal for trial before the Supreme Court, he may cause the depositions to be returned to the court which committed the accused and such court may thereupon re-open the case and deal with it in all respects as if such person had not been committed for trial as aforesaid; and if the case be one which may suitably be dealt with by such court it may be so tried and determined accordingly. "

The case was subsequently listed for hearing at the District Magis­trate's Court on November 26, 1957. The record of the court made on that date reads as follows:

" Mr. Codjoe stated he has received a letter from the Attorney­General returning the case but in view of the contents of the substance he cannot proceed with the charges before the court. He therefore asks for withdrawal of the charges .

•• By court: Leave granted. Each accused discharged."

It is obvious from this that the accused persons in whose case the depositions were taken, having been discharged, were thereafter under no obligation to appear for trial at the Supreme Court as they had undertaken to do. Furthermore, no information purporting to be based upon the depositions could be filed against the appellants.

In a letter from the Attorney-General's office dated January 10, 1958, addressed to the Assistant Commissioner, C.LD., and referring to the same case, it is stated:

"This case has been further considered and information is being filed to put the accused persons on their trial at the Accra Assizes .••

A copy of the letter was forwarded to •• District Registrar," District Magistrate's Court, Accra, with the following note:

•• Ref. my letter 42/S.F.1/Vo1.9/406 of November 8, 1957, will you please return the depositions in the above named case."

There is no record of any fresh proceedings having been initiated against the appellants after they were discharged on November 26, 1957, nor is it disputed by the Crown that the appellants were arraigned for trial on any other depositions than the previous depositions which had been returned and dealt with by the Senior District Magistrate according to law. Nevertheless" Information No. 37/58 for April 1958 •. was filed, and the appellants were arraigned, tried and convicted at the Criminal Assizes held at Accra on April 2, 1958.

The information was obviously filed without legal authority. The jurisdiction of the Supreme Court (Assize Court) to entertain criminal cases for trial is derived from the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 49, and the legal authority by which the Attorney-General is enabled to file an information in cases committed to the Supreme Court is derived from section 203 of that Code.

Crown counsel did not dispute the above facts as stated nor did he contend that the court of trial in the circumstances stated herein could properly exercise jurisdiction. He, however, contended that he relied on the decisions in Mataya v. Commissioner of Police (1) and R. v. Zenvinula and Others (2), and argued that this court should exercise its powers under the Court of Appeal Ordinance, 1957, s. 11 (5), to order a trial by a court of competent jurisdiction.

\Ve refused to accede to this request because in our view the rule upon which the request is made does not permit the court to make such order in this case. The Court of Appeal Ordinance, s. 11 (5), reads:

" Where the Court of Appeal is of opinion that the proceedings in the trial court were a nullity either through want of jurisdiction or otherwise, the court may order the appellant to be tried by a court of competent jurisdiction."

The power conferred on the court by this section clearly pre-supposes a case commenced by due process of law but wrongly adjudicated upon by a court other than the court of competent jurisdiction. In such a case, if the proceedings in the trial court are a nullity either through want of jurisdiction or any other cause, the appellant has never been tried, and it is clearly the duty of this court to exercise its powers under the section to order the appellant to be tried by a court of competent jurisdiction.

As has been stated the information filed against the appellants herein was without legal authority, and not in accordance with the due process of law enabling any existing court to try them; consequently there is no proper charge against them triable by any court which could be said to be a court of competent jurisdiction. In the case of Mataya v. Commissioner of Police (1), cited by counsel, it will be observed that the order for re-trial was made in pursuance of the provisions of section 43 of the Magistrate's Courts (Appeal) Ordinance of Nigeria specifically enabling the judge of the trial court to order a re-trial. In the case of R. v. Zenvinula and Others (2), there again the case was lawfully instituted in the court of competent jurisdiction but the trial was declared a nullity by reason of the irregularity in the proceedings. Therefore in that case there was a court of competent jurisdiction to which it could be referred.

   For these reasons we allowed the appeal, quashed the convictions and set aside the sentences.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
