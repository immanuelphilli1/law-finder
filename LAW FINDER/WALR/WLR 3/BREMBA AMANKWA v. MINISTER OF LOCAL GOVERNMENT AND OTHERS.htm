                                               October 14, 1957

    BREMBA AMANKWA v. MINISTER OF LOCAL GOVERNMENT AND OTHERS

High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court 

                                                                  Windsor-Aubrey J.

 

Constitutional -  Law-Pubic officers-Appeals commissioners and committees of inquiry appointed under State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952-Whether pubic officers-Interpretation Ordinance, c. 1, s. 3 (35) (now Interpretation Act, 1957, s. 3).

Practice-Prerogative writs-Actions against pubic officers-Judicial- Division in which application to be brought.

The applicant had been elected Odikro of Barko, in Breman Asikuma.

State. Objection had been taken to the election by certain persons (who appeared as interested parties in the present case) and this objection was made the subject of an inquiry by the state council under the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 5. The state council found in favour of the election and the objectors thereupon appealed to the Governor-General under section 11 of the Ordinance. The Governor-General, under section 12, appointed an appeal com­missioner and assessors for the purpose of hearing the appeal. The commissioner, on hearing the appeal, made certain recommendations, one of which was that a committee of inquiry should be set up to investigate the customary procedure for election to the Barko Stool. Following upon this recommendation the Governor-General, purporting to act under section 8 of the Ordinance, appointed the same appeal commissioner and assessors to constitute a committee of inquiry to hold such investiga­tion. The applicant then sought a writ of prohibition to restrain the committee from so acting on the ground that the matter with which they were concerned had already been determined by the state council; that section 8 gave no power to appoint a committee in such circumstances; and, that the purported appointment was therefore ultra vires the Governor-General.

At the hearing the preliminary point was taken that the Divisional Court of the Eastern Judicial Division had no jurisdiction to hear the application. It was argued that, in essence, the application was an action against a public officer and thus, under Order 5, r. 1 (b) should have been brought in the Judicial Division in which the cause of action arose-in this case the Central Judicial Division.

Held, (i) appeals commissioners and members of committees of inquiry, appointed under the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance are public officers within the meaning of the Interpretation Ordinance (now the Interpretation Act, 1957).

(ii) The original cause of action, in the sense of being the cause from which the present application stemmed, was the original proceedings before the state council. These proceedings were within the Central Judicial Division and therefore, under Order 5, r. 1 (b), it was within that Division that the application should have been made.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The point as to whether or not an application for a prerogative writ is an "action" within the meaning of Order 5, r. 1 (b) was not canvassed.]

APPLICATION for leave to issue an order of prohibition to restrain a committee of inquiry appointed in pursuance of powers conferred by the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 8.

E. o. Asafu-Adjaye for the applicant.

Scott, Senior Crown counsel, for the Minister of Local Government and the chairman of the committee of inquiry.

Boateng for Messrs. Braw·a and Bonda, interested parties.

'WINDSOR-AUBREY J. Counsel for the Minister has raised a pre­liminary objection on a point of law alleging that this court has no jurisdiction to entertain the application on the grounds that the matter is only triable by the Divisional Court, Cape Coast, where the cause of action arose. He relies on Order 5, r. 1 (b), of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules. This rule reads as follows:

" All actions for the recovery of penalties and forfeitures, and also all actions against public officers shall be commenced in the Judicial Division in which the cause of action arose."

Counsel for the applicant counters this argument by asserting that the cause of action is the Governor-General's appointment of commis­sioners. made in Accra. 1£ the cause of action did not arise in Accra he relies on Order 5, r. 1 (d), which deals with all other actions (that is, actions against persons who are not public officers). That rule provides that the action may be brought in the Judicial Division where any of the defendants reside.

It seems to me I must first resolve two questions-(l) are the respondents (defendants) public officers? and (2) where did the cause of action arise? Concerning the first point, by the Interpretation Ordinance a public officer is defined as follows:

" , Public Officer' or ' public department' shall extend to and include every officer or department invested with or performing duties of a public nature whether under the immediate control of the Governor or not. "

I conclude with some hesitation that under that definition the original appeal commissioner and the present commissioners are public officers. Whether or not the Governor-General is a public officer within the definition applicable to this case seems a matter of some doubt. He is the Queen's representative, but can scarcely be said, within the words of the definition, to be under the immediate control of himself. However, the point is of academic interest only as although the validity of the Governor-General's order is challenged, not he, but his Minister is the respondent (or defendant) in relation to that order.

I now have to consider the second point. Where did the cause of action arise? Is the cause of action the Governor-General's appoint­ment, or is it the original proceedings before the state council and the appeal commissioner's order leading to the Governor-General's order? I do not find the point easy to determine. The respondent's counsel, with legitimate ingenuity, passes off the Governor-General's order by describing it as an " appointment" only, referring to the words used in section 8 of the State Councils Ordinance. This is an argument not without considerable substance, but it is essential to consider the effect of the so-called appointment. In my opinion it is far more than a mere appointment, as when carried out it empowers the commissioners, if they think fit, to reverse the order of the state council. The power to make the appointment is not a formality but one requiring the exercise of discretion when the Governor-General thinks certain matters are inexpedient or undesirable and has 'the effect of nullifying or at least varying the order of a quasi-judicial body. In my opinion the Governor­General's action amounts to both an appointment and an order.

Having found that the action taken by the Governor-General amounts to an order, I still have to ask myself whether that order is the cause of action before me. Surely the proper way to look at these prohibition proceedings is that they relate to the original cause of action before the state council and to the order made by the appeal commissioner. The Governor-General's order, if and when complied with, may have the effect of upholding the decision of the state council or of varying or reversing it. I have therefore come to the conclusion that the original cause of action within the meaning of Order 5, r. 1 (b), was the proceedings before the state council, and therefore the respon­dents. being public officers, the proceedings should have been brought in the Divisional Court, Cape Coast. Consequently this court has no jurisdiction to entertain the application for an order for prohibition which is accordingly refused with costs.

Counsel for the applicant requested that if my decision was adverse to him I should refer the matter to the Chief Justice under Order 5, r. 1 (f), with a view to having the matter transferred 'to Cape Coast for hearing on November 2, 1957, and I will do so.

Application refused.

S. G. D.
