                                                                               June 17, 1957.

                                        OFORI ATTAH II AND ANOTHER v. MENSAH

(High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

Ollennu J.

 

 

Native Law and Custom-Stool land-Sub-stool created by paramount stool and vested with rights in land-Interest of paramount stool in land thus vested.

Native Courts-Native law and custom-Representation--State land-Persons competent to represent a state.

Land-Stool land-Powers of local authorities in connection with stool lands -Title to stool lands.

In this action for the recovery of land by an Odikro, the second plaintiff, on behalf of his stool, the Paramount Chief of the state to which the stool belonged had joined the action as co-plaintiff. He, the first plaintiff, supporting the second plaintiff's claim, asserted that the land the subject of the dispute was land within the state of which he was the head and that the state, having created the stool within it, had an interest in the land concerned and was thus an interested party to the dispute. The Native Court, before which the action had been com­menced, rejected the plaintiffs' claims not, apparently, on their merits but on the grounds that the state had no interest in the land and that, in any case, the first plaintiff was not the appropriate authority to litigate on behalf of the state and thus had no locus standi.

Held, (i) where a paramount stool has created a sub-stool and placed it in charge of land, a residual interest in the land concerned remains, by custom, in the paramount stool. The nature and extent of the residual interest is a question of fact in each individual case but it is one recog­nised by law and will thus support an action brought in respect of it.

(ii) Where a state has no paramount stool, state property, including such residual rights in land, vests in the state council. In such cases legal proceedings, if duly authorised by the council, may be brought in the name of the council or by some proper person duly authorised by the council in that regard.

(iii) Where a state has a paramount stool, the proper person to institute and defend proceedings on behalf of the state is the occupant of the paramount stool, acting with the consent and concurrence of the state council.

Obiter, although stool and state land have, to an extent, been brought by legislation under the control of local statutory authorities as regards management, revenue and disposition, this legislation has in no wise divested the owning bodies of their title to the land concerned and these bodies, or their duly authorised representatives, thus remain the proper authorities to bring or defend actions affecting such land.

APPEAL from a decision of Akyem Abuakwa Native Court " A " on November 8, 1956, dismissing the claim of the plaintiffs, who sued, on behalf of their respective stools, to recover possession of land.

Danquah for the plaintiffs.

The defendant appeared in person.

OLLENNU J. The order of the Native Court dismissed the claim of the plaintiffs, and was made upon submissions by the defendant in which he alleged that the dispute between him and the second plaintiff had once been submitted by the Government Agent to the first plaintiff to arbitrate upon, but that this arbitration could not be held due to some circumstances, and that by reason of that fact the joinder of the first plaintiff and the second plaintiff to institute the action was bad.

The Native Court based its said order upon two grounds, namely: (1) that the land in dispute was part of Abodom Stool (the second plaintiff's stool) land and therefore the first plaintiff, the Paramount Chief of the state, not being the occupant of the said Odikro Stool had no right to join with the Odikro to sue in respect of the land, and (2) that even if the farm were the property of the Akyem Abuakwa State it was only the state council, and not the Paramount Chief, who could sue in respect of it.

The order of the Native Court is wrong and is based upon a mis­conception of well-established native custom. The Paramount Stool, which creates the sub-stool (Odikro's Stool) and places it in charge of the village and the lands attached thereto, is presumed by custom to have an interest in that portion of the state land over which it creates the Odikro Stool. What the nature of that interest is, is a question of fact which can be determined in each particular case. That interest may be coextensive with or larger than the interest which the sub-stool has in the said land.

Even if it were not stools but individuals who join as plaintiffs to bring a suit it would not be open to a court, without hearing evidence, to dismiss the action on the ground that one or both the plaintiffs had no right to bring an action in respect of the subject-matter. Here I must say that even if evidence were led and it was established that one only of joint plaintiffs had an interest to sue, the action could not be dismissed; all that the court could do would be either to strike out the one party from the suit or to enter judgment against him, and proceed with the case of the other one found to have a right to sue.

The assertion by the Native Court that only a state council can bring an action in respect of state land is against the well-established native custom that only the head of a family or the occupant of a stool can sue and be sued in respect of the family or stool property. The head of the state is the Omanhene (the Paramount Chief). By native custom he is the proper person to sue in respect of the state land. An action instituted by a chief is presumed to be with the consent and concurrence of his elders and councillors, that is with the authority of his council, since the highest council in the state is the state council. Thus an action by a Paramount Chief, Omanhene, is instituted with the consent and concurrence of that council, the state council. It is only in states where this "soul" or the "symbol of solidarity, authority and unity," the stool, is absent tha't a state council may sue either in its own name or, what is more usual and indeed proper, depute some person or persons to act for the state council as the proper person to litigate over state land.

It appears that some recent legislation in Ghana has misled some people into believing that the stools of the country have been deprived of their ownership in their lands and that title to those lands has been transferred by statute to other bodies, for example the local govern­ment bodies, or that stool lands cannot be dealt with except by the state council in session.

It must be made clear that no such transfer of ownership of stool lands has been effected by any Ghana Act.

In my opinion the powers of management of stool lands given to local government bodies by statute and the statutory provisions that transfer of any stool land in a state should be with the consent of the state council has not changed the native custom as to who is entitled to litigate in respect of stool lands. The position remains as it was; title in stool land is vested in the particular stool, and definitely it is only the occupant of that stool who is vested with authority to litigate

the stool's title to the land. A manager of the stool land qua manager is not entitled to sue in respect of stool land.

The Native Court misdirected themselves on this customary principle and consequently erred in dismissing the suit.

Appeal allowed. Order for rehearing.

S. G. D.
