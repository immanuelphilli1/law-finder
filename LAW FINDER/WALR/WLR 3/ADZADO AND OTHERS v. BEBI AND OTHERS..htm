                                            December 3, 1957

                                         ADZADO AND OTHERS v. BEBI AND OTHERS.

                             High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court                     

                                                            Ollennu J.

 

Native Courts-Practice-Appeals-Enlargement of time-Failure to lodge notice of intention to appeal with appellate court-Whether power' to en­large time-Native Courts (Southern Section of Togoland) Ordinance, c. 106, s. 52 (I)-Native Courts (Southern Section of Togoland) Procedure Regulations, 1949, regs. 118, 120 (1) and 123 (1).

The applicants, who had been unsuccessful before the Native Court sought to appeal to the Land Court. Under the Native Courts (Southern Section of Togoland) Procedure Regulations, reg. 118 (1), an intending appellant ~s required to serve notice of intention to appeal upon the Native Court concerned and also to lodge a copy with the Appeal Court, within one month of the decision to be appealed against. The applicants had duly served notice of intention upon the Native Court but they had failed to do so upon the Appeal Court within the time limit. They now sought an order enlarging time for lodging notice of intention. Under regulation 119 a Native Court receiving notice of intention is required to send a copy of the decision appealed against to the Appeal Court. The Native Court had duly done so but their communication had not reached the Appeal Court within one month of the decision.

Held, (i) the wide discretion given to an Appeal Court by the Native Courts (Southern Section of Togoland) Ordinance, s. 52 (1), is exercisable in connection with an appeal only where the appellate court is vested with jurisdiction to entertain such appeal. Where notice of intention to appeal has not been duly filed with the appellate court the court has no such jurisdiction.

Muffat and Others v. Kpeshie and Another (1952) 14 W.A.C.A. 44 applied.

(ii) The discretion given to the Appeal Court by the Native Courts (Southern Section of Togoland) Procedure Regulations, reg. 123 (I), is exercisable in connection with an appeal only where the appellate court is vested with jurisdiction to entertain such appeal. Where notice of intention to appeal has not been duly filed with the appellate court the court has no such jurisdiction.

(iii) By the Native Courts (Southern Section of Togoland) Procedure Regulations, reg. 120 (1), a wide discretion is given to the Appeal Court to make orders upon the filing with it by the Native Court of the notice of intention. If therefore the notice served on the Appeal Court by the Native Court had been received by the Appeal Court within one month of the decision to be appealed against, this would have conferred juris­diction upon the Appeal Court that would have enabled it, if it so chose, to cure the defect resulting from the failur~ of the appellant to serve the notice.

Case referred to:

(1) Muffat and Others v. Kpeshie and Another (1952) 14 W.A.C.A. 44.

ApPLICATION for an order for enlargement of time in which to lodge notice of intention to appeal from a decision of the Kpandu Native Court .• A " given on October 1, 1957.

Apaloo for the applicant.

Bentsi Enchill for the respondent.


 

OLLENNU J. This is an application for enlargement of time within which to fulfil the statutory conditions of appeal laid down in regulation 120 of the Native Courts (Southern Section of Togoland) Procedure Regulations, made under the Native Courts (Southern Section of Togoland) Ordinance, s. 70. The judgment against which it is sought to appeal was delivered by the Kpandu Native Court" A " on October 1, 1957. Notice of intention to appeal was filed with the Native Court on October 25, 1957, but none was lodged in this court. In compliance with the provisions of regulation 118 the Registrar of the Native Court attached the copy filed in his court to a copy of the judgment in the case and transmitted it to this court. The copy of the judgment with the Notice attached was received in this court on November 4, 1957, more than one month after the delivery of the judgment.

Counsel for the respondent submitted that the court has no juris­diction to entertain the application because the applicant had not complied with the mandatory requirements of the regulation 118 (1) of the Procedure Regulations, which is a condition precedent for the exer­cise of the court's appellate jurisdiction in appeals from Native Courts. He cited the case of Muffat and Others v. Kpeshie and Another (1) to support his submission.

Counsel for the applicant submitted that Muffat and Others v.

Kpeshie and Another (1) is distinguishable from the present one, in that in this case, the provisions of the regulation 118 (1) have been partly complied with by the filing of notice in the Native Court, leaving only the lodging of the notice with this court, while in that case there was total default in compliance with the provisions of the equivalent regula­tion made under the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance Counsel further submitted that the court is given very wide discretion in section 52 (1) of the Ordinance, and can, in exercise of those powers, grant the extension of time sought in the circumstances of this case.

Section 52 (1) is an exact reproduction of section 52 (1) of the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance. The discretion by it given to the court can only be exercised when the court has become vested with jurisdiction to entertain an appeal; see the case of Muffat and Others v. Kpeshie and Another (1), cited above.

The discretionary powers of the court to grant an extension of time to fulfil the statutory requirements in regulation 120 of the Procedure Regulations are expressly given to it by regulation 123 (1), which reads as follows:

" An Appeal Court of its own motion as soon as may be conve­nient after the receipt of the Notice of Intention to Appeal and unless it shall for good cause shewn extend the period of one month within which the requirements of regulation 120 are to be com­plied with (which power of extension is reserved to the Appeal Court) shall upon being satisfied that the appellant is not entitled to appeal or that the requirements of regulation 120 have not been complied with, order the appeal to be struck out."

However, in my opinion, that power cannot be exercised unless the Appeal Court has become seised of the appeal, that is to say when the mandatory provisions of regulation 118 (1) have been fully complied with, by the fulfilment of each of the two conditions precedent therein laid down, that is: (1) Notice of appeal is filed in the Native Court, and (2) in addition, notice of appeal is lodged with the Appeal Court, both within the statutory period of one month, and default is thereafter made in complying with the provisions of regulation 120 (2).

A comparison of the present regulations 118 and 119 as amended in 1950 with the old regulations 118 and 119 as originally made in 1945, shows that the filing of the notice in the Native Court and the lodging of it in the Appeal Court are intended to be two separate and distinct processes and, therefore, the filing of the notice and its transmission by the trial Native Court with a copy of the judgment to the Appeal Court is not intended to be a substitute for the lodging of the notice with the Appeal Court. The old regulations read:

"118. An appeal (other than an appeal from an interlocutory decision or order) from a Native Court to an Appeal Court shall be commenced by the appellant filing in (duplicate) in the Native Court from whose decision he is appealing a Notice of Appeal in the prescribed form. The Notice of Appeal shall be filed within one month of the date of the decision or order appealed against.

A Native Court which receives Notice of Appeal shall (i) attach to the notice a certified copy of the decision or order to which such notice refers, (ii) endorse on such notice the cost of making up the record of appeal, (iii) within 10 days of receiving it, send such notice so endorsed, and with a copy of the decision or order attached, to the Registrar of the Appeal Court named in such notice; and the Native Court may take no further action in the matter except by the direction of the Appeal Court."

The absence from the original regulation 118 of the provision for lodging the notice of the appeal with the Appeal Court, and the insertion of that provision in the amended regulation 118 is, in my opinion, pur­poseful; and makes it obligatory that the two steps should each be taken, and they are conditions precedent for vesting the Appeal Court with jurisdiction to entertain any proceedings in the appeal.

Thus while under the old rules, it was enough simply to file the notice in duplicate with the trial Native Court, the Appeal Court becoming vested with jurisdiction when it received, from the trial Native Court, the notice of appeal filed in the trial Native Court within one month of the decision, under the amended rules the Appeal Court does not become seised of jurisdiction unless one notice of appeal is lodged with it within one month of the decision.

As pointed out above, no notice of appeal was lodged, in this case with this court, within the one month. Thus even if the one trans­mitted by the Registrar of the Native Court had been received in this court within the one month, it would not give this court jurisdiction because such transmission is only a subsidiary process as far as the vesting of jurisdiction is concerned, and must be taken independently of the lodging with the Appeal Court.

However, the provisions of the regulation 120 (1) that: "The Registrar of any Appeal Court which receives a Notice of Intention to Appeal shall file the same in the Court and such Court may make such order as it thinks fit ... " are in very wide terms. They do not specify that the copy, which must be filed in the Appeal Court before the court can make any order in the appeal, shall necessarily be the one lodged with it, and not the one filed in the trial Native Court and transmitted to it. If therefore, the notice of intention to appeal had been received and filed in this court within one month of the decision appealed from, I would have felt myself obliged .to hold that the failure to lodge would, in the particular circumstances, amount to a mere irregularity, or technicality and therefore that this court has juris­diction. Unfortunately the notice transmitted was not received here until November 4, 1957, which was more than one month after the decision. In the circumstances I am bound to come to the conclusion that this court has no jurisdiction either under section 52 (1) of the Ordinance or regulation 120 (1) of the Procedure Regulations to grant the extension of time prayed for.

Application dismissed.

S. G. D.
