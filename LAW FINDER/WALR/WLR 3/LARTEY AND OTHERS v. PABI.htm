                                                   June 12, 1958

                                          LARTEY AND OTHERS v. PABI    

                                     High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                       Ollennu J

 

 

Contract-Illegality-Moneylenders-Loan at excessive interest admitted by one not in Business as a moneylender-Whether transaction caught by Moneylenders Ordinance-Moneylenders Ordinance, c. 176, s. 3.

Moneylenders-Failure to keep statutory records-Whether requirement of keeping records applicable to a person deemed only- to be a moneylender­ Moneylenders Ordinance, c. 176, s. 19.

Moneylenders-Failure to keep statutory records-Whether transaction enforce­able in action brought against moneylender-Moneylenders Ordinance, c. 176, s. 19.

Evidence - Documentary evidence - Unambiguous statement contained in document-Whether extrinsic evidence admissible to show true situation otherwise than as revealed by the statement.

The three plaintiffs (children of the late Kwasi Darte) claimed an order under the Loans Recovery Ordinance, s. 3 (2), to reopen loans transactions entered into between them and the defendant in January 1957. Their case was that their father had raised a loan of £600 from him in 1952; from this £600 the defendant deducted £60 as "drinks." Their father's successor, one Kwadjo Boi, had raised in 1954 a further sum of £100 from the defendant, which was added to the first amount; from it the defendant deducted £10 as "drinks." The interest charged by the defendant on the two amounts of £600 and £100 (making a total of £700) brought principal and interest together to £1,505. Thereafter he added a further sum of £265, which he alleged had been spent by him in repairing a building mortgaged to him as security for payment of the various loans taken, and the interest thereon. In respect of the amount of £1,505, the plaintiffs gave the defendant a promissory note in 1957. A portion of the mortgaged premises were occupied by the defendant as tenant, at an agreed rent of £150 p.a., of which £30 p.a. was to be paid in cash, and the balance credited to the loan account. The defendant had not kept the record book stipulated by section 19 of the Moneylenders Ordinance, and the court should declare the trans­action unenforceable in virtue of section 19 (4).

The defendant's case was that he had charged interest of 30 per cent. per annum on each of the two amounts he lent, namely, the £600 and the £100. There were no deductions for" drinks." At the request of the plaintiffs he contracted to pay a building contractor the sum of £340 to carry out repairs on the mortgaged building, a portion of which he, the defendant, occupied as a tenant. The agreed rent for the part of the premises which he occupied was £30 p.a., with nothing to be credited to the loan account. The receipt given by the plaintiffs for the first monthly rent payment was expressed as "£30 being full payment of rentage. "

Held, (i) the defendant, on his own admission, had lent money at interest and was therefore, by reason of the Moneylenders Ordinance, c. 176, s. 3, a moneylender. As the rate of interest charged was much in excess of that permitted by section 13 (1) of the Ordinance, the trans­action was to be reopened in exercise of the discretion conferred by the Loans Recovery Ordinance, c. 175, s. 3 (2), and the interest chargeable was to be redetermined at the maximum permitted by section 13 (1) (a) of the former Ordinance.

(ii) Although the defendant had failed to keep a record book, as required by the Moneylenders Ordinance, s. 19, it was the plaintiffs who sought, in. the action, to reopen the transaction, not the defendant to enforce it, and therefore the provisions of section 19 (4) as to unen­forceability in the absence of a book were inapplicable to the transaction.

(iii) In any event the true construction of the Moneylenders Ordi­nance, s. 19, was to render it applicable only to persons who carried on the business of moneylending within the meaning of section 2 of the Ordinance and not to persons who were held to be moneylenders solely by operation of section 3 of the Ordinance.

(iv) In the first monthly rent receipt £30 was expressed as "being in payment of rentage ... " This was plain and unambiguous and it was therefore not open to the plaintiffs to adduce extrinsic evidence to show that the monthly rental was in fact £150.

ACTION by debtors for the reopening of various loan transactions.

Apaloo for plaintiffs. Akufo-Addo for defendant.

OLLENNU J. dealt with the facts and continued: Although the defendant is not a licensed moneylender, and is not a person now carrying on business as a moneylender, yet his admission that he has lent moneys in consideration of their repayment with interest brings him within section 3 of the Moneylenders Ordinance:

" Save as excepted in paragraphs (a), (b), (c), (d) and (e) of the definition of ' moneylender' in section 2, any person who lends money at interest or who lends a sum of money in consideration of a larger sum being repaid, shall be presumed to be a moneylender until the contrary be proved. "

I therefore hold that the defendant is a moneylender, and I must consequently consider whether the interest charged by him on the two sums was lawful under the Moneylenders Ordinance, and, if not, consider further whether or not the court should exercise the powers vested in it by section 3 (2) of the Loans Recovery Ordinance, and open the transaction between the parties.

Section 13 (1) (a) of the Moneylenders Ordinance provides that for a loan secured by either real or personal proper.ty, or by personal guaran­tee, the interest shall not exceed the rate of 15 per cent. per annum on the first £500 and part thereof or 12t per cent. on any amount in excess of £500. Section 14 (1) provides that where several sums are lent at different times, and later tacked together, the rate of interest shall be calculated on the same basis as provided in section 13 of the Ordinance: that is to say, that all the amounts put together will carry an interest of 15 per cent. per annum as to .the first £500 and interest of 12t per cent. per annum on any amount in excess of £500. It is admitted by the defendant that he charged an interest at .the rate of 30 per cent. per annum. That interest exceeded the maximum laid down in section 13, and I must therefore reopen the transaction under section 3 of the Loans Recovery Ordinance.

Apart from calculation of interest at the rate provided by law, the only matters which remain are:

(1) whether the portions of the mortgaged premises which, as agreed by the parties, are occupied by the defendant as a tenant were let to him as alleged by the plaintiffs at an annual rent of £150, or as alleged by the defendant at an annual rent of £30; and

(2) whether, as alleged by the plaintiffs, the defendant deducted an amount of £60 out of the loan of £600, and an amount of £10 out of the loan of £100, as " drinks."

On the first question (the rent) the first plaintiff, who was the sole witness for the plaintiffs, deposed that he was present when his father entered into the tenancy agreement with the defendant, .that the agreement was oral and not in writing, and that the rent agreed was £150 per annum. He said that .the defendant offered to pay £30 cash per annum to his father as part of the rent of £150, and to credit his loan account with the balance, namely £120. He admitted under cross-examination that his father gave a receipt for the said £30 paid to him by the defendant. The receipt was written by the first plain­tiff's brother (the second plaintiff), and was signed by the first plaintiff as a witness. It reads:

" Received from Mr. Otoo Pabbi cash the sum of £30 (thirty pounds) being full payment of rentage in the Awusam House from date herein July 1953 to July 1954 in advance."

That receipt is plain and unambiguous. It is clear from it that the tenancy agreement commenced in July 1953, and not in January 1952, as alleged by the first plaintiff. Moreover, it says that the rent of £30 paid was full rent for one year in advance.

Learned counsel for the plaintiffs, Mr. Apaloo, invited me to accept the evidence given by the first plaintiff that the receipt's description of the £30 as "full payment of rentage in advance" should be inter­preted to mean" £30 being that part of the rent which he had agreed to pay in cash out of an agreed rent of £150 per annum." But a court is not permitted to interpret words other than in their ordinary simple sense. It is only when the wording of a document is ambiguous that a court is entitled to receive extrinsic evidence to explain it, in order that the court may give to it its proper construction, and may imple­ment the true intention of the parties. I can see no ambiguity what­soever in the very simple words" full payment of rentage in advance" used in that document. That document therefore shows that the first plaintiff is either an untruthful witness, or one whose memory is defec­tive. In either event his evidence cannot be relied upon as to the agreed rents he deposed to. How, then, can the court accept any other evidence which he gave, based upon his recollection of incidents which happened some time ago?

Again, the first plaintiff deposed that the second sum of £100 loan was taken by and paid to his uncle the late K wadjo Boi. The promis­sory note given on the loan of £100 was tendered in evidence by the defendant through this witness after he had admitted its existence, and that he himself had signed it as witness. That receipt proved conclu­sively that the loan of £100 was taken by himself (the first plaintiff), not by his uncle as he alleged. That document satisfied me that the first plaintiff was not acting upon a faulty memory, but was deliberately trying to mislead the court by his falsehood. I cannot, therefore, accept his version of the agreement as to rents. I reject his evidence that the rent was fixed at £150 per annum, and I accept what is contained in the receipt given by the father, as written out by the second plaintiff, and witnessed by the first plaintiff. I find that the rent agreed upon was £30 per annum.

On the question of the alleged deduGtion of £60 and £10 from the loans of £600 and £100 respectively, I would say first that since the only witness who spoke for the plaintiffs has shown himself untruthful, I cannot accept his evidence on that issue also. But more than that, I accept the defendant's evidence that he paid the £600 and the £100 in full to the plaintiffs' father and the first plaintiff respectively, before the documents in respect of those sums were executed by the borrowers.

I find, therefore, that no deductions of £60 and £10 (or of any sums whatsoever) were made from the £600 and the £100.

Learned counsel for the plaintiffs has submitted that since the defendant has been shown to be a moneylender under the Money­lenders Ordinance, and has admitted that he has not been keeping books as required by the Ordinance, the court should act under section 19 (4), and should declare ~hat the document in respect of which this action is brought is null and void and unenforceable. With respect to learned counsel, I am of the opinion that the proper construction of section 19 (4), having regard to the whole of section 19, is that sub­section (4) applies only to a moneylender as defined under section 2 of the Ordinance, viz., "a person whose business is that of money­lending," and not to a person who is a moneylender only by presump­tion under section 3 of the Ordinance. It would be ridiculous to inter­pret the section otherwise. I do not gather from that section that it was the intention of the legislature to require a person giving financial assistance to his personal friend by way of loan, as was the case here, to go to the extent of procuring and keeping a book of the nature described in section 19 (2). If he was so required, he might well refuse to assist the friend in his time of need and distress. Subsection (2) states:

" Every moneylender shall keep a book (which shall be securely bound and paged so that leaves cannot be removed or inserted without apparent damage) in which he shall enter in connection with every loan made by him .... "

and subsection (4) (relied upon) says:

" Any moneylender who fails to comply with any of the require­ments of this section shall not be entitled to enforce any claim in respect of any transaction in relation to which the defaults shall have been made. He shall also be guilty of an offence under­this Ordinance and shall be liable to a fine not exceeding five pounds for each day or part of a day during which such offence continues. "

I am further of the opinion that a court is obliged to apply it only against a moneylender (however defined) who comes to court to enforce a claim in respect of such transaction and who has failed to comply with the requirements of section 19, and where the circumstances are such that the court should not allow him to enforce the transaction. In the instant case the moneylender has not come to court to enforce a claim. It is the plaintiffs-the debtors-who have come to a court of equity seeking the assistance of the court, and praying it to exer­cise its powers under section 3 (2) of the Loans Recovery Ordinance to reduce a debt which they owe to the defendant.

Further, the plaintiffs ask for relief in respect of a document of 1957. They have not thought fit to produce it, though they sue upon it; the presumption of law is that the contents of the document are against them. I cannot forbid the enforcement of a document which is not before me. It is true that there are other documents before me. They are immaterial, except in so far as they prove the background of the case, and help to determine the credibility of the oral evidence given by the parties. They are not the documents sued upon, for it is admitted by both parties that the said documents have ceased to have effect by mutual agreement. I am therefore concerned only with the claim made by the plaintiffs, and their evidence in support of it. From that evidence I can find no loan given to the plaintiffs in 1957 in respect of which books should be kept in compliance with section 19 (4) of the Ordinance.

The only conclusion which I can come to, upon the evidence before the court, is that the sums were lent, namely, £600 without any deduc­tion and £100 without any deduction. It having been agreed by the parties that an amount of £200 had been advanced by the defendant at the request of the plaintiffs towards the cost of repairs of the said house, and that the amount spent on the repairs should carry interest, I am bound to order that that amount also is payable to .the defendant, with such interest as the law allows.

I now come back to the main question, namely the interest to be paid on the various amounts advanced. In compliance with section 13 of the Moneylenders Ordinance I declare that on the first £500, the interest shall be at the rate of 15 per cent. per annum from the date of that loan, that is, June 27, 1952. The excess of £100 over the first £500 shall carry interest of 12t per cent. from the same date. The further loan of £100 shall carry interest of 121 per cent. per annum as from March 23, 1954. The sum of £200, advanced by the defendant, shall carry an interest of 12i per cent. from March 12, 1957.

This interest works out as follows:

(1) on the £600, interest of £350 up to June 26, 1958; principal and interest therefore total £950;

(2) on the first £100, interest of £75 up to June 26, 1958; principal and interest total £175 ;

(3) on £100 (i.e., the second £100), interest of £53 2s. 6d. up to March 22, 1958; principal and interest total £153 2s. 6d., and

(4) on the £200, interest of £31 5s. up to June 11, 1958; prin­cipal and interest total £231 5s.

These several sums make a grand total of £1,509 7s. 6d.

But the plaintiffs are entitled to rent at the rate of £30 per annum.

Since no rents have been paid for four years, that is, since July 1953, the plaintiffs are entitled to a credit of £120, leaving a total indebted­ness in the sum of £1,389 7s. 6d. due and owing by the plaintiffs to the defendant, for which sum I give the defendant judgment.

I hereby set aside the various documents tendered in evidence, namely, the mortgage of June 27, 1952, and the receipt for £100.

On the question of costs, counsel for the plaintiffs submitted that he has succeeded on his main claim-the claim under section 3 (2)­to reopen the transaction entered into between .the parties in 1957, and therefore his clients are entitled to their costs. I had earlier ordered the reopening of the transaction between the parties, and, had counsel for the plaintiffs been satisfied with that, there would have been no necessity for the lengthy evidence which has been taken. However, I think that the plaintiffs are entitled to costs proportionate to the extent to which they have succeeded. At the same time the defendant, who has succeeded in the main, is also entitled .to his costs. Instead of awarding the plaintiffs and the defendant separate costs, I think it will be better to award costs .to the defendant alone, and make the amount as small as possible. I fix these nominal costs at twenty­five guineas.

Judgment for the plaintiffs on the application to reopen.

Judgment for the defendant for money due and owing.
