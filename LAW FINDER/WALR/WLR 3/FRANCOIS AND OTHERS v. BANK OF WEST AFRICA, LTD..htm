                                                   July 3, 1958

                      FRANCOIS AND OTHERS v. BANK OF WEST AFRICA, LTD.

                             High Court, Western Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                       Acolatse J.

 

Trusts-Money appropriated for charitable purposes-Designated bank account in name of donor-No execution of purpose for which. account set up­ Whether a trust binding on donor's executors in absence of testamentary directions.

Miss Maude Christian (daughter of Mr. George James Christian) died, and on .April 13, 1934, her father opened a current account with £100 at the bank of British West .Africa. The name of this account was " Miss Maude Christian Memorial Fund." The account grew by further payments-in by Mr. Christian. By April 21, 1938, it had reached the total sum of £603 11s. 3d., and it was transferred to fixed deposit. The amount continued to grow by small additional deposits, and by the addition of interest, and at the time of the proceedings with which this report is concerned it stood at £83115s. 11d.

Mr. Christian executed a will on February 22, 1936, and named therein executors and trustees in respect of his real and personal estate in the Gold Coast, and also of his English investments. This will con­tained bequests to several persons, including his relatives abroad in the West Indies. A clause in the will stated: "I give, devise and bequeath all my property not hereby otherwise disposed of Unto my Trustees in Trust to convert the same into money by sale or otherwise, and to divide the proceeds, after paying funeral and testamentary expenses and debts and the legacies hereby bequeathed, in equal shares among" (certain named beneficiaries). There was no mention of the "Miss Maude Christian Memorial Fund" in the will, nor in any of three codicils dated respectively March 1938, November 1938 and March 1940. Mr. Christian died on April 17, 1940.

The purpose of the "Miss Maude Christian Memorial Fund" had been disclosed in correspondence between Mr. Christian and the Colonial Secretary of the Gold Coast. That correspondence showed that he wished the Government of the Gold Coast to accept the sum then standing to the credit of the Fund, and to devote the greater part of it to the equipment of the Maternity Ward for the African Hospital at Sekondi. The balance was to provide X-ray equipment for the hospital, and the whole gift was to be in memory of his deceased daughter, Maude Mary ,Christian. Thus, on November 14, 1939, Mr. Christian wrote to the

Colonial Secretary of the Gold Coast, saying, inter alia: "I am prepared to send a cheque for the amount, which is deposited in the bank for that purpose, at any time you are ready for it." By his reply of December 11, 1933, the Colonial Secretary in effect accepted the gift, and indicated that he was prepared to carry out the desire of the late Mr. Christian as nearly as possible, e.g., "a bronze plate suitably inscribed (instead of marking the furniture) should be fixed in a prominent place inside the Maternity Ward, indicating that the furniture and equipment had been provided by you in memory of your daughter." In this letter the Colonial Secretary accepted Mr. Christian's offer to remit the money, in these words, "I am to say that the cheque, which should be made payable to the Colonial Government, may be forwarded to me at any time con­venient to you."

At the time of his own death, four months later, Mr. Christian had neither remitted the money to the Colonial Government, nor had he disposed of it by his will. Seventeen years later again, his executors and trustees took out an originating summons in the High Court for the determination of the following question:

"Whether the sum of £831 15s. lld. held on Fixed Deposit Account by the Bank of West Africa, Ltd. (defendants), and described as the account of George James Christian (Maude Christian Memorial Fund), is subject to a trust for the perpetuation of the memory of Maude Mary Christian, and, if so, the nature of the trust; or whether the said amount, it not having been specifically disposed of under the will of the testator, forms part of the residuary estate to be held in trust by the trustees for the purpose of being distributed among the residuary beneficiaries."

Held, (i) the testator had created a Trust Fund, with himself as trustee to dispose of the fund in the achievement of the object for which he had created it. It was a perfectly created trust, in that there was an expressed declaration of trust, intended to be final and binding upon himself.

(ii) It was for the executors, as trustees under the will, to see that the aim and object of the Trust Fund were carried out.

ORIGINATING SUMMONS to determine duties of executors of a will in regard to money given and banked by the deceased as a memorial fund in respect of another deceased member of the family.

Abbensetts for the plaintiffs (the executors). Gwira for the defendant (the bank).

ACOLATSE J. dealt with the facts and continued: The testator, the late George James Christian, died on April 17, 1940, without the pay­ment over to the Colonial Government (as it then was) of the sum of money in the account" Miss Maude Christian Memorial Fund." I am unable to import into the testator's mind a change of intention as to the proposed voluntary gift or trust.

On the question posed by this originating summons, I give the opinion that the testator had in his lifetime a trust fund into which he paid periodical sums of money in the expectation that the Colonial Government would carry out his desire in respect of the object of the fund. He constituted himself a trustee to dispose of the fund in the achievement of the object for which it was created, and he was pre­pared to hand over the amount in the fund to the Government at any time when called upon. In my opinion the said memorial fund in the hands of the defendant bank should be regarded as a perfectly created trust, in that there is an expressed declaration of trust, intended to be final and binding upon the late George James Christian (as trustee and creator of the fund) through his having opened a distinct and separate account in the name of a specific subject.

I consider that the executors, as trustees under the will of the late George James Christian, stepped into his shoes, and are entitled to withdraw the money credited to the fund in the bank, for payment over to the Government as represented by the Ministry of Health. It is for the executors as such trustees to see that the aim and object of the trust fund are carried out (as far as it is practicable to do so in present circumstances) and to give a valid receipt find discharge to the defendant bank herein.

 

In any case the amount should be paid by the bank to the executors as trustees upon a valid receipt and discharge, for the executors as trustees would be the persons responsible to those entitled in law, whether it be the Ministry of Health or the beneficiaries under the residuary legacy.

Order accordingly.
