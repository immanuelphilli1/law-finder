                                                                           August 29, 1957

                                                            EWUDZI v. DADSON.

                                       High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                       van Lare and Adumua-Bossman JJ.

 

((), August 29, 1957.)

Criminal Law-Practice-Private prosecution--Summary trial-Charge dis­missed without evidence being heard-Powers and duties of Magistrates -Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, ss. 168 and 172 (1).

On the hearing of this charge, brought by a private prosecutor, the trial Magistrate accepted a submission made on behalf of the accused who had pleaded" Not guilty," and before the prosecutor's case was heard, to the effect that the charge was not properly laid. He thereupon dismissed the charge. On appeal by way of case stated:

Held, the Criminal Procedure Code contains no provision whereby, on summary trial (as opposed to trial upon information), it is open to a court to entertain objections to a charge without hearing the prosecutor's evidence. The effect of the direction in section 168 of the Code to hear the prosecutor's evidence on a plea of "Not guilty" is mandatory. The powers of a summary jurisdiction court, where a charge appears to be defective, are contained in section 172 (1) of the Code. By this section the court may alter a charge by way of amendment or by the substitution or addition of a new charge at any time before the close of the case for the prosecution.

ApPEAL by way of case stated from a decision of the District Magistrate, Cape Coast, on November 10, 1956, dismissing a charge laid by a private prosecutor.

Hayfron-Benjamin, Jnr., for the prosecutor.

Sekyi for the accused.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by VAN LARE J.:

This is a case stated arising from a private prosecution at the Magistrate's Court, Cape Coast. The accused was charged with an offence as follows:

" For that you P. Dadson, erstwhile Registrar of the Native Court ' B ' of Mankessim within the jurisdiction of this Court, on or about the 1st day of February, 1956, did falsify the Court Record Book and proceedings in the case of rrwafuhene Yankful Kurentsil for himself as Head and on behalf of the other members of Tekyina Family of Mankessim represented by Nana Ewudzi II of Man­kessim v. Ekua Notwiwa, successor of the late Robina Motwey of Mankessim with intent to defeat and obstruct the course of justice by recording therein an order: 'Cause Dismissed Judgment entered for Defendant costs assessed at £4 Is.' whereas in truth and in fact no such order was given by 'the Native Court ' B ' of Mankessim in the proceedings."

There was no finding of fact admitted or proved because the learned Magistrate did not hear evidence. It would appear that the accused having pleaded not guilty to the charge, the Magistrate upheld a sub­mission made on his behalf that the charge was not properly laid: he apparently therefore dismissed the charge and thereby determined the matter in favour of the accused.

The complainant being dissatisfied with this determination, as being erroneous in point of law, applied to the learned trial Magistrate who has given his reasons for dismissing the charge. It is unnecessary to go into the validity of the reasons given by the trial Magistrate in disposing of the matter before him, and the questions of law on which he seeks the opinion of this court do not arise, in view of the fact that by the Criminal Procedure Code, s. lEi8, the Magistrate was mandatory enjoined to proceed to hear such evidence as the prosecutor might adduce in support of the charge and he was not therefore entitled to entertain the objection which was raised by counsel for the accused or any other objection whatsoever at that stage.

In our opinion it is not competent for a Magistrate in a summary trial to entertain objections to a charge immediately after it had been read to the accused who has pleaded not guilty. That is a privilege of a judge trying a case on information, as is provided by the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 244. There is no such provision in the procedure upon a summary trial. Any defect in the charge notwithstanding a Magistrate is bound to proceed to hear evidence if the accused pleads not guilty; but in accordance with 'the provision of section 172 (1) of the Ordinance a Magistrate may, at any stage of a summary trial before the close of the case of the prosecution, make such order for the altera­tion of the charge or by way of amendment or by the subs'titution or addition of a new charge as he may think necessary to meet the cir­cumstances of the case, if it appears to the court that the charge is defective either in substance or in form.

In our view the learned Magistrate erred in determining the case as he did. For these reasons we reversed his said determination and remitted the case for a retrial.

Appeal allowed. Order for trial de novo.

S. G. D.
