                                                        November 28, 1957                                                      

                                                BASSIL v. SAID RAAD & SONS

                                                                                Court of Appeal

                                   Granville Sharp J.A., Ollennu and Smith JJ

 

 

Contract-Quasi-estoppel-Landlord and tenant-Agreement for reduction in rent.

Landlord and Tenant-Statutory tenancy-Recovery of possession by landlord -Breach of covenant by tenant not exposing him to forfeiture-Whether any right to recover possession-Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, s. 11 (1) (a).

Landlord and Tenant-Breach of covenant by tenant-Effect of subsequent acceptance of rent.

The plaintiff had entered into a tenancy agreement with the defendant for a period of five years from April 1, 1950, and at a monthly rental of £55. From January 1, 1953, the plaintiff had accepted rent at the rate of £45 a month. He claimed that this was a temporary concession to the defendant who remained liable for the balance: the defendant claimed that the reduction had been a complete and permanent one and had been prompted in the plaintiff by the fear that if he did not reduce the rent voluntarily the Rent Assessment Committee would do so compul­sorily. The plaintiff sought, inter alia, recovery of possession under the Rent Control Ordinance, s. 11 (1) (a) on the grounds of non-payment of· rent and breach of covenant. The covenant alleged to have been broken was one that prohibited sub-letting without consent: breach of this covenant did not, under the terms of the agreement, involve forfeiture. In the trial court the learned judge accepted the plaintiff's version of the rent reduction and found for hjm. On appeal,

Held, (i) receipts for rent paid by the defendant after January 1953 were couched in such terms as to indicate that the full amount of rent then due was £45, and not that £45 was received on account of the larger sum. The finding of the trial judge on this point was thus against the weight of the evidence and could not stand.

(ii) Where parties enter into an agreement with intent to create legal relationships and where, in pursuance of such agreement, one party makes a promise to another with intent that it shall be acted upon and it is so acted upon, then the party giving such promise will not be allowed to act inconsistently with it even though it may not be supported by consideration.

Central London Property Trust, Ltd. v. High Trees House, Ltd. [1947] 1 K.B. 130; [1956] 1 All E.R. 256; 175 L.T. 332; 62 T.L.R. 557 applied.

(iii) The Rent Control Ordinance supplements the common law provisions as to landlord and tenant by attaching limitations to the landlord's rights so as to give greater security to the tenant: it does not supersede the common law. Thus the Ordinance, in specified circum­stances, confers a discretion upon the court to make an order for recovery of possession, but this discretion is exercisable only if such an order could have been made at common law. In the present case the breach of covenant against sub-letting entitled the landlord only to his action on the covenant and not, in the absence of provision in the agreement, to re-entry. The court therefore had no power under the Ordinance to order recovery of possession for the breach of covenant against sub-letting.

Moubarak v. Eguakun (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 88 applied.

(iv) Even if the tenancy agreement had permitted forfeiture and re-entry for breach of the covenant against sub-letting, this right had been lost, by waiver, upon the landlords' acceptance of rent subsequent to the breach.

Cases referred to :

(1) Birmingham &: District Land Co. v. London &: N. W. Ry. (1888) 40 Ch.D. 268; 60 L.T. 527, C.A.

(2) Central London Property Trust, Ltd. v. High Trees House, Ltd. [1947] 1 K.B. 130; [1956] 1 All E.R. 256; 175 L.T. 332; 62 T.L.R. 557.

(3) Moubarak v. Eguakun (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 88.

(4) Doe d. Wilson v. Phillips (1824) 2 Bing. 13; 9 Moore C.P. 36.

(5) Doe d. Darke v. Bowditch (1846) 8 Q.B. 973; 15 L.J.Q.B. 266.

(6) Doe d. Gatehouse v. Rees (1838) 4 Bing.N.C. 384; 7 L.J.C.P. 184.

(7) Groft v. Lumley (1858) 6 H.L.Cas. 672; 27 L.J.Q.B. 321; 31 L.T.a.S. 382; 22 J.P. 639.

(8) Davenport v. The Queen (1877) 3 App.Cas. 115; 47 L.J.P.C. 8; 37 L.T. 727, P.C.

APPEAL from a decision by Quashie-Idun J., in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, in favour of the plaintiff-landlord in an action for recovery of possession.

As.afu-Adjaye for the plaintiff.

Bentsi Enchill for the defendant.

SMITH J. This is an appeal from the decision of Quashie-Idun J., sitting in the Land Court, Accra, in which he gave judgment in favour of the plaintiff for arrears of rent amounting to £423 and for recovery of possession of premises at Tudu let by the plaintiff to the defendant.

The facts are simple. The parties entered into a tenancy agreement dated April 1, 1950, in respect of certain premises known as House No. E 19/2, Boundary Road, Tudu, Accra, at a rent of £660 per annum. The tenancy was for a period of five years as from April 1, 1950. The agreement also contained the usual provision restricting the defendant from sub-letting the premises or any part thereof without the approval and consent of the plaintiff. The defendant continued in possession of the premises after March 31, 1955, the date of expiry of the tenancy, and thereafter, as the trial judge found, became a statutory tenant.

In the action brought by the plaintiff against the defendant the claim was for (a) recovery of possession of the said premises; (b) £300 damages for breach of agreement under which the said premises were held; and (c) £423 arrears of rent. That part of the claim relating to damages for breach of the provision of sub-letting was not pursued before the trial judge and was not an issue argued in this appeal.

As regards the question of the claim for arrears of rent, it was admitted that the defendant paid rent at the rate of £45 from January 1, 1953, to the end of April 1955, instead of £55. The plaintiff's evidence was that this reduction was a temporary concession by him and that the defendant promised to pay the balance when he was in a better financial position to do so. The defendant, on the other hand, said it was the plaintiff who, on being approached, had agreed to reduce the rent from £55 to £45, as he was afraid that if he did not reduce the rent the Rent Assessment Committee would do so. There was no other witness on this matter. The defendant produced some twenty-nine receipts for rent paid since January 1953.

The learned trial judge believed the evidence of the plaintiff as to the reason he gave for accepting the smaller amount and gave judgment against the defendant accordingly for the arrears of rent as he claimed in the writ. It appears from the judgment of the trial judge that his decision was based solely on the oral testimony of the parties and that he did not take into consideration the receipts for rent tendered by the defendant and these, as Mr. Asafu-Adjaye for the plaintiff conceded, supported the evidence of the defendant. In my opinion they clearly do so. The receipts are unequivocal, acknowledging payment of each month's rent from January 1954 at the rate of £45 a month. There is nothing in the wording of any of the receipts indicating or even tending· to indicate that the amount so paid was a payment on account of rent. On the contrary, the receipt for January 1954 states that the rate is £45 per mensem. In my opinion they substantiate the evidence of the defendant that the plaintiff agreed to and did reduce the rent payable from £55 to £45. It is also important to note that there was no claim or demand by the plaintiff for any balance of rent due until he brought his action over three years after the acceptance of the first payment of £45 rent instead of £55.

It is clear on the evidence that the rent was paid or tendered by the defendant at the reduced rate up to the time of the bringing of this action by the plaintiff.

As regards the variation by the parties of the terms of the original agreement, Mr. Bentsi-Enchill, counsel for the defendant, cited two authorities which are in point. They are Birmingham and District Land Co. v. London and North Western Ry. (1) and Central London Property Trust Ltd. v. High Trccs Housc Ltd. (2). In the latter case it was held "that where parties enter into an agreement which is intended to create legal relations between them and in pursuance of such agreement one party makes a promise to the other which he knows will be acted on and which is in fact acted on by the promisee, the court will treat the promise as binding on the promisor to the extent that it will not allow him to act inconsistently with it even although the promise may not be supported by consideration in the strict sense and the effect of the arrangement made is to vary the terms of a contract under seal by one of less value."

This passage covers the circumstances of this case precisely and I would allow the appeal.

OLLENNU J. I concur in the judgment read by my learned brother.

The claim for recovery of possession was brought under the Rent Control Ordinance, s. 11 (1). The main ground alleged in the state­ment of claim was breach of covenant to sub-let. The allegation of arrears of rents was a subsidiary ground. The learned judge found that each of those two grounds for the claim had been established and held that even on the grounds of arrears of rents alone the relief could be granted.

Although the findings that there were arrears of rents due have been set aside, it does not necessarily follow that the relief prayed for should be granted on the findings of fact made by the learned trial judge that there has been a breach of the covenant not to sub-let, since, as I shall show presently, there is good defence disclosed for refusing the remedy on that ground also. The Rent Control Ordinance has not done away with the common law provisions relating to landlord and tenant. All it does is to place certain limitations upon the exercise of the landlord's rights at common law so as to give greater security to the tenant. Beyond that it does not go. In this respect I adopt the view expressed by Windsor-Aubrey J. in his judgment in the case of Moubarak v. Eguakun (3) that" the object of the legislation "-the Rent Control Ordinance-" is to afford greater, not less, protection to the tenant than existed under the old common law."

In my opinion section 11 (1) is not to be interpreted as mandatory upon a court to make an order for recovery of possession whenever anyone of the grounds therein set out is proved to exist. The proper interpretation of that section should be that the court may grant an order for recovery of possession or ejectment of a tenant whenever any of the grounds stated in the section is proved, but only in circumstances in which it would at common law be obliged to make such an order.

Now at common law there can be no forfeiture for breach of covenant under a lease unless there is express provision in the lease for re-entry: Doe d. Wilson v. Phillips (4), Doe d. Darke v. Bow­ditch (5). And where there is provision for re-entry upon breach, or where the breach is not merely of a covenant but of a condition and therefore forfeiture can attach without express provision for re-entry, a claim for forfeiture will, however, be refused upon proof of waiver of the breach. See W oodfall on Landlord and Tenant, 24th ed., p. 926. At common law acceptance of rents accruing after the breach of covenant or condition amounts to waiver of the breach. Such an acceptance operates as a matter of law to waive all forfeitures then known to the lessor notwithstanding any protest on his part against such waiver. See Doe d. Gatehouse v. Rees (6), Croft v. Lumley (7), and Davenport v. The Queen (8).

The lease in this case does not contain any provision for re-entry; therefore no forfeiture can arise upon breach of the covenant. More­over, the evidence shows that the plaintiff has been receiving rents with full knowledge of the breach. He is therefore deemed in law to have waived the breach and he cannot, therefore, be entitled to recover possession.

For these reasons also I would allow the appeal.

GRANVILLE SHARP J.A. I agree.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D
