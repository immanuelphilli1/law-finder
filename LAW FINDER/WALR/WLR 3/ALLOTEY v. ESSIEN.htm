                                                        ALLOTEY v. ESSIEN

                                       High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                           Ollennu J.

 

 

Estoppel-Native law and custom-Estoppel by acquiescence-Owner of land standing by and watching another develop the land in good faith.

Estoppel-Native law and custom-Estoppel by conduct-Owner of land standing by with knowledge in previous case involving his title.

Practice-Pleading-Allegations of fact in statement of claim-Attempt by plaintiff to disprove facts alleged in claim without amending pleadings.

Evidence-Best evidence-Allegation that a written notice was served upon a party-Failure to produce original notice-Failure to seek production or admission of written notice-Whether parol evidence admissible to establish service of the notice.

The plaintiff sought a declaration of her family's title to land; the defendant, who was in possession, claimed to have acquired the land by purchase. On the evidence it was established that the defendant, who acted throughout in good faith, had spent considerable sums of money in developing the land, believing himself to have a valid title to it. Also that the plaintiff's family, knowing of their own title, had stood by without objection. It was also established that there had been previous liti­gation, concerning title to the land, between the defendant (who was then the successful defendant) and a third party, and that the plaintiff's family had, with full knowledge that the title to land over which they claimed an interest was involved, stood by without intervening in the suit.

The plaintiff had pleaded in her reply that the acts of the defendant had been done without the knowledge of her family. She sought to prove, however, that her family had known of the defendant's actions and had served both parol and written notices on him warning him that he had no right to the land. No notice to produce the written notice had been served on the defendant by the plaintiff.

Held, (i) it is a principle of native custom, as also of equity, that one who, claiming a title to land, and knowing that another has no title, stands by and watches that other in good faith doing acts to his detri­ment with regard to the land, subsequently cannot be heard to say that that other has no title to the land.

Abbey and Ashalley Okoe v. Ollennu (1954) 14 W.A.C.A. 567 and Thompson v. Mensah (1957) 3 W.A.L.R. 240 followed.

(ii) It is also a principle of native custom that one who, knowing of litigation in which the title to his land is put in issue, stands by and passively countenances such litigation without intervening to pro­tect his rights, may subsequently be estopped from asserting his rights to the land as against the person in whose favour the litigation was determined.

Ak1Cei v. Cofie (1952) 14 W.A.C.A. 143, followed.

(iii) The plaintiff, not having amended her pleadings, could not be heard to give evidence in disproof of matters alleged by her in her pleadings.

(iv) In any event the best evidence of the written notice alleged to have been served by the plaintiff upon the defendant was the notice itself (or, if the defendant had failed to produce or admit the original after due notice, a copy of it), and in the absence of such evidence, parol evidence of the service of the written notice was inadmissible.

Cases referred to:

(1) Abbey and Ashalley Okoe v. Ollennu (1954) 14 W.A.C.A. 567.

(2) Thompson v. Mensah (1957) 3 W.A.L.R. 240.

(3) Ak1Cei v. Cofie (1952) 14 W.A.C.A. 143.

ACTION for a declaration of title to land and an order for recovery of possession.

Puplampu for the plaintiff. Apalloo for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. In this case ~he plaintiff claims an order for recovery of possession of a piece or parcel of land which is fully described in her writ of summons. She pleaded that this land was a portion of a large area of land adjudged by the Land Court to be land over which her family has a possessory title, and that this judgment of the Land Court was confirmed by the West African Court of Appeal. She pleaded that without the leave and licence of her family, of which she claims to be the head, the defendant entered upon the land and erected buildings thereon.

The defence, in short, is that the defendant went upon this land some time in 1947, honestly believing that he had acquired a good title to it by purchase from one Nii Tettey Ghoke of Atukpai; that he commenced building in the same year, 1947, and completed it in 1948; and that he had been in possession and occupation of ~he land and buildings ever since. The defendant further pleaded that his entry upon the land, his erection of the buildings and his occupation of the land and buildings all these years, were facts which were well known to the plaintiff's family. Consequently he pleaded that the 'plaintiff's family were estopped from asserting their claim against him, they having stood by and done nothing, permitting him to spend large sums of money in developing the land in the honest belief that he had a good title to it.

 

In her reply to the statement of defence ~he plaintiff pleaded that she could not have warned the defendant of her family's title of the land, because between the years 1947 and 1951, when the defendant entered and built upon the land, the head of her family was old and infirm, so that he never went to the land, with the result that the family had no knowledge whatsoever of the defendant's entry upon ~he land and the erection of the buildings thereon. But in her evidence before the court, the plaintiff, in contradiction of her plea set out in the reply to the statement of defence already referred to, deposed that the family was well aware that the defendant had entered on the land; that they saw him commence the foundations of the buildings, and they saw him complete it. She says the defendant did all that in spite of warnings they had given him orally, and by writing through their solicitor, Mr. Adumua-Bossman, as he then was.

In the first place this evidence contradicts the plaintiff's pleadings and should not therefore have been allowed by me to be led without the pleadings being amended.

In the second place, if it were true that the plaintiff's family caused written warnings to be served upon the defendant, they should have served notice upon the defendant to produce those written warnings, and upon the defendant's failure to produce ~he originals of these letters, the plaintiff could have tendered the duplicates of those letters in evidence.

The plaintiff admitted under cross-examination that she and mem­bers of her family were aware of litigation, between one Amon Kojo and the defendant in this case, which took place in 1947 in the Ga Native Court. This litigation was in respect of land which she claims to be a portion of her family land and which she says forms a boundary with, and in fact includes a portion of the land in dispute in this case. The defendant on the other hand said ,that the plaintiff's family knew quite well that the only land he occupied in the area and in respect of which the said Amon Kojo sued him in the Ga Native Court was the identical land in dispute in this case, and that the head of the plaintiff's family at that time, one Kotey, the plaintiff herself, and other members of her family attended court from time to time when ~hat litigation was going on. He is emphatic that the plaintiff's family never at any time before the commencement of this suit warned him of any right, title, or interest which they have in the land, and never challenged his right to possess and occupy the land.

The buildings which the defendant has erected on the land, he said, cost him nothing less than £3,500.

Upon the evidence, I found the following facts proved: that the defendant went on the land in 1947 and commenced building thereon immediately; that he completed that building in 1948 and obtained a certificate of occupation thereof in February, 1949, and has been in occupation ever since; that he honestly believed from the moment he entered upon the land and all through the period of his occupation that he had a good title to it; that the plaintiff's family knew from 1947 of the entry of the defendant upon the land, they were well aware of his erecting a valuable building thereon but stood by and did nothing and allowed him to spend that large sum of money in that honest belief that he had a good title to the land; that the plaintiff's family knew of and were present in the Ga Native Court when the litigation between Amon Kojo and the defendant was proceeding, but took no steps to be joined as parties thereto; and that the plaintiff's family knew quite well that the land in dispute in that case was the' identical land in dispute in the present suit.

Now, the principle of equity, which is the same principle of native custom, is that where an owner of land stands by and sees another person enter upon his land, in the honest belief that he has a good title to it, and spend money to improve it, and he takes no steps to warn that person, he will be estopped later on from asserting that the person who has so entered upon the land and spent money has no right to retain possession and occupation of the land. See Abbey and Ashalley Okoe v. Ollennu (1) and the cases therein cited. See also Thompson v. Mensah (2) in which latter case the principles, as far as native custom is concerned, are fully discussed.

Again, where a person who claims an interest in land stands by and sees other people litigating their title to that land, and does nothing-takes no steps to be joined as a party-he cannot afterwards be held to say that he is the owner of the land. He will be estopped by his conduct from claiming ownership of that land as against the succj:Jssful party in the other suit. See the case of Akwei v. Oofie (3).

Having found as I have done that the plaintiff's family has acquiesced in the defendant's acts, I am bound upon the principles laid down in the cases cited above to hold that the plaintiff's claim must fail.

Judgment for the defendant.

S. G. D.
