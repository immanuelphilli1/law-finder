                                          COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. MENSAH.

                                               High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court       

                                                                           Adumua­Bossman J.

.

.

Criminal Law-Evidence-Character-Questions by prosecution tending to show previous convictions of acc1lsed-Whether conviction to be quashed if no substantial miscarriage of justice.

criminal Law-Practice-convictions-Whether stealing a "similar offence" to unlawfully possessing goods suspected of having been stolen-criminal code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 80.

Section 129 (f) of the Criminal Procedure Code provides as follows:

" A person charged and called as a witness in pursuance of this Code shall not be asked ... any question tending to show that he has com­mitted, or been convicted or been charged with any offence other than that wherewith he is then charged, or is of bad character, unless ... ;

(ii) he has personally or by his advocate asked questions of the wit­nesses for the prosecution with a view to establishing his own good character or has given evidence of his own good character, or the nature or conduct of the defence is such as to involve imputations against the character of the prosecutor or the witnesses for the prosecution."

Yaw :Mensah alias Mustapha was charged with being in possession of money, trinkets and certain other articles, reasonably suspected to have been stolen, contra section 108 of the Criminal Code. He was tried by a District Magistrate. His defence was that he had come by the articles in the course of normal trading, and he further alleged that a police witness had destroyed a statement which he had made.

To the fifth witness for the Crown the accused suggested that he (the witness) had torn up a previous statement taken from him (the accused), to which the witness replied: "I never wrote any statement, and never tore it up."

In his defence the accused said:

"I was with Taylor \V oodrow for nine months, and I left and went to Adomi, where I worked for 15 months. I brought £30 and kept it ... I took my £30 and came to Swedru, and I made up my mind to go to Takoradi to search for a job. I went to Takoradi, and saw a friend called Yankson, and told him I was in need of a job. Yankson advised me to go with him for a loan to buy broken trinkets. I told him I had £20. He told me to go to Tarkwa and Prestea. I went to Tarkwa on two occasions, and to Prestea. I went once to Abosso. When I returned to Takoradi one Whiteman told me he was working in steamer and would leave, and if I liked a scale he would sell it to me. I was then selling broken trinkets to him. I paid £2 for it (scale). When I returned to Takoradi I had on me £65, and I paid the £2 out of it leaving the balance of £63. When I got to Swedru, I saw a certain woman who gave me broken trinkets and proper trinkets. I bought them for £4 10s."

During the prosecution's cross-examination of the accused, it was elicited from him that he was not in prison nine months previously. He was convicted, and appealed to the High Court, where his counsel sub­mitted that the prosecution's question as to a previous conviction in­fringed the provisions of section 129 (f) set out above.

Held, (i) The accused's defence that he had come by the articles in question by normal trading amounted to a setting up of his good character, so depriving him of the protection of section 129 (I).

[EDITORIAL NOTE. With respect to the learned Judge this proposition hardly seems to be in accord with settled principles.]

(ii) His suggestion that a police witness had destroyed a statement taken from him involved an attack on the character of a witness for the prosecution, further depriving him of the protection of section 129 (I).

(iii) In any event, if, as a result of the questions put by the prosecu­tion the Magistrate had cause to suspect that the prisoner had previous convictions, in view of the evidence against the prisoner there had been no miscarriage of justice.

(iv) Stealing is a "similar crime" to possessing articles suspected of having been stolen, within the meaning of the Criminal Code, s. 80.

Case referred to:

R.  v. Thomas (1957) 41 Cr.App.R. 236; [1957] 1 W.L.R. 747; [1957] 3 All E.R. 350.

ApPEAL from a decision of the District Magistrate, Swedru, on January 7, 1958, convicting the appellant of having in his possession articles reasonably suspected of having been stolen.

Hayfron Benjamin for the appellant. Akiwumi, Jm. for the Commissioner of Police.

ADUMUA-BosSMAN J. referred to the facts and continued: Upon a careful examination of the answers on record, elicited by the accused's cross-examination of at least two witnesses and his own evidence-in­chief, it seems to me:

(1) that he set up his own good character in representing the acquisi­tion, by honest labour and business, of the things in respect of which he had been charged, so as to justify the question (which is now complained of) about his movements during the part nine months; and

(2) that he himself made imputations against the character of the fifth prosecution witness, thus further depriving himself of the protection of section 129 (f) of the Criminal Procedure Code.

But if the evidence to which I have referred cannot be given the interpretation which I have placed on it, and if it could be held that the accused's point of having been improperly cross-examined ought to be decided in his favour, authority is clear as to the courses which it is open to the court to follow. In R. v. Thomas Donovan J., giving the judgment of the Court of Criminal Appeal (Lord Goddard C.J., Hilbery J. and Donovan J.), referred to earlier authorities and con­cluded:

" There is thus no hard and fast rule upon the matter. Each case has to be separately considered. In the present case, if we were to conclude that the jury suspected from what was said that the applicant had previous convictions we should certainly apply the proviso. The application for leave is accordingly refused."

Those observations, in my view, would cover the appeal now before me. At its worst, the only effect of the accused's answer (that he had not been in prison during the past nine months) could have been to raise a suspicion in the mind of the learned Magistrate at the time that the accused perhaps had a previous conviction which he did not admit. Moreover, R. v. Thomas was decided by a jury, whereas this is a case tried by an experienced District Magistrate, well aware of legally admis­sible and inadmissible evidence. From my knowledge of him, he is sufficiently experienced in the art of giving the proper weight to the former, and completely disregarding the latter. Of my own know­ledge, accused persons (particularly when undefended) frequently tender unbidden a good deal of improper and inadmissible evidence about their past, which magistrates and judges disregard, as they should, in their consideration of the case. The reasons for the Magis­trate's decision satisfy me that this was a case in point, and that the putting of the question complained of (even if I were disposed .to hold that the question was improperly put-which, as already indicated, I am not) had no effect on the Magistrate's mind in arriving at his decision. I am unable, therefore, on the only point raised in the appeal, to interfere with the conviction.

As to sentence, counsel has submitted that section 80 of the Crimi­nal Code provides that upon summary conviction for crime, if there be any previous conviction for a similar crime, an accused is liable to not more than twice the maximum of imprisonment which might other­wise be inflicted. Under section 108, the accused was liable to a maximum of three months only (if he was a first offender), and having only one previous conviction for unlawful possession he was therefore liable to not more than six months' imprisonment with hard labour. But it seems to me that this argument ignores the record of eight previous convictions for stealing. Such convictions are clearly within the words "similar crime" mentioned in the provisions of the table to section 80, relied on by counsel for the accused. 'the explanations in the" Notes to the above table," subsection (3), seem to have escaped his attention. It is there stated that:

•• Any crime which is punishable under a title of ~his Code is similar to every other crime punishable under the same title."

Stealing (section 100) and unlawful possession (section 108) are both offences under Title II, and are therefore similar offences.

It follows that the accused has not one but nine convictions for similar crimes. He should be thankful that he was given only twelve months' imprisonment. I order, however, that the period shall run not from today (the date of ~he dismissal of this appeal) but from the date of conviction. I so order because the hearing of this appeal has been considerably delayed by my absence on assize, and other circum­stances. Subject to that, the appeal is dismissed.

Appeal dismissed.
