                                                    November 22, 1958.

                                                       DAATSIN v. AMISSAH                                                     

                                  (High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                       Adumua-Bossman J

 

.

Contract -'- Money lent - Native law and custom - Family law - Whether family liable for debts of member who dies insolvent.

Costs-Representation-Liability of head of family personally for costs awarded against him when suing in a representative capacity.

"Uncle Sam," a member of the Anona family, owed £28 to Kwesi Asafuah, a member of the Anona family (Kumah branch). Debtor and creditor both died, and Kwaw Daatsin (head of Asafuah's branch of the family) sued Chief Sam Amissah (head of Uncle Sam's branch of the family) for the £28.

The action was tried in the Municipal Court" B," Cape Coast, which gave judgment for the plaintiff. An appeal by the defendant to the Magistrate's Court was unsuccessful. He took a second appeal to the High Court, where it was argued that the plaintiff was not the proper person to sue for the debt, nor the defendant the proper person to be sued.

Held, (i) the family of a deceased person are not liable for the debts of the deceased and an action to recover such debts cannot be maintained against the head of the family.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. There does not appear to have been any evidence before the court as to the solvency or otherwise of Uncle Sam at the date of his death, but it can hardly be doubted that the rule enunciated by the learned judge applies only where the deceased left no self-acquired property. It would be inequitable to allow a family to take tne benefit of the undisposed of self-acquired property of a deceased member without assuming, at the same time, the burden of at least so much of the deceased member's debts as would be covered by his assets. This appears to be implicit in Asiedu v. Ofori and Another, for Deane C.J. refers expressly to deceased persons who die leaving nothing. Indeed, jt is possible that Asiedu v. Ofori and A Mther was intended by Deane C.J. as authority only for the proposition that members of a family are not liable in their persons for the debt of a deceased member.]

(ii) In any event, no cause of action was disclosed in the present case, for the property of the deceased creditor passed not to the plaintiff's family but into the creditor's matrilineal family, the head of which alone could sue to enforce the loan.

(iii). The head of a family, suing in a representative capacity, will be personally liable for any costs awarded against him.

Cases referred to :

(1) Lokko v. Konklofi (1908) Renner's Reports, Vol. 1, Pt. 2, 454.

(2) Hammond v. United Africa 00., Ltd. and Others (1936) 3 W.A.C.A. 60.

(3) Asiedu v. Ofori and Another, Divisional Court, Dec. 6, 1932, un­reported.

(4) Larkai v. Amorkor and Others (1933) 1 W.A.C.A. 323. (5) Arthur v. Ayensu (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 257.

APPEAL from a decision of the Magistrate, Cape Coast, dismissing an appeal from a decision of the Municipal Court" B," Cape Coast. The latter court had given judgment for the plaintiff, who sued in his representative capacity, for a debt due to a deceased member of his family from a deceased member of the defendant's family.

Short for the appellant.

 Sekyi for the respondent.

ADUMUA-BoSSMAN J. referred to the facts, and continued: There can be no doubt that in the distant past, when the personality of the individual member of the family or clan was virtually submerged under the more conspicuous personality of the head of the family or clan-unit, claims of this sort were of frequent occurrence, and were readily enter­tained and allowed. Sarbah speaks of that condition of affairs when, in the old days, a head was responsible for the debts of the individual members of his family (Sarbah, Fanti Customary Law (1st ed.), p. 35):

"Not only does the customary law render the person or persons who defray the burial expenses of any person liable and responsible for the debts of the deceased, but, as Bossman states, the members of a family and the head thereof are jointly and severally responsible for any family liability. If a member of a family contracts a debt which benefits the family, or commits a wrong for which he is liable to pay damages or give satisfaction, the other members of his family are bound to pay, or such member must be given up by the family to the person making the claim. If the family do not wish to be held responsible for the future acts of a certain member, there must be a public notice of their decision to that effect, and such person must be expelled from the family, thereby severing his connection with them. A person is liable for the debts and the consequences of the torts of his slaves and the members of his family under his controL"

In 1902, when Sir Brandford Griffiths C.J. was considering and discussing the question of the liability of the family (if any) for the debt of a member to a third party, in the case of Lokko v. Konklofi (1), he also referred to the old state of affairs when he spoke as follows:

   " By native law, the family property could not be seized for the debt of one of the members, but any member of the family might be panyarred, , until the family paid the debt and expenses. The English law put an end to the panyarring. "

In the circumstances alluded to by these two distinguished lawyers, it can be imagined that it would be the head of the family on either side who would be figuring more conspicuously and prominently. So far as the creditor was concerned, it would not be the relatively insignificant individual member of the family who would make demand for the debt due to him; rather would his head of family make the demand on his behalf. Equally, when the creditor had the choice to seize any member of the family, it would be the more conspicuous head of the debtor's family from whom he would demand payment, and whom he would seize on payment not being forthcoming.

This state of the old customary law has undergone radical changes, and the only trace of it still surviving today is that referred to by the West African Court of Appeal in the case of Hammond v. United Africa Co., Ltd. and Others (2), when the court said: "We are of the opinion that where a person sues as head of a family he thereby renders himself personally liable, according to native law and custom, for any costs that may be awarded against him." Thus, although the head sues in a representative capacity, he is nevertheless liable personally for costs as far as the party sued by him is concerned.

In the unreported case of Suit No. 97/32, Asiedu v. Ofori and Another (3), Sir George Deane C.J. gave detailed consideration to the state of the old customary law, in a case where the heads of the family of a deceased debtor were sued to be made personally liable, although it was accepted that the debtor had not left a penny with which any debt of his could be paid. Deane C.J. said as follows:

" Now I am satisfied by the evidence of custom given in this case that the heads of the family, if they fail to appoint a successor within a reasonable time, may themselves be sued as successors of a deceased person, and that this action is rightly brought against the defendants who, as the recognized heads, male and female, of the family of Donkor, are successors to him. I am driven to the conclusion that according to native customary law the successors of a deceased person, who died leaving nothing, are responsible for the payment of his debts. Mr. A were for the plaintiff has submitted that, since this custom is being daily enforced in the native tribunals (where, had this claim been within the jurisdiction of the tribunal, judgment would have been obtained as of course against the defendants) this court should recognise the native customary law, and itself give judgment against them."

The learned Chief Justice then referred to the passage in Sarbah to which I have already referred. He proceeded as follows:

" It is clear, however, from this passage that these ideas of the native customary law as to the liability of the family for the debts of its members belong to another age, and spring from principles which are not recognised by the courts of this colony in their en­tirety. It is no longer true that the head of a family is liable for the debts of one of its members unless he gives notice to the world that he will not be responsible, and puts the debtor outside the pale of the family or delivers him up to his creditor .... The custom is, I find, a relic of the old slavery days, when a debtor was the slave of his creditor, and when his body actually belonged to his creditor until the debt was paid."

Sir George Deane concluded as follows:

"Imprisonment for debt has been abolished in England. I regret it has not been totally abolished in this colony; but I decline further to extend its principle so as to make the defendants liable in their persons and property, not only for their own debts, but for debts of members of their family .... Such a course would, in my view, be repugnant to justice and equity."

The case of Aseidu v. Olori and Another (3) would therefore seem to cover this case, so far as it concerns the allegation that the defen­dant is liable to pay solely on the ground that he is the head of the larger Anona family, of which the late" Uncle Sam" (the debtor) was in his lifetime a member.

But it seems to me that the question of the plaintiff's right to sue to claim payment of the debt which he says was due to Kwesi Asafuah, deceased, who was a member of that Anona family (Kumah branch) of which the plaintiff claims to be head, ought also to be considered. Indeed, logically, that question ought to have been considered first, since a plaintiff's locus standi to maintain an action ought to be con­sidered before any other question arising in it. On that question-the plaintiff's right (according to the now recognised prevailing native cus­tomary law) to sue to claim a debt due and owing to a deceased member of his family-it seems to me that the decision must be adverse to the plaintiff .

The prevailing customary law on that point also was lucidly enun­ciated by the same Chief Justice in unmistakable terms, in the case of Lar7mi v. Amorkor and Others (4) in which the head of the wider family sued in respect of the property of an individual member of the family, in respect of which property the latter had died intestate. The court said as follows:

"Now the plaintiff has claimed this land as being the head of the Larkai family. Ahuru, it is true, was called 'Ahuru

The court said as follows:

"Now the plaintiff has claimed this land as being the head of the Larkai family. Ahuru, it is true, was called 'Ahuru Larkai,' and it seems was a member of the Larkai family, but individually owned property of his would, on his death intestate, become the family property, not of the Larkai family, but of his own family The plaintiff is the present holder of the Larkai family stool . but ... certainly he would not be a member ofAhuru's family so as to become the head of his family and as such entitled to the family property of Ahuru so as to sue for it in this court." Larkai v. Amorkor has been adopted and followed in many subsequent cases including Arthur v. Ayensu (5).

In the result, I am of opinion that authority is now clear and decisive that in the existing state of native customary law the plaintiff has no right to claim the debt due to a member of the family of which he is the recognised head, nOr the defendant liable for payment of the debt of a member of the family of which he is the recognised head. The decision of the Municipal Court, confirmed by the Magistrate's Court, contravenes the now recognised a>nd settled native customary law, and the decision cannot be supported.

The appeal is therefore allowed, and the judgment of the Municipal Court (confirmed by the Magistrate's Court) is hereby set aside. In place thereof judgment is entered dismissing the plaintiff's claim with costs.

Appeal allowed.
