                                            December 22 and 24, 1958

                                  BALOGUN AND OTHERS v. EDUSEI AND ANOTHER.

                                     High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                           Smith J

 

 

Contempt of court-Contemnor's knowledge of order notwithstanding no service in habeas corpus and certiorari proceedings-Deportations while applications sub judice.

On Ocotber 17, 1958, the Minister of the Interior (the Hon. Krobo Edusei) issued deportation orders against Wahabi Balogun and three other men, and on the same date he issued corresponding authorisatiom: to the Acting Commissioner of Police (Mr. Madjitey) to arrest the four men without warrant, and to deport them to Nigeria. The four men were arrested accordingly on Saturday, October 18, 1958.

On Monday, October 20, at 8.1S a.m., counsel for relatives of the four men filed an ex parte notice fuat the court would be moved that morning for writs of habeas corpus (on the ground that the four men were Ghanaians, and therefore not subject to deportation) and for orders of certiorari. On hearing counsel that morning, the court directed that notice of the motion be given to the Minister, the Acting Commissioner of Police and the Director of Prisons, and fixed October 30 for the adjourned hearing. The Acting Commissioner of Police nevertheless made special arrangements with Air Liban Service, and the four men were removed from Ghana in the late afternoon of the same day (October 20), while the applications for writs of habeas corpus were still sub judice. It was not until the following day (October 21) that the notices ordered by the court were served upon the Minister, the Acting Com­missioner and the Director.

On November 11, 1958, counsel for the four men filed notice of motion of an application that the Minister of the Interior and the Acting Com­missioner of Police be committed for contempt of court. The application was supported by an affidavit sworn by counsel's law clerk (Mr. 'W. L. Obeng), in which he deposed, inter alia, as follows:

"5. that on the 19th of October, counsel for the applicants took particular steps to notify by letter the three respondents, namely the Minister of the Interior, the Acting Commissioner of Police and the Director of Prisons, of the application to be heard the next day, and requested that to avoid complications the appli­cants should not be removed from Ghana pending the court's order;

"7. that, as the 19th October was a Sunday, these letters were delivered by hand at the residences of the respective respon­dents;

"4. that when fixing a date for the hearing of the adjourned application the Attorney-General (Mr. Geoffrey Bing) and Crown Counsel (Mr. Louis Cross) were consulted by the court before the 30th of October was fixed for hearing of the applica­tion for habeas corpus;

"8. that in the afternoon of the 20th of October, in consequence of information received by Dr. J. B. Danquah that the applicants were likely to be conveyed by air from Ghana to Nigeria, the applicants' solicitor telephoned to the Attorney-General to explain to him the complications that were likely to arise if the respondents caused the detainees to be removed from the juris­diction of the court when their applications for habeas corpus were pending in the court within the knowledge of the respon­dents, and of the Attorney-General;

"9. that I am informed by the applicants' solicitor, and verily believe, that, it appearing that the Attorney-General was attending a conference at that time and could not attend the telephone, his Secretary connected the applicants' solicitor to Mr. F. H. Battcock, Solicitor-General, to whom Dr. Danquah delivered his message for the Attorney-General;

"10. that Mr. Battcock informed the applicants' solicitor that the Attorney-General had already spoken to the Minister of the Interior on the subject, and had advised him in the matter;

"11. that a few minutes after the telephone ,conversation with Mr.

Battcock, Mr. Krobo Edusei (Minister of the Interior) tele­phoned to the applicants' solicitor and stated to him that the Governor-General's Order had been made for deportation of the four persons, and that if the court afterwards found that they were Ghanaians and should not be deported they would be entitled to return to Ghana j

"12. that the solicitor requested him not to deport the four persons, as notices would be served on him through the court on the following day in respect of the application for habeas corpus;

"13. that the Minister said he could not wait for any such notices.

He also mentioned that the Acting Commissioner of Police had come to see him with a copy of the solicitor's letter, delivered on Sunday, but that he had told Mr. Madjitey that despite the information contained in that letter the four persons should be deported. "

The Solicitor-General, on behalf of the respondents, submitted that the Minister was under a duty to carry out the' Governor-General's deporta­tion order as soon as possible, and that unless and until he was served with an order of the court (or received contrary instructions from the Governor-General) he had no authority to postpone the deportation of the persons concerned.

Held, (i) where, as in this case, the fact that the court has made an order for service on the respondents is known to them, actual service of the order is not necessary before there can be a committal for contempt. (ii) The deportations amounted to contempt, because they interfered with the parties litigant, summarily put an end to the court proceedings, and brought the administration of the law into disregard.

(iii) In the position reached in the instant case, certiorari was not the appropriate metnod of trial of the issue of Ghanaian citizenship vel non. The proper course was by way of declaratory action.

Cases referred to:

(1) R. v. Rowe (1894) 11 T.L.R. 29. (2) Barnardo v. Ford [1892] A.C. 326.

(3) United Telephone Co. v. Dale (1884) 25 Ch.D. 778 j 53 L.J.Ch. 295 j 50 L.T. 85 ..

(4) R. v. Fulham, Hammersmith and Kensington Rent Tribunal, ex p.

ApPLICATION for the committal of Krobo Edusei and E. R. T.

Madjitey for contempt of court by reason of their deportation of four persons while applications by the latter for orders of habeas corpus and certiorari were sub judice.

Danquah for the applicants.

Battcock, Solicitor-General, for the respondents.

SMITH J. As regards the application for attachment and com­mittal for contempt of court, my conclusion is that a case has been made out against the first and second respondents.

Four persons against whom deportation orders had been made were arrested on October 18, 1958. The 19th being a Sunday, they filed on the 20th applications for writs of habeas corpus. The motion (ex parte) was heard in court the same day, and the court ordered service of the motion on the Minister of the Interior and on the Acting Commissioner of Police (the first and second respondents), in accord­ance with the provisions of the Rules of Court. The date of hearing was fixed for October 30. The first respondent, in full knowledge of the proceedings, but before the court order could be drawn up and served upon him, had the four persons removed by aeroplane to Nigeria on October 21. The result of this was that when the motion on notice for the writ of habeas corpus came before the court on October 30, the learned judge who was dealing with the case had no alternative in law but to dismiss the application there and then.

The first respondent (the Minister of the Interior) was fully aware of the proceedings in court, of the course they had taken, and of the hearing date that was fixed. He was informed not only by counsel for the applicants, but by the the Law Officers of the Crown. The first respondent does not deny that he told counsel for the applicants that he was not going to wait for the notices of the court order to be served on him, but was going to deport the four persons concerned forthwith. When there was-or was likely to be-some delay in the departure of the ordinary aeroplane to Nigeria, special arrangements were made with the Air Liban Service, and the persons were moved in the late afternoon of October 20, the day before service of the motion on the respondents was effected.

The Solicitor-General submitted that the first respondent was under a duty to carry out the Governor-General's deportation order as soon as possible, and (quoting from the first respondent's affidavit) unless and until he was served with an order of the court or received contrary instructions from the Governor-General, he had no authority to postpone the deportation of the four persons concerned. With respect, I do not think that this argument can apply in the circum­stances of this particular case, from the affidavits filed. As regards the service of the court order, it is not the case that the :first respondent denies that he knew of the order, or was in any way uncertain about what had happened. In my view, there is only one inference that can be drawn from the admitted facts, and that is that it was ensured that the deportations would be carried out before formal service of the motion could be effected.

In these circumstances, I am of the view that actual service is not necessary before there can be attachment and committal for contempt. The applicants, claiming to be Ghanaian citizens, who were under arrest and about to be deported, took the one remedy open to them, and applied to the court for a writ of habeas corpus. There was nothing else they could do. It is upon them that the burden is cast to prove, if they can, that the Deportation Act does not apply to them. The deportations, in effect, summarily put an end to the court pro­ceedings, and brought the administration of the law into disregard.

I do not intend to examine the authorities in detail. The general principle is stated in Oswald on Contempt, at page 6: "Contempt of court may be said to be constituted by any conduct that tends to bring the authority and administration of the law into disrespect or disregard, or to interfere with or prejudice parties litigant or their witnesses during the litigation."

There is one case that caused me some difficulty: That was R. v. Rowe· (1). But in that case a writ of habeas corpus was ordered, and copies (not the original, as prescribed by law) was served. But that was an application for committal for disobedience to an order, and there had been an irregularity in the service. I do not think that that case is an authority that service is a sine qua non for subsequent contempt pro­ceedings for disobedience. In the present case the service was in order, but before service could be effected, steps were taken to anticipate it.

I think the case Barnardo v. Ford (2) is against the respondents.

At page 355 there is ,the following passage in the judgment of Lord Watson:

 I think it right to add that, in my opinion, no contempt is committed by a person who, lawfully or unlawfully, absolutely gives up the custody and control of a child from the mere appre­hension that by retaining it he may become liable to a writ of habeas corpus, and without any notice that such proceedings will be taken."

In the case before me there was not just mere apprehension; there was notice that proceedings had been taken.

It seems to me that it would render matters impossible if in all cases service had to be effected. I think that there are occasions like this one-as I read the authorities-where formal service is not neces­sary. I think that principles must apply like those referred to in such cases as United Telephone Company v. Dale (3). In that case, of course, there was a breach of an injunction, but the underlying prin­ciples as regards service must be the same. The headnote in that case reads as follows:

 In order to justify the committal of a defendant for breach of an injunction it is not necessary that the order granting the injunction should have been served upon him, if it is proved .that he had notice of the order aliunde, and knew that the plaintiff intended to enforce it. "

At page 787, Pearson J. said:

"The court would be to a great extent incapable of doing its duty to itself, as well as to Her Majesty's subjects, if it were to say that, with perfectly accurate knowledge of the order of the court, a defendant is at liberty to defy the court's authority, and then come to the court and say,  You cannot visit me for that breach of your order, because the order has not been served upon me.' What is the necessity for serving an order upon a defendant, if he knows perfectly well without that service what it is which he is bound ~o obey? "

In the case before me, it is not a question of disobedience to a court order, but it seems to me clear that with the knowledge which the first respondent possessed-that a court order had been made, and was virtually on the way-there was an interference with ~he admini­stration of the law, and interference with (or prejudice to) the parties litigant, who could else have given evidence during the litigation. I am of opinion that, for these reasons, the application in respect of the contempt must succeed.

So far as the application for certiorari is concerned, I do not make an order. In support of the application, there is an affidavit by the parties as to parentage, but the evidence is wholly insufficient~ for any court to accept as sufficient proof of citizenship. I have the same comment to make as regards that aspect of the matter as I did in the Amadu Baba and Othman Larden applications. I do not modify or retract a word I said then. The bald statement that a person's father or mother was born in Ghana, and nothing more, is not enough. I do not say that in no case could ~he question be dealt with by means of certiorari, but it is obvious, in the position that this case has reached,

. and on the evidence-such as it is-that certiorari is not the appro­priate method of trial. The proper course is by way of declaratory action, and the normal processes as required under the Supreme Court Rules are appropriate. The learned Solicitor-General referred me to the case of Rex v. Fulham, Hammersmith and Kensington Rent Tri­bunal, ex parte Zerek (4). In that case Devlin J. indicated that certiorari is not appropriate where the dispute turns on a question of fact. With that I respectfully agree. I think that that disposes so thoroughly of the certiorari application that it is unnecessary to deal with the other points raised in respect of this application. The applica­tion for certiorari is accordingly refused.

As regards the motion for committal, it is ordered that execution be stayed until 9 a.m. on December 24, 1958. Krobo Edusei and E. R. T. Madjitey adjudged in contempt; committal ordered, execution stayed until 9 a.m. December 24, 1958.

December 24, 1958. SMITH J. Last Monday I delivered judgment in this application, and held the two respondents to be in contempt. I considered that they might have been carrying out executive instruc­tions, but (whatever excuse they may have had for their action) they were found in law to be in contempt. The courts derive their powers from the Constitution, and are under oath to administer these powers according to law. Parliament in this country has not passed any law providing that a Minister or civil servant cannot be in contempt for any act done. No one stands above the law.

The respondents having been held in contempt, the contempt had to be purged. I considered that I should stay execution of committal to prison, to leave opportunity for advice to be given to the contemnors by their legal advisers to adopt the obvious course, namely, to express regret. It is plain that an apology would have been considered in the circumstances. I therefore adjourned the matter until today at 9 a.m. When the court resumed today, however, the acting Solicitor-General, appearing for the respondents, informed me that Parliament had held an emergency session earlier this morning, and that an Indemnity Act had already been passed. He applied for an adjournment, which I granted because it was necessary for me to see the Act itself.

I now have the Act. It is the Deportation (Indemnity) Ac~ (No. 47 of 1958), passed this 24th day of December, 1958; it has also received the Royal Assent today. Section 2 is as follows:

"The Honourable Krobo Edusei, formerly Minister of the Interior, and Erasmus Ransford Tawiah Madjitey, Commissioner of Police, shall be indemnified from all penalties for contempt of court and exonerated from all other liabilities in respect of any action taken by them in carrying out ~he deportation order in the Schedule of this Act after the institution by the persons named in those orders of proceedings by way of habeas corpus."

By the passing of this Act I take it that the court's finding that the respondents are in contempt is not challenged by Parliament, but that the intention is to neutralise any consequential order that I might make. It is plain that Parliament prefers tha~ the respondents should not apologise, and it has passed this Act in order to nullify any order which I might make in the absence of the apology. The Courts of Justice exist to fulfill, not to destroy, the law, and it would not make sense for me to record an order which is incapable of being carried out.

As to the deportations while the applications for habeas corpus were still 8ub judioe, I cannot over-emphasise the undesirability of interference by the Executive with the functions of the court. Persis­tent indulgence in such a practice could not have any other than the most serious ill-effect on the well-being of the country. Decisions of a court are as binding upon the Executive as the laws which Parliament passes are binding upon the ordinary citizen, and it is the court that enforces upon the people obedience to these laws, thereby aiding Parlia­ment in the ordering of the country.

In the result, the finding of contempt stands, but I make no further order.

No further order made.
