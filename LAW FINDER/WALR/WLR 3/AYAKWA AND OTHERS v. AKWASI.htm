                                                     October 17, 1958

                                                AYAKWA AND OTHERS v. AKWASI

                Supreme Court of Ghana, Ashanti Judicial Division, Divisional Court, Kumasi

                                                                  Smith J.

 

 

Certiorari - Prohibition - State Councils - Power of Governor-General to appoint a Committee of InquiT1J-Purported appointment after a State Council had disposed of the issue before it-State Councils (Ashanti) Ordinance, c. 4 of 1952, s. 13 (1).

Destoolment charges were preferred against N ana Akyeampong Akwasi II, Agonahene, by certain complainants. The State Council sat, and opened an inquiry into the charges. At the hearing, the Council put certain questions to the complainants, and finding as a result that the complainants had no status to prefer the charges, the Council dismissed the case. The complainants appealed to the Appeal Commissioner against the State Council's decision.

While their appeal was still pending, the complainants petitioned the Governor-General, preferring the same charges against the chief that they had made before the State Council, and requesting the Governor­General to appoint a Committee of Inquiry under section 13 (1) of the State Councils (Ashanti) Ordinance, 1952. That subsection provides as follows:

"If in the opinion of the Governor it is inexpedient that the Asanteman Council or a State Council should inquire into any matter of a constitutional nature, or if in his opinion the Asanteman Council or a State Council is unable to arrive at a conclusion on such a matter without undue delay, or if such matter is not cognisable by the Ansanteman Council or a State Council he may appoint a Committee of Inquiry consisting of three persons, of whom at least two shall be Chiefs, to inquire into such matter, and such Committee of Inquiry shall inquire into the same and shall submit a report thereon to the Governor, who may confirm, vary or refuse to confirm the findings thereof or may remit the matter to the Committee of Inquiry for further consideration with such direction as he may tnink fit as to the taking of additional evidence or otherwise. The Governor's decision upon the report shall be final and conclusive."

The Governor-General (the complainant's appeal to the Appeal Com­missioner still pending) appointed a Committee of Inquiry to inquire into the charges, and to report whether or not tney were valid according to custom, and (if so) whether or not the chief had been destooled.

The Agonahene applied to the High Court for:

(1) a writ of certiorari for the removal into the court, and quashing, of the Governor-General's order j and

(2) a writ of prohibition directed to the Committee, prohibiting it from making the inquiry.

Held, an issue had been raised before the State Council, inquired into by the Council, and disposed of by it. The situation was thus outside the ambit of section 13 (1) of the State Councils (Ashan£i) Ordinance, which confers power upon the Governor-General to appoint a Committee of Inquiry only where it is inexpedient for a State Council to hold an inquiry, or where a council (seised of an inquiry) is unable to arrive at a conclusion without undue delay, or where a particular inquiry is not cognisable by a council. The purported appointment of a Committee of Inquiry was therefore ultra vires.

Case referred to:

(1) Quansah v. Tagoe (1956) 2 W.A.L.R. 90.

ApPLICATIONS for writs of certiorari and prohibition in respect of the appointment of a Committee of Inquiry by the Governor-General on July 5, 1958, under section 13 (1) of the State Councils (Ashanti) Ordinance, to inquire into certain destoolment charges against Nana Akyeampong Akwasi II, Agonahene.

SMITH J. referred to the facts and continued: Section 13 (1) of the State Councils (Ashanti) Ordinance, 1952, is in terms similar to those of section 8 (1) of the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, which subsection was summarised in the case of Quansah v. Tagoe (1) by Windsor-Aubrey J. as follows:

" The Committee may be appointed when:

"(1) it is inexpedient that a State Council should inquire into a constitutional matter, or

"(2) a State Council is unable to arrive at a conclusion without undue delay, or

"(3) such matter is not cognisable by a State Council."

'Where paragraphs 2 and 3 do not arise, I agree with the opinion of the learned judge that paragraph 1 can be operative only in respect of a constitutional matter into which the State Council has not inquired. In the case before me the State Council sat, and began the inquiry into destoolment charges preferred against Nana Akyeampong Akwasi II, Agonahene, by certain complainants. At the hearing the State Council put to the complainants certain questions and, finding as a result that they had no status to prefer the charges, dismissed the case. In my view, the first essential matter to be decided by any State Council must be whether the complainants have the necessary status to prefer destoolment charges before the tribunal, and where such status is lacking the State Council is acting properly in dismissing the case there and then.

The complainants have appealed against this decision of the State Council and, as far as I am informed, that appeal is still pending. Thecomplainants, in addition to their appeal, petitioned the Governor­General to appoint a Committee of Inquiry, preferring the same charges against the chief as they had preferred before the State Council. The Governor-General appointed a Committee accordingly.

In my opinion, the State Council having dismissed the charges, and that decision having been appealed against to the Appeal Com­missioner, there was no matter pending before the State Council on which the Governor-General could make the order that he did make.

 I have considered the affidavit filed by the Ministry of Local Government. That affidavit refers to section 8 of the State Councils (Ashanti) Ordinance. Section 8 (1) (B) is as follows:

" Subject to the provisions of section 13 of this Ordinance the Asanteman Council shall have jurisdiction to inquire into and determine any dispute as to the validity of the abdication or destoolment of the Paramount Chief of a State within the Asanteman. "

The wording of this subsection clearly presupposes that there has taken place a purported abdication or destoolment of a Paramount Chief, the validity of which is disputed. In the case before me, there has been neither abdication nor destoolment. I could have understood a reference to the original subsection (c) of section 8, but that subsection has been repealed.

  For these reasons I think the Governor-General was acting without jurisdiction when he made the order appointing the Committee of Inquiry, and I grant the applications which have been sought in this case.

Applications granted.
