                                                        November 18, 1957

        KPANYINLI AND ANOTHER v. WESTERN NZIMA STATE COUNCIL AND OTHERS

                                                 High Court, Western Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                               Acolatse J

 

 

State Councils-Mandamus-Jurisdiction-Duty of State Council to hear and determine complaints made to it-State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 5.

State Councils-Practice-Evidence-Plea of res judicata--Whether ruling of State Council a final or an interlocutory order.

This was an application for a writ of mandamus directed to the respondent State Council requiring them to determine a matter of a constitutional nature that was before them. Complaint had been made to the State Council by certain plaintiffs and to which the applicants were defendants. At the hearing before the State Council the defendants had claimed, as a preliminary point, that the issue was res judicata by reason of a decision given in an early dispute to which the ancestors of the plaintiffs and the applicants were parties. The State Council ruled in favour of the applicants on this submission whereupon the plaintiffs appealed to the Governor-General under section 11 of the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance. At the hearing of the appeal by the appeal commissioner appointed by the Governor-General this ruling was reversed. The decision of the appeal commissioner was accepted by the Governor­General. Thereafter the applicants sought to have the original hearing before the State Council resumed and, on the refusal of the State Council to resume, made this application to the High Court for mandamus. It was argued for the respondents that the issue had finally been disposed of by the decision of the Governor-General. The Governor-General has not remitted the case back to the State Council because, having reversed their decision, there was nothing to be remitted back and thus there was nothing before the State Council to be determined.

Held, (i) the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance is peremp­tory in its manner of conferring jurisdiction upon state councils. Thus, where a person who is a member of or subject to the authority of a state council makes complaint to the state council on a matter that is within its statutory jurisdiction (i.e., a matter of a constitutional nature), the state council is required to hear and determine such matter.

(ii) In this case the respondent State Council had given a ruling on the submission of res judicata. In so doing, although it might be said to have disposed of the issue immediately before it, it left untouched the true issue which was the matter of a constitutional nature raised by the plaintiff. The decision on the submission of res judicata must there­fore be regarded as an interlocutory order and mandamus must issue to require the State Council to hear and determine the substantive issue.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. By the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 5 (3), a state council, when exercising its statutory juris­diction, is required to conduct its proceedings according to customary law. In conferring jurisdiction, s. 5 (1) goes no further than to say that a State Council "shall have jurisdiction to inquire into and determine" any matter of a constitutional matter. His Lordship's judgment makes it clear that this is in fact peremptory and a State Council is bound to hear and determine any complaint on a matter within its jurisdiction. This perhaps follows from the direction that proceedings shall be conducted according to customary law.]

APPLICATION for a writ of mandamus directed to a state council requiring it to determine a dispute on a matter of a constitutional nature.

Kurankyi-Taylor for the applicants.

Blay for the respondents.

ACOLATSE J. outlined the facts and continued: The State Council iB a judicial body constituting a court under a well-defined statute in special matters of a constitutional nature. It has the power to issue out a summons upon the application of a would-be plaintiff against a defendant and to hear and determine any cause or matter within its statutory jurisdiction.

The facts in this motion are not in dispute. The contention by counsel for the respondents was that the Appeal Commissioner, having overruled the State Council on the question of res judicata, made no further direction to the State Council to rehear or do otherwise in the matter and did not remit the case back to the State Council. Mr. Blay went on in his argument to submit that the Appeal Commissioner acted under the functions and duties provided under the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 12, and, having discharged the duties conferred upon him and submitted his report, his functions ceased there. He cannot recommend or remit the case back to the State Council for hearing upon his own order. In short, Mr. Blay's argument was that the State Council cannot reopen the cases after the appeal decision without any order to that effect in the publication of the Report by the Executive in the Gazette. He contended that the order of the State Council upon the issue of res judicata was a final judgment and not interlocutory and therefore there was no case pending before the State Council as soon as it gave the judgment upon the matter. The State Council, he alleged, became functus officio and could not rehear a matter which had already been disposed of by it and from which an appeal had been lodged against its decision successfully.

I have considered the argument raised by both counsel in this motion, together with the affidavits filed by the applicants and respon­dents. The respondents in paragraph 6 of their affidavit recited" that the State Council, instead of hearing and determining the issue before it, dismissed the matter on the plea of res judicata raised by the second defendant." It can be seen upon the petition of appeal to His Excellency the Governor-General that the plaintiffs were aggrieved by the dismissal of their case upon a technical ground which they held did not apply, and that the Council was wrong in upholding the plea of res judicata against them. There is no other conclusion and inference I can draw from the recitals in the petition of appeal but that the matter appealed from was an interlocutory order and that the matter and issue between the parties had not been heard and determined by the State Council. The view I am bound to follow is that as soon as an appeal has been lodged against an interlocutory decision of a tribunal or court the matter is suspended pending the decision by the appeal court upon the interlocutory matter. The decision of the appeal court depends upon the question submitted to it. If the appeal court in considering whether a certain cause or matter from .the lower court is res judicata or not upholds the plea of res judicata, I would unhesi­tatingly say that concludes the matter in the trial court as being final between the parties. On the other hand, if the appeal court holds against the lower court that the plea of res judicata is not maintainable and allows and reverses the order of the lower court there is no other inference to draw but that the matter is still sub judice before the lower court for hearing and determination upon the merits between the parties. It is not final as between the parties in respect of the dispute between the parties upon the cause of action and the court below cannot be said to be functus officio in a cause or matter before it which it has not heard upon the merits.

I support the contention of the applicants and I hold that as soon as the Executive accepted and published the report of the Appeal Com­missioner reversing the decision of the State Council and allowing the appeal against the plea of res judicata, it was for the State Councilor upon the application of the parties to set a date for hearing and deter­mination of the cause or matter before it. It would be a denial of justice to refuse to proceed with the hearing of a case because there was no order for rehearing. The matter before His Excellency and the Appeal Commissioner in the petition of appeal was whether a certain legal point raised by one party against the other was maintainable on law. That was the direction sought by the appellants and the answer was duly given by the Appeal Commissioner and approved by the Executive. The natural consequence of the publication in the Gazette is that the matter or cause of action is still pending in ,the State Council.

I therefore grant this motion for an order of mandamus to issue ordering the respondents to hear and determine the cases in this motion.

Application granted.

s. G. D.
