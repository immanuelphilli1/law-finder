                                                          REGINA v. ANSERE

                                                                         Court of Appeal    

                                                     Korsah C.J., Granville Sharp J.A., Ollennu J

.

 

Criminal law-Stealing by a servant-Property in custody, control or posses­sion of servant-Position where servant only in constructive and not in actual possession-Criminal Code, c. 9, s. 288 (1).

Criminal law-Practice-Evidence-Effect of failure of prosecution to call a material witness.

This was an appeal from a conviction of stealing by a servant under the Criminal Code, s. 288 (1). The accused was treasurer of a local council and when his accounts were checked the cash in his safe showed an unexplained shortage of £656 2s. 10d. It was part of the defence that, with the knowledge of the council, revenue was collected by the accused's assistant, who was also responsible for making the entries in the cash book, and that money was, or could have been, entered in the cash book which the assistant, at the time the accounting check was made, had not handed over to the accused. The assistant gave a statement to the police admitting that he held money that was entered in the books but not handed over, but the figure he gave was £69 14s. 8d. and he said further, in his statement, that he had handed this to the accused in the presence of the officer who was conducting the accounting check. This last faciJ'fhis officer denied. The assistant was not called by the prosecu­tion to give evidence and the fact that he had made a statement, together with the statement itself, were elicited from a police witness in cross­examination.

Held, (i) a court is not entitled to reject a defence simply because it does not believe it. The court must consider, short of believing a defence, whether or not it may reasonably be true.

(ii) In this case a material witness was available who, if he had been called by the prosecution, could have disposed of the case one way or another. The failure to call such a witness in such circumstances must f mean that the prosecution had failed to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt.

R. v. Kuree (1941) 7 W.A.C.A. 175 applied.

(iii) Where a servant is charged with stealing his employer's property which he has in his custody or possession, it is essential to show actual possession by the servant in order to sustain the charge. ~t cannot be sustained where the servant has, to the knowledge of the employer, dele­gated his authority to hold the property to another, even if the delegation itself be wrongful.

Aboah v. The Queen (1953) 14 W.A.C.A. 252 distinguished.

Cases referred to:

(1) Aboah v. The Queen (1953) 14 W.A.C.A. 252. (2) R. v. Abisa Grunshie (1955) 1 W.A.L.R. 36. (3) R. v. Kuree (1941) 7 W.A.C.A. 175.

(4) R. v. Abubu Adenjimah and Another (1942) 8 W.A.C.A. 193.

APPEAL from a conviction for stealing by a servant by Simpson J. sitting with assessors on January 29, 1958, at Accra Assizes.

Akiwumi, Crown counsel, for the Crown.

Dua Sakayi for the appellant.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by OLLENNU J. :

The appellant, was charged with stealing under the Criminal Code, s. 288 (1). He was tried by Simpson J. sitting with assessors, and was convicted on January 29, 1958, and sentenced to two years' im­prisonment with hard labour.

The case for the prosecution is briefly as follows: The appellant was treasurer of the Kibi Local Council. His duties were to receive revenue collected by revenue collectors of the council, enter them in a cash book and issue receipts for them; prepare vouchers for payments to be made on behalf of the council and when the vouchers were passed by the chairman make the payments and record them in the cash book. He had been treasurer for a long time. On July 13, 1956, the accounts covering the period May 5 to June 30, 1956, were checked by a Government Senior Executive Officer, and the cash books, both as to receipts and payments, were found to have been correctly entered up; but the cash on hand in the safe showed a shortage of £656 2s. 10d. The appellant was not able to show how that shortage occurred. The matter was reported to the police and the appellant was arrested and subsequently charged.

Had those been the only facts in the case, the principle laid down in the case of Aboah v. The Queen (1) would apply, and the conviction of the appellant would be good.

The defence is that the collection of revenue was made not by the appellant but by his assistant one E. Sasu Twum, popularly called " Sasu," who also kept and entered up the cash book. Sasu periodi­cally handed over the moneys he collected to the appellant who gave him an unofficial receipt for them and afterwards reconciled the accounts with Sasu to find out whether he had paid in all he had collected. Further, that the appellant had not had time to reconcile the accounts with Sasu before the checking by the Senior Executive Officer took place, to know how much money he, Sasu, had with him.

This defence put up by the appellant was in substance the same as a statement he made to the police on August 3, 1956, when he was cautioned and questioned about the shortage.

It appears that before the appellant gave the written statement he had told the police about the reconciliation with Sasu, and of the fact that Sasu had some moneys with him which he had not handed over to him at the time of checking. This was borne out by the fact that before asking the appellant to make a statement, the police sergeant detailed to investigate the case had asked Sasu to submit his own statement and in consequence Sasu wrote and sent to the police a statement which was undated. That statement was brought to light through cross-examination of the police sergeant; it was then admitted in evidence at the instance of the defence.

The defence that the collection of revenue and keeping of books, including the cash book, was carried out by Sasu was corroborated by witnesses for the prosecution, and was fully borne out by Sasu's statement to the police. More important still, Sasu in his statement admitted that, at the date of the checking of the account by the Senior Executive Officer, he had some money which he should have handed to the appellant. But he said that the amount was only £69 14s. 8d., and that after the checking the appellant called upon him to produce whatever moneys he then had and thereupon he produced and handed the £69 14s. 8d. to the appellant in the presence of the Senior Execu­tive Officer. This allegation by Sasu that he handed over money to the appellant was denied by the Senior Executive Officer, the clerk and the chairman of the council, each of whom deposed that Sasu was not in the office at any time on the occasion of the checking.

Sasu was not called as a witness by the prosecution. Thus at the close of the case for the prosecution it had been conclusively established that Sasu was the officer who received revenue from the collectors even though the appellant was the treasurer, that it was he who kept the cash book, that at the date of the checking of the accounts he still had some moneys of the council in his possession, and that he did not hand over such moneys as were then in his possession to the appellant.

Upon those facts each of the assessors expressed the opinion that the appellant was not guilty. The learned judge, however, as he was en­titled under the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 300 (2), found the appel­lant guilty and convicted him.

After stating the facts which he accepted, the learned judge in his judgment said, "That leaves two alternatives-negligence or theft by the accused": and after some observations on the defence he con­cluded:

" I do not believe the evidence of the accused. Despite absence of any evidence from his assistant treasurer the only inference I am able to draw from the facts is that the accused converted the sum of £656 2s. l0d. to his own use. "

Where facts are capable of both innocent and criminal interpreta­tion of the conduct of an accused person, a court is bound to take the innocent interpretation unless there is some definite evidence or circumstances which completely exclude that interpretation.

The only reason given by the learned judge for rejecting the inno­cent interpretation in this case is that he did not believe the evidence of the appellant. In this respect the learned judge misdirected him­self. A court is not entitled to reject a defence simply because it does not believe it. The court must consider, short of believing the defence, whether it might reasonably be true: R. v. Abisa Grunshie (2).

No doubt the learned judge came to that erroneous conclusion either because he overlooked the statement Sasu had given to the police and which was in evidence, or if he did not overlook it, he failed to appreciate its significance. Had he properly directed himself on the admissions by witnesses for the prosecution that the collection of revenue and the keeping of the cash book were done by Sasu, and parti­cularly Sasu's own admission in his statement to the police that at the date of the checking of the accounts he was in possession of moneys belonging to the council which he had not paid over to the appellant, the learned judge could not possibly have disbelieved the defence; in any event he would have found that it might reasonably be true.

The finding of the learned judge, that despite the absence of any evidence from the assistant treasurer, the only inference he could draw from the facts is that the appellant converted the money, amounts to misdirection on the onus of proof.

The principle of law is that if there is one witness whose evidence would settle the case one way or another and the prosecution fails to call that witness their case must fail since in .that event they have not proved their case beyond all reasonable doubt: R. v. J{uree (3). But the failure to call a witness whose evidence would be only of a negative character is not fatal to conviction: R. v. Abubu Adenjimah and Another (4).

In this case it appears that the prosecution tried to suppress material evidence, namely, the fact that the assistant treasurer, Mr. Sasu, had possession of some money which he should have handed over to the appellant to enable him properly to account for it. And there being evidence led by the prosecution themselves that Sasu received money from the revenue collectors, entered it in the cash book, and afterwards paid it over to the appellant, the prosecution's case would be incomplete without further evidence that all the moneys so collected by him were paid over to the appellant before or at the checking.

Sasu was therefore the one person whose evidence would have settled the case one way or the other, namely, whether or not he had paid over to the appellant all the moneys he had entered up in the cash book as collected on behalf of the council. But the prosecution did not call him. Consequently this court would have been bound to quash the conviction even on that ground alone.

In the case of Aboah v. The Queen (1), referred to above, it was held that where a shortage occurs in property which a servant holds for his employers, and is unable to produce the property or give an account as to how it was lost, the servant could properly be convicted of theft of that shortage ..

That principle can only apply to cases where the servant had actual as opposed to constructive possession of the property. It certainly will not apply to cases where the servant had to the knowledge of his employers delegated his authority to hold the property, however wrongly, to a subordinate servant; for in that case the actual possession necessary in law to sustain the charge is not in him.

The learned judge in this case misdirected himself on the legal possession requisite in cases of this nature, and as already pointed out, he either failed to give due consideration to Sasu's statement tendered in evidence, or to appreciate its significance in the case. The appellant was therefore wrongly convicted.

It is for these reasons that we allowed the appeal.

Conviction quashed.

S. G. D.
