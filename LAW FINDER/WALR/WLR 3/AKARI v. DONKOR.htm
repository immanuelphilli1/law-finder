                                            November 30, 1957

                                                      AKARI v. DONKOR.

                      High Court, Central Judicial Division, Land Court                         

                                             Adumua-Bossman J

 

 

Native custom-Stool land-Nature of stool-subject's interest in stool land in, his occupation-Whether possible for stool to vary  customary dyes and services.

The defendant, a stool-subject, was in occupation of an area of stool land to which he had succeeded on the death of his uncle and for which he paid to the stool, by way of "tribute," the annual sum of £1 4s. This figure had been negotiated between the defendant and the plaintiff's predecessor in office as holder of the stool. '1'he plaintiff, when he was enstooled, demanded increased annual tribute from the defendant and required him to enter into a fresh agreement to pay an annual sum of £2 8s. in respect of his land. The defendant refused, whereupon the plaintiff sued in the Native Court for an order compelling the defendant to enter into a new agreement and for damages. The Native Court found for the plaintiff. It was not disputed that the defendant had succeeded his uncle and had thereby become vested of his uncle's estate in the land, nor that the defendant had discharged his proper customary obligations (i.e., the giving of "aseda") when he had negotiated the annual tribute with the plaintiff's predecessor. On appeal to the Land Court:

Held, where a stool-subject is in occupation of stool land for a deter­minable estate it is not open to the stool (the holder of the reversion) unilateral1y to abrogate or vary the terms or conditions upon which the subj ect holds his estate of the stool.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The learned judge here draws the distinction between the position of a stool-subject in occupation of stool land and that of a stranger who, by express or implied agreement with the stool, is also in occupation of stool land. The former has an inheritable estate (sometimes loosely referred to as a usufructuary interest) determinable in certain circumstances but held, as this case shows, on fixed services and dues. The latter holds by virtue of an agreement with the owner of the absolute estate (the stool), usual1y on terms that the yield of the land is divisible in an agreed proportion between occupant and stool. Akofi v. Wiresi and Abagya (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 257, is authority for saying that such agreement also creates a determinable estate which (apart from forfeiture for breach of condition) is determinable only by the grantee and his successors in title.]

APPEAL from a decision of the North Birim Native Court" A " on December 14, 1956, in favour of the plaintiff in an action in which the plaintiff sought an order requiring the defendant to enter into a new agreement concerning his tenure of land.

de Graft Johnson for the plaintiff. Short for the defendant.

ADUMUA-BOSSMAN J. In this appeal against a judgment of the North Birim Native Court " A" dated December 14, 1956, the plaintiff's claim was as follows:

(a) "For the defendant to show cause why he should not be ordered to enter into tenancy agreement with the plaintiff in respect of defendant's cocoa farm situated at Bontodiase ... on the plaintiff's stool land, and,

(b) £100 damages for refusing to go to the plaintiff for the said agreement, but has continued to reap the crops from plaintiff's stool land."

The Native Court gave judgment upholding the claim and ordered the defendant to pay £25 damages together with costs forthwith, and to enter into the new agreement with the plaintiff within one month from the date of the judgment.

It was the plaintiff's case that when he was enstooled he found many people residingi in Ofoase who had entered into agreements with his stool in respect of their farms on his stool land and amongst these were the defendant and his brother, who were paying £1 4s. yearly. The plaintiff said that he called the defendant and told him that he had discovered that the yearly tribute of £1 4s. which he had been paying was in respect of five farms for himself and his people, therefore he was asking that a new agreement should be entered into whereby yearly tribute should be paid in respect of each farm separately. The plaintiff admits that the defendant then explained that originally the yearly tribute for the five farms was £2 8s. but that during the time of the plaintiff's immediate predecessor he, the defendant, had approached the predecessor and his elders for a reduction and had urged as a ground for such a request that he was connected with the stool. '1'he chief and his elders had thereupon agreed and made the reduction from £2 8s. to £1 4s. annually, and the defendant explained further that on that occasion he made or gave a " thank-offering" (" aseda ") to the chief and his elders for the favour shown him. The plaintiff stated that in spite of the defendant's explanation, he and his elders insisted that separate agreements should be entered into; that the defendant's brother, Yaw Yeboah, agreed and made a separate agree­ment for his farm at £2 8s. yearly, that two other women brought by Yaw Yeboah also made separate agreements at £1 4s. each yearly, and another brother, Yaw Duka, also entered into a new agreement at 13s. a year, but that the defendant had refused to enter into the new agree­ment, hence the action against him. The plaintiff explained that a further reason why he and his elders insisted on a new agreement by the defendant in respect of his farm, which really was inherited by him from his uncle, was that he as a relative of the stool family had been allotted a portion of stool forest for free cultivation and he had cultivated and was occupying that farm free of any tribute.

The defendant's case was explained by the plaintiff during the course of his case, i. e., that agreement had been reached between him and the plaintiff's predecessor for the payment of an annual rate of £14s.

The issue therefore which the Native Court had to determine, it seems to me, was whether or not, by native custom, the plaintiff as the new occupant of the stool could abrogate the tenancy agreement for £1 4s. yearly made by his predecessor and insist on the defendant accepting a new agreement with new terms from him.

The Native Court in their judgment said as follows:

" The agreement which he is being asked to do is in respect of· the farm he succeeded from his uncle. The farm therefore is family property. It is perfectly right in accordance with 'native custom for the plaintiff to have asked the defendant to slaughter sheep even though if we accept the fact that he is a genuine prince to the Stool"

With great respect I cannot agree with that view of the Native Court. They appear to me have missed the substance of the matter, which is, whether it is open to a new chief to abrogate a binding agreement duly entered into with his predecessor. There would be no security for any tenant, if such were the position according to native custom. If it is accepted that for the five farms, including that in respect of which a new separate agreement is being insisted on, the stool had, during the time of the plaintiff's immediate predecessor agreed to accept an annual tribute of £1 4s. then there is an end of the matter: the plaintiff cannot abrogate or alter that agreement.

Where stool land has been cultivated by a stranger, i.e., a person not a subject of the stool, without the permission of the stool occupant and in circumstances amounting to trespass on the part of the stranger, there is generally some form of local custom or usage in every locality providing for the payment to the stool by the stranger of some portion of the produce resulting from such cultivation without permission. In most areas there is one-third (" abusa") but in some, one-half (" abusu "). Where, however, the cultivation was originally with the knowledge and permission of the stool occupant and on clear and express terms agreed between him and the stranger farmer, then those terms can afterwards be varied only by the mutual consent of the parties, not at the whim of the stool occupant or of his successors in title.

For the foregoing reasons the appeal is hereby allowed, the judgment of the Native Court set aside and in place thereof is substituted a judgment dismissing the plaintiff's claim.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
