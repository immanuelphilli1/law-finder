                                                    November 28, 1958.

                       COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. BOAME AND ANOTHER.

                         High Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                             Murphy J         

 

 

Criminal Law - Police - Forfeiture at recognisances - Procedure to be followed- Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10, s. 104.

Felix Boateng was charged with conspiracy to defraud. He was released on a bail bond for which Kwasi Boame and another became sureties. Boateng failed to surrender to his bail.

Section 104 of the Criminal Procedure Code provides as follows:

"(1) Whenever it is proved to the satisfaction of a Court by which a recognizance under this Code has been taken, or when the recognizance is for appearance before a Court, to the satisfaction of such Court, that such recognizance has been forfeited, the Court shall record the grounds of such proof, and may call upon any person bound by such recognizance to pay the penalty thereof, or to show cause why it should not be paid.

"(2) If sufficient cause is not shown and the penalty is not paid, the Court may proceed to recover the same by forfeiting any sum deposited in pursuance of section 99 or by issuing a warrant for the attachment and sale of the moveable property belonging to such person or his estate if he be dead ....

"(4) If such penalty is not paid and cannot be recovered by such attachment and sale, the person so bound shall be liable, by order of the Court which issued the warrant, to imprisonment without hard labour for a term which may extend to six months .... "

Boateng's two sureties were brought before the Senior Magistrate, Kumasi, to show cause why their recognisances should not be forfeited. A police officer gave evidence in which he said inter alia that the sureties had given wrong addresses (an allegation which the sureties denied at the subsequent hearing of their appeal). They were not called on to give evidence, but both said tnat they could produce Boateng if given an opportunity.

The Senior Magistrate ordered that the recognisances be forfeited, and that each surety pay £1,000, or suffer six months' imprisonment in default. They were then committed to prison, though no prior attempt had been made (as required by section 104 (4)) to recover the penalty by attachment and sale under section 104 (2). They appealed to the High Court.

Held, upon an order for payment of money by a surety after for­feiture of his recognisance, no order for committal to prison in default of payment may be made unless a warrant for the attachment and sale of his property has issued, and the amount has not been recovered thereunder.

ApPEAL from a decision of the Senior Magistrate, Rumasi, on September 25, 1958, forfeiting the appellants' recognisances.

The appellants appeared in person. Amissah for the respondent.

MURPHY J. stated the facts and continued: Under section 104 (2) of the Criminal Procedure Code it is necessary in such cases, in default of payment, for the court first to issue a warrant for the attachment and sale of property. It is only if the amount cannot be recovered by such attachment and sale, that an order of committal to prison can be made. The Senior Magistrate's order should have been that each surety was to pay £1,000, or to suffer six months' imprisonment in default of distress.

Further, I think it might have been desirable to give the appellants an opportunity to produce the accused before making any order.

I therefore allow the appeal, and set aside the Senior Magistrate's order. I further order that the appellants are to produce Felix Boateng before the Magistrate's Court on or before December 19, 1958, or to show cause in the Magistrate's Court on that date why their recognisances should not be forfeited. In the event of the' Magistrate's deciding to forfeit their recognisances again, he should consider exercising his power of remission under section 104 (5) of the Criminal Procedure Code, having regard to the term of imprisonment (a little over two months) which has been served by the appellants in conse­quence of the previous order.

Appeals allowed.

S. G. D.
