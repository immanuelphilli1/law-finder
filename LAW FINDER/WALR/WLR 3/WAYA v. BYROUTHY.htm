                                                          May 17, 1958

                                                          WAYA v. BYROUTHY

                                             High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                                       Adumua-Bossman J

.

 

Sale of Goods-Credit sale-Agreement for payment by instalments but without agreement as to size and frequency of instalments-Subsequent seizure and purported sale by vendor-Purported hire-purchase agreement between subsequent and first purchasers-Whether subsequent agreement enforce­able.

Evidence- Written agreement-Illiteracy of party to document-Necessity for other party relying on the document to prove that the illiterate under­stands the document.

In 1955 Mustapha Waya bought a Bedford lorry from Appiah Wilson on credit for £950 (plus the insurance premium of £98 14s.). Waya was to pay this sum to Wilson by instalments. There was no agreement as to the amount or the frequency of the instalments. The first-£110-was paid on November 24, 1955, and by January, 1957, Waya had paid £782. In January and February, 1957, he paid Wilson nothing, and towards the end of February Wilson seized the lorry.

Waya had previously been in the employ of one, Byrouthy, for many years, and on March 4, 1957, Waya and Byrouthy called on Wilson, Byrouthy handing to Wilson the unpaid balance in full. At Waya's request Wilson made the receipt out in favour of Byrouthy. The same day Byrouthy had a hire-purchase agreement prepared, which Waya (an illiterate) signed with his thumbprint; under it Waya was to hire­purchase the lorry from Byrouthy, who was given by the agreement the right of seizure in the event of Waya's default on the instalments. On July 17, 1957, Byrouthy seized the lorry, and subsequently sold it.

Waya sued Byrouthy, claiming the return of the lorry or its value (£700), with damages for unlawful seizure and for loss of earnings at £8 a day as from the date of seizure.

Held, (i) the initial seizure by Wilson was wrongful, since there was no agreement as to the amount and frequency of the instalments to be paid. Even if there had been such agreement, there was no evidence of agreement that property in the lorry should remain in Wilson, or that he should have any other right of seizure. The transaction was thus one of credit-sale and therefore the property in the lorry passed to Waya at the time of the original transaction. In consequence Wilson had no property in the vehicle to pass to Byrouthy in 1957. Byrouthy was thus incapable of entering as hirer, into a hire-purchase agreement with Waya by which he reserved the right of seizure upon default of payment.

(ii) Where an illiterate executes a document, any other party to the document who relies upon it must prove that it was read over, and, if necessary, interpreted to the illiterate.

Cases referred to:

(1) Koney v. Union Trading Co., Ltd. (1934) 2 W.A.C.A. 188. (2) Tarling v. Baxter (1827) 6 B. & C. 360; 108 E.R. 484.

(3) Kwarnin v. Kufuor. Judgments of the Privy Council on Appeal from the Gold Coast, 1874-1928, p. 28.

(4) McEntire and Another v. Orossley Bros., Ltd. [1895] A.C. 457. (5) Koft v. Mensah (1930) 1 W.A.C.A. 76.

(6) Sei v. Ojori (1926) Full Court, 1926-1929, p. 87.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. This last case was overruled by Delor v. Foli (1952) 14 W.A.C.A. 54.]

ACTION by a borrower to obtain the return of a lorry unlawfully seized by his lender.

Larbi for plaintiff. Franklin for defendant.

ADUMUA-BosSMAN J. outlined the facts and continued: The short point for determination is: in whom was the general property in the vehicle at the material time?

The plaintiff's evidence was that the vehicle was his property from 1955. He arranged with a friend of his, one Appiah Wilson, a store­keeper of G. B. Ollivant, Swedru, who bought it for him, and agreed to receive repayment of the purchase-price by instalments. "When Wilson delivered the vehicle to him, Wilson also issued to him a pass-book in which the price was entered as £950, together with insurance £98 14s. Od., and he was to bring the pass-book with him, whenever he was coming to make any payment on account, for it to be recorded in the pass .. book. As appeared in the pass-book, beginning with a first instalment payment of £110 on November 24, 1955, the plaintiff had by January, 1957, made part payment of £782 on account of the purchase price of the vehicle.

It is worthy of mention that beyond the oral agreement, and the pass-book to show the state of accounts, there was no written agree­ment. It was the simplest possible form of sale on credit.

Appiah Wilson, a witness for the plaintiff, gave the following evidence, which in my view was very material on the question of the ownership of the vehicle:

" In 1955 plaintiff was the owner of Bedford 3-Ton lorry No.

AE.1321. He even asked me before I bought the lorry for him at £950-1 gave the lorry to him. "

From the further evidence of Wilson it appears that during January and February, 1957, the plaintiff made no instalment payments on account of the balance outstanding in respect of the vehicle, whereupon he seized the vehicle. The seizure was clearly wrongful, since by the oral agreement no fixed instalments had been agreed on to be paid monthly. The plaintiff was to pay whatever amount he could, and no time had been fixed when the payment of the price was to be com­pleted. Moreover, Wilson had transferred ownership upon delivery of the vehicle, and had therefore no right to re-take it because part of the purchase price was still outstanding. Be that as it may, continuing his evidence Wilson said:

" About a week after (the seizure) he came with defendant, Byrouthy. They paid me the balance of the amount. When he was paying the amount I asked plaintiff whether the receipt should be issued in his own name or his master's name."

At this stage the Court interposed and asked him:

" But why were you asking him in whose name receipt should be issued? "

His reply was:

"Well, I knew the receipt should be in Mustapha's name, because he asked me and I bought the lorry for him, but I wanted to be sure."

Continuing, the witness said:

" He told me to issue 'the receipt in his master's name, so I did that. Then Mustapha took the lorry away. The payment received from plaintiff was £352. I added cost of insurance and cost of 4 outer covers I had supplied him, so his account with me about the lorry was completely cleared."

As to how the defendant had come to accompany plaintiff to Wilson to pay the balance outstanding on the vehicle, the plaintiff explained that when he was sore pressed for the money, as the defendant had been a man he had served for a long number of years as store-boy and servant, he went to him and explained the whole circumstances to him, and told him that" I knew nobody to whom I could go except to him, so he should help me and advance me the amount to payoff Appiah Wilson." As to what happened after the settlement with Wilson, the plaintiff's evidence was that he inquired from the defendant what interest he would charge on the amount advanced, but the defendant replied:

" I do not propose to charge you any interest; but you will make a 'paper' and we will put it down and when you have finished payment we'll destroy it. He told me this in pidgin English. Then he called his son by name J amil and instructed him to prepare the paper. The son prepared some typed docu­ment. When it was finished, defendant called me and said' this is the paper; put your thumb-print to it '-which I did .... He did not give me a copy."

The defendant's evidence was as follows:

" I was in my store, Mustapha Waya (the plaintiff) came and told me that his own lorry (had been) seized by store-keeper of G. B. Ollivant, Mr. Wilson. If you can try to go and buy it for me, I will take it from you and pay you under hire-purchase I went and paid £352, and Mr. Wilson gave me a  agreement. receipt. "

The defendant therefore relies on the receipt and the hire-purchase agreement as giving him the legal right to seize the vehicle.

In so far as the defendant's evidence is in conflict with the plain­tiff's, I have no hesitation in preferring the plaintiff's version. I do not believe that the plaintiff told him" If you can go and buy it for me, I will take it from you, and pay you under hire-purchase agree­ment." I am satisfied that the plaintiff, a completely illiterate man, does not even know the import of a hire-purchase agreement, and most certainly did not mention it when he approached the defendant for help that morning.

At any rate it was never suggested to him in cross-examination that he asked the defendant to buy the lorry from Wilson, and that he, the plaintiff, would then buy it on hire-purchase from the defendant. From my own experience of life in this country I am satisfied that what the plaintiff was more likely to have asked the defendant for was a loan with which to payoff Wilson, the loan to be repaid by instal­ments in due course. This is in a measure confirmed by the following evidence given by the defendant during his examination-in-chief:

" In consideration of my purchasing the lorry and plaintiff not having any deposit to put down-I put some money on top £200."

The defendant, as already indicated, relies on the receipt, which reads: " Received from Mr. R Byrouthy the sum of Three Hundred and fifty-two pounds (£352) being full settlement of one Bedford 3-Ton Lorry No. AE.1321."

But I cannot see that the terms of the receipt help the defendant. It is quite consistent with the plaintiff's case that the defendant paid that amount on the plaintiff's behalf in settlement of the amount then outstanding on the vehicle.

The questions for this court to resolve are:

(1) whether there was any purported sale of the vehicle by Wilson to the defendant; and, more important,

(2) whether Wilson had any dominium (i.e., ownership or general

property in the vehicle) to pass to him.

The latter is essentially a matter of law, and, the defendant being a non-native, the law applicable is the English common law of sale (Kaney v. Union Trading 00., Ltd. (1)).

As far back as 1827, in the case of Tarling v. Baxter (2):

" Where A on 4th January agreed to sell to B a stack of hay for £145 to be paid on 4th February the same to be allowed to stand on A's premises till 1st May and B stipulating that the hay should not be cut until it was paid for-Held this was a contract for an immediate not a future sale and that the property in the hay passed by it immediately to the vendee; so that the same having been on 20th January before the date for payment of the purchase price destroyed by fire the loss fell on the vendee. "

Holroyd J. said:

" Now in the case of a sale of goods, if nothing remains to be done on the part of the seller as between him and the buyer before the thing purchased is to be delivered, the property in the goods immediately passes to the buyer and that in the price to the seller. "

Tarling v. Baxter is followed by a host of other subsequent cases to the same effect. It follows that Wilson had no ownership of the vehicle on March 4, 1957. Nemo dat quod non habet. Wilson could not transfer any general property or ownership in the vehicle to the defendant, even if he had attempted or purported to do so. But his evidence was clear. He did not sell to the defendant, but merely took from defendant the outstanding balance due from the plaintiff on the vehicle, and issued him the receipt to indicate that the money was paid by him on behalf of the plaintiff.

As to the hire-purchase agreement, I accept (as already indicated) the plaintiff's evidence as to how he came to sign it by means of his thumb-print. Lord Kinnear, reading the judgment of their lordships of the Privy Council in Kwamin v. Kufuor (3) laid it down that:

" There is no presumption that a native of Ashanti who does not understand English and cannot read Or write has appreciated the meaning and effect of an English legal instrument because he is alleged to have set his mark to it by way of signature."

As has always been held in this court, the burden is always on the party relying on the document to prove that it was read over (and, if necessary, interpreted) to the illiterate person, and that it was apparently understood by him. No such evidence has been adduced on behalf of the defendant in this case. But in any event, as also already indicated, the defendant not having any" general property" in the vehicle, he is legally incapable of making an agreement in respect of it reserving any right to seize it upon default in payment of money due to him on the equitable security of the vehicle (McEntire and Another v. Orossley Bros., Ltd. (4), Kofi v. Mensah (5) and Sei v. Gfori (6).

In the result I am satisfied that the legal position is clear that the seizure by the defendant was wrongful, and constituted trover or conversion.

The defendant admits that he sold it after holding it for some time following the seizure. He is therefore liable for the value thereof as claimed in the writ, £700. Damages at £200 and mesne profits at £8 per day from date of seizure (July 17, 1957) have also been claimed. Both cannot be claimed. But I am satisfied that the plaintiff is entitled to damages in respect of his loss of the vehicle's earnings for a period of three months during all working days (i.e., excluding Sundays) from July 17,1957, during which, by due diligence, he could have prosecuted an action in this court to obtain possession of the vehicle. I assess such damages at £6 per working day for 79 working days, amounting to £474.

 

Judgment for the plaintiff.
