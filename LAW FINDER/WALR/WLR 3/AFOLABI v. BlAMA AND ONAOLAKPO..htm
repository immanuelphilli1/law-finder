                                                         January 25, 1958.

                                            AFOLABI v. BlAMA AND ONAOLAKPO.

 High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

Simpson J.

                                                 

 

Contract-Licence-Agreement conferring mining rights-Implied condition that land concerned not subject to prior, conflicting, rights.

The plaintiff and his deceased brother had entered into an agreement with the defendant, as caretaker of the stool lands concerned, whereby the former were granted a licence to mine for diamonds over a defined area. It subsequently transpired that the land the subject of the licence was part of a larger area the subject of a previous concession by the stool to a mining company.

Held, it was an implied condition of the agreement between the plaintiff and the defendant that the land over which the licence was granted was not subject to any prior rights that would conflict with those granted to the plaintiff. As such prior rights were in existence the defendant was in breach of the agreement and was therefore liable to the plaintiff in damages.

ACTION for damages for fraud. The claim was amended during the trial to include a claim for damages for breach of condition.

Bentsi Enchill for the plaintiff.

Dua Sakyi for the defendant.

SIMPSON J. The plaintiff in this case claimed damages for fraudu­lent misrepresentation of the defendant's title to allow the plaintiff to dig for diamonds. It was alleged that the defendant falsely represented that the part of the Akwabusu Stream granted to the plaintiff and his deceased brother had not been leased to any mining company. In the course of the trial the claim was amended by the addition of an alterna­tive claim for damages for breach of condition that the area in question had not been previously leased to any mining company.

It was not disputed that the plaintiff and his brother were partners, nor that the plaintiff was successor to the property of his brother. Nor was it in dispute that they approached the defendant through the medium of one Jima Onaolakpo to obtain a lease of land for the pur­pose of digging for diamonds. The defendant as Odikro of Takorawase was caretaker of the land concerned. An agreement dated May 1, 1952, was entered into between the Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa State and the defendant on the one part and the plaintiff's brother on the other part allowing him to dig for diamonds in the K waku Esua Stream specified on the plan attached" not yet leased to any Mining Company." On production of the plan to the Chief Inspector of Mines a digging licence was refused on the ground that the area was within a mining concession. Another plan was prepared-it is dated January 14, 1953-showing an area of land along the Akwabusu Stream but a licence was again refused. The defendant then, according to the plain­tiff, said that as he and his brother had no proper surveyor she herself would have a plan prepared. A third plan was then prepared by an­other surveyor; it is dated February 14, 1953. This clearly shows the same stretch of the stream as the second plan but in the key plan the Akwabusu Stream is extended for approximately one mile beyond the limits shown in the field sheet from which it and the key to the second plan were traced and the area leased to the plaintiff's brother is shown on this extension. This plan was approved both by the mining company concerned and by the Chief Inspector of Mines and a digging licence was granted on March 18, 1953. The first of the monthly returns submitted by -the plaintiff's brother to the Mines Department was in respect of that month.

In November 1953 the plaintiff was arrested for working within the concession of the mining company. The plaintiff claimed that he was working within the area leased to his brother and that he was told in the presence of the defendant that all the land given to him was within the concession. This was corroborated by the evidence of the police sergeant. The defendant contended that the plaintiff was working outside that area and that he and his brother were not stopped from working within the area leased to them. If this is the case it is difficult to understand why she offered other land after the arrests, as the plaintiff stated in his evidence. If the plaintiff is speaking the truth it follows that the area in question had previously been leased to the mining company. (There is no suggestion of any subsequent lease to the mining company concerned.)

I was favourably impressed by the plaintiff as a witness and after carefully checking his evidence with the documentary evidence I have no hesitation in concluding that he was a truthful witness. Not only from her demeanour but also from inconsistencies in her evidence I find the defendant a less reliable witness. I accept the evidence of the plaintiff that he was arrested while working within the area leased to his brother.

Counsel for the plaintiff did not attempt to press his claim in respect of fraudulent misrepresentation. I need say no more than that I found insufficient evidence to support such a claim. I am, however, satisfied that there was a breach of condition and ~he defen­dant is the party to the agreement mainly responsible. It is clear that none of the plans showed an area not yet leased .to any mining company. The third plan shows the same stretch of stream as the pre­vious plan. The key plan shows this stretch outside the C.A.S.T. concession and a digging licence was granted. The plaintiff started to dig within this stretch of the stream and was arrested while digging there. It is clear that this third plan wrongly showed the area outside the concession. As I have said I found .the plaintiff a truthful witness. In spite of some inconsistencies with his evidence at the earlier trial, when a larger amount was claimed, I accept his evidence as to the amount totalling £1,101 paid to various persons in respect of the negotiations, the execution of the agreement and the preparation of the plans. Admittedly he was not present when £100 was paid to the clerks at Kibi on the first occasion but corroboration was provided by the witness Jima who had been co-defendant in the earlier action and on that account was unlikely to err in favour of the plaintiff. The plaintiff was not present when £70 was paid to the clerks on the second occasion but he knew how much his brother had taken with him and clearly had no reason to distrust his brother as a partner. No evidence was adduced nor argument put forward in support of damages beyond these amounts. There will be judgment for the plaintiff against the defendant for £1,101,

Judgment for the plaintiff.

S. G. D.
