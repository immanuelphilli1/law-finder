                                                    M. K. HINA WI & CO. v. BASSIL.

                                                                Court of Appeal

                             Korsah C.J., van Lare and Granville Sharp JJ.A    

 

 

Landlord and Tenant-Rent control-Standard rent-Contractual tenancy at rent lower than standard rent-Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, ss. 5 (3) (a) and 7 (1).

Statutes-Interpretation-Legislation to be interpreted in light of existing law-Necessity for clear intention to abrogate existing common law.

Landlord and Tenant-Recovery of possession--Non-payment of rent-Re­lief in discretion of Court.

The defendant tenant held certain properties of the plaintiff com­pany on leases that expired in 1959 and 1964 and at a total rental of £66 13s. 4d. per month. The plaintiff landlord, during the currency of these leases and by virtue of the Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2, of 1952, ss. 5 (3) (a) and 7 (1), made application to a Rent Assessment Committee for this rent to be varied. On January 8, 1957, the Rent Assessment Committee made an order varying the existing standard rent of £66 13s. 4d. per month to £92 per month. The plaintiffs, notwith­standing the currency of the leases, demanded the higher rent forthwith from the defendant and, when the latter refused to pay the increase, commenced this action, seeking to recover the excess as arrears of rent

and also seeking an order for possession. There was no dispute between the parties on the facts, and the only matter in issue was as to whether a standard rent, fixed in accordance with the provisions of the Ordi­nance, and being of greater amount than a rent payable under the terms of a contractual agreement freely negotiated between landlord and tenant, was, by reason of the Ordinance, enforceable against a tenant notwithstanding the currency of the existing contractual agree­ment. The defendant asserted that, although such a standard rent could be determined during the currency of a contractual agreement by the Rent Assessment Committee, it could not be enforced until the existing contractual tenancy was determined under the terms of the tenancy or by operation of law.

Held, (i) it is a principle of the interpretation of legislation that clear words are necessary, or in default of them words from which a necessary implication is to be derived, if vested rights under the common law, whether arising from contract or otherwise, are to be affected by the legislation.

(ii) (Korsah C.J. dissenting.) No such clear intention is manifest in the Rent Control Ordinance and, therefore, it confers no power upon a Rent Assessment Committee to avoid the terms of a contract freely entered into by the parties to it, either of the Committee's own motion or upon the application of a party to the contract.

Per Granville Sharp and van Lare JJ.A: "It would in our opinion be a novel and dangerous innovation to introduce into our law, to hold that such an administratively appointed Committee (Rent Assessment Committee) could of its own motion avoid contracts between parties and make for the parties some new contract fundamentally different from that into which they had freely entered. Weare of opinion that equally the Committee cannot destroy a contract upon the application of any party to it."

Amah v. Picas (1954) 2 W.A.L.R. 79 overruled.

(iii) The standard rent of premises merely fixes an upward limit, and a landlord is free to negotiate a letting at a lower figure if he chooses to do so. Conversely a landlord cannot, if there is a current lease, demand from the tenant an increase of the contractual rent to the standard rent if that is higher. The contractual rent remains the recoverable rent until the termination of the current tenancy.

Noshie v. Amarteifio (1947) 12 W.A.C.A. 115 doubted.

(iv) On any interpretation of the Rent Control Ordinance the facts of the present case, where the defendant, supported by legal authority, had done no more than decline to pay an excess over the rent he had agreed to pay, were not such as would justify a court from refusing its equitable relief against re-entry for non-payment of rent.

APPEAL from a decision of Manyo-Plange J. on June 11, 1958, in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, in favour of the plaintiffs in an action for arrears of rent and recovery of possession.

Bentsi Ene hill for the appellant.

Apaloo for the respondents.

KORSAH C.J. This is an appeal from the judgment of the Land Court in a suit whereby the plaintiff, as landlord, claimed the sum of £460 as arrears of rent due by the defendant firm as tenants of one store and two wholesales in House No. D974J3, Knutsford Avenue, Accra.

The plaintiff and the defendants had originally agreed upon a rental of £66 13s. 4d. per month, but this figure was subsequently varied to £92 per month by an order of the Accra Rent Assessment Committee dated January 8, 1957, upon the application by the landlord under the Rent Control Ordinance, 1952, s. 7.

Section 7 of the Ordinance reads:

" (1) Any landlord or ,tenant or person interested in premises may make application to the appropriate committee for the assess­ment of a standard rent, or the variation of the standard rent, for the premises of which he is the landlord or ,tenant or in which he is interested.

(2) If an application for the fixing or variation of a standard rent is made by the landlord it shall be duly served on the tenant as respondent; if such application is made by ~he tenant it shall be duly served on the landlord; if such application is by an interested person or the Committee is proceeding on its own motion or upon a reference to the Committee by the Minister both land­lord and tenant shall be duly served with notice of the intention to proceed to the fixing or 'variation of ,the standard rent of the premises.

(3) Any person claiming to be interested in any proceedings before a Committee may apply to be made a party to such pro­ceedings.

(4) Before making any order or determination the Committee shall give all the interested parties an opportunity of being heard and of producing such evidence, oral or documentary, as seems relevant to the Committee."

The Committee by virtue of the powers vested in them by section 5 of the Ordinance, exercised jurisdiction and varied the original rent by increasing it to £92 per month and they made the order of January 8, 1957.

Section 5 (3) of the Rent Control Ordinance reads thus:

" (3) A Committee shall, subject to any restriction of juris­diction imposed under the provisions of subsection (1) of this section have power in relation to premises to which the provisions of this Ordinance apply, within the area subject to the authority of the local authority by which it was appointed:

(a) of its own motion, or upon a reference to a Committee by the Minister or upon an application authorised under the provisions of this Ordinance, to make an order fixing a reasonable rent having regard to the general level of rents for premises of a similar type, in a similar locality and in a similar condition on the first day of January, 1948, to all the terms and conditions of the lease, and to all other circumstances, and such rent shall be the standard rent of such premises notwithstanding any lease to ~he con­trary; and whenever the Committee makes an order fixing or varying the standard rent and the premises to which such rent relates are sub-let in whole or in part .the Com­mittee shall proceed to consider the fixing or variation of the standard rent of every portion of such premises separately sub-let.

Provided that a Committee shall not entertain an application for the variation of a standard rent, or upon its own motion or on a reference by the Minister consider the variation of a standard rent, when such standard rent has been fixed by a Committee, or by a Magistrate under the provisions of section 9 of ,this Ordinance, unless the Committee is first satisfied that:-

(i) the circumstances affecting the question of rent of particular premises have materially altered since the order was made; or

(ii) the order fixing the standard rent was made in consequence of any fraud, misrepresentation or mistake; or

(iii) fresh evidence of a material nature, which could not by the exercise of reasonable diligence have been produced when the order was made, is avail­able; or

(iv) the order was made in the absence of any neces­sary or proper party whose absence was not due to any default or neglect on his part; and

(v) where in the opinion of the Committee substantial injustice has been occasioned by the order;

(b) to receive complaints relating to the actions of a landlord or tenant connected with a lease and to determine whether the Chairman of the Committee shall institute proceedings before a :Magistrate relating to the commission of an offence against the provisions of this Ordinance; and

(c) to determine any other matter, the power to determine which is given to a Committee under the provisions of ,this Ordinance. "

The tenants refused to pay the rent of £92 per month, but continued to pay the original rent of £66 13s. 4d. per month in spite of repeated demands by the landlord for the full rent fixed by the order of the Rent Assessment Committee. The plaintiff, as landlord, there­upon instituted this action on April 1, 1958, claiming (1) £460 as arrears of rent, (2) recovery of possession.

The defendants admitted that the rent for said store and wholesales was by the order of the appropriate Committee raised to £92, but con­tended that: "An application for variation of the said order has been made and is pending before the Rent Assessment Committee." In fact, the application for variation of the order was not made until the writ of summons in this suit was served on the defendants. In any case it is admitted that the application failed and it was accordingly dismissed by the Rent Assessment Committee. The defendants, how­ever, contended that plaintiff is estopped from claiming any rent other than is reserved by the leases between them and the plaintiff.

It is not disputed t·hat the order made by ~he Rent Assessment Committee was so made in accordance with the provisions of the Rent Control Ordinance, and that it is in full force and effect. Nor is it contended that any court of competent jurisdiction has at any time set aside or varied the said order.

No evidence was taken at the trial as the facts were not in dispute.

The learned judge at the trial made the following note: "Both counsel agree on the pleadings only question of law raised. Therefore no evi­dence necessary."

On behalf of the defendants, counsel contended that by virtue of two leases, one expiring in 1959 and the other in 1964, the defendants are not liable to pay rents in excess of the sums reserved in those leases. what the Assessment Committee declared is the rent which can be charged; not that it displaces any other agreed upon by the parties.

The learned judge of the Land Court in a well-reasoned judgment held that by virtue of the Rent Control Ordinance the Rent Assess­ment Committee could upon application fix a reasonable rent having regard to the general level of rents for premises of similar type in a similar locality and to all the terms and conditions of the lease and to all other circumstances; also that such rent; when fixed or varied by the Committee, becomes the standard rent recoverable notwithstand­ing any lease to the contrary.

The appeal to this court is based on three grounds:

. (a) There is no provision in the Rent Control 0r:dinance making it mandatory to demand and pay the standard rent and nothing less.

(b) Because having regard to the penal provisions contained in section 12 it is clear that standard rent is intended by the Ordinance to be a maximum rent chargeable and not both a maximum and a minimum rent chargeable.

(c) Because the learned trial judge misconstrued sections 5 (3) (a) and section 7 (1) of the said Ordinance by unneces­sarily importing thereinto an implied power on the part of a landlord to break an existing contract of tenancy by demanding higher rent so long as the standard rent was not exceeded."

In this court counsel for appellant has based his argument on the English Rent Restriction Acts and Rent Control Acts, 1920-1952, and also on English decided cases dealing with the Rent Control Acts which operate in England. It is contended that by these Acts the common law rights of the parties to a contract have been preserved, except when a lease has expired and the tenant is in possession as a statutory tenant.

Upon examination of the English Acts it will be observed that each of those Acts was designed and intended to apply to rents of specific type of dwelling-houses and under special circumstances far too numerous to enumerate here.

The Increase of Rent and Mortgage Interest Restriction Act, 1920, the first of the English Acts to which reference has been made, is an Act to consolidate and amend the law with respect to the increase of rent and recovery of possession of dwelling-houses to which the Act is applicable. It does not prohibit but merely regulates circumstances under which increased rent may be payable irrespective of the rent reserved in a lease. Section 2 of the Act permits increases, for example (a) where a landlord incurs expenditure on the improvement or structural alterations of a dwelling-house, (b) an amount subject to certain conditions, (c) in addition to any such amounts as aforesaid an amount not exceeding 15 per cent. of the net rent, and (d) in addi­tion where the landlord is responsible for .the whole of repairs an amount not exceeding 25 per cent. of the net rent, etc.

Under the English Acts the power to increase rent has been restricted as in the 1920 Act, s. 3 (1), to a statutory tenancy; it is also provided in section 3 (2) that where the Act is applicable notice of intention to increase rent should be given by the landlord to the tenant. Section 1 (1) of the 1923 Act provides that notice of intention to in­crease rent operates as notice of intention to terminate the existing tenancy and thereafter if the tenant continues in possession he becomes statutory tenant, vide Aston v. Smith (1).

In Dean v. Bruce (2) it was held that a term of the original contract of tenancy whereby the rent charged would be less than the standard rent was not a term" consistent with the provisions of " the Increase of Rent and Mortgage Interest (Restrictions) Act, 1920, within the meaning of section 15 (1) of that Act and therefore the ~enant, on retaining possession of the house, was not entitled under the subsection to the benefit of that term. Phillips v. Copping (3) (dicta of Maugham L.J.), and Combe v. Combe (4) are cases decided on the same principle, that" where premises to which the Acts apply, are let at a lower rent than the statutory rent, the landlord may give notice terminating the tenancy and increasing the rent to the standard rent. " The Rent Con­trol Ordinance on the contrary gives jurisdiction .to the Rent Assessment Committee of its own motion or on application of a landlord or a tenant to vary the rent, and to fix the standard rent in respect of all premises to which the provisions of the Ordinance apply.

In each of the cases cited, viz., Dean v. Bruce (2), Phillips v.

Copping (3) and Regional Properties, Ltd. v. Oxley (5), the ratio decidendi was that although the reserved rent is lower than the stan­dard rent, the landlord is entitled to claim the higher rent, if that is the statutory rent; but is not obliged to accept the lower rent contained in the contract of lease.

These judicial decisions on the English Acts show that in all cases of tenancies in which the Acts permit increase of rents, it is the inten­tion of the legislature that the statutory rent should be substituted for the common law contractual rent.

Similarly the provisions of the Rents Control Ordinance show that it is the intention of the legislature that where a Committee under section 5 (3) of the Ordinance fixes or varies a standard rent in respect of any premises, subject to a tenancy to which the Ordinance applies, that rent shall be substituted for the contractual rent, and the land­lord is not bound to accept the contractual rent if it happens to be lower than the statutory rent.

That being the case, the Ordinance has to be looked at and inter­preted upon the rules of construction to ascertain what are premises and tenancies to which the Ordinance applies.

The language of section 2 of the Ordinance under the marginal note is significant. It specifically excludes certain premises and tenancies from the scope of the Ordinance. The only proper interpretation to be placed upon that section is that the Ordinance is applicable to all other premises and tenancies. Section 16 defines a statutory tenant. Section 11, under which a tenant may be ejected from premises upon an order of the court, has special provisions regarding statutory tenants, indicating that the other provisions apply to contractual tenants as well, otherwise why should the section specify statutory tenants in sub­section (1) (e). Section 12 (1) (b) and (d) prohibit the demand and receipt of consideration as a condition for the grant, renewal, continu­ance, or assignment of any tenancy. The terms of these subsections of section 12 have application only to tenants who are not statutory tenants, whilst section 15 (1), which also creates a prohibition, is stated in clear terms to apply only to statutory tenants. It reads:

"If a tenant to whom section 16 of this Ordinance applies, demands or receives the payment of any fine or premium or any other consideration for giving up possession of premises to the landlord or to any other person ... such tenant shall be guilty ... "

The words " a tenant to whom section 16 applies" must have a meaning, and that is: "not all tenants as defined in section 3 but only the tenant as defined in section 16."

Examination of the whole Ordinance and particularly of the sections referred to reveals .that where the legislature intended a particular part or section of it to apply to a particular tenancy only, and to exclude another, it employed language which made that clear, and subject to those exceptions, the Ordinance applies .to all tenancies-common law and statutory. That being the case, the English decisions cited above fully support the judgment of Mark Wilson C.J. in Amah v. Picas (6).

" Held: a standard rent, fixed by a Rent Assessment Commit­tee under the provisions of the Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952 (which Ordinance exists for the protection of landlords and tenants alike), is recoverable by a landlord from his tenant, not­withstanding the currency of a lease between them which preserves a contractual rent that is lower than the standard rent. In this respect the Rent Control Ordinance differs from the English Rent Restrictions Acts."

It followed the decision in Noshie v. Amarteifio (7), in which it was held: "That the Committee had power to alter a rent fixed by a lease then current. "

For these reasons I am clearly of the opinion that the decision of of the Land Court is right. The standard rent fixed by the Rent Assessment Committee is recoverable. It follows that the appellants defaulted in refusing to pay the standard rent within one month after the date on which it became lawfully due, that is, in February 1957.

On the claim for possession, I also agree with the view, taken by the learned judge of the court below, of the behaviour of defendants. In my view he was justified in refusing to exercise his discretion in favour of the defendants. It has been the practice of the Supreme Court to order recovery of possession in cases where defendant's con­duct has been far less defiant and objectionable. I would dismiss ~his appeal.

GRANVILLE SHARP J.A. My brother van Lare J.A. and I have con­sidered this matter together and this is our joint judgment:

The appeal raises two questions the answers to which are of great importance to landlords and tenants. The first of these arises in connec­tion with the interpretation of certain of the provisions of the Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, and may be stated thus: When on the hearing (at the instance either of the landlord or tenant or other person interested) of an application under section 7 of the Ordinance, the appropriate Committee assesses or varies the standard rent of premises at a figure higher than the contractual rent agreed in a lease or other instrument of letting as between the landlord and the tenant, can the landlord claim from and enforce against the tenant with whom he has contracted payment of future rent at a rate which will include the excess between the assessed or varied standard rent and the contrac­tual rent during the subsistence of the lease?

Two cases involving this question have already been decided in the courts of this country, the one contradictory of the other. In Amah v. Picas (6), the earlier of these, Sir Mark Wilson C.J. answered" Yes" and in Mansour v. C.p.A.a. (8), Windsor-Aubrey J. without consider­ing the earlier case which was unreported gave a contrary answer, basing his decision on a consideration of the judgments in cases in the English courts under the provisions of the Rent Restrictions Acts in the United Kingdom. Sir Mark Wilson C.J. in Amah v. Picas (6) felt himself guided, if not bound, by the decision of an earlier case, Noshie v. Amareifio (7), which was decided under then subsisting Emergency Defence Regulations and at a time long before the Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of ·1952, came into operation. In the instant case Manyo-Plange J. has followed the decision of Sir Mark Wilson C.J. in Amah v. Picas (6).

It will be necessary to return to a consideration of these cases at a later stage in this judgment, but before doing so it will be convenient to refer to the relevant provisions of the statute here in question.

They are:

1. By section 2 of the Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, the interpretation of the expression" standard rent" is as follows (so far as they are material):

.. standard rent' with respect to any Premises and tenancy ~o which this Ordinance applies means-

(a) subject to the provisions of paragraph (b) of this defini­tion, the rent at which the premises were let on the first day of January, 1948, or where the premises were not let on that date the rent at which they were last let before that date, or in the case of premises which were first let after that date, the rent agreed, at the time on the first letting after such date, between a land­lord and tenant:

Provided that, if a rent has been fixed for the pre­mises by a Committee or Magistrate under the provi­sion of this Ordinance or by a tribunal acting under powers conferred by an Ordinance repealed by this Ordinance such rent shall be the standard rent; ... "

2. Section 5 (3) is as follows (so far as material):

 A Committee shall, subject to any restriction of jurisdic­tion imposed under the provisions of subsection (1) of this section, have power in relation to premises to which the pro­visions of the authority of the local authority by which it was appointed-

(a) of its own motion, or upon a reference to a Committee by the Minister or upon an application authorised under the provisions of this Ordinance, to make an order fixing a reasonable rent having regard to the general level of rents for premises of a similar type, in a similar locality and in similar condition on the first day of January, 1948, to all the terms and conditions of the lease, and to all other circumstances, and such rent shall be the standard rent of such premises not­withstanding any lease to the contrary; and whenever the Committee makes an Order for fixing or varying the standard rent and the premises to which such rent relates are sub-let in whole or in part the Committee shall proceed to consider the fixing or variation of the standard rent of every portion of such premises. separately sub-let;

Provided that a Committee shall not entertain an application for the variation of a standard rent. or upon its own motion or on a reference by the Minister con­sider the variation of a standard rent, when such stan­dard rent has been fixed by a Committee, or by a Magistrate under the provisions of section n of this Ordinance, unless the Committee is first satisfied that-

(i) the circumstances affecting the Question of rent of particular premises have materially altereo since the order was made,; or

(ii) the order fixing the standard rent was made in consequence of any fraud, misrepresentation or mistake; or

(iii) fresh evidence of a material nature, which could not by the exercise of reasonable diligence have been produced when the order was made, is available; or

(iv) the order was made in the absence of any neces­sary or proper party whose absence was not due to any default or neglect on his part; and

(v) where in the opinion of the Committee substan­tial injustice has been occasioned by the order;

(b) to receive complaints relating to the actions of a land­lord or tenant connected with a lease and to determine whether the Chairman of the Committee shall institute proceedings before a Magistrate relating to the com­mission of an offence against the provisions of this Ordinance; and

(c) to determine any other matter, the power to determine which is given to a Committee under the provisions of this Ordinance."

3. Section 7 (1) is as follows:

" Any landlord or tenant or person interested in premises may make application to the appropriate Committee for the assessment of a standard rent, or the variation of the standard rent, for the premises of which he is the landlord or tenant or in which he is interested."

4. Section 12 (1) (a) is as follows:

" Every person who ... demands or receives more than the standard rent for any premises notwithstanding any lease to the contrary whensoever made, shall be guilty of an offence."

"Lease" is defined in the interpretation clause as including" every agreement for the letting of any premises whether oral or otherwise."

Section 11 (1) (a) reads as follows in stating the conditions in which

possession of premises may be recovered:

" where any rent lawfully due from the tenant has not been paid Or tendered within one month after the date on which it became lawfully due or where any other obligation of the tenancy, so far as the same is consistent with the provisions of this Ordinance, has been broken or not performed; ... "

On examining these provisions (in the light of the facts of this case) generally, it appears that so long as the tenant pays the rent lawfully due his landlord cannot eject him or recover possession unless other reasons for doing so, for which provision is made in the section, exist. These provisions are not here material in the light of the facts of this case where the tenant continued to pay the rent lawfully due, which was the rent fixed by the lease and which at that time was the standard rent of £66 13s. 4d. per month. The landlord could not demand or receive any more than this without committing an offence under section 12 of the statute.

 

Taking the view that the agreed rent was inadequate he sought to increase it by recourse to the machinery of section 7 (1) and section 5 (3) of the statute and on January 8, 1957, the Accra Rent Assessment Committee raised the then existing standard rent and, on an assess­ment, fixed the reasonable rent at £92 per month. This the landlord says became immediately payable by the tenant inasmuch as it became the standard rent. In considering this claim, after rejecting a contention which he understood Mr. Bentsi Enchill for the tenant to have made (and in this learned counsel says he was mistaken), that while a tenant could secure a reduced assessment which would enure for his benefit immediately even during the subsistence of a lease, the landlord could not similarly benefit, Manyo-Plange J. stated that " the language of sections 5 (3) and 7 (1) of the Ordinance is clear and free from any ambiguity."

The late Sir Mark Wilson C.J. expressed the same views when he said, in Amah v. Picas (6): " I am not attracted by the argument that the provisions of the Rent Control Ordinance, 1952, relating to the fixing or varying of standard rent are for the benefit of the tenant only" and " There is nothing whatever to suggest that this is a one­way control favouring and protecting only the tenant." He continued:

" The argument that the Ordinance allows a landlord under a subsisting lease the right to get a standard rent fixed, but does not allow him to demand payment of anything more than the con­tractual figure in the lease seems to me to fly in the face of the whole tenor of the Ordinance and is one which on first principles I find myself entirely unable to accept and in the absence of any clear authority for that submission I must reject it."

He had at an earlier stage in his judgment described the submission in question as involving the proposition" that the decision of the com­mittee was a purely academic proceeding which could have no effect until after the date of the expiry of the lease."

In the first place we would respectfully record our opinion that a decision that the Act does not enure for the benefit of the tenant only, does not in the least assist in considering the problem that arises here to be resolved. We go further and say that, unless the Act either expressly or by necessary implication states the contrary, neither the tenant nor the landlord can take advantage of .the Ordinance to derogate from the agreement between them as to rent until such agreement has been determined either by effiuxion of time or by notice to quit duly served. To hold otherwise would be to conflict with the long established principle of mutuality in relation to the rights and obligations arising out of contract at common law.

The real question therefore in the case in suit now before us is whether the Act either by express words or by necessary implication has the meaning attributed to it by Manyo-Plange J. It is a question that demands searching consideration in the light of certain first prin­ciples with which it is involved.

The Courts Ordinance, s. 83, makes it clear that subject to the terms of the Ordinance the common law and doctrines of equity in force in England are applicable in the courts of this country, so that, aside and apart from the cases, if the Ordinance has used apt words, i.e., express words or words containing a necessary and clear implica­tion, from which can be derived an intention to abrogate the common law rights which arise out of the contract between a landlord and a tenant, so that either of the parties or both of them can enforce a departure from the contract terms, then that is the meaning by which this court will be bound.

'l'he Rent Control Ordinance, 1952, was enacted in time of peace and some time after the expiry of an earlier enactment, the Defence (Rent Restriction) Regulations, 1943, made under the Emergency Powers (Defence) Acts, 1939 and 1940, as extended to the Gold Coast. The objects of the Ordinance are stated to be "to consolidate and amend the law with respect to the control of rents and recovery of premises in certain cases and for purposes connected therewith." Intervening between the 1952 Ordinance and the Rent Restriction Regulations, 1943, was the Rents (Control) Ordinance, 1947, which was repealed by section 24 of the enactment as present under consideration.

At page 313 of MaxweU on the Interpretati(Jn of Statutes, 10th ed., appears the following passage:

"Where it is gathered from a later Act that .the legislature attached a particular meaning to certain words in an earlier cognate one this would be taken as a legislative declaration of its meaning there. Subsequent legislation on the same subject may be looked into in order to see what is the proper construction to be put upon an earlier Act.

It must be taken for granted that the legislature is acquainted with the actual state of the law and of the practice of .the courts. 'therefore, when the words of an old statute are either incor­porated in, or by reference made part of, a new statute this is understood to be done with the object of adopting any legal interpretation which has been put on them by the courts, though not necessarily all the reasons for that interpretation. So, the same words appearing in a subsequent Act in pari materia, the presumption arises that they are used in the meaning which has been judicially put on them and that, unless there be something to rebut that presumption, the new statute is to be construed as the old one was."

These principles of interpretation are fully supported by a volume of authorities which it is not necessary here to cite, but it may be well to point out that the rules so laid down are not without limits, as was pointed out by Denning L.J. in the case of The Royal Crown Derby Porcelain Co. Ltd. v. Raymond Russell (9) when he said:

" I do not believe that whenever Parliament re-enacts a pro­vision of a statute it thereby gives statutory authority to every erroneous interpretation which has been put upon it. The ~rue view is that the court will be slow to override a previous decision on the interpretation of a statute when it has long been acted upon, and it will be more than usually slow to do so \vhen Parliament has, since the decision, re-enacted the statute in .the same terms. But if a decision is, in fact, shown to be erroneous, there is no rule of law which prevents it being overruled."

The case of Noshie v. Amarteifio (7) was an interpretation of ~he 1943 Regulations and it is therefore necessary to see what in that case was the precise provision upon which interpretation was given and to examine the subsequent legislation to see (a) whether it is in pari materia with the Regulations, and (b) whether what was there inter­preted by the West African Court of Appeal has since been re-enacted by Parliament.

The action in Noshie v. Amarteifio (7) was a claim to an increased rent by a landlord under the provisions of Regulation 7 (1) of the 1943 Regulations. These succeeded the 1942 Regulations, which, applying only to dwelling-houses, expressly gave power to the Committee to increase the rent on the ground, inter alia, that the rental charged on September 3, 1939, was unreasonably low on account of special circum­stances, or to reduce the rent in the case of a dwelling-house that had been first let after September 3, 1939, on the ground that the rent charged was excessive having regard to the rents obtaining in the locality in respect of similar accommodation.

It is not unfair to assume that both sets of Regulations were intro­duced in order, during the continuance of the emergency which began on September 3, 1939, to protect tenants against exploitation in regard to rents or arbitrary eviction by landlords, and to protect landlords against any general fall in rental values and against any possible exploitation resulting from tenants .taking undue advantage of their situation.

To the extent that by 1947 and 1952 the emergency had passed the Regulations cannot in our opinion be said to be in pari materia with the later enactments, though to the extent that each Regulation or Ordinance dealt with rents and associated questions as between land­lords and tenants they may be said to deal with cognate matters.

However this may be, the special words before the court contained in the Regulations were those in Regulation 7 (1) as follows:

" (a) to fix of its own motion or upon the application of either the landlord or the tenant a fair and reasonable rent for any particular premises having regard to all the circumstances;

(b) to permit upon the application of the landlord an increase of rent on the ground that under the terms of the tenancy the rent is inclusive of rates and the rates on the premises have been increased since the contract of tenancy was entered into."

The appropriate Committee considered the landlord's claim to have the rent fixed on two grounds which he must be taken to have' established:

(1) that since the date of the lease the rates for which he was respon­sible had been increased;

(2) that the sub-lessee had sub-let the premises without his consent

at a rent of £15 monthly.

The Committee considered the matter and fixed the rent at £180 in lieu of the £36 per annum fixed by the lease and upon the tenant subsequently refusing to pay more than the £36 the landlord brought his action and recovered judgment in the Divisional Court. The tenant appealed and the \Vest African Court of Appeal considered .the provi­sions of the Regulation already cited in the light of sections 3 and 6 which read as follows:

"3. ' Standard rent' means-

(a) the rent fixed for any particular premises or class of pre­mises by the appropriate Committee: provided that, where the rent of any particular premises is fixed by the Commit­tee of its own motion, notice of the rent so fixed has been given to the landlord; or

(b) where no rent has been so fixed

(i) in the case of premises which were let on the 3rd day of September, 1939, the rent at which the premises were then let; or

(ii) in the case of premises which were vacant on the 3rd day of September, 1939, the rent at which the premises were last let before that date; provided that where the rent of any dwelling-house has been assessed under the provisions of the Defence (Rent Restriction) Regu­lations, 1942, the rent so assessed shall, until such time as the same is varied by the appropriate Commit­tee, be deemed to be the standard rent for the purpose of these Regulations."

" 6.-(1) No person shall be entitled to receive for any premises a rent in excess of the standard rent.

(2) A tenant may recover, either by civil proceedings against, or by deduction from any rent payable to, his landlord, any rent in excess of the standard rent paid after the commencement of these Regulations."

The learned judges held that under all these provisions the Committee had power to alter a rent fixed by a lease then current.

With very great respect we for ourselves entertain some perplexity in attempting to understand fully the reasons for this decision, except that the learned judges felt that some injustice might result from a contrary decision. M'Carthy J. put it in this way at pages 117-118:

" It will be seen that where no rent has been fixed by a Com­mittee, in the case of premises which were let on September 3, 1939, the rent at which the premises were then let becomes the standard rent .. Also that as from May 22, 1943 (the date of commencement of the Regulations), no person is entitled to receive for any premises a rent in excess of the standard rent. This means, if Mr. Akufo-Addo's interpretation of Regulation 7 (1) (a) is correct, that if a person lawfully re-Iet premises after September 3, 1939, and before the enactment of any Rent Restriction legis­lation, no increase of rent agreed upon by the parties could be recovered under the present Regulations. The Committee would have no jurisdiction to sanction or fix any such increase, the tenancy being under a lease, although the Committee under the 1942 Regulations could have done so in special circumstances. It seems reasonable to suppose that actually Regulation 7 (1) (a) provides the necessary powers. "

The matter referred to in this passage of their lordships' judgment plainly indicates a casus omissus in the Regulations, but this can only be supplied if it is possible within the ordinary canons of interpre­tation, and if these are applied to Regulation 7 (1) we find it difficult to understand why if under 7 (1) (a) of the Regulations the Committee can increase the rent under a current lease, "having regard to all the circumstances," 7 (1) (b) should appear in the Regulations at all, inasmuch as the facts that the tenant under a current lease is paying a rent which is inclusive of rates and that the rates have been in­creased since the contract of tenancy was entered into would all be circumstances within the meaning of the final words of 7 (1) (a).

In our view what in effect seems to have been decided is that the words in 7 (1) (a) "fix ... a reasonable rent" mean the same as the words in 7 (1) (b) "permit an increase of rent." It is one of the canons of interpretation that where the legislature uses different expres­sions it is not supposed to use words without a meaning and they must be interpreted in their ordinary sense. Even analogous words, which is not the case here, may be presumed to be susceptible of a separate and distinct meaning. Perhaps we may be permitted to add that the words of section 6 forbidding receipt of more than the standard rent do not by any necessary implication confer on a landlord a right to exact that if he has agreed to accept less.

We have stated the reasons for the doubt we entertain as to this decision because it may be of some assistance so to do when considering whether, in enactments which followed upon it, statutory authority has or has not been given to it.

The 1947 Ordinance, entitled" An Ordinance to make Temporary Provision for the Control of Rent in respect of Small Premises," was passed and came into operation on January 1, 1948. By section 3 of this Ordinance "rent" was defined as meaning" any valuable con­sideration whatsoever demanded or paid in consideration of the occupa­tion of any premises." "Standard rent" was defined as meaning:

" (a) The rent fixed for any particular premises or any class of premises by the appropriate Committee; provided that where the rent of any particular premises is fixed by the Commit­tee of its own motion notice of the rent so fixed must be given to the landlord; or

(b). Where no rent has been fixed, then

(i) in the case of premises which were let on 3rd of Septem­ber, 1939, the r811t under which the premises were then let, or

(ii) in the case of premises which were vacant on the 3rd September, 1939, the rent at which the premises were last let before that date;

Provided that where the rent of any premises has been fixed under the provisions of the Defence (Rent Restriction) Regulations, 1943, the rent so fixed shall until such time as the same is varied by the appropriate Committee be deemed to be the standard rent for the purposes of this Ordinance."

By section 6 it was enacted:

" (1) No person shall be entitled to receive for any premises a rent in excess of the standard rent.

(2) A tenant may recover either by civil proceedings against or by deduction from any rent payable to his landlord, any rent in excess of the standard rent paid after the commence­ment of this Ordinance. "

Section 7 is in the following terms:

" (1) The appropriate Committee should have the following powers and duties:

(a) to fix of its own motion or upon application of either landlord or tenant a fair and reasonable rent for any particular premises having regard to all the circumstances notwithstanding any stipulated rent in any contract of tenancy between the land­lord and the tenant.

(b) To permit upon the application of the landlord an increase of rent on the ground that under the terms of the tenancy the rent is inclusive of rates and the rates on the premises have been increased since the contract of tenancy was entered into.

(2) In addition to the duties and powers specified in sub­section (1) of this section the appropriate Committee may upon notice in the Gazette fix for any class or classes of premises a fair and reasonable rent having regard to all the circumstances notwithstanding any stipulated rent in any contract of tenancy."

It will be noticed that, looking at the definition of standard rent and section {) of this Ordinance, the tenant, who, up to the commence­ment of the Ordinance, had paid a rent in excess of the standard rent, could not recover the excess so paid; and it would appear that it would be appropriate in such a case after the commencement of the Ordi­nance for the landlord or the tenant or both to apply to the appropriate Committee to have the rent fixed as a fair and reasonable rent in the circumstances that the parties had by agreement between them so decided it to be.

The language of section 7 " notwithstanding any stipulated rent in any contract of tenancy between the landlord and tenant" is more definite than the language of the Regulations of 1943, but the same confusion is introduced by sub-paragraph (b) of subsection (1) as existed in the previous Regulations. And again it is difficult to understand how this sub-paragraph can be consistent with the view that sub­paragraph (a) gave power to the Committee to interfere either to increase or reduce rent during the subsistence of an agreement between the parties. It is worthy of note that the Ordinance is stated to apply to small premises and that these tenancies would for the most part be terminable by short notice, the power of the landlord to eject the tenant or recover possession from him of any premises being restricted by sections 8 and 9 of the Ordinance. If the landlord wished for an increase or the tenant for a reduction of the rent, it would be open to either of them to terminate the tenancy and give notice of an application to the appropriate Committee to fix a fair and reasonable rent notwithstanding that the rent of the contract of tenancy had been agreed between them: the rule being that where two persons, equal in the sense that neither is exercising upon the other any fraud, duress or undue influence, and each of whom is of sound mind and of full age, have agreed upon the rent in a contract of tenancy between them that rent is presumed to be the reasonable rent. This is a reasonable con­struction to place upon the provisions of section 7 of the Ordinance of 1947, despite the strong words "notwithstanding any stipulated rent, etc.," and there are no words which clearly and unambiguously indicate the intention of the Ordinance that the Committee should have power to increase or reduce the rent payable under an agreement during the subsistence of the agreement. Nor could the Ordinance be applied in any event to premises that could not fall within the category of small premises: the title to the Ordinance being " An Ordinance to make Temporary Provision for the Control of Rent in respect of Small Premises." The Ordinance does however appear to have given statu­tory approval to the interpretation placed upon similar words in the 1943 Regulations by the decision in Noshie v. Amarteifio (7).

The Ordinance of 1947 was repealed by section 24 of the Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, and the legislature replaced it by the latter Ordinance to consolidate and amend the law with respect to the control of rents and recovery of possession of premises in certain cases and for purposes connected therewith. In amending the pre­vious enactments in this consolidating Ordinance there were notable departures from the language previously used and it may be said that by selecting January 1, 1948, as the material date in the definition of standard rent the legislature was introducing into the 1952 Ordinance the intention of the 1947 Ordinance relating to small premises, though this is a matter which cannot be said to be decisive of the answer to the question with which this court is mainly concerned, though it sheds some light upon it.

The landlord in the present case made his application to the Com­mittee under the provisions of section 7 (1) of the current enactment for a variation of the standard rent. The Act makes it reasonably clear, and even if it were not so first principles will dictate, that in assessing or varying the standard rent the figure assessed or resulting from the variation cannot be an arbitrary figure and, for the guidance of the Committee, section 5 (3) states the matters to which the Com­mittee should give consideration in arriving at a standard rent, which is not arbitrary, but which is based upon what the Committee deem to be a reasonable rent for premises of a similar type in a similar locality and in similar condition on January 1, 1948 (the date when the 1947 Ordinance came into operation), to all the terms and conditions of the lease and to all other circumstances. The Ordinance then provides that such rent shall be the standard rent of such premises notwith­standing any lease to the contrary. The Committee, in deciding what shall be a reasonable rent, would naturally look at the lease for its terms and conditions and the rent fixed thereby, and would say that. notwithstanding that the two persons had as between themselves fixed what was prima facie a reasonable rent, for the purposes of standard rent that agreement should be ignored, but there are no words which clearly and unambiguously direct that such standard rent shall im­mediately apply as between the parties to the lease.

Section 12 (1) (a) makes it an offence for a landlord to demand or receive more than the standard rent for his premises, notwithstanding any lease to the contrary whensoever made. This is a penal clause and cannot confer upon the landlord an unquestionable right to receive the standard rent and no less. It means no more than that the landlord cannot shelter himself behind a lease which provides for a rent in excess of the standard rent merely on the ground that the tenant agreed to pay.

One would have supposed that, in connection with a consolidating and amending enactment of rent control, if the legislature had intended to empower the Committee to derogate from the terms of the subsisting agreement they would have taken the opportunity to express such an intention in plain and unambiguous language such as was used in the first Rent Restrictions Ordinance, which empowered the appropriate Committee to increase or reduce the rent in certain specified circum­stances where the tenancy was a statutory tenancy or governed by contract. It is not difficult to imagine many forms of simple language by which such an intention could be expressed without overburdening the Ordinance and in fact in this case we are invited on behalf of the landlord to insert in section 5 (3) (a) after the words, " notwithstanding any lease to the contrary," words such as: "and such rent shall be substituted for the rent reserved by any lease between the parties thereto notwithstanding that such lease is unexpired. "

\Ve believe such or similar words to be essential to fulfil the requirement of clear and unambiguous expression. A lease may be current or expired and in either case would be material for consideration by the Committee in fixing a reasonable rent.

We for ourselves do not think that by the provisions of sections 7 (1) and 5 (3) of the Ordinance the legislature was in the least concerned to give statutory approval to the judicial interpretation in the case of Noshie v. Amarteifio (7) and even if it did we would be prepared to act upon the principle laid down by Denning L.J. previously cited in this judgment.

Section 19 of the 1952 Ordinance reads as follows:

" Nothing in this Ordinance shall be so construed as to prevent a landlord from collecting from his tenant the rates for the time being payable in respect of any premises ... if the obligation of paying rates ... was assumed by the tenant under the terms of the lease or when the standard rent has been fixed by a Com­mittee, if the obligation to pay rates was stated in the order fixing the standard rent to be payable by the tenant. "

We cannot find in these words any indication of an intention that the landlord can collect rates from the tenant if he himself has accepted the obligation to pay them or that in such a case the Committee should have the power during the currency of a lease to shift the burden of the rates from the landlord to the tenant, as appears to have been done in the case nf Noshie v. Amarteifio (7).

In the previous enactments, as we have already stated, the Committee was empowered in a case where the tenant had in a lease assumed the obligation to pay rates to " permit an increase of rent" when the rates were increased after the contract of tenancy was entered into. There is nothing derogatory of the contract in such a provision, and the words are clear. They do not, however, support the view expressed in the last cited case, which has the effect of inserting into the Regulations of 1943 a provision that the Committee can burden the tenant with the rates even when a landlord in a subsisting lease has assumed that burden himself.

Accepting as we do the principle that clear words are necessary, or in default of them words from which a necessary implication is to be derived if vested rights under the common law, whether arising from contract or otherwise, are to be affected by the legislature, we cannot accept the proposition that in order to resolve the question here in favour of the landlord we should read into and supply to the enactment words that do not there appear and that it would have been easy for the legislature to supply for itself.

In the words of Bowen L.J. in the case of Rendall v. Bailey (10):

" Such legislation is possible but ... we ought no~ to assume without the clearest language that Parliament intended to destroy common law rights of Her Majesty's subjects."

Section 3 (the interpretation section) of the 1952 Ordinance contains the following proviso:

" Provided that:

(i) where the rates in respect of any premises have been or are altered after the date on which ... the standard rent has been or is fixed;

(ii) the terms of the lease do not require the tenant to pay rates ... etc.; and

(iii) in the case of such an increase in rates the landlord shall have informed the tenant of the amount of the increase in rates assessed for or attributable to the premises;

the standard rent shall be altered by the amount by which the rates assessed for ... the premises comprised in an assessment have been or are altered ... etc."

Reading this together with section 19 of the Ordinance the effect would appear to be that where the lease does not impose the burden of rates on the tenant the amount of any increase of rates is auto­matically added to the standard rent if the landlord has given the requisite notice, and if the lease has placed the burden of rates on the tenant the landlord can automatically demand the amount of any increase as and when it takes place.

In neither case is any intervention by any appropriate Committee called for, and in each case the provision is in aid and not in derogation of the contract because in the former case it is presumed that the rates payable at the date of the lease were taken into account when the rent was agreed, and in the latter case, section 19 apart, the tenant is clearly under an obligation to pay such rates as are assessed for the premises from time to time. In each case the language of the Ordinance carefully preserves the contract.

In our view it is not only a justifiable but also a necessary inference that it was not intended that the Committee should have power to vary the rent payable under a subsisting contract, the legislature having clearly indicated the circumstances from which, and from which alone, this result will follow.

The provisions to which we have just referred are not to be found in the 1943 Regulations, nor were they included in the Ordinance of 1947, and it may be taken that the legislature in 1952 intended to clarify the position in view of the kind of doubt that had arisen from the decision in Noshie v. Amarteifio (7). Certainly there cannot be read into those provisions any expressed intention to lend to that case any statutory authority, nor any intention to endorse the view that, on an application to the Committee, the Committee could either of its own motion or otherwise destroy the contract between the parties.

The words" of its own motion or upon application" are important words, and in our view mean that the Committee can do of its own motion everything that it could do upon application being made to it. It would in our opinion be a novel and dangerous innovation to introduce into our law to hold that such an administratively appointed Committee could of its own motion avoid contracts between parties and make for the parties some new contract fundamentally different from that into which they had freely entered.

Such an intention would have to be expressed in the clearest possible language, and this we do not find in any material part of the Ordinance. We are of opinion that equally the Committee cannot destroy a contract upon the application of any party to it.

The sanctity of contract is something that is fundamental to a well-ordered society and in a contract of tenancy the most important part is the provision as to the rent agreed between the parties and fixed by a lease. This in our view remains the rent for all purposes until the contract is ended and is not susceptible to alteration either upwards or downwards at the caprice of either landlord or tenant or of any person or body of persons whatever. If Parliament in the days of the emergency thought fit to direct otherwise the conditions under which such direction was indicated did not obtain in 1952 and it is no doubt for that reason that the language and provisions of the 1952 Ordinance differ in material respects from what appeared in the earlier enactments.

    Having regard to the reasons that have led us to the conclusion that this appeal should be allowed upon this part of it we do not find it necessary to consider the English cases further than to state that they are in principle in support of the argument put forward on behalf of the tenant, and two of them may for the sake of reference be here cited, viz., the judgment of Romer L.J. in R. v. Padding ton and Marylebone Rent Tribunal (11) and the case of Dean v. Bruce (2). It may be convenient to add that the general effect of these decisions leads to the conclusions that Acts of the kind here in question are intended to control rents and fix upward limits for such rents, but in doing so are not intended to confer rights upon landlords in substitu­tion for their rights under lawful contracts from which, and not from the statute, their right to receive rents derives. Further: the rights and obligations under a contract are not exhausted until the contract is lawfully brought to an end and cannot, apart from a clearly expressed intention on the part of the legislature to the contrary, be vacated by either party or by any other person or committee at the behest of either party, during its subsistence. We therefore hold the view that the tenant is under no obligation to pay and the landlord has no right to claim any rent in excess over and above the rent provided in the lease notwithstanding that the Committee has fixed a higher standard rent. This state of affairs will continue until the proper determination of the lease.

We hold that under the law as it stands the standard rent of premises merely fixes an upward limit, and a landlord is therefore perfectly free t{) negotiate a letting at a lower figure if he chooses to do so. Conversely a landlord cannot, if there is a current lease, demand from the tenant an increase of the contractual rent to the standard rent if that is higher; but he has a remedy or a way out, if he is anxious to recover the increased rent, by giving due notice of termination of the lease as and if provided in the agreement. The contractual rent therefore remains the recoverable rent until the termination of the current tenancy.

If we had come to a different conclusion with regard to this part of the case we would nevertheless have allowed the appeal from the learned judge's refusal to make an order for possession conditional upon the tenant continuing to pay the increased rent. The provisions of the Ordinance of 1952 as to recovery of possession for non-payment of rent are similar to those in the English Acts and are not in any sense derogatory to the right to relief in equity. A proviso for re-entry in a lease has always been regarded as security for the landlord and the power of a court to order possession is in no different case. In neither case is relief excluded. The mere non-payment of rent is not regarded as a reason for the refusal of relief, and relief is refused only in exceptional circumstances (see Chandlcss v. Nicholson (12) and Gill v. Lewis (13) and the judgment therein of Hodson L.J.). 

  In the present case there was no failure on the part of the tenant to pay the rent which he had in the lease agreed to pay. What he refused to pay was an excess over and above the agreement. He had the support of at least one judgment of the court in this country for his objection to pay the excess rent claimed by his landlord, but even this he paid after the decision of Manyo-Plange J., up to the date of the appeal. We do not regard the finding of the learned judge that the appellant had gambled in the action and lost as an adequate or even relevant reason for the refusal of relief and we would therefore have substituted for the three months' stay granted by the learned judge an order for possession conditioned to be stayed so long as the appellant continued to pay the lawful rent.

For these reasons we think the appeal should be allowed, and would add our regret that in doing so we have, not without diffidence, come to a conclusion which is at variance with that reached by our brother the Chief Justice.

Appealed allowed.
