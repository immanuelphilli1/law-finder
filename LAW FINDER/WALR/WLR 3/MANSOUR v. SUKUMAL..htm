                                                          April 4, 1958

                                         MANSOUR v. SUKUMAL.

                                     High Court, Western Judicial Division, Divisional Court         

                                                                             Acolatse J.

.

 

Landlord and tenant-Negotiation for a lease-No agreement as to rent but agreement as to manner of determining rent plus entry and payment of determined rent-Whether a lease in existence-Whether landlord entitled to benefit of Rent Control Ordinance, c. 2 of 1952, s. 11 (1) (e) (i).

Michael Mansour granted to D. Sukumal and Sons under seal a lease of certain premises in Sekondi on April 2, 1949. The premises provided residential quarters on the upper storey, and business premises (with storage beneath) on the ground floor. The lease was for seven years, at an annual rent of £400.

This lease expiring on March 31, 1956, on April 10, 1956, the land­lord wrote to his tenants inviting them to Accra for consultation on the position. Thereafter, the landlord visited Sekondi, taking with him a fresh lease which he had already executed in favour of the same tenants. In essence it was a renewal of the expired lease, with a variation in the rent. It was for a term of seven years from April 1, 1956, at an annual rent of £984, payable by monthly payments of £82, each in advance. The new lease was dated April 28, 1956, and (on the face of it) it was executed by the parties.

Subsequently the parties met in Accra, and agreed both to submit the question of the appropriate revised rent to the Rent Assessment Com­mittee, and to abide by the Committee's decision. The landlord accordingly applied to the Committee to vary the standard rent. The parties appeared before the Committee on June 13, 1956, and on July 4, 1956, the Committee varied the rent to £1,207 10s. per annum (£100 12s. 6d. per month).

The tenants, being aggrieved, caused a case to be stated by the Com­mittee to the District Magistrate, Takoradi. On September 19, 1956, the Magistrate discharged the order of the Committee, and ordered that the rent should remain as revised in 1952, i.e., £600 per annum. The tenants paid rent as agreed for April, May and June, 1956, and rent for July, 1956 in accordance with the Committee's assessment.

On January 24, 1957, the landlord gave the tenants statutory notice to quit under section 11 (1) (e) (i) of the Rent Control Ordinance, on the ground that he intended to remodel the premises. The tenants, by their solicitors, replied on February 11, 1957, that they held under a lease for seven years from April 1, 1956, and that they would not accept the notice to quit.

The landlord commenced proceedings for recovery of possession. He contended that notwithstanding the executed document there was no completed agreement between the parties and therefore no lease, and thus, the tenants became statutory tenants on the expiration of the lease dated April 2, 1949. The tenants denied that they were statutory tenants, claiming that complete agreement had been reached and that a new lease had thereby come into existence.

Held, it was apparent that at the meetings between the parties all the terms of a new lease had been agreed save for a rent. Although the rent was not agreed a method for determining a rent and, therefore (id certum est quod certum reddi potest) complete agreement, was reached. The defendants were thus tenants, and not statutory tenants, and the plaintiff was not therefore entitled to claim the benefit of the Rent Control Ordinance, s. 11 (1) (e) (i).

ACTION for recovery of possession of premises under section 11 (1) (e) (i) of the Rent Control Ordinance.

Blay for the plaintiff. Gwira for the defendant.

ACOLATSE J. stated the history of the matter and continued: A " lease," under the Ordinance in question, includes every agreement for the letting of any premises, whether oral or otherwise. When the written lease of April 2, 1949, expired, in my view a new lease (whether oral or written) was granted under the Ordinance in consideration of the rents which were in fact subsequently fixed and paid. In my opinion there was a novation of the original agreement, giving the pos­session of the premises to the defendants under new terms after April 1, 1956, in consideration of the payments of the new rents. The parties agreed to go before the Rent Assessment Committtee upon the question of the rent, and ultimately the standard rent was fixed at £600 per annum by the District Magistrate, upon a case stated in September, 1956.

The plain tiff seeks recovery of possession under section 11 (1) (e) (i), that is to say he claims that the lease has expired, that the tenant is a statutory tenant, and that he (the landlord) intends to pull down or remodel the premises. To succeed under subsection (1) (e) the plain­tiff, then, must satisfy me, inter alia, that the defendants are statu­tory tenants, a term which is defined by section 16 (1) of the Ordi­nance. I am unable to see how the defendants could be classified under any of the three headings in that definition.

In the circumstances, the plaintiff is not entitled to obtain an order of possession in repect of House No. 93/2, Prince of Wales Road, Takoradi, under section 11 (1) (e) (i) of the Rent Control Ordinance. I therefore dismiss the plaintiff's claim against .the defendants, with costs.

Judgment for the defendants
