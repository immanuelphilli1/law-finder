                                 In re EBURAHIM, DECEASED. ANSAH v. ANKRAH.

                                High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

Adumua­Bossman J.

This was an administration suit in which the plaintiff, a Fanti, sought a grant of letters of administration to the estate of his late uncle. He claimed to be entitled to apply for a grant by reason of having been appointed by the family to succeed his uncle. His application was opposed by the defendant, a niece of the deceased, who claimed that although the plaintiff had been appointed caretaker of his late uncle's property, he was not the duly appointed family head. The defendant asserted that there had in fact been no appointment of a successor and that by custom this was a matter for the head of the wider family group to which the deceased belonged; such head would in due course make an appointment in the presence of the principal members of the family.

The defendant urged at the outset that as the issue before the court was basically as to whether there had been an appointment as successor in accordance with custom, this was a question which could more appro­priately be decided by a Native Court and that therefore it should be referred to the appropriate Native Court for determination.

Held, (i) the jurisdiction of Native Courts to inquire into suits and matters relating to succession to the property of deceased natives does not oust the jurisdiction of the High Court in succession cases. And where, as with succession, the principles of native custom have crystallised in a series of decisions in the courts, it may well have become pointless and unnecessary for the High Court to exercise its power of referring questions of custom to a Native Court for determination.

Amene v. Amanor (1931) 1 W.A.C.A. 126 referred to.

(ii) The normal practice of the court is to grant a letter of administra­tion to the estate of a deceased person to the person who has, by custom, been appointed by the family of the deceased as his successor or, if no such family appointment has been made, to the person who has, by custom, the right to prior consideration by the family for appointment as successor. Such grant of letters of administration is limited to the self-acquired property of the deceased: family property which the de­ceased administrated on behalf of the family does not come within the scope of such a grant.

(iii) The self-acquired property of a deceased person, not otherwise disposed of, becomes family property upon his death. The family, in this context, is not the wider family to which the deceased belonged but his immediate family (i.e., in Fanti areas all those tracing descent from the deceased's closest maternal ancestor, and if none, all those tracing through the next closest, and so on). It is the principal members of this immediate family, not the wider family who, by custom, appoint a suc­cessor from among themselves to the self-acquired property of the deceased.

Cases referred to:

(1) Amene v. Amanor (1931) 1 W.A.C.A. 126.

(2) Angu v. Attah (1916) Judgments of Privy Council on Appeal from

the Gold Coast, 1874-1928, 43.

(3) Villars v. Baffoe (1909) Renner's Reports, Vol. I, Pt. II, 549.

(4) Larkai v. Amorkor and Others (1933) 1 W.A.C.A. 323.

ADMINISTRATION suit in which the defendant challenged the plain­tiff's right to apply for a grant of letters of administration to the estate of a deceased person.

Quashie Sam for the plaintiff. Abadoo for the defendant.

ADUMUA-BosSMAN J. In this administration suit, the plaintiff's claim to be the duly appointed successor of the late Kofi Eburahim and as such the person entitled to be granted letters of administration to his personal estate, was opposed by the defendant on the ground, set out in paragraph 2 of the statement of defence, that:

"The plaintiff herein, Rofi Ansah, has neither been duly appointed successor to the estate of the deceased as alleged or claimed by the recognised head of the said family in the presence of the principal members thereof in accordance with native custom and usage, nor at any time been given customary possession or control of the personal effects and things of the deceased."

The short issue raised for determination therefore would appear to be whether or not the plaintiff was duly appointed successor of the deceased. There appeared at first sight to be a further subsidiary question, whether or not the plaintiff was customarily given control and possession of the personal effects and belongings of the deceased. Nothing however turns on this, inasmuch as the defendant and her witnesses in their evidence stated that the plaintiff was given charge of the properties of the deceased as caretaker. Before dealing with the issue, however, reference should be made to the fact that by paragraph 4 of the defence, the defendant contended that the issue outstanding for determination in the suit should be referred to the appropriate Native Court of the state to which the deceased and the parties belonged for them to inquire into it and submit their findings to the court. I was, however, unable to accede to that contention, but decided to retain and determine the issue, relying on the hitherto unquestioned authority of Amene v. Amanor (1), in which it was held that in spite of the sections of the Native Administration Ordinance (now replaced by the Native Courts Ordinance), whereby exclusive original jurisdiction was conferred on the native tribunals (now the Native Courts) in " Suits and matters relating to succession to the property of any deceased native who had at the time of his death a fixed place of abode within a state," the jurisdiction of the High Court is not thereby ousted. The headnote to that case reads:

" On a case stated by a Divisional Court it was held that:

(1) the effect of the sections is not to deprive the court of jurisdiction to grant letters of administration in respect of the estate of a deceased native;

(2) it is not necessary that an applicant for letters of administra­tion should have first established his right to succeed in a native tribunal;

(3) when the application for letters of administration develops into a dispute as to the right of succession, the cause or matter does not thereby become one properly cognisable by a native tribunal so as to oust the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court and make it necessary to refer the parties to a native tribunal."

It seems unnecessary to point out that that case does, however, recog­nise and accept the position that the court, acting under the section of the Courts Ordinance which gives it a discretion to refer questions of native customary law which may arise in any suit or matter before it for inquiry and report by a native tribunal, may, in its discretion, make such reference to a native tribunal or court at any time.

I was fortified in my reaction against making a reference to the Native Court in this particular case, first by the fact that quite a volume of improper and questionable correspondence from persons not parties to the suit, clearly intended to prejudice the trial of the suit, was transmitted to me before the commencement of the trial, and it therefore became obvious that a considerable amount of out· side influence, prejudicial to a fair trial, would be exerted on whatever panel members would be charged with the duty of dealing with any reference; secondly because, in any event, the principles of native customary law of succession involved in or applicable to the determination of the issue raised in the suit, by reason of a considerable number of previous decisions, have now become crystallised out in the manner indicated in Angu v. Attah (2), that is to say become so notorious that the court takes it to be sheer unwarrantable delay and an attempt to augment the costs and expenses of the litigation, to refer the parties to the Native Court and back again.

Turning now to the facts of the case, it was established that the deceased, I\:ofi Eburahim, belonged, as every native generally does, to a wider family, known as the Twidan family, which has branches in Adam, Obiri and Nglesi in the Gomoa Asin State. He belonged to the Obiri branch or section, and in his lifetime appears to have been head of that section about the time he died. In the matter of grant of letters of administration to administer his personal estate however, it is his self-acquired property only, not property held by him in his capacity as head for and on behalf of any family, with which the court is solely concerned. In this connection, reference might be made to the case of VilLars v. Baffoe (3) where the Full Court considered the question of law submitted by a special case stated for its consideration and gave in answer:

"whether under the grant of letters of administration of the deceased's estate, the aforesaid chattels, assumed for the purposes of this question to be ' family property,' do or do not pass to the administrator-it being admitted for the purposes of this special case that the aforesaid chattels were in the control and possession of the deceased until his death .... The decision of the court on the special case submitted is that the chattels regarded as ' family property' do not pass to the administrator under the letters of administration granted to him."

In the second place, and in our particular case this is the more impor­tant point, it is the group of persons who, by virtue of their relationship to the person who owned the personal estate, are permitted by native customary law to inherit or have beneficial enjoyment of the estate, which the court has to ascertain and to which regard must be had. Too often when mention is made of the family of a person, attention is focused on the wider family to which he belonged rather than his own immediate or small family, i.e., the group of persons entitled by custom to inherit from him. In our particular case, as the deceased and the parties are, according to the evidence before the court, of the Fanti tribal group, one of the groups whose system of inheritance or succession is matrilineal, the group of persons entitled by custom to the beneficial enjoyment of his estate consists of all the persons emanating or tracing from the same womb as the deceased, and in the absence of any such persons, then those emanating from the same womb from which the mother of the deceased emanated, and so on. As pointed out by Deane O.J. in Larkai v. Amorkor and Others (4):

" Ahuru, it is true, was called Ahuru Larkai and it seems was a member of the Larkai family; but individually owned property of his would, on his death intestate, become the property not of the (wider) Larkai family, but of his own family .... The plaintiff is the present holder of the Larkai family stool; but he would not be a member of Ahuru's family so as to become head thereof and as such entitled to the family property of Ahuru so as to sue for it in this court."

See also 'l'amakloe and Others v. Attipoe and Tamakloe and Others (5).

Having regard to that settled state of the native customary law it seems to me that the stress which, throughout the trial, learned counsel for the defendant has been laying on, and the importance which he has been inviting me to attach to, Kwame Saah as the head and other principal elders of the larger or wider Twidan family is mis­conceived and beside the point. It is the immediate family of the deceased at Obiri to which regard must, as already indicated, primarily be had.

From the evidence available, it is established and indeed conceded by the defendant himself that the deceased, Kofi Eburahim, was settled at Obiri with several nephews and nieces, the most senior being Kwesi Afadzi, followed by the plaintiff, Rofi Ansah. 'fhe defendant, one of the nieces, is admitted to be junior in rank to the plaintiff by reason of the fact that her mother was junior by birth to the plaintiff's mother, although both were daughters of the same mother who gave birth to the deceased, Kofi Eburahim. It is clear therefore that by native customary law it is those surviving nephews and nieces of the deceased tracing from the same common ancestress, i.e., the mother of the deceased, Kofi Eburahim, who properly constitute the family of the said deceased. They are the group of persons entitled to inherit or succeed to the self-acquired properties of the deceased in respect of which he died intestate-not Kwame Saah the head and other princi­pal elders and members of the wider Twidan family.

In this connection the question put to the expert witness of the defendant, Nana Tandoh, and his answer thereto, are most pertinent:

Q.  This deceased having died leaving nephews and nieces, who are by custom beneficially entitled to his personal estate?

A. The nephews and nieces.

Equally pertinent is the question to and answer from Kwame Saah, the head of the wider family, as follows:

Q.  Is the position accepted by you that his personal properties can be inherited only by his nephews aud nieces?

R.   Yes-that is correct.

It follows therefore that the group of nephews and nieces who properly constitute the family of the deceased are the persons duly authorised and empowered by customary law to elect or select one of their number and appoint him as " successor" to the deceased, to take charge and control of the estate and manage it for them all. This indeed appears to have been accepted and acknowledged by the mem­bers of the Adam and Nglesi branches who went to represent those branches at the funeral obsequies of the deceased, and who, according to the evidence, were present at the meeting and without protest acquiesced in the appointment of the plaintiff by Kwesi Afedi and the other nephews and nieces-although they say the appointment was only as caretaker.

Turning now to the material facts: it was the plaintiff's case that, on the eighth day after the burial of the deceased, a meeting was held of the family consisting of the nephews and nieces and the children of the deceased in the family house at Obiri and in the presence of the representatives of the Adam and Nglesi branches. The meeting was presided over by Kwesi Afedi, admitted to be the most senior of the nephews and nieces, and his evidence-in-chief as to his position was as follows:

" At the time Eburahim died I was head. That was because before the deceased died, who was in his lifetime the head, he appointed me to act in his stead."

He was cross-examined and gave answers as follows:

Q. Who was responsible for the funeral? A. It is the head of the family.

Q. Who is that head?

A. lam.

It is one of the defendant's counsel's main arguments that as Kwame Saah was the head of the family, i.e., the whole or larger family, Kwesi Afedi had no authority by custom to appoint a successor to the deceased, Kofi Eburahim. As to this, I have already pointed out the misconception on which the argument is founded, namely the confusion of the identity of the wider 'rwidan family with that of the small Kofi Eburahim family. Applying words similar to those used by Deane C.J. in Larkai v. Amorkor (4) to the position of Kwame Saah, one can say that Kwame Saah is the present holder of the Twidan family stool; but he would not be a member of Kofi Eburahim's family so as to become head of his (Eburahim's) family and as such entitled .to the family property of Eburahim so as to sue for it in this court. I am satisfied therefore that K wesi Afedi was de facto and de jure the acknowledged head of the Kofi Eburahim family at the material time.

It is the further case of the plaintiff that the meeting presided over by Kwesi Afedi as aforesaid appointed him successor to his deceased uncle Kofi Eburahim, authorised him to take charge and control of all his properties and, in the meantime, to take steps to get his name substituted for that of his uncle in a concession matter in which the uncle was involved in the Concessions Division of the court at the time of his death. Almost the whole of the circumstances as to this meeting are admitted by the defendant and her witnesses, save only the nature of the appointment which was made of the plaintiff on that occasion.

As to this, whereas it is the case of .the plaintiff that he was appointed successor to his uncle, it is the case of the defendant and her witnesses that the plaintiff's appointment was only as caretaker .to take charge and control of the properties of the deceased until after the performance of his customary funeral, when a successor would be appointed. On that issue of fact as to the nature of the plaintiff's appointment I have no hesitation whatsoever in accepting the plain­tiff's version of the case and in rejecting that of the defendant and her witnesses. The decisive pieces of evidence in favour of the plaintiff are first, the fact that it is admitted that the whole family were aware that the deceased was engaged at the time of his death in a case in the Concessions Branch of the court here in Cape Coast about a parcel of land in the Nyakrome area and in respect of which he held a docu­ment in his own name, and that it therefore became necessary to appoint a successor to apply to be substituted for the deceased for the prosecution of the case. It is admitted that the whole family expressly authorised the plaintiff to take steps to get substituted to prosecute the case and even gave or provided him with funds for that purpose. I am unable to believe that he was authorised to get substituted to prosecute the case not as successor but as caretaker. Secondly, the unchallenged evidence of the plaintiff's first witness, Yerenchie, Pro­duce Supervisor for S.A.'r. at Swedru, who said he came specially to Obiri to ascertain who was .the duly appointed successor of the deceased, to whom a substantial sum of money was due and owing for cocoa supplied by him prior to his death, and that Kwesi Afedi and the Obiri family assured him not only orally, but by written confirmation signed inter alia by the literate brother of the defendant, that the plaintiff, Kofi Ansah, had been duly appointed the successor to whom he could pay the amount due to the deceased.

Able and learned counsel for the defendant has endeavoured to con­vince me that it is not possible according to native customary law and usage to make a proper and valid appointment of a successor until after what is generally called the " funeral custom" of the deceased. The "funeral custom" referred to is not the burial of ~he deceased with all necessary customary rites, but a custom whereby, some con­siderable time after the burial, there is a kind of memorial gathering by the members of his clan and his friends and acquaintances at which there is a display of mourning in the course of an amount of drumming and traditional dancing. Counsel put forward an expert witness who gave evidence to support his contention, but it is significant that the witness was cross-examined and admitted as follows:

Q.  Do you say you do not know of cases where, as soon as a man dies, his successor is appointed and it takes about three, four or five years before his customary funeral is performed?

R.   Yes-1 know of such cases.

If in the far distant past it was the custom, because there was no pressing necessity for the immediate appointment of a successor, it certainly is not the custom and usage now, when it is the more usual practice to appoint the successor with all convenient speed to step in the shoes of the deceased to look after his affairs and his dependants such as his widow and children, and to prepare for and see to the per­formance of his "customary funeral." What actually took place in this particular case is the clearest and strongest possible refutation of learned counsel's contention.

In the result therefore, I am convinced beyond a shadow of doubt that the plaintiff was duly elected not as mere caretaker, but as custo­mary successor of his deceased uncle and I give judgment in his favour that he is entitled to a grant of letters of administration as against the defendant. As, however, there has been evidence of moneys of the deceased which have come to his hands since his appointment but which have not been satisfactorily accounted for to Kwesi Afedi and the other members of the immediate family of the deceased, which conduct on the plaintiff's part would appear to be the cause of the present opposition against him, I hereby order that the issue of formal letters of administration to the plaintiff shall be subject to (1) his filing a fresh inventory disclosing all moneys and other personal properties of the deceased which have come into his hands since his appointment; (2) filing a statement of account of such moneys indicating such portion of it (if any) as has been spent and for what purposes; and (3) furnish­ing adequate security by two personal guarantors each in a sum amount­ing to the value of the personal estate for the proper administration of the estate. The said conditions to be complied with within two months from date, failing which liberty is hereby reserved to the family headed by Kwesi Afedi to apply.

Judgment for the plaintiff.

S. G. D.
