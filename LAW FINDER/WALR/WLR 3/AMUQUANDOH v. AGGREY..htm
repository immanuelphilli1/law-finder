                                                                               July 11, 1957

                                                   AMUQUANDOH v. AGGREY.

                                           High Court, Central Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                                  Adumua-Bossman J

                                    

 

 

Land-Landlord and tenant-Action for use and occupation-Defendant ~n adverse possession- Whether action maintainable.

The plaintiff sought to recover a sum of money, stated in the statement of claim to be rent, in respect of the defendant's use and occupation of the plaintiff's land. There had been previous litigation between the parties in which the issue had been as to the ownership of the land concerned, and this had been determined in favour of the plaintiff. There­after the defendant had quitted the land. The rent sought to be recovered in the present action was in respect of the whole period during which the defendant had been in occupation of the land.

Held, in order that the action for use and occupation be maintainable there must have been entry by the defendant for the purposes of occupation under an agreement with the plaintiff. The action is thus founded on contract and although agreement will be implied, as where nothing appears, except that the plaintiff is entitled to land which the defendant has occupied, yet it follows that the occupation must necessarily have been with the plaintiff's permission. Thus where the occupation has, as here, been adverse to the plaintiff (or wrongful, as by a trespasser) agreement is negated and no action for use and occupation can lie.

Cases referred to:

(1) Church ward v. Ford (1857) 2 H. & N. 446 j 26 L.J.Exch. 354; 157

E.R. 184.

(2) Hellier v. Silcox (1850) 19 L.J.Q.B. 295.

(3) Phillips v. Homfray (1883) 24 Ch.D. 439; 52 L.J.Ch. 833 j 49 L.T. 638.

(4) Attorney-General v. De Keyser's Royal Hotel [1920] A.C. 508.

ACTION for rent for use and occupation of land.

Awoonor Williams for the plaintiff. Bannerman Hyde for the defendant.

ADUMUA-BoSSMAN J. In this suit Ollennu J., on March 15, 1957, on the hearing of a summons for directions, made an order that: "the issues to be tried are those disclosed on the pleadings" and fixed adate for trial. Thereafter, upon the suit coming before me for trial on May 29, 1957, when counsel for the parties and the parties were present, I was obliged to raise the point of law which emerged clearly enough from the plaintiff's statement of claim. This was whether, in the circumstances pleaded by the plaintiff alleging an adverse entry and possession of the land by the defendant, and which made it necessary for the plaintiff to institute an action for a declaration of title and damages for trespass, which action was finally determined in his favour, the statement of claim on the face of it discloses any cause of action.

Counsel for the plaintiff argued that the defendant's occupation of the plaintiff's land entitled the latter, by an implied contract, to sue for use and occupation. with great respect, however, to learned counsel, the authorities do not appear to support his arguments. In Churchward v. Ford (1) Pollock C.B. said as follows:

" There are authorities to the effect that where nothing appears except that one person is entitled to land which another has occupied and enjoyed, an action for use and occupation may be maintained, because a contract may be implied. That explains the decision in Hellier v. Silcox (2). But the taking possession as of right by a disseisor could not be turned into a contract, on the notion that the trespass may be waived and some imaginary contract substituted."

Then in Phillips v. Homfray (3) Bowen L.J. said as follows:

"Actions for use and occupation, according to the better opinion, have been confined to the class of cases where the defen­dant is not a trespasser setting up an adverse title, and where there are no circumstances that negative the implication of a contract. "

Finally, in Attorney-General v. De Keyser's Royal Hotel (4), Lord Atkinson, who reviewed all the earlier authorities, said as follows:

"The authorities establish, I think, this proposition, that in order to recover in the ordinary action for use and occupation, the plaintiff must prove the existence of an agreement, express or implied, between him and the defendant to the effect that the latter shall at least be the tenant at will of the former of the lands or premises occupied and shall pay for that occupation."

In the light of the authorities above referred to, it seems to me beyond doubt that, to use the words of Pollock C.B. in Churchward v. Ford (1):

" the taking possession as of right by a disseisor (as the defendant clearly was) could not be turned into a contract, on the notion that the trespass may be waived and some imaginary contract substituted. "

The action in "use and occupation" is therefore, in my view, misconceived, and the statement of claim must be struck out as disclosing no cause of action against the defendant.

s. G. D.
