                                                         SARFO v. BONSU

                                                                                 Court of Appeal

                                  van Lare. Ag.C.J., Granville Sharp J.A., Ollennu J.

 

 

 

 

 Estoppel-Estoppel by deed-Grant of concession by joint lessors-Whether grant raises estoppel as between lessors in dispute as to ownership of land.

Estoppel-Estoppel by conduct-Concessions-Grant of concession by joint lessors-Concession by agreement proved by one lessor on behalf of all­Whether such agreement raises estoppel in dispute as to ownership of land.

      The plaintiff and defendant, on behalf of their respective stools, had submitted a dispute as to the boundary between them to an independent arbitrator. While the boundary demarcation was in progress they, together with two other land-owning stools, were approached by a pros­pective concessionaire who wished to obtain a grant of timber rights over a large area which included the land in dispute. There was no suggestion that the four stools between them could not make a valid grant affecting the concession area and on this basis a grant was duly made. It was not accepted, however, by either the plaintiff or the defendant that this grant determined the dispute between them. The plaintiff entered into the grant on the assumption that the finding of the arbitrator would result in a revision in his favour of the area of land defined by the grant as belonging to his stool: the defendant did so without prejudice to his prior assertion that the plaintiff's stool in fact owned no land at all within the area. Subsequently the plaintiff sought, as between his and the defendant's stool, a revision of the ratio in which the rent reserved under the grant was to be divided between them. The defendant refused to agree to such revision, and the plaintiff commenced this action.

For convenience, and by agreement between the four stools concerned, the grant of the concession was proved in the Concessions Court by the plaintiff on behalf of all the grantors.

At the hearing it was argued that the defendant was estopped from asserting that the plaintiff's stool owned no land at all within the grant area, first, because of the grant, which plaintiff and defendant had jointly executed, and secondly by reason of the defendant's conduct in agreeing to the grant being proved by the plaintiff on behalf of all the grantors.

Held, (i) there can be no estoppel arising out of a deed where the action is not founded on the deed, but is wholly collateral to it. For an estoppel to arise between the parties to a deed they must be bound to each other: a recital in a deed executed by joint grantors cannot be deemed to have created a binding obligation as between these grantors.

(ii) Even if it could be said that the defendant's act in agreeing to the plaintiff proving the grant on behalf of all the grantors amounted to an admission that the plaintiff had an interest in the land the subject­matter of the grant, yet such an admission could not give rise to an estoppel, for it created at best no more than a rebuttable presumption that the defendant admitted such interest.

Cases referred to:

(1) Heath v. Orealock (1874) 10 Ch.App. 22; 44 L.J.Ch. 157; 31 L.T. 650.

(2) General. Finance, Mortgage and Discount Co. v. Liberator Permanent Benefit Building Society (1878) 10 Ch.D. 15; 39 L.T. 600.

(3) Abiam Stool v. Enyimadu [1953] A.C. 207, P.C.

(4) Carpenter v. Buller (1841) 8 M. & W. 209; 10 L.J.Exch. 393.

APPEAL from a decision of Benson J. in the Land Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, on May 14, 1956, upholding a decision of the Asantehene's Court A.2 on March 4, 1954, rejecting an appeal from a decision of the Kumasi Division BA Court on November 30, 1953, in an action by the plaintiff's stool for a declaration of title to land.

Hayfron Benjamin and Siriboe for the plaintiff. Asafu-Adjaye, Akujo-Addo and Prempch for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. This is an appeal by special leave from the judgment of Benson J. sitting in the Land Court, Kumasi, delivered on May 14, 1956, dismissing an appeal from a judgment of the Asantehene Court " A.2." The plaintiff had lost both in the trial Native Court, the Kumasi Division Grade" BA," and in the Asantehene Court" A.2."

The history of the dispute is as follows: The plaintiff and the defendant are owners of adjoining lands. About seven years before the institution of the action, the Odikro of Kunsu, a sub-chief to the defen­dant, challenged the right of the plaintiff's people to collect snails on the land in dispute. Subsequent to and in consequence of that challenge an agreement was reached between the parties for the boundary between their lands to be demarcated and surveyed, and Otumfuo the Asantehene appointed the Adumhene to demarcate the boundary and each party paid his share of the fees for the work.

Meanwhile Messrs. A. Lang & Co. Ltd. wanted an area of land for a timber concession, and they approached the Asantehene who invited four chiefs, including the parties hereto, the alleged owners of land in the area, to Kumasi. This was to negotiate the lease with Messrs. Lang & Co. Ltd. in respect of their respective portions of any land which might be included within the concession area.

A lease was eventually executed by the parties in this suit jointly with the other two chiefs in favour of Messrs. A. Lang & Co. Ltd. Under that lease 5 per cent. of the rents reserved were payable to the plaintiff. By consent of all the joint lessors the plaintiff proved the grant of the concession in the Concessions Court on behalf of all four grantors, and a certificate of validity of the concession was granted to Messrs. A. Lang & Co. Ltd. The concession is known as Nyase Timber Concession.

According to the plaintiff it was agreed, before the execution of the lease, that his share of the rent reserved was to be revised after the extent of his portion of the land within the area of the concession had been ascertained as a result of the demarcation of the boundary between his land and that of the defendant. He says, however, that the defen­dant afterwards refused to agree to such revision, whereupon this action commenced.

Learned counsel for the plaintiff submitted that the defendant was estopped from denying that the plaintiff owned a portion of the land the subject-matter of the concession. The estoppel arose, first, by reason of the indenture of lease which the defendant executed with the plaintiff together with the other chiefs as grantors; and secondly by his conduct in holding out the plaintiff as a joint owner by agreeing to him alone proving the grant of the concession before the Concessions Court on behalf of all four grantors.

Counsel argued that the execution of the lease by the defendant jointly with the plaintiff was an acknowledgment or admission that the plaintiff had some interest in the land. Counsel further submitted that by agreeing to the plaintiff proving the concession before the court on behalf of all the grantors to obtain the grant of the certificate of validity, the defendant must be deemed to have conceded the plaintiff's interest in the subject-matter of the concession. Counsel therefore submitted that in view of section 32 (2) of the Concessions Ordinance the defendant cannot be heard to deny that fact and consequently the only issue which called for determination in the suit is the extent of the plaintiff's land within the area of the concession. He submitted that the Native Court failed to appreciate that issue and that they did not adjudicate upon it, with the result that there has been a miscarriage of justice.

Counsel for the defendant submitted that the defendant's execution of the lease cannot operate against him as an estoppel, nor can his agreement to the plaintiff proving the grant of the concession in the Concessions Court, because the evidence on record shows that the defendant had never at any time admitted or conceded any interest of the plaintiff in the subject-matter of the concession, and that the plaintiff agreeing to the defendant joining in the execution of the lease was an accommodating act to oblige the concessioners, Messrs. A Lang & Co. Ltd.

On this question the evidence of the plaintiff himself affords It useful guide. In the early part of his evidence he spoke about a boundary dispute between his land and that of the defendant, and said:

" I and Hiahene (the defendant) agreed upon the boundary being demarcated by a surveyor. Otumfuo the Asantehene appointed the Adumhene, who collected £25 from me and £25 from Hiahene. Adumhene started the demarcation and while the demarcation was in progress I was invited to I\umasi by the Hiahene who informed me that Messrs. A. Lang & Co. wanted the area in dispute for a timber concession. Bantamhene, Akwa­muhene, Hiahene and I met at Asantehene's house where the representative of the company met us. The company's represen­tative said I was entitled to 5 per cent. of the whole area which was owned by Hiahene, Bantamhene, Akwamuhene and me. I said that my boundary with Hiahene was under construction and until it had been completed it was not possible to determine my percentage of the area. The Asantehene's secretary said I should agree to the 5 per cent. for the purpose of completing the conces­sion agreement, and after the construction of the boundary I would be at liberty to ask for amendment of my percentage of the area of the concession. I agreed."

Towards the end of his evidence-in-chief the plaintiff repeated the evidence recited above and concluded as follows:

" After the construction of the boundary the defendant was reluctant to accept the amendment of the percentage so I swore the Great Oath on him that the land which stretches from a stone called Serebuo to a place called Bepokokoo the righ t side of this direction was defendant's and the left side was mine. The land in dispute is Nyasehahan. Defendant responded that I had no land there."

The defendant's version of the circumstances relating to the execu­tion of the lease is as follows: [199 ]

.. Just recently Messrs. A. Lang & Company approached the Otumfuo to allow them to fell timber on the Nyase land from Tinte Beposo to Offin. All the landowners of the place were summoned by the Asantehene. Nana Akroponghene, Nana Akwamuhene, Nana Krontihene and Nana Hiahene, myself, appeared before him, the Asantehene. The matter was put before us and we gave our consent. I opposed to the plaintiff's (Nana Akroponghene) signing the agreement which was to be prepared on it. I had to obey the advice of the Asantehene's private secretary, Mr. Boateng, who advised that I should be patient until the plan of the land was brought. When the plan came in I found that the plaintiff (Akroponghene) had no share in the concession."

\Vhen these two versions are considered, together with the account given by the Asantehene's private secretary, Mr. Boateng, referred to by each of the parties, no room is left for doubt that at the date of the execution of the deed of lease the plaintiff had not agreed on what his share, if any, in the area of the concession was; and the defendant had not agreed that the plaintiff owned any portion of the area of the con­cession and that both plaintiff and defendant only agreed to execute the lease because Mr. Boateng advised them to do so to accommodate the concessioners, with the assurance that the execution would not preclude any of them from having the contents of the lease revised to conform with the result of any decision which might be arrived at resulting from a demarcation of their boundaries.

A recital in a deed of lease that a lessor is well and sufficiently entitled to an estate free from incumbrance creates no estoppel for the purpose of vesting a legal estate in the lessor. A deed of that nature, though it would amount in equity to a representation to a lessee, does not amount in law to an admission by the lessee that the lessor has a right to grant. 'ro operate as an estoppel the recital must be shown to be precise or unambiguous: Heath v. Crealock (1); see also the case of Gener.al Finance, Mortgage and Discount Company v. Liberator Permanent Benefit Building Society (2), where many of the authorities are reviewed.

In this case the parties are agreed with a difference that the recitals in the lease of title of the grantors, and the statement of their respec­tive shares of the rents, is not a conclusive admission by the defendant that the plaintiff has a share in the subject-matter of the concession.

It was in the contemplation of the parties at the date of the execution of the lease that the plaintiff's rights in the concession would have to be determined subsequently to finalise or determine what interest, if any, the respective grantors had in the concession.

In the circumstances neither the execution of the lease nor the proving of the concession by the plaintiff as alleged for the purposes of the granting of a certificate of validity can operate as an estoppel [200] against the defendant in the stand he now takes. The plaintiff will have to prove that he owns land, a portion of which comes within the area of the concession, and that is what he set out to do in the present suit. Thus by his writ of summons and the evidence led in the suit, the plaintiff put in issue his ownership of an area of land. In my opinion the trial Native Court properly directed themselves on that issue. Learned counsel for the plaintiff endeavoured to show that the judgment was against the weight of evidence. In my view he has not succeeded in doing so. The decision on the facts is based partly on evidence of tradition, ancient grant by the Asantehene, and partly upon evidence of exercise of acts of ownership over the years. The trial Native Court accepted the tradition related by the defendant and rejected that deposed to by the plaintiff. They also found the weight of. the evidence as to possession in favour of the defendant. Apart from seeing and hearing the witnesses the trial Native Court are the custodians of the traditions and customs of the area and should be deemed to be able to apply it. Their judgment was confirmed both by the Asantehene's Court" A.2 " and the Land Court. It has not been shown that this case comes within any of the exceptions laid down by the Privy Council in the case of Abiam Stool v. Enyimadu (3), upon which a court of appeal can set aside concurrent findings of lower courts.

For these reasons I would dismiss the appeal.

VAN LARE Ag.C.J. The history of this case and the facts are fully set out in the judgment just delivered and it is not necessary for me to go over them.

The only ground of substance argued before this court is that by the recital in the lease or concession granted to Messrs. A. Lang & Co. Ltd., where the plaintiff's name appears as one of the grantors, an estoppel was created whereby the defendant, whose name also appears as one of the grantors, cannot be permitted to say that the plaintiff has no land in the area claimed by him because some portion of the disputed area comes within the concession. This is a novel proposition and appears to be confused with the proposition of law which is that parties are bound by recitals in deeds thus:

" If a distinct statement of a particular fact is made in the recital of a bond, or other instrument under seal, and a contract is made with reference to that recital, it is unquestionably true, that as between the parties to that instrument, and in an action upon it, it is not competent for the party bound to deny the recital, notwithstanding what Lord Coke says on the matter of recitals in Coke Littleton, 352 b: .... But there is no authority to show that a party to the instrument would be estopped, in  [201] an action by the other party not founded on the deed, and wholly collateral to it, to dispute the facts so admitted .... " per Baron Parke in Carpenter v. Buller (4).

Commenting on this passage Taylor on Evidence, 12th ed. , Vol. 1, p. 93, says the following:

" From this passage it would appear that, to make a recital operate as an estoppel, there must be, first, a distinct statement of some material particular fact; secondly, a contract made with reference to such statement; and, thirdly, either an action directly founded on the instrument containing the recital, or one which is brought to enforce the rights arising out of such instrument."

It is obvious, therefore, that a party to plead a recital in a deed as an estoppel must be a party bound to the other; it must be remembered that every estoppel must be reciprocal; in other words, the recital must operate as a contract between the parties concerned. But such is not the case here and I cannot therefore realise how joint grantors or several lessors in a deed can be deemed to have entered into a contract between themselves by reason of the recital. In my opinion as between joint lessors in a deed no question of estoppel arises, because the instrument can create no contract between themselves, except as between them on the one side and the lessee on the other. The point argued was therefore wholly misconceived and accordingly fails.

Even if the appellant does not base his estoppel on a recital in a deed but claims that the respondent should be presumed in law to have admitted that the appellant was the owner of a portion of the land granted by the lease, which admission is not conceded, it must be pointed out that it is well settled law that admissions are not estoppels and not conclusive against those against whom they are tendered. They always have the right to prove the circumstances or to show that they were due to erroneous conception of the law, or to ignorance of the real facts or to other circumstances which sufficiently explain them. It is, however, for the trial court to decide the issue and to give weight to the alleged admissions and give consideration to the explanatory facts or circumstances.

In this case the trial Native Court was not satisfied that the respondent ever admitted that the appellant had any land within the area granted in the lease, and the evidence is abundant in explaining the circumstances under which the appellant, the respondent and others jointly granted the lease in question to Messrs. A. Lang & Co. Ltd. It is plain that although the recital showed four chiefs as grantors in leasing the area to the grantees the transaction was such as to accommodate the grantees to enable them to commence operation on the land and that the grantors were left to determine  [202] between themselves the extent of land, if any, which they respectively owned.

It would appear that although the circumstances of the present action were connected with the lease in question, this action was not even instituted to determine an extent of land claimed by the appellant and included in the lease granted to Messrs. A. Lang & Co. Ltd., but is rather an action in respect of a declaration of title to a large stretch of a defined area which in part includes the area of the concession, and is in part outside it, but is in no way identical, and I am unable to appreciate how the question of estoppel or admission can arise in connection with the subject-matter of this suit. In the result I do not consider that there is any ground for interfering with the judgment appealed from and I would also dismiss the appeal.

GRANVILLE SHARP J.A. I agree.

Appeal dismi8sed.

S. G. D.
