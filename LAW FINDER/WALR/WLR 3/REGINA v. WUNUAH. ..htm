                                                                 REGINA v. WUNUAH. .                                                       

                                                      Court of Appeal

                                van Lare Ag.C.J., Granville Sharp J.A., Ollennu J

                                                   

 

Criminal law-Murder-Provocation-Importance of considering standards and way of life of community to which accused belongs-Proper direction to jury-Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 250.

Criminal law-Practice-Evidence-Summing-up-Onus of proof-Satisfac­tion of jury as to accused's guilt.

The appellant stood convicted of the murder of his wife. It had been his defence and, it was established that, his wife had acted in a provoca­tive manner towards him. This had led to a struggle during the course of which the appellant had, admittedly, stabbed his wife with a penknife. The trial judge had, in his summing-up, directed himself to the effect that it was for the court to decide if the provocation had deprived the accused of his self-control so that, only if loss of self-control were proved, would the defence of provocation succeed.

Held, (i) where a defence of provocation is raised it is an improper direction that the court must consider whether or not the alleged pro­vocation was such as deprived the accused of his self-control. A proper direction would be as to whether the provocation was such as was likely to deprive, 01' did in fact deprive the accused of his self-control.

(ii) Further, where provocation is put forward, it is important to consider the standards and way of life of the type of community to which the accused belongs as a background to the question as to whether the conduct of the deceased which is relied upon was such as to be likely to deprive the accused of his self-control.

(iii) Generally, it is improper to approach a defence on the basis of requiring it to be shown to be true, thus placing the burden of proof on the accused. Regardless of whether a defence be proved to be true, an accused is entitled to acquittal if his defence, in the view of jury, might reasonably be true. In this situation the prosecution must be regarded as having failed to discharge the onus upon it.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Guidance may be derived from the judgment of Lord Goddard C.J. in R. v. Hepworth and Fearnley [1955] 2 Q.B. 600; [1955] 2 All E.R. 918: "The jury should be directed first, that the onus is always on the prosecution, and secondly, that, before they convict, they must feel sure of the prisoner's guilt." See also R. v. Abisa Grunshie (1955) 1 W.A.L.R. 36.

On the subject of provocation the Criminal Code, s. 250, operates to reduce murder to manslaughter where, in the case of a person who has intentionally caused the death of another, it is " proved on his behalf. ... " Similarly, section 252, dealing with the cases where provocation is excluded, commences:

"Notwithstanding proof on behalf of the accused person .... " The apparent contradiction between the express wording of these sections and the directions of the Court of Appeal is perhaps explainable by the rule that, where in criminal cases a burden of proof is carried by the accused, the standard of proof required is that appropriate to civil, and not to criminal, issues-see R. v. Carr-Briant [1943] K.B. 6rtl; 29 Cr.App.R. 76; [1943] 2 All E.R. 156; and Sodeman v. R. [1936] 2 All E.R. 1138; [1936] W.N. 190.]

APPEAL from a conviction for murder by Smith J. sitting with assessors on September 7, 1957, at Kumasi Assizes.

Smith, Crown counsel, for the Crown.

Nyaterkyi for the appellant.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by VAN LARE Ag.C.J.: This is an appeal by Francis Wunuah alias Ondwa against his conviction for the killing of his wife Titi Yawonde. The trial was before Smith J. sitting with assessors at the assizes holden at Kumasi on September 7, 1957.

The case arose out of the following circumstances. The wife without the knowledge and consent of the husband, who appeared to have been very much in love with her and had spent a loot of money according to his standards upon her, got herself employed as a house-maid to a Syrian. The appellant, the husband, upon returning home hungry from work one evening and finding that there had been no food prepared for him went to inquire the position at the house where his deceased wife lived. It was then he discovered that his wife had got herself employed as mentioned. He was led by a young brother of the deceased to the Syrian's house and there requested the wife to come away with him.

According to the evidence the deceased wife acted in a manner which was both by words of mouth and demeanour insulting to ,the appellant who, in an attempt to prevent her from returning to her employment, held her by the hand, whereupon she slapped him on the face several times and pushed him, saying that she would continue to do her work for the Syrian and would not go with the appellant. A struggle suddenly ensued, both fell to the ground, and in the course of it the appellant inflicted wounds on the deceased with a pen-knife which he apparently normally carried on his person. One of the wounds severed the main blood vessels on the left side of the neck, from which she died.

There was no dispute as to the deceased dying as a result of an unlawful harm caused by the appellant, but .the defence right from the very beginning was that the act was done at a time when the appellant had lost his power of self-control as a result of provocation proffered by the deceased. That ~here was provocation was not in dispute but the learned trial judge appeared to have directed himself in holding that it was the function of the court to find whether the provocation did in fact deprive the appellant of his power of self­control. Weare of the opinion that the learned trial judge erred in this as the proper direction ought to have been whether the provocation was one likely to deprive or did deprive the appellant of his power of self-control, not simply whether it in fact did so.

The learned trial judge found that on the evidence there was " totally insufficient provocation to reduce the charge to manslaughter." We respectfully disagree. In this connection we draw attention with approval to what Lord Goddard C.J. said in R. v. Semini (1), at page 57: "No one doubts that blows or probably threatening gestures if near enough and serious enough to cause loss of control may justify and indeed require the killing to be regarded as manslaughter only."

We think that, had the learned trial judge considered whether the provocation was one likely to deprive the prisoner of the power of self­control in the circumstances in which he then was, especially having regard to his character and the community to which he belonged, he might fairly have proceeded to find further that the provocation was such as did in fact deprive the appellant of the power of self-control. The judge appears to have found only the one without considering the other, and he did so without regard to the evidence in this case that in the community to which the appellant belonged the conduct which the deceased exhibited towards the appellant would amount to such extreme provocation as to deprive a reasonable man of his power of self-control.

On this issue the judge simply rejected the appellant's defence that he acted at a time when he was deprived of the power of self-control and did not appreciate, until after the event, what he had done. In this the judge again erred by failure to consider the alternative arising, following upon the appellant's explanation not being accepted, whether such explanation might reasonably be true. It would appear to this court that there is a tendency growing on the part of trial courts in this country to confine considerations in cases where the explanation of an accused person is in issue, to whether or not the explanation is true and not to consider the alternative narrower issue whether the explanation of the prisoner might reasonably be ~rue. This tendency in our view is dangerous and we must seriously deprecate it. Concerning the rules to be followed, on giving directions as to the weight attaching to adefence which might reasonably be true, we cannot over-emphasise the importance of the case of R. v. Murtagh and Kennedy (2).  

The position of the law in our opinon on the authorities is that if the explanation given by the prisoner is not accepted by the court, nevertheless if it might reasonably be true a doubts as to guilt arises, and in that event the prosecution has failed to discharge the onus on it, and the prisoner is entitled to the benefit of such doubt.

We would not have interfered with the verdict in this case if the learned trial judge had adequately directed himself and pointed out the alternative on the narrow issue of fact concerning the reasonableness of the appellant's explanation. We think that had the proper considera­tions of the facts in this case been given the verdict would have been that of manslaughter and not of murder.

For these reasons we allow the appeal. The conviction for murder is quashed and set aside and a verdict of manslaughter is substituted. The appellant is sentenced to ten years' imprisonment with hard labour.

Conviction for manslaughter substituted for conviction for murder.

S. G. D.
