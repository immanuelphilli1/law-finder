                                                   June 9, 1958

                                REGINA v. JACKSON. Ex parte OFORI ATTA II.

                                                               Court of Appeal

                           korsah C.J., Granville Sharp J.A. and Ollennu J

 

 

Prohibition-Natural justice-Audi alterem partem rule--Refusal of tribunal constituted under Stool Boundaries Settlement Ordinance to allow a party to be heard or to allow cross-examination of a witness.

By an order dated June 30, 1953, made by the Governor of the Gold Coast under the Stool Boundaries Settlement Ordinance, Mr. John Jackson, Stool Boundaries Settlement Commissioner, was appointed to inquire into and to determine the boundaries of the State of Akwamu with that area known as the Tongu Confederacy, and with any State having boundaries contiguous with that of Akwamu.

By a similar order dated August 8, 1953, Mr. Jackson was also appointed to inquire into and to determine the boundaries of the State of Manya Krobo with any State having boundaries contiguous thereto.

By virtue of these orders the Settlement Commissioner caused notices to be served on persons who, in his opinion, had boundaries contiguous with the boundaries of the States concerned. Acting under the Stool Boundaries (Manya Krobo) Order, 1953, the Settlement Commissioner caused notice to be served on the Okyenhene of the Akyem Abuakwa State; but no notice was served on him with respect to the Stool Boundaries (Akwamu) Order, 1953.

Evidence of witnesses in the two inquiries had been concluded, but only the findings of the Stool Boundaries (Akwamu) Order had been published.

     The Okyenhene of the Akyem Abuakwa State made application to the High Court for leave to issue a writ of prohibition, directed to the Com­missioner, to prohibit him from proceeding further with" the inquiry and finding under the Stool Lands Boundaries (Manya Krobo) Order, 1953." Some of the grounds filed in support of the application related to the views expressed by the Settlement Commissioner on controversial matters affecting the interest of the appellant, and contained in Part II of the said findings, described as the Akwamu Findings. Among the grounds were the following:

" 3. Deciding without hearing evidence: Without previously hear­ing the evidence of the Akyem Abuakwa Stool, first given on the 25th October, 1955, the Commissioner decided and pre-judged several issues pending before him in the Manya Krobo Enquiry (Manya Krobo Order) by the Akwamu Finding Part II given on the 4th November, 1954, in the Akwamu Enquiry (Akwamu Order, 1953).

"6. Excess of Jurisdiction: By using Exhibit No. 130 put in by the Akyem Abuakwa Stool in the Manya Krobo Enquiry, in which the Akyem Abuakwa Stool is a contesting party, in the Akwamu Enquiry, in which the Akyem Abuakwa is not a contesting party, and by deciding the issues raised by the said Exhibit against the Akyem Abuakwa Stool in the Akwamu Finding G.N.111j1956.

"7. Absence of Jurisiliction: By declaring in the Akwamu Find­ing Part II (Akwamu Order) that' the same consequences' declared by the Commissioner in the Akwamu Finding' follow in respect of all that land west of the river Pawmpawm,' which is the land in respect of which the Akyem Abuakwa Stool and the Manya Krobo Stool are contesting parties in the separate Manya Krobo Enquiry (Manya Krobo Order) and in respect of which at the date of the Akwamu Finding Part II, viz., 4th November, 1954, evidence was still pending to be heard by the Commissioner."

It was also alleged, with respect to the Stool Boundaries (Manya Krobo) Order, 1953, that at the sitting at Odumase, Nene Mate Kole (Konor of Manya Krobo, and a principal witness for the Manya Krobo Stool) had produced a number of diaries and records dating from the year 1906 to the year 1910, particular pages of which were tendered to the Commissioner. These referred to cases affecting Akyem Abuakwa lands, and purporting to have been heard by the late Nene Mate Kole, Konor of Manya Krobo, or in a joint assembly of the Konor of Manya Krobo and the Benkumhene of Akyem Abuakwa, Begoro, a senior Divisional Chief in the Akyem Abuakwa State. Some of these diaries and records were not entituled as being in a tribunal or court, while others were transcriptions in what purported to be a letter book. The Commissioner had acceped the diaries and records, as well as a typewritten copy of a judgment, and made notes from certain passages which had been marked by a person unknown. These noted portions were pointed out to him by the Konor, and the Commissioner made such notes part of the proceedings before him. It was alleged, however, that when counsel for the Okyenhene of the Akyem Abuakwa State expressed a desire to cross-examine the Konor of Manya Krobo, who had produced those diaries and records, the Commissioner refused the application and stated he would not permit any cross-examination of the Konor on the diaries and records produced by the Konor, and from which the Commissioner had made notes.

Held, (i) on an application for a writ of prohibition the court would look at affidavit evidence filed in support of the application.

(ii) If the . allegation in the appellant's affidavit (that his counsel was refused the right to cross-examine the principal witness for the Manya Krobo Stool), were true, there had been a departure from the rules of natural justice such as would induce the court to grant the leave sought against a tribunal constituted under the Stool Boundaries Settlement Ordinance.

(iii) If the appellant had not been given the opportunity to be heard, this was a further departure from the rules of natural justice.

Cases referred to:

(1) R. v. Wandsworth Justices, ex p. Read [1924) 1 K.B. 281; [1942) 1 All E.R. 56.

(2) R. v. North, ex p. Oakey [1927) 1 K.B. 491, C.A.

ApPEAL from a decision of Benson J. on January 19, 1957, refusing an interested Stool-occupant leave to issue an order of prohibition to the Stool Lands Boundaries Settlement Commissioner.

Danquah (Dua Sakyi with him) for the appellant. Akiwumi, Crown counsel, for the respondent.

KORSAH C.J. stated the facts and continued: In Halsbury's Laws . of England (3rd ed.), Vol. 11, p. 64, at para. 122, it is stated:

" A power analogous to that of restraining by means of the orders of prohibition and certiorari any excess of jurisdiction by a person or body exercising judicial or quasi-judicial functions is the power to restrain or to quash the decisions of those persons or bodies if they fail to observe what have been called the rules of natural justice."

It was held in The King v. Wands worth Justices (1), that where there has been a denial of justice before a court of summary juris­diction, resulting in the conviction of a defendant, his remedy is not by way of case stated, or appeal to quarter sessions, but by an applica­tion to the High Court for an order of certiorari to remove and quash the conviction. On such an application the court, to ascertain the facts, would look at affidavit evidence filed in support of the application. Humphreys J. said:

" If a person can satisfy this court that he has been convicted of a criminal offence as the result of a complete disregard by an inferior tribunal of the laws of natural justice, he is entitled to the protection of the court. "

If the allegation contained in the appellant's affidavit, filed in support of his application, to the effect that his counsel was refused the right to cross-examine the principal witness for the Manya Krobo Stool, be true, then in my view there has been a departure from the rules of natural justice such as would induce the court to grant the leave sought.

In Ex parte Oakey. The King v. North (2), Bankes L.J. said:

" The rule of law applicable to such a case is thus stated in Broom's Legal Maxims, 9th ed., p. 78: 'It has long been a received rule that no one is to be condemned, punished, or deprived of his property in any judicial proceeding unless he has had an opportunity of being heard.' "

A number of other grounds have been filed and canvassed by the appellant in support of his application, but I consider those to which I have referred are sufficient to enable the court to grant ,the leave sought, in order that the respondent may be heard and justice may be done to all parties. For these reasons I would allow this appeal and would grant leave to the appellant to issue the writ of prohibition to the respondent, in order that the questions raised may be deter­mined according to law, the appellant to bear his costs.

Appeal allowed.
