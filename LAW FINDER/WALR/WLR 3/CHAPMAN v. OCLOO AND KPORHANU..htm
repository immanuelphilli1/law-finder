                                                                               August 30, 1957

                                          CHAPMAN v. OCLOO AND KPORHANU. .

                                       High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                                                 Ollennu J.

 

 

Native Law and Custom-Family property-Stool property-Evidence­ Person suing on behalf of stool or family-Capacity put in issue.

In this action the plaintiff purported to sue on behalf of his family in respect of what he claimed to be family property. The defendants challenged the plaintiff's authority to sue on behalf of his family but the plaintiff adduced no evidence to establish that he had such authority.

Held, (i) where a party to an action sues in a representative capacity, on behalf of a stool or a family, and his authority so to sue is put in issue by the defendant, the burden of establishing authority is upon the plaintiff.

Sokpui II and Others v. Tay Agbozo III and Gatsikpo and Others (1951) 13 W.A.C.A. 241 applied.

(ii) Save in exceptional circumstances the proper person to sue on behalf of a family, in respect of family interests, is the head of the family. The burden of proof where authority is challenged will thus be discharged, by establishing headship. Where the person challenged is not the family head the burden will be discharged only where the express authority of the family is established by that person.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. So also a" defendant, sued as representing a stool or family, can put the plaintiff to proof of his (the defendant's) authority. The burden rests always on him who relies upon the representative capacity to maintain his action: Keelson v. Mensah (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 271.

It would appear that precisely the same principles apply where repre­sentation in respect of stool interests is concerned. Here the '~exceptional circumstances," which if fully applicable would normally require a chief to litigate personally on behalf of his stool, are somewhat wider, in that there is a class of persons besides the chief who are the stool's natural representatives. See Ofori Atta II and Others Y. Boateng (1957) 3 W.A.L.R. 38. See also Ofuman Stool Y. Nchima and Bmnam Stools (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 229.]

Case referred to :

Sokpui II and Others Y. Tay Agbozo III (HuJ/ Gatsikpo and Others (1951)  13 W.A.C.A. 241.             ""

APPEAL from a decision of the Anlo " A " Native Court on Septem­ber 24, 1956, finding for the plaintiff who sought, on behalf of his family, to recover land.

Koranteng Addo for the defendants.

The plaintiff did not appear and was not represented.

OLLENNU J. In this case the plaintiff sued in a representative capacity, that is, " for himself and on behalf of his brothers constituting the family of the late W. H. Chapman."

At an early stage in the proceedings the defendant challenged the plaintiff's authority to institute the action, and put the following question to him: "Have you authority to institute this action?" To that question the plaintiff replied: "H is not necessary." Maintaining that contention the plaintiff led no evidence to prove that he had been duly authorised by the Chapman family to bring the action.

\Vorse than that, both by his oral and documentary evidence the plaintiff proved that the property in dispute was owned not by the Chapman family exclusively, but by them jointly with the Ocloo family, of which the defendant is a member, and that the two families acting together had appointed one Robert Kojo Ocloo caretaker of the property. This man, Robert Kojo Ocloo, gave evidence immediately after the plaintiff, and is described in the proceedings as " witness for both parties." Thus making proof of the plaintiff's authority to sue all the more necessary .

. When the authority of a person to sue in a representative capacity is challenged the onus is upon him to prove that he has been duly authorised. He cannot succeed on the merits without first satisfying the court on this important preliminary point. See the case of Sokpui II and Others v. Tay Agbozo III and Gatsikpo and Others (1).

In the case of action on behalf of a stool or a family, proof that the plaintiff is the occupant of the stool or the duly constituted head of the family is sufficient to shift that onus.

From his answer quoted above, and his conspicuous failure to adduce any evidence on this issue of representation, it was made clear that the plaintiff did not consider that he needed authority to sue, and that he has, in fact, not been authorised to sue.

The Native Court failed to direct themselves on this most important preliminary issue. If they had properly directed themselves they would have had no alternative but to dismiss the plaintiff's claim and enter judgment for the defendants .

. Since the plaintiff's authority to sue was not established the other two points raised by Mr. Koranteng Addo, learned counsel for the defendants, namely (1) whether one branch of the family can maintain an action in respect of the joint property without the consent and con­currence of the other branch, and (2) whether a family can maintain an action for trespass against a member of the family occupying a portion of the family land, do not call for determination.

One further point, however, deserves to be mentioned. The plaintiff deposed in his evidence-in-chief that the joint family had disposed of the land in dispute, as to one portion to one Borluvi and as to the other portion to one Kporhanu. If that is the case the family no longer has any interest in the land to support an action. Thus even on the merits the plaintiff's case must fail.

Consequently both on the preliminary issue and on the merits the Native Court erred in entering judgment for the plaintiff. The appeal is accordingly allowed; the judgment of the Native Court is set aside, including the order as to costs, and it is ordered that any sums paid in pursuance of that order should be refunded. The plaintiff's claim is dismissed, and judgment entered for the defendants.

 

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
