                    NUNOO AXD OTHERS V. NYIMFA AND OTHERS .

High Court, Central Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                                        Adumua-Bossman J.

Concession--Landlord and tenant-Agreement for a lease-Whether an instru­ment within Concessions Ordinance, c. 136, s. 2.

Landlord and Tenant-Agreement for a lease-Local government-Whether a disposal of stool land within the meaning of the Local Government Ordi­nance, c. 64, s. 75.

Landlord and Tenant-Agreement for a lease-State councils-Party put in possession under agreement-Whether an alienation within the meaning of the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 16.

Contract-Implied term-Agreement to convey land or an interest in land-­Necessity for consents before conveyance effective-Duty of parties to seek consents-Local Government Ordinance, c. 64, s. 75; State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 16.

The plaintiffs sought specific performance of an agreement to execute a lease. They were the successors to one John Carahalios, who was the proprietor of a business known as the Apam Salt Producers and who worked some 200 acres of Apam Stool land under a lease for 99 years granted in 1944. Carahalios sold the business and assigned the lease to the plaintiffs in 1953. They negotiated a fresh lease with the stool authorities and, having done so, sought its ratification in the Concessions Court as a concession. The defendants, as holders of certain traditional offices under the stool, obtained leave to oppose, their grounds being that they were persons whose assent to the lease should have been but in fact had not been obtained. In the course of the concession inquiry the parties were able to come to terms. These terms replaced the negotiated lease, were reduced into writing and were made an order of the court (as to the effect of which, see Nunoo and Others v. Nyimfa and Others (1956) 2 W.A.L.R. 161). They provided that a new lease was to be drawn up, and stipulated who should sign on behalf of the grantor stool; provided for the plaintiffs to remain in possession until the new lease should be executed; stipulated the rents payable; and, after various other provisions, concluded in clause 9: " Form of the lease as finally drafted to be approved by the parties before execution and grant of certificate of validity." It was this agreement that the plaintiffs now sought to enforce.

It was argued for the plaintiffs, inter alia, that clause 9 was analogous to the" subject to contract" clauses common in agreements for the sale of land and that until a final draft was produced and approved it effectively prevented any enforceable agreement from coming into exist­ence. Further it was argued that if agreement was in fact complete it was nevertheless unenforceable because it purported to grant or demise an interest in land but had not been registered by the court as a concession, as is required by the Concessions Ordinance, c. 136; alternatively it was void because it involved a disposal of an interest or right in land by a stool but had been made without the concurrence of the appropriate local authority, as is required by the Local Government Ordinance, c. 64, s. 75; or because it was an instrument, or transaction or agreement, within the meaning of the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 16, which purported to "alienate or pledge" stool property without the consent of the state council concerned.

Held, (i) although the agreement between the parties contemplated the preparation and execution of a formal lease it was nevertheless a complete agreement so far as it went, and, being intended by its parties to be enforceable as between them and being supported by the consideration of refraining to pursue the concession inquiry, was so enforceable.

(ii) The agreement did not purport to "grant or demise" a "right title or interest in or to land" and was therefore not an instrument within the meaning of the Concessions Ordinance, c. 136, s. 2. It thus did not require to be validated by the court before it became enforceable.

(iii) The agreement did not purport to be a disposal, by a stool, of "any interest or right in land which involves the payment of any valuable consideration" within the meaning of the Local Government Ordinance, c. 64, s. 75. It thus did not require the concurrence of the urban or local council for the area concerned to become effective.

(iv) In view of the fact that provision was made in the agreement for the plaintiffs to continue in possession until such time as a formal lease was prepared, it must be regarded as a purported alienation of stool property. The consent of the appropriate state council not having been obtained the agreement was thus null and void by the operation of the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 16.

(v) Where parties enter into an agreement to convey land or an interest in land, which conveyance when executed cannot be effective until necessary consents have been obtained, then it is an implied term of the agreement that the parties themselves will take all due steps to ensure that such consents are obtained. It is not open to a party who has made no move to obtain the necessary consents to take the objection, as against the other party, that because of the absence of consents his agreement cannot be enforced as against him.

ACTION for specific performance of an agreement for a lease, for an injunction, and for damages. At the first hearing on September 20, 1957, preliminary points were taken by the defendants to the effect that the court had no jurisdiction to entertain the action, on the grounds, inter alia, that the agreement was ineffective by the operation of each of the Concessions Ordinance, c. 136, the Local Government Ordinance, c. 64, and the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952. After holding against the defendants the case was heard on its merits and judgment given on November 8, 1958. On this occasion the learned judge reserved certain questions of law to the Court of Appeal under the Court of Appeal Ordinance, c. 35 of 1957, s. 6. It subsequently became apparent that the Court of Appeal would not be able to deal with the matter for some time and the learned judge thereupon delivered final judgment on the questions he had reserved on November 29, 1958.

B entsi Enchill for the plaintiffs.

Mercer and Sackeyfio for the defendants.

   September 20, 1957.

ADUMUA-BOSSMAN J. On the defendants' point that the agreement is an instrument which has not been validated by the court as a concession, and is thus now ineffective by the operation of the Con­cessions Ordinance, c. 136, s. 8, it is only necessary to refer to the reply of counsel for the plaintiffs in the reply as follows:

., In further answer to paragraph 6-the plaintiffs say that the said settlement is not a concession within the meaning of the Concessions Ordinance, but an agreement to grant a concession."

It would appear as though learned counsel for the defendants did not carefully enough study the contents of the document dated December 2, 1954, that is being sued on. That document contains no words of " grant or demise" which constitutes an instrument under section 2 of the Ordinance. The document, containing the words in clause 2 thereof "A new lease to be drawn up and substituted therefor" is, on the face of it, an agreement or undertaking (providing other requirements of a valid agreement are available) for a lease, or rather the execution of an indenture of lease to effect a demise, and clearly falls outside the definition of a concession in section 2 of the Ordinance. It is the distinction between the agreement for the convey­ance and the formal instrument or indenture of conveyance which appears to have been overlooked, and I must hold, therefore, that this second point is also misconceived.

The next point raised by the defendants is to the effect that the agreement is ineffective because the provisions of the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, have not been complied with.

As to this point, there is no doubt that as the law stands at present, two enactments affect stool lands intended to be transferred. The provisions of the Local Government Ordinance, s. 75, are in the follow­ing terms:

" Any disposal of any interest or right in land which involves the payment of any valuable consideration by a stool shall be subject to the concurrence of the urban or local council as the case may be for the area concerned and shall be of no effect unless and until such concurrence has been obtained and certified in writing under the hand of the chairman or clerk of the council."

The provisions of the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 16, are in the following terms:

" It shall not be lawful, without the consent of the state council concerned, to alienate or pledge any stool property and any instru­ment or any transaction or agreement which purports to effect any such alienation or pledge shall, in the absence of such consent for all purposes be null and void."

It would appear as though the traditional authority, the state council, as well as the local council controlling the stool concerned, are both now required to sanction "disposal" or "alienations" or " pledges" of stool land. From the wording of both sections set out above it seems clear that if the agreement, whether verbal or in writing, amounts to a transfer of stool property then it is not valid without the consents prescribed.

I am not able, however, to read in the words of either enactment that a preliminary agreement for the " disposal" or " alienation" or ., pledge" is not valid until it has the sanction of the local or state council. Here again I think the distinction is between the agreement for the conveyance on sale, lease, pledge or mortgage, and the formal conveyance. The words of both enactments clearly and unambiguously point only to the latter, i.e., the conveyance (or document reciting and confirming an outright transfer by form of native customary law) by which there is a " disposal" of interest in land to satisfy section 75 of c. 64, or "an alienation or pledge of land" to satisfy section 16 of c. 8/52.

It was also argued by learned counsel for the plaintiffs that the objection as to the absence of the consent of the council would not be open to one party to the contract. There seems to be force in the argument, since the confirmation or approval of the councils must be of something which has been sought by two parties, a transferor and transferee. At the stage where either party is sued to enforce his contract to conveyor accept a conveyance, I do not think it can be open to him to plead the non-consent of the councils unless application has been made by both parties and for some reason one or both councils have refused consent. But I cannot see how stool representa­tives who, in pursuance of an express contract, are bound to execute a conveyance for the alienation of stool land to a purchaser, can plead or rely on absence of consent for their defence, before they have executed the conveyance and thus before the inchoate conveyance can ever be submitted to the councils. It would be manifestly contrary to the principles of equity to allow such a plea.

For the foregoing reasons, I am of the opinion that the court is properly seised of this suit and that the writ and statement of claim disclose a sufficient cause of action and that accordingly trial of the case will proceed in due course.

November 8, 1958.

His Lordship dealt with the facts of the case (as set out above) and, turning to the defendants' argument that clause 9 of the agree­ment left outstanding matters to be settled by the parties, with the effect that there was so far no complete agreement between them and thus nothing that could be enforced, he reviewed the authorities and continued:

Applying then the tests laid down in the cases, into which class of agreements does the agreement fall? It is clearly not a case where the parties have agreed that, to borrow the words of Jessel M.R. in Winn v. Bull (1):

" up to a certain point the terms shall be the terms of the contract but that the minor terms shall be submitted to a solicitor and shall be such as are approved by him. "

It is also certainly not a case where, to borrow the words of Lord Hatherley in Rossiter v. Miller (2)

" It is stated in so many plain and express terms that one of the very terms of the agreement itself was that ... it should not be a concluded agreement until a solicitor intervened and drew up a formal contract."

It is to be particularly observed that, unlike all the cages relied on by counsel for the defendants, the agreement does not even contem­plate the drawing up, approval and signing of any formal agreement or contract, before the drawing up of the formal lease. In my construction of it, it falls within the class of complete or concluded agreements. The very background to, and the circumstances surrounding the making of it, in my view, completely negative the suggestion that it was no more than mere negotiation to be subsequently converted into a concluded agreement only after further discussions to agree on other terms, besides those clearly expressed and such as were necessarily implied by law, as now contended. It is to be remembered that here was not a case where the parties were ignorant of or uncertain as to the terms of the lease to be granted. There was a lease with all its terms clearly before them, challenged only in so far as some of the persons who signed as representing the stool or State of Apam was concerned. In the course of the judicial combat on that point, the parties, to their credit, had called a truce and come to an agreement or compromise that: "The present lease the subject-matter of the inquiry to be cancelled-A new lease to be drawn up and substituted therefor." Then provision was expressly made in respect of the main matter in dispute as follows: "The new lease to be executed by the Ohene and principal Elders and Councillors for the time being of the Stool of Apam. Signatories to be made up of grantor and four persons nomi­nated by him and four persons nominated by opposers." Had the document ended there, followed by the signatures of the parties, I should have thought that all that was necessary was the retyping of the old lease with all its terms as a new lease to be signed in the manner newly agreed on. In so far as other terms were introduced which might have been inconsistent with the terms of the old lease, the old lease was to be modified or altered to that extent. The agree­ment is certainly capable of that construction. In any event, without reference to the terms of the old lease, the new agreement was complete and self-sufficient by itself, as I have endeavoured to indicate above. In such circumstances the last clause of the document can mean no more than what it says, that the "Form of the lease as finally drafted" should be approved before execution, no doubt to see that it was in conformity with the terms agreed on, and contained no new terms not agreed on, or covenants to which either party was not entitled by law. The agreement does not stipulate, as I read and understand it, for any more "formal agreement or contract to be approved," to bring a concluded contract into being, before the formal lease is drafted. The instrument presupposed and accepted the sub­sistence of a concluded agreement or contract, gave possession under that concluded agreement, and merely stipulated that the "form of the lease as finally drafted" (necessarily in accordance with the terms, expressed and implied by law, of that concluded agreement) should be approved before execution. That right to approve the final draft lease before execution surely existed, or was implied by law, even without the express provision in the agreement. Those words construed by learned counsel for the defendants as introducing a new term into the agreement, would seem to have been inserted ex abundanti cautela to use the words of Lord Cairns in Hussey v. Horne-Payne (3) as: " meaning nothing more than a guard against its being supposed that the final draft lease would be accepted without its being approved in the usual way." Therein, I think, lies the all-decisive, real and vital difference between our particular case, and those cited and relied on by counsel for the defendants. Our particular case, it seems to me, is more like that referred to by Lord Hatherley in Rossiter v. Miller (2) when he said:

" Both parties desire that it shall be put into a formal shape by a solicitor who in that case will not be able to vary the agreement on one side or the other but only to put into a more formal and professional shape the agreement which had been completely formed with unity of purpose."

It approximates more closely to the cases of Cayley v. Walpole (4), Rossiter v. Miller (2), Chipperfield v. Carter (5), Branca v. Cabar1'o (6) and to Eadie v. Addison (7). As pointed out by Lord Wright in the House of Lords in the case of Scammell v. Guston (8):

 The court will do its best, if satisfied that there was an ascer­tainable and determinate intention to contract, to give effect to that intention looking at substance and not mere form."

I am satisfied of such ., ascertainable and determinate intention to contract," both from 'the substance and from the form of the instru­ment. I accordingly hereby give effect to that intention by holding the contract comprised in the instrument to be a concluded contract.

The matter, however, does not end there, and the question has to be considered whether what I have found to be a concluded contract is affected by either or both of two local Ordinances, 'the Concessions Ordinance, c. 136, and the State Councils Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952. The question was raised by counsel for the defendants and considered by me in my interlocutory ruling given on September 20, 1957, but at the final hearing he asked and I gave leave for the matter to be re-argued in the light of authority discovered by him since that ruling. His first argument was that the instrument fell within the definition of a " concession" and had become void by reason of non-compliance with the provisions of the Concessions Ordinance. He referred to Elkali v. Fawaz (9), and stated that he relied on it. In that case it was stated in the judgment that the ground of objection to the admis­sibility in evidence of the document, an agreement. to grant a lease, was that it was-

. an instrument affecting land and not having been registered, was inadmissible under 'the provisions of section 15 of the Land Registration Ordinance, 1924, of Nigeria."

The full text of section 15 of the Ordinance 36/24 and the definition of . instrument" under it were, however, not given in any part of the reported judgment. However, in the subsequent case of Yaya v. Mogoga (10) the text of section 15 of the Ordinance 36/24 of Nigeria was given as follows:

. No instrument shall be pleaded or given in evidence in any court as affecting any land unless the same shall have been registered. "

The definition of instrument under section 2 of that Ordinance was also set out as follows:

"Instrument means a document affecting land in Nigeria wherein one party ... confers transfer ... in favour of another party ... any right or title to or interest in land in Nigeria."

In that case a document which constituted an agreement for the sale of a house and an acknowledgment of the payment of the purchase price was held by the court" Not to transfer any right or title " and therefore not an instrument under the Ordinance.

But in the subsequent case of Ogunbambi v. Abowab (11) the court held that a document purporting to be a purchase-receipt" was an agreement for sale coupled with an acknowledgment of receipt for the purchase-money and a covenant to execute a conveyance on demand" and was an "instrument" defined by section 2 as a  "document    whereby one party ... confers ... in favour of another party    any right or title to or interest in land in Nigeria." Verity Ag.P. went on to say as follows:

" In view of this definition, I think 'the submission that the document in this case is an instrument within the meaning of the Ordinance is well founded, and that it could neither be pleaded nor given in evidence as affecting this land."

It appears, however, that 'the definition under the Nigerian Ordinance whereby the mere" conferring of any right to land," e.g., the right to call for a conveyance or demise, constitutes a document or writing an " instrument," is much broader and more general than the much more restricted definition under our Concessions Ordinance, s. 2, whereby a document must" purport to grant or demise an interest in land" before it can satisfy the definition of a concession. The further arguments of counsel for the defendants as to the instrument not being enforceable because it contravenes the Concessions Ordinance have therefore not been such as to persuade me to alter my interlocutory decision in respect of 'that particular matter.

As to the second and further argument, however, that the document is one which from its nature comes within the purview of the State Councils Ordinance, s. 16, and as such requires to have the consent of the state council to validate it, I have had to reconsider the con­tention and in the light of the Nigerian authorities to which I have referred, principally Ogunbambi v. Abowab (11), and I am obliged to accept the view that the agreement does fall within the purview of section 16.

The definition of " alienation" in Stroud's Judicial Dictionary, to which counsel for the plaintiffs himself referred me, contains the following: "Alienation ... to alter or put the possession from one man to another." In the view I have taken of 'the document, that it gave possession to the plaintiffs as from December 2, 1953, it is not now possible to hold that the document does not "purport to effect alienation" within the contemplation of section 16. The matter cannot, however, be said to be entirely free from doubt, having regard to the divergence of views expressed in Yaya v. Mogaga (10) and the subsequent case of Ogunb,ambi v. Abawab (11). In each, the document constituted an obvious agreement for transfer by sale of premises, yet whereas in the first 'the court held the agreement did not operate to transfer right or title, in the second case it held that the document did operate to transfer (or at any rate confer) right to the premises the subject-matter of the transaction.

In the circumstances, I do hereby pursuant to the Appeal Court Ordinance, s. 6, reserve for the consideration of the Appeal Court the following questions of law arising in the suit:

(1) Whether, as held by me, upon a true construction of the docu­ment, it constitutes a concluded contract.

(2) Whether, as held by me, the document does not" purport to grant or demise land" and is therefore not a concession under the Concessions Ordinance, s. 2.

(3) Whether, as held by me, the said document constitutes "an agreement which purports to effect an alienation of stool property" and is therefore void without the consent of the state council under the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 16.

Final judgment will be delivered in 'the light of the Appeal Court's decision on the said points.

November 29, 1958.

On Saturday, November 8, 1958, I pronounced my findings of fact and law in this suit, but reserved certain questions of law for the con­sideration of the Appeal Court under the Appeal Court Ordinance, s. 6.

I was then under the impression tha't the Appeal Court would be sitting until the end of the term and would be able to deal with the matter before the vacation. It now appears, however, that the Appeal Court's session is over, and I am reliably informed that there may not be another session till the return of the Chief Justice some time in February. Under 'the circumstances, I am obliged to, and do hereby, rescind my order referring certain questions of law for the considera­tion of the Appeal Court, and in accordance with the findings I have pronounced, proceed now to final judgment in the suit.

As I have found, there was a concluded agreement, but this was one which operated to give or transfer immediate possession of stool land, and came therefore under the purview of section 16 of the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 16, which provides:

., It shall not be lawful, without the consent of the state council concerned, to alienate ... any stool property; and any instrument or any transaction or agreement which purports to effect any such alienation ... shall, in the absence of such consent for all purposes be null and void."

It would appear that when the agreement in that particular form was discussed, i.e., the form that immediate possession should continue in the lessees pending execution of the formal lease, the matter should have been at that stage adjourned and the concurrence of the state council concerned sought and obtained before the agreement was finally drawn up and signed and recorded in the court record book; or else the concurrence of the state council should have been obtained at the earliest opportunity even after the signing of the agreement and recording of it in the court record book. The consent of the state council concerned-the Gomoa Asin State Council, whose secretary gave evidence as second witness for the plaintiffs-was, however, not obtained before the action brought, and it follows from the wording of the Ordinance that the agreement" purporting to effect alienation" cannot be enforced without that consent.

Judgment must therefore be and is hereby given against the plaintiffs rejecting their claim for an order for specific performance and the other reliefs sought.

As to costs, in the view I have taken that the agreement was a concluded agreement, I am satisfied that the conduct of the defendants in repudiating it, when there was the legal obligation on them to co-operate in seeking and procuring the consent of the state council, disentitles them to the costs which they would otherwise be entitled to. Accordingly the judgment in their favour shall be without costs.

Judgment for the defendants.
