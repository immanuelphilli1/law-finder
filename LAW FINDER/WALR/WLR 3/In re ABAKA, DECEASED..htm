                                                        August 27, 1957.                                                      

                                                In re ABAKA, DECEASED.

                                                High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                         Adumua­Bossman J.

 
 

Probate-Wills-Will drawn in English form-Applicability of Wills Act, 1837 - Will not signed" at foot or end thereof" -Admissibility of customary will to probate.

The testator had drawn his will in the English style, using a printed will form the first page of which, opening with the usual introductory directions, ended with provision for the signatures of the testator and his attesting witnesses. These signatures appeared in the appointed place on this page but the testamentary dispositions continued for three further pages and these bore no signatures. On an application for the whole document to be admitted to probate.

Held, (i) two types of will are admissible to probate in Ghana. The first, by reason of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4, s. 17, comprise those wills admissible to probate in England in accordance with the law and practice for the time being in force there: to these wills the provisions of the Wills Act, 1837, and the Wills Act Amendment Act, 1852, apply. The second type, by reason of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4, s. 87 (1), comprise those wills that are recognised as valid testamentary dispositions by native custom.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. This is the view expressed by Hayes-Redwar in "Com­ments on Some Gold Coast Ordinances" and is to be preferred to that of Francis Smith J. in Be Isaac Anarman, deceased (1894) Sarbah, Fa.nti Oustomary Law, 2nd ed., p. 221.]

(ii) The document propounded by the executors on its face purported to be a will drawn in English style to which the provisions of English law were intended to apply: it did not purport to be a customary testamentary disposition. Its validity as a will therefore was governed by the require­ments of the Wills Act, 1837, and the Wills Act Amendment Act, 1852. These, inter alia, require a will to be signed at the foot or end thereof. The only part of the document which complied with these requirements and was therefore valid as a will and could be admitted to probate, was its first page.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. At the time this judgment was delivered there was no authoritative ruling as to all the essential requirements of a valid customary testamentary disposition. The courts might therefore have found themselves in the position of rejecting a document in the style of an English will for want of compliance with the statutory requirements but yet admitting it to probate on the grounds that it complied with the requirements of a customary testa­mentary disposition. This situation should not now arise in view of the decision of Ollennu J. in Summey v. Yohuno and Others, decided in the High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court, on March 29, 1960, and which summarised the requirements of a customary testamentary disposition as follows:

(1) the disposition must be made in the presence of witnesses, who must hear what the declaration is and know its contents;

(2) members of the family of the testator who would have succeeded to him upon his death intestate must be among the witnesses in whose presence the declaration is made; and

(3) there must be acceptance by or on behalf of the beneficiaries, indicated by the giving and receiving of drinks.

The learned judge then continued:

" Native custom knew no writing, but I am of the opinion that in these modern days the declaration of will made in accordance with customary law may be recorded at the moment it is made and signed by the person making it and attested by the witnesses. Such a document could, in my view, if proved, be used as evidence of the making of a customary will. One of the main differences between a will made in accordance with the Wills Act, 1837, and a will made in accordance with customary law is that in the former the attesting witnesses need not know the contents of the will, while in the latter they should know."

It might perhaps be added that the customary disposition-samansiw-in its origin at least had points of similarity to the English donatio mortis causa. ]

Cases referred to:

(1) Ocran v. Banda/oo (1873) Sarbah, Fanti Oustomary Law, 2nd ed:, p. 210.

(2) In re Archer (1871) L.R. 2 P. & D. 252. (3) In re Anstee [1893] P. 283.

APPLICATION on motion by executors for probate. The applicants appeared in person.

ADUMA-BOSSMAN J. This is an application by motion for a grant of probate of the last will and testament of one James Kofi Abaka, deceased, of Cape Coast, by the executors named and appointed under his said will. The instrument is a printed form, and the front or first page contains certain typewritten matter which constitutes clauses 1, 2, and part of clause 3 of the will. This first page bore the date, the signature of the testator at the end or foot of the typewritten matter and the signatures of the two attesting witnesses just below the signature of the testator. Then followed three other pages num­bered 2, 3 and 4 of typewritten matter, constituting by far the most important dispositions of properties of the testator to his wife and children in clauses 3 to 13 inclusive. Unfortunately, not a single one of these subsequent pages, 2, 3 and 4 bore the signature of the testator or the signatures of the attesting witnesses.

It is my painful and unpleasant but bounden duty, in the state of the law, to declare the will defectively executed in so far as the more important pages 2, 3 and 4 are concerned, but duly and properly executed in respect of the relatively unimportant writing on the first page only. Hayes-Redwar J., in his invaluable" Comments" under the heading "Application of Wills Act, 1837, to the Gold Coast" stated as follows:

" This Act has been held by. the Supreme Court of the Gold Coast to apply to all wills, except native nuncupative wills, which are sometimes met with and are governed by local native custom. It has always been assumed that, independently of section 14 (of the Supreme Court Ordinance, now section 15 of the Courts Ordi­nance) this Act is made to apply by the provisions in section 16 (now section 17) that probate matters are to be governed by , the law and practice for the time being in force in England.' Upon a careful perusal however of section 16 (now section 17) it will be seen that the jurisdiction conferred is expressed in such terms as to be merely permissive, and not compulsory, the words used being that' the jurisdiction hereby conferred upon .the Court in Probate Divorce and Matrimonial Causes may ... be exercised by the court in conformity with the law and practice for the time being in force in England.' "

The distinguished author then goes on to observe as follows:

"With regard to this permissive character of the probate jurisdi0tion it is submitted that it is reasonably clear, after a perusal of sections 16 and 19 (now sections 17 and 87 (1)) that the intention of the Legislature must have been to permit the admission to probate of wills of natives not executed with the formalities prescribed in English law."

At the first blush it would seem at this point as though he meant that any document purporting to be a will of a native but "not executed with the formalities prescribed by English law" may be admitted to probate. His further observations however make it quite clear that his statement as to the intention of the Legislature to permit the admission to probate of wills of natives not executed with the formalities prescribed by English law, was intended to refer only to nuncupative wills duly made in accordance with native customary law, concerning which latter form of will he says as follows:

" By section 19 (now s. 87 (1)) it is enacted that nothing in the Ordinance is to deprive any native of the benefits of any native custom not repugnant to natural justice, and especially in certain named matters, including testamentary dispositions: in other words, there is a plain indication of an intention to uphold and give effect to any testamentary dispositions of individual property (not family property) according to native custom, which would not require a will to be in writing or signed by the testator. .. , That verbal wills are, as a fact, made by natives appears to be clear from the statement made by the chiefs, etc., in the case of Ocran v. Bandafoo (I). Bearing in mind the express enactment in section 19, and the fact that the probate jurisdiction in section 16 is expressed to be only permissive, there seems no reason why the court should not, in its discretion, admit to probate. in the manner suggested above, a native nuncupative will, if satisfied of the veracity of the witnesses called to prove it

The true position being thus explained, there can be little or no room for doubt that any document purporting to be a will or other testamentary instrument made by a native, as distinct from the customary oral nuncupative will, must conform to the Wills Act, 1837, as amended by the Wills Act Amendment Act, 1852, which provides that the will shall be " signed at the foot or end thereof." In this connection reference might be made to two cases. The first case is In re Archer (2) in which, according to the headnote:

" A will was written across the second and third sides of a sheet of note-paper, the lower part of such sides being left blank. The attestation clause and signatures of the testator and witnesses were written at the back of the will and therefore across the top of the first and fourth sides of the paper-the testator writing the will in the presence of the witnesses immediately before execut­ing it. Held the will was well executed under 15 & 16 Vict. c. 24 (Wills Act Amendment Act, 1852)."

The second case is In re Anstee (3) in which, according to the headnote:

"The signature of the testator and the attesting witnesses appeared at the bottom of the first page of a will-immediately after an unfinished sentence which was completed overleaf on the second page.

" Held-that Probate might be granted of the first page of the will.

" Per the President (of the Probate Division): I grant probate

of the will down to the bottom of page 1 only."

It is this state of the law which compels me, as already indicated, to declare as I do hereby declare, the writing on the first page only of the document purporting to be the testator's will, which first page bears the testator's signature and the signatures of the attesting witnesses and the attestation clause, to have been duly and properly executed, and therefore probate thereof, i.e., the writing on .the first page only, is hereby granted.

As to the writing on the second, third and fourth pages of the said document, which do not bear the signatures of the testator and his witnesses and any attestation clause, I hereby declare the same to be defectively and improperly executed, and therefore probate thereof must be and is hereby refused. It is a matter of some degree of satis­faction that, in the circumstance of the testator's intestacy resulting from the defective execution of this will, his successors according to native customary law entitled on his intestacy have concluded a fair and binding agreement according to native customary law for an equitable distribution of the estate with the widow and children who had been made beneficiaries under the defective will, and the agree­ment bearing the signatures of the parties has been filed in this matter and is hereby made by me an appendix to this ruling.

 

In conclusion, I would venture to express the hope ~hat my ruling in this unfortunate matter, may be some sort of warning to many who seem to scorn the advice of trained legal men in the conduct and management of their important affairs, in respect of which legal direction and guidance seem absolutely essential.

Application granted but limited to first page of propounded document.

S. G. D.
