Native Law and Custom-Land-----Demarcation of boundaries-Necessity for adjoining landowners to act in conjunction.

       The plaintiff complained that the defendant had trespassed upon his land and cut down certain valuable trees. He sought damages for trespass and a declaration of title. The defendant admitted the plaintiff's title to the land upon which the latter was farming and he admitted further that he had felled the trees: he contended that he farmed land contiguous to that of the plaintiff, that the boundary between the two farms was undefined and, that it was while he was demarcating the boundary that

the trees had been destroyed. The true issue was thus as to the owner­ship, not of the plaintiff's farm but, of the area of land in the immediate vicinity of the alleged boundary. The plaintiff however, instead of relying on his possession as providing sufficient title to defeat a trespasser, put into issue his title to the whole of his land. In the Native Court he succeeded, the court taking into consideration things they had observed on a visit to the locus, but on appeal to the Land Court this decision was reversed, the learned judge taking the view that the plaintiff had failed to establish his title to the land and also that the trial Native Court had given undue weight to their own observations on the visit, as opposed to the evidence that had been adduced before them.

Held, (i) where, on the pleadings, from the openings or from the general nature of a case involving land, it is apparent that title is not in fact put in issue, a plaintiff need not prove his title. Thus, in an action the true nature of which is a claim for trespass to land, it is sufficient for the plaintiff to establish possession.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Much of the litigation reaching the courts concerns title to land, and in such litigation it is common form for one or both parties to ask for a declaration of title. The consequences of failing to do so can be costly, witness Yiboe v. Duedu, Court of Appeal, November 4, 1957, un­reported (reported in the Land Court at 2 W.A.L.R. 293). Here a person successfully defended against a claim for a declaration of title up to the 'West African Court of Appeal but did not obtain a declaration of his own title, although he had established it by evidence, because he had not asked for it in his pleadings. Subsequently he sought to correct his omission by suing himself for a declaration. This should have been a purely formal proceeding with the defendant estopped per rem judicatem from denying the plaintiff's title, but in fact the plaintiff was forced to go a second time to the Court of Appeal before he succeeded. The courts are well alive to the desirability of achieving certainty where land disputes are concerned and encourage claims to be cast in a form that includes a request for a declaration of title. Thus they have repeatedly stressed, particularly with claims that originate in Native Courts, that they look to the content rather than the form of the complaint and, following upon this, have said frequently that the real effect of the lines taken by the parties to a dispute may be that of putting title into issue, even if such effect is not at first apparent, and in such cases the litigant must prove his title or fail. Thus van Lare Ag.C .. J. in Yiboe v. Duedu (above): "Surely a claim founded in trespass must involve the question of ownership in the plaintiff if the defendant puts up and claims absolute ownership of the land." This normally works satisfactorily, both in cases where two persons, relying on competing and conflicting grants, lay claim to one piece of land and also in cases where adjoining landowners are in dispute as to the location of their boundaries. But there are cases where the tendency to regard a claim to ownership by one party as putting the ownership of the other in issue can create difficulties, and the present case would appear to be such a one. Here the plaintiff claimed damages for' trespass and in addition appeared to put his title to the whole of his land in issue by asking for a declaration. In truth the dispute was as to the whereabouts of a boundary; the defendant admitted the plaintiff's right to the general mass of his land. Because the plaintiff had, unnecessarily it is suggested, put the title to the whole of his land in issue, he failed in the Land Court, for he was either unable to or neglected to establish that title. The Court of Appeal was able to avoid this conclusion by pointing out that the defendant had admitted the plaintiff's right to his farm. In reality, however, this did not resolve the dilemma. What the defendant in fact was doing was admitting the plaintiff's right to land upon which he, the defendant, had not ventured, but setting up a claim of right of his own to those boundary areas on which he had ventured. And by an application of the principles generally applied by the courts, this put the plaintiff to proof of his title to those particular areas. Since the plaintiff's title was one and indivisible he could only answer by attempting to prove his title to the whole of his land, and this he failed to do, at least to the satisfaction of the Land Court. This result would not have been reached if the courts were more ready to take the view that a claim of absolute ownership by one party does not automatically put the other to proof of his title. It does not do so where that other person is in possession. Thus, Halsbury, 2nd ed., vol. 27, p. 807: "a party must prove that he is entitled to recover the land as against the person in possession. He recovers on the strength of his own title, not on the weakness of his opponent. Possession in itself is a good title as against everyone except the true owner (Asher v. Whitlock (1863) L.R. 1 Q.B. 1; Perry v. Clissold [1907] A.C. 73, P.C.) and if one who has been in possession has been wrongfully dispossessed, he is entitled to recover possession against the wrongdoer, notwithstanding that the true title may be shewn to be in a third party." Moreover, of course, possession is sufficient to found a claim in trespass, see the remarks of van Lare J. in Oyete and Ntim v. Edurnawn and Adno (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 278. It is suggested, there­fore, that the true situation in this case was that the plaintiff sought damages for trespass and was met with a claim to the ownership of the trespass area. If the defendant failed to establish his own title to these areas (and he did so fail) and if the plaintiff demonstrated his possession (as he did) he was entitled to succeed. This, therefore is a case, contrary to the general pattern, where although the form of the claim was that of a declaration of title, the true content of the claim was otherwise.

On the points as to procedure by a Native Court when visiting a locus in quo, see also Boakye v. Baabn (1956) 2 W.A.L.R. 183 and Ketekn v. Dzogbenuku (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 134.]

(ii) ,Although a Native Court should not allow their impressions, gathered on a visit to the locus in quo in a dispute concerning land, to become a substitute for the evidence they have heard in court, yet this is not to say that they should 110t attach to those impressions whatever weight they think appropriate in the circumstances of the case. Where plans are not tendered in evidence, a visit to the locus in quo will frequently form an effective and acceptable substitute for such plans and in these circumstances the impressions gathered by the members of the Native Court can be treated on the same lines as the impressions gathered by them from the demeanour of witnesses in court.

(iii) It is a principle of native custom that where a boundary is to be demarcated, this is to be done by the owners of the bounded areas acting in conjunction with each other. A unilateral attempt to demarcate by one owner is ineffective.

Cases referred to:

(1) Dza v. Komia (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 145.

(2) A rnponu and Others v. Badoo and Othen, West African Court of Appeal, February 28, 1949, unreported.

(3) Sampson v. Ocquaye, West African Court of Appeal, May 28, 1932, unreported.

APPEAL from a decision of van Lare J. on March 19, 1957, in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, reversing a decision of the Akyem Abuakwa Native Court " A" given on October 18, 1956, in favour of the plaintiff in an action for trespass to land.

Asafu-Adjaye for the plaintiff. Dua-Sekyi for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. This is an appeal from a judgment of van Lare J., as he then was, sitting in the Land Court, Accra, reversing a judgment of the Akyem Abuakwa Native Court" A "Asamankese. The plaintiff sued as successor to and representative of the family of his late uncle, Kofi Donkor, and the claim was for a declaration of title and damages for trespass to certain lands with farms thereon. The cause of action was the entry by the defendant upon that land and the destruction of a large number of economic trees. The land in dispute was fully described in the writ of summons. In proof of his case the plaintiff and his witnesses gave evidence that the land as described in the writ was cultivated by his uncle, the late Kofi Donkor, that he succeeded to Kofi Donkor and, that about two months before the issue of the writ, the defendant entered upon a portion of the land and cut a line through it, destroying a quantity of cocoa, cola and other valuable trees thereon.

The defendant in his evidence admitted that the late Kofi Donkor owned the land and that the plaintiff had succeeded to him and was the present owner of the land. He also admitted felling the large number of economic trees. But his defence was that his own farm forms a boundary with the plaintiff's land and that although the boundary between the two lands is known, yet there are no fixtures or recognised boundary trees indicating it and so he cut the bush along that boundary to make it clear and it was in the process of such demarcation of the boundary that he destroyed the trees in question. Inferentially, he means that the trees destroyed were on the boundary between their two lands.

The plaintiff and his witnesses, one of whom was a close relation of the defendant, denied that the defendant has a farm which forms a boundary with the land in dispute. And with one exception, each of the five witnesses called by the defendant denied that the farms of the defendant have a boundary with the plaintiff's farm.

Upon that evidence the issues which the Native Court had to determine were: whether the plaintiff's farm forms a boundary with any farm of the defendant and, if the answer to this is in the affirmative, what the situation of that boundary is; in other words does the plaintiff's land cover the area where the trees were felled? After the conclusion of the oral evidence and before delivering judg­ment the Native Court inspected the area in dispute in the presence of the parties. Their judgment, which is fairly short but well written, is in two parts. In the first part they reviewed the oral evidence before them and came to clear and definite findings of fact thereon. They accepted the evidence led by the plaintiff and his witnesses, but found the defendant and his witnesses to be untruthful and rejected their evidence. In the second part they commented upon what they themselves had observed and on the things shown to them by the parties and on incidents which happened at the inspection of the land, stating that while the plaintiff was able to point out his land with noticeable signs of his occupation and with recognised features clearly marking out the boundaries, the defendant on the other hand was unable to show them any signs of his occupation of any land ill the vicinity which would indicate that he has any boundary with the plaintiff, and that when he found himself in difficulties he tried to cut a line through a very thick farmstead which he alleged to be the boundary. The Native Court, in this second part of their judgment, also came to the conclusion that they ought to believe the plaintiff and not the defendant. They therefore entered judgment for the plaintiff on his claim. Their judgment was set aside by the Land Court on two grounds, namely: because the plaintiff failed to prove the origin or root of his title and, because the Native Court based part of their findings upon facts which they collected at the inspection without having had those facts recorded as evidence in the proceedings.

In my opinion the learned judge of the Land Court misdirected himself on the standard of proof required in this case. It is not necessary in every case of a claim for declaration of title that a plaintiff should prove his root of title before he can succeed. Where, upon the pleadings. openings, or from the general nature of the case, title in the strict sense is not put in issue, a plaintiff need not prove his root of title. It is only where there is specific or general denial of his title that a plaintiff cannot succeed without proving his root of title.

As pointed out above, the defendant admitted the original owner­ship of the land in dispute by Kofi Donkor, and the plaintiff's ownership as rightful successor to the said Kofi Donkor. What he disputed was the extent of the plaintiff's land and thus raised the two issues set out above.

To establish the first issue, namely, whether or not the defendant owns land which bounds the land in dispute, proof of root of title is superfluous in the circumstances of this case. And to establish the second issue, namely, the boundary, if any, between the plaintiff's land and any adjoining land which may be owned by the defendant, proof of the plaintiff's root of title is unnecessary. All that is needed is evidence of eye-witnesses who can testify to the extent to which Kofi Donkor, the plaintiff's predecessor, as a subject of the Asamankese Stool, cultivated the forest land.

The learned judge cited a passage from the judgment in the case of Dza v. KomIa (1) to support the second ground upon which he allowed the appeal. With respect I do not see how that passage supports that ground. The ratio decidendi in that passage is that in deciding land cases a court should not attach too much weight to impressions gained on viewing the land, to the detriment of the weight which they should attach to the d~meanour of witnesses when giving evidence on oath and to the oral evidence given. In this case, the trial Native Court directed themselves properly in conformity with the passage quoted from the judgment of Dza v. KomIa (1). They first dealt with the oral evidence and the credibility of the witnesses and came to a decision in favour of the plaintiff. After indicating what their judgment upon the oral evidence would be, they continued as follows: "This court, still feeling to give the defendant a chance, moved on \Vednesday, October 17, 1956, with parties and inspected the land in dispute." That passage from the judgment of the trial Native Court shows that the Native Court treated the inspection as a subsidiary matter which they indulged in for the accommodation of the defendant in the hope that it might yield something in his favour. Under the Native Court (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 23, the procedure in a Native Court must be in accordance with native custom except as regards any particular procedure which is expressly laid down by the Ordinance or regulations made thereunder. Viewing of land in land cases is peculiarly within native customary procedure. It serves in the Native Court tbe purpose which a plan serves in the Supreme Court. Native custom knew no writing and therefore by native custom no record would be made of the thing found at inspection of the land. The impression a Native Court forms at such inspection from things they see, or what the parties say to or show them at an inspection of land in dispute, can be treated on the same lines as the impressions they form from the demeanour of witnesses; and as they are entitled to act upon impressions in observing the demeanour of witnesses without recording the facts upon which they formed those impressions, so in my opinion they are entitled to act upon facts they collect at an inspection of land without first having those facts recorded as evidence on the record. It stands to reason that if any of the facts stated by the Native Court in their judgment as having occurred at the inspection were not correct, the party against whom those untrue or inaccurate facts were used would readily bring the mistakes to the notice of the Appeal Court.

I have carefully scrutinised the argument of learned counsel for the defendant before the Land Court, but failed to discover a challenge of any sort to any of the facts stated in the judgment of the trial Native Court as having occurred or been found at the inspection. The only complaint I could discover is that there is no record which shows that the trial Native Court did in fact inspect the area, in other words, a report of the inspection is not incorporated in the record.

In my opinion a record of what took place at the inspection is desirable but certainly its omission is not fatal to a judgment of the Native Court. This same point was raised and forcefully argued before the \Vest African Court of Appeal in Ampong and Others v. Badoo and Others (2) and Lewey J.A. in the course of his judgment in that case said:

" I desire, however, to refer in more detail to the first of the grounds of appeal, which is based on the criticism that there is nothing on the record to show that any inspection had been made of the area in dispute. Mr. Abbensetts, who appeared for the appellants, devoted a considerable part of his argument to this aspect of the case, and went on to argue that it was a fatal defect that the proceedings did not include the report of any inspection carried out by the Native Court.

I am unable to accept that contention. There is a clear indication on the record that members of the Native Court did inspect the locus and that they gave detailed attention to the disputed boundaries and carefully considered, in that connection, the contents of the document referred to as Exhibit' E.' In my view, therefore, it cannot be said, in this case, that there is any serious defect in the proceedings on the record of the Native Court in relation to their inspection of the area concerned. It may be thought desirable in certain cases that a record of the result of an inspection should be available, but it seems to me to be going much too far to say that it is necessary in every case, or that the absence of such a record is a fatal defect. No legislation or authority has been cited to us to establish Mr. Abbensetts's contention, nor have we anything before us to show that there are any Native Court procedural regulations dealing with the point.

We were referred, on behalf of the appellants, to the case of Sampson v. Oequaye (3), which is not reported but which was decided by this court on May 28, 1932. An examination of the recorded judgments in that case does not, however, support the argument that it is an authority for the proposition that it is a necessary part of the procedure of Native Courts that the details of an inspection of a site by the court must always be recorded as evidence. Sampson v. Oequaye (3) was an appeal from the judgment of a Provincial Commissioner, and the only relevant reference occurs in the judgment of Macquarrie J., and is no more than an obiter dictum by the judge as to the desirability, in his view, of recording as a matter of evidence the results of an inspection of a site with the parties. "

And Blackall P. also said:

"With great respect I do not agree with the obiter of Macquarrie J., in Sampson v. Ocquaye (3). A view of the locus in quo by judge and jury is a common feature of trial by jury but it is not the practice to record anything more than the fact that there had been a view. In the absence of any native law and custom to the contrary I see no reason therefore why Native Courts should be required to go further in this regard than the Supreme Court."

In my view neither of the two grounds upon which the learned judge set aside the judgment of the trial Native Court can be sustained.

I will observe that by native law and custom it is wrong for an owner of land by himself alone to demarcate a boundary between his land and adjoining land without the owner of the adjoining land acting together with him. So that even if it had been proved that the defen­dant had land which formed a boundary with the plaintiff's land, his unilateral act of demarcating the boundary would be wrongful all the same.

For these reasons I would allow the appeal.

GRANVILLE SHARP J.A. As we are differing from the learned judge in the Land Court, as he then was, I would like out of respect for his Lordship to add a few observations to the judgment that has just been read, and with which I agree.

It seems to me that the learned judge was in error in that he found himself persuaded to accept an argument, urged before him no doubt as it was before us by Mr. Dua-Sekyi with great force and persuasion, which ignored certain important parts of the evidence and other significant features of the case and, in addition placed rather too much emphasis on the treatment by the Native Trial Court of the results of their viewing of the disputed land.

The argument proceeded upon the initial thesis that it was essential in such a case as the present that the plaintiff should affirmatively establish root of title. I cannot from my understanding of the matter see why this should be so. The plaintiff claimed the land as the successor to his deceased grand-uncle and it is noteworthy that when the defendant gave evidence he made the following statement: "I have a farm boundary with the farm of the plaintiff as has been shown on his writ by his appointment as the successor of Rofi Donkor." The farm of the late Rofi Donkor was identified, it is true in very general terms, by the plaintiff's third witness who said that his own uncle, Kwamena Aboagye, had a farm at Abodwoye which formed a boundary with the farm in dispute which belonged to Kofi Donkor and that his uncle had shown him the boundaries of his farm of which he, the witness, was at the time of the hearing in occupation. The plaintiff's second witness said that the defendant's father owned a farm near the farm in dispute. I think therefore that, taken with this evidence, the defendant's admission provided sufficient evidence of the plaintiff's inheritance and that it was immaterial for him to go further into past history or ancestry in order to prove root of title. As inheritor he was entitled to protection against invasion of or trespass upon the land, even without proof that he had in the physical sense been placed in possession of his inheritance. Mr. Dua-Sekyi made a forceful complaint that the procedure of the Native Trial Court in regard to their viewing of the land led them to place an emphasis upon their findings there which was not justified by the oral evidence and which amounted to the members of the court constituting themselves as witnesses as to matters not deposed to on oath by other witnesses. I agree that the procedure did not accord with the counsels of perfection that have been indicated in other cases but I cannot see that it was so irregular as to justify disturbing the judgment of the Native Trial Court. Both parties were present throughout the view, and each had an opportunity of assisting the court in informing itself as to the facts existing on the land in dispute as they affected boundaries. Furthermore, as I understand it, the defendant had opportunity even in the Land Court to object to the findings by application for a survey supported by an affidavit stating the grounds of his objection. I think in the circumstances the use made by the Native Trial Court of their findings is not open to serious objection.

For these reasons and for the reasons given by Ollennu J. I also would allow this appeal.

SMITH J. I agree.

Appealed allowed.

S. G. D.
