                                                          April 22, 1958.

                                                          FREMPONG v. EFFA

                                                High Court, Central Judicial Division, Land Court  

                                                                      Adumua-Bossman J.)

.

 

Land-Estoppel-Claim for declaration of title to land-Failure of successful defendant to counterclaim-Whether unsuccessful plaintiff estopped per rem judicatem from asserting ownership in a subseq1lent action.

Land-Native law and custom-Claim for declaration of title to land- Neces­sity for occupants of land to attorn tenant to declared owner-Rights of declared owners in event of occupants failing to attorn tenant.

In 1949 or thereabouts the Ohene of Aperade, representing the Stool of Aperade, sued the Ohene of Tarkwa, as representing the Stool of Tarkwa, in respect of an area of land within which were included some cocoa farms cultivated by the lVIankralo of Aperade (Kwaku Effa). The Tarkwa Stool resisted the claim of the Aperade Stool on the ground that ownership of the area was in the Tarkwa Stool, but there was no formal counterclaim for a declaration of ownership on behalf of the Tarkwa Stool. The suit was tried in the Land Court, Cape Coast, where the Mankralo of Aperade, who represented his superior chief and the Aperade Stool, gave evidence in support of the Aperade Stool's claim to ownership of the area in dispute. The Land Court gave judgment for the Aperade Stool.

On appeal to the West African Court of Appeal the decision of the Land Court in favour of the Aperade Stool was set aside, on the ground that that stool had not sufficiently discharged the onus of establishing its ownership of the land in dispute. A judgment of nonsuit was substituted against the Aperade Stool, in favour of the Tarkwa Stool, and this judg­ment was confirmed by a subsequent decision of the Privy Council.

On October 9, 1956, the Tarkwahene of Achiase (Barima Darko Frem­pong II) issued a writ against Kwaku Effa in the South Birim Native Court" A." The plaintiff claimed recovery of possession of certain of the lands in question which were (he alleged) a part of those covered by the Privy Council decision, and which were occupied by Effa as a farmer at Aperade; together with damages for trespass, and mesne profits. He alleged that he had by letter given the defendant opportunity to attorn tenant to him, but that the defendant had failed to do so.

The Ohene of Aperade and others on the Aperade side applied un­successfully to be joined as co-defendants. The Ohene's case was that the land in dispute was a part of his stool land, and that the defendant was his subject, with the customary right to farm on the stool lands.

The Native Court gave judgment for the plaintiff. On appeal:

Held, (i) where judgment is entered against a plaintiff who has failed to establish his title, in an action for a declaration of title to land, the plaintiff and his privies will subsequently be estopped per rem judicatem from asserting ownership to the land in a subsequent action brought by a successful defendant (or by those in privity with him) who, in the original action, failed to counterclaim for a declaration in his own favour.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Although an estoppel is thereby raised against the former plaintiff it remains the case that the defendant who, having failed to counterclaim, commences an action himself, can succeed only on the strength of his own case: he is not entitled to succeed simply on the grounds that the former plaintiff is estopped from contesting the claim.]

(ii) It is a principle of custom that persons in possession of land which has been the subject of a claim for a declaration of title should attorn tenant to the stool, family, or person in whose favour the declara­tion of title was made. In the event of failure to attorn the declared owners of the land are entitled to enforce their right against such possessors.

Cases referred to:

(1) Kwesi and Another v. Kwadjo and Another, West African Court of A ppeal, February 22, 1944, unreported.

(2) Kwajo and Another v. Kwesi and Another, West African Court of Appeal, February 17, 1947, unreported.

(3) Ababio v. Nsemfoo (1947) 12 W.A.C.A. 127.

ApPEAL from a decision on October 3, 1957, of the South Birim Native Court" A," entering judgment for the plaintiff for £50 damages, ordering the defendant forthwith to enter into an agreement with the plaintiff, and granting the plaintiff an interim injunction against further trespass by the defendant pending such agreement.

Sekyi for appellant. Benjamin for respondent.

ADUMUA-BosSMAN J. outlined the facts and continued: As was explained by Kingdon C.J. (Nigeria) in the unreported decision of the West African Court of Appeal, Kwesi and Another v. Kwadjo and Another (1), in a similar case where, though the defendant had not counterclaimed, the trial Magistrate nevertheless made a declaration in his favour:

" It is, however, most unfortunate that although the case was brought by agreement in order that a decision might be given regarding the divisional boundary, the suit took the form of a claim for ownership by the plaintiff only. It is well established that when that is the case, a declaration of ownership and posses­sion cannot be made in favour of the defendant, since there is no claim by him before the court .. " In such cases the proper course is merely to dismiss the plaintiff's claim. This, of course, does not mean that the matter is any the less res judicata in favour of the defendant. "

The defendant in that case subsequently claimed (as plaintiff in a substantive writ) a declaration of ownership, and other reliefs such as damages and injunction. He pleaded the former judgment to bar the now defendant (the former plaintiff) from traversing the finding in the former suit that he (the now defendant) had no ownership of the land the subject-matter of the suit. The trial court held that the matter was res judicata, and gave judgment for the then plaintiff.

Upon the matter going on appeal to the West African Court of Appeal, Kingdon C.J. (Nigeria) referred to the earlier appeal, and said:

"It is clear that the learned judges in that case were en­deavouring to make it clear that although a declaration of owner­ship and possession could not be given in the particular case before the court, because of the omission on the part of counsel for the defendant to enter a counterclaim to that effect, neverthe­less the judgment would be a bar to any further proceedings be­tween the parties. The Magistrate at the end of the instant case decided quite correctly that the matter was res judicata" (Kwajo and Another v. Kwesi and Another (2)).

In the instant case it is clear, therefore, .that the judgment of the West African Court of Appeal nonsuiting the Aperade Stool in respect of its claim to ownership of the land in dispute (a decision which was subsequently confirmed by the Privy Council), was binding and con­clusive against the stool and all its subjects, precluding them forever from asserting ownership to any portion of the area that formed the subject-matter of the litigation.

\Vhat, in such circumstances, is the customary practice? The recognised customary practice is for all farmers who have made farms or villages on land in the belief that it is stool land of the stool to which they are subject, and as a result of litigation or otherwise it is adjudi­cated that the land belongs to a stool other than the stool in whose right they purported to enter and occupy, to lose no time in entering into terms of occupation with the stool adjudged to be the owner of the land. That is quite clearly laid down in Ababio v. Nsemfoo (3), where the following statement of native customary law, as enunciated by the Asantehene's Court " A," was approved and confirmed by ~he West African Court of Appeal:

" According to native customary law the only remedy of the defendant (who had been declared owner of the land) was to notify the farmer-tenants on the land to come before him for tenancy agreements and if they failed to appear or attend before him, he could sue them in a court of competent jurisdiction to show cause why they farmed on his stool land and refused to enter into a tenancy agreement with him."

That customary practice is not restricted to Ashanti; it prevails also in Southern Ghana. The plaintiff has pursued the proper procedure sanc­tioned by the decided cases, and the judgment of the Native Trial Court is unimpeachable.

Appeal dismissed.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The unsuccessful defendant took a second appeal to the Court of Appeal of Ghana which allowed the appeal on May 17, 1960, and remitted the case to the Land Court for rehearing. The Court of Appeal said:

"When this appeal came before the court on April 25 we observed that although the plaintiff relied on a plan tendered in evidence in a previous case between plaintiff-respondent and the landlord or overlord of the defendant-appellant herein, the plan was not tendered in evidence in those proceedings. It is observed, however, that the judgment appealed from is based upon an assumpbon that the area the subject-matter of the dispute is within the area in respect of which the defendant-appel­lant's overlord had been nonsuited in the previous case. There is no satisfactory evidence on record to support this assumption, which could not justly be made without the production of the plan which the plaintiff relied upon. For these reasons we allow this appeal, and remit the case to the Land Court for rehearing, and we direct that the plan upon which the claim is based be produced, and evidence of the alleged trespass be taken with respect thereof."]
