                                                         April 15, 1957

                                          ADU  v. NKANSAH AND OTHERS

                                                       High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                               van Lare J.

 

 

-Estoppel- Estoppel by conduct-Grantee of land supported by grantor before an official inquiry-Subsequent dispute between grantor and grantee concerning grant.

Native Courts-Practice-Powers of Native Court-Absence of power to make order for partition of land-Absence of power to make an order not sought by parties.

 The plaintiff, suing as representative of a land "company," sought an order for possession of land which was being worked by subjects of the first defendants' stool. In 1926 there had been a Forest Reserve Inquiry to ascertain, for compensation purposes, the owners of an area of land which included the area in dispute and, at this inquiry the defendants' stool had, as the plaintiffs' grantors, supported the plaintiffs' claim for compensation.

The trial Native Court had purported to make an order for partition of the land.

Held, (i) the defendants, by holding themselves out as the plaintiffs' grantors and supporting the latter's claim to ownership made before the 1926 inquiry, were by their conduct, now estopped from denying the plaintiffs' title to the land.

(ii) A Native Court has, save with the consent of the parties, no power to make an order for the partition of land nor, indeed, any other order not sought by a party in his claim.

APPEAL from a decision of the Kwahu State, Grade " A " Native Court, on February 7, 1956, ordering, inter alia, partition of an area of land for which an order for possession was sought by the plaintiff.

Koi-Larbi for the plaintiff.

B entsi Enchill for the defendants.

VAN LARE J. This is a simple case in which the plaintiffs claim damages for trespass in respect of an area of land which the defendants and co-defendants on behalf of the Nteso Stool caused their subjects to till in defiance of plaintiffs' right of possession. It is quite clear on the evidence that the plaintiff " company" bought a large tract of forest land from the Nteso Stool, and before they, the plaintiffs, could begin farming thereon the Forestry Department acquired the area, including the area the subject-matter of this suit. During the inquiry by the Forest Reserve Commissioner in the year 1926 the Nteso Stool sup­ported the plaintiffs as alienation claimants in respect of their claim to land, including the area concerned in this case. The plaintiff " company" were adjudged to De entitled as alienation claimants-as they claimed through the N£eso Stool-to receive compensation as owners; and they received the compensation due to them. Now that portions of the forest reserve have been released at the instance of the plaintiff " company" the }'Tteso Stool permitted their subjects to till portions of the released area. There is no doubt that the area now being tilled by the subjects of the Nteso Stool is a portion of the larger area which the Nteso Stool had long ago sold to the plaintiff " company" and in respect of which the Nteso Stool supported the plaintiffs as alienation claimants during the Forest Reserve Inquiry in 1926.

The Nteso Stool now says that the area sold to the plaintiff " company" could not have extended to include 2,195 acres as found and confirmed by the Reserve Settlement Commissioner. The Nteso Stool is now trying to say that what they sold measured" 62t ropes by 62t ropes." What this acreage means in native calculations is not clear; but what 2,195 acres means is definitely certain for record purposes. In my view it is now too late for the Nteso Stool to be heard to say that not all the area included in what was adjudged to have been sold by the stool of the plaintiff ., company" was in fact sold, nor 'that the trespass area could not have been included in what was sold to the plaintiff" company." Once the Nteso Stool supported the plaintiff "company" as alienation claimants in respect of the portion of Nteso Stool land in the Forest Reserve Inquiry, and as the trial Native Court finds as a fact the trespass area to be a portion of the Forest Reserve released to the plaintiff "company," the Nteso Stool is estopped from disputing the plaintiffs' title to the area in dispute and there must therefore be an adjudication in favour of the plaintiffs for damages for trespass.

The trial Native Court was, as conceded by counsel for the respon­dents, bound to enter judgment upon the trial of the issues and was wrong in saying that it could not give judgment for either party and in dismissing the case to the detriment of the plaintiffs.

In the exercise of my powers, therefore, I would enter judgment for the plaintiffs, the party entitled to judgment both on the facts and law.

 I therefore set aside the order of the trial Native Court dismissing the plaintiffs' case, and I also set aside the order decreeing the disputed land to be shared equally between the parties; a Native Court has no jurisdiction to make a partition order save by consent, in an action affecting title to land, nor to decree that a party should do any act !which is not a relief sought by a claim before the court. The order decreeing the plaintiffs to attend the State Council of Kwahu to have their purchase deeds signed by the President is therefore ultra vires and is hereby set aside.

 In the result there will be judgment for the plaintiffs for damages for trespass. The plaintiffs are also entitled to recovery of possession.

 

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
