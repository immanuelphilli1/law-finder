                                                              TAWIAH v. GYAMPO.

                              High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                               Ollennu J.

                                                   

 

 

Native Custom-Stool land Interest of stool subject in stool land Right of subject to maintain his action against the stool.

Native Custom-Stool land ~Stool subject in occupation and possession Presumption as to title.

The plaintiff, a stool subject, sought as against the stool a declaration of his title to stool land that had been in his possession. He also sought damages for trespass and the recovery of possession. His case was that as a stool subject he was entitled to occupy an area of stool land and that in any case, apart from his own basic rights as a stool subject, the land concerned had been granted by the stool to his grandfather and on the laiter's death had become the property of his family. The defendant stool claimed that the land concerned had not been first cultivated by the plaintiff but by an "abusa" tenant of the stool, so that the plaintiff was not entitled to claim it as of right. Further, that the plaintiff's rights were limited to land that had been granted to his grandfather and that the land in dispute was beyond the limits of this land. On the evidence as to who had first cultivated the land the Native Court found as a fact that it had been the plaintiff. His evidence, which was accepted, showed that no specific limits had been put upon the area granted to his grand­father: the latter had been given a boundary point and shown the direction in which he was entitled to extend his cultivation. From this, and in the absence of evidence contra, there was nothing to show that the plaintiff had cultivated beyond his gra.ndfather's limits.

Held, (i) the plaintiff had cultivated the land and had been in occupa­tion of it until dispossessed. The presumption tnerefore was that he held the land as of right, which right was that, as a stool subject in occupation of stool land, he held it for a determinable estate. There was no evidence to rebut this presumption.

(ii) One who holds land of a stool for a determinable estate is entitled to maintain his action against the stool for the protection of his estate.

ApPEAL from a decision of the Buem-I\:rachi Native Court" A " on June 13, 1957, in favour of the defendant in an action by a stool subject against his stool for a declaration of his title to an area of stool land.

Amorin for the plaintiff.

Opoku Acheampong for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. This is an appeal from a judgment of the Buem-Krachi Native Court" A," Jasikan, delivered on June 13, 1957, in favour of the defendant. The claim is for a declaration of ownership of, damages for trespass to, and recovery of possession of a piece of land with a cocoa farm thereon. The Native Court properly found upon the proceedings that the declaration sought was not as to the absolute ownership but as to the usufruct of a portion of Dapa Stool land which the plaintiff had farmed.

The plaintiff admitted that the land was the property of the Dapa Stool, of which the defendant is the occupant. His complaint was that the defendant had wrongly seized the farm which he made in 1941 on the stool land and was depriving him of the enjoyment of the fruits of the said farm. He maintained that he was a subject of the Dapa Stool and is entitled by custom to occupy a portion of the stool land. He contended further that in any event the area he farmed was a portion of land originally farmed by his grandfather, a subject of the stool, and is therefore, by custom, the property of his family which he as member of the family was entitled to occupy.

The defence was that the particular farm in dispute was not made by the plaintiff but by an abusa tenant of the defendant; and, in the alternative, that the plaintiff's right to occupy stool land was limited to an area formally granted to his grandfather, and that the area he now claimed was outside the limits of the land so granted.

The plaintiff's evidence that he was a subject of the stool was not seriously contested. In fact the defendant admitted that he was not a stranger to the stool. On the question of the cultivation of the farms, the Native Court found, as shown in the last paragraph of their judg­ment, that it was the plaintiff who had cultivated them.

The evidence given by the defendant to show the area granted to the plaintiff's grandfather was that he was shown a spot from,. and the direction towards, which he could farm. By custom a grant made in this form confers upon the grantee and his family usufructuary title in so much of the land as they are able to cultivate; the usual description of that area is: "as far as his cutlass or hoe could reach or until he comes into an area farmed by another subject or grantee of the stooL" There is no evidence to show that the area in dispute in this case is outside any particularly defined limits granted to the plaintiff's grandfather. The presumption therefore is that it is in the direction in which the plaintiff's family is entitled to farm.

All the title that a subject acquires in stool land cultivated by him is the usufructuary title. He is entitled to maintain an action even against the stool in which the absolute ownership is vested for a declara­tion of that title in stool land, and for damages for interference with his possession and occupation of that portion of the stool land over which he has acquired such usufruct. As pointed out above, the Native Court properly directed themselves on the exact nature of the claim; there­fore having found, as they did, that the farm was cultivated by the plaintiff, they should have granted him the reliefs he sought since his evidence of the defendant's interference with his rights was not refuted. Unfortunately, as it appears, the Native Court were misled by the defendant's claim to .the absolute ownership of the land by his stool, into thinking that the person in whom the absolute ownership s vested is entitled to interfere with the possession and occupation of the usufructuary owner. In this they erred. Native custom protects the usufructuary title of the subject and will not permit interference with that right by the absolute owner. In the same way it protects the title of the absolute owner and will not allow the usufructuary owner to dispose of his rights in the land to the detriment of the title of the absolute owner.

Since the judgment proceeded upon wrong principles it cannot be allowed to stand. The appeal is therefore allowed and the judgment of the Native Court set aside.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
