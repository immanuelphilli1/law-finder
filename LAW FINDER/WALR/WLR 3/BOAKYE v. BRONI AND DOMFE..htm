                                                    November 20, 1958.

                                               BOAKYE v. BRONI AND DOMFE.                                                      

                                  High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                            Ollennu J.

 

 

Land-Conveyancing-Native law and custom-Necessity for publicity to be given to a transfer of land-Nature of publicity.

Land-Gift-Irrevocable nature of gift once transfer is completed and donee is in possession.

The plaintiff, as successor to his late brother, sought a declaration of title to a cocoa farm which, he claimed, had been created by and was the self-acquired property of his late brother. The first defendant, the stepfather of the plaintiff, claimed that he, the defendant, had brought the farm under cultivation; that he had made a gift of it to the plaintiff's late brother; that at the time the gift was made the usual attendant ceremonies had not been performed; and that he therefore was entitled to revoke the gift. The trial Native Court found that the defendant had brought the farm under cultivation, that he had given it to the plaintiff's late brother and, in the circumstances, that he was entitled to revoke the gift.

Held, (i) although there was evidence which pointed to the farm having been brought under cultivation by the plaintiff's late brother, the finding of the trial Native Court was a finding of fact for which there was some evidence in support, and which the court therefore could not interfere with.                                                                      '

(ii) Although publicity is essential to an alienation of land by customary methods, this is not confined to meaning that there must be a formal transfer before witnesses. Any act done or steps taken by or conduct of the transferor which manifests to persons, other than the parties to the transaction, an intention to transfer, or which amounts to an assertion to such other persons that property in the land has passed, will comprise publicity sufficient to validate the transaction.

(iii) A gift of land, once the gift is completed and the donee has been placed in possession, is irrevocable.

(iv) On all the evidence, the defendant had openly acknowledged the plaintiff's late brother, and on his death, his family, as the owners of the farm, so that, even if there had been a want of form on the original transfer, this had subsequently been cured.

Cases referred to:

(1) Amarfio and Another v. Ayorkor (1954) 14 W.A.C.A. 554. (2) Ebu v. Ababio (1956) 2 W.A.L.R. 55.

APPEAL from a decision of the Kwahu Native Appeal Court on April 3, 1957, affirming a decision of the Kwahu Native Court" B " on May 10, 1956, dismissing a claim for a declaration of title to land.

Osei Tutu for the plaintiff.

- Puplampu for the defendants.

OLLENNU J. This is an appeal from a judgment of the Kwahu Native Appeal Court, which upheld a judgment of the Kwahu Native Court " B," Mpraeso, which dismissed a claim of the plaintiff for a declaration of his title to a certain cocoa farm as successor to his brother, one Kodwo Owusu, who died about four years ago. The first defendant is the stepfather of the plaintiff and his deceased brother, the said }{odwo Owusu.

The case put up by the plaintiff is that the farm in dispute was acquired some forty years ago by his brother }{odwo Owusu with his, the plaintiff's, assistance, by cultivation of the virgin forest.

It is common ground between the parties that about twenty years after the farm was made, the-late Owusu was enstooled a chief of Danteng, and that before his enstoolment he put the plaintiff in charge of the farm and all his other self-acquired property to prevent this from becoming merged into stool property upon his succession to the stool. Upon the death of Owusu a dispute arose as to who should succeed him. That dispute was finally determined four years later as a result of which the plaintiff became successor to Owusu's self­acquired property, whilst one Kwame Ohemeng succeeded him on the stool. During the four years of the succession dispute, the family neglected the farm and the defendant took charge of and maintained it; and upon the determination of the disputes he, the defendant, claimed from the family the expenses he had incurred in the maintenance of the farm. The first defendant also claimed from the family an amount of forty pounds, being a loan which he said the late Kodwo Owusu had obtained from him just before his death.

The defence put up is that the defendant was the one who started to make the farm in question; that he later gave it to the late Kodwo Owusu, his stepson, who occupied it as aforesaid; that, when the farm was neglected by the family during the four years of the succession dispute, the first defendant maintained it; and further, that the first defendant gave a loan of forty pounds privately to the late Owusu without a witness and without taking from him a promissory note or other written acknowledgment.

The defendants said that, when the first defendant called upon the Owusu family to pay this loan of forty pounds, they refused to pay unless he could produce documentary or other independent proof of the giving of the loan; that in consequence of the family's refusal to pay the debt, the first defendant revoked the gift he had made of the farm to the late Owusu, because he said, the gift, like the loan, was not made in the presence of witnesses.

The evidence on this point was given by the co-defendant and is as follows:

" After the funeral my uncle ~he defendant wen~ and demanded the £40 from the family of the late Owusu but they refused to repay the same on grounds that there was no document on it. The defendant therefore reclaimed this farm on the grounds that the farm was also given Owusu his son in camera, and if they have failed to repay the £40 borrowed to late Owusu in camera as well. "

"Later the head member of the plaintiff's family rendered apology for the farm and defendant granted same on condition that they should pay the £40. But they failed so to do."

Upon these facts the trial Native Court" B " said ,they believed that the defendant was the one who originally made the farm on the land, and that there was no " aseda " when he made ~he gift of it to the late Owusu.

There was some evidence led on behalf of the defence which tends to show that even if the defendant was the one who started to cultivate the virgin forest and gave the area he cleared to Owusu, Owusu culti­vated more forest and extended the farm. But since there is evidence which, if believed, justifies the findings of the trial Native Court" B " that the farm belonged originally to the defendant; this court cannot interfere with those findings of fact. But, those findings of fact do not necessarily determine the case, even though the basis of the plaintiff's claim was Owusu's ownership by original cultivation. In my opinion, where a plaintiff claims title to land and pleads a particular root of title to support that claim, and the evidence proves that title is vested in him .but that it was acquired through another root, the trial court is not entitled to dismiss the claim upon that finding, for to do so would lead to a miscarriage of justice, in that the plaintiff would, in the circumstances, be deprived of property which, upon the evidence, was undisputedly vested in him. In such a case it is the duty of the court to do justice between the parties and uphold the plaintiff's title, even though upon grounds different from those he pleaded. That is the principle underlying the decision of this court in the case of Amarfio and Another v. Ayorkor (1), which was upheld by the West African Court of Appeal and also in Ebu v. Ababio (2), by the Privy Council. In the former of these two cases the plaintiff claimed a declaration that a certain house was the property of his family, pleading that it was the self-acquired property of " P.," a deceased member of the family, and became family property in accordance with native custom upon " P. 's " death intestate. 'l'he defendant on .the contrary pleaded that the house was not the self-acquired property of "P," but of "A.," his father, another member of the family, who also died intestate. The Land Court, on the facts, found against .the plaintiff as to which member of the family it was that originally acquired the property, and held that it was the self-acquired property of "A." In spite of the plaintiff's failure to obtain findings in his favour on the facts, the court nevertheless entered judgment for him on his claim, and declared the property family property, on the grounds that since " A," the original individual owner of the property, died intestate, his property became, by native custom, the property of the plaintiff's family, the" A.-M." family, to which both" P." and" A." belonged.

In the second case the Privy Council held that although the court made a finding of fact against the plaintiff on his tradition as to how his stool acquired the land in dispute, yet judgment should be entered for him since his evidence as to acts of possession was accepted. They said:

" there are cases in which a party can succeed even if he fails to obtain a finding in his favour on the traditional evidence."

In my opinion the present case is one such case.

Now, the defence that the gift made about forty years ago to Owusu could be revoked because it was not made in the presence of a witness, is to my mind untenable. An essential element in alienation of land by native custom is publicity of the fact that .title in the land has passed from the transferor to the transferee. The usual way of publicising this is by carrying out the transaction of transfer in the presence of witnesses. But this is not by any means the only method of giving publicity to a transfer. Any act done or steps taken or conduct of the transferor which manifests to persons, other than parties to the transaction, an intention to transfer, or amounts to an asser­tion that property in the land has passed from .the original owner to the other party, is quite sufficient.

In this case the evidence shows that for twenty years, Owusu, .to the full knowledge of the defendant, occupied and used this farm as his individual self-acquired property without let or hindrance, and when he was to be enstooled, Owusu, with the knowledge of the defendant and the stool family, handed the farm, together with all the rest of his self-acquired property to the plaintiff in order that .those properties might not, upon his enstoolment, become stool property in accordance with custom; and finally when the defendant, the stepfather of Owusu, had need to maintain the farm during the four years of the succes­sion dispute, he later presented a bill for his expenses thereon to the family for payment. The only irresistible conclusion which can be drawn from that conduct of the defendant in all the circumstances is that he acknowledged Owusu, and after his death, his family, as the people in whom ownership of the farm is vested.

A gift of land made inter vivos is irrevocable once it is completed and the donee is placed in possession. The defendant therefore is not entitled to revoke the gift, which upon the evidence he effectively made some forty years ago to the plaintiff's predecessor.

Again, even if the defendant could lawfully revoke the gift and in fact did revoke it, the evidence that:

" Later the head member of plaintiff's family rendered apology for the farm and the defendant granted same on condition that they should pay the £40,"

taken together with the evidence that the defendant thereafter accepted a promissory note from the plaintiff, and then received part payment of the £40, is evidence of a fresh completed grant made for valuable consideration, that consideration being the undertaking by the family to pay a debt which the defendant could not either at law or by native custom prove against the estate of a deceased member of the family.

Thus, upon the defendants' own showing, title in the farm has legally passed to the plaintiff, and the Native Courts should have given effect to that legal position by entering judgment for the plaintiff.

For the reasons given above, I would allow the appeal.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
