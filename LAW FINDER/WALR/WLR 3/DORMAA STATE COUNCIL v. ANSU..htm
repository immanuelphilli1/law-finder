                                                    December 10, 1957

                                                      DORMAA STATE COUNCIL v. ANSU.                                                       

                                                         Court of Appeal

                                  van Lare Ag.C.J., Granville Sharp J.A., Smith J.

                                                   

 

 

Criminal law-Practice-Appeals from Magistrate's Courts-No power for prosecutor to appeal to Court of Appeal from decision of High Court 011 appeal from Magistrate-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 338.

The respondent had been convicted by a Magistrate of the offence, under the State Councils (Ashanti) Ordinance, 1952, s. 23 (2), of having failed to deliver up stool property when ordered to do so. From this conviction he appealed to the Divisional Court, where the appeal was allowed and the conviction quashed. From this decision tne State Council, the original prosecutors, sought to appeal to the Court of Appeal. They prayed in aid the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 338, which confers a right of appeal on "any person aggrieved" by a decision of the High Court given in the exercise of its appellate jurisdiction over Magistrate's Courts.

Held, a prosecutor is not a person aggrieved within the meaning of the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 338, and no right of appeal lies at the suit of a prosecutor from a decision of the High Court given in exercise of its appellate jurisdiction.

Commissioner of Police v. Kwashie (1953) 14 W.A.C.A. 319 overruled.

Cases referred to:

(1) Commissioner of Police v. Kwashie (1953) 14 W.A.C.A. 319.

(2) President of the Akim Kotoku State Council v. Ntiamoah Kofi III (1955) 1 W.A.L.R. 26.

(3) R. v. Keepers of the Peace for the County of London (1890) 25 Q.B.

357.

(4) Foss v. Best [1906] 2 K.B. 105.

APPEAL from a decision of Ollennu J. on January 23, 1957, in the Divisional Court, Kumasi, quashing a conviction by the District Magis­trate, Sunyani, on October 22, 1956, for failing to deliver up stool property after being ordered to do so, contrary to the State Councils Ordinance, 1952, s. 23 (2).

Smith, Crown counsel, for the Attorney-General.

Heward-Mills for the appellant State Council.

           '--                 .

Taylor for the respondent.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by GRANVILLE SHARP J.A.: This matter came before the court by way of appeal from a decision of Ollennu J. who in the Divisional Court at Kumasi on January 23, 1957, set aside a conviction and quashed the sentences imposed upon the defendant by the District Magistrate at Sunyani on October 22, 1956, in connection with an alleged offence under the pro­visions of the State Councils (Ashanti) Ordinance, 1952, s. 23 (2). The specific offence charged was that the defendant, having been ordered by the complainants, the Dormaa State Council, had failed without reasonable excuse to obey the order which enjoined him to deliver up the stool property then in his keeping. Ollennu J. held that, in his judgment, the District Magistrate before convicting and punishing the defendant had failed to give full and proper consideration to the question, before finding that the excuse put forward by the defence was a reasonable excuse. He therefore quashed the convic~ion and sentence and acquitted the defendant, entering a verdict of not guilty. The complainants have sought to challenge this decision before us, ground­ing their attempt on the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Ordi­nance, s. 338, which reads as follows:

" Any person aggrieved by a decision of the Supreme Court in its appellate jurisdiction under this part may appeal to the Court of Appeal on a matter of law (not including severity of sentence) but not on a matter of fact except with the permission of the Court of AppeaL"

Dr. Taylor on behalf of the defendant at the outset raised a prelimi­nary objection that the Dormaa State Council had no locus standi before the court as they could not in any legal sense be described as a " person aggrieved" within the meaning of the section inasmuch as the section upon a proper interpretation of its meaning and intent confers no right on a prosecutor to appeal against a decision of the Supreme Court in its appellate jurisdiction.

Mr. Heward-Mills on behalf of The Dormaa State Council argued he contrary and sought to substantiate his contentions by reference to two decisions of the West African Court of Appeal, Commissioner of Police v. Kwashie (1) and Akim Kotoku State Council v. Ntiamoah Koft III (2), in .the first of which their Lordships decided that it was competent for a prosecutor to appeal under the provisions of the then relevant and precisely similar section, now section 338, and in the second it must be taken that they so decided by entertaining the appeal.

Learned counsel then tried t'o fortify his reliance on these autho­rities by reference to the words used in the section by contrast with the wording of section 326 referring to " the party against whom the conviction or order is made." vVe were invited to accept the view that by the change of words in the later section the legislature clearly indicated an intention to include persons not included by the earlier section and that prosecutors must therefore be held competent to appeal as a " person aggrieved. "

Dr. Taylor drew our attention to United Kingdom cases and statutes involving the interpretation of precisely similar words and contended that the decisions in question must be preferred to the West African Court of Appeal cases in which it does not appear .that the point was argued or that the attention of the court was drawn to the earlier English decisions.

In R. v. Keepers of the Peace for the County of London (3) the judgments of Lord Coleridge C.J. and Wills J., at pages 359 et seq., clearly decide that a prosecutor cannot be regarded as a "person aggrieved" so as to entitle him to appeal against a decision causing him annoyance or other reason for complaint.

The ratio decidendi in that case was followed in Foss v. Best (4) and was not questioned in the case of Stokes v. Mitcheson (5). In a later case, R. v. Newport Justices (6), proceedings were instituted under the Summary Jurisdiction Act, 1857, s. 2, in which the words are "either party to the proceedings who is dissatisfied" and it was argued that these provisions were by implication repealed when in the subsequent Summary Jurisdiction Act, 1879, the legislature used the different form of words" any party aggrieved." This contention was rejected and a prosecutor was held competent to appeal by way of case stated on a point of law under .the earlier Act, though it was not suggested that the decision would have been the same if the material words had been those in the later Act. The three earlier cases were considered by the court and were not disapproved, Lord Hewart C.J., at page 426, saying only:

" I find nowhere a clear expression that an unsuccessful prose­cutor may not still under the Act of 1857 apply for a case stated because he is dissatisfied with a determination of justices as being erroneous in point of law."

The learned Chief Justice had already, at page 425 of the report, said:

"It may well be that the unsuccessful prosecutor is not a person aggrieved within the meaning of the Act of 1879 but it does not follow that he does not come within the Act of 1857."

Mr. Heward-Mills sought to dismiss this line of authorities as being merely persuasive. We cannot find it possible to deal with the matter in so perfunctory a way.

They seem to us to have the full force contended for by counsel for the defendant, and must be considered in the light of the presumption that the legislature is acquainted with the actual state of the law (see Lord Blackburn in Young v. Leamington (Mayor) (7)), and that there­fore when the words of an old statute are incorporated in a new statute this is understood to be done with the object of adopting any legal interpretation which has been put upon them by ~he courts-In re Earl Fitz-William's Agreement (8).

It appears to us therefore that the continued use in the law of this country of the words" person aggrieved" must be taken to indicate an adherence to the interpretation given to them in the earlier English cases, and cannot be said to show an intention to depart from it.

Mr. Heward-Mills' further point upon the significance of the dif­ference between the words in section 326 and those in section 338 is not sound when consideration is given to other and superior canons of interpretation. It is only rarely that a mere change in words can be said to indicate a change of intention. It has been said from the earliest editions of Maxwell that some of the reasons for this are, a natural desire to avoid repetition of phrases, and the fact that many hands are known to go to the drafting of statutes as well as to their amendment.

The guiding canon in all these cases is that general words and phrases must be interpreted by strictly adapting the meaning to the particular subject of the enactment, it being necessary to give them the meaning which bests suits the scope and object of the statute; that is to say they must be understood as used with reference to the subject­matter in the mind of the legislature, and limited to it. See Wands­worth Board of Works v. United Telephone Co. (9). The VIIIth part of the Criminal Procedure Code has for its subject Appeals from Magistrates and its object, quite clearly disclosed in sections 326 to 339, is to facilitate and provide for appeals by convicted persons.

To hold that the words in section 338 import unsuccessful prose­cutors within the scope of the enactment would not be limiting their meaning to what was in the mind of the legislature. It would be an unwarranted enlargement of the scope and object of the Code. Our view upon this is strengthened by parenthetical words in the section which exclude from the matters upon which appeals may be made "severity of sentence." Such words are wholly inappropriate to prosecutors.

We do not find ourselves able to follow the earlier cases decided in this country which were brought to our attention and must prefer the earlier English cases because it would appear as we have already said that in the West African Court of Appeal cases the court really allowed the answer to the question to go by default, and made no reference to the earlier English authorities. It may be noted in this connection that Mr. Smith. Crown counsel, who appeared before us as amicus curiae stated that the Attorney-General had instructed him to say that the Crown would not now contend that the Crown as a prosecutor is entitled to appeal against an acquittal. We think that a private prose­cutor cannot be in any different case.

 

We are in the circumstances of the opinion that the Dormaa State Council has no locus standi before us and that regrettably we are pre­cluded from giving any consideration to the question whether the defen­dant in fact proffered a reasonable excuse for his disobedience of the order made to him. The appeal fails in limine and is accordingly dismissed.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
