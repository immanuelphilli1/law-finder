                                                     October 17, 1958

                                  COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. WENYONU.

                                                           Court of Appeal

                           Korsah C.J., van Lare and Granville Sharp JJ.A

 

 

Criminal Law-Practice-Statement of charge-Effect of failure to disclose particulars of offence-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10, ss. 111 (1) and (3).

Criminal Law-Appeals-Discretion to refrain from quashing conviction beca1/se no substantial miscarriage of justice-Whether discretion exer­cisable where charge contains insufficient particulars-Court of Appeal~ Ordinance, c. 35 of 1957, s. 10 (1).

The appellant, a police sergeant, had been charged with two offences: the first charge, which was dismissed, alleged aiding and abetting theft; the second, on which the appellant was convicted, alleged neglect of duty. The second charge read: "No. 7526 G/Sgt. Joseph Wenyonu, Police Sergeant on the same date and place aforesaid, was guilty of neglect of duty."

Held, (i) the charge as written failed to disclose such particulars of the alleged offence as would be necessary to give reasonable information as to the nature of the charge.

(ii) The principles upon which the court will exercise its discretion to refrain from quashing a conviction because there has been no substan­tial miscarriage of justice do not go so far as to justify the use of the discretion in cases where an accused person has not been given reasonable information as to the charge against him.

, 1l. v. Bandoh «1944) 10 W.A.C.A. 190) applied.

APPEAL from a decision of Murphy J., in the Divisional Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, on July 26, 1958, dismissing an appeal from a conviction by the Senior Magistrate, Kumasi, convicting the appellant of neglect of duty contrary to the Police Force Ordinance, c. 37 of 1951, s. 20.

Crabbe, Senior Crown counsel, for the Commissioner of Police.

Prempeh for the appellant.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by Granville Sharp J.A.: The appellant was convicted on June 17,1958, on a charge of neglect of duty, contrary to the Police Force Ordinance, s. 20. The particulars of offence following upon the said charge were as follows:

"No. 7526 GjSgt. Joseph Wenyonu, Police Sergeant on the same date and place aforesaid, was guilty of neglect of duty." The" same date and place aforesaid" was a reference to the particulars of offence on another charge, namely aiding and abetting stealing, and these were as follows:

"On the 29th day of May 1958 at Kumasi in the Ashanti :Magisterial District and within the jurisdiction of this court did aid and abet one Amuzu Mensah for the commission of a crime to wit stealing of one pistol value £25 the property of Ghana Government. "

Shortly put, the evidence by which the prosecution sought to establish the appellant's guilt on both charges was that while the said Amuzu was in his custody as a deportee awaiting deportation, Amuzu, who had been allowed out of prison for a short while under guard, was seen by the appellant to produce and attempt to sell to a third person a pistol that, as it later transpired, had been stolen from the exhibits room at the police station; that the appellant did not interfere in the course of this transaction by asking the deportee to explain his possession of the weapon or by confiscating it or making any other inquiry. Later it was said by the prosecution witnesses that when it was discovered that the pistol was missing from the exhibits room the appellant, as the result of other information, was permitted to take the deportee out in order to make inquiries as to the whereabouts of the pistol and if possible recover it, and that in the course of this expedition the deportee escaped and was still at large.

The facts as to this latter event were that the person in the charge of the appellant, whilst the appellant's attention was busy in the search for the pistol in a cupboard, took advantage of such opportunity to make a get-away behind his back. As to this the learned magistrate before whom the appellant was convicted of neglect of duty said:

" It is also rather strange that Amuzu Mensah should tell the accused that he knew where the gun could be found on the 4th June, 1958, and that the accused should leave with Amuzu Mensah for enquiries and return without Amuzu Mensah whom the accused said had escaped. I believe that the accused permitted Amuzu Mensah to escape as he knew of the consequence if Amuzu Mensah was arrested and charged.

" I could not find the accused guilty on the first count because there is no evidence that he knew anything about the gun until after it was stolen by Amuzu Mensah. However, the accused has admitted that if it was true that Amuzu Mensah had the gun, he should have become suspicious and arrested Amuzu Mensah and reported the matter. I find as a fact that it was Amuzu Mensah who had the gun, and who pledged it with the first prosecution witness for £1. "

He then proceeded to convict the appellant on the second count of neglect of duty.

By acquitting the appellant on the first count and convicting him on the second count the learned magistrate showed clearly that he did not suppose that the words" at the same date and place aforesaid" meant that by relation back the particulars of offence in count 2 could be said to be contained in the particulars of offence in count 1, otherwise he would have acquitted on both counts.

In these circumstances we acceded to a request by counsel for the appellant and allowed him to argue a ground of appeal not up to then formally included, viz: that the particulars of offence in count 2 were wholly insufficient to satisfy the legal requirements contained in the Criminal Procedure Code, s. III (1) and (3). These are:

"(1) Subject to the special rules as to information hereinafter mentioned, every charge, complaint, summons, warrant, or other document laid, issued, or made for the purpose of or in connection with any proceedings before any court for an offence, shall be sufficient if it contains a statement of the specific offence with which the accused person is charged, together with such particulars as may be necessary for giving reasonable information as to the nature of the charge. "

"(3) After the statement of the offence, necessary particulars of the offence shall be set out in ordinary language, in which the use of technical terms shall not be required. "

Senior Crown counsel relied upon the Police Force Ordinance, s. 4, which reads:

" The Force shall be employed for the prevention and detection of crime, the apprehension of offenders, the preservation of law and order, the protection of persons and property and the due enforcement of all laws and regulations with which it is directly charged. "

The case of R. v. Bandoh, a charge of perjury, is an authority in point.

The following is an extract from the judgment of the court:

" It is obvious that the ground of appeal is correct in stating that the charge does not allege that the appellant's statement was false to his knowledge or that he did not believe it to be true. The particulars do not even aver that the statement is untrue. In the form of indictment for perjury given at page 1221 of the 31st Edition of Archbold's ICriminal Pleading, Evidence and Practice, the essential words" knowingly falsely swore" are used; and we are of opinion that it is essential in an information for perjury that the particulars should contain an averment either in those terms or in terms which make it clear that the statement alleged to have been made is also alleged to be false, and false to the knowledge of the accused or that he did not believe it to be true.

" \Ve are of opinion that the particulars given in the present case fail to state what is necessary for giving reasonable information as to the nature of the charge, and that consequently the information is bad. "

We are of opinion in the present case that the same reasoning applies. There is nothing whatever in the particulars of offence to indicate to the accused person which of his multifarious duties he had neglected or in what respect he had fallen short of his duty. Further­more we cannot feel ourselves able to exercise our discretion so as to find that no miscarriage of justice has occurred.

The learned judges in the cited case held that the principles under which such a discretion is exercised "do not go so far as to justify the use of the proviso where an accused person has not been given reasonable information as to the nature of the charge against him."

For these reasons we allow the appeal, sentence and enter a verdict of not guilty. is ordered to be repaid to the appellant quash the conviction and The fine, if already paid

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
