                                                                        August 1 and 12, 1957.

                              LARDAN v. ATTORNEY-GENERAL AND OTHERS (No.1). .

                                          High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                                                  Smrth J.

 

 

Aliens-Deportation of aliens-Practice-Appropriate procedure for testing validity of deportation order-Power of court to issue injunction--­Deportation Act, 1957, s. 3 (1).

Practice-Injunction---Motion ex parte for interlocutory injunction---Necessity for applicant to show fair prima facie case-Civil Procedure Rules, 1954, Ord. 50, r. 3.

The applicant was the subject of a deportation order made, on July 30, 1957, under the provisions of the Deportation Act, 1957, ss. 4 (1) (c) and 5 (1). On July 31 he filed a writ seeking a declaratory order to the effect that he was a Ghanaian citizen and thus, by section 3 (1), not within the ambit of the Act. He also moved ex parte for an interim injunction and supported this with an affidavit dealing with his place of birth and the place of birth of his mother. An interim injunction issued until August 8.

Held, (i) the Deportation Act, 1957, vests power in the Government, the body charged with the task of maintaining the safety of the country, to make orders which it deems to be necessary for the public good. It is of the essence of this power that, for the proper implementation of the Act, the Government should be able to act speedily. The normal civil procedure by which an issue may be determined (e.g., as to whether a person against whom a deportation order has been made is or is not an alien) is inappropriate to circumstances such as these, for it can conduce to delay. The appropriate procedure for a person affected to use is that of habeas corpus.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The Civil Procedure Rules, Ord. 50, r. 3, are in sub­stance the same as R.S.C., Ord. 50, r. lA, which, on an application for an inj unction, permits the judge, in his discretion, to order speedy trial.]

(ii) Since it is within the competence of the court to quash a deporta­tion order made without jurisdiction (i.e., where the subject of the order is not an alien and is thus not within the ambit of the Act) the court may also, in appropriate cases, issue an injunction.

(iii) In an application for an interlocutory injunction the applicant must show a fair prima facie case in support of the right claimed. On the facts of the present case the applicant, with his single, preliminary, bare affidavit, had failed to establish such prima facie case and the interlocutory injunction must therefore be discharged.

Cases referred to:

(1) R. v. Home Secretary, ex parte Ohateau Thierry (Duke) [1917] 1 K.B. 922 (C.A.).

(2) Preston v. Luck (1884) 27 Ch.D. 506.

(3) Dyson v. Attorney-General [1911] 1 K.B. 410 (C.A.). (

4) Tamaki v. Baker [1901] A.C. 561 (P.C.).

ACTION for a declaration as to citizenship: motion for an injunction to restrain the operation of a deportation order made against the applicant.

Bing, Q. 0., Attorney-General, for the respondents. Taylor for the applicant.

August 1, 1957.

SMITH J. The plaintiff has filed a substantive writ seeking a declara­tion that he is a citizen of Ghana. He asks for an injunction against his deportation. It appears that a deportation order has been made against him, signed by the Minister of the Interior and Justice. The plaintiff, in support of the motion of injunction, has filed an affidavit claiming that he is a Ghanaian citizen and that, therefore, under section 3 (1) of the Deportation Act, 1957, he is not liable to deportation.

There is no question tha't the making of an order under section 4 (1) (c) of the Act is entirely within the power of the Governor-General and cannot be questioned, provided it is the case that the person con­cerned is an alien. It would appear that where that is n6t the case the order can be questioned. In the case of R. v. Home Secretary, ex parte Ohateau Thierry (Duke) (1) there is the following passage per Swinfen Eady L.J. :

" If it were intended to do something illegal under a valid order, that would be good ground for restraining and preventing the illegal act, but not for quashing a valid order. A Secretary of State is not required to justify in a court of law his reasons for making a deportation order in the case of an alien. In the event of it being disputed that the subject of a deportation order js an alien, the matter must be determined by the court, and unless it be proved that the person is an alien the order must be quashed as made without jurisdiction; but I am not aware of any other ground upon which such an order can be quashed."

In 'the present case the deportation order is not brought to be quashed, but I see no reason, if that can be done, why an injunction cannot be made, where a substantive writ has been filed seeking a declaration on the only one issue, i.e., of citizenship, which the court is bound to go into. An injunction in the present case merely suspends the order, and if it is possible to quash it I cannot see where there is any difficulty in this matter in granting the injunction as craved.

The injunction must be served on the parties, the Attorney-General, the Minister of the Interior and the Commissioner of Police, as well, of course, as the substantive writ. The injunction is for one week from today. This matter can then be the subject of further argument if necessary.

August 12, 1957.

A deportation order dated July 30, 1957, was made against one Alhaji Alufa Osmanu Lardan Lalemie under the Deportation Act, 1957, ss. 4 (1) (c) and 5 (1). In view of this order the person concerned filed a writ on July 31 claiming a declaratory order or judgment that he is a citizen of Ghana; that he is not, therefore, liable to deportation under the Deportation Act; and that the deportation order made is null and void. At the same time a motion ex parte was made on his behalf for an injunction. The motion came before me on August 1. I heard argument on that motion and I subsequently made an order granting an interlocutory injunction until August 8. I think I should emphasise here that this order for injunction was restricted solely to deportation. There was no injunction as to anything else. I also ordered that notice of the motion be given to the Attorney-General and the other defen­dants. It was obvious that the matters involved-both of principle and procedure-were of the greatest importance, and it was essential to have a full argument. At the hearing on August 8 Mr. Bing, for the Attorney-General, Minister of Interior and Justice and the Com­missioner of Police, argued that the interlocutory injunction be dis­charged and at the same time he gave an undertaking or assurance that the parties concerned would not be deported out of the country pending the investigation of the claim for citizenship.

The onus of proving that he is a Ghana citizen is under the Act cast entirely on the person claiming that status. I do not agree that the wording of section 3 presupposes that an investigation of fact must take the form of a trial based on an ordinary writ and following out the procedure of an ordinary civil action.

I deal now with the interlocutory injunction made on the plaintiff's claim. The affidavit of the plaintiff merely states that he was born in Kumasi and his mother (unnamed) in Krachi. In dealing with the motion on July 31, I bore in mind that there was little or no time for further affidavits and evidence to be obtained. Nor in the circum­stances do I attach any real importance to certain technical omissions in the affidavit pointed out by Mr. Bing. Nothing more, however, was filed to supplement the plaintiff's bare affidavit between July 31 and August 8. In the case of Preston v. Luck (2), which was cited to me, there is the following passage:

" to entitle the plaintiffs to an interlocutory injunction it is neces­sary that the court should be satisfied that there is a serious question to be tried at the hearing and that on the facts before it there is a probability 'that the plaintiffs are entitled to relief."

It is also relevant to refer to the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 50, r. 3. That rule clearly shows that a court in exercising discretion on an application for an injunction must go into the whole merits on affidavit or other evidence for the purpose of the application. It has also been put this way, when dealing with the principles governing the issue of a temporary injunction, that "the applicant must show a fair prima facie case in support of the right claimed." That puts the matter succinctly and is in accord with the authority already quoted. On the barest of information given in this application for injunction it is impossible for this court to say that a case for the continuation of the interim injunction has been made out.

That is enough to dispose of the motion before me but I want to give shortly my views of the procedure adopted in this case and the effect on an order for deportation. It must be very clearly understood that the motives and reasons for the order made are not for the court. The court is indeed concerned with the liberty of the subject but there are other safeguards for that. This is a matter of a deportation order made under the Act that the presence of the person concerned in the country is not conducive to the public good. Power is given under the Act to the government in office to deport aliens whose presence is no't conducive to the good of the country. None of the authorities cited are in point on a matter of this sort. There is all the difference in the world, in my view, between a case of this sort and those cited to me-one dealing with the validity of Inland Revenue notices, Dyson v. Attorney­General (3), and one dealing with a title to land, Tamahi v. Balwr (4). It seems to me wrong that in a case where such an order as this has-been made, that the party concerned can, if he so prefers, put himself in the role of plaintiff, file a civil writ and obtain an interlocutory injunc­tion and then sit back comfortably until the ordinary routine of civil procedure is exhausted. That might drag on for months or a year and it would be an impossible situation for whatever government is in power-where that government is responsible for the safety of the country and has made an order which it deems necessary for the public good. This procedure would render the Deportation Act unworkable or at least nullify its effect. It would mean that anyone might come into the country and by a mere personal tatement of citizenship effect whatever his purpose was, while the formalities and procedure of an ordinary civil action took its course through the court. I cannot hold that such should be the position. All the cases of this nature are under habeas corpus proceedings and such proceedings provide sufficient safe­guard for the liberty of the subject. In the result the interlocutory injunction must be discharged. Order is made accordingly.

 

S. G. D.
