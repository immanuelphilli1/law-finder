                                                       November 4, 1957

                         AZAMETSI AND ANOTHER v. GADRI AND ANOTHER

                                                                             Court of Appeal

                  van Lare Ag.C.J., Granville Sharp J.A., Adumua-Bossman J.   

 

 

Practice-Representation-Arbitration-Family property- Whether family estopped by arbitration award-No authority given to arbitrate on behalf of family.

The plaintiffs, representing their family, sought a declaration of their family's title to a creek. The defendants, representing their family, denied that the creek was owned exclusively by the plaintiffs' family and claimed it to be owned jointly by the two families. They relied on an alleged arbitration award in which a ruling as to such joint ownership was given and they claimed that the plaintiffs were estopped by this award from claiming exclusive ownership. It appeared that the plaintiffs had installed two men to work the creek and that in their (the plaintiffs') absence the defendants had interfered with this user. The two caretakers had thereupon complained to the paramount chief of the area and he had summoned the defendants to show cause, whereupon they (the defendants) had asserted their family's title. The resultant" award" held the owner­ship of the creek to be vested in the plaintiffs' and the defendants' families jointly.

Held, the rule that, save in exceptional circumstances, only the head of a family can sue or be sued in respect of family property extends also to arbitration proceedings. The two caretakers had not established at the arbitration that they had the authority of the plaintiffs' family to arbitrate on their behalf, nor had such authority in the caretakers been established by the defendants, as would otherwise be necessary. There­fore, even if the inquiry by the paramount chief were assumed to be an arbitration properly so called, the plaintiffs' family was neither a party nor privy to a party to it and the award therefore could neither bind them nor estop them from asserting their title as against the defendants' family.

APPEAL from a decision of Manyo-Plange J., given in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, on February 18, 1955, reversing a decision of the Anlo Native Appeal Court given on August 23, 1954, and restoring the decision of the Anyako Native Court" B " on May 1, 1954, in an action brought on behalf of a family for a declaration of title to a creek.

Apaloo for the plaintiffs. Asafu-Adjaye for the defendants.

VAN LARE Ag.C.J. This is an appeal from the decision of Manyo­Plange J., sitting in the Land Division of the High Court, Accra, on February 18, 1955, allowing an appeal from a decision from the Anlo Native Appeal Court, which reversed the decision of the Anyako Native Court " B. "

The point to be decided in this appeal is a simple one, that is, whether a certain arbitration award affecting the title and fishing rights in the Ziwe creek is binding upon the plaintiffs in this case. The plaintiffs represent the Aweku family of Badadzi and the defendants the Soglo family of the same place. Both sides claim ancestry from a common great-grandfather and to be descended respectively from two of the sons of this ancestor. It is not the case that the property and the rights to fish in the creek constitute family property which both sides may enjoy, but the case is that the disputed creek is the separate property of the Aweku family alone, which the plaintiffs represent.

There does not appear to be any quarrel concerning the findings of the trial Native Court as to the root of title descending upon the plaintiffs' family but, according to the evidence, they satisfactorily established absolute ownership in their favour. It appears, however, that in the absence of the plaintiffs from home, their cousins, that is to say the defendants, interfered with the use and possession of the creek by two men, Egui Tsidi and Dakohu Damawu, whom the plaintiffs had placed in charge as labourers or agents. These two men, in the absence of their principals, the owners of the creek, complained according to the normal practice to the late Togbi Sri II, the Paramount Chief of the area, against the defendants when they purported to challenge their rights to the creek and for fishing therein. It would appear that when the defendants were called and questioned about their attitude to these two men at a meeting, which is now considered as an arbitration, the defendants asserted their title to the creek. A note evidencing the result of this arbitration shows that the plaintiffs were described as representing the Aweku, that is, the plaintiffs' family. This note, purporting to be the arbitration award, shows that the defendants' family was found to be one not to be deprived" of their portion of the fishing hamper inasmuch as both parties are beneficiaries thereto and accordingly vested in them ownership thereof."

The present suit arose out of what happened in the plaintiffs' absence and was instituted by the plaintiffs to establish the absolute ownership of the Aweku family as against the defendants' family. The defendants' family, relying on the "arbitration" award before Togbi Sri II in 1952, say that the plaintiffs are estopped from litigating the question of absolute ownership of the creek and the fishing fence and dams therein.

I am unable to hold that, by submitting to the arbitration, though concerning the same creek and the same rights, the two men employed to work on the creek in the absence of their principals had authority to litigate over the absolute ownership of the creek. In my opinion those two men who complained against the defendants' family were mere agents and not principals of the family who could represent the family in litigation concerning family property. It is settled law that only the head of the family or properly accredited representative can litigate over family property. Egui Tsidi and Dakohu Damawu, who were not even shown to be members of the Aweku family, cannot be held to represent that family in arbitration concerning absolute ownership by that family. Even if the two men at the arbitration purported to hold themselves out as representing the family it must be shown that they were competent to do so. At the best it might be presumed that the two men joined issue, presumably on behalf of their principals, as to title; even if it were so they cannot be presumed to be representing the Aweku family. The circumstances suggest that these men at the arbitration must have been concerned with a right or interest, personal to themselves, which was tantamount to a usufructuary" right," that is to say, not denying the title of Aweku family but admitting it, and saying that they were in possession of the disputed land with leave and licence of the true owners, but of course at the same time denying ownership of the defendants' family. A decision in a matter such as this will only affect the parties to it.

 

I hold, therefore, that the plaintiffs in this case were not parties nor privies of the parties to the arbitration relied on, and that the arbitration award as pleaded cannot be binding on them in respect of the subject-matter in dispute.

r hold that the decision of the trial Native Court was therefore right and that the Native Appeal Court erred in law in reversing that judg­ment; and the learned judge of the Land Court, for the reasons given, was justified in setting aside the judgment of the Native Appeal Court and restoring the decision of the trial Native Court.

Mr. Asafu Adjaye, for the defendants' family, has requested this court to consider referring the case back to the trial Native Court for a specific finding as to whether or not the said Tsidi and Damawu were members of the plaintiffs' family; he has argued that failure to make such a specific finding of that fact renders the trial unsatisfactory. I do not consider the trial unsatisfactory on that ground or on any other ground, nor do I think the question arises before a decision can be taken in this court. There is sufficient material on record which satisfies me that by the decision of the trial Native Court the necessary inference is that the two men, Tsidi and Damawu, were found not to be members of the plaintiffs' family. In any case I do not see the reasonableness of a grant of this extra indulgence to the defendants' family to re-open the matter on an issue which they think was material to their case in order that they might do what they had every oppor­tunity of doing in the three courts below. I am not prepared to concede to the suggestion and would dismiss the appeal.

GRANVILLE SHARP J.A. I agree.

ADUMUA-BosSMAN J. I also agree.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
