                                                    December 3 1958.

                     COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. DIKO PIM AND ANOTHER.

                                    High Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                 Smith and Murphy JJ.

 

 

Constitutional Law-Legislation-Order made under authority of Act-Date of coming into force-Necessity for prior publication unless there is direction to the contrary-Whether recital of date of making order suffi­cient direction to the contrary-Interpretation Act, 1957, ss. 3 (1) and 5.

Constitutional Law-Subsidiary legislation-Test to be applied to determine whether an order has legislative or administrative effect-Order made under Statute Law Amendment (No.2) Act, 1957, s. 4 (2)-Interpretation Act, 1957, s. 12.

The Ejisu Stool Property (No.2) Order, 1958, made under the Statute Law Amendment (No.2) Act, 1957, s. 4 (2) authorised Nana Kofi Atta to seize the stool property pertaining to the Ejisu Stool, and to eject the occupants of all the land and buildings forming part of the stool property. The Order recited that it was" made at Accra this 12th day of September 1958," but it was not published in the Gazette until September 20.

Meanwhile, Nana Kofi Atta had already commenced to execute the Order, and it was alleged that on September 12, Kofi Pim and another had hindered him in the exercise of his duty under the Order to seize the stool property, and to eject the occupants of the land and buildings forming part of the stool property. Pim and the other were charged before the Senior Magistrate, Kumasi with a breach of the order.

Their counsel submitted (a) that the Order was not published in the Gazette as provided by section 4 (3) of the Act, (b) that the Order was subsidiary legislation, and (c) that the Order had not the force of law until published in the Gazette. In reply, Crown counsel submitted (a) that the Order was not subsidiary legislation but a mere executive order signed by the Minister, and (b) that the Order had no legislative effect, and therefore did not come within the ambit of the interpreta­tion of "subsidiary legislation," as provided by section 3 of the Interpretation Act, 1957.

The Magistrate discharged the accused persons, holding that the Order, being made under section 4 (2) of the Statute Law Amendment (No.2) Act, its publication, as provided in section 4 (3) of the Act, was a condition precedent to its enforcement. Any person who interfered with a person in the exercise of his authority under this Order committed an offence (section 7 (1) of the Act), as also did anyone who failed to deliver up possession of stool property as required by the Order (section 7 (2) ). The two accused persons were charged with a breach of the said Order, which had legislative effect, and therefore came within the ambit of the definition of "subsidiary legislation." By virtue of the provisions of section 12 (2) (d) of the Interpretation Act, the Order had not the force of law on September 12, 1958, because it was not published in the Gazette on or before September 12, 1958, and the Order did not purport to have retrospective effect.

On the application of the Attorney-General the Magistrate stated a case, and submitted the following questions of law for the opinion of the Supreme Court:

(1) Whether the Order was subsidiary legislation or a mere executive order without legislative effect 1

(2) If the Order was subsidiary legislation, whether the two accused persons could commit a breach of it before it was published in the Gazette 1

(3) If the Order was a mere executive order without legislative effect, whether the two accused persons could be properly charged with a breach of it?

(4) Whether the provisions of section 4 (3) of the Act make publica­tion in the Gazette a condition precedent to the enforcement of the Order 1

Held, (i) the charge alleged an offence which was not created by the Statute Law Amendment (No.2) Act, and which was not otherwise known to the law. The accused persons were therefore rigl'~:;..~harged by the Magistrate.

(ii) In virtue of section 5 of the Interpretation Act, 1957, an Act of Parliament does not come into operation until the date of its publica­tion in the Gazette, unless otherwise provided in the Act. By definition (section 3 (1» an "Act" includes any order duly made under the authority of an Act. The recitation in the Order in question that it was made on September 12, 1958, was not tantamount to providing that it was to come into operation prior to publication in the Gazette. The Magistrate was therefore right in holding that the Order did not have the force of law until its publication in the Gazette.

(iii) To determine whether any particular Order is of legislative or merely administrative effect the Order concerned must be examined in conjunction with its parent statute. The Order in question was not the mere exercise of a power of appointment, nor was it purely adminis­trative. It gave certain rights to the person nominated, the exercise of which might have far-reaching legal consequences. Because its publica­tion was therefore most desirable, it must be regarded as subsidiary legislation, which legislation (in virtue of section 12 (2) (d) of the Interpretation Act, 1957) has effect only when published in the Gazette.

APPEAL by way of case stated (on the application of the Attorney­General) by the Senior Magistrate, Kumasi, after his discharge of the prisoners in Commissioner of Police v. Diko Pim and Another on September 30, 1958.

Amissah, Crown counsel, for the Commissioner of Police. Owusu and Reindorf for the respondents.

The court answered as follows the questions submitted for its opinion. One of the questions set down in the case for the consideration of this court is whether the respondents could be properly charged with the breach of the Order. The answer to this question is clearly" No," since no such offence is created by the Act. The charge does not state an offence known to the law. The respondents were, therefore, properly discharged, and on this ground alone we would dismiss this appeal.

This, however, does not dispose of the matter, because the reason for which the Senior Magistrate discharged the respondents was an entirely different one, and it is this reason which has given rise to this appeal, and which has been the subject of the greater part of the argument before us.

The charge alleged the commission of the offence on September 12, 1958. The Senior Magistrate took judicial notice of the fact that, although the Order was made on September 12, it was not published in the Gazette until September 20. He held that no offence could have been committed on September 12, in relation to the Order, since the Order did not come into force until published in the Gazette. On this point learned Crown counsel based his argument before us main.!.r on the proposition that the Order is a merely executive action, haL"'~ no legislative effect, and that therefore it does not come within the ambit of section 12 (2) (d) of the Interpretation Act, 1957, which provides that subsidiary legislation shall have the force of law immediately upon publication in the Gazette unless otherwise specified.

We think, however, that the matter is covered by the definition of " Act" or " Act of Parliament" in section 3 (1) of the Interpretation Act, 1957, and by section 5, to which learned counsel for the respond­ents drew our attention. Under section 3 (1), "Act" or " Act of Parliament" includes, inter alia, any order duly made under the authority of an Act; and section 5 provides that unless it is otherwise provided in an Act" that Act shall take effect and come into operation as law on the date of publication in the Gazette. By reason of the definition, section 5 must apply equally to an order made under an Act. Any such order will therefore take effect on the date of publication in the Gazette, u~less otherwise provided. Turning to the Order now before us, we cannot agree that the words" Made at Accra this 12th day of September 1958" are tantamount to otherwise providing. We therefore think that the Senior Magistrate was right in deciding that the Order did not have the force of law until its publication in the Gazette on September 20.

This court is also asked for its opinion (although in view of what we have said so far, this is academic) whether the Order is subsidiary legislation or an executive order without legislative effect. We are of the opinion that the Order is subsidiary legislation, and for this reason also has effect only when published in the Gazette, as provided for in section 12 (2) (d) of the Interpretation Act, 1957.

The publication of an order in the supplement under the heading " subsidiary legislation" does not necessarily imply that the order is therefore legislative. Subsidiary legislation is defined in the Interpreta­tion Act, 1957, as follows:

" Subsidiary legislation means any proclamation, rule, regula­tion, order, notification, bye-law Or other instrument made under any Act or other lawful authority and having legislative effect."

It is therefore a matter of examining the particular Order made in conjunction with the parent statute to determine whether the Order is legislative Or administrative. This can be, as counsel for the Crown conceded, an extremely difficult matter to determine, and there does not appear to be a great deal of authority available here to assist us.

Under section 4 (1) of the Statute Law Amendment (No.2) Act, 1957, the Minister is given powers to authorise any person to take possession, seize stool properties, eject any occupant if necessary, as he may decide. Interfering with, hindering, Or knowingly giving false information to, a person in the exercise of his authority under the Act is punishable under section 7 (1) of the Act, and that section is opera­tive only when the Order has been made under section 4 (1). The Order made in this case gives certain rights to the person nominated, the exercise of which may have far-reaching legal consequences. \Ve can­not agree that the Minister by making this Order is merely exercising a power of appointment under the Act.

We were referred to section 76 of the Pharmacy and Poisons Ordinance, but that section is purely administrative. Likewise, the power given to the Minister under section 21 of the Builders Brigade Act is clearly legislative. These references only afford clear-cut examples (that no one could dispute) of administrative or legislative matters, respectively.

Mr. Amissah referred the court to the Law Quarterly Review (Vol. 71) of October 1955, air pp. 492-493. This is of some assistance. At p. 493 there is the following passage:

"For example, there was an Act passed not long ago which said that a railway company (if authorised by order) might subscribe capital for mail and passenger services of boats on the west coast of Scotland, and thereunder the Ministry of Transport made an Order naming the particular railway company which might do so. Prima facie the order was executive, I agree, but at the same time if you did not know what had been done you would not know the Act was operating: so in that case the order was treated as a legislative one."

This examples is difficult to distinguish in principle from the case with which we are dealing.

On p. 493 of the same Review there is also this passage:

" The question whether any document is legislative or executive seems to be answered in practice by another question, 'Is it desirable that this document should be published?' If the answer is' Yes,' then it is called legislative."

That is a quotation from Principles of Administrative Law (Griffith and Street). If this is the criterion we can only say that publication of the Ejisu Stool Property (No.2) Order, 1958, was most desirable.

For these reasons we would hold that the Ejisu Stool Property

(No.2) Order, 1958, is subsidiary legislation as defined under section 3 of the Interpretation Act, 1957. We would therefore answer the first question in this stated case by saying that the Order is subsidiary legislation, and that the answer to the second question, therefore, is in the negative.

The appeal is accordingly dismissed.

Appeal by way of case stated dismissed.
