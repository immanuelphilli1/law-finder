                                                    December 3 1958.

                                  TOKU v. ADUKORANG AND OTHERS. 

                             High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court 

                                               Adumua-Bossman J.

 

 

Practice-Native law and custom-Claim to be recognised as holder of customary office-Whether any cause of action disclosed by refusal to accord recognition.

The plaintiff, who held a customary office as Nkwakwahene (leader of the young men), had agreed with his members to give support to a particular candidate at a local council election. Subsequently, and against the wishes of the members, he had come out in support of a different candidate. Complaint had thereupon been made against him by some of his members (the defendants) and at a customary arbitration he had apologised and given pacification. Thereafter the defendants, who had stated at the arbitration proceedings that they had other com­plaints against the plaintiff, refused to recognise him as Nkwakwahene. He thereupon claimed to be reimbursed the pacification for which he had paid and also sought an award of damages.

Held, (i) the customary arbitration had been concerned with termina­ting an existing dispute: it was not concerned with determining the future relationship between the plaintiff and the defendants.

(ii) In any event no cause of action was disclosed by the plaintiff's claim, the relationship between Nkwakwahene and young men not being such (either in status or in contract) as would give rise to an enforceable obligation.

APPEAL from a decision of the Magistrate's Court, Dunkwa, on June 5, 1958, setting aside a decision of the Twifu Native Court" B " in favour of the plaintiff in an action in which he claimed damages for the failure of the defendants to recognise him as the holder of a customary office.

Sekyi for the appellant.

Renner for the respondents.

ADUMUA-BoSSMAN J. In the trial Native Court the plaintiff's claim was as follows:

"Defendants to show cause why they after settling certain differences between the plaintiff and defendants in which the plaintiff was mulcted into a debt of £7 12s. 6d. and which settle­ment was sealed by an oath sworn by the defendants and the plaintiff now refuse absolutely to recognise the plaintiff as the Nkwakwahene of Nyenasi."

It is admitted that he was appointed Nkwakwahene (leader of the young men) of Nyenasi about one and a half years ago. About July 1956, when a bye-election was about to take place for the election of a candidate for Ward "H" to serve on the Twifu-Heman-Lower­Denkyira Local Council, the defendants made complaint that, after the plaintiff and the young men had agreed to support one Mr. Otsin as candidate in the bye-election, he had betrayed them by turning round to canvass for one Mr. Arhin. Upon the plaintiff appearing before the Krontihene of Nyenasi, he admitted his fault and tendered an apology to the defendants, who appear to be the leading figures among the young men. In his evidence before the court, he said:

" I compensated the defendants with one live sheep, £2 8s., and two bottles of rum. I paid again 27s. as a judgment fee to the Ohene and his Elders; the total costs of the arbitration award was £7 12s. 6d. In token of peace terms settlement, according to custom, one Kwesi Addai swore the oath of Twifu State on behalf of the defendants and I swore the same oath. "

The plaintiff maintains that because of this settlement in connection with the election matter, the defendants were not entitled subsequently to refuse to recognise him as their leader, and that upon their refusal so to recognise him they were liable to refund the amount paid by him as pacification at the arbitration before the Krontihene and/or pay him damages. It is, however, significant that he himself admitted:

" Before the oath was sworn the defendants made me under­stand that they have [other] slight differences to settle at home; this was not in favour of [in connection with] the election. "

He further gave evidence that about one month after the settlement he also brought a complaint against the defendants and others before the youth-leaders of Edujaa, Darmang and Abodom, but was found in the wrong, and when he offered 13s. pacification to the defendants they refused to accept this and gave him an intimation that the young men would no longer recognise him as their leader.

The trial Native Court held that:

" According to the evidence on record the defendants are legally estopped by the arbitration finding in respect of Arhin's bye­election ... the court sees no reason why the defendants deliberately ignored the plaintiff as their leader or Nkwakwahene ";

and they proceeded to award £20 damages plus £9 assessed costs against the defendants. It is clear the trial court erred in their view that " the defendants are legally estopped by the alleged arbitration finding in respect of the bye-election," because the arbitration decided nothing about the future, and no question of estoppel whatever arises. If, as the defendants complained, he as leader together with the young men had agreed to select and support a particular candidate for the bye-election, and he had even gone to the extent of formally nominating Mr. Otsin as the young men's candidate (as was the defendants' evidence), and then he had turned round and betrayed them by canvassing for another candidate, according to prevailing social etiquette he was liable to apologise and make some amends in the form of offering pacification, and this without the defendants being liable to refund that pacification if at any time thereafter they should feel and declare they no longer wished to recognise him. Even constitutionally elected Ahinfu (Chiefs) are not entitled as a matter of course to compensation or damages when their subjects refuse, rightly or wrongly, to continue recognising them; how much more then a person in the position of " leader of young men," in respect of whom the trial Native Court themselves say:

"Nkwakwahene, according to custom, has no legal status in Twifu State Council."

The position is therefore that there has been no breach of any legal duty as such, owed to the plaintiff, the breach of which could give him the right to claim damages, and the learned Magistrate was therefore quite correct in his view that the plaintiff's claim disclosed no cause of action.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
