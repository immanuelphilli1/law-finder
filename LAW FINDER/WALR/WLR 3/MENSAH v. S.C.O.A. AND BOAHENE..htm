                                                         January 25, 1958.

                                                         MENSAH v. S.C.O.A. AND BOAHENE.

 High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

.Ollennu J.

                                                 

 

 Evidence-Land--Conveyancing-Presumption as to application of English law following from 11se of English-style conveyance-Presumption dis­placed by evidence aliunde.

Land-Life interest-Family law-Mortgage-Mortgage of land by family member in possession of family property-Effect of purp01·ted mortgage.

The plaintiff, suing as head of his family, sought a declaration of the family's title to land. The land concerned had been purchased by the plaintiff's two uncles, both of whom were now deceased, and it had been conveyed to them by conveyance in English form. After the death of tne first uncle the survivor had mortgaged the land to the first defendants, who in due course, and in exercise of their powers as mortgagees, had sold it to the second defendant. The plaintiff claimed that the land was family property j that the interest of his uncles in it was limited to their own lives; and that upon the determination of the life interests the interest of the mortgagee automatically determined, leaving them with nothing of which they could validly dispose. There was evidence that after the purchase of the land by the two uncles, other members of the family (i.e., their mother's family) had assisted with the erection of buildings upon it.

Held, (i) the presumption of a joint tenancy as in English law, follow­ing upon the use of an English-style conveyance when the property was purchased, was displaced by the evidence that the development of the land had been a family matter. This indicated that, notwithstanding the form of the conveyance, the intention of the purchasers had been throughout that the relationship between them should be governed by custom, rather than by English law. As such, the property was impressed with the nature of family property, the family being that of the pur­chaser's mother and of which the plaintiff was the head.

(ii) As the property was family property, rather than individually owned property, the interests of the purchasers therein was at most an interest limited to their lives and to the life of the survivor of them. This then was the greatest interest they, or the survivor of them, could alienate. The interest of the first defendant as mortgagee therefore determined upon the death of the surviving purchaser, notwithstanding that he had purported to mortgage the property as beneficial owner.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The position would have been otherwise if there had been evidence that the mortgage was entered into with the consent and con­currence of the mortgagor's family.

This case illustrates well the dangers facing the potential purchaser (or mortgagee) of land. At first sight it might appear unjust that one who, in good faith and without notice, has purchased what is held out to him as an absolute estate in land should be deprived of the fruits of his purchase when it subsequently transpires that the vendor (or mortgagor) had not the estate he held himself out as possessing. The view has been expressed that the courts should accept that the person administering family property does so as trustee for the family and should use the provisions of the Courts Ordinance, c. 4, s. 87 (1), whereby native law and custom is enforced if not" repugnant to natural justice, equity, and good conscience" to extend to the purchaser the protection of the English principles of equity. The bona fide purchaser for value without notice of the family's equities would then not be subjected to them. In fact, however, the injustice is apparent rather than real, for the Ghana common law, as the English, requires the purchaser to investigate his vendor's title. Also, it has a strong presumption that land is communally, as opposed to individually, owned. Conveyancing is not yet general in Ghana and there are no provisions comparable to the Law of Property Act, 1925, ss. 51 and 52; land is most usually conveyed by what can fairly be described as livery or livery and seisin, with all its attendant publicity-cutting guaha or yi ba foo. A potential purchaser, offered land by one who claims to have an absolute estate therein, is put on inquiry by the very nature of his vendor's estate and, apart from making such documentary searches as may be appro­priate, takes at his peril if he does not make open public inquiry locally as to the nature of his vendor's title (e.g., among neighbouring landholders). If such inquiry is made but reveals nothing adverse and if the transaction is then completed in traditional form, i.e., with attendant publicity, it is unlikely that any subsequent claim by a family, or other person with an equitable interest, will succeed against a bona fide purchaser for value who took without notice of such equities. Among other defences, that of the equitable estoppel by acquiescence might well be raised. On the other hand the practice of conveying land by grant by deed is growing and must continue to grow. Conveyances are normally drawn in English form and this fact will enable the courts to hold that the parties thereto have evinced an intention to have their transaction regulated by English law. So, if the vendor has shown good root of title the courts will no doubt give protection to the pur­chaser. A good root of title will presumably be that specified by the Vendor and Purchaser Act, 1875, i.e., one going back forty years.]

ACTION for a declaration of title to land brought by the head of a family in connection with land claimed by him to be family land. A deceased member of the family appearing on ,the face of a conveyance as beneficial owner had purported to mortgage the land to the first defendant company who, in exercise of their power of sale, had purported to sell to the second defendant.

Moore for the plaintiff.

The second defendant appeared in person.

No appearance was entered by the first defendant.

OLLENNU J. The plaintiff in this case claims a declaration of title to a piece or parcel of land with buildings thereon situate at Aburi and for an injunction restraining the defendants from interfering in any manner whatsoever with his ownership, possession and occupation of the land. The claim is made by the plaintiff in his capacity as head of the family of Adolf Senhard Amankwa Mensah to which the late Martin G. Donkor and Albert Afwireng Donkor belonged. In his statement of claim, the plaintiff pleaded purchase of the said land by the two brothers Martin G. Donkor and Albert Afwireng Donkor from one A. D. Schall. He alleged that the first defendant company filed notices for the sale of the said property and in spite of warnings they wrongfully entered thereon and caused it to be sold in satisfaction of ,a debt due to them from his uncle, the late Martin G. Donkor; and, that the second defendant was the highest bidder at the sale and now claims ,ownership of the property. In the statement of the defence filed on behalf of the defendants the averment of ~he plaintiff that the property was family property purchased by the two brothers was denied, and it was therein averred on the contrary that it was individual self­acquired property of the said Martin G. Donkor.

The plaintiff led oral evidence that the property was purchased jointly by the two brothers, his uncles, the said Albert Afwireng Donkor and Martin G. Donkor, who later built upon it, and that the premises ,became family property upon the death of these two brothers. In support of the oral evidence the plaintiff tendered in evidence an inden­ture of conveyance dated April 18, 1934, executed by one Augusta Deisa Schall of Teshie in favour of the said Albert Afwireng Donkor .and Martin G. Donkor.

It has not been denied that the subject-matter of the indenture is the property now in dispute. The indenture is therefore evidence that the property in dispute is not the individual self-acquired property of Martin G. Donkor, but the joint property of Albert Afwireng Donkor .and the said Martin G. Donkor. If there had been no evidence as to the erection of the building on the land in dispute and the dispute had been in respect of the bare land, it might be said, under Errglish law, that the conveyance to the two brothers created a joint tenancy with

.a right of survivorship such that the survivor of .the two brothers, namely Martin G. Donkor, became the absolute owner on the death -of his joint-owner Albert Afwireng Donkor. But the evidence as to' the erection of the buildings on the land by .the two brothers with the ;assistance of members of their family shows that the two brothers did • not intend that their relationship should be governed by English law. That evidence therefore takes the case completely out of English law .and makes the property one the tenure of which should be governed .by native customary law. 'rhus the purchase of the property by the two brothers and the erection of the buildings upon it by them with the aid of other members of the family make the property family property-the family being the family of their mother-in which the two brothers had only a life interest.

Martin G. Donkor having only a life interest in the property could not .alienate any estate or interest in it which can subsist after his death. 'The evidence is that Martin G. Donkor was dead at the date of the :sale. All that a purchaser at the auction sale acquired was the right, title and interest of Martin G. Donkor in the property. Consequently :since all his right, title and interest in the property determined upon .his death the purchaser who purchased that right, title and interest of .Martin G. Donkor got nothing.


 I am satisfied also upon the evidence that the first defendant com­pany had sufficient warning of the family's ownership of the property before they purported to sell. But, as indicated in their letter of August 31, 1956, it appears that they were content to rely solely upon a mortgage which had been given to them by .the late :Martin G. Donkor without taking the trouble to investigate his title properly or to seek legal advice as to whether the right, title and interest of Martin G. Donkor-if any-in the property still existed at that date.

Both the sale at the instance of Messrs. S.C.O.A., and the purchase by the second defendant therefore constitute trespass and render them liable for damages. But the plaintiff had made no claim for damages; he only claims a declaration of title and an injunction. The plaintiff has fully discharged the onus which the law lays upon him. The defendants have not been able to meet the plaintiff's case. There will therefore be judgment for the plaintiff for a declaration of title and an injunction restraining the defendants from interfering with the plain­tiff family's ownership, possession and occupation of the land in any manner whatsoever.

The assessor agrees with this judgment.

Judgment for the plaintiff.

S. G. D.
