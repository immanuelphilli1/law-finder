                                                    December 21958.

                                            CHANDIRAM v. MENSAH AND ANOTHER. .

                                                          Court of Appeal

                             Korsah C.J., van Lare and Granville Sharp JJ.A    

 

 

Mortgage-Equitable mortgage in favour of bank"-Subsequent attachment under writ of ft. fa.-Purported legal mortgage after attachment in favour of second bank, which thereupon discharged the first bank­ Nature of estate bought by purchaser at the subsequent sale.

Barclays Bank, Takoradi, gave to one Holomah "time credit and/or banking facilities and accommodation"; for this consideration, by a formal memorandum Holomah deposited with the bank the title deeds and other documents in respect of his landed property at Accra. One Mensah was a tenant of premises thereon, paying the rent into Holomah's account. On September 4, 1954, Mr. Franklin (a solicitor who held the title deeds on behalf of Barclays Bank) forwarded the deeds at Holomah's request to the Bank of the Gold Coast (as what is now the Ghana Commercial Bank was then called), for that bank to hold them for and on behalf of Barclays Bank. On September 25, 1954, the property was attached under a writ of ft. fa. issued by the Divisional Court, Sekondi in respect of an unsatisfied judgment-debt owed by Holomah. On October 27, 1954, the Bank of the Gold Coast paid to Barclays Bank the amount due and owing by Holomah, and on the same date Holomah purported to execute a legal mortgage of the attached property to the Bank of the Gold Coast. On April 16, 1955, the attached property was sold to D. T. Chandiram. The Bank of the Gold Coast persuaded Mensah not to attorn tenant to Chandiram, on the argument that what Chandiram had purchased was not the legal estate, but a mere equity of redemption. On September 26, 1956, Chandiram issued a writ in the Land Court, Accra, against Mensah and the Bank of the Gold Coast, the name of the second defendant being formally amended to "Ghana Commercial Bank" on June 25, 1957. The plaintiff claimed:

(1) declaration of title;

(2) £500 general damages for trespass;

(3) £2,025 mesne profits to September 15, 1956, with any further mesne profits accrued to the date of judgment;

(4) recovery of possession.

Held, (i) at the date of the attachment of the property, the legal estate was in Holomah.

(ii) The purported mortgage to the Bank of the Gold Coast, sub­sequent to the attachment, was null and void by virtue of Order 43, rule 7 of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954.

(iii) The purported mortgage being null and void, the legal estate remained in Holomah, and at the sale of the property under writ of ft. fa. it was the legal estate which the purchaser Chandiram bought.

Obiter, this was not a case where the Bank of the Gold Coast paid off Holomah's debts to Barclays Bank in consideration of collecting the title deeds from Barclays Bank with intent to create an equitable mortgage.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. This decision was reversed by the Privy Council on June 20, 1960, on the ground that, although the legal mortgage taken by the Ghana Bank was null and void by reason of Order 43, rule 7, of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, the attachment of September 25, 1954, was subject to the existing equitable mortgage in favour of Barclays Bank, and the Ghana Bank became entitled to the benefit of this equitable mortgage when they paid off Holomah's indebtedness to Barclays Bank. For a full report of the Privy Council decision see the Ghana Law Reports.]

Case referred to :

Dadzie v. Kojo and Another (1940) 6 W.A.C.A. 139.

APPEAL from a decision on September 3, 1957, of Ollennu J. in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, granting a declaration of title to the plaintiff and recovery of possession, £100 damages for trespass and £2,925 mesne profits, as against the defendants jointly and severally.

Lokko for the appellants. Ak1lfO-Addo for the respondent.

VAN LARE J.A. On April 16, 1955, the respondent in this case purchased at a public auction the landed property of one Holomah. The sale was in execution of a writ of fi. fa. properly issued by the Divisional Court, Sekondi, to satisfy a judgment debt against the said Holomah. The attachment of the property under the fi. fa in question was effected on September 25, 1954, and at all material times remained fully effective.

At all times material to the date of attachment, Barclays Bank, Sekondi, had an incumbrance on the property by way of equitable mortgage. The owner, Holomah, in consideration of that bank giving him" time credit and/or banking facilities and accommodation," by a formal memorandum deposited with the aforesaid bank the "!"le deeds and other documents in respect of the attached property. The first appellant (Mensah) was a tenant of the premises, paying his rents into the account of the owner, but, at the request of the second appellant (the Ghana Commercial Bank), he has refused to attorn tenant to the respondent.

The law is clear that a purchaser of a property under the execution of a ft. fa. steps into the shoes of the judgment debtor, and purchases no more than the estate of the judgment debtor in the property. In other words, what is sold and what is bought at a sale in execution is the right, title and interest of the judgment debtor (Dadzie v. Kojo and Another (1)). It therefore follows that, if certain property which is attached under execution by way of ft. fa turns out to be in any way incumbered, the purchaser buys subject to that charge or incumbrance.

The trial of this case would have taken a different turn had Barclays Bank still remained at the date of sale the party interested in the incumbrance. But, due to certain intervening circumstances, Barclays Bank disappeared from the picture, and the Ghana Commercial Bank came into it, creating the difficulties which resulted in the institution of this action.

On September 4, 1954, that is to say on a date before the attach­ment of the property, Mr. Franklin, a solicitor, who held the title deeds on behalf of Barclays Bank, forwarded them at the request of Holomah to the Ghana Commercial Bank, for the latter bank to hold them for and on behalf of Barclays Bank. I quote the relevant portion of this letter:

"Mr. Holomah has requested me to hand these to you, and I have obtained the permission of my client to do so against your undertaking to hold them on their behalf. "

It is to be observed that the undertaking by the Ghana Commercial Bank was not to give credit or loan, but simply to hold the title deeds on behalf of Barclays Bank. It is my view that the reception of the title deeds by the Ghana Commercial Bank created no interest in them, nor can it properly be suggested that Barclays Bank had transferred their interest in the property to the Ghana Commercial Bank. The latter bank held the title deeds merely as custodians for Barclays Bank, in the same way as Mr. Franklin had held them. Whether or not Holomah later turned them into security in favour of the Ghana Commercial Bank is immaterial. I cannot accept the proposition that, by the mere custody of title deeds, the Ghana Commercial Bank became a mortgagee by deposit of title deeds without their becoming Holomah's creditors at that time. No mortgage can be created without a debt, because a mortgage is a thing put into the hands of a creditor.

   According to legal writers, "a mortgage is the creation of an interest in property defeasible (i.e., annullable) upon performing the condition of paying a given sum of money, with interest thereon, at a certain time. This conditional assurance is resorted to when the debt has been incurred, or a loan of money or credit effected, in order to secure either the repayment of the one or the liquidation of the other" (Wharton's Law Lexicon, 14th ed., p. 669).

There is no evidence that Holomah owed money to the appellant bank at the time he caused the title deeds to be forwarded to them, or until the execution of a legal mortgage on October 27, 1954, when the appellant bank paid to Barclays Bank (presumably at Holomah's request) the amount due and owing by Holomah. On the authorities, before Holomah bcame a debtor to the Ghana Commercial Bank, there could be no question of mortgage (equitable or otherwise) between him and that bank. In the absence of a formal assignment of Holomah's debt by Barclays Bank to the Ghana Commercial Bank, it cannot in my opinion be successfully contended that the latter stepped into the shoes of Barclays Bank, who were the only party interested in the equitable charge on the property on the date of sale. The execution of the legal mortgage on October 27, 1954, was a transaction only between Ghana Commercial Bank and Holomah to the exclusion of Barclays Bank, and there can be no question of any transfer of interest by Barclays Bank to the Ghana Commercial Bank.

The appellant bank, therefore, relying on an estate believed to have been created in their favour upon the execution of this legal mortgage on October 27, 1954, in respect of the property in dispute, refused to acknowledge that the ownership of the property had passed to the purchaser Chandiram on April 16, 1955, and would not let the first appellant attorn tenant to him. On the other hand, the appellant bank considered themselves entitled to the legal estate on the date of sale, and collected the rent from the first appellant (who attorned tenant to them) on the assumption that the purchaser was entitled only to an equity of redemption.

What is an equity of redemption? Before 1926 it was the equitable estate or interest left in a person after he had mortgaged his property; but now (that is, since 1926) it is the right to call for a reconveyance of a legal estate, or of an equitable interest in property, from the mortgagee on payment of principal, interest and costs.

The appellants, therefore, believing in the existence of a legal mortgage involving the property in dispute, in their defence to the action instituted against them by the respondent for (a) declaration of title of ownership, (b) damages for trespass, (c) mesne profits and (d) recovery of possession, pleaded that the respondent's action was misconceived, mainly on the grounds that:

(1) on the date of attachment in execution the legal estate in the property was vested in Barclays Bank, and that the only attachable interest of Holomah, the judgment debtor, was an equity of redemption;

(2) the interest of Barclays Bank in the property was transferred during the attachment to the appellant bank when, upon execution of a deed of mortgage on October 27, 1954, between Holomah and the apellant bank, the latter paid Holomah's debts to Barclays Bank, and therefore the only attachable interest of the said Holomah in the property purchased by the respondent was an equity of redemption;

(3) the first appellant attorned tenant to the appellant bank, and paid rents in respect of the property to the appellant bank, which said payments the appellant bank was lawfully entitled to retain in reduction of the debt due by Holomah under the mortgage of October 27, 1954.

It was agreed and settled at the trial that the only issue was: in whom was the legal estate vested (tt the date of sale? The appellants contended that the legal estate was vested in the appellant bank, and not in the purchaser, whose action ought to be dismissed, because (as was contended by the appellants) the respondent had purchased only an equity of redemption.

The right or equity of redemption is an essential attribute of a mortgage, and for the appellant bank to aver that the respondent is entitled to an equity of redemption from them, the appellant bank must presume themselves to have a valid mortgage existing in respect of the property which the respondent purchased. But this is far from the fact, as will appear hereafter.

On October 27, 1954, when the alleged legal mortgage was executed, the property was then under attachment under a due process of law, and by virtue of Order 43, rule 7, of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Holomah (the judgment debtor) was prohibited from alienating the property in any way, including by way of mortgage. Further, by rule 11 of the same Order any such alienation without leave of court, as in this case, was null and void. I have no alternative, therefore, but to hold that the mortgage of October 27, 1954, by the judgment debtor to the appellant bank was null and void, and in the result the said bank could have no interest whatsoever to compete with the estate or interest which the respondent bought at the sale on April 16, 1955.

There can be no doubt that at! the date of sale the legal interest in the property was vested in the original owner, the judgment debtor; and it was exactly that interest which the respondent bought. This is, therefore, a complete answer to the appellants' overall plea, for the legal estate was neither in Barclays Bank nor in the appellants' bank upon attachment of the property. It remained in the original owner, Holomah, the judgment debtor. This appellants' plea, there­fore, that the respondent could only buy (and/or did only buy) an equity of redemption is without support, was wholly misconceived and was rightly rejected by the trial judge. It becomes unnecessary, therefore, to consider whether or not Barclays Bank transferred any interest either before Or during the attachment to the appellant bank.

Before this court, however, Mr. Lokko appeared to have realised his mistake in the stand he took in resisting the action at the trial, and he would have preferred to treat the interest which the judgment debtor had in the property at the date of sale as an equitable charge. Although his argument at the trial was that the appellant bank had a legal interest as against an equitable interest or charge, what he has now argued before us is that it was only the residue of the beneficial interest of the judgment debtor in the property that was sold and bought by the respondent. He put forward a proposition that a stranger who paid off a mortgage must in law be presumed to intend to keep the mortgage alive for his own benefit. He now appears to concede to a declaration of title in favour of the respondent, but argues that it should be subject to the equitable charge which, according to him, was subsisting at the date of attachment. But the question is: was such a charge still subsisting at the date of sale in view of the intervening circumstances? Barclays Bank had disappeared from the picture, and there had been no formal assignment of the debt to the Ghana Commercial Bank.

These are admittedly new issues, and certainly not those on which the appeal is grounded. In his opening before us, Mr. Lokko (for the appellants) said: "The only question in issue is: what property was seized and sold-an equitable interest or a legal interest in the property? "

:Mr. Akufo-Addo for the respondent has submitted (and rightly, in my view) that this is not a case where the appellant bank paid off the debts of Holomah to Barclays Bank in consideration of collecting Barclays Bank's title deeds with intent to create an equitable mortgage. The points now raised by the appellants (namely, that the Ghana Commercial Bank was at the time of attachment an equitable mortgagee in the shoes of Barclays Bank, and must therefore enjoy priority over the respondent purchaser under an execution by the court at the instance of a judgment creditor) certainly did not form the basis of the defendants' case at the trial, and were not canvassed in the court below. I think, however, that in this judgment I have said enough in relation to these points raised. I wish, however, to make it clear that such observations must be regarded as obiter only, in case the appellants or Barclays Bank may be advised to institute other proceedings concerning such alleged equitable interest or charge in or on the property purchased by the respondent.

In the result the trial judge was right in decreeing that the respondent was entitled to a declaration of title of ownership to the property in dispute. This legal estate the appellants had unsuccess­fully resisted; by such resistance they challenged the respondent's right to possession, thereby rendering themselves liable to damages for trespass. I would therefore confirm the judgment appealed from in this respect, and also the decree for recovery of possession. With regard to the mesne profits, however, it is admitted that there was an arithmetical error in the learned judge's calculation, which must be corrected. It is agreed that the total of such profits in respect of which the respondent is entitled to judgment should be £2,175 and not £2,925. Subject to such variation I would dismiss the appeal.

KORSAH C.J. I agree.

GRANVILLE SHARP J.A. I also agree.

Appeals dismissed, subject to correction of arithmetical error in mesne profits.
