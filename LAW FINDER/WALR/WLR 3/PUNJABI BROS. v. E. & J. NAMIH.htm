                                                          April 9, 1958.

                                        PUNJABI BROS. v. E. & J. NAMIH

                                              High Ashanti Judicial Division, Divisional Court     

                                                                   Sarkodee-Adoo J

.

 

Practice-Injunctions-Interlocutory injunctions-Factors to be considered by the Oourt-Civil Procedure Rules, 1954, Ord. 50, r. 3.

Punjabi Bros., merchants of Kumasi, by an agreement in writing dated March 27, 1947, became tenants of Elias and John Namih in respect of a store in House No. 143-145, Bogyawi Street, Kumasi.

On November 5, 1957, the landlords obtained judgment against the tenants in the Divisional Court, Kumasi, for immediate recovery of pos­session. On the following day the tenants filed an appeal, but on November 14 the tenants paid to the landlords the sum of £3,500, and the parties executed an agreement in writing, the operative part of which was as follows:

•• 1. That the said Namih Brothers agree to permit the said Punjabi Brothers to occupy the said premises rent free for three (3) months from the date of this Agreement.

2. That in consideration of the above the said Punjabi Brothers under­take to occasion no further legal proceedings in respect of the said premises and also undertake to withdraw the Notice of Appeal filed in the Suit Elias and John Namih versus Punjabi Brothers and to abandon the appeal therein to the Ghana Court of Appeal, thus putting an end to the litigation.

3. That at the end of the said period of three (3) months the said Namih Brothers undertake to grant the said Punjabi Brothers a sub­lease of the said premises provided that the terms and conditions thereof are mutually satisfactory."

On February 25, 1958, the landlords wrote to the tenants as follows:

" Further to our letter of 19th February, 1958 we have to state that as we have not reached any agreement mutually satisfactory to both of us, it is not possible for our solicitor to prepare a sub-lease. We have in the circumstances decided to ask you to surrender possession of the pre­mises to us forthwith and hereby notify you that unless you quit by the 27th February, 1958, we shall be compelled to have recourse to legal process. "

On March 28, 1958, the landlords ejected the tenants, and removed the latter's goods from the premises. The next day the tenants issued a writ against the landlords for a declaration that they (the tenants) were statutory tenants, for recovery of possession, for specific performance of the agreement of November 14, 1957, and for the return of the £3,500. They also filed notice of an ex parte motion

"(a) for leave to serve on the defendants herein, along with the writ of summons and before the time limited for appearance of defendants, notice of motion praying this Honourable Court for an interim order of injunction restraining the defendants, their agents, servants, assigns, representatives and successors from further trespass to the premises the subj~t-matter of the suit herein,

"(b) for an interim order of injunction in similar terms pending the hearing of the said motion."

Held, (i) in dealing with an application for an interlocutory injunc­tion in such circumstances, the court does not attempt to determine the rights of the parties, but merely seeks to keep the property in its existing vcondition until the legal rights can be established.

(ii) The applicant for an interlocutory injunction must show a strong prima facie case in support of the title which he asserts, and must make out a probability of the respondent's case failing.

(iii) Where (as in the instant case) the respondent disputes the legal title of the applicant to the property in dispute, the court will seldom (however clear the case may, in its opinion, be) grant an injunction before the applicant has established his legal right.

(iv) In dealing with such applications the court must consider the relative convenience or inconvenience which might result to the parties from granting or withholding an interlocutory injunction. The respon­dents being in de jure and de facto possession, on that criterion the hardship would be greater upon them if the applicants failed to establish their title.

APPLICATION for an injunction to restrain the respondents in posses­sion of certain premises from continuing to trespass thereon, pending determination of a suit wherein the applicants sought recovery of possession.

OWU8U (with him Henry Prempeh) for the applicants. Hayfron-Benjamin (with him Swanzy) for the respondents.

SARKODEE-ADOO J. The power of the court to issue an injunction is not limited to the circumstances mentioned in Order 50 of the Rules of the Supreme Court, but extends to the protection of legal rights to property from damage pending litigation. There is an inherent jurisdiction to issue an interim injunction when this appears necessary in order to preserve the status quo pending the hearing and determina­tion of a suit. This appears especially necessary in a genuine suit for trespass, where much may depend on proof of possession.

In the instant matter, however, the respondents have recovered possession of the Four-Door Store in dispute, by a writ of possession under a judgment in their favour, so that possession is not radically or genuinely in dispute. They dispute any equitable right, interest or legal title of the applicants, and they further deny the creation and/or existence of a tenancy vis-a-vis the relationship of landlord and tenant between them.

It is unnecessary to make a finding on these points one way or

. the other on this motion, for in dealing with an application in such circumstances for an interlocutory injunction the court does not attempt to determine the right of the parties, but merely seeks to keep the property in its existing condition until the legal rights can be estab­lished (vide, Harman v. Jones (1), Black Point Syndicate, Ltd. v. Eastern Ooncessions, Ltd. (2), Leney & Sons, Ltd. v. Oallingham and Thompson (3), Jones v. Pacaya Rubber & Produce 00., Ltd. (4)).

The court, then, interferes on the assumption that the party who seeks its interference has the legal right which he asserts, but needs the aid of the court for the protection of the property in question until the legal right can be established. In consequence, a man who seeks the aid of the court must be able to show a strong prima facie case in support of the title which he asserts. He is not required to make out a clear legal title, but he must satisfy the court that he has a fair question to raise as to the existence of the legal right which he sets up. The court must, before disturbing a respondent's legal right, or stripping him of any of the rights with which the law has clothed him, be satisfied that the probability is in favour of his case ultimately failing in the final issue of the substantive suit (vide, 18 Halsbury (2nd ed.), pp. 28, 29; Kerr on Injunctions (5th ed.), pp. 16, 17).

A man who seeks the aid of the court by way of interlocutory injunction, must, as a rule, be able to satisfy the court that its interference is necessary before he can establish his legal right upon trial, in order to protect him from that species of injury which the court calls irreparable. By the term " irreparable injury" it is not meant that there must be no physical possibility of repairing the injury; all that is meant is that the injury would be a material one, and one which could not be adequately remedied by damages. By the term

" inadequacy of the remedy by damages" is meant that the remedy by damages is not such a compensation as will in effect, though not in specie, replace the parties in the position in which they formerly stood.

This application must be concluded upon the evidence before the court and must not be based upon moral or sentimental considerations. The applicants called Mr. Amankwatia in support of their application. From Mr. Amankwatia's evidence I find it difficult to discern any nexus between the £3,500 and the agreement. On the evidence (written and oral before the court, and counsel for the parties having been heard, it is apparent that the proper course for the court to follow is that indicated in Kerr on Injunctions (5th ed.), p. 26:

"If the defendant disputes the legal title of the plaintiff or denies the fact of its violation the court would seldom, however clear the case might in its opinion be, grant an injunction without putting the ·plaintiff to establish his legal right."

The result of such motions as this must turn upon the relative convenience or inconvenience which might result to the parties from granting or withholding the injunction. In the instant case the balance is in the respondents' favour, as being in de jure and de facto posses­sion as owners, for the hardship is greater upon them if the applicants fail to establish their title (vide, Bramwell v. Halcomb (5».

For these reasons the injunction must be refused.

Application dismissed.
