                                                       MILLS v. ADDY

High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

(Ollennu J.

This was an action between members of an Accra Ga family concerning the ownership of land. Both plaintiff and defendant were descendants of a common ancestress, Adaku Mansah. The deceased father of the de­fendant, by name Amoo Addy, had been the son of Amorkor, who was one of Adaku Mansah's three daughters. The plaintiff was the daughter of another of Adaku Mansah's daughters and thus had been a first cousin of Amoo Addy. At the time of this action there was no duly appointed head of the family. Amoo Addy had been the head but no-one had been appointed to succeed him. He was not survived by any male descendants of Adaku Mansah of the same generation as himself: the plaintiff was the most senior female descendant of the same generation, was acknow­ledged as such and had been authorised by the principal members of the family to institute this action on the family's behalf.

The plaintiff's claim was for a declaration of title to two properties which, she averred, had been the self-acquired property of her grand­mother, Adaku Mansah, and were thus now the property of the family she (the plaintiff) represented. The defendant, who denied the plaintiff's right to institute the action on the ground that she was not the head oi the family, claimed that the houses had been the self-acquired property not of Adaku Mansah but of her daughter (his grandmother) Amorkor. He claimed further that because his father Amoo Addy had been the only child of Amorkor, by Ga custom her self-acquired property passed ab­solutely to his father. His own claim to the properties he based on a specific devise of them to him by his father or, if the devise was ineffective, on the fact that as a child of a six-cloth marriage, he had a definite interest in his father's property. In reply the plaintiff claimed that even if the properties had been self-acquired by Amorkor instead of by Adaku Mansah, the direct line of inheritance from the former (the female line) had failed on the death of Amoo Addy (he having no sisters) and that therefore the properties had passed into the wider family of Amorkor's mother's descendants.

Held, (i) where there is no duly appointed head of a family, or wherethe duly appointed head is absent, the right to represent the family, to take charge of the family property and generally to exercise the powers of head of the family is exercisable by the oldest male member of the most senior generation. In the absence of a male in that generation these powers of head of the family are exercisable by the oldest female of the same generation. As no appointment of head of the Adaku Mansah family had been made after the death of Amoo Addy and, as the plaintiff was the eldest surviving female member of the most senior surviving generation of the family (there being no male members of the family), she was the person to exercise the powers of head of the family and was entitled to do so.

(ii) In the matrilineal areas of Ghana every woman who, being married, has children, originates a family. The family so originated is a branch of the wider family to which the originator belongs. The self­acquired property of such an originator, dying intestate, becomes the family property of her family (although it may be subject to prior life interests in the mother and the collaterals of the deceased). Upon the failure of her family and the sub-families created within it by her daughters or remoter female issue in the female line, this family property reverts to the wider family of which the originator was a member: thus, "once family property, always family property"-the fact that the members of a class entitled for the time being to the enjoyment of family property are reduced to one does not cause the property to lose its character as family property and become the absolute property of that person.

(iii) Where, in matrilineal areas, family property is enjoyed by mem­bers of a faniily, the nature of the interests of each member therein is that of a joint life interest for the lives of all of them, continuing as a life interest for the survivor of them. The property concerned cannot be disposed of by such life tenants or tenant, either inter vivos or by testa­mentary disposition or otherwise. The contraction of a marriage under the Marriage Ordinance (or in Accra, of a customary Ga six-cloth marriage), by a male life tenant followed by the procreation of children within such a marriage in no wise affects this disposition of the property for such children are not members of the family to which their father belonged and, although they have rights in their father's self-acquired property they can thus have no rights in the family property.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Although the life tenants or tenant have no power to dispose of the property in which they have such limited interest it is clearly established that they have power to dispose of their individual interests: see Mensah v. S.C.O.A. and Boahene (1958) 3 W.A.L.R. 336. However, it is well established that arrangements for the enjoyment of family property are a voluntary matter for the members jointly entitled to the enjoyment of that property and a dispute within the family as to such arrangements gives no cause of action as' between one member and another. (It is otherwise if one member of a family-e.g., the head-denies that other members have any interest in family property.) It will thus be seen that it is in the interests of one to whom an alienation of a limited interest'is made to secure the concurrence to the transaction of the other members of the family enjoying concurrent limited interests. Ii this is not done a grantee of a limited interest (such as a life interest) can well find his right of action against his grantor to be valueless since he can, at best, be no more than subrogated to the position of his grantee and as such will have no right of action to enforce his interest against other interested persons. Again their limited powers here should not be confused with their power to deal in family property whilst it is in their control as, for example, by varying the investment of the property: confusion has been caused in this respect by some authorities who have loosely spoken of family property as being inalienable.

In Nelson v. Ocansey and Tackie (1950) unreported, referred to by the learned judge, there are dicta that suggest that the self-acquired property of a woman which becomes the family property of her children upon her death intestate, will, as an invariable rule, be administered for these children by a member of the wider family to which the deceased belonged. On the other hand, the learned judge in the present case suggests, obiter, that the head of the wider family appoints one of the children as successor and administrator. These in turn differ from Be Eburahim, deceased; A nsah v. A nkrah (1958) 3 W.A.L.R. 317, where it was ruled that a successor to administer the property will be appointed by the children from among their number.]

The decision here reported was appealed from, and the appeal was heard by the Court of Appeal on January 26, 1960. The judgment in the Court of Appeal reads as follows: "This appeal is dismissed for the reasons given in the full and able judgment of Ollennu J., which must be taken to be incorporated in the judgment of this Court."

Akufo-Addo for the plaintiff. Bentsi Enchill for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. dealt with the facts outlined above and continued:

Upon the pleadings two main issues were raised for determination.

They are: whether the plaintiff is entitled to sue on behalf of the Adaku Mansah family; and whether the properties in dispute are the property of Adaku Mansah's family, whether acquired by the said Adaku Mansah herself, or by her daughter, Amorkor Hammond. The first is a question of mixed law and fact. The second is purely a question of law, i.e., native law and custom.

Ey consent the evidence in chief of the plaintiff and of her witness were heard on February 24, 1958, and the case was adjourned to February 27, 1958, with liberty to Mr. Enchill, counsel for the defen­dant, to have each of them recalled for cross-examination. When the case was called on February 27, 1958, Mr. Enchill submitted that evidence was not necessary for a determination of the second issue, and that there was no point in taking any further evidence on the first issue, since a decision on the second issue would determine the whole matter. Mr. Akufo Addo agreed to this; I therefore heard arguments of counsel. the evidence led, the portions relevant for a ruling on the legal argument are as follows: Adaku Mansah, grandmother of the plaintiff and great grandmother of the defendant, had six children: three daughters, Ameley, Amorkor and Amakai, and three sons, Teiko, Boi and Komey. Amorkor had an only child, E. G. Amoo-Addy, the father of the defendant. The plaintiff is a daughter of Amakai, and is the eldest or most senior surviving direct uterine grandchild of Adaku Mansah. There is no direct male uterine descendant of Adaku Mansah in the same category as the plaintiff. At the time of his death, Amoo­Addy was the eldest direct uterine descendant of Adaku Mansah and was the head of the family.

Owing to certain events which occurred after the death of Amoo Addy, no-one has been formally appointed head of the family to succeed Amoo Addy, but the plaintiff is acknowledged by all members of the family as the head, and is authorised by all principal members of the family to institute this action. So much for the facts.

By native custom, absence of a duly elected head of the family, whatever the cause of that absence, is not permitted to prejudice the interest of the family. Therefore in the absence of a duly appointed head, the eldest male member of the family, and failing him, the eldest female member, acts as head of the family and takes charge of family property. See Sarbah, Fanti Oustomary Law (1896), p. 38. Thus, although the plaintiff has not been formally appointed head of the family, she is by native custom the acting head of the family and the proper person to represent the family. That disposes of the first issue.

On the second issue Mr. Akufo Addo for the plaintiff submitted that even if the properties were the self-acquired properties of Amorkor Hammond, the mother of the defendant's father, they are properties in which the defendant's father had only a determinable and not an absolute estate and, upon his death and the failure of direct uterine descendants of Amorkor Hammond, the property remained the property of the maternal family of the same category or status, as does the self-acquired property of a male member of the family who dies in­testate. In support of that submission learned counsel referred the court to the judgment of M'Carthy J. in Nunoo v. Ole land (1) and to the judgment delivered by me in Acquaye v. Deedei and Orabbe (2).

Mr. Enchill for the defendant referred the court to the opinion expressed by Mr. Edmund Bannerman, as reported in Sarbah's Fanti Oustomary Law (1896), p. 109, where Mr. Bannerman in answer to the court expressed the opinion that:

" By Ga custom self acquired property of a woman descends to her children and their children, but failing those, to her brothers and sisters."

Counsel submitted that the words "children and their children" should be interpreted in their ordinary meaning, and that when so interpreted they would include both male and female children. Learned counsel submitted that upon that construction the defendant, being a child of the only child of Amorkor Hammond, the original owner of the property, became the sole owner of the property upon the death of his said father Amoo-Addy.

In the alternative learned counsel submitted that upon the death intestate of Amorkor Hammond her self-acquired property vested exclusively in her only son Amoo-Addy as his self-acquired property, which the said Amoo-Addy could dispose of as he liked during his lifetime, and devise under his will; and that upon his death intestate, his children by a Ga six-cloth marriage would, by Ga custom, have an interest in the said property, or that the property would be affected by the provisions of the Marriage Ordinance regarding succession if he were married under the Ordinance.

I shall now review the authorities on the subject. The question put by Hutchinson C.J. to which Mr. Edmund Bannerman gave the answer referred to by Mr. Enchill in Sarbah's Fanti Customary Law is as follows:

" Does a woman's property, acquired by her, descend in the same way (i.e., as man's) or do her children inherit it before her brothers and sisters? "

The answer given is as follows:

"A woman's property acquired by herself descends to her children and their children; failing them to brothers or sisters according to age."

The question presupposes that brothers and sisters have an interest in the self-acquired property of their deceased sister, and the purpose of the question is to ascertain whether that interest vests in the brothers and sisters for immediate enjoyment upon the death of the sister, or whether it is postponed to vest upon certain contingencies. And the answer in my opinion indicates that the immediate beneficial interest devolves upon and remains in her children and their children, but upon failure at any stage of such children and their children the beneficial interest vests in brothers and sisters.

The case of Nunoo v. Cleland (1) is of great assistance in this matter.

It was a suit to determine the successor to the estate of one Mrs. Glover Addo. She died intestate, without a child, seised in possession of certain real properties under two deeds of gift made in favour of her­s~lf, her brothers and sisters, and their mother. At the time of her death she was the survivor of all the donees under both deeds, and therefore the sole owner of the said properties. There was no direct uterine family starting with herself, or even with her mother or her grandmother either, who could be considered. In those circumstances the court went two and three steps higher up to the wider uterine family originating with her great grandmother, Amakai, and held that the properties Mrs. Glover Addo died seised of, became the property of the family of her great grandmother Amakai, and became vested in the head of that wider family, to be administered by him.

      In the case of Nelson v. Ocansey and Tackie (3) a woman died possessed of self-acquired property. She was survived by two sisters, a son and a daughter (who was the co-defendant). Both her children were of age at the date of her death. It was held that although by Ga custom her child_ren inherited the property and were the persons immediately entitled to the beneficial interest in it, yet by native custom the sisters were her successors, and had some interest in the property, and it was not until the death of the last of the sisters that her children could, by themselves, exercise over it the control which the successor or head of the family usually exercises over family property. In other words, while the sisters of the deceased lived, the eldest of the sisters and eventually the survivor of them was the head of the family and administered the property for the benefit of the children even though they were the inheritors of the deceased's self­acquired property and were of age.

       In the case of Acquaye v. Deedei and Crabbe (2) applying the prin­ciple in Nelson v. Ocanscy and Tackie (3), I held as follows:

     " By native custom a woman's self-acquired property descends to her children upon her death intestate .... Upon the death of the last surviving child, that -property, by Accra custom, becomes the property of the maternal family of that woman: children of her sons are not members of that family and have no interest in the grandmother's property."

       Sarbah in his book on Fanti Customary Law (1896), at pp. 102 and 103, states the principle of succession as follows:

       " The owner of self-acquired real property dying intestate is not succeeded by his sons, they being outside the line of inheri­tance, but by his mother and her issue according to seniority." There are four kinds of successors, viz.: Real, Proper, Ordinary and Extraordinary. The Real successor of a person is his mother.

       We call those persons Proper successors who are the uterine brothers and sisters of the deceased, and the issue of such sisters, but never can the pedigree be traced out in the line of the male."

      The paragraph cited from Sarbah relates to succession in areas where the family is the maternal family. Accra, as distinct from the rest of the Ga State, is one of those areas.

        From consideration of the authorities cited above, and from the general principles of native custom regarding succession, I have come to the following conclusions on the native custom: every woman becomes the originator of a family. The family she so originates is a branch of her mother's or grandmother's family; that branch family remains forever part of the trunk of her mother's and grandmother's family. 'Whenever the branch ceases to grow, there is reversion or retracing of steps to the trunk family. When a person, male or female, dies intestate his or her self-acquired property becomes family property to be administered by the head of his family. The head of the family by custom entrusts that property, as distinct from ancestral property, to the" real" or the" proper" successor as the case may be. The question of who constitutes that family, and in the absence of the "real" successor-the mother-the question of who are the " proper" successors, are questions to be decided in each particular case upon well-defined principles of native custom. In the case of a woman, the proper successors in the family are her children; and her children's children in the female line. The words" children's children" should be construed to mean children's children who belong to the family and are by custom in the line of succession, therefore where the family is the maternal family, the phrase children's children can only mean the " children of daughters." Upon failure of " proper" successors, suc­cession is taken a step higher up the tree to the trunk family of the real successor, i. e., mother of the original owner of the property, and where necessary a step higher still to the trunk family of the grand­mother of the original owner.

It is only upon failure to trace any such maternal family trunk that recourse can be had to descendants of male members of the family: see Ankrah and Another v. Aryeh and Others (4).

The interest which children take in the self-acquired property of their mother upon her death intestate is not an interest by purchase or of absolute ownership; it is only a life interest determinable upon the life of each such child. The beneficial interest in such property is enjoyed by direct descendants in the female line; but upon failure of such children and grandchildren in the female line, the property assumes the character of ancestral property and must be under the direct control and management of the head of the immediate family, or of the wider family, as the case may be, of the original owner.

By native custom, the defendant, as a child of the male issue of Amorkor Hammond, is not a member of Amorkor Hammond's family; consequently he does not come within the category of "children's children" who are entitled to succeed. Amoo Addy had only a life interest in the property of his mother Amorkor Hammond; he is not a purchaser of the properties, consequently they will not, upon his death, be affected by the provisions of the Marriage Ordinance or of the incidents of Ga six-cloth marriage. Therefore, upon the death of Amoo Addy, the only child of Amorkor Hammond, the properties came under the control of the sisters and brothers of Amorkor, or the children of Amorkor's sisters, that is, they became properties of Amorkor Hammond's maternal family. The plaintiff is a child of Amorkor's sister Amakai, and is therefore, upon the authorities and the native custom, the present successor to and head of the family of Adaku Mansah, and oCAmorkor Hammond

. In the circumstances I rule that whether acquired by Adaku Mansah or by her daughter Amorkor Hammond, the properties in dispute are now the property of the Adaku Mansah family, and that the family, acting by the plaintiff, are entitled to the remedies they seek.

There will therefore be judgment for the plaintiff for a declaration of her family's title to the two premises; an injunction restraining the defendant, his servants and agents from interfering with the family in their ownership, possession and occupation of the said premises; and recovery of possession of the said premises.

Judgment for the plaintiff.

S. G. D.
