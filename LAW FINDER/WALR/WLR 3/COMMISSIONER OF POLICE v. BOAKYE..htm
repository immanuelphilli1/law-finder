                                                    December 21958.

                                       COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. BOAKYE.

                             High Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                           Smith J    

 

 

Criminal Law-Appeals-Amendment of charge- Effect of failure of court to call upon accused to answer to amended charge-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10, s. 172 (1).

Criminal Law-Evidence-Conspiracy-Further charge of aiding and abetting-Whether acquittal on conspiracy charge necessarily requires acquittal of aiding and abetting.

Joe Boakye (and another) were charged before Mr. Charles, Senior Magistrate, Kumasi, on various counts. Those charged against Boakye were (1) conspiring with the other accused to steal, (2) falsifying ac­counts, and (3) stealing (with the other accused) goods worth £280. He pleaded Not Guilty to all charges.

After nine prosecution witnesses had been heard, and at a later hearing, the Magistrate recorded the following note on his proceedings:

" At this stage prosecutor applies to Court to amend charges and substitute another charge sheet with amendments.

"Court shows amended charge to Mr. Lassey counsel for No. 1 accused. Counsel for No. 2 accused is absent. Court adjourns for Mr. Lassey to study amended charges.

"Court resumes and Mr. Lassey informs court that he has no objection to amendment. Mr. Owusu Afriyie who now appears also informs court that he has no objection to the amendment. Court also enquires from both counsel whether their clients are prejudiced, in which case court would be willing to grant an adjournment, and both counsel inform court that their clients are in no way pre­judiced by the amendment and that they are prepared to continue the hearing of the case today.

" Court informs both counsel of their right to recall any witness for cross-examination."

So far as Boakye was concerned, the amended charges :

(1) particularised the conspiracy charge by giving the value of the goods to be stolen as £280, and laying the property in the United Africa Company, Ltd.,

(2) particularised the falsification, and

(3) substituted (for the charge of stealing) a charge of aiding and abetting, with the other accused, some person unknown in stealing.

When the charge is altered in such circumstances, section 172 (1) of the Criminal Procedure Code provides that " the court shall thereupon call upon the accused person to plead to the altered charge." In the instant case the Magistrate did not comply with this requirement.

In his judgment the learned Magistrate observed, in acquitting Boakye on the charge of conspiracy with the first accused but convicting Boakye on the charge of aiding and abetting stealing:

"Mr. Owusu Afriyie submitted that as Nos. 1 and 2 accused were discharge on the 4th count for conspiracy to steal goods to the value of £280 the No. 2 accused could not be found guilty on the 6th count of aiding and abetting, and in support of his submission he cited the case of Beach &. Owens [1957] Crim.L.R., October, at p. 687. In my opinion the facts of this case can be distinguished from the facts of the case cited, because in Beach tf; Owens the evidence did not establish a conspiracy, whereas in the instant case the evidence certainly did establish a conspiracy, by No. 2 accused with another person (or persons) unknown, to steal. I had to dis­charge No. 2 accused on the conspiracy charge because he was not charged with conspiracy with person or persons unknown. If No.2 accused was charged with conspiring with person or persons un­known, I would have found No. 2 accused guilty on the conspiracy count.

"I therefore find No. 2 accused guilty on the 6th count and I convict him accordingly."

Boakye appealed to the High Court, where his grounds of appeal included the following:

"1. That the learned trial Magistrate by failing neglecting or omitting to take the pleas of the accused persons, when the charges were altered or amended, acted contrary to section 172 (1) of the Criminal Procedure Code, which is fatal to the conviction.

"2. That the purported conviction in count 6 is bad in law in that when the learned trial Magistrate acquitted the appeHant on the charge of conspiracy to steal as contained in count 4 he ought to have acquitted the appellant on count 6 which is aiding and abetting stealing."

Held, (i) the omission to take a fresh plea on the amendment of a charge is not necessarily fatal to a conviction. The criterion is whether or not that omission has resulted in a miscarriage of justice, and in the instant case there had been no such miscarriage. Commissioner of Police v. Bonsu and Others (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 334 applied.

(ii) Where the facts which have to be proved on a conspiracy charge are the same as those which have to be proved on a count joined with it, and the evidence on the conspiracy charge is the same as that on the other charge, an acquittal on the former charge means an acquittal on the latter. In the instant case the facts to be proved on the charge of conspiring with the first accused were not the same as those to be proved on the charge of aiding and abetting a person or persons un­known in stealing, and an acquittal on the former did not preclude a conviction on the latter.

Cases referred to :

(1) R. v. Eronini (1953) 14 W.A.C.A. 366.

(2) Commissioner of Police v. Bonsu and Others (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 334. (3) Commissioner of Police v. Williams (1948) W.A.C.A. Cyclostyled

Reports, May-July 1948, p. 54.

(4) R. v. Beach and Owens unreported: C.L.R., Oct., 1957.

ApPEAL from a decision of the Senior Magistrate, Kumasi, on June 30, 1958, convicting the appellant of aiding and abetting stealing.

Afriyie for the appellant.

A missah for the Commissioner of Police.

SMITH J. There are two main points taken in this appeal. The first is the omission of the Senior Magistrate to take a fresh plea from the accused when the charge against him was altered from one of stealing to one of aiding and abetting stealing. Mr. Owusu Afriyie submitted that section 172 (1) of the Criminal Procedure Code is mandatory in this respect. At first sight it appears to be so. Further, Mr. Afriyie cited in support of his contention the case of R. v. Eronini (1), a case which seems unanswerable.

Learned counsel for the Crown, however, referred me to the case of Commissioner of Police v. Bonsu and Others (2). In the latter case the Court of Appeal referred to another case, which had been decided by the West African Court of Appeal (Commissioner of Police v. Wil­liams (3)), which is in conflict with Eronini. In Commissioner of Police v. Bonsu and Others the Court of Appeal followed Williams' case, and considered that the criterion was whether or not the omission to take a fresh plea had resulted in a miscarriage of justice. Applying that principle to the case before me, it is quite clear from what took place between the Magistrate and counsel for the two accused persons at the trial that there was no conceivable question of any miscarriage of justice.

As regards ~he second ground of appeal, the Magistrate acquitted the appellant of conspiracy with the other accused because, having discharged the first accused of conspiracy, he was bound in law to discharge the second accused also. He said in his judgment, however, that he would have convicted the second accused (the appellant) if ~he charge had been that he had conspired with a person or persons un­known to steal the goods.

 

I agree with the distinction made by the Magistrate between the case he had before him and the case of R. v. Beach and Owens (4), in which it was held that where the facts which had to be proved on a conspiracy charge were the same as those which had to be proved on a count joined with it, and where the evidence on the conspiracy charge was the same as the evidence on the other charge, an acquittal on the conspiracy charge means an acquittal on the other. In the instant case, the facts to be proved on a charge of conspiring with the other accused to steal, were not the same as the facts to be proved on a charge of aiding and abetting a person or persons unknown to steal. An acquittal on the one did not preclude a conviction on the other.

On the facts there was evidence to support the conviction, and I accordingly dismiss the appeal.

Appeal dismissed.
