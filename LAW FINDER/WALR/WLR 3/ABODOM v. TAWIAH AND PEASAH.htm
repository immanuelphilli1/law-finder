                                                             June 17,1957.

                                     ABODOM v. TAWIAH AND PEASAH

High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

Ollennu J

 

Fraud-Alienation in fraud of creditors-Sale to purchaser for value without notice-Whether sale can be set aside.

This was an appeal by a purchaser of land from an order of a Native Court setting aside the sale on the ground that it was invalid as being in defraud of the vendor's judgment creditor. From the evidence at the hearing before the Native Court it appeared that the sale had been made with the concurrence of those persons whose consents were necessary to validate the transaction, that it had been carried through with due publicity and that the purchaser had given value. There was no evidence to show that the purchaser had any knowledge of the position of the vendor and of the liability of his property to attachment.

Held, notwithstanding any fraud there may have been by the vendor towards his creditors, the purchaser was a bona fide purchaser for value who, taking without notice, must therefore be protected in his purchase.

APPEAL from a decision by the Akyem Abuakwa Native Court " A.2 " on March 1, 1957, in an action by a judgment creditor. Opoku Afare for the judgment creditor plaintiff. Boateng for the defendant purchaser.

OLLENNU J. The main point in this appeal is whether the sale of the farm in dispute by the judgment debtor to the claimant was made in defraud of the plaintiff judgment creditor. The Native Court found it was.

In doing so they misdirected themselves, as appears on the face of their judgment, on the principles applicable; because the grounds upon which they based their decision show that they concerned themselves solely with the evidence showing fraud on the part of the vendor. They did not give sufficient consideration to the evidence which shows that 'the claimant acted bona fide and gave valuable consideration for the transfer of the property to him, and had no knowledge of the fraud of the judgment debtor. In matters of transfer of property in defraud of creditor, it is essential to prove either that there was no consideration for the transfer, or that the purchaser had notice of the creditor's debt and knew, or ought in the particular circumstance to have known, that the purpose of the transfer to him was to deprive the creditor of his right to fall upon the property transferred to satisfy his debts.

As counsel for the respondent admitted, the evidence shows, on the face of it, that the sale to, the claimant was made in the presence of responsible citizens and with the consent and concurrence of the chief of the village whose consent and concurrence in sale of land in the area is requisite by native custom. There is evidence that the claimant paid value for the transfer. On the other hand there was no evidence that he was aware at the time of the transaction that the vendor was indebted to the plaintiff or to any person, or that any action was pending in court against the vendor, the result of which might cause the property to be attached in execution.

That being the case the only conclusion that I can come to on the evidence is that the claimant is a bona fide purchaser for value without notice. He cannot therefore suffer loss of the property no matter how clearly the fraud of the defendant may be established.

If the Native Court had properly directed themselves on this important principle, it is not certain they would have given judgment against the claimant, and I having directed myself to that principle hold that there was a valid sale to the claimant and that title to the property was vested in the claimant at the date of the attachment, and that at the date of the said attachment the defendant judgment debtor had no attachable interest of any sort in the property.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
