                                                                               July 11, 1957

                                       COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. WEMAH.

                                                                              Court of Appeal

                                           Quashie-Idun Ag.C.J., Manyo Plange and van Lare JJ.

 

 

Criminal Law-Extortion-Distinction between extortion and corruption by public officers-Meaning of expression "under colour of his office" ­Criminal Code, c. 9 0/1951, ss. 413 and 437.

The appellant, a member of a municipal council and thus, by statute, a public officer, had obtained money from a number of persons on the strength of representations made by him to them that he could, by virtue of his position, authorise or arrange for the authorisation of their build­ing projects. In fact the duty of authorising building is statutorily vested in municipal councils and an individual councillor has no power to give such authorisations. The appellant was convicted of extortion for unlawfully demanding money "under colour of his office." On the learning of the appeal it was argued for the appellant that he had not represented himself as having a duty to perform (in the discharge of which duty he might be influenced by being given money) and therefore he could not be said to have acted "under colour of his office" within the meaning attached to this phrase by the Criminal Code, s. 437 .

. The Criminal Code, s. 413, reads: "Every public officer or juror who is guilty of corruption, or of wilful oppression, or of extortion, in respect of the duties of his office ... " Extortion is explained in section 437, where the phrase" in respect of the duties of his office" does not appear a second time. Extortion arises where a public officer, "under colour of his office, demands or obtains from any person ... any money or valuable consideration which he knows that he is not lawfully authorised to demand or obtain ... " The explanation as to corruption contained in section 429 reads: "A public officer, juror or voter is guilty of corruption in respect of the duties of his office or vote, if he directly or indirectly agrees. or offers to permit his conduct to be influenced ... "

Held, (i) where a public officer has a duty to perform and it is alleged that he allowed his decision given in discharging that duty to be influenced, that is, where corruption is alleged, it is essential to prove the duty concerned. Where, on the other hand, extortion is alleged, the essence of the offence is that the accused has used his occupancy of an office, either by representing or misrepresenting his duties or powers, to obtain advantage. This is acting "under colour of his office" and it follows that it is not necessary to show any particular duty owed within, or by virtue of the office.

Commissioner of Police v. Appiah, W.A.C.A., June 10, 1947, un­reported, followed.

R. v. Minimah (1940) 6 W.A.C.A. 192, distinguished.

APPEAL from a decision of Windsor-Aubrey J. in the Divisional Court, Accra, on April 26, 1957, rejecting an appeal from a conviction, for extortion by a public officer, in the Magistrate's Court, Accra.

Akiwumi, Crown counsel, for the Crown. A. Asafu-Adjaye for the appellant.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by VAN LARE J.:

This is an appeal from the decision of Windsor-Aubrey J., dismissing an appeal by the appellant against his conviction by the Magistrate's Court, Accra, on four counts of extortion, contrary to the Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 413.

The appellant was a councillor of the Accra Municipal Council for the ward covering Nima, and as such a public officer. Under the Municipal Councils Ordinance, c. 9 of 1953, s. 67 (1):

" a Council may, with the prior approval of the Minister, make regulations for the control, under permit or otherwise, of the con­struction of buildings and other structures and of the execution of work on existing buildings and structures, and may by such regula­tions prescribe the conditions subject to which the construction of buildings and other structures and the execution of work on existing buildings and structures may be undertaken and carried out. "

It is unnecessary to point out that no single member of a council. can take decisions, which must be by a majority of the members present and voting thereon at a meeting of the council; but according to the evidence the appellant told the inhabitants of Nima that buildings were not allowed without his permission and that if they paid him certain sums of money he would see the council officials and obtain authorisation for such buildings. As found by the learned trial Senior Magistrate, some persons wishing to erect buildings in the area went to see the appellant and on the strength of representations made by the appellant many victims paid him various sums of money so that they might be allowed to erect their houses. The people undoubtedly went to the appellant because of his office or position as councillor and he by virtue of his said office or position authorised the people to go ahead and build, or assured them that the houses when built would not be demolished. There was no question that he did not know that he was not lawfully authorised to demand or obtain such moneys.

The fact that the appellant made the representations and obtained the various sums of money was not in doubt. It was, however, argued that inasmuch as the appellant had not represented himself as having any duty to perform or to refrain from performing such duty by reason of his office as councillor, in relation to the demand alleged to have been made by him, he had in law made no demand " under colour of his office," and his conviction was therefore wrong jn law and should be set aside. \V e were unable to accept this argument. The particulars of the charges preferred against the appellant clearly stated that he obtained the various sums of money ., under colour of his office"; nowhere in any of the charges nor in the evidence was it suggested that the appellant demanded or obtained the moneys in relation to the performance of his duty or in respect of the duties of his office.

We must point out that although three offences appear together to be created by one and the same enactment in the Criminal Code, s. 413, which reads:

" Every public officer or juror who is guilty of corruption, or of wilful oppression, or of extortion, in respect of the duties of his office, shall be liable to imprisonment for five years"

the courts have nevertheless held ad nause,am that each is a separate and distinct offence. This court recently held in Commissioner of Police v. Bonsu and Others (1) that:

" in any indictment where such offences appear in separate counts a verdict on one count is entirely independent of a verdict on the other. "

Trial courts often find some difficulty in deciding whether the par­ticulars of the offence alleged, or the facts, amount either to corruption or extortion. In our view there is no need for such difficulty. The test to be applied is as follows: if the particulars of offence or action complained of are committed in respect of the duties of an office, or in other words, committed in the performance of the duties of an office, then clearly the offence contemplated is corruption; but if the particu­lars or the acts are committed under colour of the office then the offence contemplated is extortion .. f

In explaining the offences of corruption and wilful oppression by a public officer, sections 429 and 438 respectively of the Criminal Code clearly show that these offences may be committed •• in respect of the duties of his (the accused's) office"; but when we come to the explana­tion of the offence of extortion as provided by section 437, we find that the phrase" in respect of the duties of his office" is dropped and the phrase " under colour of his office " is used. It is our opinion that the distinction is deliberate. A public officer may be guilty of corruption or wilful oppression" in respect of the duties of his office," that is, in the performance of the duties of his office; or guilty of extortion, that is, by demanding or obtaining" under colour of his office."

In the unreported case of Oommissioncr of Police v. Appiah (2), where the accused had been convicted on five charges of extortion contrary to the Criminal Code, s. 413 (then s. 394), as explained by section 437 (then section 418) of the Code, Verity C.J., Nigeria, delivering the judgment of the court, had this to say:

" The first ground of appeal argued was, in substance, that the evidence did not establish that the appellant when he obtained money from the complainants did so ' under colour' of his office. Counsel cited the Nigerian case R. v. Minimah (3). We would point out that the decision in that case was to the effect that where the charge relates to the performance of the duty of the accused it is necessary to prove what was that duty. It is not so under the section relevant to the present charges. There is clearly a distinction between' the performance of the duties of an office' and' under colour' of that office. In the latter case it is necessary to establish no more than that the accused held the office averred in the charge and that it was' under colour' of that office, that is to say, by his representation or even misrepresentation of the duties thereof that he demanded or obtained money from the complainant. "

Again in another Gold Coast case, Commissioner of Police v. Sawyerr and Others (4), it was submitted that the first appellant had no authority by virtue of his office to demand or receive a sum of £100 received by him, and that as there was no direct evidence that he represented that he had authority to demand that amount there was no extortion within the definition of that offence in our Code. These submissions were rejected by the court, which held that as it was by the use of his position as a public officer, namely a Custom Preventive Officer, he was able to extract the money from his complainant, his act amounted to extortion under colour of his office.

In order to appreciate the decision in the Nigerian case of R. v. Minimah (3) it is necessary to draw attention to the difference in the law concerning extortion by a public officer in the two countries. In this Nigerian case the accused was charged with extortion contrary to the Nigerian Criminal Code, s. 99, which read as follows:

" Any person who, being employed in the public service, takes or accepts from any person, for the performance of his duty as such officer, any reward beyond his proper pay and emoluments, or any promise of such reward, is guilty of a felony, and is liable to imprisonment for three years."

The particulars of the offence in that case clearly alleged that the appellant took or accepted the sum of £4 from the complainants " for the performance of his duties as clerk of the Native Court, etc., etc." It was therefore held that it was essential in a prosecution under the section that the duty which the accused person had to perform should be proved by evidence and that it was for the performance of that duty that the reward was taken.

Also, in the subsequent Nigerian case of Commissioner of Police v. Mataya (5), the appellant was charged with the offence that, while being employed in the public service, he corruptly and under colour of his employment demanded and took some money from the complainant, contrary to the Nigerian Criminal Code, s. 404 (1) (a). The facts of the case, as briefly reported, were that the complainant handed the appellant, a railway clerk, a waybill for the delivery of some kola nuts. The appellant threw it back saying that the custom was to pay three­pence for each bundle to him and the complainant had to pay two shillings before obtaining delivery. The trial court found this demand, which finding was accepted by the Court of Appeal, to be a " private charge." It was held that to constitute an offence under the section there must be not only a corrupt demand, but also a pretence that the party making it was lawfully empowered to do so by reason of his employment. In the light of this interpretation of the section under which the appellant was charged the court held that the evidence did not disclose a pretence by the appellant that he was entitled to the demand of threepence a bundle as a legitimate perquisite of his post; it was further held that the appellant simply intimated that he would not carry out his duty by delivering the kola nuts unless he received a •. dash." The conviction of the appellant was therefore quashed.

To appreciate the decision in this Nigerian case we must also refer to the wording of the relevant section in the Nigerian Criminal Code as follows:

•. (404) 1. Any person who, being employed in the public service of Nigeria, or in that of any other Government, corruptly and under colour of his employment-

(a) demands or takes property from any person; or, etc., is guilty of a felony, and is liable to imprisonment for five years."

But in our Criminal Code, section 437, which defines our law of extortion by public officer, is as follows:

•. A public officer is guilty of extortion who, under colour of his office, demands or obtains from any person, whether for public purposes or for himself or any other person, any money or valuable consideration which he knows that he is not lawfully authorised to demand or obtain, or at a time at which he knows that he is not lawfully authorised to demand the same."

It is clear that the Nigerian law of extortion by a public officer is different from our law. The ingredients necessary to support such offence in the two countries are different and the case law must also therefore be different.

Neither the ratio decidendi in the Minimah case (3) nor in the Motayo case (5) would avail on si~m: circumstances jn a prosecution upon a charge of extortion by a public officer in this country.

Under our law it is not essential in a prosecution for an offence of extortion, contrary to the Criminal Code, s. 413, to prove the duty which the accused person has to perform nor that it was for the performance of that duty 'that a valuable consideration was taken. It is also not essential that the evidence should disclose a pretence by the accused that he was entitled to the valuable consideration demanded or obtained by him. All that is essential to be proved in our law is to be found in the judgment delivered in the Appiah case (2), where it is stated that:

" it is necessary to establish no more than that the accused held the office averred in the charge and that it was' under colour of ' that office, that is to say by his representation or even misrepre­sentation of the duties thereof that he demanded or obtained money from the complainant."

In our view the requirements as to extortion by a public officer in this country is not different from what appears in Archbold, 33rd ed., p. 1279:

" The gist of the offence lies in taking under colour of office from any person any money or valuable thing which is not due from him; if the act is done otherwise than in good faith. " And it is equally extortion where a greater fee is exacted than what is legally due, as where money is exacted as a fee, where none what­ever is payable .... For a gaoler to detain the corpse of a prisoner for a debt due to him is a misdemeanour."

Nor what is the meaning of the pregnant words, "under colour of"? We can do no better than quote with approval the following from the Mataya case (5) at p. 116, per Blackall P.:

" ... The expression 'under colour of' is to be found in legal phraseology from medieval times ... as ' an act evil done by the countenance of an office, and it bears a dissembling face of the right of the office.' "

Again, at p. 117, we find:

" But, although 'the expression is frequently met with in judg­ments, ... this court has not been referred to any decided case to show that this expression is to be understood in any peculiar sense in the criminal law. It must therefore be given its ordinary grammatical meaning. In the Oxford Dictionary, Vol. 2, the phrase ' under colour of ' is defined as ' on the pretext or pretence of, under the mask or alleged authority of.' "

Adopting the language of the learned President in this cited case we also share the opinion that the offence of extortion relates to a public officer extorting money or its worth under the guise of legality; his claim is not genuine, though such ostensibly under his office, but this is really a pretext for making the claim and a veil to give it the appearance of legality.

In this appeal, as there was no doubt that the appellant held the public office averred in each of the charges and that it was under colour

of that office, in other words under the pretence that the complainants could not erect houses without his permission as councillor that he obtained the moneys from them, we were satisfied that he had been properly convicted and we therefore dismissed the appeal.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
