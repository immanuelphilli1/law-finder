                                                    October 25, 1957

                                   ACQUAYE v. DEEDEI AND ANOTHER

(High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                           Ollennu J.

 

 

Succession-Intestacy-Family law- Revolution of self-acquired property­ Meaning of " family" for succession purposes-Person entitled to main­tain action.

Succession-Intestacy-Family law-Custom of Gas of Accra-Children of deceased male intestate-Circumstances in which children have interest in self-acquired property of deceased father.

Succession-Family property-Nature of interest of members of family in family property.

This was an action in which one who claimed to be the head of an Accra family sought a declaration of the family's title to two adjacent plots of land in Accra. The female founder of the family had had four sons and a daughter, and the action was commenced by the daughter, who was the last survivor of the children. She, suing as head of her mother's family (or, more strictly, as head of her mother's branch of the lattBr's family), claimed that the land concerned had been the self­acquired property of her mother and thus had become family property (i.e. of the branch) upon her mother's death intestate. The defendants were sons of one of the original plaintiff's brothers-the last of them to die. They claimed ownership of the property, denying that it had belonged to their grandmother and asserting that it had been the self­acquired property of their father, who, they said, had granted leave to the plaintiff to occupy part of the premises. They did not claim to be the children of a marriage contracted by their father under the Marriage Ordinance or as a Ga six-cloth marriage. Before the action came on for hearing the original plaintiff died and her place as head of her mother's branch and as plaintiff in the action was taken by her daughter, who was thus the granddaughter in the female line of the foundress of the branch. The right of the plaintiff to sue as head of the family was put in issue.

Held, (i) on the evidence the plaintiff's mother was shown to have been the duly appointed head of the branch of which the latter's motherhad been the foundress, and her daughter had been duly appointed to succeed her in this office. Thus, although the plaintiff was not the head of the wider family to which her grandmother had belonged, she was the head of that branch of the wider family to which her grandmother's property had passed and, as such, was the proper person to maintain an action in defence of such property.

(ii) Accra Ga custom, which, in this respect, unlike the other Ga areas, is largely similar to Akan custom, holds family membership and succession to be matrilineal, although it confers upon the children of a man married by a Ga six-cloth marriage certain rights in the self-acquired property of that man upon his death intestate. 'The defendants were thus not members of their father's family and, not being his children by a Ga six-cloth marriage, had no rights in his property.

(iii) Thus, even if the defendants could establish their case that the property in dispute was the self-acquired property of their late father, this would avail them nothing, for upon the death intestate of their father his self-acquired property became the family property of the immediate branch of the family to which he belonged, and of this branch the plaintiff was the head.

(iv) On the facts the property in dispute had been the self-acquired property of the plaintiff's grandmother, so that the interest of the defendant's father in it had been limited to an interest held jointly with his brothers and sisters for their lives.

Cases referred to:

(1) Nelson v. Ocansey and Others (1950) West African Court of Appeal, March 11, 1950, unreported.

(2) Vanderpuye and Others v. Botchway (1951) 13 W.A.C.A. 164. (3) Amarfio and Another v. Ayorkor (1954) 14 W.A.C.A. 554.

ACTION for a declaration of the title of the plaintiff's family to land.

OLLENNU J. The suit was commenced by Akua Crabbe, the only daughter of Effy Affuah, otherwise known as Afia Tsuru or Afia Ootuwa, and sister of the whole blood of four brothers: Azzu, Lloyd, Richardson and Victor Crabbe. At the date of the writ she was the sole surviving child of the said Effy Affuah. The defendants are children of the last brother, Victor Crabbe. The dispute is in respect of a piece of land situate at James Town, Accra, a portion of which has a building thereon, with the other portion serving as courtyard or compound to the house. The cause of action is entry by the defendants and their erecting wooden sheds upon the compound without leave and licence of Akua Crabbe, and their insulting behaviour 'towards her. The defendants contend that both the house and land were the self-acquired property of their late father, the said Victor Crabbe.

By her statement of claim Akua Crabbe claimed a declaration of title to the land forming the courtyard; recovery of possession thereof; ejectment of the defendants from the family house, i.e., the other portion of the land; and an injunction restraining the defendants from interfering with her right to control the property. She claimed in a representative capacity, "as successor to her late mother and brothers "-which included Victor-" and as head of her family."

Akua Crabbe died on March 18, 1957, and the present plaintiff, Patience Adukwei Acquaye, her eldest surviving daughter, was, on May 31, 1957, substituted for her. In paragraph 4 of the affidavit in support of her application for substitution, the present plaintiff deposed that the family had appointed her customary successor to her mother and head of the family. Her application for substitution in 'that capacity was granted without opposition. The present plaintiff there­fore, standing in the shoes of the original plaintiff, claims in her capacity as successor to her mother and to each of the four brothers­Azzu, Lloyd. Richardson and Victor-and also as head of the family, namely, the Effy Affuah family.

In paragraph 3 of her statement of claim the plaintiff pleaded that the portion of the land forming the courtyard or compound was the self-acquired property of the late Effy Affuah, the originator of the family, by purchase from one Quao, and in paragraph 4 that the portion on which the house stands was acquired by Effy Affuah under a deed of conveyance dated May 12, 1892, from one James Ahuma Solomon. In paragraph 5 she further pleaded that the house was built by Lloyd, was later added to by Victor, and the compound was used by the whole family, particularly by Victor, who kept horses there.

The defendants, on the other hand, pleaded in paragraphs 3, 4 and 5 of their statement of defence that the piece of land forming the compound was the self-acquired property of their father, the late Victor Crabbe, and was in his exclusive possession without let or hindrance. They pleaded in paragraphs 7, 8 and 9 that the house also was the self-acquired property of their late father and that it was with his leave and licence that the original plaintiff lived in the house. In paragraph 9 they pleaded that their claim to the house and land " is based on occupation of self-acquired property of their father the late Victor Emmanuel Crabbe."

Upon those pleadings the first issue raised is the present status of the property; in other words the family's ownership of the whole area of land consisting of the buildings and the compound. In view of the facts pleaded, that issue is a question of law which can be disposed of very easily. By native custom a woman's self-acquired property descends to her children upon her death intestate: see Nelson v. Oc.ansey and Others (1). Upon the death of the last surviving child that property, by Accra custom, becomes the property of the maternal family of that woman: children of her sons are not members of that family and have no interest in the grandmother's said property.

Again, according to Akan and Accra native customs, as distinguished from other Ga towns, upon intestacy a man's self-acquired property becomes vested immediately in his family as family property. And as far as Accra is concerned, only his children by a Ga six-cloth marriage are entitled by custom to an interest in this property. The interest amounts only to a right of support out of the father's estate, depending upon the decision of the head of the family, assisted by other members of the family: see Vanderpuye and Others v. Botehway (2), cited with approval in the case of Amarfio and Another v. Ayorkor (3), at pp. 556-557.

Thus whether the property in dispute in this case, consisting of the buildings and the yard, was acquired as pleaded by the plaintiff or as pleaded by the defendants, it is now the property of the family to which Akua Crabbe, Victor Crabbe, and their three brothers belonged during their lifetime, that is, their mother's family, the Effy Affuah family.

The defendants have never alleged that they are children of Victor Crabbe by a Ga six-cloth marriage. Consequently eveil if the property were the self· acquired property of their late father, they would by native custom have no interest whatsoever in it. And if it is proved that the original plaintiff was the head of the family, and that the present plaintiff is now the head of that family, the defendants' inter­ference with that property without the permission of the head con­stitutes a wrongful act. And that will be so even though the plaintiff has claimed that the property was acquired by a member of the family other than Victor Crabbe, the same position which operated in Amarfio and Another v. Ayor/wr (3). See also the judgment of Windsor Aubrey J. in the same case, which was then entitled Oequaye v. A marfio and Another and which was upheld by the West African Court of Appeal on this particular issue.

'1'he next issue is the first of those set out on summons for direc­tions, namely, the headship of the family. As I pointed out earlier on, the grounds upon which the present plaintiff sought to be substi­tuted for the original plaintiff is that she had been appointed successor to the original plaintiff and head of her family. '1'hat application was granted without opposition. The plaintiff led evidence of her appoint­ment. Her evidence in that respect was supported by the evidence of her witness, I\ofi Atwere, who is the oldest male member and head of the family consisting of the descendants of his mother Dansuah and his aunt Effy Affuah. It was admitted by the plaintiff that Nii Yao Duade Crabbe is the head of the entire Crabbe family. Her evidence, however, shows that the only connection which Nii Yao Duade Crabbe has with Effy Affuah is that his mother and the said Effy Affuah were married to the same man. '1'hat fact is not disputed. Nii Yao Duade Crabbe, in the circumstances, cannot by native custom be a member of Effy Affuah's family, and is not entitled to be consulted in the affairs of that family, particularly in the appointment of the head of the family.

I accept the evidence of the plaintiff and of Kofi Atwere. I am satisfied upon that evidence that up to the date of her death Akua Crabbe was the head of the Effy Affuah family; further, that the appointment of the plaintiff as the head of her grandmother's immediate family was made by the proper persons and in accordance with Ga native custom, and I hold that she is at the date hereof the properly constituted head of the Effy Affuah family, that is, the family of Akua Crabbe and Victor Crabbe, and the only person now entitled by native custom to deal with and manage the property of the family.

[His Lordship then went on to deal with the issue as to who had originally acquired the land. After pointing out that, in view of the decision he had already reached, a decision on this issue was not essential for the purpose of disposing of the action, he went on to say that he would nevertheless give a ruling in order to make clear the status of the property. After discussing the evidence his Lordship accepted that of the plaintiff, rejected that of the defendant, and made the following findings: ]

I hold that the property was the self-acquired property of the late Effy Affuah; that the said property upon her death intestate devolved upon her four sons and her only daughter; and, the last of her children having now died, the land has by custom become the property of her family consisting of the descendants of her only daughter Akua Crabbe, failing which, the wider family, namely, the descendants in the female line of the mother of Effy Affuah and her sister Dansuah.

I now turn to the question of the behaviour of the defendants to the head of the family. I am satisfied upon the second defendant's own evidence that for twenty years before her death he was not on speaking terms with his aunt, the late Akua Crabbe, the head of the family, although he lived with her in her own family house all those years. That shows he had no regard for her and treated her with base contempt; and all this in spite of the fact that he knows quite well that she was his father's only sister and successor. I accept the evidence of the Akumadjey Mantse Nii Ayikai II that after the arbitra­tion he advised the defendants to approach their aunt courteously and beg her for permission to use the land. The evidence of the defendants satisfies me that they defied the head of the family both prior to and subsequent to the arbitration, and that they treated the sound advice given to them by the Mantse as nonsensical and stupid and rejected it. They have persisted in that attitude up to this moment. The second defendant maintained to the last that all the courtesy his late aunt was entitled to from him was to be informed of what they were doing on the land; as for seeking her permission, he considered that out of the question. He will not look at the present head. No head of family can tolerate this, even from a person who has an interest in the family property. Such behaviour towards the head and such challenge of the family's authority would render even the person with an interest liable to ejectment from the family house; how much more persons in the position of the defendants who have no interest in family property and whom the head of the family has been accommodating out of pure generosity of heart and humanitarian considerations, they being children, however begotten, of her full-blood brother.

 

The plaintiff has discharged the onus upon her and is entitled to the reliefs she seeks. There will be judgment for the plaintiff for a declaration that the land on which the defendants have erected the wooden sheds is the property of the Effy Affuah family under manage­ment and control as head of that family; recovery of possession of that portion of the land; a declaration of the family's title to the house occupied by her and the defendants; an injunction restraining the defendants or either of them, their agents or servants, from inter­fering with her control and management of the whole land including the building and the yard; and a declaration that the defendants are liable to ejectment. I shall not make any specific order for ejectment of the defendants immediately. I leave it to the defendants, if they are so advised or if they think fit, to approach the plaintiff for reconcilia­tion and for terms upon which they could continue to occupy a portion of her family house. I reserve liberty to the plaintiff to apply for an order for ejectment upon the defendants' failure to make the approach within a reasonable time.

Judgment for the plaintiff.

S. G. D.
