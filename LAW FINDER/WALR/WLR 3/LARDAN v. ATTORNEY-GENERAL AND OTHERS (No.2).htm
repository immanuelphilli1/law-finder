                                       LARDAN v. ATTORNEY-GENERAL AND OTHERS (No.2)

High Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                                                      Smith J..

 

 

Constitutional Law-Interpretation of statutes-Whether Act determining proceedings impugning deportation order determined proceedings in which a declaration of citizenship was sought-Deportation (Othman Larden and Amadu Baba) Act, 1957, s. 4 (2).

Constitutional Law-Deportation Whether Act empowering deportation of named persons unconstitutional as being discriminatory-Deportation (Othman Larden and Abadu Baba) Act, 1957-Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, 1957, s. 31 (2).

Constitutional Law-Jurisdiction of Supreme Court-Power of Parliament to legislate for "peace, order and good government "-Whether essential validity of legislation open to question by courts-Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, 1957, s. 31 (1).

Following upon the proceedings reported above (Lardan v. A ttorney­General and Others (No.1), p. 55) the Ghana Parliament passed the Deportation (Othman Larden and Amadu Baba) Act, 1957. By section 4 (2) of this Act all proceedings instituted for the purpose of impugning the validity of the deportation orders already referred to, were deter­mined. It was argued that the extant proceedings were concerned primarily with establishing citizenship and were therefore not affected by the Act. It was argued further that the Act itself was unconstitu­tional and ullra vires in that it infringed the Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, 1957, as being discriminatory within the meaning of section 32 (2), and as being repugnant to the powers of making laws for peace, order and good government as contained in section 32 (1).

Held, (i) notwithstanding the form of the original proceedings it was clear that their primary purpose was that of impugning the deportation orders made under the Deportation Act. This being so, the proceedings were caught by section 4 (2) of the Deportation (Othman Larden and Amadu Baba) Act, 1957, and were thereby determined.

(ii) The Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, 1957, s. 32 (2) prohibited legislation of racial discriminatory effect. On the face of the Deportation (Othman Larden and Amadu Baba) Act, 1957, there was nothing within it to indicate that the persons the subject of the order were being discriminated against on racial grounds. The Act was there­fore not caught by section 32 (2) of the Order in Council.

(iii) The Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, 1957, s. 31 (1), empowered the Parliament of Ghana to make laws for the peace, order and good government of Ghana. The effect of the section was not to describe, and thus to limit, the powers conferred so as to permit of inquiry as to whether any Act, duly passed, was or was not conducive to peace, order and good government, but to confer plenary powers the due exercise of which could not be called into question otherwise than by Parliament itself. Save, therefore, where the question was as to whether a restriction imposed elsewhere in the Order in Council had been infringed, in which case a purported Act, or part of a purported Act would be ultra vi1'es and invalid, it was not open to the courts to call into question the validity of legislation.

ACTION for a declaration as to citizenship.

Bing Q.C., Attorney-General, for 'the respondents.

Quass Q. C. for the applicant.

October 25, 1957.

SMITH J. Parliament of Ghana passed an Act on August 23, 1957, the short title of which is the Deportation (Othman Larden and Amadu Baba) Act, 1957, giving power to the Minister responsible for deporta­tion to order the deportation from Ghana of these two persons. Orders were in fact duly made. When I use the word " Act" alone, I am referring to this particular Act of Parliament.

Section 4 (2) of 'the Act is as follows:

"On any order of deportation being made hereunder, any deportation order made under the Deportation Act, 1957, in respect of the same deportee shall be automatically revoked and if the deportee is in custody under the provisions of the Deportation Act, 1957, he may, notwithstanding any proceedings in any court, whether pending or determined, be retained in custody for the purposes of this Act without being released and such custody shall be deemed to be legal custody; and any proceedings in any court instituted for the purpose of impugning the validity of the Alhaji Alufa Othman Larden Lalemie Deportation Order, 1957, or the Alhaji Amadu Baba Deportation Order, 1957, shall be auto­matically determined."

At the time of 'the passing of this Act there were pending jn the Kumasi Divisional Court substantive actions by these two men. These actions were brought after and in consequence of deportation orders which had been made under the Deportation Act, 1957. They are the orders referred to in section 4 (2) of the Act quoted above.

The claim in each of these actions was the same:

" for a declaratory order or judgment that being a citizen of Ghana by virtue of section 4 of Ghana Nationality and Citizenship Act, 1957, he is not by virtue of the provisions of section 3 of 'the Deportation Act, 1957, subject to deportation and that any deporta­tion order issued in respect of plaintiff is null and void."

In the pleadings which were filed later it is put as follows: " The plaintiff seeks against the defendants:

1.   That he is a citizen of Ghana by birth.

2.   That the Deportation Act, 1957, does not apply to him.

3. That the said ALHAJI ALUFA OTHMAN LARDEN LALEMIE Deportation Order made against him is null and void. "

The Attorney-General argued the preliminary point that these two actions were automatically determined under section 4 (2) of the Act. Mr. Quass, counsel for the plaintiff, contended at the outset that even assuming the Act was valid, section 4 (2) did not terminate 'the actions which had been brought, and he submitted that he should open his case and call witnesses to prove that the two men were Ghanaian citizens; in other words, to go ahead with the actions in the ordinary manner. After argument I ruled that the question of the validity of the Act and interpretation of section 4 (2) should be first decided. It seemed to me 'that it was Parliament's intention in the Act that~ the present actions should be determined, and it was a matter of construc­tion whether the wording of this particular section of the Act had achieved that object. Tha't Parliament can so determine court pro­ceedings is, I think, unquestioned and there are precedents. In addition to those quoted to me by the Attorney-General, there is, for example, the Parliamentary Papers Act, 1840, in England. Is 'the wording of section 4 (2) sufficient in the present case? Counsel for the plaintiff submitted that any action can have more than one purpose and that if there is any doubt in 'the interpretation of an Act it should be construed in favour of the individual affected, particularly where the liberty of such person is involved. I agree. But deportation orders dated July 30, 1957, were made against these two men under the Deportation Act, 1957. 'these actions for declaration for citizenship were filed on July 31, and it is obvious that they were only filed for one purpose, namely, to impugn the validity of the deportation orders. It is true, as Mr. Quass pointed out, that there are other grounds than citizenship afforded by the Deportation Act, 1957, on which deporta­tion orders can be challenged but they were not available in this case. The only course open to 'the two men concerned was to challenge the deportation orders on the ground that they were citizens of Ghana and were therefore not liable to deportation. This they did by bringing the actions I have referred to. Citizenship was the only ground on which the deportation orders were impugned. I cannot hold otherwise than that the proceedings brought when they were, on the one ground available, were brought for the purpose of impugning the validity of the deportation orders. The word "proceedings" used in the Act covers the substantive actions which I consider were proceedings brought for one purpose, namely, the purpose as expressed in section 4 (2) of the Act. I cannot consider it to be otherwise, taking into account the way in which the writs were worded, the circumstances under which they were brought, the date they were filed and 'the language used in the Act. It is, of course, only the present actions that have been automatically terminated by Act of Parliament.

I now approach the question whether the Act is ultra vires the Constitution or not. The Act provides for the deportation of two persons "notwithstanding the provisions of the Deportation Act, 1957," under which only aliens can be deported, inter alia, on the ground that their presence in this country is not conducive to the public good. It follows that the Deportation (Othman Larden and Amadu Baba) Act was passed to enable 'these two persons to be deported irrespective of the fact whether they were citizens of Ghana or not. This Act, Mr. Quass submits, transgressed two fundamental rights, namely, the right of a citizen 'to live in his own country and the right to have recourse to the court. That is so.

The Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, s. 31, is as follows:

" (1) Subject to the provisions of this Order, it shall be lawful for Parliament to make laws for the peace, order and good govern­ment of Ghana.

(2) No law shall make persons of any racial community liable to disabilities to which persons of other such communities are not made liable.

(3) Subject to such restrictions as may be imposed for the purposes of preserving public order, morality or health, no law shall deprive any person of his freedom of conscience or the right freely to profess, practise or propagate any religion.

(4) Any laws in contravention of subsection (2) or (3) of this section or section 34 of this Order shall, to the extent of such contravention, but not otherwise, be void.

(5) The Supreme Court shall have original jurisdiction in all proceedings in which the validity of any law is called in question and if any such question arises in any lower court, the proceedings in 'that court shall be stayed and the issue transferred to the Supreme Court for decision."

It is also appropriate to refer here to the Ghana Independence Act, 1957, of the Imperial Parliament, which provides in the First Schedule:

" (1) The Colonial Laws Validity Act, 1865, shall not apply to any law made on or after the appointed day by the Parliament of Ghana.

(2) No law and no provision of any law made on or after the appointed day by the Parliament of Ghana shall be void or inopera­tive on the ground that it is repugnant to the law of England .... (3) The Parliament of Ghana shall have full power to make laws having extraterritorial operation."

As Mr. Quass pointed out, the fact that there is a written constitu­tion does not detract in any way from the independence or sovereignty of any state. He contended. however, that in addition to the undoubted power of the court to inquire into legislation passed in connection with the Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, s. 31 (2) and (3), the court had power to inquire into legislation passed under section 31 (1), that is, whether in this case the Act of Parliament is for" the peace, order and good government" of the country of Ghana. If that contention is correct, it would mean that every single Act of Parliament would in theory be subject to the scrutiny of the court. This is a tempting proposition but hardly feasible.

So far as section 31 (2) and (3) is concerned, the court can and will interfere if it is transgressed.

Mr. Quass conceded 'that he could not attack this Act under section 31 (3). As regards the interpretation of section 31 (2), I cannot agree with his submissions. The meaning that this subsection conveys to me is that no persons shall be discriminated against because they belong to a particular racial community, whether it be Hausa, Ga, Fanti or European. This special Deportation Act applies to two persons who, incidentally, claim to be Hausas. There is nothing in the Act or otherwise to show that these two persons are being deported, and are being made to suffer such disability, because they are Hausas. Any person of any racial community may be similarly affected.

As regards section 31 (1), Mr. Quass submitted that 'the words "peace, order and good government" were of very wide import, giving the legislature very wide discretion but not plenary powers. In the case of Ashbury v. Ellis (1), a case on appeal from the Court of Appeal of New Zealand, the Privy Council did inquire into the merits of a particular law passed for the" peace, order and good government of New Zealand." But in the New Zealand Constitution the legislature had power to make laws for the peace, order and good government of New Zealand, provided that no such laws be repugnant to the laws of England. In that proviso there is a limitation or restriction which is not found in section 31 (1) of the Ghana Constitution.

In the case of Attorney-General of Ontario v. Attorney-General of Canad,a (2) the question related to the respective powers of the Dominion Parliament and the provincial legislatures. The Dominion Parliament cannot trench upon the power of the provincial legislatures in the exercise of its general power to legislate for the peace, order and good government of Canada unless the matter in question has" attained such dimensions as to affect the body politic of the Dominion." I refer in particular to the passage at p. 361:

., if it were once conceded that the Parliament of Canada has authority to make laws applicable to the whole Dominion, in relation to matters which in each province are substantially of local or private interest, upon the assumption that these matters also concern the peace, order and good government of the Dominion, there is hardly a subject enumerated in section 92 (of the Constitution) upon which it might not legislate, to the exclusion of the provincial legislatures."

In the other authority relied upon by Mr. Quass, that of Attorney­General of Saskatchewan v. Canadian Pacific By. and Attorney­General of M.anitoba (3), the constitutional question was whether the Dominion Parliament in setting up the new Province of Saskatchewan in 1905 had powers to impose a constitutional limitation upon the right to taxation which was ordinarily possessed by the four original Canadian provinces under the British North America Act, 1867, s. 92.

I do not think the Canadian authorities assist so much where, as here. the Parliament of Ghana has been given sole power to legislate for the peace, order and good government of the whole country. Here lies the fundamental difference between a unitary government and a federal government. In Canada the Dominion Parliament is invested with the duty of making laws for the peace, order and good government of Canada subject to expressed reservations. Certain subjects are reserved exclusively to the provincial legislatures. The cases cited seem to me to arise out of the conflict of legislative authority (Dominion and provincial) or, as in the case of Attorney-General of Canada v. G.ain (4), in relation to one of the matters assigned 'to the Dominion Parliament, under the British North America Act, 1867, s. 91, and on the question then of extraterritorial constraint.

In the Constitution of Ghana there are no reservations or restric­tions of subject on the Parliament of Ghana to make laws for the peace, order and good government of the country, except the two subsections to which I have referred. There has not been included in the Constitu­tion, as is provided for in the constitution of many other countries, a provision for the safeguarding of "fundamental rights." It is sub­mitted by the Attorney-General for Ghana that Parliament has plenary powers to pass legislation under section 31 (1), and that it is not for the court to inquire into such legislation and that the validity of any Act can only be challenged in a court of law on the ground that it infringes section 31 (2) or (3). I am, of course, presupposing that there is no other objection to the validity of any statute on the ground of procedural informality or contravention of any other express pro­vision of the Ghana Constitution.

In support of his contention the Attorney-General based his argument on the following passage from Jennings and Young, Constitutional Laws of the Commonwealth, 2nd ed., p. 47. The passage was quoted in court and it is as follows:

"The words . peace, order and good government' are not descriptive of the powers of the local legislature, in the sense that it must be asked of every local statute whether it is conducive to  peace' or to ' order' or to ' good government.' Neither a judge nor any other person is entitled to say that any statute is invalid because it is conducive to disorder or bad government. The phrase is a compendious means of delegating full powers of legislation, subject to any limitations which may be expressed and to any overriding legislation."

That statement of the law is in my view borne out by the authorities.

In Hodge v. The Queen (5) it was decided that a colonial legislature has, within the limits prescribed by the statute which created it, " An authority as plenary and as ample as the Imperial Parliament in the plenitude of its power possessed and could bestow."

In the case of Riel v. The Queen (6) there is a passage from the judgment of Lord Halsbury, at p. 678, that is relevant. It is a Canadian case but bears on the powers of the court to consider whether a particular act is not conducive to good government. This passage is as follows:

"The first point is that the Act itself under which the petitioner was tried was ultra vires the Dominion Parliament to enact. That parliament derived its authority for the passing of that statute from the imperial statute, 34 & 35 Vict. c. 28, which enacted that the Parliament of Canada may from time to time make provision for the administration, peace, order and good government of any territory not for the time being included in any province. It is not denied that the place in question was one in respect of which the Parliament of Canada was authorised to make such provision, but it appears to be suggested that any provision differing from the provisions which in 'this country have been made for administration, peace and good government cannot, as matters of law, be provisions for peace, order and good government in the territories to which the statute relates and further that, if a courts of law should come to the conclusion that a particular enactment was not calculated as matter of fact and policy to secure peace, order and good government, that they would be entitled to regard any statute directed to those objects, but which a court should think likely to fail of that effect, as ultra vires and beyond the competency of the Dominion Parliament to enact.

" Their Lordships are of opinion that there is not the least colour for such a contention. The words of the statute are apt to authorise the utmost discretion of enactment for the attainment of the objects pointed to."

Although that passage of Lord Halsbury was criticised by both counsel as to the language used and by Mr. Quass as to its authoritative weight, Lord Macmillan referred to the words and authority of Lord Halsbury with approval in the case of Croft v. Dunphy (7). Lord Macmillan, at p. 164, further says:

" Legislation of the Imperial Parliament, even in contraven­tion of generally acknowledged principles of international law, is binding upon and must be enforced by the courts of this country, for in these courts the legislation of the Imperial Parliament cannot be challenged as ultr.a vires: per Lord Justice-General Dunedin in Mortensen v. Peters (8). It may be that legislation of the Dominion Parliament may be challenged as ultra vires on the ground that it is contrary to the principles of international law, but that must be because it must be assured that the British North America Act has not conferred power on the Dominion Parliament to legislate contrary to these principles. In the present case, however, there is no question of international law involved. "

In The Queen v. Burah (9), referring to the power of the Indian legislature, Lord Selborne says, at p. 905:

" If what has been done is legislation, within the general scope of the affirmative words which give the power, and if i't violates no express condition or restriction by which that power is limited, it is not for any court of justice to inquire further or to enlarge constructively ,those conditions and res'trictions."

British Coal Corporation v. The Ring (10) is yet another authority for the proposition that plenary powers are implied in the words peace, order and good government. That was the submission at p. 505 of counsel appearing for Attorney-General for Quebec and Canada. That submission was not qualified or questioned by the judgment of the court.

I have considered all the other authorities cited to me and they illustrate the same point that 'the words "peace, order and good government" with no reservation attached, no restriction as to specific subjects and without qualification, give to any country 'the same plenary powers as are possessed by the Imperial Parliament in England. It was conceded that this Act of Deportation, and that was a momentary indulgence in fantasy, if passed by the Imperial Govern­ment would be a matter for Parliament only and the English courts would be powerless to interfere. If, as I hold, the words" peace, order and good government," unqualified in any way, are plenary powers possessed by Parliament and no less than the powers possessed by the Imperial Parliament, then it follows just as it would in England that the court here has no power to inquire into such an Act.

I consider, 'therefore, that any law which comes within the ambit of the Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, s. 31, and which does not contravene any other provision in the constitution can only be challenged in a court of 1 a,;" if it violates or purports to violate sub­section (2) or (3) of the section.

On 'that view, the Act deporting these t\\·o persons, not being in contravention of any expressed limitation in the Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, 1957, is a matter for Parliament.

It is the same position in the case of South Africa, where the Constitution provides 'that Parliament shall have" full powers to make laws for the peace, order and good government of the Union." It has been held in the case of Ndlwana v. Hofmeyer N. O. (11)" Parliament's will, therefore, as expressed in an Act of Par­liament, cannot now in this country, as it cannot in England, be questioned by court of law whose function itis to enforce that will, not to question it." :

 

In conclusion, there are two passages I will quote. One is from May's Parliamentary Practice, and the other from Lord Wright's speech in Liversidge v. Anderson (12), which I think are appropriate. The former is that:

" The Constitution has assigned no limits to the authority of Parliament over all matters and questions within its jurisdiction. A law may be unjust and contrary to the principles of sound government. But Parliament is not controlled in its discretion, and when it errs it's errors can be corrected by itself."

The second quotation, from Liversidge v. Anderson (12), is:

"All the courts today, and not least this House, are as jealous as they have ever been in upholding the liberty of the subject .... In the constitution of this coun'try there are no guaranteed or absolute rights. The safeguard of British liberty is in the good sense of the people and in the system of the representa­tive and responsible government which has been evolved."

In England it is no't open to the court to invalidate a law on the ground that it seeks to deprive a person of his life or liberty contrary to the court's notions of justice and, so far as the Ghana (Constitution) Order in Council, s. 31 (1), is concerned, that is the position in which I find myself.

For 'these reasons I hold that it is not for the court to inquire into this particular Act and the pending actions before the Kumasi Divisional Court are determined under the provisions of section 4 (2) of the Act.

S. G. D.
