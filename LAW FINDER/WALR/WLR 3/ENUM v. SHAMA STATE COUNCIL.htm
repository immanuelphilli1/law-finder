                                            ENUM v. SHAMA STATE COUNCIL

                                                  High Court, Western Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                                 Acolatse J.

 

 

State Counci1s-Prohibition-Destoolrnent charges against a headrnan­ Whether a " constitutional matter " within the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 5 (1).

The applicant was the headman of a fishing community who sought a writ of prohibition against the Shama State Council, to which he was subject. A group of members of the community had made complaint to the State Council about him, and he was thereupon summoned before the State Council to answer destoolment charges. The applicant contended that it was ultra vires the State Council to hear such charges.

Held, a headman is not a chief within the contemplation of the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, and therefore the destoolment of a headman is not a constitutional matter within the meaning of the Ordinance. As the jurisdiction of a State Council is expressly conferred by ordinance and is limited to such constitutional matters, it followed that the Shama State Council were acting ultra vires in purporting to entertain destoolment charges against the applicant and prohibition must therefore issue.

Cases referred to:

(1) Kolero and Others v. Enurn (1953) 14 W.A.C.A. 313.

(2) Abude and Others v. Onano and Others (1946) 12 W.A.C.A. 102.

APPLICATION for a writ of prohibition to issue against the Shama State Council.

Blay for the applicant. Mercer for the opposers.

ACOLATSE J. This is an application by Kofi Enum for an order of prohibition to issue from this court prohibiting the State Council of Shama from compelling his attendance before it, there to answer alleged destoolment charges against him by the members of the Fishing Community of Aboadzi (who oppose the application) and from proceeding with the hearing of the summons issued in the matter.

The charges preferred against the applicant are that the opposers are dissatisfied with his reign as the headman of the fishing community of Aboadzi and they enumerated sixteen items embodying the charges in the summons.

11'Ir. Blay, for the applicant, moved the court in terms of the motion with a supporting affidavit under Order 59, r. 4. He submitted that the State Council of Shama has no jurisdiction to entertain the summons before it. The summons is attached to the motion paper dealing with the charges against the applicant. He contended that the jurisdiction of a State Council is prescribed by the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 5.

Counsel for the opposers, :Mr. Mercer, admitted that the State Council has no jurisdiction to hear the proceedings as it is not a constitutional matter. He, however, contested that although the summons does not come within the scope of the jurisdiction of a State Council under the Ordinance, it is an ordinary summons by the opposers against the applicant to answer certain charges of dcstool­ment against him as the headman of the community. He conceded that a Native Court is not a competent court to deal with the matter but that the State Council is the only body or the highest native authority to deal with the questions involving the destoolment of a headman under its executive and administrative powers. He referred the court to and relied upon the judgments in [{okra and Others v. Enum (1) and Abude and Others v. Onano and Others (2).

Mr. Blay in reply contended that the State Council has no jurisdiction unless the parties agree to submit their disputes to it. In this case the State Council has issued a summons to compel the applicant to appear before it to answer the opposers' charges.

In dealing with the argument advanced by both counsel, it is clear on the face of the record that the opposers conceded the fact that the State Council cannot hear the proceedings under powers vested III it by the Ordinance. Now section 2 of the Ordinance defines a State Council as the highest council within a state in which the administration of the state· is vested by customary law. Its judicial [204]

powers are prescribed by section 5 (1), under which it has jurisdiction to inquire into and determine any matter of constitutional nature, arising within the area of its authority, over its subjects, under the provisions of the Ordinance or according to customary law. The interpretation of a " chief" in section 2 of the Ordinance does not cover a headman of a community but includes a Paramount Chief and a divisional chief. A matter of a constitutional nature means a cause, matter, question or dispute relating to chiefs under the same section of the Ordinance.

The question before the court arising in this motion is fully discussed and explained in Kokro and Others v. Enum (1). It, was laid down in that case that: "It was not the duty of the Council to elect a headman but rather to decide who had been elected headman by the village and exercise the function of confirming or rejecting that appointment. " It follows, therefore, if I am right in my conclusion over the above df\cision, that the State Council cannot entertain an application for a summons to issue for it to hear destoolment charges against a headman and since it cannot elect, it cannot destool. It can only confirm or reject the appointment of a headman submitted by the village for its decision.

The applicant is the headman of the fishing community of Aboadzi and there is no indication in the charges enumerated in the summons that he has been ousted and a new headman appointed in his place. The pith of the charges rests on the footing of asking the State Council to try the defendant and destool him from his position as the headman of the village. That it cannot do. "The extent of its judicial activity is to inquire and determine any matter of a constitutional nature.

The motion is granted in terms as prayed: let the order Issue accordingly.

Application granted.

S. G. D.
