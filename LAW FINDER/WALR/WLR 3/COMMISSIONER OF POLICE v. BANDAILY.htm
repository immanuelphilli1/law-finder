                                      COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. BANDAILY

High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

(Ollennu J.

                                                 

 

Criminal law-Accepting bribe to infl1wnce public officer-Money obtained on pretext of it being paid to public officer-Whether valuable consideration m1ist move to person accepting bribe-Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 418.

  Interpretation of statutes-Penal statutes-Effect of omission of proposition ­Whether courts will supply omissions,

      The appellant was convicted by a Magistrate, under section 418 of the Criminal Code, for accepting a valuable consideration-£60-under pre­tence of being able to influence a public officer. The evidence given by the- witnesses for the prosecution, and which was quite unambiguous, showed that the demand by the appellant for the money had been made with the false pretence that the money was intended for the public officer concerned. On the appeal it was argued that it was of the essence of an offence under section 418 that the person charged should have held himself out as taking the valuable consideration for his own benefit, and a holding out that the consideration was for the benefit of another, was outside the ambit of the section.

Held, (i) it is of the essence of the interpretation of a penal statute that the court will be slow to extend the ambit of the statute beyond what is necessary to give effect to its express provisions. The courts will not do violence to the language of a statute in order to bring a person within its terms.

(ii) Where other offences under the Criminal Code, of the same funda­mental nature as that created by section 418, are concerned (i.e., the offences of corruption and extortion) the express language of the Code refers to a consideration received by an accused person " for himself or for any other person." This language differs conspicuously from the terms of section 418, which speaks only of "whoever accepts or agrees to accept .... " It is a necessary implication from this, that the legislature did not intend acceptance of valuable consideration, within the context of section 418, to extend to acceptance for or on behalf of someone other than the acceptor.

Cases referred to:

(1) Fordyce v. Bridges (1847) 1 H.L.Cas. 1.

(2) Income Tax Commissioner v. Pemsel [1891] A.C. 534. (3) In re A Deutor (No. 335 of 1947) [1948] 2 All E.R. 533.

(4) Underhill v. Longridge (1859) 29 L.J.M.C. 65; 24 J.P. 148. (5) Commissioner of Police v. Sarpong (1946) 12 W.A.C.A. 26.

ApPEAL from a conviction by the Senior Magistrate, Accra, on November 12, 1957, for receiving a valuable consideration under pre­tence of being able to influence a public officer, under the Criminal Code, s. 418.

Battcock, Crown counsel, for the Commissioner of Police. Koi Larbi for the appellant.

OLLENNU J. The appellant was tried by the Senior Magistrate, Accra, and on November 12, 1957, was convicted and sentenced. The charge against him was laid under section 418 of the Criminal Code, c. 9, and is as follows:

" For that you on the 30th day of September, 1957, at Swedru III the Swedru .Magisterial District and within the jurisdiction of this Court, did accept a valuable consideration to wit, cash the sum of £50, from one :Moses Hanson Boateng under a pretence of being able to influence Mr. Bruce Lyle, District Magistrate, Swedru, a Public Officer in respect of his duties as such Public Officer. "


     At the close of the case on November 8, 1957, Mr. Enchill, who was then appearing for the appellant, submitted that the charge as laid under section 418 had not been proved and the appellant was entitled to acquittal, because there was no evidence that the appellant had received any valuable consideration for himself. In this court, Mr. Koi Larbi, who appeared for the appellant, raised the same point. He submitted that upon proper construction of section 418 the valuable consideration must be received or demanded by the person making the pretence, for himself, and not for any other person; and therefore the Crown failed to prove the charge as laid.

Mr. Battcock, on the contrary, submitted that having regard to the words "having unduly influenced" appearing in section 418, which show that the valuable consideration may be for the past services, the said valuable consideration may be remuneration for the service ren­dered, reimbursement of expenses incurred in the influencing, which may include an amount given to the public officer influenced or to be influenced, or both. Counsel therefore submitted that the charge under section 418 was brought home to the defendant upon the proof that he demanded the valuable consideration for the person to be influenced.

The question therefore is: what is the proper legal construction to be placed upon the said section 418?

The learned Senior Magistrate in his judgment delivered on Novem­ber 12, 1957, made the following findings of fact:

., After careful consideration of the evidence as a whole I accept the evidence of the first and second prosecution witnesses that the accused asked for £60 in order to influence Magistrate Bruce Lyle. I am satisfied that £50 was paid to the accused by the first prosecu­tion witness .... I have also taken into account any discrepancies in their evidence (meaning the first, second, third and fourth prosecution witnesses). Such discrepancies are not, in my opinion, so material as to affect the main evidence before me that the accused did in fact receive £50 from the first prosecution witness under pretence of being able to influence the Magistrate."

The learned Senior Magistrate then continued:

" I do not accept counsel's argument that in order to succeed on a charge under section 418 the valuable consideration must be accepted by the accused for himself. The section does not state so, and if a person makes a pretence of being able to influence another knowing he cannot, he obviously has no intention of handing any valuable consideration he receives to the person even though he pretends he is going to do so."

Had the facts found by the learned Senior Magistrate been sup­ported by the evidence on the record, the learned Senior Magistrate's direction of himself on section 418, which as will appear presently, is, in my opinion, a misdirection, would not have mattered very much. But the facts found by the court are not warranted by the evidence:

I shall refer to material pieces of evidence given by the witnesses for the prosecution to show this.

Giving his evidence in chief, the first prosecution witness said, inter alia:

"Accused said the Magistrate who was to hear the case was his best friend, and the Magistrate would take £80 so as to set me free in the case .... Accused put his hand into his pocket and brought out a large quantity of currency notes saying that it was money someone had brought to him to be taken to the Magistrate."

" I took out £50 in currency notes. I counted it all and showed it to him (the accused). I told him I could only get £50. Accused took it and counted it .. He said I should have brought £80 so the £50 would not do. He asked me how I was going to make up the £10. I told him I could not get any more. He said he would add his own £10 to it to make £80 and he would charge £5 drink or interest on it so I should give him a Promissory Note of having bought one case of Schnapps for £15 to be paid for on Thursday after my case had been finished. I agreed and gave him the Promissory Note."

The second prosecution witness under cross-examination also said, inter alia:

,. I understood that accused was asking the £80 to give to the Magistrate. He told us so."

And the third stated in evidence in chief:

" The first witness counted the money £50 in notes. Accused said I have been to see him and he said he would take £80. What about the £10 balance? Accused said he had gone to see the Magistrate. The first witness said all he could get was £50 and he had no £10 to add to it at the time. Accused said he would assist him with £10 loan but he would charge £5 interest."

This specific and unambiguous evidence led by the prosecution witnesses, whose evidence the learned Senior Magistrate accepted, is that the appellant demanded £80 and received £80, i.e., £50 from the first witness plus the loan of £10 he gave to the witness to be given to the Magistrate, and not for himself. Thus the findings of fact made by the learned Senior Magistrate that the appellant asked for the £80 in order to influence the Magistrate is not borne out by the evidence. Those findings of fact are therefore wrong.

Now to the learned Magistrate's directions on the law. In inter­preting a statute the court has to be guided by certain canons of construction to ascertain the intention of the legislature. Among hose canons are: 1. The statute is to be expounded " according to the intent of them that make it ": Fordyce v. Bridge (1). 2. If the words of the statute are themselves precise and unambiguous no more is neces­sary than to expound those words in their natural and ordinary sense, the words themselves in such case best declaring the intention of the legislature: Income Tax Commissioners v. Pemsel (2). 3." You must not imply anything in them which is inconsistent with the words expressly used," per Lord Greene M.R. in Re a Debtor (No. 335 of 1947) (3). See also Maxwell on the Interpretation of Statutes, 10th ed., pages 1-3.

In respect of construction of penal statutes, the learned authors of Maxwell on the Interpretation of Statutes, at pages 263-264 of the tenth edition of that book, stated as follows:

" But the rule of strict construction requires that the language shall be so constructed that no cases shall be held to fall within it which do not fall both within the reasonable meaning of its terms and within the spirit and scope of the enactment; where an enactment may entail penal consequences, no violence must be done to its language to bring people within it, but rather care must be taken that no one is brought within it who is not within its express language."

Further, in respect of penal statutes, the principle is that if it is inferable from the text of the statute that an omission was the result of accident it is not to be supplied. See the case of Underhill v. Long­ridge (4) and Maxwell on the Interpretation of Statutes, pages 273-274 under the sub-heading " Omissions not supplied." I shall now apply those canons to this case.

Section 418, under which the appellant was charged, reads as follows:

" Whoever accepts, or agrees or offers to accept, any valuable consideration, under pretence or colour of having unduly influenced, or of agreeing or being able so to influence, any person in respect of his duties as a public officer or juror, is guilty of a misdemeanour. "

This section contains two elements: 1. Acceptance, agreement or offer to accept a valuable consideration for; 2. Pretence of service already rendered or to be rendered in the future; the service being influencing a public officer or juror.

In this section the acceptance, offer or agreeing to accept valuable consideration is not in any way qualified. But in section 429, which defines "corruption," section 437 which defines "extortion," and section 439 (1) which both defines and creates the offence of " corrup­tion of and by agents," the acceptance, offer or agreement to accept " valuable consideration" or simple consideration is qualified. Section 429 reads:

 A public officer, juror or voter is guilty of corruption in respect of the duties of his office or vote, if he directly or indirectly agrees or offers to permit his conduct as such officer, juror, or voter to be influenced by the gift, promise, or prospect of any valuable consideration to be received by him, or by any other person, from any person whomsoever."

Section 437 reads:

 A public officer is guilty of extortion who under colour of his office, demands or obtains from any person ... for himself or any other person, any money or valuable consideration, etc."

And section 439 (1) reads, quoting only the relevant parts:

" If an agent corruptly accepts or obtains or agrees to accept­or attempts to obtain, from any person, for himself or for any other person, any gift or consideration, etc."

The words" for himself or for any other person" which occur in sections 429, 437 and 439, are omitted from section 418 of the same Ordinance. The necessary inference is that the legislature did not intend that the acceptance, etc., of the valuable consideration should include acceptance, etc., for any person other than for the person himself who accepts, agrees or offers to accept the valuable consideration.

In my opinion the learned Senior Magistrate misdirected himself upon the construction to be placed upon section 418, when he held that the acceptance of the valuable consideration alleged for the Magistrate and not for the appellant himself, is covered by section 418.

Moreover, the fact that valuable consideration might be for past services by itself indicates that it must be for the person himself who rendered such service and not for any other person.

But, apart from the wrong interpretation of section 418, the

direction of the learned Senior Magistrate that:

" if a person makes a pretence of being able to influence another knowing he cannot, he obviously has no intention of handing any valuable consideration he receives to the person even though he pretends he is going to do so."

leaves no room for doubt the Senior Magistrate thought he was trying a case of false pretences, which of course he was not.

Thus the appellant was convicted of an offence with which he was not charged. Therefore applying the principle laid down in the case of C.O.P. v. Sarpong (5) this conviction cannot stand and must be quashed.

In the result the appeal is allowed, the conviction and sentence are quashed. The appellant is acquitted and discharged.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
