                                                    December 18, 1957

                                                      YEBOAH v. TSE AND ANOTHE.                                                       

                           High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                              Ollennu J.

                                                   

 

Native custom - Conveyancing- Essential ingredients of a valid sale and conveyance-Necessity for publicity.

Native custom-Land-Self-acquired property-Bight of owner to alibnate without consents.

Native Courts-Practice-Res judicatar-Privity of parties and identity of subject-matter-Whether estoppel raised when not pleaded.

Native Courts-Practice-Evidence-Representation-Duty of representative as regards giving evidence-Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance,

c.  98, s. 22.

The plaintiff sought a declaration of title to land. The defendants claimed that the land had been sold to their predecessor in title by the plaintiff's father. The plaintiff claimed that the land had been the property of his grandfather upon whose death it had become not the property of his father but family property, and as such his father had no exclusive right to dispose of it. He claimed further that if there had been any transaction it was one of pledge not of sale and that if it had been a sale it was invalid as not being in accordance with native custom. At the hearing before the Native Court one Ankah had appeared as the plaintiff's representative and had given evidence in the first person, i.e., as if he were the plaintiff. There had also been admitted before the Native Court the proceedings and judgment in a previous suit concerning the same land, in which the plaintiff's uncle (his late father's brother), suing as the father's successor and on behalf of the family, had sought a declaration of title as against the present defendants on the ground that the sale to their predecessor was invalid. The Native Court, apparently taking the view that the burden of proof was upon the defendants to establish the validity of the sale, found for the plaintiff. On appeal:

Held, (i) the one essential ingredient of a sale by custom is that publicity shall be given to the transaction, i.e., that the conveyance is \ made in the presence of witnesses. On the plaintiff's own evidence it

was abundantly clear that the sale and conveyance had been so witnessed.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. As might be expected there is a close parallel here with the practice in England before conveyances in written form became general and when conveyance was by livery of seisin. As the learned judge here points out it is often desirable for some of the witnesses to be members of the grantor's family: the severely practical reason is that it is from the grantor's family that any subsequent objection to the grant is most likely to come

(ii) On the plaintiff's own evidence, particularly that of the earlier proceedings, it was apparent that the land concerned had been the self­acquired property of the plaintiff's father (the vendor). As such it was freely alienable by the plaintiff's father, without the necessity for family consents.

(iii) In any event the present dispute was res judicata by reason of the decision in the previous suit. The subject-matter was the same, the defendants were the same and the present plaintiff was in privity with the then plaintiff.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. In the High Court an estoppel must be expressly pleaded. If it is not so pleaded the previous judgment merely becomes an item of evidence in favour of the person for whom it was given: V vogt v. Winch (1819) 2 B. & Ald. 662. There are no pleadings before a Native Court and it would therefore appear that the rule in Voogt v. Winch does not apply there and that res judicata raises an estoppel.]

(iv) Evidence given by a representative before a Native Court must conform to the rules of evidence (insofar as they apply to Native Courts). Evidence given by the representative as if he were the principal is inadmissible.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. See on this point Nyarko v. Abunyawah (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 137 and Yeboah v. Bonko (1956) 2 W.A.L.R. 107.]

APPEAL from a decision of the Buem-Rrachi Native Court" A " on June 13, 1957, in favour of the plaintiff in an action for a declaration of title to land.

Wiredu for the plaintiff.

Akufo-Addo for the defendants.

OLLENNU J. This is an appeal from a judgment of the Buem­Rrachi Native Court" A " delivered in favour of .the plaintiff on June 13, 1957. The claim as amended is one of those forms of writs usually found in native courts. When analysed it is for: (a) a declaration that a sale of land with two farms thereon made by the plaintiff's father, one Fritz Yeboa, to one Gustav Kofi, the predecessor of the defendants, is null and void, the said sale not having been made in accordance with native custom, and consequently (b) a declaration of the plaintiff's title to the land and farms as successor, according to native custom, of the said Fritz Yeboa.

The grounds upon which the Native Court gave judgment for the plaintiff are very difficult to appreciate: they are (1) that since both the vendor and the purchaser are dead, the defendant could not tell what actually took place, and (2) that the contention of the plaintiff that the sale was illegal is correct. The first ground of the judgment shows that the court directed themselves that the onus was upon the defendants to establish that the sale was valid, while the second shows that the Native Court could not evaluate the evidence in the case, particularly the documentary evidence tendered.

The plaintiff alleged that the sale was unconstitutional, I think he means it was not made in accordance with custom. The onus was upon him to establish that fact. The evidence led on his behalf that the farms in dispute were or might have been made by Kwadjo Dei, ~he father of the vendor Fritz Yeboa, was flatly contradicted by two affi­davits, one sworn to on his behalf on February 19, 1953, by A. W. Ankah, the very man who represented him and gave evidence on his behalf in the present suit, and the other sworn ,to on August 28, 1952, by the plaintiff himself. These were admitted in evidence; each of them had already been filed in connection with an interlocutory matter in this suit. It is also contradicted by the writ of summons and the judgment in a suit entitled Yaw Wilson v. Kwame and Daniel Tse, which was tendered and admitted in evidence.

In paragraph 4 of the second affidavit the plaintiff C. K. Yeboa deposed as follows: "That the two plots of cocoa farms situate and lying at Abuabon River are bona-fide properties of my late father." The writ claimed the land and farms as the property of the late Fritz Yeboa which he had pledged to Gustav Kofi, the predecessor of the defendants. In paragraph 4 of the first affidavit A. W. Ankah, the plaintiff's representative, swore that Yaw Wilson, the plaintiff in the suit mentioned above, was a brother of the late Fritz Yeboa, and he prosecuted the suit as caretaker of the property appointed by Fritz Yeboa's family. The judgment in that case established that the land and farms in dispute were the property of the late Fritz Yeboa which he sold to Gustav Kofi, the predecessor in title of the defendant.

According to native custom a man can dispose of his self-acquired property without reference to his family. All that is necessary is that publicity should be given to the transaction, for example, the sale and conveyance must take place in the presence of witnesses, some of whom may be members of his family. That was exactly what took place in this case; in fact one of the witnesses is admitted to be the chief of the town. Both the oral and documentary evidence show that the sale of the properties was made in the presence of witnesses and that the defendants and their predecessor in title remained in possession of the property exercising acts of ownership undisturbed up ~o 1950. The oral evidence shows that since the judgment of 1950, the defendants have continued in such undisturbed possession.

Indeed if the farms had been the self-acquired property of Kwadjo Dei, the father of Fritz Yeboa and Yaw Wilson, he, Yaw Wilson, would have a better right to it than his brother's son, the present plaintiff, C. K Yeboa, who is of the third generation from Kwadjo Dei. One would have expected that in the 1950 case he would have claimed the land and farms as the property of his father to which he had succeeded and not as the property of his brother.

Further, in the writ of summons in Wilson v. Tse, Yaw Wilson claimed an order for redemption on the ground that his brother Fritz Yeboa, the owner, had pledged it to the predecessor of the defendants. The defendants on the other hand counter-claimed for a declaration of their title to the property on the ground that it was sold outright by Fritz Yeboa to their predecessor, the late Gustav Kofi. Thus one of the main issues the Native Court had to decide in that suit was the validity or otherwise of the sale by Fritz Yeboa to Gustav Kofi­precisely the same issue raised by the plaintiff in the present suit. The decision in that case was in favour of the defendants and was that the sale by Yeboa to Gustav Kofi was valid.

There was no admissible evidence led on behalf of ~he plaintiff. The only person who gave evidence for him was A. \V. Ankah, who gave his evidence as if he was the plaintiff himself giving evidence; thus, when asked in cross-examination the question: "Who is to inherit F. Yeboa after his death "? he replied: "I, C. K. Yeboa and others could inherit him." Such evidence is inadmissible. The practice has been condemned many times by the High Court and the West African Court of Appeal but the Native Court does not seem to have taken any notice of it.

Thus both on the question of original ownership and the question of validity of the sale of the land there was overwhelming evidence in favour of the defendants and almost none in favour of the plaintiff. But the Native Court failed to direct their attention to that state of fact on the proceedings.

Now apart altogether from the plaintiff failing to produce any admissible evidence to prove his case, and the existence of the over­whelming evidence produced in support of the defendants, the issue raised in the suit is res judicata by reason of the judgment in Wilson v. Tse because, as pointed out earlier on, the plaintiff in that case, Yaw Wilson, was a brother of Fritz Yeboa and an uncle of the plaintiff and prosecuted the suit upon appointment by the family to take charge of the property. The defendants in that former case are the same persons sued in this case, and the subject-matter is identically the same as that in the present suit.

Had the Native Court properly considered the implications of this judgment they would have come to the conclusion that the matter in dispute between the parties had been settled between them by the 1950 judgment and therefore the claim was res judicata.

The judgment of the Native Court cannot be supported on any ground whatsoever and must be set aside.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
