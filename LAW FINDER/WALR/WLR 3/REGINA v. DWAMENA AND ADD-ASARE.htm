                                                        November 22, 1957

                                       REGINA v. DWAMENA AND ADD-ASARE

                                                                                 Court of Appeal

                                  van Lare Ag.C.J., Granville Sharp, J.A., Smith J

 

( (.), November 22, 1957.)

Criminal Law-Stealing by a servant-Whether unpaid servant or volunteer within ambit of offence-Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 288 (1).

The appellants stood convicted of the offences of conspiracy to steal and of stealing by a servant, the latter being the aggravated offence under the Criminal Code, s. 288 (1)-" stealing anything of which he had custody, control, or possession, or to which he had the means of access, by reason of any office, employment, or service." The office occupied by the second appellant was that of treasurer to a co-operative union, from which the theft was made and, this being an honorary office, it was argued on his behalf that he was not a servant within the meaning of section 288 (1).

If eld, the payment of wages not being an essential feature of the master-servant relationship, it made no difference to the charge that the accused rendered services that were not rewarded by payment.

(1) Commissioner of Police v. Addae (1945) 11 W.A.C.A. 42 distin­guished.

(2) R. v. Foulkes (1875) L.R. 2 C.C.R. 150; 32 L.T. 407; 13 Cox 63 applied.

APPEAL from convictions of conspiracy to steal and stealing by a servant by Adumua-Bossman J. on March 1, 1957, at Cape Coast Assizes.

Smith, Crown counsel, for the Crown. OWU8U for the appellants.

GRANVILLE SHARP J.A., who delivered the judgment of the court, dealt first with the evidence and continued: In our view there is no substance in the contention urged before us by Mr. Owusu that the verdict cannot be supported on the evidence. It is abundantly clear on the evidence that the two accused betrayed their trust and mis­appropriated the money that was in their custody. We have therefore dismissed the appeal in each case.

It is, however, necessary to decide on a point of law raised by learned counsel for the appellants, on the authority of the decision in Commissioner of Police v. Addae (1), that the learned trial judge mis­directed himself in holding that the second accused was an employee within the meaning of section 288 (1) of the Criminal Code.

In Addae's case the facts were briefly that a District Commissioner handed to a schoolmaster a number of ammunition permits for trans­ference to the State Secretary at Akropong so that they might be distributed by the latter to those persons who were entitled to them.

The accused in that case was a mere gratuitous bailee, casually assisting the District Commissioner. He stole a permit, one belonging to the Krontihene of Akropong, and the gravamen of the charge as stated in the particulars of offence was that he had stolen "the property of the said Krontihene of which you had the custody as a messenger," the charge being framed under section 271 (1) of the Criminal Code, now section 288 (1).

It is to be observed that in that case the accused was not said to be a servant and is not so described in the charge. He was a mere bailee as a messenger without reward or expectation of reward. \Ve do not, therefore, differ from the learned Appeal Court judges' decision in that case, which is clearly distinguishable from the present. We think, however, that the proposition enunciated in the judgment, on which Mr. Owusu relied, that" to bring an accused person within the sub­section he must be a paid employee" is too wide. It is also in our opinion obiter, and if it were treated in any other way it would appear to be in conflict with earlier decisions.

In the present case the second accused was clearly a servant, the only question being whether if he was an unpaid servant he falls within section 288 (1) of the Criminal Code, which reads as follows:

" whoever is convicted of stealing anything of which he had the custody, control, or possession, or to which he had the means of access, by reason of any office, employment, or service, shall be liable to imprisonment for seven years."

The evidence establishes that ever since 1943, when he was appointed, he had acted as treasurer of the Oda Co-operative Union; that he had not been paid a salary; that treasurers serving the co­operative unions are not paid, but local unions may arrange some honorarium.

Ever since the case of R. v. Foulkes (2) it has been supposed that the test in such cases is not whether the accused is in receipt of wages or salary in connection with the work performed by him, but whether de facto he is a person employed in doing work ordinarily done by a clerk or servant.

In Foulkes' case Brett J. put forward the suppositious case where a baker's man who carried out bread and received money became ill and a friend of his volunteered to the baker to do this man's work until he got well, and he was allowed to do it although there might be no contract and no pay, " would he not be employed in the capacity of a servant? "

In the present case it is our opinion that the second accused is not to be treated as a gratuitous bailee of the moneys placed in his charge, but that he was employed after appointment as treasurer to deal with the money as directed by his employees and to safeguard it on their behalf.

As was suggested by Bramwell B. in the earlier case referred to, so in the present case in a civil action the evidence would seem to support a claim by the second accused to a quantum meruit for his services.

In our view, therefore, it is immaterial that the second accused was not in receipt of a salary and that he was properly charged under section 288 (1) of the Criminal Code.

The point is in a sense academic because the court would in any event have power to alter the convictions to one under section 287 (1) of the Criminal Code, and the sentence, not being excessive under that section, would stand unaltered.

As already indicated, each appeal was dismissed.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
