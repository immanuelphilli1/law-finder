                                                          VADO v. SEMPEDE

(High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

Ollennu J.

 

     

Native Law and Custom-Arbitration-Evidence-Effect of valid customary arbitration-Onus of proof·

This dispute as to the ownership of land arose when a local authority, seeking to carry out quarrying operations in the area, sought the owner for the purpose of negotiating terms. The dispute reached a Native Court only after there had been two" arbitrations" or, more probably, attempts to arbitrate, and the Native Court and subsequently the Native Appeal Court concerned themselves solely with giving effect to one or other of the " arbitration" awards, one of which had favoured the plaintiff and the other the defendant.

Held, (i) the essence of a valid, binding, arbitration is consent in advance by the parties to be bound by the arbitrator's award. Thus, although there is a right of access to court to enforce an award, or to set aside arbitral proceedings, as for example on the grounds of lack of consent or excess of jurisdiction, there is no right in either party to reopen an arbitrated issue by initiating fresh arbitration proceedings or by commencing a court action, save where this also is done by consent.

(ii) To establish that an arbitration award exists that is to operate

,., as an estoppel per rem judicatem the onus is upon the party relying upon it to prove that the essential requirements of a binding arbitration have been complied with. If this is done the award will be binding unless the other party can rebut, e.g., by proving that the purported arbitration was invalid or by showing subsequent agreement to avoid its effect.

(iii) In the present case the proceedings in the Native Courts below had been directed towards the merits of the dispute and appeared to have moved on the assumption that both arbitrations were effective. In fact no evidence had been adduced below to establish either of the arbitra­tion proceedings, so that the proceedings in both Native Courts fell to the ground.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. See Ankrah and Others v. Dabra and Olaga (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 89 and Twumasi v. Eadu (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 204 for a considera­tion of the essential characteristics of a customary arbitration.]

ApPEAL from a decision of the Kpandu District Na'tive Appeal Court on September 21, 1956, reversing a decision of the Akpini Native Court .. B " on January 30, 1956, in a dispute concerning the ownership of land. ~

Beckley for the plaintiff.

Apaloo for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. The plaintiff is the Sub-Divisional Chief of Sovie Abayeme, and the defendant is one of the citizens of Sovie Abayeme; the subject-matter of the suit is a piece of grassland called by the plaintiff "Wakyi" land and by the defendant "Avuletoe" land. The plaintiff claimed the said land to be communal land for all the people of the Sovie Abayeme community, upon which any member of the community could farm with customary permission granted by him, while the defendant counterclaimed it as his exclusive family land.

The dispute arose when the local council of the area wanted to carry out quarrying operations on the land and wanted to know the proper person or persons with whom they could negotiate terms for the use of it. Evidence was led on both sides that before the matter got to the Native Court two arbitrations were attempted; one by the elders of Sovie Abayeme town, whose award was in favour of the plaintiff, 'the other by the Divisional Chief of Sovie and his Divisional Council, including the elders of Sovie Abayeme who had taken part in the former alleged arbitration. The award of the latter arbitration was in favour of the defendant.

The Native Court" B " upheld the award of the second arbitration, namely, the one by the Divisional Council, while the Native Appeal Court upheld the award of the first arbitration, namely by the elders of Sovie Abayeme town.

The question for determination by this court is whether both or any of the two awards are binding upon the parties, and if only one of them is binding upon them, which one. A board of arbitrators by native custom is a special tribunal chosen by the parties to a dispute; it derives its jurisdiction entirely from the consent of the parties to the dispute. One of the essential elements about it is that its award is final and does not form the subject of appeal to any court. However, it is open to either party to sue in a court to enforce an award of arbitration or to have it set aside on the ground that what purports to be an arbitration is not a valid arbitration according to native custom.

Again, although there is no right of appeal, the parties are entitled, should both of them consider that the award has not satisfactorily settled the matters or all the matters in dispute between them, to submit the whole matter again or some part only of it to further arbitration. This they can do even after a final judgment by a court of competent jurisdiction: see the case of Kuturka Yard.om v. Kurankyi Minta III (1).

Thus if it is shown on the record that the first arbitration is valid according to native custom, and further, that the parties by consent submitted the matter to a second valid arbitration, the awards of both arbitrations will be valid, and the latter award will supersede or qualify the former, as the case may be.

Now after a careful study of the record of proceedings in this case, and after hearing counsel for both appellant and respondent, I have come to the following conclusions: (1) That there is not enough evidence on the record as to how the matter came before the elders of Sovie Abayeme as arbitrators; maybe it went before them by the consent of both parties; maybe upon complaint of the plaintiff, the defendant agreeing to it afterwards; maybe on an offer by the said town elders themselves: neither the Native Court" B " nor the Native Appeal Court inquired into this. (2) That the arbitration by the Sovie Divi­sional Council was held by way of appeal taken by the defendant against the award of the first arbitration, and not upon agreement by both parties that it was necessary for a further arbitration to be held. Again, neither the Native Court" B " nor the Native Appeal Court concerned itself with inquiring into that matter.

Thus there was no proof of the validity or otherwise of either of the two arbitrations and consequently of the awards published by either or both of them.

The two Native Courts, it appears, simply assumed the validity of each of those arbitrations and consequently based their decisions on either the one or the other that each considered as applicable to the case.

In the result I hold that neither the judgment of the :Native Appeal Court nor that of the Native Court" B " can stand if justice is to be done.

I therefore allow this appeal, set aside the judgment of the Native Appeal Court, as well as the judgment of the Native Court" B," each including the order as to costs. The case is remitted to the Native Court" B " for trial de novo. It will be open to the parties at such trial to prove the validity of either or both the alleged arbitrations.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
