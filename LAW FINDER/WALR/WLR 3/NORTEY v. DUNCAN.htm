                                                   NORTEY v. DUNCAN.

High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

(Ollennu J.

                                                 

.

Land Native law and custom-Stool land-Conveyancing-Control vested in sub-stool-Authority of sub-stool to grant determinable estate to stool subject-Whether concurrence of superior stool necessary.

Land--Native law and custom-Stool land-Conveyancing-Control vested in sub-stool-Authority of sub-stool to grant absolute estate-Whether converence of superior stool necessary-Effect of concurrence by superior stool on an otherwise invalid grant.

Two actions, brought by brothers, were here consolidated.. The plain­tiffs each claimed a declaration of title to an area of Osu Stool land and each relied upon a deed of gift made to him by one who described himself as the acting mankralo (i.e., the occupant of the Mankralo Stool of the Osu Stool). These deeds were dated. November 1949 and had been duly registered. with the Deeds Registry. The defendant also claimed. a declara­tion of title to the two areas of land. He relied upon a deed. of gift executed in March 1951 by one who described. himself as the mankralo, which deed was also registered in the Deeds Registry. The defendant claimed that the person who executed the plaintiff's deeds had no authority to act as mankralo and that in consequence these deeds were void of effect. The evidence disclosed that the Osu lIIankralo Stool fell vacant in 1945 and that the plaintiffs' grantor then became acting mankralo. In January 1948, however, there had been two formal appointments to the stool, the second of which was of the defendant's grantor. The dispute engendered by this duality of appointment was resolved by the State Council concerned

in October 1948, when they upheld the second appointment (that of the­defendant's grantor). This decision was upheld by the Governor and in 1955 an attempt by the sponsors of the first appointee to upset it in thE!" High Court also failed.

The plaintiffs argued that the assent of the Osu Stool itself had been given to their grants and, that as the Osu Stool was the absolute owner of the stool lands, these assents cured any defect that might have flowed from any want of formal authority in their grantor.

Held, (i) whatever the authority of the plaintiffs' grantor, as acting mankralo, might have been, it was clearly determined by the events of 1948. Thereafter a substantive mankralo existed and a purported grant by anyone other than that person was therefore void of effect.

(ii) Where, as with the Osu Stool, the care and administration of the stool lands is vested in sub-stools (e.g., the stool of a quarter, such as the Mankralo Stool in this case) it is the sub-stool which has the authority to make grants of lands within its care to stool subjects (i.e., for a deter­minable estate): the consent or concurrence of the superior stool to such grants is unnecessary and, if given, cannot validate an otherwise invalid grant.

(iii) The consent of the superior stDol is requisite in the case of a grant of an absolute estate but here again the authority actually to make the grant is that of the sub-stool in controL Thus, in the absence of a valid grant by the sub-stool, a purported confirmation or concurrence by the superior stool is void of effect, there being, in substance, nothing that the superior stool can effectively confirm.

Afriyea v. Omaboo and Captwn (1955) High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, January 3, 1955, unreported, followed.

CONSOLIDATED ACTIONS in which the plaintiffs sought declarations of title to adjoining areas of land.

Doe for the plaintiffs. Sowah for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. The two adjoining pieces of land in dispute in these consolidated suits are admitted by all parties to be the property of the Osu Ashanti Blohum Stool, which is the Mankralo Stool to the Osu Stool; in other words land of the Osu Stool, under the control and management of the Osu Mankralo Stool. Each of the parties .to the suits, namely the two plaintiffs and the defendant, bases his claim upon a deed of gift allegedly executed in his favour by the said Mank­ralo Stool. 'fhe deeds of the two plaintiffs were each executed by one Narh Yeboah who described himself as acting mankralo. These deeds are each dated November 11, 1949, and are respectively regis­tered in the Deeds Registry as No. 888/1949 and 889/1949. 'l'he defendant's deed was executed by Nii Nortey Afriyea II, Osu Mankralo. It is dated March 20, 1951, and is registered in the Deeds Registry as No. 386/1957. Had the three deeds been executed by the same person for and on behalf of the stool, the decision would have turned upon priority based upon registration, or upon effective occupation. The defendant, however, disputes that Narh Yeboah was acting mankralo at the date when he executed ,the deeds of the plaintiffs, and contends consequently that Narh Yeboah had no authority then to represent the stool. The onus was therefore thrown upon the plaintiffs to prove that Narh Yeboah was acting mankralo, and that he was vested with full authority to act for and on behalf of the stool.

, The history of the matter is as follows. The Mankralo Stool fell vacant in 1945, whereupon Narh Yeboah became acting mankralo. In January 1948, Narh Yeboah and Nii Adotei Twi III, Osiahene to the stool, installed one Yeboah Kwame as mankralo, while Philip Tetteh Botchway, the Dsasetse of the stool, installed Nii Nortey Afriyea. as mankralo. A dispute then arose as to which of the two claimants to the title was the occupant of the stool; in other words which of the two enstoolments was valid in accordance with native law and custom. For the determination of this issue one Yeboah Nortey, proxy for Philip Botchway the Dsasetse, instituted proceedings in the Ga State Council against Narh Yeboah and Nii Adotei Twi III, Osiahene. By statute the State Council is the sole body vested with jurisdiction to hear and determine matters of a constitutional nature, such as the one affecting the position of Yeboah Kwame and Nortey Afriyea. A judg­ment of the State Council in such matters which receives the approval of the Governor is final and conclusive. In their judgment, delivered on October 11, 1948, the Ga State Council held that the enstoolment of Nii N ortey Afriyea II, on J an uary 29, 1948, was the valid one. A certified copy of the judgment was tendered on behalf of the defendant through Nii Adote Twi III, the second witness for the plaintiffs. It was accepted by the Government. The validity of this enstoolment of Nii Nortey Afriyea II as mankralo was called into question by Nii Okwei Amaboe, Osiahene of Osu Stool, then Acting Osu Mantse, and by Nii Adotie Twi III in 1955, in a suit entitled Afriyea II Omaboe and Others, before the Divisional Court, Accra, presided over by 'Windsor-Aubrey J. In a ruling given on January 8, 1955, the learned judge held the dispute as to the election and installation of Nii Nortey Afriyea II, to be res judicata by reason of the judgment of the Ga State Council.

A person can only act in an office where there is no substantive holder of the office. In January 1948 either Yeboah Kwame, enstooled by Narh Yeboah, or Nortey Afriyea II, enstooled by Philip Tetteh .Hotchway, Dsasetse, was substantive mankralo. It necessarily follows that by the enstoolment of those two persons the stool thereupon be­came occupied by one or other of them; the stool was therefore not vacant from and after January 1948; consequently there could be no question of an acting mankralo from and after that date. In any event, whatever doubts might have existed in the matter from January 1948, the same was removed by October 1948 when the decision of the Ga State Council was accepted by the Government. Therefore, from that date at least, neither Narh Yeboah nor Nii Adotei Twi III could plead ignorance of the fact that the Mankralo Stool was not vacant. and that Narh Yeboah could not therefore act as mankralo; he could only be an imposter when he described himself as acting mankralo in the two deeds of gift upon which the plaintiffs in these suits rely for their title to the respective pieces of land. It follows ~hat these deeds of gift were not executed by the occupant of the Mankralo Stool.

The one indispensable person in dealing with stool or family land is the occupant of the stool or the head of the family for the time being. Any document executed without him by the principal elders of the stool or the family, no matter who their members, is null and void:

Agbloe and Others v. Sappor and Another (1). Consequently I am bound to hold that the plaintiffs' deeds are null and void, and did not vest in either of them any estate or interest in the respective portions of land they claim in this suit. Further, I must hold that the defendant's deed of conveyance is a valid deed.

It was submitted on behalf of the plaintiffs that the Osu Stool, in which absolute ownership of all Osu lands is vested, had consented to and concurred in the execution of their deeds and therefore that any defect in each of these documents was thereby cured. This argument is untenable, because even if the consent and concurrence of the Osu Stool has been conclusively proved, .the native law and custom of Osu is that the stool lands are in the care and management of the four quarters of Osu, namely, Kinkawe, Ashanti Blohum, Alata and Anar­hor. The head and elders of each quarter can grant portions of the land under the control of the quarter to a subject, and the consent and concurrence of the Osu Stool in such grants is not necessary. Further the power of absolute alienation of such lands is 31so vested in the said heads and elders of the respective quarters, and it is only in such case of absolute alienation that the consent and concurrence of the Osu Stool becomes necessary. See the judgment of Manyo-Plange J. delivered on January 3, 1955, in Afriyea v. Omaboo and Captan (2). It is only when a deed has been executed by the proper person that a concurring party can concur in it. \Vhere a deed is not executed by the proper person entitled by custom .to execute it, the deed is null and void and concurrence cannot make it valid.

Thus in the case of a disposition of any particular portion of Osu Stool lands, the Osu Stool is in no better position than any of the prin­cipal elders of the quarter whose consent and concurrence are requisite and necessary to the valid alienation of the land. So if on the face of the document making the alienation it appears that it has been executed by the head of the quarter, with .the requisite consents and concur­rences evidenced by the signatures of some of the necessary concurring parties, the document can only be voidable, and not void; it may be set aside by the proper persons, if they act timeously.

In this case .the plaintiffs' deeds evidence grants to subjects, there­fore the consent and concurrence of the Osu Stool is not required. Further, even if the deeds evidence absolute alienation, the purported consent and concurrence of the Osu Stool will make no difference, since the one indispensable person, the mankralo, was not the person who made the alienation. The consent and concurrence is required to alienation by the head of the quarter, and not to alienation by any other person, which is null and void.

Thus each of the plaintiffs has failed to discharge the onus which is upon him and his claim must fail.

 

The assessor agrees with the judgment.

Judgment for the defendant.

S. G. D.
