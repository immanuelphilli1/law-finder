                                                        November 28, 1957                                                      

                                                     BINORTSOR v. NORDOR

                                                                                Court of Appeal

                                   Granville Sharp J.A., Ollennu and Smith JJ

 
 

Practice-Appeals-Land Court-Power of Land Court as an appellate court to direct retrial by a Native Court-Manner of exercise of power-Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 98, s. 50.

The plaintiff commenced an action for a declaration of title in a Native Court. Before the action came on he applied to the Native Court with a request that the president of the court should not sit as a member of the panel concerned with trying this case. This application was refused. At the trial the plaintiff gave no oral evidence and called no witnesses: he put in some documentary evidence and stated that he relied on his submissions. The trial Native Court found the plaintiff to be estopped by res judicata: this finding was upheld on appeal in the Land Court where the learned judge remarked that even if res judicata did not apply there was not sufficient evidence to permit the plaintiff to succeed. In the Court of Appeal it was argued that there had been a breach of the nemo index causa sua rule, in that a possibility of bias existed: further, that because his application had been refused the plain­tiff had been inhibited from presenting his case properly and adducing all his evidence and that in these circumstances the Land Court ought to have exercised its statutory power to order a rehearing.

Held, (i) to disqualify a person from acting in a judicial or a quasi­judicial capacity on the ground of interest (other than pecuniary or proprietary) a real likelihood of bias must be shown. In the present case the president of the Native Court and the defendant were both members of a State Council, but this alone was insufficient to show a likelihood of bias.

(ii) The power of an appellate court to order a re-hearing is a power that must be exercised judicially. An exercise of the power can only be judicial where there has been some material irregularity in the proceed­ings in the court below, or a strict insistence on technicalities resulting in a miscarriage of justice. The fact that a party has failed to advance sufficient evidence to prove his case or has made erroneous assumptions of the facts or the legal quality of the facts required to support his case does not call for the exercise of the power.

Cases referred to :

(1) Oottle v. Oottle [1939] 2 All E.R. 535.

(2) R. v. Oamborne Justices, ex parte Pearce [1955] 1 Q.B. 41; [1954] 2 All E.R. 850.

(3) Hoystead v. Oommissioner of Taxation [1926] A.C. 155; 94 L.J.P.C. 79; 134 L.T. 354; 42 T.L.R. 207.

APPEAL from a decision of Lingley J. in the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, affirming a decision of the Anlo Native Court" A " on September 19, 1955, in favour of the defendant in an action for a declaration of title to land.

Apaloo for the plaintiff.

Asafu-Adjaye for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. The plaintiff commenced this suit in the Anlo Native Court "A" by a writ issued on February 17, 1955. He sued on behalf of six people described as members of the Gbagba family and claimed, among other things, a declaration of title and damages for trespass to a piece of land situate at Blekusu bounded on the north by Sakitsi, on the south by Bobui and Amegayi, on the east by the sea and on the west by a lagoon. The cause of action, as alleged in paragraphs "b," "c" and" d " of the writ of summons, was the defendant pointing out the land as his to the Native Court" B " of the Some area when that court inspected the land in dispute as the subject-matter of a suit then being tried by them.

On September 19, 1955, the plaintiff moved the Native Court for an order that Chief Djisam III, the President of the Court, should not sit on the case, and that :Mr. J. K. Aidam, the Registrar of the Court, should not be the recorder of the proceedings. The affidavit in support of the application contained three paragraphs: of these, the first and third are formal; the second reads as follows:

"2. That the recording Registrar, J. K. Aidam and the President, Chief Djisam III, bias to one party Mensah Nordor and at the same time a panel member of the same Native Court " A" of Anlo State, Reta, and also a Councillor of the same Anlo State (Some area). And the President also a Councillor of Anlo State, Keta, therefore all cannot be panel members in the same Native Court "A" of Anlo State, Keta, to administer justice in this above title case. The above subject-case will cause malice and malfeasance in the Native Court" A " of Anlo State, Keta. "

The Native Court dismissed the application as frivolous. On March 15, 1955, the return day, they had dismissed a similar application by the plaintiff. Thereafter the case proceeded to trial.

The plaintiff filed certain submissions contending that the matter in dispute was res judicata, and the case proceeded to trial. Having been sworn, he gave evidence in chief as follows: "I am a transport owner and a trader. I only rely on my submissions. I will not further give any statement than to tender my papers as demanded in my submission. " He then tendered certain documents in evidence and rested the whole of his case upon that submission and the documents. He was cross-examined.

The defendant too contended that the matter was res judicata. He adopted the same method as the plaintiff. After he had been sworn he gave evidence that he relied upon submissions he had filed and tendered some documents in support of those submissions; he was then cross-examined.

After the defendant had closed his case, the Native Court recalled the plaintiff and examined him upon some of the documents tendered by the defendant. They then duly considered the evidence, oral and documentary, tendered by the parties, and came to the conclusion that the plaintiff was estopped per rem judicatem by reason of one of the judgments relied upon by the defendant, and they dismissed the plaintiff's claim.

From that decision the plaintiff appealed to the Land Court. After the appeal had been fully argued by counsel, Lingley J. dismissed the appeal, concluding his judgment in the following words:

"I consider the trial court was right in finding this matter had already been dealt with in the courts. In any event it is difficult to appreciate how the trial court could have found for the plaintiff on the strength of his written submissions alone."

It is from that judgment that the plaintiff has appealed to this court. The first point taken by Mr. Apaloo for the plaintiff is the Native Court erred in permitting Chief Djisam III, the President of the Court, to take part in adjudicating on the case after the plaintiff had challenged him on the grounds that he was biased. Counsel submitted that the mere allegation of bias was enough to disqualify Chief Djisam from sitting on the case. He submitted that in the circumstances justice has not appeared to have been done, and the judgment of the Native Court and of the Land Court should be quashed and a fresh trial ordered. Counsel relied on the case of Cottle v. Cottle (1) in support of his proposition.

The principle laid down in the case Cottle v. Cottle (1) is, that to disqualify a judicial officer from adjudicating on a particular case on grounds of bias, it is not necessary to prove or even show that the officer was in fact biased, it is enough to produce evidence from which an impression might reasonably be formed that the officer cannot give the case before him an unbiased hearing. In the case of R. v. Camborne Justices, ex parte Pearce (2), the test of bias is stated as follows:

"To disqualify a person from acting in a judicial or quasi­judicial capacity on the ground of interest (other than pecuniary or proprietary) in the subject-matter of the proceedings, a real likelihood of bias must be shown ... and, further, the real likelihood of bias must be made to appear having regard, not only to the material ascertained by the party complaining, but also to such further facts as he might readily have ascertained and easily verified in the course of his inquiry. "

The matters alleged in the plaintiff's affidavit against the President of the Native Court do not satisfy that test. The only ground alleged is that both the President and the defendant are members of the Native Court " A" and members of the Anlo State Council. Had the suit been in respect of acts of the Anlo State Councilor of the Anlo Native Court" A," the dictum of Lord Goddard, C.J. referred to in Camborne Justices case (2) would have been applicable. The Lord Chief Justice said:

,. If the court were asked to express an opinion they would say that it would be better that a member of a County Council should not sit as a clerk to justices in cases where the prosecution is conducted on behalf of the Council of which he is a member."

But that is not the case here. This ground of appeal must therefore fail.

On the facts learned counsel properly conceded that there is not enough evidence upon which judgment can be entered in favour of the plaintiff. He submitted, however, that the plaintiff must have been so adversely affected by the Native Court's ruling on his applica­tion to have the President of the Native Court removed from the panel, that he was compelled in spite of himself to follow the procedure he adopted by resting his case solely upon his submissions and documentary evidence. He therefore submitted that in those circum­stances the proper thing which the Land Court should have done was to have exercise its powers under the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 50, and remitted the case to the Native Court for a fresh trial. Counsel therefore urged this court to set aside the judgments of the Native Court and of the Land Court and remit the case to the Native Court for fresh trial.

There is no doubt that there is power in this court to make orders which the court below could properly have made under section 50. But the power of the Land Court under the section must be exercised judicially. It is only when there has been some material irregularity in the proceedings before the Native Court, or where there has been strict insistence upon technicalities resulting in a miscarriage of justice that the exercise of the power can be judicial. Where a party has failed to advance sufficient evidence to prove his case, or has made erroneous assumptions of the facts or legal quality of the facts required to establish his case, it will not be a judicial exercise of the powers given by section 50 for an Appeal Court to order a fresh trial to enable the party to make good the defect.

Lord Shaw, delivering the judgment of the Privy Council in the case of Hoystead v. Commissioner of Income Tax (3), at p. 165 stated as follows:

" Parties are not permitted to begin fresh litigation because of new views they may entertain of the law of the case, or new versions which they present as to what should be a proper appre­hension by the court of the legal result either of the construction of documents or the weight of certain circumstances.

" If this were permitted litigation would have no end except when legal ingenuity is exhausted."

That principle, which forms the basis of plea of res judicata provides a good guide in determining what is and what is not a judicial exercise of the powers of an appeal court under section 50 of the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance to remit a case for fresh trial. There is nothing to show that the plaintiff was in any way prevented from putting before the Native Court all the evidence he had in support of his claim; there is nothing to show that he was so upset or otherwise affected by the ruling the Native Court made on his objection to the President of the Native Court sitting on this case, as to disable him in any way whatsoever in conducting his case in the proper manner. On the contrary it appears that he went to court on the day his motion was argued and decided, that is, September 19, 1955, already fortified with his submissions and determined to rest his case wholly upon them. 'l'his is so because hearing proceeded on September 19, 1955, immediately after the ruling had been made, and the court notes show that the plaintiff had by then already filed his submissions. The plaintiff did not behave as a man who had been upset; he went into the witness-box in the normal way, took his oath, and then stated in plain language that he relied upon his submissions and would not lead any evidence other than to tender documentary evidence.

The plaintiff has not been placed at any disadvantage by reason of any technicality; there has been no irregularity appearing on the face of the proceedings to occasion any miscarriage of justice. Therefore this ground of appeal must fail.

[His Lordship then dealt with the arguments of the plaintiff on the matter of the finding of res judie,ata, and rejected them.]

GRANVILLE SHARP J.A. I agree.

SMITH J. I agree.

Appeal dismissed.

s. G. D.
