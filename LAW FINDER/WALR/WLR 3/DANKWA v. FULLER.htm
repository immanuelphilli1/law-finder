                                                       November 4, 1957

                                                      DANKWA v. FULLER

                                                                             Court of Appeal

                  van Lare Ag.C.J., Granville Sharp J.A., Adumua-Bossman J.   

 

 

Practice-Appeals from Native Courts-Application for extension of time to appeal-Whether jurisdiction to entertain application-Method of making application-Native Courts (Ashanti) Procedure Rules, No. 32 of 1949, r. 129 (1).

    The plaintiff, who won in the Native Court of first instance but lost in the Native Appeal Court, applied ex parte to the High Court for an order enlarging time in which to file grounds of appeal. An order for enlargement was made. When the appeal came on for hearing in the High Court, the learned judge ruled that applications under the Native Courts Procedure Rules for enlargement of time should be made by motion on notice and not ex parte. The learned judge also expressed the opinion that the Procedure Rules gave no power to the High Court to enlarge time and that the enlargement that had been ordered was a nullity.

        Held, (i) the order enlarging time was a valid order of a competent court and was thus binding unless and until set aside by the judge on review or by a competent appellate court. It was not within the power of a court of co-equal jurisdiction, or of the same court differently constituted, to declare such order invalid.

                 (ii) Apart from this, the Native Courts (Ashanti) Procedure Rules, 1949, r. 129 (1), by necessary implication confers upon the High Court jurisdiction to enlarge or extend time. Further, an order of enlargement made under r. 129 (1) is not appealable to the Court of Appeal. R. v. Akiwumi and Bannerman, ex pU1·te Dako (1957) 3 W.A.L.R. 137, referred to.

                (iii) Rule 129 (1), which confers the jurisdiction to enlarge time, does not specify that notice of applications for enlargement shall be served. It is not therefore essential, although it is desirable, that the court to which the application is made shall order service of notice of the application, and the court may therefore entertain such application ex parte.

      [EDITORIAL NOTE. This finding overrules the Practice Note reported at 1 W.A.L.R. 138.]

 Cases referred to:

  (1) R. v. Alciwumi and Bannerman, ex parte Dako (1957) 3 W.A.L.R. 137.

  (2) Anane v. Efriyea (1940) 6 W.A.C.A. 169.

      APPEAL from an order of Manyo-Plange J., in the Land Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, on October 22, 1956, quashing an order of Benson J. given in the same court on March 6, 1956, granting an ex parte application for an order enlarging time in which to lodge the grounds for an appeal to the Land Court from a decision of the Asantehene's Court A.2 on January 5, 1956.

   Asafu-Adjaye for the plaintiff.

   Swanzy for the defendant.

VAN LARE Ag.C.J. This is an appeal by special leave from a decision of Manyo-Plange J. striking out an appeal from the Asante­hene's Court" A.2 " to the Land Court, Kumasi, on the ground that the appeal was not properly before him.

The grounds of appeal argued simply involve a question of law, and the facts upon which that question of law depends are 'not in contro­versy. It is not disputed that notice of intention to appeal was properly filed and in time; and it is conceded that the appellant obtained from the Land Court, constituted by Benson J., an enlargement of time in which to fulfil the requirements of rule 126 (2) of the Native Courts (Ashanti) Procedure Rules, No. 32 of 1949, which govern appeals from the Native Courts to the Supreme Court. The filing of grounds of appeal is one of the requirements of rule 126. To put the facts more specifically, I must point out that the period of one month in which to file the grounds of appeal within rule 126 (2), as it relates to this case, expired on February 5, 1956; on February 18, 1956, Benson J., in the Land Court, granted the appellant's application ex parte for enlargement of time to February 21 in which to file grounds of appeal; these were filed on February 9, 1956.

Upon the appeal coming up for hearing Manyo-Plange J. upheld a preliminary objection that because the grounds of appeal were filed outside the limit of the period of one month, as required by rule 126 (2), the appeal was not properly before the court. He also held that:

" where time has expired and the court proposes to extend, the respondent should be given opportunity of being heard as he may have some good grounds of objection."

He also expressed the opinion that the court has no jurisdiction to exercise the discretion given it by rule 129 (1) to extend the time where the period of one month has expired.

The learned judge therefore held that the order of Benson J. enlarging the time was ultra vires and treated it as a nullity. I am of the opinion that this ruling of Manyo-Plange J. is open to serious objection because he was not competent to pass judgment on the order made by the same court differently constituted. The order of the High Court enlarging time has been recently held by this court to be a binding order which is not even appealable: R. v. Akiwumi and Bannerman, ex parte Dako (1).

Even if the order of Benson J. were ineffective it can only be reviewed by himself or so declared by the Court of Appeal, that is, this court. The Land Court as constituted by Benson J. was a competent court which made the order, and unless it is set aside it is binding.

This is precisely what the court had to say in Anane v. Efriyea (2):

" It is simply the blunt and inescapable truth that the Com­missioner's Court, having given an interlocutory judgment in a particular suit, that judgment stands unless and in so far as it may be reviewed by the particular judge who pronounced it or altered by a competent Appeal Court. No other judge of the Provincial Commissioner's Court can come along and say-either ex proprio motu, or on application of one of the parties-' I do not agree with the interlocutory judgment already pronounced by this court and I shall ignore it' ... "

What is forbidden is exactly what the learned judge has done in this case and there can be no alternative but to say that he erred in law.

Further, it must be pointed out that Benson J. had power to strike out an appeal for failure to comply with the requirements of rule 126 and he had a discretion by rule 129 (1) not to do so if he extended the time in which to comply with the requirements of rule 126.

Rule 129 (1):

" An appeal court of its own motion as soon as may be con­venient after the receipt of the Notice of Intention to Appeal and unless it shall for good cause shown extend the period of one month within which the requirements of rule 126 are to be complied with (which power of extension is reserved to the appeal court) shall upon being satisfied that the appellant is not entitled to appeal or that the requirements of rule 126 have not been complied with, order the appeal to be struck out."

If this rule does not give power to the court to extend time I do not know what language is. I hold the view that this rule by necessary implication, although it is not so specifically stated, gives jurisdiction to the appellate court to extend or enlarge time in which to fulfil the conditions of appeal; the ruling appealed from, that there is no pro­vision in the law to enlarge time which had expired in respect of appeals from the Native Courts to the Supreme Court, is therefore not well founded.

With regard to the question whether there must be notice to the respondent where an appellant applies for enlargement of time in which to fulfil conditions, reference to decided cases relating to the question of ex parte applications cannot be of much assistance unless it can be shown that the rules governing appeals in those cases are the same as the rules with which we are here concerned. Appeals are strictly regulated by the particular rules in force at the particular time.

Rule 129 (1), under which the appellate court may extend time in which to fulfil the requirements of rule 126 (2), is silent as to whether notice shall be given to the other side when granting extension of time. Although judges normally direct notice, there is no binding order requiring such notice to be given. In the absence of express enact­ment I am of the opinion that the Land Court is not bound to direct service or notice on the respondent when granting application for extension of time although it is desirable that it shall do so; neverthe­less it is my view that the Land Court is competent to entertain such an application ex parte.

For these reasons I would allow the appeal and set aside the order striking it out, together with the order as to costs. The appeal is referred back to the Land Court, Kumasi, for hearing and determina­tion.

GRANVILLE SHARP J.A. I agree.

ADUMUA-BosSMAN J. I also agree.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
