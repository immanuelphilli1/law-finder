                                                   June 27, 1958

                                          REGINA v. ANTOR AND OTHERS.

                                                    Court of Appeal

                           Korsah C.J., Granville Sharp J.A., Ollennu J.

 

 

Criminal Law-Practice-Jury trial-Directions to be given to jury as to their right to seek further guidance from judge.

Criminal Law-Practice-J ury trial-Majority verdicts-Distinction between statement by jury as to lack of unanimity and statement of agreement to disagree-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 9, ss. 294, 295, and 298.

Criminal Law-Attacking with armed force-Whether" preparing to attack" a substantive offence-Criminal Code, c. 9, s. 342.

Criminal Law-Practice-Joint trials- Verdicts-Necessity for taking ver­dicts from jury at the same time and in respect of an the accused.

The appellants had been convicted of conspiring to prepare to attack with armed force, contrary to the Criminal Code, ss. 49 (1) and 342. At the trial the jury had returned to court for further directions and on their second return had pronounced unanimous verdicts in respect of some of the accused men but had reported lack of unanimity in respect of four others. In the latter cases the foreman of the jury had reported the degree of unanimity: in three of these this was 4-3 and in the fourth, 5-2, although there was no indication as to whether the majority view was for acquit£al or conviction. The trial judge had thereupon given further directions to the jury, at their request, and they had returned for a second time. When they finally returned they pronounced a unanimous verdict in respect of three of the four accused and a majority verdict of 5-2 in favour of the acquittal of the fourth man.

Held, (i) the combined effect of ss. 294, 295 and 298 of the Criminal Procedure Code is to draw a distinction of substantive importance between a statement by a jury of lack of unanimity and a statement by a jury that they have agreed to differ and that shows the numerical division of the jury. In the former situation it is not improper for the judge to urge upon the jury the desirability of their reaching a verdict. :In the latter situation the statement is a decision and upon it the judge must either discharge the jury or, save in the case of capital offences, if the majority is not less than 5-2, accept this majority view as the verdict of the jury.

R. v. Oreasey (1953) 37 Cr.App.R. 179 referred to.

(ii) It is irregular, where persons are jointly indicted and tried, to take verdicts from the jury otherwise than together and in respect of all the accused at the same time.

Sohoni, Oode of Oriminal Procedure, 13th ed., p. 716, quoted with approval.

(iii) In every case where the jury wish to retire to consider their verdict they should be clearly informed that if at any stage of their deliberations before reaching a conclusion they need or would be assisted by some further directions from the judge on any specific aspect of the case, they are at liberty to communicate a request to the judge who, after considering it, will invite them to return to the court where he will read the request and will give them such direction as is covered by the request. In no case where they have reached a conclusion and announced either a unanimous or majority verdict or an agreed lack of unanimity can any further directions in connection with the case be either requested or given.

(iv) The Criminal Code, s. 342, discloses two distinct and substantive offences, "attacking with armed force" and "preparing to attack with armed force." Either of tnese can be associated with s. 49 (1) to give a charge of conspiracy.

Cases referred to :

(1) R. v. Oreasey (1953) 37 Cr.App.R. 179. (2) R. v. Walhein (1952) 36 Cr.App.R. 167. (3) R. v. Philpot (1912) 7 Cr. App.R. 140.

ApPEALS from convictions by Manyo-Plange J. on March 11, 1958, at Accra Assizes convicting the accused of conspiring to prepare to attack with armed force, contrary to the Criminal Code, c. 9, ss. 49 (1) and 342.

Akiwumi, Crown counsel, for the Crown.

Koi Larbi, Heward-Mills and Danquah for the first, second, third and fourth appellants.

Sakyi for the fifth and sixth appellants.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by KORSAH C.J. :

This appeal arises from the conviction of the six appellants who, with four others who were acquitted, were tried before the Assize Judge in Accra on a charge of: "Conspiracy to prepare to attack with armed force persons within Ghana contrary to sections 49 (1) and 342 of the Criminal Code.

" Before dealing with the case generally on the evidence it will be convenient to dispose of the case of the first two accused, Senyo Gatro Antor and Rojo Ayeke, whose appeal is founded upon what their counsel has urged before us as being an irregularity amounting to illegality in connection with the taking of the jury's verdict. This raises a very important question of procedure under the provisions of sections 294-298 of the Criminal Procedure Code.

Section 294 reads:

" When the jury have considered their verdict, the foreman shall inform the judge what is their verdict, or that they are not unanimous. "

It is important to note that under this section the jury shall either inform a judge of a verdict, which means in the context a unanimous verdict, or a general statement of want of unanimity.

Section 295 reads:

" If the jury are not unanimous, the judge may require them to retire for further consideration. After suc!. period as the judge considers reasonable, the jury may deliver their verdict, or state that they are not unanimous."

This section empowers the judge upon the statement of lack of unanimity to require the jury to retire for further consideration, and, in this connection, it would not be improper for a judge to indicate to the jury the importance from the point of view of general. con­venience of their reaching a definite conclusion upon the matter.

In the case of R. v. Oreasey (1), the jury, after retiring to consider their verdict, returned into court and announced that they were un­able to agree. The judge then directed them:

"It is very desirable that you should come to a conclusion one way or the other, because it only means some other jury have got to do your work for you all over again if you do not and that is why it is highly essential that you should come to a definite conclusion. It is a hardship upon all concerned if you do not. The way that juries arrive at their verdict is of course by a method of one member listening to what the other has to say and by a process of give and take. Of course it is a verdict which you must all be agreed upon one way or the other."

The jury retired and convicted the appellant and it was held by Lord Goddard C.J., Sellers and Havers JJ. that the direction was proper. There are other decisions such as R. v. Walhein (2) which apply this principle ..

It is important to note, however, that these cases apply to English juries whose verdict is by long practice required in law to be infamous, whereas the Criminal Procedure Code in this country makes express provision for a majority verdict. This provision is contained in section 298 of the Criminal Procedure Code which reads:

" (l)-(a) When the jury are unanimous in their opinion, the judge shall give judgment in accordance with that opinion.

" (b) If. the accused person is found not guilty, the judge shall record a judgment of acquittal. If. the accused person is found guilty, the judge shall pass sentence on him according to law.

" (2) If. the jury are not unanimous in their opinion, the judge shall, after the lapse of such time as he thinks reasonable, dis­charge the jury:

" Provided that a verdict of a majority of not less than five t~ two shall, in any case where the accused is not charged with an offence which is punishable by death, be held, taken to be, and received by the court as the verdict of the whole jury."

Upon a comparison of sections 294, 295 and 298 it is in our opinion clear that a distinction is drawn between a bare statement of lack of unanimity under the former sections and a verdict showing a lack of unanimity after a decision to differ either to the extent of a majority of 6-1, 5-2 or 4-3. In these instances the decision so announced is a verdict which is entirely different from a mere statement that the jury are not unanimous, and section 298 expressly provides that if the jury have reached such a conclusion, the judge shall in the case of a verdict of majority less than 5-2 discharge the jury and in the case of a majority of not less than 5-2 shall hold, take to be, and receive such verdict as the verdict of the whole jury.

In the present case, it is recorded that the jury retired at 3.25 p.m. and there is the following note by the judge upon the record:

" Court: At 5.20 p.m. jury returns for further direction on the evidence of Adjapong, 7th prosecution witness, as to date and time he was at Hodzokope and time he saw 7th prisoner.

" Court: Relevant parts of Adjapong's evidence read over to the jury and they retire again at 5.40 p.m. Jury returned finally at 7 p.m."

Up to this stage no objection can be taken to the procedure as the jury had not announced any decision and it is clear from the note that the learned judge fully appreciated that the jury had returned, as he termed it " finally," in order to announce their verdict, which he recorded as follows:

" Foreman:

4th prisoner: verdict not guilty. 5th prisoner: verdict guilty.

6th prisoner: verdict guilty.

8th prisoner: verdict guilty. 9th prisoner: verdict guilty.

1st prisoner: verdict not unanimous 4-3. 2nd prisoner: verdict not unanimous 4-3. 3rd prisoner: verdict not unanimous 4-3. 7th prisoner: verdict not unanimous 5-2."

This, in our opinion, is clearly a single verdict, with unanimous findings in respect of the fourth, fifth, sixth, eighth and ninth accused and majority findings with regard to first, second, third and seventh under the provisions of section 298 of the Criminal Procedure Code.

Before proceeding to consider the subsequent conduct of the learned judge we cite the following passage from Sohoni's Code of Criminal Procedure, 13th ed., at p. 716, under the heading:

" Verdict should be obtained once for all at the end of the trial and not piece-meal. ... It is illegal for a judge to take verdicts in respect of some of the accused and then to hear argu­ments and charge the jury and take their verdict in respect of other accused."

Section 301 of the Indian Criminal Procedure Code upon which our Criminal Procedure Code is based contains the following provision, Sohoni at p. 717:

" When the jury have considered their verdict the foreman shall inform the judge what is their verdict or what is the verdict of a majority."

This, in our opinion, is language appropriate to the general pro­vision of section 298.

The majority verdict in the cases of first, second, third and seventh accused was incomplete and it was for the learned judge to direct the jury to make it complete so that he could properly receive and record it. This he could have done by asking them to disclose which opinion was held by the majority, "guilty" or "not guilty." Upon their answering this question he should have received and recorded the complete majority verdict: see R. v. Philpot (3).

Weare of the opinion that it is highly irregular and is illegal after a jury has announced a verdict of a majority, even if the jury ask for further directions, for a judge to direct the jury in respect of such a verdict and return them to the jury room for reconsideration of the matter. This, however, in relation to the first, second, third and seventh accused, is precisely what the learned judge did in this case. His note reads as follows:

" After asking for direction in respect of the evidence against 1st, 2nd, 3rd and 7th accused the jury think with another try they may arrive at a unanimous verdict. They retire again at 7.20 p.m."

It is to be observed from this, that between fifteen and twenty minutes were occupied by the learned judge in complying with the jury's request, but~ unfortunately he did not record any note of what he said to the jury at that time, though he had previously recorded the directions he gave in response to the jury's earlier request for directions as to the evidence of Adjapong. It is to be noted too that the learned judge redirected even with regard to the seventh accused, in respect of whom the jury had given an incomplete verdict of a majority of 5-2, which it was his bounden duty, after directing the jury to make it complete, to hold, take to be, and receive as the verdict of the whole jury according to law.

The learned judge's note continues as follows:

" Jury returned at 7.40 p.m.

" Unanimous verdict in respect of 1st prisoner and 2nd prisoner, 3rd prisoner not unanimous, unanimous verdict in respect of 7th prisoner.

Verdict:

1st prisoner: guilty. 2nd prisoner: guilty.

3rd prisoner: majority verdict of 5-2 not guilty. 7th prisoner: not guilty.

Foreman: In respect of 3rd prisoner there is no possibility of arriving at a unanimous verdict.

Court: Majority verdict in respect of 3rd prisoner accepted. Order: 3rd prisoner, 4th prisoner and 7th prisoner each ac­quitted and discharged."

We are strongly of the opinion that this procedure was wholly irregular and illegal, it being the plain duty of the learned judge in regard to the foreman's first announcement of a majority verdict of 4-3 in the cases of the first and second accused, who are before us as appellants, to have directed the jury to complete their verdict and then to have discharged them after the lapse of a reasonable time, and to refuse their request for further direction, particularly when, according to the learned judge's own notes, this was a request" for further direction in respect of the evidence against first, second, third and seventh accused."

In our view any direction upon any aspect of the case at that stage of the proceedings would have been illegal. For these reasons the convictions of the first and second accused cannot possibly be allowed to stand.

In order to prevent any repetition of the illegal procedure that was unfortunately adopted in the present case, we give the following gene­ral procedural direction: That in every case where' the jury wish to retire to consider their verdict they should be clearly informed that if at any stage of their deliberations before reaching a conclusion they feel that they need or would be assisted by some further directions from the judge on any specific aspect of .the case, they are at liberty to communicate a request to the judge who, after considering it, will invite them to return to the court where he will read the request and will give them such direction as is covered by that request; but that in no case where they have reached a conclusion and announced either a unanimous or a majority verdict can any further directions in con­nection with the case be either requested or given.

The second matter that falls for consideration, and the decision upon this affects all of the appellants, is the summing-up of the learned judge. The charge of conspiracy to prepare to attack is by no means a straightforward charge, and we at first were inclined to the view that it amounted to no more than conspiring to conspire or preparing to prepare, neither of which would be an offence known to the law. After hearing [earned counsel for the Crown however, and upon further consideration, we are persuaded that it is a charge proper to be placed upon an indictment as a conspiracy to commit a substantive offence under the Criminal Code under sections 49 (1) and 342.

Section 342 reads:

" Whoever attacks or prepares to attack with armed force any persons within Ghana, or proceeds or prepares to attack with armed force any persons without Ghana, shall be liable to im­prisonment for life."

We think it is clear from this that the offence of preparing to attack is a substantive offence; so that it can be associated with section 49 (1) which reads as follows:

" (1) If two or more persons agree or act together with a com­mon purpose for or in committing or abetting a crime, whether with or without any previous concert or deliberation, each of them is guilty of conspiracy to commit or abet that crime, as the case may be."

Nevertheless the charge is a complicated charge and the presenta­tion of it to a jury of laymen called for the exercise of the greatest possible care to avoid any expression which might confuse the minds of the jury or cause them to act upon conjecture, surmise or suspicion, against which elements in a case such as this they ought to have been expressly warned.

We find in the course of the learned judge's summing-up the follow-. ing definition of the charge:

" The offence is a very peculiar one. It is not that the accused persons were actually preparing to attack persons with armed force. The charge is that they did together some act the purpose of which was to prepare to attack. It is essential to understand the offence with which they are charged.

" According to the case for the Crown, the act which consti­tutes the conspiracy was an act of training men and, the purpose of this act was that these men when trained were to be prepared to attack with armed force persons in Ghana. The Crown's case is not that the accused were preparing to attack with armed force. The evidence by which the Crown has sought to prove the charge consists of training of men for that purpose. From the evidence I do not think .there is any dispute that there were two camps at Alavanyo and Hodzokope; you have seen pictures of these. In the bush there were two clearings which could be used as parade grounds for drilling."

There follows after some reference to material found at the clearings in question, a reference to section 350 of the Criminal Code in the following terms:

" Military training of three or more persons is unlawful except you get authority. Section 350 of our Code: people who have authority for training do not do it in the bush. "

Section 350 of the Code, which is an offence triable by a magistrate, reads as follows:

" If three or more persons meet or are together for the purposes of military training or exercise, without the permission of the Governor-General or of some officer or person authorised by law to give such permission, each of them is guilty of a misdemeanour."

It is not possible for us to say that ~he jury did not at almost the very outset of the learned judge's summing-up, as the result of what is in our opinion a serious misdirection, form the impression that if they concluded that an offence under section 350 of the Code had been com­mitted they were entitled to convict of the major offence of conspiracy to prepare to attack with armed force, which is punishable with imprisonment for life.

Subsequent passages of the learned judge's summing-up do not dispel our concern as to this misdirection, as for instance when the learned judge returned to the matter on the second day of his summing­up:

"I said yesterday that the purpose of training of men is to resist the assertion of Ghana sovereignty over Togoland."

And again:

" You have therefore found with one of the conspirators, Togbe Gadze, letters showing that he had also sent men to some place. for some course and asking for guns to be sent to them. Some­thing exactly what Adedze, according to his letter, had done. We have the evidence that there was also collection of money for training of Togo soldiers."

And again he states:

" that these men went to Hohoe and returned to Cementpoadze because training does not start until Friday."

As to the reference to ., one of the conspirators Togbe Gadze " we cannot leave this without commenting in passing that the question as to whether there was or was not a conspiracy was the whole question which the jury and the jury alone were charged to answer and Togbe Gadze was not even before the court.

Although the learned judge referred to other matters of alleged acts of conspiracy, such as meetings, speeches and exchange of letters, he so connected these with what he had earlier referred to as the act of training, which amounts to an offence under section 350, and with references to incitement to violence that we cannot say that there was ever displaced from the mind of the jury the erroneous impression that had been created by the misdirection to which we have already referred.

There is however the further objection raised by the appellants' counsel that the learned judge did not adequately present .to the jury the case of the defence of any of the appellants. We feel ourselves obliged to accept this contention upon perusal and careful considera­tion of the whole of the summing-up. As .the learned judge himself intimated to the jury, proof of the prosecution's case depended wholly upon inferences to be drawn from highly circumstantial evidence and was, as we have previously indicated, in our view susceptible to in­ferences based upon conjecture, surmise and suspicion. It was there­fore important that where explanations were offered by the accused the jury should have been invited to consider carefully whether such explanations could bear a meaning consistent with innocence, and that the whole of the evidence of the defence should have been placed before the jury in the same detail as in the case for the prosecution.

In any case the misdirection to which we have referred is such that we are not able to say that if it had not been given, the jury must inevitably have reached the same conclusion. We therefore feel that the convictions cannot be justified in law, and we allow the appeal of each of the appellants, set aside the verdict, quash the sentence, direct a verdict of acquittal in each case and order each accused to be discharged.

Appeals allowed: Convictions quashed

S. G. D.
