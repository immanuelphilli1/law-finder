                                              ASAMOAH v. KOUFU.

                               High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                          Ollennu J

                                                 

 

 


 

 


 

(High Court, Central Judicial Division, Land Court (Adumua-Bossman J.), January 8, 1958.)

Native Oourts-Jurisdiction--Land cause-Action by state in Native Oourt of state-Whether dispute properly cognizable by Native Oourt.

Oosts-Appellate court-Point taken by court not raised by parties.

The head of a state claimed land, on behalf of the state, against a sub-chief of a neighbouring state. The action was commenced in the Native Court of the plaintiff state and judgment was given for the plaintiff. On appeal the question was raised by the court, without having been taken by the parties, as to whether the trial N a£ive Court was the proper forum for the hearing of the action.

Held, (i) it is irregular for a Native Court of a state to adjudicate in a matter which might involve its members in a conflict between their regard for the due and proper administration of justice and their loyalty to the state or stool to which they owe allegiance.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. See Denkyem State v. Abum State (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 235 for a fuller discussion of this principle. Also Quanin v. Adubra and Otu (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 244.]

(ii) Although costs are always in the discretion of the court the normal practice where the deciding point in a case is taken by the court itself without being raised by either party is for an order that the parties do bear their own costs. There was no reason in the present case to displace this general rule of practice.

Ewua v. Wusu (1926) Full Court 1926-1929, 160 applied.

(1) R. v. Sussex Justices; ex p. McOarthy [1924] 1 K.B. 256; 93 L.J.K.B. 763; 130 L.T. 510; 88 J.P. 3; 40 T.L.R. 80.

ApPEAL from a decision of the Awutu Native Court "B " on August 23, 1957, in favour of the plaintiff in an action for a declaration of title to land.

Dua Sakyi for the plaintiff. Sackeyfio for the defendant.

ADUMUA-BosSMAN J. "It is of fundamental importance," said Lord Hewart C.J. in R. v. Sussex Justices, ex parte McCarthy (1), " that justice should not only be done but should manifestly and un­doubtedly be seen to be done." In the light of this principle, it seems to me that where one state, as such, is suing by its head, and therefore for and on behalf of everyone of its subjects, another state or even an individual of another state, concerning any property, the members of

the panel of the Native Court of the state of the suitor or plaintiff, must necessarily have sufficient enough interest in the suit to dis­qualify them from dealing with the same, and in effect acting as judges in their own cause. Common sense and the dictates of ordinary fair­ness require that they should not deal with the suit.

Accordingly, in this case where the Head of the Awutu State is suing a sub-chief of the Fetteh State claiming land for and on behalf of the Awutu State, it seems to me to be impossible to connive at a trial by the Awutu Native Court, however conscientiously, fairly, and objectively, the members of that court may have dealt with the suit. "It is essential," said Swift J. in R. v. Essex Justices, ex parte Perkins (2), "that justice should be so administered as to satisfy reasonable persons that the tribunal is impartial and unbiased."

For the above reasons I have no option but to set aside the judg­ment by the Awutu Native Court in favour of the Awutu Head Chief, and direct that the writ be remitted to the Awutu Native Court, for the plaintiff to take steps to move the Magistrate's Court to report the pendency and circumstances of the suit for the purpose of it being transferred to the Land Court for trial.

As to costs, the point on which the appeal is allowed having been taken, not by the appellant, but by the court, according to established practice the appellant will be deprived of his costs. See Ewua v. Wusu (3) where each of the judges constituting ~he Court stressed the point: Smyly C.J. "As the question of jurisdiction was raised by the Court ... I am of opinion each side should pay its costs." Hall J. "Inasmuch as the point of jurisdiction was, in the first instance, taken by the Court itself, I consider each side should pay its own costs." Gardner Smith J. "In accordance with the practice when the point has been raised by the Appeal Court itself, each side should pay its own costs. "

Appeal allowed.

S. G.
