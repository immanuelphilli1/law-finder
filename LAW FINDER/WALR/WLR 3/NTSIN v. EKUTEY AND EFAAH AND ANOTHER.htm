                                    April 25, 1957

                                   NTSIN v. EKUTEY AND EFAAH AND ANOTHER

                                     High Court, Central Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                       Adumua-Bossman J.

 

Evidence-Documentary evidence-Admissibility-Applicability of rules to proceedings before Native Courts.

Trespass to land-Evidence-Necessity for plaintiff, to show either ownership or exclusive possession.

On the hearing of this claim for damages for trespass to land the plaintiff, before the trial Native Court, adduced what purported to be a copy of a judgment of a native tribunal, given in 1918, and supporting his assertions as to the boundaries of his land. The copy was not certified and so the trial Native Court sought to compare it with the entry in the original record book. The relevant parts of the record book, however, were found to have been destroyed and, as it was thus impossible to substantiate the copy, the trial Native Court rejected it. The oral evidence then adduced by the plaintiff failed to establish either his ownership or his possession of the land.

Held, (i) although a Native Court is not bound by the strict rules of evidence, and in particular by the rules as to admissibility of documents, as is a superior court, yet it would have been wrong to admit an uncerti­fied copy of a document without any proof a8 to its authenticity, and the copy was therefore rightly excluded.

(ii) On the evidence adduced the plaintiff had failed to establish either ownership or possession of the land concerned and therefore was unable to maintain his action in trespass.

Cases referred to:

(1) Kokuma Kwamin v. Dadzie Kwaiku (1926) Full Court, 1926-29, 137.

(2) Abotche Kponuglo v. Adja Kod(jdja (1933) 2 W.A.C.A. 24.

APPEAL from a decision of the Asebu Native Court" B " on Novem­ber 9, 1956, rejecting the plaintiff's claim for damages for trespass to land.

Abadoo for the plaintiff. Short for the defendants.

ADUl\IUA-BoSSMAN J. '1'he plaintiff claimed:

"£50 damages for trespass committed on the plaintiff's land known as and called' Kuku ' ... the said trespass consists of the defendant entering into the said land and ploughing casava, cocoa and yam farms without the plaintiff's knowledge and consent."

He sought to establish that there was an old boundary between his family land and the adjoining land of the defendant's family and that the boundary line, consisting of or marked by certain trees and other features, had been inspected, recognised and affirmed as far back as 1918 in a case between his predecessor, Kwamin Kyei, and the defen­dant's predecessor, Kwao Nkromah, heard and determined in the old native tribunal of the Omanhene of Asebu State, and that the defen­dants had recently crossed or gone over that settled boundary. To substantiate his case he tendered what purported to be a copy of the judgment of the said suit decided in 1918, but upon inspection of the document the native court found it to be uncertified, and they ruled that the old record book of the old native tribunal should be produced by the registrar, who was alleged to have taken the record of pro­ceedings in 1918, for comparison to be made with the copy produced, before they could give their final ruling as to the admissibility of the plaintiff's copy produced.

Upon the production of the original tribunal record book, however, unfortunately for the plaintiff, it was discovered that a substantial part of it had been destroyed by white ants, and no trace could be found of the original proceedings and judgment of the case referred to for com­parison to be made with the plaintiff's copy, wherefore the Native Court had to reject the uncertified copy.

That, of course, was in strict accordance with local authority, one of which was the case of Ko/wma Kwamin v. Dadzie Kwaiku (1), in which Hall J. said as follows:

"During the hearing before the native tribunal, a document was produced by the plaintiff and accepted in evidence by the tribunal, and marked ' A.' The question whether this document was rightly received is of considerable importance in this case.

It is alleged that' A ' is a copy of proceedings and award by the Chief of Dixcove and his Councillors and the Councillors of Kwesi Krome in favour of plaintiff, in respect of these very lands as against the defendant .... According to the plaintiff, he got , A ' not very long after the arbitration took place, but it is not certified, and there is no evidence to show who wrote it. I do not consider that it is to be expected that a native tribunal should require such strict proof of a document as is required by an English court; but at the same time, there must be some limit to the acceptance of un certified copies of proceedings and such like documents, without any proof whatsoever, as in this case. I consider therefore that exhibit' A ' should not have been received by the tribunal, unless some proof of its genuineness and admis­sibility was forthcoming."

The plaintiff being unable to establish the judgment he relied on by means of a certified copy, was left with the alternative of establishing his settled boundary aliunde with other evidence, and for that purpose he gave oral evidence of the boundary line inspected and the trees and features found thirty-eight years ago, and called some of the members of the old native tribunal as well as the registrar, who, in a measure, supported him.

On inspection by the trial Native Court, however, no trace could be found of this old boundary to which the plaintiff and his witnesses had deposed-the trial court observing as follows:

" We therefore inspected the land in dispute and to our utter regret, none of the boundary marks of the disputed area were in existence.' .

But more significant, and still worse for the plaintiff, was the fact that he was unable to show or establish any" occupation and use" whatever of the area in dispute, from which the trial court could draw the inference that the area belonged to him or his family.

The trial court, with a commendable and creditable appreciation of the legal position, said: "In our opinion to succeed in a claim as such, absolute possession should be proved." One is urged to commend the trial court, when one recalls the dictum of Lord Alness delivering the judgment of the Privy Council in Abotehe Kponuglo and Others v. Adja Kodadja (2) as follows:

•• The respondent's claim being one of damages for trespass . his claim postulated, in their Lordships' opinion, that he is either the owner of Bunya land, or had had, prior to the trespass complained of, exclusive possession of it ...

And it is decisive, in their Lordships' view, that the respondent has failed to prove exclusive possession by him, such as is necessary to instruct a title to claim the remedy which he seeks."

The trial court in this case, with the like outlook, found as a fact that:

 ., The whole farms, including cocoa farm in the disputed area, with ages ranging six-eleven years are for the defendants, while the plaintiff had nothing on the disputed area. On the whole we find as a fact that the defendant had not trespassed on plaintiff's land and therefore give judgment for him."

I was satisfied that the judgment of the trial court was well reasoned and amply supported by the evidence, particularly of the use of the land, and I accordingly confirmed it, and dismissed this appeal.

Appeal dismissed.

s. G. D.
