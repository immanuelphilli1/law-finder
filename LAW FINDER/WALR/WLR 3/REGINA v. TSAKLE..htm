                          .                                 REGINA v. TSAKLE.

                                          High Court, Central Judicial Division, Cape Coast Assizes

                                                                      Adumua-Bossman J

 

 

Criminal Law-Practice-Evidence-Judge's power to recall witness-Failure of prosecution to adduce evidence as to an essential ingredient of the offence-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 118.

Criminal Law-Practice-Evidence-Defilement of female-Burden of proof as to period within 1L'hich offence committed-Criminal Code, c. 9 of 1951, s. 184.

The accused was charged under the Criminal Code, s. 184, with unlaw­fully and carnally knowing a female of between ten and thirteen years of age. It is a requirement of this section of the Code that no prosecution shall be commenced more than three months after the commission of the alleged offence and it appeared, after the close of the cases of both the prosecution and the defence but before the final addresses, that there had been no sufficient evidence adduced to establish this requirement.

The Criminal Procedure Code, s. 118 provides: "Any court may at any stage of any enquiry, trial, or other proceeding under this Code call any person as a witness or recall and re-examine any person already examined; and the court shall examine or recall and re-examine any such person if his evidence appears to it essential to the just decision of the case. "

On the preliminary points as to the burden of establishing that the prosecution was commenced within the time limit and as to whether the court could properly order the recall of a witness after the close of the cases for the prosecution and the defence.

Held, (i) where, by statute, a time limit is imposed for the prosecution of an offence under the statute, the burden is upon the prosecution to establish that the prosecution is brought in due time. This is an essential ingredient of the charge and failure to establish it must be fatal to the prosecution's case.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. But ct. the remarks of Lord Mansfield C.J. in R. v.  (1756) 1 East 643n. ; 1 Burr 148.]

(ii) A judge has complete discretion whether a witness who has given evidence shall be recalled after the prosecution have closed their case. This is the effect of the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 118, but it is also a general principle of criminal law quite apart from the Ordinance.

R. v. McKenna (1956) 40 Cr.App.R. 65 followed.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It would appear to be something of a paradox that this principle should stand side by side with the rule as to the admission of rebutting evidence. Thus Archbold, 34th ed., para. 552, p. 190: "In order that injustice may not be done to the prisoner, the calling of (rebutting) evidence after the close of the case for the defendant should be limited to cases where something has arisen ex improv·iso, which no human ingenuity could foresee, in the course of the defence." Also R. v. Harris [1927] 2 K.B. 587 (C.C.A.) and the cases there cited. It may perhaps be said that if the prosecution are precluded from calling evidence in rebuttal of matter raised by the defence, unless this course of action by the defence was unforeseeable, how much less should the prosecution be permitted, after the close of the defence, belatedly to adduce evidence of a matter which far from being unfore­seeable, forms an integral and essential part of their case. Perhaps the answer lies in the closing words of the quoted extract from R. v. ]U cKenna:

"This court will not interfere with the exercise of his (the judge's) discretion unless it appears that thereby an injustice has resulted."]

ADUMUA-BoSSMAN J. In this part-heard criminal cause, after the close of the respective cases of the prosecution and defence but before addresses, the court raised the point of law whether there was sufficient evidence to establish the date of the commencement of the proceedings against the accused. The offence was alleged to have been committed on January 29, 1957, and the requirement of the proviso to the section of the Code under which the accused was charged, section 184, is that " No prosecution shall be commenced for an offence under this section more 'than three months after the commission of the offence." I invited arguments on the point and as to what should be done, or the legal position, in the circumstances.

Counsel for the prosecution argued first, in substance, that the maxim omnia praesumuntur rite acta esse should be applied and the court should hold that the proceedings were commenced in proper time and that it was rather for the defence to raise the point and establish that the proceedings were commenced out of the statutory period. Secondly, that in any event the court has a discretion to recall the police witness on the authority of R. v. Sullivan (1) on the question of the date of commencement of the proceedings, and counsel would apply to the court to exercise that discretion.

Counsel for the accused submitted that the maxim referred to has no application in a criminal case, in which every material fact must be strictly proved, and that in the absence of evidence to establish the date of commencement of the proceedings the court cannot be satisfied that the prosecution was commenced within the time prescribed by the proviso to the section and that was fatal. As to recalling witnesses within the ambit of the principle in R. v. Sullivan (1), that is limited to evidence in the nature of rebutting evidence to rebut something which has cropped up ex improviso during the case of the defence, and not otherwise.

Upon anxious and careful consideration of the arguments in the light of the authorities, I am satisfied that the argument of counsel for the prosecution to the effect that the maxim omnia praesumuntur rite acta esse should be resorted to is misconceived. The English decisions concerning prosecutions under certain statutes under which time limits are prescribed for the prosecu'tions for offences under those statutes uniformly establish the principle that it is part of the case of the prosecution, in addition to adducing evidence of the acts which constitute the offence, to adduce evidence also of the date of com­mencement of the criminal proceedings or prosecution.

A great number of the cases relate to prosecutions under the Night Poaching Act, 1828, by section 4 of which " The prosecution for every offence punishable upon indictment, or otherwise than upon summary conviction, by virtue of this Act, shall be commenced within twelve calendar months after the commission of the offence." It will be sufficient to refer to two cases under that statute. The first is R. v. Parker and Smith (2). In that case Pigo'tt B. reserved the following case for the decision of the Appeal Court:

" The prisoners were indicted for night poaching, being armed with offensive weapons. The evidence showed that the offence was, in fact, committed on January 26, 1861, and to prove that proceedings were commenced within the time required by section 4 of the statute, viz., twelve months from the time of the offence, the warrant under which the prisoners were apprehended was put in evidence, dated February 5, 1861. No information was given in evidence; and the respective arrests took place of Smith on November 27, 1862, and of Parker on January 14, 1864. Counsel for the prisoners objected that the warrant, without the informa­tion, was not legal evidence that proceedings were commenced within twelve months as required by the statute."

The decision of the Appeal Court, given by Pollock C.B., was: "We are all of opinion that the information should have been given in evidence, and as this was not done, the conviction must be quashed." The second case is R. v. Casbolt (3) where Byles J., the trial judge, said of the defence that proceedings had not been commenced within twelve months:

. I am of opinion that this is a good defence both on principle and authority; upon principle, because the statute says that the criminal proceedings must be commenced within a certain date, twelve months. I have no information here that any criminal proceedings for this offence were commenced within twelve months ... , The information is not produced .... That being so and the prosecution being too late, I have in one sense no jurisdiction to try the prisoner and must direct the jury that there is no evidence to find the prisoner guilty, and their duty will be to find him Not Guilty"

Two other cases, concerning prosecutions for offences of the type with which we are now concerned under the Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1885, might be referred ,to. The first is R. v. We8t (4), where Lord Russell C.J. said:

. The question is, is it properly said that the prosecution for the offence of which the prisoner was convicted was commenced within three months after the commission of the offence? I think it was, on the short ground that a prosecution for rape is in fact and in substance a prosecution for any offence of which, on an indictment for rape, the prisoner could have been found guilty.

I think the principle omne maju8 continct in 8e minu8 applies. "

The second case is R. v. Wakeley (5), the headnotes of which are as follows:

. An information preferred within a time limited by statute may be amended after the expiry of that period provided that the date substituted is within the period"

and in which Lord Reading C.J. said as follows:

. It was said by counsel that we must regard this prosecution as having been started not earlier than May 13, 1919, the date when the information was amended, which was beyond six months after November 4, 1918: the date of the commission of the offence .... There is no substance in it, as the date on which the offence was committed was within six months before the date of the original information. If the amendment had resulted in charging the commission of the offence on a date outside the period of six months there would have been great force in the argument. But that is not the case. The information as amended does not charge a different offence, and the amended information cannot be said to be the institution of the proceedings. It was a step taken during the proceedings."

It therefore appears reasonably clear, from the reference to the above cases, that clear evidence of the date of commencement of the proceedings has to be adduced by the prosecution by the production of the information, or other process whereby the prosecution was com­menced, in all the cases where the Act has imposed or prescribed a 'time limitation for the prosecution under the particular Act or statute, so that the court might be satisfied that the prosecution is within the prescribed time. It follows that prosecuting counsel's contention on this point is not well founded and must be rejected. He has, however, asked the court to exercise its discretion and recall a witness to produce the criminal summons, of which he gave evidence but which he did not produce, and has urged that the court has the discretion as exemplified by the authority of R. v. Sullivan (1). In that case, Avory J., delivering the judgment of the Court of Criminal Appeal, said as follows:

" It is objected that the judge himself committed irregularities in recalling witnesses, partly after the accused's evidence and partly after counsel's speech for the defence. So far as that was rebutting evidence to answer evidence set up by the accused for the first time, no objection could be taken. All the witnesses so recalled properly came within the principle of rebutting evidence. The court relies on the decision in R. v. Crippen (6)."

As I indicated to counsel during the course of the arguments, I do not consider that Sullivan's case (1) quite covers our case under con­sideration, the ratio decidendi of it being that the evidence from the witnesses recalled was evidence in rebuttal, and the evidence sought to be adduced here not being such evidence in rebuttal. There is, however, an authority which almost completely covers our case, and that is the case of R. v. McKenna (7):

" A judge has complete discretion whether a witness who has given evidence shall be recalled after the prosecution have closed their case and a submission that there is no case to go to the jury has been made by the defence."

When Byrne J., delivering the judgment of the Court of Criminal Appeal, said:

" At the end of the case for the prosecution, he [counsel for the appellant] submitted on behalf of the appellant that there was no case to go to the jury, on the ground that there was no evidence that the articles in question were made wholly or mainly of iron or steel. The learned Commissioner was undoubtedly impressed by that submission which was quite obviously, on the face of it, highly technical, because, as has been pointed out in the hearing of the appeal, it is not a very great leap of the imagination to think that steam-rollers or traction engines are made mainly of iron or steel. However, the learned Commissioner decided to recall a witness who had already given evidence for the prosecution. That witness having been recalled and having solemnly pronounced on the material of which the articles were made, 'the learned Commis­sioner then held that there was a case to go to the jury. Mr. Caplan's [appellant's counsel] first point is that, the case for the prosecution having been closed, the learned Commissioner was not entitled to take the course which he did. It is, in the opinion of this court, sufficient to say that the course taken by the learned Commissioner is one which has been recognised as being a perfectly proper course for many years. It is only necessary to refer to R. v. Sullivan (1), where it is at once to be seen that a judge, in the circumstances in which the learned Commissioner acted in this case, has complete discretion whether a witness shall be recalled, and this court will not interfere with the exercise of his discretion unless it appears that thereby an injustice has resulted."

It will be noticed from that authority that the trial judge was of the opinion that there was at the close of the case for the prosecution no sufficient evidence for the case to be submitted to the jury and he recalled a witness to adduce evidence to make good that defect, yet the appeal court was satisfied that he had discretion to do so.

Another eminent judge, Darling J., has, in another famous case, R. v. Crippen (6), put the test in this way:

" The matter, however, was one within the discretion of the judge presiding at the trial ... to determine whether it was really fair to allow it (the new evidence) to be given, and whether it did or did not expose the defence to a disadvantage to which it ought not to be exposed."

The letter and spirit of that principle accords with the letter and principle of the provisions of our Criminal Procedure Code, s. 118, which is in the following terms:

" Any court may at any stage of any trial ... under this Code ... recall and re-examine any person already examined; and the court shall ... recall and re-examine any such person if his evidence appears to it essential to the just decision of the case. "

I am satisfied, therefore, that it is but fair to both the prosecutrix and the accused that Corporal Quao should be recalled to produce the criminal summons of which he spoke, but which he did not produce, to establish the date on which it was issued, so that if it should turn out that the date was outside the statutory period of three months as from January 29, 1957, when the offence is alleged ~o have been committed, this prosecution might be struck out as being out of time, but if it should turn out to be within the period of three months the trial might continue to a finality.

s. G. D.
