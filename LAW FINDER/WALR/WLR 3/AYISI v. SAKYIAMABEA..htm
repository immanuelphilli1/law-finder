                                         September 18, 1957

                                         AYISI v. SAKYIAMABEA.

(High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                                                     Ollennu J

 

Land-Family land-Alienation of absolute estate by family-Whether any residual interest in grantor sufficient to support an action.

Land-Family land-Alienation of determinable estate-Forfeiture-Nature of acts in denial of title sufficient to cause forfeiture.

Land-Stool land-Distinction between proprietary and jurisdictional attri­butes of land-Effect of alienation upon jurisdiction.

Estoppel-Action for declaration of title to land-Overt acts of ownership by grantee-Estoppel by acquiescence.

The plaintiff, in this action for a declaration of title to land, admitted that the land concerned had been alienated to the defendant's predecessor in title some fifty years before the action and that the grantees had, ever since, exercised full and open acts of ownership over it. He contended, however, that the original grant had not been attended with the usual customary formalities and on this ground he claimed the right to avoid it. Alternatively he averred that the defendant had contended that his (the defendant's) predecessor had not acquired the land from his (the plaintiff's) family but from another and claimed that this was behaviour constituting a denial of his family's title and entitling him to avoid the grant. The trial Native Court held for the plaintiff on the ground that, by custom, one who grants an estate in land, nevertheless retains title to it.

Held, (i) on the evidence the plaintiff's family had received the pur­chase price of the land, demarcated it, placed the defendant's predecessor in title in possession and conveyed it away for an absolute estate. Their proprietary interests in the land were thereby extinguished and there thus remained in them no interest to be protected and sufficient to support an action.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. Essien v. Duncan (1956) 2 W.A.L.R. 155 shows that the grantors of an absolute estate have a right to escheat in the event of the land becoming bona vacantia but the same case is also authority for saying that this right is not of itself sufficient interest to defeat possession.]

(ii) Even if the grant by the plaintiff's family had been for less than an absolute estate (i.e., for a determinable estate), the acts relied upon by the plaintiff did not amount to such denial of the grantor's title as would cause forfeiture of the defendant's estate in the land.

(iii) In any event, after the passage of forty-nine years during which, to the knowledge of the vendors, the purchaser had exercised overt acts of ownership over the land, the vendors would be estoppd from asserting any rights they might have retained.

(iv) Distinction must be drawn between the proprietary and the juris­dictional aspects of land tenure: an alienation of an absolute estate in land will extinguish the proprietary rights of the grantor but, the juris­dictional attributes of the land attach to and run with it. Thus, for example, where a stool exercising jurisdiction over land conveys away the land for an absolute estate, its proprietary interests are extinguished but, save where the alienation is to another stool, the grantee takes subject to the continuance of the jurisdictional attributes and he owes such duties as this implies to the stool grantor.

The plaintiff appeared in person. Akufo-Addo for the defendant.

APPEAL from a decision of the Akwamu Native Court" B" on May 25, 1957, in favour of the plaintiff in an action for a declaration of title to land.

OLLENNU J. This is an appeal from a judgment delivered in favour of the plaintiff by the Native Court" B " of Akwamu on May 25, 1957. The plaintiff's writ claimed a declaration of his title of ownership of a piece of land the boundaries of which are clearly set out in the writ of summons. He also claimed an order for an interim injunction.

It was admitted by the plaintiff and his witnesses that the land in dispute was alienated to the defendant's mother, one Tanowah, as long ago as 1908, and that the woman and her successors have been in possession ever since, cultivating it and exercising full and open acts of ownership.

In the Native Court and before me the plaintiff contended that although the land had been alienated to the defendant's mother yet his family's title to it remained unaffected because the defendant had never given sheep and drink to his family, in accordance with custom, for boundary stones to be fixed on the land for her; and secondly, because the defendant had been contending that her mother purchased the land not from the plaintiff's uncle, one Ayiripe, but rather from a Krobo man called Sogah, a purchaser from the plaintiff's uncle, and that Ayiripe had only negotiated the sale.

The Native Court found that the sale to the defendant's mother was made by Ayiripe and not by Sogah and held that, according to Akan custom of sale of land, the vendor retains his title to land he sells. Therefore they entered judgment for the plaintiff for a declara­tion of his title to ownership of the land.

The evidence of the defendant that Ayiripe was not the vendor to her mother but only an intermediary was supported by a witness of the plaintiff, and it is unreasonable 'that the Native Court should have rejected that evidence on the grounds that the evidence of the defendant's witnesses was conflicting.

But even if the Native Court was right in its findings that it was Ayiripe, the head of the plaintiff's family, who made the sale, their declaration of the Akan custom is definitely wrong and quite outrageous.

According to native custom, if a family or stool makes an uncondi­tional alienation of family or stool land to a stranger, receives the purchase price, demarcates the land for him, and places him in posses­sion thereof, the family or stool completely divest themselves of all their right and interest in that land, and retain nothing upon which they can afterwards litigate. Distinction must be drawn between the beneficial interest which is alienated and the jurisdictional interest exercised by 'the stool. The sale completely alienates the beneficial interest but the jurisdictional interest of the stool remains unaffected except in the case of sale by one stool to another stool, where other considerations arise.

Obviously the Native Court confused the beneficial interest with the jurisdictional interest. But the plaintiff's claim is not on behalf of a stool, and it is not for jurisdictional interest; it is for beneficial interest.

In any event in a case like this where the sale was made some forty-nine years ago and the purchasers have all those years, to the knowledge of the vendor's family, been in undisturbed possession exercising all the rights of an absolute owner, it would be against all principles of justice for any court to make a declaration of title of ownership in favour of the vendor's family.

The judgment of the Native Court in this case is wrong both as regards its findings of fact and its declaration of native custom. Such a judgment cannot be allowed to stand.

The appeal is allowed, the judgment of the Native Court is set aside including the order as to costs; any costs paid should be refunded.

Judgment for the defendant.

S. G. D.
