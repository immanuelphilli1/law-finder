                                                     November 13, 1958.

                                                        CHUKU v. NKRUMAH.

                              High Court, Western Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                  Sarkodee-Adoo J.)

 
 

Defamation -Slander-Imputation of offence punishable corporeally-" Rascal thief "-Words not spoken without consideration or in heat of passion.

The plaintiff's claim read as follows in his writ of summons:

"The plaintiff claims from the defendant £100 damages for the defendant having, on the 30th September, 1957, at about 11 a.m., come to the plaintiff's office, insulted plaintiff that plaintiff is a " rascal thief," stretched his finger into the face of the plaintiff, and also knocked the plaintiff's mouth, owing to the trade transacted between the plaintiff and defendant."

The action was tried in the TarkwafAbosso Native Court, which found for the plaintiff, awarding him £20 damages for slander. The defendant appealed to the Magistrate's Court constituted by the Government Agent, Tarkwa, who reversed the trial court's decision. The now-unsuccessful plaintiff appealed to the High Court, where consideration was given to the question whether or not the appellation" rascal thief" is actionable per se.

Held, to call a man a ". rascal thief" imputes that he has committed an offence for which he can be made to suffer corporeally. It is therefore actionable without proof of special damage, unless the circumstances demonstrated that the words were words of vulgar abuse, spoken without consideration and in the heat of the moment, and therefore did not impute a felony.

Cases referred to :

(1) Oheetham v. Bannerman (1881) Fanti Law Report 23.

(2) Webb v. Bea.van (1883) 11 Q.B.D. 609; 52 L.J.Q.B. 544; 49 L.T. 20l. (3) A moah v. Djabi (1926) Divisional Court 1926-1929, 43.

(4) Marks v. Samuel [1904] 2 K.B. 287; 73 L.J.K.B. 587; 90 L.T. 590; 20 T.L.R. 430.

(5) Hulton & 00. v. Jones [1910] A.C. 20; 79 L.J.K.B. 198; 101 L.T. 831; 26 T.L.R. 128.

ApPEAL from a decision of the Magistrate's Court constituted by the Government Agent, Cape Coast, reversing a decision of the Tarkwaj Abosso Native Court (Grade" A ") on October 24, 1957. The latter court had given judgment for the plaintiff on a claim in slander.

Sackeyfio for the appellant. Baidoo for the respondent.

SARKODEE-ADOO J. referred to the pleadings, and continued: In Cheetham v. Bannerman (1) it was held that a person cannot succeed in an action for slander in the absence of proof of special damage, if the words complained of merely allege that defendant called the plain­tiff a thief, without specifying what he was guilty of stealing.

That case was decided in 1881, but in 1883 it was laid down in Webb v. Beavan (2):

" The exact offence need not be specified; words involving a general charge of criminality will suffice, provided they impute that the plaintiff is guilty of some offence for which he can be made to suffer corporeally by way of punishment."

In such case no proof of special damage is required. In my view, to call a man a thief naturally imputes that he has been" guilty of some offence for which he can be made to suffer corporeally by way of punishment. "

Sarbah in his Treatise on Fanti Customary Laws, at pp. 93-94, states:

" Words which cause or produce any injury to the reputation of another are called defamatory, and, if false, are actionable. False defamatory words, when spoken, constitute slander. Where a person has been found guilty of using slanderous words, he is bound to retract his words publicly, in addition to paying a small fine by way of compensation to the aggrieved party. 'Words im­puting witchcraft, adultery, immoral conduct, crime, and all words which sound to the disreputation of a person of whom they are spoken, are actionable."

'1.'0 call a man a "rascal thief" naturally implies that he has committed an offence for which he can be made to suffer corporeally. It is actionable without proof of special damage, unless the circum­stances under which the words were spoken demonstrate that they were words of vulgar abuse, such as might be spoken without con­sideration and in the heat of passion, and do not therefore impute a felony (vide Amoah v. Djabi (3)). In the instant case the Native trial Court did not accept the defendant's version of a quarrel and exchange of insults, in the course of which he called the plaintiff a " rascal rogue."

It cannot be contended that to call a man a " thief," or "rascal thief," or " rascal rogue," because he will not pay you a debt, is not actionable. I am not aware of any authority for such a contention, and in my opinion it is not sound law. It is not necessary that the words should have been intended by the speaker to charge a crime; it is sufficient if the hearers might reasonably have understood them as so doing (vide Marks v. Samuel (4) and H1dton and Co. v. Jones (5)).

On the evidence I consider that the plaintiff proved his case, and for the above reasons I am satisfied that the decision of the Native trial Court was right. The appeal is therefore allowed with costs and the judgment of the Native trial Court is restored.

Appeal allowed.
