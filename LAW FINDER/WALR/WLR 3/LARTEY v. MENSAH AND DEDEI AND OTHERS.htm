                                    LARTEY v. MENSAH AND DEDEI AND OTHERS.. 

                                             High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                                                   Ollennu J.

.

.

Family Law-Head of family-Appointment-Requirements as to convening . of meeting of family at which appointment is made.

The plaintiff, suing as head of his family, sought to recover possession of certain deeds. His authority to sue as head was challenged by the defendants. On the plaintiff's own evidence there had been dissension within the family and, at a meeting convened to settle the outstanding differences, the co-defendant, who, it was admitted, had been the head of the family, resigned, whereupon those elders present elected the plaintiff as head in his stead. It also appeared from the evidence that some of the members of the family who would have been entitled to participate in making the appointment had not been notified of the intention to appoint and had not attended the meeting.

Held, the appointment of head of a family should be made by all the principal elders of the family at a family meeting. When it is intended to make an appointment, a notice convening a family meeting and stating the intention to appoint at such meeting should be given to all those entitled to attend and participate in the appointment. Failure to give such notice renders invalid any appointment made at a meeting from which any elders entitled to participate in the appointment are absent, unless such absent elders subsequently ratify the appointment thus made.

ApPEAL from a decision of the Magistrate's Court, Accra, on November 25, 1957, reversing a decision of the Accra Municipal Court on May 19, 1957, in favour of the defendants in an action for the recovery of possession of title deeds.

da Rocha for the plaintiff.

Amoo Lamptey for the defendants.

OLLENNU J. The plaintiff sued, as head of the family of the late Taiko Adam of Accra, claiming the recovery of possession of certain title deeds, the property of his family. His authority to sue as head of the family was challenged. The onus therefore fell upon him to prove conclusively that he had been appointed head of the family in accordance with the native custom.

From the evidence led by him and his witnesses it appears that the co-defendant was the accredited head of the family, but that at one time differences arose among members of the family and a number of family meetings were summoned for the settlement of those differences. At one of those meetings the co-defendant renounced his position as head of the family, whereupon members of the family there present proceeded to appoint the plaintiff as head of the family in his place.

According to native custom the head of a family is appointed at a meeting of all the accredited elders of the family summoned for that purpose. The meeting at which an appointment is to be' made should be convened for that purpose and notice of it should be given to all members of the family entitled, by custom, to participate in the appointment. If then some elders stay away from the meeting, those who do attend can make an appointment in the absence of the former. Where notice given to the members of the family shows that some particular business is to be transacted at a meeting, for example the settlement of disputes, and a head is appointed at that particular meeting, that appointment of a head is null and void, prior notice of the appointment not having been given to all concerned. A member of the family may not be interested in the settlement of disputes between other members of the family but he has by custom an inherent right to a say in the appointment of the head; and it is unjust that some only of the members of the family should appoint the head when others have not had an opportunity of being heard on the matter.

As it appears, on the plaintiff's own case, that notice of the proposal to appoint a new head was not duly given to some members of the family, which members were admitted by the plaintiff to be entitled by custom to take part in such appointment, the plaintiff's alleged appointment is invalid; and unless it was acquiesced in by the other members, it is ineffective. The plaintiff therefore failed to prove that he was entitled to sue on behalf of the family.

It must be mentioned in passing that, while Nee Owieley Ablorh, admitted by all parties to be the most elderly female member of the family in the maternal line, says there are about six branches of the family, neither she nor the plaintiff was able to show that all the different branches were notified to attend even the meeting for the settlement of the differences. For that reason also the appointment, made when the principal branches of the family had not been notified of the meeting to enable them to be represented, cannot be valid.

It is true that the judgment of the Accra :Municipal Court was based on grounds which probably cannot be supported. But a court of appeal is entitled to uphold a judgment, if proper grounds exist upon the record to justify the judgment, even though it cannot be supported for the reasons given by the court which gave it. The Magistrate, to whom an appeal was lodged from the decision of the Accra Municipal Court, set aside the findings of the Municipal Court and entered judgment for the plaintiff, holding that the plaintiff had been properly appointed the head of the family, and that the co-defendant had abdicated. In that, he misdirected himself. It is not enough for the plaintiff to allege that he has been appointed the head of the family; he must prove that he has been appointed in accordance with the requirements of native custom. Had the Magistrate properly directed himself on the evidence as to the alleged appointment of the plaintiff as head of the family and on the native customary procedure appertaining to such appointment, he certainly could not have come to the conclusion that the appoint­ment of the plaintiff was valid.

For these reasons the judgment of the Magistrate cannot stand.

The appeal is therefore allowed, the judgment of the Magistrate is set aside including the order as to costs. Any costs paid to be refunded. The judgment of the Accra Municipal Court is restored but on the grounds stated herein.

Appeal allowed.
