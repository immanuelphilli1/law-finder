                                                          April 26, 1958.

                                                            AMEGBE v. TEPA.

                                               High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                                                 Ollennu J.

.

 

Defamation-Native law and custom-Innuendo that elected chief an imposter -Whether a" matter of a constitutional nature "-Jurisdiction of Native Court-Native Courts (Southern Togoland) Ordinance, c. 106, s. 14A.

Evidence-Judgments-Decision of competent tribunal on chiefly status­Whether judgment in rem or in personam.

Some time prior to the action with which this report is concerned, it was alleged that Odikro Amegbe had been deposed, and proceedings were instituted by the Regent to determine whether or not he had indeed been deposed in accordance with native custom. In those proceedings Tepa identified himself with the Regent, and gave evidence that Amegbe had been elected and installed as Odikro, but that he had been deposed. Those proceedings terminated in favour of Amegbe, the tribunal holding that he was an Odikro and had not been deposed.

Subsequently, Tepa (as was alleged) published words to the effect that Amegbe must cease applying the title of "Odikro" to himself, and that, if he did not, he (Tepa) would sue him before a competent court to restrain him from doing so. Amegbe instituted in the Kpandu District Native Court "A" an action against Tepa for slander. That court found for Amegbe.

Tepa applied to the High Court for the issue of a writ of certiorari to remove the proceedings and judgment to the High Court for quashing for lack of jurisdiction. Counsel for Tepa admitted that Amegbe was a chief properly elected and installed, and submitted that the dispute con­cerned only Amegbe's right to apply to himself the title "Odikro," a Twi title, when Amegbe was a chief in an Ewe state. The assumption of this title, it was argued, therefore put in issue the political or con­stitutional relationship under native customary law between himself and other chiefs in his state. A dispute arising from a warning to him to cease applying that title to himself was necessarily "a cause or matter of a constitutional nature," in which matters jurisdiction is expressly taken away from Native Courts by section 14A of the Native Courts (Southern Togoland) Ordinance.

Held, (i) the judgment of a competent tribunal as to chiefly status is a judgment in rem.

(ii) An action for defamation based upon the publication of words to the effect that a chief, declared by a competent tribunal as such, was an imposter, is not a "cause or matter of a constitutional nature" but a personal suit and within the jurisdiction of a Native Court.

ApPLICATION for a writ of certiorari directed .to the Kpandu District Native Court" A " for want of jurisdiction.

Danquah for the applicant.

The respondent appeared in person.

OLLENNU J. The applicant in this matter prays for a writ of certiorari to remove the proceedings and judgment of the I\pandu Dis­trict Native Court "A," in the suit entitled 'l'ogbc Odikro W. K. Amegbe v. Kwami Tepa, into the High Court for the purpose of quash­ing them for lack of jurisdiction.

The grounds for seeking the relief are ,that the claim before the Native Court was a matter of a constitutional nature, and that causes and matters of a constitutional nature are expressly excluded by statute from the jurisdiction of the Native Court. Section 14 (a) of the Native Courts (Southern Togoland) Ordinance provides:

"Notwithstanding any other provision of this Ordinance, a Native Court shall not have jurisdiction to hear and determine,

" Cause or matter of a constitutional nature" is defined in section 2 of the Ordinance as follows:

" Cause, matter, question or dispute relating to-

(a) the election or installation of any person as a Chief or the claim of any person to be elected or installed as a Chief; or

(b) the deposition or abdication of any Chief; or

(c) the right of any person to take part in the election or instal­lation of any person as a Chief or in the deposition of any Chief; or

(d) the recovery or delivery of stool property in connection with such election, installation, deposition or abdication; or

(e) political or constitutional relations under customary law between Chiefs; "

Section 5 of the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, 1952, gives the State Councils jurisdiction to inquire into and determine a " matter of a constitutional nature." When that section of the State Councils Ordinance is read together with section 14A of the Native Courts (Southern Togoland) Ordinance, and with section 88 of the Courts Ordinance, it is found that section 5 of the State Councils Ordinance gives the State Councils exclusive jurisdiction in " causes or matters of a constitutional nature." There are material differences between the definitions of " Chief" and "matter of a constitutional nature" as given in the State Councils Ordinance on the one hand, and those given in the Courts Ordinance and in the Native Courts Ordi­nance on the other; but nothing turns upon those differences in ~hese proceedings, and I shall not go into them.

The writ of summons by which the proceedings in the Kpandu District Native Court were commenced claims da~ages for slander. The defamatory words alleged to have been published were to the effect that the plaintiff must cease applying the title of " Odikro " to him­self, and that, if he did not, the defendant would sue him before a competent court to restrain him from doing so.

The innuendo appears to be that the plaintiff is not a chief elected and installed according to native custom, and .that he is an imposter. Had the writ of summons been the sole material from the Native Court before me, I would have been inclined to the view expressed by Dr. Danquah, learned counsel for the applicant, that the issue raised for determination is a " matter of a constitutional nature," namely " the election and installation" of the plaintiff as a chief in accordance with native custom. But that is not the case. In suits from native courts, the whole of the proceedings must be looked at to ascertain what were the real issues joined between the parties. It is not enough to look only at the writ of summons.

Learned counsel submitted that it is not in dispute that the plain­tiff is a chief properly elected and installed, an admission which is borne out by the judgment of the Native Court. The dispute, counsel submitted, is the right of the plaintiff to apply to himself the title " Odikro," a Twi title, when the plaintiff is a chief in an Ewe state. By assuming the title" Odikro " the plaintiff put in issue the" politi­calor constitutional relationship under native customary law between himself and other chiefs" in his state. A dispute arising from a warning to him to cease applying that title to himself is therefore necessarily " a cause or matter of a constitutional nature," the juris­diction in which is expressly taken away from the Native Court by section 14A of the aforesaid Native Court Ordinance. I am unable to agree with this submission.

First, the allegation that the title" Odikro " is a Twi term unknown to the Ewe constitution in itself shows that an assumption of that title by the plaintiff cannot raise any question of political or constitu­tional relationship between the plaintiff and any other chief. Had the title" Odikro " been known to the Ewe constitution, there would be some force in the submission, because in that case there would be by custom and tradition a recognised political and constitutional relationship between the holder of the title and other chiefs in the state; and consequently the assumption of the title by a chief must raise the issue of those political and constitutional relationships. But none of those issues can arise where the title is unknown.

Secondly, the proceedings and judgment of the Native Court dis­close that some time ago it was alleged that Amegbe had been deposed, and that proceedings were instituted to determine whether or not he had indeed been deposed in accordance with native custom. It is on record that Tepa gave evidence in that case and stated that Amegbe had been elected and installed" an Odikro " in accordance with native custom, but that he had been deposed. The proceedings in that dispute were determined in favour of Amegbe, that he was an Odikro, and that he had not been deposed from that traditional office.

That decision is a judgment in rem settling the constitutional status of the plaintiff. It is binding on the defendant, as upon all other persons. Even if it were a judgment inter parties between the plaintiff and the alleged Regent, against whom the plaintiff successfully con­tested the claim that he had been deposed, the decision would operate as an estoppel by record against the present defendant, who identified himself with, and gave evidence in support of, the Regent in his claim that the plaintiff had been deposed as Odikro. In my opinion, that decision determined:

(a) that the title of the occupant of the post held by the plaintiff is " Odikro," and

(b) that the plaintiff is an Odikro, and has not been deposed.

The constitutional status having been thus finally determined by a competent tribunal, a suit based upon the publication by the de­fendant of the alleged defamatory words, to the effect that the plaintiff is not an Odikro, is, in my opinion, not a suit for the " hearing and determination of a cause or matter of a constitutional nature." It is a personal suit, over which a Native Court" A " has jurisdiction.

The application is therefore refused, with costs.

Application refused.
