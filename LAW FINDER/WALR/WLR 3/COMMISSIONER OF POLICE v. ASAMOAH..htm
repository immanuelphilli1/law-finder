                                               September 26, 1958

                                  COMMISSIONER OF POLICE v. ASAMOAH.

                                                           Court of Appeal

                           Korsah C.J., van Lare and Granville Sharp JJ.A

 

 

Criminal Law-Procedure when decision of court given-Necessity for record­ing reason for decision-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10, s. 173.

In allowing this appeal from a conviction by a Magistrate the court drew attention to the necessity for a Magistrate to record the reasons for his decision. The proper interpretation to be attached to section 173 of the Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10, which speaks of the reasons for a decision being recorded "where necessary," is that a record is always necessary in the case of a conviction unless the reason for the conviction is clearly obvious and beyond doubt from the facts and circumstances of the case.

Atunde v. Commissioner of Police (1952) 14 W.A.C.A. 171 followed.

Orabbe, Senior Orown counsel, for the Commissioner of Police. The appellant appeared in person.

The judgment of the court was delivered by van Lare J.A., who dealt with the evidence and continued:

In the case before us, and there have been some others in the past, the trial magistrate did not record any reason for his decision and we have been ourselves unable to infer any. We wish, therefore, to take this opportunity to draw the attention of trial magistrates to the desirability of briefly recording reasons for their decisions. This is essential in order not only to help the appellate court but also to satisfy the public and the offenders who are entitled to know the reasons for decisions pronounced against them.

We are aware that the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 173, requires a magistrate to record reasons for decisions only" where necessary." We think however, as was held in the case of Atunde v. Commissioner

of Police (1), that the quoted words nullify to a large extent the main provision of the law which is as follows:

" The court, having heard what each party shall have to say as aforesaid and the witnesses and evidence so adduced, shall consider the whole matter and determine the same and shall either convict the accused and pass sentence upon or make an order against him according .to law or shall acquit him as the case may be, and the court shall give its decision in the form of an oral judgment, and shall record such decision briefly together with the reasons for such decision, where necessary."

We quote with approval the opinion expressed in the above cited case at p. 173 that:

" It is certainly desirable that reasons be given except where the reason for a decision is quite obvious."

In our view reasons for a decision are necessary where there is a conviction unless the reason is so clearly obvious and beyond doubt that guilt can be inferred from the facts and circumstances of the case. If, however, in any case the reason for a decision is not so obvious to an appellate court then the trial court has erred in not recording its reason. We should remember that what may appear obvious to a magistrate or a judge may not appear to be so to the public. Nobody likes a decision against him, more especially in a criminal matter, if it is given summarily without a reason.

In spite of all their zeal for expeditious dispatch of the business of the courts trial magistrates are warned that recorded reasons are better than no reasons at all or vaguely expressed sentiments.

The appeal is allowed; the conviction is quashed; the appellant is acquitted and discharged.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
