                                                           August 27, 1957

                                        POH v. KONAMBA AND OTHERS.

High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                                         Adumua-Bossman J. 

 

Evidence - Traditional evidence - Pedigree - Admissibility of hearsay in matters concerning family rights-Whether such evidence can be given by one who is not a member of the family.

Family Law-Founding-Effect of adoption into family. Succession-Appointment of successor on an intestacy-Nature of right to succeed-Power of court to set aside family appointment-Circumstances in which appointment will be set aside.

The third defendant had been appointed as successor to one Kweku Nte deceased. The plaintiff sought declarations from the trial Native Court to the effect that she, and not the third defendant, was the proper person to be appointed as successor and that she should be declared to be so appointed.

It was not in dispute that the deceased Kweku Nte had died without leaving, in his maternal line, any apparent successor; that he was the descendant of a female foundling, Yaah Boyow; and that there were no descendants of Yaah Boyow (descending through females) who were entitled to succeed him. Both the plaintiff and the defendants were members of the same family group and there were, in effect, two issues between them. The first was as to which of their respective branches of the family group had adopted the foundling Yaah Boyow and should thus be looked to, in the absence of direct descendants of Yaah Boyow, to provide the successor to Kweku Nte. The second was as to the extent of the family's power to pass by a person properly entitled to succeed and to appoint another.

The trial Native Court had found Yaah Boyow to have been adopted into the plaintiff's branch of the family and had granted the plaintiff the declarations she sought. They had noted also that pedigree evidence led on behalf of the defendants was, in one instance, given by a witness who was not a member of the family. On appeal to a magistrate this finding had been reversed on the ground, inter alia, that there had been a due appointment of a successor by the family and that this appoint­ment could not be disturbed.

Held, (i) hearsay evidence of tradition is admissible in matters affecting family rights only if given by a member of the family concerned. Further, the question of admissibility of such evidence must be distinguished from the weight to be attached to it if it is admitted: it is of lessened consequence when brought into collision with a series of definite incidents inexplicable if the tradition is accepted as accurate.

(ii) By custom, the effect of adoption is that of assimilating the adopted person for all purposes into the adopting family.

(iii) Although it is the case that the power of appointment of a succes­sor to a deceased person vests in the family and, although the family in appointing a successor may pass over a person with a prior right to succeed if they are satisfied that in the family interest it would be desirable to do so, yet the person so displaced may not be passed over arbitrarily or capriciously and if this appears to have been done then the court will intervene and order the appointment of the person properly entitled to succeed. In the present case there was nothing to show that the plaintiff was unfit to be appointed as successor or that the family had even considered this aspect of the matter. She was therefore entitled to an order that she be declared appointed as the. successor.

Cases referred to :

(1) Bura and Amonoo v. Ampima (1891) F.C.L. 214.

(2) Kweku Dua III v. Kwamin Tandoh (1926) Judgments of Privy Council on Appeal from Gold Coast, 187~1928, 109.

(3) In re Asamankese Arbitration (1929) Divisional Court, 1926--29, 220.

(4) In re A cguisition for Residential A rea Extension (1931) Divisional Court, 1929-31, 77.

(5) Pappoe v. Wingrove & 00. and Another (1922) Divisional Court, 1921­25,20.

(6) Solomon v. Botchway (1943) 9 W.A.C.A. 127.

(7) A ttipoe and Another v. Shoucaire and Others, Eastern Judicial  Division, May 4, 1948, unreported.

APPEAL from a decision of the Magistrate's Court, Cape Coast, given on October 25, 1956, reversing a decision of the Breman-Asikuma Native Court" B " on March 13, 1956, in favour of the plaintiff in an action for a declaration of the plaintiff's right to succeed a deceased intestate.

Bannerman-Hyde for the plaintiff. Sekyi for the defendant.

ADUMUA-BoSSMAN J. The reasons for the decision of the court given on March 27, 1957, allowing the appeal of the plaintiff and restoring the judgment of the trial Native Court" B " of Breman-Asikuma State in her favour, are as follows:

By her writ the plaintiff in effect claimed a declaration that she was the proper person to succeed to the estate of the late Kweku Akomaning alias Kweku Nte and, a further declaration that the alleged appoint­ment of the third defendant, through the instrumentality of the first and second defendants, as successor of the late Kweku Nte was contrary to native customary law. As she herself put it tersely at the end of her evidence-in-chief:

.• I sue the defendants to show cause why this court should not order that I am the rightful successor of late Kweku Akomaning alias Kweku Nte."

The plaintiff traced the descent of the deceased Kweku Nte as follows: one Yaah Boyow, a woman, begat a daughter, Ampiaah alias Toku, who in turn begat a daughter Nsonwaah, who in turn begat Kweku Nte, succession to whose estate is the subject of the dispute. The plaintiff traced her own descent by her evidence as follows:

" My mother was one Adjoa Akyirefiwa who lived at Asikuma and died. The mother of my mother was' Abanja' who lived and died at Asikuma."

She then explained the connection between her grandmother, Abanja, and the deceased Kweku Nte's grandmother, Yaah Boyow, as one in effect of mother and child, by the following narrative.

"Abanja's sister Mpiti who was sick was sent to a village known as Odua in Breman Asikuma State for medical treatment many years ago. On one Thursday, Abanja and her sister went to Boyow Stream to fetch water ... , When Abanja descended into the Boyow Stream to fetch water she saw a girl who shouted for my grandmother Abanja at the bank of the stream. As my grandmother was afraid, she ran to her sister .Mpiti and Abonyi (her husband) who was standing a few yards from the stream and reported the matter to them. Abonyi followed his wife Abanja to the stream to see the girl. The girl was brought to Odua village . . . . The girl was sent to Trakase village under the care of Akomaning (friend of Abonyi) .... The girl was named after the stream Boyow and the day, Thursday, on which she was acquired as ' Yaah Boyow.' ... Nana Akomaning fell in love with Yaah Boyow and with the permission of my grandmother Abanja, Akomaning married Yaah Boyow .... N ana Akomaning and Yaah Boyow had issue, namely Koramba (f), Ampiaah alias Toku (f), Asamoah (m), Asanewa (f), Dappah (m), Amoakwa (m) and Baah (m) .... The female children of Yaah Boyow had issue but all their children died, except Ampiaah alias Toku. Ampiaah alias Toku begat Adomako (m), Yaw Botwey (m), Nsonwaah (f) and six others. Nsonwaah also begat Kweku Akomaning alias Kweku Nte and one Abena (f) who are also dead."

It is through the females Nsonwaah and Ampiaah alias Toku that by custom the deceased Kweku Nte's family relationship has to be traced.

As against the above-recited story of the plaintiff, .the defendants, by the second defendant who gave evidence for them all, put forward a story admitting that the girl Yaah Boyow was a foundling but that her discovery was by a woman, Sintiwaah, of their family, their narrative being as follows:

"Sometime ago the Headman of this street (quarter) was Aforo-Nkroma who had a cottage known as Odua-Amanfosu where his relatives used to live. One day Aforo-Nkroma's sister Sinti­waah and her son Bannin arrived from Odua-Amanfosu and informed him that they went to bush near stream Boyow and saw a certain girl in the bush and questioned her as to what she was doing in the bush. The girl replied that they should take her home. They accordingly conveyed her to Odua-Amanfosu and thence to Asikuma and reported to Aforo-Nkroma .... Aforo­Nkroma sent the girl to Akomaning who was then living at Trakase village .... The girl lived with Akomaning for many years .... Akomaning married Yaah Boyow, the girl who was acquired by sudden fortune, from the hands of Aforo-Nkroma .... The following were the children of Yaah Boyow: Baah (m), Amoakwa (m), Ampiaah (f), Asamoah (m) and Konamba. Ampiaah's children were the following: Adomako (m), Kweku Fuah (m), Yaw Botwey (m), Nsonwaah (f) and Effuah (f). Nsonwaah's son was Kweku Akomaning alias Kweku Nte. There is no one presently alive from' Yaah Boyow's ' direct relatives."

It will thus be seen that the parties were in substantial agreement, except as to who did find or discover the girl Yaah Boyow, who subse­quently came to be the grandmother of the man Kweku Nte, succession to whom is now the subject of dispute between the said parties.

It was also common ground that both parties belonged to a wider family having a family stool and known as the Ohua Asona family of Asikuma. It was, however, the plaintiff's main point that the parties belonged to different and distinct branches of this wider family, and to emphasise her point she gave evidence of how some time before the present dispute, on the death of one Atta or Atta Panyin, at a meeting of the wider family to appoint a successor to the said Atta, Kweku Nte, succession to whom is now in dispute, was nominated to be the successor to the said Atta. However, the first defendant had protested that Kweku Nte was not of the same branch of the family or of the direct line as the deceased Atta to be entitled to succeed him, and following her protest she had been asked by the meeting to nominate one from the direct line of the deceased Atta. She had nominated her own son called Yaw Banin, who had thereupon been duly elected as successor to the said Atta or Atta Panyin.

The plaintiff's case as to the discovery and acquisition of the girl Yaah Boyow by her grandmother Abanja, accompanied by her husband Kofi Abonyi, was corroborated by her first witness, I\obina Dabasum, the grand-nephew and successor of the late I{ofi Abonyi. As to the second important point, namely, the first defendant's own declaration on the occasion of the appointment of a successor to the late Atta or Atta Panyin, that Kweku Nte was not of the same branch of the family or of the direct line as Atta, this was supported by the evidence of the same first witness; by the second witness, Safohene Bonku, and by the third witness, Yaw Darko, the brother-in-law of Kweku Nte.

On the other hand the defendant's case that the discovery and acquisition of the girl Yaah Boyow was by their relative Sintiwaah was corroborated by their second witness, Kweku Krall. This witness, however, went a long way towards destroying any force which his evidence might have had by his admission in cross-examination by the Native Court as follows:

" All what I have said to this court was told me by my father, Kweku Appiah. My father Kweku Appiah belonged to the Bretu Clan, and not to the Ohua Asona Clan of the defendants ... "

because the type of evidence, "traditional evidence" as it is called, which he gave, is generally receivable only from the members of the same family or clan, to whom it is reasonably to be expected that former elders and members of the same family or clan would impart such information as the history and traditions of the family or clan. In this connection, Hayes-Redwar, in Comments on Gold Coast Ordinances, at p. 87, has referred to that species of evidence as " evidence as to declarations of deceased members of a family."

Be that as it may, the trial Native Court had before them the two conflicting stories as to who discovered and thereby acquired the girl, Yaah Boyow, and it became their privilege to consider all the circum­stances and to decide which they believed or accepted. They decided in favour of the plaintiff that the girl Yaah Boyow had been discovered by her grandmother Abanja, giving as one of many reasons, the circum­stance that on the occasion of the appointment of a successor to Atta or Atta Panyin the first defendant herself had declared that Kweku Nte was not of the same branch or line of the family as the deceased Atta and herself. The concluding portion of the trial Native Court's judgment is significant, and I set it out as follows:

" Although the stories about Yaah Boyow as told by each party were hearsay stories, yet in view of the above facts we found clearly that the plaintiff's ancestress Abanja ... was the real person who acquired Yaah Boyow by sudden fortune with her husband Kofi Abonyi, and therefore the plaintiff is the rightful person, or anyone from her house, to succeed Kweku Akomaning alias Kweku Nte."

On appeal to the Magistrate's Court constituted by the Government Agent, Cape Coast, the learned magistrate, in the teeth of the clear evidence on record by both parties, said as follows:

" It appears impossible to discover from the evidence before the court who did find her. Is it material? In my view it is more important that the plaintiff has not proved to the court below, nor in this court, that she has any right to succeed to the property, therefore she cannot be given judgment."

With great respect to the learned magistrate, I think he did not quite understand or appreciate the case, which involved the question of family relationship according to native customary law, and which, to a non-native, often appears intricate and difficult to understand. I think that the learned magistrate erred in the views he expressed.

As to who discovered or found and acquired the girl Yaah Boyow, the evidence for both sides, to which I have already referred, was quite clear, and the trial court's acceptance of the plain tiff's version so definite, that the learned magistrate should not have entertained any doubts about it nor questioned it. True, the basic evidence was not of eye-witnesses but hearsay evidence; as the trial Native Court them­selves were careful to observe. But it is now common knowledge that the type of evidence, usually designated as " traditional evidence," is widely admissible in support of cases in the courts involving the history and traditions of families, clans and tribes in this country. Thus Hayes-Redwar, ibid., at p. 86:

" In cases relating to pedigree, inheritance, boundaries of land, hearsay evidence or evidence of common reputation is admissible, and it was held in Bum and Amonoa v. Ampima (1) that a much greater latitude is permissible in these matters than is allowed by English law, because of the absence of written memorials in a country where everything, the native law itself included, depends on oral tradition. Besides evidence as to declarations of deceased members of a family, hearsay statements of linguists and of other persons with presumable knowledge of the matters in dispute were admitted. "

In Omanhene K weku Dua I I I v. Omanhene K wamin Tandoh (2) Lord Buckmaster, giving the judgment of the Board, whilst acknow­ledging and recognising the admissibility of traditional evidence, gave a timely, important and necessary caution as to its use or value in the following words:

•• This evidence was traditional, handed down by one chief linguist to another, and as a tradition there is no reason to think that it was not fairly given. But tradition, though of great value when supported by action and facts, becomes of lessened conse­quence when brought into collision with a series of definite inci­dents inexplicable if the tradition be regarded as accurate."

This classic caution has been adopted and acted on in many important cases involving the consideration of traditional evidence, including In re Asamankese Arbitration (3) and In re Acquisition for Residential Area Extension (4), in both cases per Hall J.

Earlier in these reasons I have set out the concluding portion of the judgment of the trial Native Court and observed that it was significant. It was, in my view, significant in that the panel, who could not have been aware of Lord Buckmaster's classic caution to which I have referred, nevertheless by their innate good sense applied a similar test and looked for confirmation or support of the traditional stories told by the respective contending parties, in some concrete action or circum­stance or occurrence established before them when they said:

•• Although the stories about Yaah Boyow as told by each party were hearsay stories, yet in view of the above facts we found clearly that the plaintiff's ancestress Abanja ... was the real person who acquired Yaah Boyow by sudden fortune."

In the light of these considerations it is in my view impossible to sustain the learned magistrate's view that" It appears impossible to discover from the evidence before the court who did find her."

As to the question of the materiality of the evidence as to who found or discovered the girl Yaah Boyow, one would have thought the parties themselves made that obvious enough, namely, that her finder or discoverer became her owner or mother into whose family she b0came absorbed, and from or through whom her family relationship had to be traced according to native customary law.

Now, having regard to the admission by both sides that there was no person tracing direct from Yaah Boyow entitled to succeed, it is clear that by the application of the strict rules of native law and custom of succession, the eldest surviving person claiming through a female or females from Yaah Boyow's adoptive mother Abanja is the lawful successor, as the trial Native Court held. The learned magis­trate erred in holding that plaintiff, who established that she was such person, had not proved either in the court below nor in his court that she had any right to succeed.

However, counsel for the defendants, called upon to support the magistrate's judgment in favour of his clients, argued that the wider family had elected the third defendant and as it was their privilege the election could or should not be disturbed. Now, it is true that succes­sion does not take place as a matter of course, but subject to the approval of the wider family. Thus, in the case of Pappoe v. Win­grove d': Co. and Another (5) Smyly C.J. said:

"In the present case the defendant has admitted that the plaintiff, in the ordinary course, would be the successor according to native law and custom, but claims that (a) before taking posses­sion of the property he must have the approval of the family; (b) that the family have not approved of him; and (c) that the family have superseded him. On the evidence I find ... that the successor has to be approved by the family who ha.ve power to supersede or refuse to approve any member of the family who does not meet with their approval either by his previous or subse­quent conduct, should they consider it necessary or advisable in the interest of the family."

That was a Ga case, but it has been held that, with the modifica­tion that children of six-cloth marriage have a share in their father's self-acquired property, the customary law of succession in Ga Mashie, i.e., Accra proper, is the same as the Fanti customary law of succes­sion: see Solomon v. Botehway (6). Again in 1948, in an Ewe case (where succession is by children), Attipoe and Another v. Shoueaire and Others (7), Coussey J. (as he then was) said as follows:

" In native custom all succession is a question of election by relatives, not of inherent right; the only rigid rule is that the election shall be constitutionally carried out at a proper meeting of the relatives."

But the right given to the family to elect or approve a person entitled to succeed cannot be exercised capriciously and contrary to customary law. A person who, by virtue of his relationship to the deceased, is entitled to succeed, cannot be passed over by the family unless he has disqualified himself by any act which is recognised by custom as a disqualification, for example profligacy, obvious inability to manage affairs or the like. In any event the decision of the family is always subject to review by the Native Court which has the final authority to decide and pronounce whether the decision of the family is in accordance with custom and should remain or is contrary to custom and should as such be set aside.

    I am satisfied that the learned magistrate unjustifiably overruled the well-considered judgment of the trial Native Court. For the above reasons his judgment in favour of the defendants is set aside and the judgment of the trial Native Court in favour of the plaintiff restored.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
