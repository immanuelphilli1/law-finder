                                                      February 26, 1958. 

                                         KORANG v. ATTA AND OTHERS.

High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

(Ollennu J.

                                                 

Land-Trespass-Acts necessary to reduce forest land into possession.

In this action for damages for trespass to land, the evidence of the plaintiff was that some thirty years prior to the action she had gone onto the land, then virgin forest, with the intention of reducing it to cultiva­tion. She had cut wild climbers on a part of it but had not got so far as to demarcate the area she intended to develop. Later, just prior to the commencement of this action, she had returned to the land and, finding that someone else appeared to have taken possession of it, had planted cocoa-yams upon it.

Held, unclaimed forest land cannot be regarded as having been reduced into possession, sufficient to support an action for trespass brought by one seeking to develop and cultivate the land, until such time as the would-be possessor has established the boundaries, cleared the land and, brought it under cultivation.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It would appear to be of the essence of this decision that, applying the ordinary principles relating to the securing of possession, the plaintiff must fail because she had not done all that might reasonably be expected, in all the circumstances, to reduce the land into possession. Thus, the law will presumably protect one who, at the time when the dispute arises, has the animus of possession, is engaged in clearing virgin land and has done as much towards that end as can reasonably be expected. In this case the long lapse of time after the plaintiff had first gone onto the land must be regarded as having caused her to lose whatever embryonic possession she might have had at the outset.]

APPEAL from a decision of the Akyem Abuakwa Native Court " A " on October 31, 1957, in favour of the plaintiff in an action for damages for trespass to land. The defendant claimed to be in possession and denied that possession was, in law, in the plaintiff.

Ampaw for the defendant.

The plaintiff was not represented and did not appear.

OLLENNU J. This is an appeal from a judgment of the Akyem Abuakwa Native Court" A," Tafo, delivered on October 31, 1957, in favour of the plaintiff. The claim is for damages for trespass to land alleged by the plaintiff to be a farmstead and forest situate at Tenponse or Kwayeme near Banso. The elements of a claim for trespass are: possession by the plaintiff, and invasion of that possession by the defen­dant. On the question of possession the only evidence led by the plain­tiff is that some thirty years or so ago she cut the wild climbers on a portion of the area with a view to cultivating the forest into a farm, but, owing to ill-health, she was unable to make the farm, or even mark the forest trees to indicate an area which she intended to develop into a farm. She says that a few months before this action commenced, that is a few months before August 8, 1957, she went upon the land, and finding that someone had cultivated it, caused cocoa-yams to be planted on a portion of it. She was not able to identify the land she claims with any certainty.

The defence was that the plaintiff had never at any time occupied the land in dispute and that even if she had cut the wild climbers thirty years ago as she alleged, that does not by native custom make the area a farm redeemed into her possession, which could later become her farmstead.

As the Native Court themselves properly observed, the acts alleged by the plaintiff, if indeed they took place, could not amount to any­thing more than preparation to cultivate the virgin forest. In my ,opinion forest land cannot be said to have been redeemed into posses­sion until the forest is cut down and the area cultivated into a farm. In my opinion too, an area cannot in native custom be called a farm­stead until it has been once developed into a farm if the farm has become disused, and the area grown back into bush. 'thus even if the evidence of the plaintiff as regards to cutting of the climbers is accepted in toto, she must be held to have failed to prove possession.

The Native Court found that the plaintiff had not been able to identify the land in respect of which she made .the claims, and that· when they went to inspect the land they could not find any partition which distinguished it from Kwaku Ansa's land, the land claimed by the defendant. They, however, gave judgment for ~he plaintiff in the' following terms:

•• Though the farm of the plaintiff contains no cocoa trees and other crops but the land contains a few cocoa-yams planted this­year. we are, therefore, satisfied that the plaintiff really cut the wild climbers for her occupation on this land. we therefore enter judgment for the plaintiff with costs to be taxed."

Such a judgment exposes justice to ridicule. All it means is .that as· the plaintiff found her way upon the land a short period before she commenced the action, an allegation made by her that she had been on the land thirty years ago for a short time must be believed and accepted as conclusive evidence of her possession. In my opinion this finding is unreasonable and totally against logic and good sense. More­over, as I have already observed, mere cutting of wild climbers in the hope later to develop a forest area into a farm, without doing anything more for a considerable time, does not by itself amount to redeeming that area into a person's possession such as to give him the right to maintain an action for trespass.

Upon the evidence on the record, the only reasonable conclusion that a court can come to is that the plaintiff failed to prove that she is owner in possession, as she sought to prove, and her claim should have been dismissed. For these reasons I allow ~he appeal and set aside the judgment of the native court including the order as to costs, any costs paid to be refunded. I dismiss the plaintiff's claim and enter judgment for the defendants with costs.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
