                                                     October 29, 1958

                              AWADZI AND ANOTHER v. ADZONU AND OTHERS.

                                                               Court of Appeal

                               Korsah C.J., van Lare and Granville Sharp JJ.A

 

 

Practice-Native Courts-Visit of members of court to "lows in quo"­Procedure to be followed in reporting to court.

In the course of dismissing an appeal from the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Division, in an action that had commenced in a Native Court, van Lare J.A. made the following comments on the procedure to be followed when a visit to the locus in quo is made by a Native Court.

"In dismissing this appeal however, it is convenient to take this opportunity to refer to a procedural matter. It is noted that when the Native Appeal Court visited the locus in quo, the full panel of the court as constituted did not attend, but the missing member, who was in fact the president, joined in delivering the judgment of the court; it is further noted that after the inspection a member of the panel descended into the witness box and gave a report of the inspec­tion on oath, thereby becoming a witness liable for cross-examination. Although such practice may be permissible by native custom this court frowns upon it.

"The dictum of Lingley .T. in Keteku and Others v. Dzogbenuku and Others (1) is quoted with the approval of this court: 'If the viewer is a member of the court it is sufficient if he makes a report that can be attached to the proceedings in writing. It is unnecessary for the report to be given on oath and most undesirable that the parties should be allowed to cross-examine a member of the court. There should be nothing in the practice to give rise to any appearance of a conflict of evidence or testimony between a member of the court and either of the parties. Members of the court should not descend into the arena of battle in this way. While it is not essential it is desirable that all the panel members should inspect the land; the parties, of course, being present.' "

But there can be no reason against the Native Courts adopting the established practice in the other courts of the country such as is set out in the criminal case of R. v. Dogbe (2) :

" The practice, and to our minds an excellent practice, in several Colonies and we believe in England, is for tne court accompanied by the accused and his advocate together with the advocate for the Crown and such witnesses for the prosecution and the defence as may be deemed material to proceed to the locus.

"The witnesses there point out such places and things as are material to the case and distances are stepped off or otherwise arrived at. The court then re-assembles and the persons who were used at the view are put into the witness box and, on oatn, state what part they took in the recent visit to the locus and what they each did. The defence or the Crown, as the case may be, are then given an opportu­nity of cross-examining."

The practice as above quoted is strongly recommended for adoption by all Native Courts in regard to inspection of locus in quo in land cases.

Cases referred to:

(1) Keteku and Others v. Dzogbenuku and Others (1956) 1 W.A.L.R. 134. (2) R. v. Doghp. (1947) 12 W.A.C.A. 184.

S. G. D.
