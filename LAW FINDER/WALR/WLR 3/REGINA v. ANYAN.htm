                                                        November 8, 1957.

                                                        REGINA v. ANYAN

                                                                               Court of Appeal

                                  van Lare Ag.C.J., Granville Sharp J.A., Adumua-Bossman J

 

 

Criminal Law-Evidence-Opinion of expert witness-Directions to be given to jury.

               The appellant was convicted of murdering his wife by striking her on the head with an iron bar. By his evidence the appellant stated that his wife picked up the bar, that he took it from her and, that as he turned to throw it through a window, raising the bar over his shoulder, his wife came up behind him and received the blow on her head. This evidence was contradicted by that of a doctor called for the prosecution who said that from the extent of the injury it was caused by a deliberate blow aimed at the deceased. In summing up the trial judge spoke of the question of accident as being ruled out by the medical evidence.

Held, where an expert witness, giving opinion evidence, expresses an opinion on the very matter which is in issue before the court (a situation which is in itself undesirable and to be avoided if possible) it is essential that the directions given to the jury should emphasise that the question of fact on which opinion has thus been expressed is a matter for them alone and that they should not regard the opinion evidence as conclusive of the matter.

Cases referred to :

(1) R. v. Rivett (1950) 34 Cr.App.R. 87.

(2) R. v. Murtagh and Kennedy (1955) 39 Cr.App.R. 72.

APPEAL from a conviction for murder by Acolatse J. on Septem­ber 10, 1957, at Sekondi Assizes. Amissah, Crown counsel, for the Crown. Koranteng Addow for the appellant.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by GRANVILLE SHARP J.A.

The appellant in this case appeals against conviction for the murder of his wife. The trial was before Acolatse J., at Sekondi, on Septem­ber 10, 1957.

The history of the matter as the accused presents it is that he, Amale Anyan, a fisherman, had for some time lived with his wife Abrama Alata at Princes Town. They had been married for four years. About two weeks before the death of Abrama Alata, which caused the charge of murder to be preferred against the accused, she had persuaded her husband to move and live with her at Axim, and there her conduct towards the accused was such as to displease him. He stated in evidence that she did not care for him; she did not cook for him and he was a stranger in the town. Four days before her death he was angry with her because she did not wash his cover cloth early enough for it to be dried before night. From then onwards she ceased to sleep with him and in other ways neglected him.

Ultimately she separated from him on the day before her death and slept away from the house, telling him on the following day that she had slept at her father's house. The accused made up his mind to return to Princes Town alone and then divorce his wife. He invited her to the house at Axim for the purpose of collecting her belongings and when she was doing this she took away to pack a cover cloth belonging to the accused, and he tried to take it back. She struggled to retain it and pushed him against the wall, for which he slapped her face.

She picked up an iron bar that was lying on the floor. He struggled with her and took it away from her, and when he was aiming to throw it away she came from behind towards his back and the iron bar hit her on the forehead as he was throwing his arm back over his shoulder in order to pitch the bar through the window. She immediately fell unconscious and the accused left her lying on the floor and ran to the police station and made a statement reporting the matter. Later, after warning, he made a statement which was written down and he signed it after it had been read to him.

The statement was in essence the same as that he made before the jury when he gave evidence at the trial. He was the only person in the room with his wife when she was injured. She died later that day in hospital.

The trial took an unfortunate course when the medical witness, in answer to a question put to him by defending counsel in cross-examina­tion, said that" from the extent of the injury it was a deliberate blow aimed at the deceased." The wound caused by the blow was a perpen­dicular laceration down the forehead three inches long and half an inch deep, and the implement that had inflicted it was an iron bar of considerable weight.

As has already been said, the accused was the only person who knew what had happened at the time when his wife was injured, and when the medical witness gave the evidence referred to great care was necessary on the part of the trial judge to instruct the jury fully upon two matters: first, as to the value of the medical evidence, and secondly, as to the way in which they should approach the explanation that had been given by the accused bearing in mind that it was not for him to establish his innocence. A proper direction to the jury as to the first of these questions would have been that it was not for the medical witness, however eminent, or for the judge himself, but solely for the jury as judges of fact to decide whether the blow was accidental or deliberate (R. v. Rivett (1), per Lord Goddard C.J., at p. 94), and that if they thought that the medical evidence was wrong or that there was a possibility that it was wrong it would be their duty to acquit.

On the question relating to the accused's explanation it was essential for the judge to make clear to the jury three possible positions in which they might find themselves; that is to say, (1) if they accepted the explanation they must acquit; (2) short of accepting the explanation of the accused, if it left them in doubt they must acquit; (3) on considera­tion of the whole of the evidence they must be satisfied of the guilt of the accused of the crime alleged against him. This direction is taken from the judgment of the court of five judges, read by Hilbery J., in the case of R. v. Murtagh and Kennedy (2), at p. 83.

Unhappily the learned judge in this case dealt with the matter in a wholly different way. On the question of medical evidence his summing up contains several unfortunate passages:

(1) "The medical evidence was that from the extent of the injury it was a deliberate blow aimed at the deceased. Thus the question of accident was considered and ruled out by the medical evidence." (2)" Was the defence of accident consistent with the extent and nature of the wound on deceased?" (as to which medical evidence has been given). (3)" Manslaughter to be con­sidered if the defence of accident is ruled out" and (4) " medical evidence must be examined and if the cause of death was the result of the unlawful harm deliberately and intentionally inflicted then the accused must be presumed to have foreseen the conse­quences of his act and the charge of murder would have been proved as consistent with the medical evidence."

\Ve are of the opinion that these directions must have left the jury with the impression that the medical evidence was conclusive of the answer to the very question that it was for them to decide.

On the question of the explanation given by the accused it cannot be said that there was any direction to the jury beyond that they must consider it.

It was hardly enough for the learned judge to say just before the jury retired that " Accused is innocent until verdict given. Benefit of doubt to go to accused if not satisfied with evidence for Crown."


      Apart from the medical evidence, the only other evidence material for consideration on the issue of guilt was the prisoner's statement to the police, which the Crown used. This included the accused's explanation which of course the Crown did not accept.

In the circumstances the last sentence of the summing-up could hardly be interpreted to direct the jury to give the necessary full and careful consideration to the accused's explanation. It is not, more­over, a direction to consider the whole of the evidence before answering the question whether they were sure that the guilt of the accused had been proved.

For all these reasons we hold that the conviction must be quashed. We allow the appeal. The prisoner is acquitted and discharged.

Conviction quashed.

S. G. D.
