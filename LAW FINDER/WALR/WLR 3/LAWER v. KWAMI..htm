                                                        LAWER v. KWAMI.  

                                  High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                            Ollennu J.

 

 

Land-Evidence-Claim for declaration of title-Admission of original title of plaintiff-Burden of proof.

Land-Licence-Effect of revocation of licence.

Estoppel-Equitable estoppel by acquiescence-Occupation of land-Necessity for occupancy to be inconsistent with title ot true owner.

The defendant, in this action for a declaration of title to land, admitted the original ownership of the land by the plaintiff's family, but claimed that he had acquired an absolute title to the land by purchase, executed in accordance with custom, from one Vanderpuye who, the defendant claimed, had purchased from the plaintiff's family. Evidence led by the plaintiff showed that Vanderpuye had at one time occupied the land but indicated that his interest therein was not more than a licence: the defendant was not able to rebut this, but he claimed that, whatever the nature of Vanderpuye's interest, he, the defendant, had been in occupation of the land for a considerable time, to the knowledge of the plaintiff, and had expended money on the land. He claimed, therefore, that the plaintiff should be estopped from asserting any title he, the plaintiff, might have.

Held, (i) a plaintiff, seeking a declaration of tit1e to land, must succeed on the strength of his own case, but where a defendant admits the original title of the plaintiff, the onus of proof shifts to the defendant to show that the plaintiff has been divested of his title. If the defendant fails to establish such divesting the plaintiff is entitled to succeed.

(ii) On the evidence Vanderpuye had been a licensee of the plaintiff.

Therefore, whatever the form of the transaction between Vanderpuye and the defendant, the former could not give and the latter could not receive any greater interest.

(iii) At most, therefore, the defendant was no more than a licensee of the plaintiff and, as such, became a trespasser upon the revocation of his licence that followed from the plaintiff's demand to him to quit the prem1ses.

(iv) Estoppel can arise only where occupation of land is of such a nature as to be inconsistent with the title of the true owner. In the present case the defendant was no more than a licensee and tnerefore his occupancy could not be said to be inconsistent with the plaintiff's title.

Kuma v. Kuma (1938) 5 W.A.C.A. 4.

ApPEAL from a decision of the Manya Krobo Native Court " A " on September 23, 1957, in favour of the plaintiff in an action for a declaration of title to land.

Sowah for the plaintiff.

Apaloo for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. This is an appeal from a judgment of the Native Court " A" of Manya Krobo given against the defendant. The claim, although clumsily worded, is in effect one for a declaration of title, and damages for trespass, to the piece of land the subject-matter of the suit.

The plaintiff's case was that the land was the property of his family; that some years ago his family permitted a stranger, one Vanderpuye, to use it for rearing fish in a pond he made on the land and to grow some trees. The defendant admitted the original owner­ship of the land by the plaintiff's family. His case, however, was that he purchased it from Vanderpuye, a purchaser from the family, and thereby acquired the absolute ownership of it. The suit was instituted when the defendant refused to acknowledge the plaintiff's title to the land.

The general principle of law is that a plaintiff who claims a declara­tion of title to land must succeed upon the strength of his own case and not upon the weakness of the defendant's case. "Where, however, as in this case, a defendant admits that the property in the land was originally vested in the plaintiff and that he acquired his title or other interest in the land from the plaintiff by purchase or other form of alienation, the onus sh-ifts to him to prove the transfer and the nature of the interest or title transferred to him. If he fails to discharge that onus, judgment will go against him.

The only evidence on the record as to the interest which Vander­puye had in the land was that given on behalf of the plaintiff. If that evidence is believed, it proves that all the interest Vanderpuye acquired in the land was a licence from the plaintiff's family to occupy the land.

All the evidence led by the defendant and his witnesses was that Vanderpuye had some transaction with the plaintiff's predecessor in consequence of which, he, Vanderpuye, occupied the land. They did not know the nature of that transaction, they did not know the nature of the title given to Vanderpuye, and they did not know the extent of land given to Vanderpuye. Furthermore, according to the defen­dant, Vanderpuye did not even show him the extent of the land he sold to him.

Thus, the evidence given on behalf of the plaintiff that Vanderpuye was a mere licensee was uncontradicted, indeed I would say the evi­dence that Vanderpuye did not show the defendant the extent of any land he owned tends to support the plaintiff's case that Vanderpuye only had a licence over, and not an estate in, the land. The defendant cannot acquire a higher interest or title than his vendor Vanderpuye had.

The Native Court therefore properly directed themselves in accept­ing the plaintiff's evidence, and making a declaration of title in his favour.

It was submitted by learned counsel for the defendant that he, the defendant, has, to the knowledge of the plaintiff, been in occupa­tion of the land for a long time and has planted permanent trees on it; and therefore, in the first place, he could not be a trespasser, and in the second place, the plaintiff is estopped by his conduct from now asserting that ownership of this land is not vested in the defendant.

As to the trespass, a licensee in possession becomes a trespasser when he continues in possession upon the revocation of his licence. Therefore the moment the defendant refused to vacate the land when he was notified to do so and surrender it to the plaintiff, he became a trespasser and liable for an action for damages for trespass, and for ejectment.

On the question of estoppel, I would only say that so long as a stranger's occupation is of a nature which is not inconsistent with the title of the true owner, no estoppel arises. When such an occupier begins to deny the title of the owner, or begins to do acts inconsistent with, and adverse to the title of the owner, he renders himself liable to ejectment. See Kuma v. Kuma.

Finally, it must be observed that the statement by the Native Court that "the defendant has totally failed because he has not produced any deed of conveyance to support his case" is a misdirection, since, as appears from the record, the transaction between the defendant and Vanderpuye was one entered into in accordance with native custom. Any document made on it would therefore only be evidence of the transaction and not the transaction itself. However, since that mis­direction has not occasioned any miscarriage of justice, but in fact asserted the principle that once the onus shifted to the defendant, the defendant was bound to discharge it or fail, there can be no quarrel with the judgment of the Native Court.

Appeal dismissed.
