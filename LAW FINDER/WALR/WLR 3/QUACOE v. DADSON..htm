                                                          April 30, 1958.

                                                            QUACOE v. DADSON.

                                               High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                                       Adumua-Bossman J

.

.

Defamation-Slander actionable per se-Imputation of commission of criminal offence-" You are an ex-convict."

The plaintiff claimed £200 damages for defamation, averring that the defendant, in a public place in Anomabu, and in the hearing of many persons, had said that he (the plaintiff) was an ex-convict. The statement of claim set out the circumstances as follows:

On the 3rd August, 1957, the plaintiff was playing draughts in Anomabu with Kwesi Ferguson, whilst another person (a school teacher, George Arthur) was looking on. The defendant, accompanied by three friends, arrived on the scene, and one of them (a local authority police sergeant, by name Yorke) disturbed the game, and scattered the pieces with his hand. The plaintiff then put away the board and pieces, and was going away, when the defendant shouted to him to halt, threatened to slap him and began to abuse him. When plaintiff inquired from defendant what he had done, the de­fendant further abused him, and said that he (plaintiff) had been convicted and imprisoned many times, and that he was now" showing off" in Anomabu. When plaintiff inquired from defendant whether he meant what he said-that he (plaintiff) had been convicted and imprisoned many times-defendant replied: 'Yes. You are an ex­convict.' The defendant added that if plaintiff should choose to take action in the High Court, he (defendant) would prove the truth of his statement."

The statement of defence averred that the allegation in the whole state­ment of claim were untrue; that on August 3, 1957, the defendant was not in Anomabu, but in Saltpond; and that the statement of claim disclosed no cause of action, and should be struck out.

Held, the words used were actionable without proof of special damage.

 [EDITORIAL NOTE. In Gray v. Jones [1939] 1 All E.R. 798, on facts similar to the present case, it was pointed out that the basis of such an action as this is not that the words used put the defamed person in jeopardy of a criminal prosecution, but that they cause other people to shun that person and to exclude him from society.

It would appear that the attention of the learned judge was not drawn to Kuku v. Addo (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 306 in which defamation in local

custom was discussed. In the present case, as' in Kwaku v. Addo, it would appear from the size of the claim for damages that the action was based upon English law. The fact that native law and custom recognises a par­ticular cause of action and provides a remedy for it does not preclude a native, entitled to the benefit of native law and custom, from recourse to any other remedy by law provided.]

ACTION for slander.

The plaintiff and the defendant appeared in person.

ADUMUA-BosSMAN J. outlined the facts and continued: There are outstanding for determination (a) the issue of fact whether the de­fendant was in Anomabu at the time alleged, and came across the plaintiff, and published against him the words complained of and set out in the writ and statement of claim; and (b) the issue of law whether, assuming the defendant did utter the words complained of, any cause of action is disclosed.

It is, I think, necessary to remind ourselves that the civil wrong of slander is known to and recognised by native customary law. Sarbah deals with it in Chapter VIII of his invaluable Fanti Customary Law (2nd ed.), p. 113, where he says:

" \V ords which cause or produce any injury to the reputation of another are called defamatory, and, if false, are actionable. False defamatory words when spoken, constitute slander .... \Vords imputing (inter alia) immoral conduct, crime, and all words which sound to the disreputation of a person of whom they are spoken, are actionable."

He also refers to the customary form of punishment in the old days, whereby a person " found guilty for using slanderous words is bound to retract his words publicly, in addition to paying a small fine by way of compensation to the aggrieved part

The civil wrong of slander, then, is known to, and recognised by, native customary law, and the Courts Ordinance expressly provides that:

"nothing in this Ordinance shall deprive the court of the right to observe and enforce the observance or shall deprive any person of the benefit of any native law or custom existing in the Gold Coast .... Such laws and customs shall be deemed applicable in causes and matters where the parties are natives."

The question arises, therefore, whether in all cases where one native complains of slander uttered by another native against him, the court is not bound to apply the native customary law. The old reported cases are all referred to in Griffith's Digest (cols. 57, 58) under the heading" Defamation." They include Cheetham v. Bannerman (1), Afful v. Yambah (2), Accromah v. Frempong (3), Carnor v. Otum (4) and AmoaJ~ v. Djabi (5). These cases were all clearly between natives, and nothing appears in the reports of any of them to warrant the case being taken out of the purview of native customary law, yet all were decided in accordance with the English law of slander. The reason may have been that in none of these cases was the court's attention called to the existence of the native customary law, and to the man­datory terms of the section of the Courts Ordinance to which reference has already been made. The whole question, it seems to me, needs to be reviewed sometime by the Court of Appeal.

Be that as it may, in the instant case I think that regard must be had to the position of the parties, both of whom are literate persons and clerks. Regard must be had, too, to the fact that the whole concept of opprobrium resulting from a conviction and imprisonment for crime is essentially an English concept introduced to the people of this country. Having regard to these considerations, in my view the English law is properly applicable to the determination of the instant suit.

Turning, then, to deal first with the issue of fact, I have no hesitation whatever in accepting the plaintiff's case that the defendant, sometime between 6 p.m. and 7 p.m. on Saturday, August 3, did arrive from Anomabu in the company of three companions (including local authority police sergeant Yorke), saw the plaintiff, and uttered against him the words complained of, in the circumstances set out by the plaintiff in his statement of claim, and substantiated by his sworn testimony.

The plaintiff's case was convincingly supported and corroborated by all his witnesses, particularly the defendant's own companion at the time, local authority police sergeant Yorke, with whose evidence I was specially impressed. He explained that the defendant gave as a reason for his sudden vituperative attack on the plaintiff, that the defendant had been writing anonymous letters to his employers with a view to undermining his position. I find, therefore, that the de­fendant did publish orally of the plaintiff that he had been convicted and imprisoned many times, and was an ex-convict.

I disbelieve and reject the defendant's evidence that he was not at Anomabu at all on August 3, 1957, and that he did not utter the words attributed to him. According to him, after finishing work at about 3 p.m. he went to the store of a friend (one Amonoo) with whom he spent the whole afternoon and evening, until the latter took him in his car and dropped him at his (the defendant's) house about 9 p.m. It is, however, of decisive significance that Amonoo was not subpoenaed, and did not attend to testify on the defendant's behalf at all.

There remains the legal question whether the particular utterance complained of constitutes an actionable slander without proof of special damage. Fortunately we have direct authority to guide us, and need not search through the labyrinth of judicial decisions on slander and defamation. As was said by Bray J. in one of the leading cases (Hellwig v. Mitchell (6)):

" Slanders are not actionable wrongs except in certain cases.

One of those cases in which slander is an actionable wrong without showing special damage, is when the words complained of impute a criminal offence punishable by imprisonment."

In Fowler v. Dowdney (7) it was clearly laid down that:

" An action will lie for saying of the plaintiff' he is a returned convict,' though the words impute that the punishment has been suffered." .

In that case the declaration averred, as special damage, the loss of the customer to whom the words were spoken, the plaintiff being a tradesman; but the proof of the special damage failed. Thereupon counsel for the defendant contended that the words were not actionable in themselves, inasmuch as they imputed no present liability to punish­ment; for (conceding that an offence for which transportation was the punishment was imputed) the words imply that the party had already suffered the punishment. Lord Denman C.J., however, ruled as follows:

" My opinion is that these words are actionable because they impute to the plaintiff that he has been guilty of some offence, for which parties are liable to be transported. That is, I think, the plain meaning of the words, as set out in the declaration. They import, to be sure, that the punishment has been suffered-but still the obloquy remains."

Two earlier decisions on the same lines were Gainford v. Tuke (8) and Boston v. Tantam (9). The report in Gainford v. Tuke was to the following effect:

" , Thou wast in Launceston gaol for coining.' The plaintiff replied: 'If I was there, I answered it well enough.' 'rhe defen­dant said: 'Yes! you were burnt in the hand for it.' Upon not guilty pleaded, but verdict for the plaintiff after trial, it was moved in arrest of judgment, that these words are not actionable; for to say that one was in gaol for coining, no action lies because it is no affirmation that he did coin ... , nut the court resolved to the contrary that these are malicious words, and show intent to accuse him for being imprisoned for coining ... wherefore it was adjudged that the action well lay."

The report in Boston v. Tantam was to the following effect:

" Action for these words: 'He was a thief and stole my gold.' After not guilty pleaded, and (verdict) found for plaintiff, it was moved in arrest of judgment that these words are not actionable, for it may be that it was when he was a child or in the time of Queen Elizabeth, or before, since which hath been divers general pardons so as there cannot any loss happen to him, nor any scandal, when the time is so uncertain; for' was' intends the past time and not that he is so at the time of the speaking. It shall be intended to be maliciously spoken and to discredit him. And it is a great scandal to be once a thief; for although a pardon may discharge him of the punishment, yet the scandal of the offence remains, for poena potest redimi, culpa pc Tennis erit, and it ought not be intended that it was when he was a child. 'Wherefore it was adjudged for plaintiff."

Those were cases where the plaintiff recovered judgment though he had actually been convicted, and where there might be some excuse for a layman, ignorant of the intricacies of the law of slander, and not knowing that he should be careful in speaking of a person's past. No such excuse is available to the defendant in this case, for there was no truth whatsoever in the imputation that the plaintiff had committed criminal offences, had been convicted and sent to prison several times, and had returned. The slander in this case, not sought in any way to be justified and obviously quite incapable of justification, is clearly actionable per se, and I am satisfied that the defendant is liable in damages. In all the circumstances I award to the plaintiff £50 damages against the defendant, with costs.

Judgment for the plaintiff.

.
