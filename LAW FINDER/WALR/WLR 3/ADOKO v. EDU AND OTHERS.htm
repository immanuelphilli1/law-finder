                                                    July 19, 1958

                                        ADOKO v. EDU AND OTHERS.

                             High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                       Ollennu J.

 

 

Prohibition--State Councils-Excess of jurisdiction--Whether a dispute con­cerning participation in traditional functions a "matter of a constitu­tional nature "-State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 2.

State Councils-Jurisdiction- Whether State Councils entitled to use statutory powers for compelling attendance in connection with statutory jurisdiction-State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s.5.

State Councils-Jurisdiction--Means to be used for enforcing non-statutory jurisdiction--State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 8 of 1952, s. 32.

The Senior Supi (Kwesi Adoko), and other members of the No. 3 (Anafo) Asafo Company of Mankessim complained that Kobina Edu and some other residents of the Anafo Quarter were refraining from taking part with the No.3 Company in traditional functions and Asafo displays, and were no longer joining with the Company in the administration of the Anafo Quarter. The Senior Supi and his supporters issued a writ in the Mankessim State Council on May 10, 1958, requiring Edu and his friends to appear before the State Council to answer the complaint.

Edu and his friends applied to the High Court for an order of prohibition to be directed to the State Council, on the ground that the Council had no jurisdiction under the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, 1952, to entertain the matter.

Held, (i) the use of the powers conferred upon a State Council by section 5 (3) of the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance is con­fined to circumstances in which the State Council is exercising the jurisdiction conferred upon it by section 5 (1) of the Ordinance; the powers may not be used for the exercise of any other jurisdiction that may be possessed by a State Council.

(ii) If a State Council resorts to the powers conferred on it by section 5 (3) of the Ordinance, it is purporting to exercise the jurisdiction conferred on it by section 5 (1), i.e., jurisdiction in "matter of a consti­tutional nature." The State Council had so resorted in the instant case.

(iii) The complaint in the instant case was not" matter of a consti­tutional nature" as defined by section 2 of the Ordinance, and an order of prohibition must issue.

Obiter. It would appear that, if a State Council possesses a juris­diction over and above that conferred by the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, the procedural means for exercising such jurisdiction may be found within section 32 of the Ordinance.

Case referred to:

Kokro and Others v. Enum and Kokro and Others v. Tandoh and Others (consolidated) (1953) 14 W.A.C.A. 313.

ApPLICATION for order of prohibition against the Mankessim State Council in connection with the trial of a suit entitled Adoko v. Edu and Others.

Short for the applicant.

Bcntsi Enchill for the respondent.

OLLENNU J. referred to the pleadings, and continued: The ground for the application is that the State Council of Mankessim has no jurisdiction under the State Councils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, 1952, to entertain the matter which is the subject-matter of the writ in the case of Adoko v. Edu and Others.

In reply it has been contended on behalf of the respondent that the judicial powers of a State Council are not limited to the matters in respect of which jurisdiction is given to it by the Ordinance, but that a State Council (as the highest council in the state) is by native custom vested with inherent jurisdiction to inquire into matters which are not purely "matters of a constitutional nature" as defined by the Ordinance, and which do not normally come within the jurisdic­tion of a Native Court. In support of that, submission counsel has referred the court to the cases of Kokro and Others v. Enum, and Kokro and Others v. Tandoh and Others (consolidated).

The decision in the case cited was given on the basis that the vesting of the State Councils with certain statutory powers under the State Councils Ordinance did not take away the inherent jurisdiction and powers which a State Council by native custom possesses as the highest council in the administration of the state. Section 5 of the Ordinance gives the State Council that additional jurisdiction; sub­section (1) reads as follows:

" Subject to the provisions of subsection (2) of this section and of section 8 of this Ordinance, a State Council shall have jurisdiction to inquire into and determine any matter of a con­stitutional nature, arising within the area of authority of such State Council, in which the parties are members of or subject to the authority of such State Council under the provisions of this Ordinance or according to cus,tomary law."

Subsection (3) provides as follows:

"In the exercise of the jurisdiction conferred upon it by subsection (1) of this section, a State Council shall conduct its proceedings according to customary law, but for the purposes of compelling the attendance of parties and witnesses and .the pro­duction of documents, a State Council shall, subject to the pro­visions of section 35 of this Ordinance, have the like powers as are possessed by a Magistrate's Court in the exercise of its civil jurisdiction. "

The powers of a Magistrate's Court in the exercise of its civil juris­diction to compel the attendance of parties include the powers to issue writs of summons and hearing notices.

Section 32 (1) of the Ordinance provides as follows:

" It shall be the duty of every person who, under customary law, is subject to the jurisdiction of a State Councilor a chief, when thereto required by such State Councilor chief, to assist in carrying out the duties imposed upon such State Councilor chief by this or any other Ordinance or by any customary law; and every person so required by a State Councilor a chief shall be deemed to be empowered to do all that may be reasonably neces­sary to give effect to any lawful order given by such State Council or chief."

Subsection (2) provides:

" Every person who, being required by a State Councilor a chief to assist in carrying out the duties imposed upon such State Councilor chief, by ... any customary law, fails without reason­able excuse so to do shall be guilty of an offence and shall be liable to a fine not exceeding five pounds."

In my opinion, this section 32 indicates that there are methods, other than the powers and jurisdiction given to it by section 5, by which a State Council can ensure the attendance of parties in matters in which the State Council is exercising its powers and jurisdiction if it requires the attendance or assistance of a person subject to its jurisdiction, particularly in the exercise of those powers inherent in it under native customary law.

It is my opinion, therefore, that where a State Council issues a writ of summons as provided under section 5 (3) of the Ordinance, it does so only in the exercise of the statutory jurisdiction vested in it by section 5 (1) of the Ordinance. Consequently, I must hold that the issue of the writ of summons in this case on May 10, 1958, was done by the Mankessim State Council in the purported exercise of its juris­diction under section 5 (1) of the State Councils Ordinance, and not otherwise.

Now the jurisdiction which section 5 (1) gives to a State Council is "to inquire into and determine any matter of a constitutional nature." That expression is defined in section 2 as follows:

"Matter of a constitutional nature means a cause, matter, question or dispute relating to-

(a) nomination, election and installation of any person as a chief or the claim of any person to be elected or installed as a chief; or

(b) the deposition or abdication of any chief; or

(c) the right of any person to take part in the election or instal­lation of any person as a chief or in the deposition of any chief; or

(d) the recovery or delivery of stool property in connection with any such election, installation, deposition or abdication; or (e) political or constitutional relations under customary law between chiefs."

The question for me to determine, therefore, is whether the com­plaint on the indorsement of the writ in this case comes within any of the five classes of " matter of a constitutional nature" as defined under section 2 of the Ordinance. Having examined the "Tit very care­fully I have come to the conclusion that it does not do so, and that in consequence there was no jurisdiction in the Mankessim S~ate Coun­cil under section 5 (1) of the Ordinance to hear and determine that suit. There being no jurisdiction in the said State Council, I am bound in law to prohibit it from hearing and determining that suit. I so order.

I have already made it clear in this ruling that the inherent juris­diction and powers of a State Council as the highest council in the state to hear and determine matters which cannot be described as " matters of a constitutional nature" (as defined under the Ordinance) is in no way affected by the enactment of the said State Councils Ordinance. But it appears to me no less clear that, if the State Council purports to compel attendance of parties and witnesses by the process whereby a Magistrate's Court is by law entitled to compel attendance of witnesses, then the only legal interpretation to be placed upon its action in issuing such process is that it is purporting to exercise the statutory jurisdiction vested in it by section 5 (1) of the Ordinance.

For these reasons I grant the prayer of the applicants, and order prohibition to issue, with costs.

Prohibition to issue.
