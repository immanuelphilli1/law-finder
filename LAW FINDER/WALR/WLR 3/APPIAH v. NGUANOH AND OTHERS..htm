                                                                           August 27, 1957

                          .              APPIAH v. NGUANOH AND OTHERS.

                                        High Court, Central Judicial Division. Divisional Court

                                                                      Adumua-Bossman J

 

 

Bills of Exchange-Promissory note-Whether joint or joint and several liability-" We, the Elders, Stoolholders, Supis and Councillors hereby promise ... "-Bills of Exchange Ordinance, c. 195 of 1951, s. 86 (1).

Bills of Exchange-Promissory note-Agency-Whether signatories in a repre­sentative character or incurring personal liability-Signatures in form of traditional styles or titles-Bills of Exchange Ordinance, c. 195 of 1951, s.26.

The plaintiff sued to recover £450 on a promissory note signed by the twelve defendants, whom the plaintiff claimed to be jointly and severally liable. The promissory note was in the following terms:

" We, the undersigned Elders, Stoolholders, Supis and Councillors of the Edina State, do hereby faithfully promise to pay to Mr. T. E. W. Appiah, at the expiration of one year's time from today's date the sum of (£450) Four hundred and fifty pounds being amount borrowed to meet the legal expenses incurred in connection with the Buranta dispute.

Dated at Elmina this 10th day of October, 1951."

To this were subscribed the signatures of the defendants, who signed not in their personal names but by their traditional styles or titles as office­holders in the state.

The defendants took the preliminary point that the plaintiff's claim failed to disclose any cause of action; they further denied joint or joint and several liability.

By the Bills of Exchange Ordinance, s. 86 (1), "A promissory note may be made by two or more makers, and they may be liable thereon jointly, or jointly and severally, according to its tenor." This is in identical terms with the Bills of Exchange Act, 1882, s. 85 (1). The Bills of Exchange Ordinance, s. 26, reads :

     " (1) 'Where a person signs a bill as drawer, endorser or acceptor, and adds words to his signature, indicating that he signs for or on behalf of a principal, or in a representative character, he is not personally liable thereon; but the mere addition to his signature of words describing him as an agent, or as filling a representative character, does not exempt him from personal liability.

(2) In determining whether a signature on a bill js that of the principal or that of the agent by whose hands it is written, the con­struction most favourable to the validity of the instrument shall be adopted. "

Held, (i) givjng their plain grammatical meaning to the words used on face cif the note no individual or several liability attached to the signatories.

(ii) In view of the following the promise, " We, the Elders, Stoolholders, Supis and Councillors of the Edina State do hereby faithfully promise ... "; the styles of the various signatures; and, applying the Bills of Exchange Ordinance, s. 26, the signatories must be regarded as agents, acting for and on behalf of the Edina State as principal and without incurring personal liability.

Cases referred to:

(1) Leadbitter v. Farrow (1816) 5 M. & S. 345; 105 E.R. 1077.

(2) Rew and Another, Exors. 0/ Newton v. Pettet and Others (1834) 1 Ad. & E. 196; 110 E.R. 118l.

(3) Dutton v. Marsh and Others (1871) L.R. 6 Q.B. 36l.

(4) Elliott v. Bax-Ironside [1952] 2 K.B. 3Ol.

(5) Healey v. Story and Another (1848) 2 Exch. 3; 154 E.R. 73l.

 (6) Ex parte Honey, re Jeffery (1871) 7 Ch.App. 178.

(7) Lindus v. Melrose (1858) 3 H. & N. 177; 157 E.R. 434.

(8) Chapman v. Smethurst [1909] 1 K.B. 73; [1909] 1 K.B. 927.

(9) Britannia Electric Lamp Works, Ltd. v. D. Mandler & Co. and D. Mandler [1939] 2 K.B. 129.

(10) A bba v. Koom Assan II and Others, Supreme Court of Gold Coast, February 5, 1935, unreported.

ACTION on a promissory note.

   Sekyi for the plaintiff.

  Benjamin, Jnr., for all the defendants save the second and eleventh.

ADUMUA-BOSSMAN J. The argument or contention of counsel for the defendants who raised the point of law was to the effect that, in so far as individual or several liability is concerned, the form or tenor of the note does not disclose any such individual or several liability; and that as far as joint liability is concerned, the note, on the face of it, sufficiently indicates that the signatories were the representatives of the Edina State and the note that of the said state. The argument or contention of counsel for the plaintiff, on the other hand, was to the effect that although the signatories to the promissory note describe themselves as

We, the undersigned Elders, Stoolholders, Supis and Coun­cillors of the Edina State"

it is nowhere expressly stated on the face of the note that they were acting for, or on account, or on behalf of, or as representing, the Edina State, in the making or signing of the note, and therefore the signatories are personally liable, jointly and severally. I will deal first with 'the contentions of counsel for the plaintiff, and will concede that a line of authorities lends some support to his contention. The first is Leadbitter v. Farrow (1), where Ellenborough C.J. said at p. 349:

" Is it nut an universal rule that a man who puts his name to a bill of exchange thereby makes himself personally liable, unless he states.upon the face of the bill that he subscribes it for another or by procuration of another, which are words of exclusion? Unless he says plainly: 'I am the mere scribe ' he becomes liable per­sonally .... Giving full effect to the circumstance that the plaintiff knew the defendant to be an agent, still the defendant is liable like any other drawer who puts his name to a bill without denoting that he does it in the character of procurator. The defendant has not so done, and therefore has made himself liable."

A second case is Rew and Another, Exors. of Newton v. Pettet and Others (2), according to the head notes of which:

" A Parish Vestry resolved to borrow money from one Henry Newton who advanced it and took promissory notes for the amount made by the defendants who were Churchwardens and Overseers and who added to their signatures the titles of their respective offices.

Held, inter alia, that the addition of their titles to their signatures did not destroy their individual liability. "

A third case is Dutton v. Marsh and Others (3), according to the headnotes of which:

" Four directors of a joint-stock company signed their names to a promissory note: 'We the Directors of the Isle of Man Slate Company Ltd. promise to pay to John Dutton, Esq., the sum of £1,600 with interest at 6 per cent. till paid, for value received.' Richard Marsh (Chairman), Joseph Higgins, Samuel Broadbent, Henry Johnson."

And at one corner of the note the company's seal was affixed with " Witnessed by L. L.

Cockburn C.J., at p. 364, said as follows:

" The effect of the authorities is clearly this, that when the  parties in making a promissory note or accepting a bill describe them­selves as directors, or by any similar form of description, but do not state on the face of the document that it is on account, or on behalf, of those whom they might otherwise be considered as representing-if they merely describe themselves as directors but do not state that they are acting on behalf of the company-they are liable individually. But, on the other hand, if they state that they are signing the note or the acceptance on account of, or on behalf of, some company or body of whom 'they are directors and the representatives, in that case, as the case of Lindus v. Melrose (7) fully establishes, they do not make themselves liable when they sign their names but are taken to have been acting for the company, as the statement on the face of the document represented. "

A fourth case is Elliott v. Bax-Ironside (4), according to the head­notes of which:

" A bill of exchange addressed to a limited liability company was accepted in the following form:

Accepted payable at the Westminster Bank Ltd., H. O. Bax­Ironside, Ronald A. Manson, Directors-Fashions Fair Exhibition Ltd.

And the bill was as requested by the drawer, indorsed by the same two directors as follows:

Fashions Fair Exhibition-H. O. Bax-Ironside & Ronald A. Manson, Directors.

Held, that even if the indorsement stood by itself, as the defen­dants' signatures did not in terms state that they were signing on behalf of the company, the addition to their names of the word  Directors' must be treated as a word of description only-and not as excluding their· liability. "

Scrutton L.J., after referring to the earlier cases, Leadbitter v. Farrow (1), Rew v. Pettet (2), Dutton v. Marsh (3), observed as follows:

. Those cases were before the Bills of Exchange Act, and when Parliament came to codify and to some extent alter the law in that Act, they did so in this way: Section 26, subsection (1), provided that: where a person signs a bill as drawer, indorser or acceptor, and adds words to his signature, indicating that he signs for or on behalf of a principal or in a representative character, he is not personally liable thereon; but the mere addition to his signature of words describing him as an agent or as filling a repre­sentative character, does not exempt him from personal liability. And by subsection (2): In determining whether a signature on a bill is that of the principal or that of the agent by whose hand it is written, the construction most favourable to the validity of the instrument shall be adopted."

Section 26 of the Bills of Exchange Act, 1882, referred to by Scrutton L.J. is identical in numbering and wording with section 26 of our Bills of Exchange Ordinance.

The plaintiff's case, however, fails to be altogether covered by these

uthorities, in two important aspects or respects. First, the joint promise contained or expressed in the note under consideration does not run side by side with any individual or several promise, to make a joint as well as individual or several liability coexist under the note as claimed or contended on behalf of the plaintiff. In this connection reference may be made to Healey v. Story and Another (5), the head­notes of which are as follows:

" The defendants, who were directors of a joint-stock newspaper company, gave the plaintiff a promissory note in part payment for the purchase of a newspaper which the company had agreed to purchase from him, the promissory note being in the following form: 'On demand, we jointly and severally promise to pay Mr. L. Healey or Order the sum of £250, value received for and on behalf of the Wesleyan Newspaper Association.       

Held, that the words' jointly and severally' were equivalent to 'jointly and personally' and the defendants were therefore personally liable to the plaintiff on the note.

Per Alderson B.: 'The plain grammatical meaning of the words is: We jointly promise and we severally promise; that is to say, we personally promise.'

That is clearly the true meaning of the words."

See also Ex parte Honey, re Jefjery (6), in which the form of the note was as follows:

" One day after demand in writing, we jointly and severally, or any two, three or more of us, promise to pay Mr. Joseph Hancock or order the sum of £12,150 with interest ... value received. "

The note in that case was signed by two members of a firm, by the firm, and by several other persons. The firm having become bankrupt, the holders of the note carried in proofs against the joint estate of the firm and against the separate estates of the two partners who signed the note. It was held that the holder was entitled to prove against and receive dividends from both the joint estate of the firm and the separate estate of the two partners who had signed the note. Thus, James L.J.:

" There was the ' joint contract' of the firm: the firm having joined in the security in the character of a firm in such a way that the members of the firm were not liable severally, but the firm was liable jointly. Then a certain member of the firm other than the original borrower, and certain strangers, have, in a distinct and several character as distinct individuals, put their names to a document by which each says, 'I bind myself severally to pay this sum' and the consequence is ' a distinct and several' contract binding upon him; and it is not the less a several contract, because it and other similar contracts are put together into one instrument bearing one stamp."

There being therefore no individual or several promise or under­taking appearing side by side with the joint promise contained and expressed in the note under consideration, in the light of the authorities just referred to, it follows that no individual or several liability is disclosed in the plaintiff's writ and amended statement of claim and, the point of law raised by the counsel for the defendants to that effect must be sustained.

Secondly, the signatories to the note in question here are not individual private names, as in Dutton v. i'r[arsh and Others (3), but traditional office or stool names or titles or styles, so that the indivi­duality or persona of each and every office-holder who signed is com­pletely out of the picture. By analogy, it is as though the name or style of a firm or limited liability company or corporation engraved on its rubber stamp or its seal is just impressed on the note, without any of the directors or officials signing his private name at all.

But this point, as being adverse to and against the claim and con­tention on behalf of the plaintiff, can be more appropriately elaborated upon a consideration of the contention on behalf of the defendants, to which I now come. The defendants' counsel's contention is that the form or tenor of the note under consideration here adequately and sufficiently indicates that the signatories are agents and representatives of the Edina State acting for that state and, that the promissory note therefore is the note of the state. There appear to be authorities which tend to support this contention also. The first is Lindus v. Melrose and Others (7), the head notes of which are as follows:

" The following promissory note was signed by three persons describing themselves as 'Directors' of a joint-stock company incorporated with limited liability, and was countersigned by one

E.   G., who described himself as Secretary to the Company.

, London-Dec. 31, 1856. Three months after date we jointly promise to pay or order six hundred pounds for value received in stock on account of the L. & B. Company Limited.'

Held, in the Exchequer Chamber (affirming the judgment of the Court of Exchequer), that the directors who signed it were not personally liable upon the note. "

Per Coleridge J.:

"The question for determination is, has the note made the defendants personally liable, or only bound the joint stock company of which they were directors .... Now strictly confining our­selves to what is apparent on the face of the note, or to be reason­ably collected from it, we think the inten'tion is clear. If the words for value received in stock' be read as in a parenthesis (which was suggested in the court below as the proper mode) there is then a promise to pay expressly on account of the company, and this promise is made by the directors, describing themselves as directors, and the promise is countersigned by E. G. describing himself as Secretary. Anyone reading such a note and bringing to it the ordinary knowledge which must be presupposed, would assuredly conclude that the note was made by the defendants as officers, and not as individuals-they promise on account of the company-they sign as directors-and their signature is counter­signed by the Secretary .. " Many cases were cited on the argument, but we do not think it necessary now to go ;through them, for each depends on its own circumstances, and this must be decided on the same principle. We are of opinion that the judgment must be affirmed."

The second is Chapm-an v. Smethurst (8), a decision of the Court of Appeal. The case had been tried before Channell J., in the King's Bench Division, reported at p. 73, and the form of the promissory note was as follows:

" Six months after demand I promise to pay Mrs. M. Chapman the sum of £300 for value received together with six per cent. interest per annum. J. H. Smethurst's Laundry & Dye Works Ltd. J. H. Smethurst, Managing Director."

Channell J., referring, inter alia, to the case of Lindus v. Melrose (7), proceeded as follows:

"In the latter case Coleridge J., delivering the judgment of the Exchequer Chamber, stated the rule to be ' that an agent putting his name to a mercantile instrument is liable as principal unless the instrument distinctly shows that he signs as agent.' That is no doubt the governing rule in all these cases. It is not sufficient for the party signing to describe himself as agent; he must show that he signs as agent and contracts as agent. But where he shows by the use of such words as ' for' or ' on behalf of ' that he contracts only as agen't, the use of the personal ' I ' will not impose upon him any personal liability .... In the present case I think that , it ' means Mr. Smethurst, the defendant. He has promised to pay and has not qualified that promise by saying that he promises , as managing director' or ' for' or ' on account of ' the company. The result is that I must hold him personally liable."

On appeal this decision was reversed by the Court of Appeal, Vaughan Williams L.J. saying as follows:

" I think that anyone reading this promissory note would at once say that it was one which made the company liable .... It is true that in the present case the promissory note does not profess to be signed' for' or ' on account of ' the company, but we have that which is equally strong to show that the company [is] intended to be bound by the note, that is, the stamped signature of the company at the foot with the defendant .... The decisions are bound to run rather near each other when the court has to determine whether the company or the directors who sign a promissory note are liable upon it; but when, as in the present case, the authority of the managing director to make this document on behalf of the company is once admitted, there is very little more required in order -to establish the company's liability."

And Kennedy L.J. said as follows:

" In the present case I endeavour to put myself in the position of a person who asks himself the question whether he has a good cause of action against the company .... If the company had power to issue promissory notes and Smethurst, the defendant, had authority from the company to sign promissory notes for them, what possible defence could the company have to an action? I agree that in this class of case it is not easy to form a confident opinion, but the document in question here does not, I think, present any great difficulty."

Ohapman v. Smethurst (8) was followed by Branson J. in Brit,annia Electric Lamp W{)rks Ltd. v. D. Mandler & 00. and D. Mandler (9), in which the plaintiffs, claiming to be holders for value, sued upon a document in the following form:

" , £100 September 23, 1937-Two months after date pay to our order the sum of £100 for value received.'

No signature of drawer or maker appeared, but across the document was written: 'Accepted and payable at 2 Manville Road, Balham.' Then followed a rubber stamp D. M. & Co. Ltd., a gap filled with the signature of the managing director, and the words ' Managing Director.'

Held, inter alia, (1) That the signature on the face of the document was the company's-not D. Mandler the Managing Director. "

Branson J. said as follows:

" 'fhe first point that is urged upon me is that I should read the document as constituting a promissory note, and that I should neglect the rubber stamp altogether and read it as a promissory note made by D. Mandler. I am quite unable to do that. I think the case of Ohapman v. Smethurst is ample authority for the proposition that in reading documents of this sort one must not shut one's eyes to the way in which the signature is put upon them. In fact I think it would be doing the utmost violence to the writing upon this instrument ,to construe it as anything but the signature of the company."

What, therefore, is there about the promissory note, the subject­matter of this action, appearing on the face of it, which excludes not only any individual personal liability, as I have already held, but joint personal liability also, yet which, on the contrary, indicates sufficiently clearly that the note is that of some principal for whom the signatories jointly were acting. The feature, in my opinion, is the fact that in the body of the note the promise to pay is made by " the Elders, Stool­holders, Supis and Councillors of the Edina State," and that the signatures are not of the personal names of the signatories, but official or traditional titles andlor styles, i.e., Divisional Chief Kojo Nguan­doh II, Stoolholder George Asmah, Supi R. Vandyck, and Councillor Kwamina Adziaye, and all the others. Separate liability of each office­holder as such is clearly excluded, not only by the clear terms of the note" We ... do faithfully promise to pay," but by the fact of which the court must take judicial notice that each office-holder's authority to bind his particular office or stool or the group within the larger Edina community or state which he represents, can be legally and effectively exercised only with the concurrence of the elders of that particular stool or office, and that without such concurrence the office­holder's contract as such is of no effect. Indeed, the interpretation of the note in this case would seem to be necessarily governed by the terms of section 26 (2) of the Ordinance, whereby in determining whether a signature on a bill is that of principal or agent" a construc­tion most favourable to the validity of the instrument" must be adopted. This must be that the note executed by all the traditional office-holders of the Edina State, who together constitute the body or group authorised by native law and custom to represent, contract for, and bind the state, is the promissory note of the state, and not of the signatories individually or jointly.

There is a great dearth of local authority, because in almost all the cases the note clearly enough expresses or states that the signatories are contracting and signing for and on behalf of such and such a stool or state. But it may not be out of place to refer to Abba v. Koom Assan II and Others (10). Deane C.J. said as follows:

" In this case the plaintiff sued two defendants on a promissory note. One of them, the first defendant, signed the note as Omanhene, and the second as Tufuhene, of Essikuma. Before the case could be heard the plaintiff applied that one Nyanie should be joined as defendant on the ground that the first defendant had been destooled and Nyanie was now Regent in his stead, and that was done. Later the present appellant was substituted in place of Nyanie as the present Omanhene. By this application for substitu­tion, plaintiff in effect was setting up that the promissory note was signed by the first defendant in a representative capacity as Omanhene, and the case right through proceeded on that under­standing, although there is no allegation to that effect in the writ, as required by the Rules .... I have amended the writ so as to bring it into conformity with what all the parties conceived ~o be the case between them .... It is the chief point in this case to say whether, when the first and second defendants signed the promissory note, they had authority to represent the stool."

Applying that test, I have no hesitation in holding that the whole body of the" Elders, Stoolholders, Supis and Councillors of the Edina State" is the proper body having power by native law and custom to represent, contract for, and bind the State of Edina, and that the promissory note in this case, being one duly made by such lawfully authorised agents and representatives according to native law and custom, binds the state and must be deemed the promissory note of the state.

In the result I am obliged, having already held earlier in this ruling that no several liability is disclosed in the writ or amended statement of claim, to hold further that no joint personal liability also is disclosed, and that the point of law raised by counsel for the defendants must be sustained. I am, however, prepared, should counsel for plaintiff so desire, to grant an amendment of the writ and statement of claim by the addition of the words" as Representatives of and for and on behalf of the Edina State" after the names of the defendants, to make the writ and statement of claim accord with the true intent and effect of the promissory note, and thereafter to deal with the claim on the basis of that amendment.

Order for amendment of statement of claim.

S. G. D.
