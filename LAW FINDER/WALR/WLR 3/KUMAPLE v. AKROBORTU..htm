                                                    January 9, 1958.

                                       KUMAPLE v. AKROBORTU.

                               High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                         Ollennu J

                                                 

.

Native Courts-Jurisdiction-Power of Native Court to make order not sought by the parties-Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Procedure Regulations, 1945, reg. 40.

Evidence-Burden of proof-Trespass to land-Defendant establishing prima facie rights to the land-Burden of proof on plaintiff.

The plaintiff claimed damages for trespass to land before a Native Court. He relied upon a grant to him by the T. family. The defences were that the defendant's family were not trespassers because they had an interest in the land and that the grant to the plaintiff by the T. family was void. The defendant relied upon a judgment of the Native Court in a previous action between the T. family and the defendant's family. In this action the '1'. family sought a declaration of their title to an area of land which included the land now in dispute. In their judgment the Native Court held the defendant's family to be a female branch of the T. family and entitled to enjoy the family property jointly with the T. family (there appeared to be some ambiguity in the judgment in that in parts the expression "the T. family" was used as meaning the male branch of the family while in other parts it was meant to cover both branches, i.e., the male branch and the defendant's female branch jointly). The judgment further declared that the land was to be shared between the two branches in the proportion of two to one. The defendant claimed that this judgment declared the land concerned to be jointly owned hy his family and the T. family (male branch); that the purported grant to the plaintiff was by the male branch alone; and, that because the defendant's branch were not joined in the grant, this was void and without effect. The plaintiff claimed that the previous judgment of the Native Court gave the defendant's family only an equitable interest in the land and, that as the plaintiff was a bona fide purchaser for value without notice, he took free from the defendant family's equitable interest.

Held, (i) although the previous judgment of the Native Court con­tained an order that was not sought by the parties (i.e., as to the sharing of the land) this was nevertheless an order that the Native Court was competent to make, by reason of the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Procedure Regulations, 1945, reg. 40, which gives a Native Court power to make an order which it considers necessary for doing justice, regardless as to whether such order has been asked for or not.

(ii) The true construction of the Native Court's judgment was that the land concerned belonged jointly to both branches of the T. family and that therefore, until such time as the land was partitioned between the branches, any alienation of the land must be made by the two branches acting together.

(iii) The defendant having established the interest of his family in the land in dispute, the plaintiff could succeed only if he established that there had been partition between the two branches and that the land granted to him came within that part of the whole area demarcated to the male branch, his grantors. This he had failed to do.

APPEAL from a decision of the Anlo Native Appeal Court on July 2, 1957, reversing a decision of the Keta Native Court" B " on March 26, 1957, in favour of the plaintiff in an action for trespass to land.

Apaloo for the plaintiff. Alwfo-Addo for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. By his writ issued in the Native Court" B " of Keta, the plaintiff claimed damages for trespass to a piece of land sold to him by the head and principal members of the Titili family. The trial Native Court entered judgment for the plaintiff on his claim. That judgment was, however, set aside on appeal by the Anlo Native Appeal Court. It is from that judgment of the Anlo Native Appeal Court that the plaintiff has now appealed to this court. Mr. Apaloo, learned counsel for the plaintiff, properly indicated that he was not relying upon the judgment of the Native Court" B " of Reta. But he sub­mitted that, upon a proper direction in law, the judgment should, on the evidence on record, have been entered in favour of the plaintiff.

I shall not waste time by discussing the judgment of the Native Court "B." It will be enough only to observe that the court took upon themselves to discuss at length points of law with which they were not competent to deal, with the natural result that they got so muddled up that they strayed far away from the issues they were called upon to determine. The sooner Native Courts and their registrars realise that the law they are empowered to administer is native cus­tomary law, and such other law or ordinances as laid down in the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 98, s. 15, the better it will be for themselves and the litigants who go before their courts.

The main issue in the case is whether the plaintiff acquired a good title to the land in dispute by purchase from the Titili family. The Native Appeal Court held that the sale was not binding upon the defendant by reason of the fact that a judgment of the Native Court " B " of the Anlo State, given in a suit between the plaintiff's vendors and the defendant, held that the plaintiff's said vendors, the Titili family, and the defendant's family were to share a large area of land, of which the land in dispute is a portion, in the proportion of two to one, and therefore that the sale made by the Titili family alone was contrary to native custom.

Mr. Apaloo submitted that the Native Appeal Court misdirected themselves on the effect of this judgment. He submitted that the last paragraph of that judgment, directing the partitioning of the land, is an order, and does not form part of the operative section of the judgment, and should not therefore be held to have given the defen­dant any estate in the land. Counsel argued further that even if the order is part of the judgment the said order is, at the most, a judgment in per8onam, not a judgment in rem, and gave the defendant a mere equitable interest in the land, declaring the title to it to be vested in the Titili family. Consequently, he said, a bona fide purchaser for value from the Titili family without notice of the defendant's equitable interest took free from the said equitable interest.

Mr. Akufo-Addo for the defendant referred the court to Regulations 23, 40 and 41 of the Native Courts Procedure Regulations made under the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance. Regulation 23 is as follows:

" When the case on both sides is closed, the Native Court shall consider the whole matter and give its decision which shall be and Regulation 40 gives the Native Court discretion, within its power and jurisdiction, and within certain limits, to make an order which it considers necessary for doing justice, whether such order has been asked for by the party entitled to it or not. Mr. Akufo-Addo sub­mitted therefore that the last paragraph is the judgment or order of the Anlo Native Court" B " upon the claim made by the Titili family as set out in their writ of summons. He submitted further that the order in the said judgment that the Titili family and the Akrobortu family should divide the land between them was within the com­petency of the Native Court, it being an order made in the Native Court's discretion under Regulation 40.

I agree with those submissions of Mr. Akufo-Addo. In my opinion the findings of fact which a Native Court makes and the statement of the native law and custom in the body of the judgment constitute reasons for the judgment which is set out in the order. In the reasons for the judgment, the Native Court held the Akrobortu family-the defendant's family-to be a female branch of the Titili family, entitled to enjoy the property with the male branch. Therefore, upon proper construction of the judgment as a whole, that is, the judgment or order and the reasons for it, the only meaning which can be given to the finding •. that the land then in dispute is Titili family land" is: that the land belongs to the Titili family in its entirety, that is, to all its component parts, male and female. That order was within the competency of the Native Court on the claim for a declaration of title made in the Titili family's writ of summons.

In my opinion, therefore, the judgment or order declared the status of the land then in dispute to be property owned by the male and female branches of the Titili family jointly and gave to the defendant's family a legal estate, not a mere equitable interest, in the said land. That being the case, no valid disposition of the land or any portion of it can be made by the male section of the TitiIi family-properly called the Titili family when contrasted with the female section, the Akrobortu-without the consent and concurrence of the female section, the Akrobortu family.

The defences put up to the claim for trespass are (1) title in the defendant, and (2) invalidity of the alienation relied upon by the plaintiff. That plea put the title of the plaintiff in issue. The plaintiff therefore cannot succeed unless he is able to establish that he obtained a good title under the sale, either originally, or subsequently by opera­tion of the partitioning of the whole family land; that is to say, that the portion sold to him fell wholly within the two-thirds portion allotted to the Titili (male section) of the family upon the demarca­tion; or that a particular portion of it fell within it. That the sale to the plaintiff was made without the knowledge and consent of the defendant is beyond dispute. Consequently, the sale is invalid except as regards any portion which, upon the evidence, is shown to have fallen within the Titili portion in the partitioning. The onus was therefore upon the plaintiff to establish the situation of the line of demarcation or to satisfy the court that the land which he claims or any defined portion of it lies wholly on the Titili side of the boundary. He led no evidence whatsoever to establish that fact, I cannot accept Mr. Apaloo's submission that the onus was upon the defendant to establish where the line of demarcation lay. The person who would fail if the demarcation is not proved is the person upon whom the onus lies to establish the partition.

In this case it is the plaintiff who asserts the validity of the sale to him, and so he is the person who must fail if it is not established that his vendors had power to alienate that portion to him, in other words that the land sold to him fell within the boundaries of the land demarcated to his vendors. He failed to discharge that onus. His claim cannot therefore succeed.

Appeal dismissed.

S. G. D.
