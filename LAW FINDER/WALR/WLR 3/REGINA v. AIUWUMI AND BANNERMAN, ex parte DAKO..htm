                                             May 24, 1957

                                          REGINA v. AIUWUMI AND BANNERMAN, ex parte DAKO.

                                                High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                              Windsor-Aubrey J.                                   

 

practice-Mandamus-Whether time during which applications can be made is limited--Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 59, r. 3.

Practice-Time-Circumstances in which general power to enlarge time is limited or excluded-Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 64, r. 6.

Practice-Certiorari-Time within which application must be made-Whether court has power to enlarge time-Supreme 0011rt (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 59, r. 3 and Ord. 64, r. 6.

The Accra Municipal Council, in exercise of powers conferred upon them by the Municipal Councils Ordinance, c. 9 of 1953, sought, by way of ex parte motion in the court of the District Magistrate, an order for the sale of a house, the property of the applicant. The council's applica­tion was heard by the magistrate in November 1955, and the order sought was granted. In May 1956, the property was sold, under the order, by public auction. The purchasers, who were the opposers in the present matter, applied for a certificate of purchase in respect of the property, and this was granted by the Senior Magistrate in June 1956. In January 1957, the applicant, who was by then out of time, applied under the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 64, r. 6, for an order enlarging time and, under Order 59, r. 3, for leave to apply for an order of certiorari to quash the proceedings in the District Magistrate's Court and to set aside the subsequent certificate of purchase, or in the alter­native for leave to apply for an order of mandamus directed against the District Magistrate. At the hearing of thp, application the point was taken by the opposers that Order 64, r. 6, differed in substance from the corresponding rule of the English Rules of the Supreme Court, and that when read in conjunction with Order 59, r. 3, it gave no power to the court to enlarge time in an application for an order of certiorari or mandamus.

Held, (i) notwithstanding the marginal note to Order 59, r. 3, the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, impose no time limit within which an application for an order of mandamus must be made to the court.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. There is similarly no time limit for mandamus in England. Order 59, r. 3, is in identical terms with the English R.S.C., Ord. 59, r. 4 (2).]

(ii) The general power to enlarge time conferred upon the court by Order 64, r. 6, is not limited or excluded uIL1ess it appears expressly or by necessary implication in any particular rule applying a time limit, that the general power to enlarge shall by limited or excluded in the case of that rule. Order 59, r. 3, which imposes a time limit for applica­tions for orders of certiorari, makes no such limitation upon the effect of Order 64, r. 3, and therefore there is no restriction upon the power of the court to enlarge the time in which applications for certiorari may be made.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. It may be noted that Order 39, r. 8 (3) (which deals with limitation of time for review) contains such a provision limiting the power to enlarge conferred by Order 64, r. 6. Unlike most of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, which are based on the English rules, this former rule appears to have been inspired by the Civil Procedure Rules of Uganda, and this may afford the explanation for the words" Subject to any provisions to the contrary in these Rules ... " appearing in Order 64, r. 6, when they do not appear in the equivalent English rule.

The Land Court case referred to by the learned judge was Atsiavishie and Others v. Tsili and Others, Land Court, Accra, June 7, 1955, unreported. This decision turned upon Order 39, r. 8 (3) and is therefore completely in accord with the decision in the present case.]

ApPLICATIONS under 'the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, Ord. 64, r. 6, for enlargement of time in which to apply for an order of certiorari and, under Order 59, r. 3, for leave to apply for an order of certiorari or in the alternative for an order of mandamus.

Davies for the applicant.

E. O. Asafu-Adjaye and Akufo-Addo for Danawi and Sons, the opposers.

Bentsi Enehill for the Accra Municipal Council.

WINDSOR-AUBREY J. In this case the applicant has applied to the court for an order of enlargement of time for applying for leave for an order of certiorari or mandamus, concerning an order of the District Magistrate made on November 9, 1955, for the sale of a property known as House No. 727/4 Accra.

At this stage I am only concerned with the application for an enlargement of time so it would be out of place to consider the merits of the applications, except in so far as that is necessary for me to determine whether I should exercise any discretion I may have under Order 64, r. 6. It will suffice to say that both the Senior Magistrate,

Accra, and the Divisional Court, Eastern Division, have already come to the conclusion that the applicant has suffered great misfortune and that a serious miscarriage of justice has occurred. On the information at present before me I see no reason to differ from that view.

As far as 'the respondents, Messrs. Danawi and Sons, are concerned I can see no injustice to them in extending time for the application if I am so empowered. The land cannot disappear and already there is an injunction over it. They have paid a negligible sum for valuable property which is not a wasting asset. If the applica'tion ultimately proves successful they would probably be able to recover their money by civil proceedings, without the necessity of having to prove fraud.

As to the alleged delay on the part of the applicants, I consider there has been none. The legal position is exceedingly complex and although the Divisional Court has indicated the possibilities of a civil action for fraud against the Council I respectfully agree with the applicant's counsel that, it may well turn out impossible to prove deliberate fraud, although it appears that negligence on the part of the Municipal Council should be easy to establish.

It is now necessary to consider whether the court has power to enlarge time. Order 59, r. 3, provides that leave to apply for an order of certiorari shall not be granted unless the application is made not later than six months after the date of the proceeding. There is no similar limitation in applying for mandamus except the marginal note to that rule which refers to both certiorari and mandamus. The limited effect of marginal notes which are not strictly part of a statute is well known, and this inaccurate note cannot override or affect the clear provisions of the rule. Consequently I hold that there is no restriction imposing a time limit for an application for mandamus.

It is not, however, disputed that the application for certiorari is out of time, but the applicant's counsel asserts that time can be extended under Order 64, 1'. 6, of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954. Order 64, r. 6, is in the same terms as Order 64, r. 7, of the Rules of the Supreme Court in England except for the opening words of our rule, which are absent from the English rule. These words are as follows: "Subject to any provisions to the contrary in these Rules

This phraseology is commonly used in legal drafting both in statutes and rules. Counsel for the applicant argues that if the rule is con­strued to prohibit a time limit from being extended, the rule is mere surplusage and is redundant. There is much cogency in that argument. He points out that where no time limit is imposed a court can clearly fix any limit and, that if a time limit prohibits an extension of time the rule can never operate. That seems to be a correct statement.

Counsel for the opposer, on the other hand, asserts that where a time limit can be extended the particular rule so provides and he refers to Order 20, r. 1, and to Order 21, r. 6, as cases in point. He has also referred me to a ruling in· the Land Court by the Chief Justice that the time for review fixed by Order 39, r. 3, cannot be extended. I have not, however, had the advantage of perusing the actual judgment.

In any event while naturally paying the greatest respect to a judg­ment of the Chief Justice in the Land Court it is a court of concurrent jurisdiction to this court and I am not bound by such a decision. With great respect to counsel for the opposer, if his argument is correct it does not overcome the difficulty that it renders Order 64, r. 6, completely nugatory.

Order 64, r. 6, must have some purpose and meaning even if of a limited character. I have come to the conclusion that while Order 64, r. 6, is not so wide as the equivalent English rule which gives the court unfettered power to extend time, the Ghana rule gives this court power to extend a fixed period unless the rule fixing that time limit expressly excludes Order 64, r. 6, by some such words as: "Notwithstanding Order 64, r. 6, the time limit shall not be extended."

Accordingly I hold that I have power to enlarge the time for applying for an order of certiorari and I exercise that power and extend the time to cover this application. I have already held that no extension of time is necessary for the mandamus application.

Application for enlargement of time granted.

S. G. D.
