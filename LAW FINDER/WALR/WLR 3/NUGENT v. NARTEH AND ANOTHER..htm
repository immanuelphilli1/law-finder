                                                    December 11, 1958

                                       NUGENT v. NARTEH AND ANOTHER.  

                               High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                       Acolatse J.

 

 

Succession--Family law-Intestate not survived by issue or by issue of her mother-Family to whom self-acquired property of intestate passes.

Succession--Family law-Property purchased with family money-Effect on nature of property thus purchased.

The plaintiff's case was that Sarah Idun, whom he alleged to be a member of the Bernasko family, owned a house (her self-acquired property) in Cape Coast. She died intestate on January 27, 1953, and the house became family property. The family appointed James Alexander Williams (sole surviving maternal uncle) as sucr;essor to her estate, real and personal, according to the customary law of the Fantis. Williams took out letters of administration of the personal property on May 20, 1954.

A few months after tne death of Sarah Idun, Williams approached the family and told them that he had land in Accra. He said that he wished to sell the family house in question at Cape Coast, and to use the money to put up a building on his Accra land for the use of the family of Sarah Idun. The family agreed to this, and the house was sold under a deed of conveyance dated May 24, 1954. Williams began operations on the Accra land through his contractors, who built a house there for the sum of £750. Williams died in 1955, and by his will (of which he appointed Alice Narteh and Jane Acquaye executrices) he purported to dispose of the now nearly completed house in Accra to them.

On June 21, 1956, the following document was executed by Henry Will Mensah, head of the Bernasko family:

"I, Henry Will Mensah, alias Kwesi Mensah, of Sekondi and Cape Coast, present head of the Bernasko family, do hereby authorise Mr. Robert William Nugent, alias Kobina Nugent, my nephew, to act as the head of the family on my behalf in all matters affecting the interest of the said Bernasko family, and to do an things that in his opinion will be beneficial to the said family."

Nugent (who claimed to be himself a member of the Bernasko family) issued a writ, directed to the two executrices, on August 20, 1956. The title of the writ recited that Nugent sued as representing the family of Sarah Idun and James A. Williams, both deceased, but the only autho­risation to sue which was produced at the subsequent trial was the document from the head of the Bernasko family. The writ claimed declarations:

(1) that the will of the late Williams was invalid, on the ground of improper execution; and,

(2) that the house at Farrar Avenue, referred to in the said will, was not the self-acquired property of Williams, but family property, it having been built with funds obtained from the estate of Sarah Idun.

The claim for a declaration that the will was invalid on the ground of improper execution was witndrawn by counsel at the early stages of the case, a separate action being brought on that issue in the Divisional Court, Accra.

Held, (i) the house in Accra, having been built with family money, became itself impressed with the nature of family property and was thus not property of which the late Williams was competent to dispose by will.

(ii) The late Sarah Idun had died without issue and was not survived by her mother or issue of her mother. For the purposes of succession to her self-acquired property therefore, her family comprised the issue and remoter descendants of her maternal grandmother. There was no evidence to show that this family was the Bernasko family, represented by the plaintiff, and therefore the action must fail.

ACTION for a declaration that a certain house was family property and had therefore been ineffectually disposed of by will.

Sowah for the defendants.

ACOLATSE J. referred to the history of the matter and con­tinued: The plaintiff claimed the house as the family property of the Bernasko family because Mr. Williams had used family money to build the house and had never accounted (as successor and administrator) to the Bernasko family. It was not disputed that the house in Cape Coast was the self acquired property of Sarah Idun, and that upon her death it became the family property of her immediate family. But the family must be traced from her grandmother, for it was admitted that Sarah Idun had died leaving no issue and no sisters, and that her mother and her mother's brothers predeceased her, leaving J. A. Williams (now himself dead) surviving as her sole maternal uncle. Upon the face of the evidence Sarah Idun was not a member of the Bernasko family on her mother's side, nOr was her grandmother a

                                                                                Bernasko on the maternal side.          '

The plaintiff claims locus standi in this action upon a document of authorisation which deals exclusively with the interests of the Bernasko family. It cannot therefore be implied, directly or indirectly, that it referred to the interests of Sarah Idun's family, of which the surviving uncle (Mr. Williams) was the head and, as such, the due successor to her estate for and on behalf of her family. Mr. Williams was not on the maternal side a member of the Bernasko family.

The plaintiff did not give any evidence whereby the head of the Bernasko family could, under customary law, succeed to the family property of Sarah Idun. Nor is there evidence before me that the plain­tiff was appointed by Sarah Idun's family to represent the said family in this action, and there is no evidence by any immediate member of Sarah ldun's family that the plaintiff had been appointed head of that family as successor to J. A. Williams. No suggestion of such authorisa­tion or appointment is to be found among the signatures to the document on which the plaintiff relies, for he says that all of them were those of members of the Bernasko family.

J. A. Williams in his will made bequests to "my sister Doris, otherwise Mrs. Orlando Martins ... in the United Kingdom ... if alive." There is no evidence before me whether or not Mrs. Orlando Martins is alive, but it would appear that, if she is alive, as his uterine sister she would be a direct and immediate member of Sarah Idun's family, as Sarah Idun's aunt.

I am satisfied that under native law and custom Sarah ldun's self-acquired house became family property upon her death. It was inherited by her only surviving maternal uncle, who had the sole supervision and management of the house without accounting to anyone, and who could dispose of it in his lifetime. He had a life interest only in the family property, and upon his death the property, if it had not been disposed of, would necessarily have gone to the immediate relation of Sarah Idun. I am also satisfied that Williams sold the house, and used the proceeds (or some part of them) in putting

up the house in Accra upon his own land. The latter house, although built on his land, became family property by reason of the contribution from the assets of family property. I am unable, nevertheless, to give judgment for plaintiff, because he has not shown that he is the head of Sarah ldun's family and successor to the late 'Williams, nor has he shown authority to sue on behalf of that family. The action is misconceived. I accordingly dismiss the plaintiff's claim, and give judgment for the defendants, with costs against the plaintiff herein in his personal capacity.

Judgment for defendants.
