                                                                               July 15,1957

                                                      KARIOLA v. MAHAMA.

                                          High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                                  Adumua-Bossman J

                                    

 

 

Practice-Evidence-Admissibility-Admissibility of previous conviction or acquittal in subsequent civil proceedings-Criminal Procedure Code, c. 10 of 1951, s. 147.

This was an action for conversion in which the plaintiff sought to recover the value of a quantity of kola nuts from the defendant. It was the case of the plaintiff, who was a dealer in kola nuts, that they had been stolen from him by the defendant. The civil proceedings had been preceded by a criminal prosecution in which the defendant had been charged and acquitted of the larceny of the nuts and an order had been made that they, at that time in the possession of the police, be restored to him. The question was raised as to jurisdiction of the court to enter­tain the civil claim and, in answer, reference was made to the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 147 (2), which provides that where, under the section, a magistrate has made an order for delivery of property which has come into the possession of the police in connection with a criminal charge, such an order shall not affect the right of any person to take proceedings for the recovery of the property but that this right is to cease on the expiration of six months from the date of the order.

Held, a previous conviction or judgment is not even admissible, let alone conclusive, evidence of the facts upon which it was founded when those facts are in issue in subsequent civil proceedings. Therefore, quite apart from the express provision of the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 147 (2), the previous acquittal on the charge of larceny offered no impediment to the prosecution of the civil action for conversion.

Hollington v. Hewthorn & 00., Ltd. [1943] K.B. 587; [1943] 2 All E.R. 35, applied.

Cases referred to :

(1) Winter v. Bancks and Lings (1901) 84 L. T. 504 j 17 T.L.R. 446 j 19 Cox 687.

(2) Buller & 00., Ltd. v. Brooks (1930) 46 T.L.R. 233.

(3) Hollington v. Hewthorn &; 00., Ltd. [1943] K.B. 587 j [1943] 2 All E.R. 35.

(4) In the Estate of Orippen [1911] P. 108.

(5) Partington v. Partington and Another [1925] P. 34. (6) O'Toole v. O'Toole (1926) 134 L.T. 542.

ACTION for damages for the conversion of goods.

ADUMUA-BossMAN J. dealt with the evidence and continued:

At the commencement of the trial I expressed doubt to .the plaintiff's counsel as to whether, having regard to the learned District Magistrate's order, ordering the seven bags of kola nuts to be delivered to the defen­dant, this action against him for conversion could be maintained. Counsel thereupon referred me to the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 147, which is in the following terms:

" (1) Where property has come into the possession of the police in connection with any criminal charge, a Magistrate may, on application either by or on behalf of the Commissioner of Police or by a claimant of the property, make an order for delivery of the property to the person appearing to the Magistrate to be the owner thereof.

(2) An order under this section shall not affect the right of any person to take within six months from the date of the order, legal proceedings against any person in possession of pro­perty delivered by virtue of the order for the recovery of the property; but on the expiration of those six months the right shall cease."

Counsel furthermore pointed out that, following the District Magis­trate's order dated November 13, 1956, the writ was issued on February 18, 1957, within the prescribed period, so that it is quite clear that the issue as to the ownership of the seven bags of kola nuts which were the subject of the unsuccessful criminal prosecution of the defendant, is, by express local legislation, properly open for determina­tion by this court.

Since reserving judgment in this case, however, I have satisfied myself that on general principles also the action is maintainable; and that it is well established and settled that criminal proceedings and subsequent acquittal or conviction are not admissible in any ensuing civil proceedings, and must be completely disregarded.

In the case of Winter v. Bancks and Lings (1) a county court judge so disregarded criminal proceedings that had ended in the acquittal of the person charged with theft of a chattel, and decided as to the ownership of the chattel and found a police officer who, following the acquittal, had returned the chattel to the person from whom it was taken for purposes of the criminal prosecution, liable in trover, and a Divisional Court of the King's Bench, hearing an appeal against the county court's decision, confirmed it. The headnotes of that case are as follows:

" A bought a gig. It was stolen from him, and afterwards found by the police in the possession of B. B was indicted for larceny of the gig and acquitted. B on his acquittal wrote to the police officer in possession of the gig demanding delivery of it to him, and A afterwards by letter and personally also applied for delivery over to him. The police officer, acting on the instruc­tions of a superior officer, after giving notice to A of his intention to do so, delivered 'the gig to B as the person from whom it had been taken by the police. Held, that the police officer so delivering it was liable in trover to ' A ' who was found to be the true owner of the gig."

Then in 1930 followed the case of Buller &; Co., Ltd. v. Brooks (2).

In that case, a civil action for conversion of some jewellery, Wright J., constituting the King's Bench Division Court, completely ignored the criminal conviction for larceny of the jewellery of the person from whom the defendants received it in pledge, and proceeded to find that there had been no larceny as found by the criminal court but rather an obtaining by false pretences, and further that the plaintiffs were not the owners of the jewellery to be able to maintain the action for con­version. In the course of his judgment, at p. 235, he said as follows:

"The criminal court which, under the Larceny Act, 1916, s. 45 (1), had a discretionary power to order restitution, having refused to exercise that discretion, the matter must be considered in a civil action between the plaintiffs and the defendants on the evidence before this court, which is not bound by the finding of the jury in the criminal case."

Then followed Hollington v. Hewthorn &; Co. Ltd. (3), in which Goddard L.J., as he then was, reading the judgment of the Court of Appeal, said as follows:

" The first observation we would make on this part of the case is that it has been the invariable practice of the judges for many years, certainly for so long as any member of the court has been in the profession, to reject this class of evidence (criminal convic­tion of the defendant). So that nowadays counsel have ceased to tender it in accident cases."

The learned Lord Justice then wont on to review all the authorities concerning the admissibility or inadmissibility of criminal proceedings and judgments or convictions in civil proceedings, from the earliest to modern days, and after expressing disapproval of three cases purporting to support the contention that a criminal conviction may be admitted in evidence in civil proceedings, to wit, In the Estate of Crippen (4), Partingt,on v. Partington and Another (5), and O'Toole v. O'Toole (6) in the following words:

" In our opinion, these three cases go beyond and are contrary to the authorities and ought not to be followed in future."

He concluded as follows:

    " Without dealing with every case and textbook that were cited in argument, we are of opinion that both on principle and authority the conviction was rightly rejected."

          The important legal point, clearly raised for us here in this country, IS how far the concluding words of the Criminal Procedure Code, s. 147 (2), "but on the expiration of those six months the right shall cease," can legally operate to bar or preclude an owner of a chattel, in respect of which a restitution order has been made under section 147, from maintaining a civil action to establish his ownership after the expiration of the period of six months. Fortunately for me I am not called upon to decide this point of law in these proceedings.

         It is now necessary to determine the issue of fact as to the owner­ship of the seven bags of kola nuts, in respect of which the defendant was prosecuted for stealing but acquitted, and which were subsequently restored to him following the District Magistrate's order for restitu­tion. On that issue I am satisfied and have no hesitation in saying that plaintiff has proved his ownership or title to the bags of nuts. The plaintiff himself gave the value to the police as £84 and he is entitled to that amount of £84, being the market value of the converted merchandise, as damages for the conversion.

 

 

Judgment for the plaintiff

 S. G. D.
