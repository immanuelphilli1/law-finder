                                               September 26, 1958

                                                       ARHIN v. POBEE

                             High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                     Adumua-Bossman J

 

.

Practice-Jurisdiction Appeals from Native Courts-Test for determining whether or not an action is a succession suit-Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 98, 2nd Sched.

Chief Kweku Arhin III (Opanyin Kweku Enyima) claimed from James Kobina Pobee recovery of possession of certain stool properties "unlawfully retained by the defendant in spite of several demands." The plaintiff's case was that the properties in question had belonged to one of his predecessors, Chief Arhin I; had devolved upon the latter's successor Chief Arhin II; and then devolved upon him (the plaintiff) as successor after the demise of Arhin II.

The suit was tried in the Municipal Court, Cape Coast. If the suit related to succession, that court had unlimited jurisdiction in respect of value; if the suit was a personal suit, its jurisdiction was limited to £50, and the value of the properties claimed exceeded that sum. The defendant did not admit that the plaintiff was successor to the deceased Arhins, and the plaintiff had to adduce evidence on the point, including his letters of administration. The trial court treated the suit as one relating to succession, and gave judgment for the plaintiff. On appeal it was argued that, at the trial, the defendant had admitted the succession of the plaintiff, with the effect that, as succession was no longer in issue, the suit could not be said to be a succession suit.

Held, (i) the test for determining whether or not a suit or matter relates to succession to property is, "has a claimant to establish his succession to a deceased native before he can receive the relief he claims 1 "

(ii) On the facts, there had been no admission in the trial court as to succession by the plaintiff but, even if there had been such an admission, this could not affect the nature of the plaintiff's claim, which was based on succession.

Cases referred to :

(1) Vanderpuye and Others v. Botchway (1956) 2 W.A.L.R. 16. (2) Hagan and Others v. Adum and Others (1939) W.A.C.A. 35. (3) Kwow v. Eku II (1934) 2 W.A.C.A. 180.

ApPEAL from a decision of the Magistrate's Court constituted by the Government Agent, Cape Coast, on February 7, 1958, upholding a decision of the Municipal Court, Cape Coast, on May 9, 1957. The latter court had given judgment for the plaintiff on a claim for the recovery of certain stool properties.

Sekyi for the appellant.

de Graft Johnson for the respondent.

ADUMUA-BosSMAN J. referred to the pleadings and continued: The short point, canvassed in the two lower courts, and raised for deter­mination in this appeal by learned counsel for the defendant, was whether the suit does not fall within the category of jurisdiction desig­nated " personal suits" in the Second Schedule to the Native Courts Ordinance, in which Schedule the various civil jurisdictions of those courts are set out. If it does, then inasmuch as by the terms of the Schedule the quantum of claim is limited to £50 so far as the trial court in this case is concerned, the suit was outside its jurisdiction, the value of the properties claimed herein being admittedly over £50. The argument was that the plaintiff's claim to be successor was con­ceded, and that what really remained outstanding for determination before the trial court were two questions of fact: (a) whether the articles enumerated and claimed were ever left by Arhin I at all as his properties on his demise, and (b) if so, whether they ever got into the possession of the defendant.

Learned counsel for the plaintiff has pointed out that the premises on which the argument for the defendant was based were factually incorrect. He has pointed out passages in the defendant's evidence in which the latter appeared not to admit that the plaintiff was suc­cessor of Arhin I as claimed. He has also pointed out that the plaintiff had to adduce evidence, including the production of letters of administration granted to him after a contest with the defendant, to establish the succession which he claimed. Counsel's contention appears to be well-founded, and would seem to dispose of the argu­ment on behalf of the defendant.

I would, however, deal with the defendant's argument on its merits as put forward, i.e., on the hypothesis that the defendant did admit plaintiff's claim to succession, so that " it did not become an issue," as counsel put it. Did the mere fact, then, that the plaintiff's claim to be successor was admitted, operate to make the suit cease to be one" relating to the succession to the property of a deceased native"? I think not-and I am satisfied that the matter is covered by direct authority. The authority is Vanderpuye and Others v. Botchway (1) adopting and following Hagan and Others v. Adum and Others (2). In the former case the plaintiffs therein claimed as persons entitled in succession, and the defendants admitted that they were such. The West African Court of Appeal held that there was no issue of succession, but only an issue as to land, i.e., what portions of the lands and houses should go to each side, and the court proceeded to make the apportionment. On further appeal, the Privy Council held that the suit was one relating to succession. Lord Cohen, deliver­ing the judgment of the Board, pointed out that in the earlier case of Hagan v. Adum (2) before the Board:

"It was argued that subhead (f) (succession suits) included only suits as to the right to succeed, and did not include such matters as valuation of the estate, etc .... Their lordships rejected this contention, saying that they saw no reason for such a narrow construction of the words' suits and matters relating to the succession to property.' "

The learned lord went on to say:

" Their lordships hold that a cause is not a land cause ... if the substance of the issue between the parties is succession to the property of a deceased native."

Then, commenting on the case relied on by the West African Court of Appeal (Kwow v. Eku II (3)), he observed as follows:

"There is nothing to indicate that the plaintiff's claim was

based on any right of succession."

Those latter significant words, it seems to me, supply the crucial test for determining whether a suit is one relating to succession or not. Has the plaintiff to establish his succession to a deceased native as a sine qua non to getting the relief he claims? If he has, then (even though the task be made quite easy for him because his claim to succession is admitted, or because he has a decision in some former proceedings by which to estop his defendant from denying that fact, and even though there be other subsidiary. issues) the substantial basic issue is succession, and the suit is a succession cause. Applying that test, I am satisfied that this suit was one relating to succession, and properly within the jurisdiction of both courts belo

w.

It follows that in my view the contention on behalf of the defen­dant is not well-founded. The appeal fails and is hereby dismissed with costs.

Appeal dismissed.
