                                                    August 1, 1958

                           OFORI ATTA II v. MINISTER OF LOCAL GOVERNMENT

                             High Court,Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                       Ollennu J.

 

 

Practice-Injunction-Interim' injunction What must be established by applicant.

On October 17, 1957, the Paramount Chiefs (Recognition) (Partial Revocation) Instrument, 1957, revoked the names of "N ana Ofori Atta II" and "Akyem Abuakwa" where they appeared in an earlier instru­ment of recognition.

On June 6, 1958, there was a press release, subsequently alleged (and not denied) to be a Government release, the last paragraph of which was as follows:

" Nana Ofori Atta II will cease to exercise any power or func­tions which he possesses by virtue of his position as a Paramount Chief. "

On June 13, 1958, a meeting consisting of the Adontenhene of Akyem Abuakwa and others passed the following resolution:

" ... Whereas the Government has withdrawn recognition from N ana Ofori A tta II, Okyenhene, and divested him of all functions attached to his office,

"Whereas Chiefs and people of Akyem Abuakwa approve of the Government's action ...

" Be it resolved and it is hereby resolved as follows:

" That N ana Ofori Atta II, Okyenhene, is deposed forthwith."

On the same day (June 13, 1958), Nana Ofori Atta II, acting in his capacity of Omanhene and President of the Akyem Abuakwa State Council, issued an originating summons under Order 54 of the Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, directed to the Minister of Local Govern­ment (the Hon. A. E. A. Ofori Atta). He claimed that he had been adversely affected by the Instrument of 1957, and in paragraph (f) he raised for determination the following question, inter alia:

" (f) Whether notwithstanding the said Instrument Nana Ofod Atta II, plaintiff herein, is not entitled to preside at, consult with, and conduct without let or hindrance, all proceedings of the Akyem Abuakwa State Council, including jurisdiction in matters of a constitutional nature vested in the said Akyem Abuakwa State Council by law, now and during his occupancy of the Stool of Akyem Abuakwa. "

He also claimed an injunction to restrain the Minister of Local Govern­ment, and other persons in the service of the Crown, from interfering in any manner with him in the performance of his duties and the enjoy­ment of his rights as Omanhene and President and also with the Akyem Abuakwa State Council in the performance of its duties and the enjoy­ment of its rights as the highest Council within the Akyem Abuakwa State, in which Council the administration of the Akyem Abuakwa State was vested by law.

On June 20, 1958, the Acting Permanent Secretary to the Ministry of Local Government (Mr. N. O. Dobbs) filed his affidavit on behalf of the Minister in reply to the summons. This affidavit included the following submission:

"4 .... It is my respectful submission .

"(b) That the said Instrument in no way affects or interferes with the plaintiff's customary status or duties as Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa State or as a member or President of the Akyem Abuakwa State Council.

"5. I swear to this affidavit in support of the Defendant's contention: ...

" (c) that the membership of the Akyem Abuakwa State Council has not been varied by the said Instrument;

" (d) that the said Instrument does not interfere with or in any way affect the office of a Chief as existing by customary law and usage, and was not made in breach of the Ghana (Constitution) Order-in­Council, 1957 ; and

" (f) that notwithstanding the said Instrument, the plaintiff is entitled to exercise those functions set out in paragraph (f) of the originating summons."

On July 8, 1958, the Government Agent, Kibi (Mr. W. C. Brown), addressed the following letter to " The Acting President, Akyem Abuakwa State Council" :

" Sir,

CONCURRENCE IN STOOL LAND PROPERTY AGREEMENTS

"I am directed by the Regional Commissioner to ask the State Council of Akyem Abuakwa to appoint some persons other than the Okyenhene, in respect of whom Government has withdrawn recog­nition, to authorise documents on behalf of the State Council.

"2. I should be grateful to learn the names and appointment of the persons so appointed.

" I have the honour to be, etc.

" The Acting President,

Akyem Abuakwa State Council.

On July 12 N ana Ofori Atta II filed an ex parte notice of motion for an interim injunction in the same terms as in his claim for a perpetual injunction. In support, he filed an affidavit, of which para. 5 was as follows;

"That on or about June 13, 1958, soon after the issue of the originating Summons the Adontenhene of Akyem Abuakwa ... and others ... expressly acting on the Paramount Chiefs (Recognition) (Partial Revocation) Instrument, 1957, and also on a Government press release dated June 6, 1958, which said press release purports to be based on the said Instrument, purported to declare me destooled as Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa and to declare me no longer a member or President of the Akyem Abuakwa State Council."

To this affidavit he attached a copy of the press release dated June 6, 1958 (which he said was issued by Government), and a copy of the letter dated July 8, 1958, written by the Government Agent, Kibi.

In answer to the allegations made in the applicant's affidavit of July 12, the Principal Assistant Secretary of the Ministry of Local Government (Mr. W. H. Redhead) filed an affidavit in which he deposed, inter alia, as follows:

" .. 3. The respondent has at no time by himself or by his agents or by any other person or persons in the service of the Crown interfered with the applicant in the performance of his duties and the enjoy­ment of his rights as Omanhene of Akyem Abuakwa or as a member or President of the Akyem Abuakwa State Council. The respondent is in no way responsible for the actions of the Adontenhene of Akyem Abuakwa or of his supporters referred to in paragraph 5 of the applicant's affidavit. It is my respectful submission, and I verily believe, that the said actions were in purported exercise of his rights under customary law and in law are in no way attributable to the making of the Paramount Chiefs (Recognition) (Partial Revocation) Instrument, 1957, which in any event was signified by the respondent as long ago as October 17, 1957, that is to say over seven months before the actions referred to. It is my respectful submission that the very fact that the Adontenhene of Akyem Abuakwa deemed it necessary to summon a meeting on June 13, 1958, to resolve (as set out in the document marked 'B' attached to the applicant's said affidavit) that 'Nana Ofori Atta II, Okyenhene, is deposed forth­with' is in itself some evidence of the fact that the making of the said Instrument did not interfere with or in any way affect the applicant's office of a Chief as existing by customary law and usage ....

"5. I have read the document marked' A' (the letter from the Government Agent, Kibi) attached to the applicant's affidavit sworn to on July 11. In my respectful submission the Government and any person acting on its behalf is entitled to request a State Council to nominate a person or persons to authorise documents on its behalf, and furthermore to indicate a desire that someone other than a specified person should be so nominated."

Counsel for the applicant submitted that the acts complained of in his client's affidavit were an infringement of the applicant's legal rights which, if allowed to continue during the pendency of the suit, would result in injury to him.

For the respondent it was submitted that th.e legal rights of the applicant were not denied (as indicated in the affidavits of Mr. Dobbs and Mr. Redhead), and that in such circumstances the court would not, in law, grant an injunction.

Held, (i) the question before the court was whether or not the applicant had shown a strong case of probability that his legal rights, which the respondent admitted, were likely to be violated by acts done (or threatened to be done) by the respondent, or by anyone in the service of the Crown. If the court found that the applicant had made out such a strong case of real, apprehended or threatened acts, as against a mere fear, the court (on the authorities) should grant th~ application.

(ii) The proper interpretation of the terms of the press release, of Mr. Brown's letter and of Mr. Redhead's submission on the latter was that although the applicant was by law and native custom the President of the State Council, and the proper person to execute and authorise documents on behalf of the State Council, yet Government by some special powers not disclosed was claiming to direct the members of the State Council to disregard what was by law and by the custom of the State established, and to appoint a person other than the person entitled by law and custom to exercise specific duties.

(iii) The applicant had therefore proved conclusively that what was going on was calculated to infringe his rights, and the court was bound to grant an interim injunction, in order that matters should remain in statu quo while the questions before the court on the originating summons were finally determined.

Cases referred to :

(1) Attorney-General v. Long Eaton Urban District Council [1915] 1 Ch. 124; 111 L.T. 514; 31 T.L.R. 45.

(2) Attorney-General for the Dominion of Canada v. Ritchie Contracting and Supply Co., Ltd. and Others [1919] A.C. 999; 121 L. T. 655; 36 T.L.R. 1, P.C.

ApPLICATION by Nana Ofori Atta II for an interim injunction to restrain the Minister of Local Government and other servants of the Crown from interfering with him (as Omanhene and President of the Akyem Abuakwa State Council), and with the said State Council, in the enjoyment of their respective rights and the performance of their respective duties.

Swanzy for the applicant.

Battcock, Solicitor-General, for the respondent.

OLLENNU J. referred to the history of the matter and con­tinued: As a general rule an injunction (interim or perpetual) is not granted where the legal rights claimed are admitted, and where there is no proof of imminent threat of invasion of the legal rights, or proof of an intention to violate the legal rights. In Halsbury (3rd ed.), Vol. 21, pp. 356-7, para. 745, the principle is stated as follows:

. where the plaintiff's legal right is not disputed, an injunction may be granted to restrain the commission of an apprehended or threatened act, on the ground that the act, if done, will violate the plaintiff's legal right, if he can show a strong case of probability that the apprehended mischief will in fact arise; but no one can obtain a quia timet order by merely saying , Timeo '; he must aver and prove that what is going on is calculated to infringe his right. "

(See the cases of Attorney-General v. Long Eaton Urban District Council (1); Attorney-General for the Dominion of Canada v. Ritchie Contracting and Supply Co., Ltd. and Others (2).)

The same principle is stated in Kerr on Injunctions (5th ed.), the work cited by the learned Acting Solicitor-General, at pages 17-18, as follows:

" If the legal right is not disputed, a man who seeks the aid of the court must be able to show that the act complained of is in fact a violation of the right, or is at least an act which, if carried into effect, will necessarily result in a violation of the right. The mere prospect or apprehension of injury or the mere belief that the act complained of mayor will be done is not sufficient; but if an intention to do the act complained of can be shown to exist, or if a man insists on his right to do so, or begins to do, or threatens to do, or gives notice of his intention to do, an act which must, in the opinion of the court, if completed give a ground for an action, there is a foundation for the exercise of the jurisdiction. The mere denial by a man of his intention to do an act or to infringe a right will not prevent the court from interfering."

The court must therefore decide upon the evidence before it whether the applicant has shown a strong case of probability that his legal rights, which the respondent admits, are likely to be violated by acts done (or threatened to be done) by the respondent, or by any person Or persons in the service of the Crown. If the court finds that he has made such a strong case of real, apprehended or threatened act, as against a mere fear, it should grant the application.

Now two very important documents have been exhibited. They are: (1) the press release of June 6, 1958, and

(2) the letter dated July 8, 1958, written by the Government Agent, Kibi (Mr. W. C. Brown).

As regards the press release, the allegation of the applicant that it is a Government release has not been denied. The last paragraph of it reads:

"Nana Ofori Atta II will cease to exercise any power or functions which he possesses by virtue of his position as a Paramount Chief.'·

In my opinion that paragraph, unambiguous in its language and terms, is an assertion by Government that the applicant should not exercise the duties and privileges of his office.

The letter of the Government Agent, Kibi (Mr. W. C. Brown) reads as follows:

" Sir,

CONCURRENCE IN STOOL LAND PROPERTY AGREEMENTS

" I am directed by the Regional Commissioner to ask the State Council of Akyem Abuakwa to appoint some persons other than the Okyenhene, in respect of whom Government has withdrawn recognition, to authorise documents on behalf of the State Council.

"2. I should be grateful to learn the names and appointment of the persons so appointed.

            " I have the honour to be, etc.

" The Acting President,

" Akyem Abuakwa State Council, " IGbi."

Of that letter Mr. Redhead said, in paragraph 5 of his affidavit:


 

" In my respectful submission the Government and any person acting on its behalf is entitled to request a State Council to nominate a person or persons to authorise documents on its behalf, and furthermore to indicate a desire that some one other than a specified person should be so nominated. "

By addressing that letter to "The Acting President" of the State Council, the Government Agent must be understood to mean that the applicant is no longer to exercise the duties of his office as President of the Council.

My interpretation of Mr. Redhead's submission on Mr. Brown's letter is that although the applicant is by law and native custom the President of the State Council and the proper person to execute and authorise documents on behalf of the State Council, yet Govern­ment by some special powers not disclosed is entitled to direct the members of the State Council to disregard what is by law and by custom of the State established, and to appoint a person other than the person entitled by law and custom to exercise those specific duties.

Thus, by the press release, the letter of the Government Agent, and the affidavit of the Principal Assistant Secretary to the Ministry of Local Government, the applicant has proved conclusively that what is going on is calculated to infringe his rights. In those circumstances the court is bound, upon the authorities cited above, to grant an interim injunction against the respondent, in order that matters should remain in statu quo until the questions before the court on the originating summons are finally determined. I therefore grant an order for an interim injunction in terms of the application.

Interim injunction to issue.
