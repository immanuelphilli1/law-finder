                                                 September 12, 1957.

                                    REGINA v. ARTHUR AND OTHERS.

                                                 High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                Quashie­Idun Ag.C.J., Windsor-Aubrey J.

 

 

Criminal Contempt-Practice-Jurisdiction of court to entertain proceedings for contempt-Whether proceedings for contempt of one branch of High Court maintainable before another branch.

Pmctice-Jurisdiction of Chief Justice-Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 7.

Applications were made to the Divisional Court, Accra, for orders of attachment to issue against certain journalists in respect of newspaper articles dealing with a matter which, at the time they were published, was before the Divisional Court, Kumasi. The articles, it was alleged, were in contempt of the latter court. In the course of the application it was argued that as the Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 7 (2), gave jurisdiction to the Chief Justice to hear any cause or matter arising in any part of Ghana, the application could properly be received by the Chief Justice sitting in Accra although the alleged contempt was of a court at Kumasi.

Held, (i) the allegation was of a contempt of a Divisional Court; in such cases the only court with jurisdiction to entertain an application

for attachment for contempt is the court in respect of which the contempt is alleged to have been committed. In this instance therefore the Divi­sional Court at Accra had no jurisdiction to entertain the application, which should have been made to the Divisional Court, Kumasi.

In the matter of an application for attachment for contempt of court (1886) 2 T.L.R. 351 followed.

(ii) The Courts Ordinance, c. 4 of 1951, s. 7, confers equal power, authority and jurisdiction upon all judges of the Supreme Court but, this power, authority and jurisdiction can be exercised by such judges only within the judicial divisions to which they are assigned. An exception to this rule is created by the same section in respect of the Chief Justice who, apart from his powers of transfer, has jurisdiction in all judicial divisions. This jurisdiction however does not empower the Chief Justice, without exercising his power of transfer, to hear a matter in a court which otherwise would have no jurisdiction to receive the matter.

Case referred to :

(1) In the Matter of an Application for Attachment for Contempt of Court (1886) 2 T.L.R. 351.

ApPLICATIONS for orders of attachment to issue against Ian Colvin and against S. Arthur and others in respect of conduct alleged to be in contempt of the Divisional Court, Ashanti Judicial Division, Kumasi. The applications were made to the Divisional Court, Eastern Judicial Division.

The following ruling of the court was given by QUASHIE-IDUN Ag.C.J. This is a ruling on the submission made by counsel on behalf of the respondents that this court has no jurisdiction to hear the applications for an order of attachment against the respondents for contempt of court, as the publication complained of, and alleged to be contemptuous were made in respect of proceedings pending in the Divisional Court, K umasi, in the Ashanti Judicial Division.

There are two applications before the court and the court intimated that it would hear them separately. It was agreed, however, that counsel would be at liberty to adopt the submissions in respect of the point of jurisdiction in both cases.

In support of his submission, Mr. Christopher Shawcross has referred us to In the Matter of an Application for an Attachment fOT Contempt of Court (1). In that case an application was made to the Queen's Bench Division to commit for contempt the printer and publisher of Punch for publishing an alleged libellous comment on proceedings pending in another court. In the course of the hearing of that application, counsel for the applicant submitted that the court had a general jurisdiction in matters of the kind to punish persons guilty of contempt. Lord Coleridge, the Lord Chief Justice, then asked whether there was any precedent for that court exercising its prerogative in respect of contempt of another court. Counsel for the applicant admitted that he knew of none, but submitted that as there had been a flagrant interference with justice, the court should make a precedent.

The Lord Chief Justice said that he had never even heard it sug­gested that the court could imprison a man for contempt of court committed in another place, and that the court had no jurisdiction to grant the application. Mr. Justice Hawkins, who sat with the Lord Chief Justice, in concurring stated that the court should only punish for contempt of itself.

Mr. Bing has referred the court to the Courts Ordinance, c. 4, s. 7, and has submitted that, in his view, the provisions of the section confer on the Chief Justice jurisdiction to entertain the application even though the alleged contempt was committed in respect of proceedings pending at the Divisional Court, Kumasi.

The section reads as follows:

" (1) All judges of the Supreme Court shall have in all respects, save as is herein otherwise expressly provided, equal power, authority and jurisdiction.

(2) Any judge of the Supreme Court may, subject to this Ordinance and any Buies of Court, exercise all or any part of the original and appellate jurisdiction, civil and criminal, vested by this Ordinance in the Supreme Court and for such purpose shall be and form a court: Provided that in the case of the Chief Justice, in addition to and independently of the special powers otherwise expressly vested in him with respect to the transfer of causes and matters or otherwise, the said Chief Justice shall have and may exercise, in any place in Ghana, jurisdiction throughout Ghana, and that with respect to any cause or matter arising in or with respect to any part of Ghana, any other enactment or statutory provision notwithstanding."

In our view the section confers equal power, authority and jurisdic­tion on all the judges of the Supreme Court, but 'this can be exercised only in their respective judicial jurisdictions; it empowers the Chief Justice to exercise jurisdiction throughout Ghana, independent of the special powers vested in him with respect to the transfer of causes and matters from one judicial division to another. It means, in our view, that the Chief Justice can hear any cause or matter pending in any Divisional Court of the country and at that court, if he does not wish to exercise his powers of transfer. It does not, in our view, confer any jurisdiction on the Chief Justice to hear any cause or matter in any court other than the court which has jurisdiction, or where such cause or matter has been instituted in a court which has no jurisdiction, to entertain the case.

We are of the considered opinion that, as the proceedings in respect of which the alleged contempt was committed were pending in the Divisional Court, Kumasi, the present application should have been commenced in Kumasi. We hold, therefore, that this court has no jurisdiction to entertain the application and it is accordingly refused.

We do not think that it would be proper for us to deal with the other submissions affecting the question of the jurisdiction of this court, as in our view not only would it prejudice any future proceedings which may be instituted in the matter, but also, as we have no jurisdic­tion to hear the applications, we cannot give a ruling on matters cognisable only by a court that has jurisdiction.

Orders refused.

S. G. D.
