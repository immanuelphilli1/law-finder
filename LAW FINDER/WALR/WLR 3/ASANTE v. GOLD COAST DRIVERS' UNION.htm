This was an appeal in an action commenced in a Native Court in which the plaintiff sought damages for breach of an alleged agreement to let three rooms in a house at a fixed monthly rental. The defendants were an unincorporated association sued under their business name.

 

By the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 15 (b), it is provided that Native Courts shall administer, inter alw, the provisions of any law binding between parties subject to the jurisdiction of Native Courts: "provided that in any civil cause or matter a Native Court shall not, unless the parties agree thereto, have jurisdiction to determine any cause wherein it shall appear either from express contract or from the nature of the transaction out of which such cause or matter may have arisen, that the parties agreed either expressly or by necessary implication that the obligations arising out of such transactions should be regulated exclusively by English law."

Held, (i) an agreement for a tenancy of a house or of rooms is an agreement which finds no place in native law and .custom. It follows, therefore that the rights and liabilities of the partIes to such an agree­ment can' be determined only by reference to English law. In the absence, therefore of aar~ement between the parties, by the operation of the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 15 (b), the trial Native Court is deprived of jurisdiction to entertain the suit.

McCarthy J., reading the judgment of the court, said:

" There is in our opinion no ground for holding that 'the said agreement should be governed exclusively by English law, for the promise in the memorandum of sale is not necessarily one for transfer of the property by deed. "

A fortiori, in the case under consideration where the agreement is not even in writing, it would seem at the firs't blush that" there is no ground for holding that the said agreement should be governed exclusively by English law."

Upon a more careful consideration of the situation, however, the reason for the \Vest African Court of Appeal coming to the conclusion that there is no ground for holding that the agreement is to be governed exclusively by English law may be discovered in the fact 'that transfer of land and/or house on outright sale, far from being quite foreign and unknown to native customary law, is quite well known and well recognised. Hayes-Redwar J., in his invaluable Commentaries, at p. 75, observed as follows:

" According to the ancient na'tive law (before alterations were brought about by the gradually solvent action of contact with European laws and usages) there was no such thing as the absolute alienation of land, and the mere usufruct was all that could be obtained, by the consent of the owner in the manner which has been mentioned above. Gradually, however, verbal sales and mortgages or pledges of land in the presence of witnesses came into use, mos't probably under pressure from creditors of the family to whom the community was heavily indebted."

Is the transfer of houses and/or rooms on tenancy also equally well known and recognised by or under native customary law?

To find out the answer, it seems necessary 'to turn to Sarbah's inestimable Fanti Customary Law, 2nd edn., under the chapter dealing with" Tenures." At p. 66, he states as follows:

" The granting of permission to others and outsiders to reside on or cultivate the lands of a family or stool or a village community, is a practice of the greatest antiquity and was in times past more universal than sale of land which is of comparatively modern growth ...

At p. 67 he refers further to:

" The making of grants 'to strangers, particularly to Europeans, of waste lands and lands abandoned or never under cultivation by anyone, and of minerals and of concessions of forest land for a term of years, though said to be modern comparatively speaking, is not necessarily illegal according to customary law."

As to the above-mentioned grants, we know that the documents take the form of English leases and have to comply with the provisions of the Concessions Ordinances in force for the time being.

He refers further to:

" The simplest and most common kind of tenure is wha't may be called sowing tenure. Here the owner of a plot of land usually gives to a person ... leave to have the use of this land for one sowing season. In the absence of agreement the owner of the land is en'titled to take ... a small proportion about one-tenth of any crops grown on such land. There is also known what may be called an ' Annual tenure' running from year to year. The person having once got the land has full right to cultivate it for any duration of time until the owner by due notice 'terminates the tenancy. The rent usually reserved in the absence of special or other agreement, is the help which the tenant is bound to render the landlord a't the periods or seasons of sowing and reaping, usually three days in 'the week (see pp. 68-69)."

He speaks finally of:

" Grants of land for building purposes are frequently made in the form of perpetual leases, either for some valuable consideration, or by way of reward for pas't services or on the ground of mere affection or friendship."

And he then goes on to describe the incidents attached to such grants of lands for building (see pp. 70-74).

But not a word is said throughout this invaluable book-our ca'techism of native customary law-as to letting or hiring of houses or rooms.

It is also worthy of note that as Appendices I and II to the same book are printed: (a) Report, published by the Gold Coast Govern­ment, of " Opinion on the Native Tenure in 'the Gold Coast" by Mr. Justice Francis Smith, sometime Ag.C.J., and (b) Report upon the " Customs of the Non-Mohammedan Tribes in the Gold Coast in regard to tenure of land" by Bruce-Hindle-and that whilst both reports mention transfers of land on outright sale as well as on mortgage or pledge by form or method of native customary law, no mention what­ever is made of any known or recognised custom or usage of hiring or letting of houses or rooms.

But perhaps most significant and interesting is the fact that among the invaluable collection of cases in the Fanti Customary Law as well as the Fanti Law Report there is only one, hereunder referred to, dealing with an oral agreement for the hire or letting of a room and that in that case the Divisional Court on appeal from the Magistrate's Court, held that "this case must be decided according to English law," against the contention of the plaintiff that 'the case should be decided according to native law. The reason for the decision of the court that English law rather than native customary law must be applied to the determination of the rights of the parties under the particular transaction, concurred in and affirmed by the judge of 'the Divisional Court, can hardly be because the agreement was made by an infant. It is more intelligible and reasonable to hold that it was because the agreement was for hire of premises, a transaction unknown and unrecognised by na'tive customary law, as contrasted with other transactions well known and recognised such as transfers on sale (see Basel Mission Factory v. Bruce (3)), or on mortgage or pledge (see Amonoo v. Abakuma (4) and Agbo J(ofi v. Addo [{ofi (5)).

The only other reported case in respect of an oral agreement to hire premises appears to be Johnson and Alwther v. Golightly (6). In that case the defendant Golightly was occupying the premises of one J{orang as tenant when the premises were sold by public auction and purchased by the second plaintiff Ankrah, whereupon Ankrah entered into an oral agreement to sell and/or lease the premises to Golightly, but afterw&rds and in breach of the agreement sold to the plaintiff Johnson, and the two of them then joined in an action to obtain recovery of possession of the premises from Golightly the defendant. Smyly C.J. said:

" Assuming for the moment that there was an agreement, it appears to me to be immaterial whether the subject-matter was freehold or leasehold, as it could only relate to whatever interest Ankrah acquired at the sale to him; and its alleged transfer to Golightly. "

Held: that the agreement was governed by English law and subject to the equitable principle of part performance.

It seems reasonably clear, therefore, from the foregoing survey of the situation, that the agreement for hiring and/or letting of a house or room has not been one of the transactions known and recognised by native customary law, having well-defined formalities or rites for

its creation and with well-recognised incidents and results such as the transactions of outright sale, mortgage or pledge, or tenancy of land. It is one of the many useful forms of transactions introduced to us by our contact with European merchants, and must no doubt have started when merchants required premises for their trading activities in the large towns in the early days.

In the case of Vanderpuye v. Plange and Others (7) the court said as follows:

" This is a suit claiming damages for breach of a covenant of title. A covenant of title has no existence in native customary law; the rights and liabilities under a covenant of title can be ascertained only by reference to English law ... \Ve are of opinion that it clearly appears that the parties by implication agreed that their obligations in connection with the transactions should be regulated substantially according to the provisions of English law and that 'the only law properly applicable thereto is English law, and that therefore the jurisdiction of the native tribunal is ousted by the proviso."

As it appeared to the West African Court of Appeal in respect of a covenant for title, even so it appears to me that an agreement or contract for hiring or letting of a house or rooms has no existence in na'tive customary law, and the rights and liabilities under such an agreement for hiring or letting can be ascertained only by reference to English law.

It follows, therefore, that even if the defendants, an association of persons operating or trading under a business name or style, can be said to come within the definition of "Natives" under the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, the jurisdiction which the North Birim Native Court" A " would otherwise have to entertain, hear and determine the suit was clearly ousted by the proviso to section 15 (b) hereinbefore referred to.

But I am further of the opinion that the plaintiff not having sued any number of individuals together in their private names as defen­dants, but an association, body or group of individuals, who though unincorporated are operating together in a business name or style, the defendant is not a native as contemplated and envisaged by the Ordinance, and a suit against such an unincorporated association, body or group in its business name or style, in my view, is beyond the competence and jurisdiction of the Native Court.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
