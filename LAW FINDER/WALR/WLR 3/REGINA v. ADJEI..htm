                                                    December 22, 1958

                                                           REGINA v. ADJEI.   

                                                            Court of Appeal

                               van Lare Ag.C.J., Granville Sharp J.A., Acolatse J

 

 

Evidence-Admissibility-Matter for judge alone. Evidence-Criminal law-Confessions-Procedure where a voluntary state­ment is taken by police.

At the trial of the appellant, who stood convicted of murder, a con­fession writ en in the appellant's own hand, in the presence of the police, was admitted in evidence. There had been no lay witness present when the confession was made and it was argued, inter alia, that this rendered the statement inadmissible.

At the trial, the judge had allowed the confession to go to the assessors with the direction- that they "should disregard it if they thought it a reasonable possibility that., it was not made voluntarily."

          Held, (i) the desirability   thatt an independent lay witness who under-

stands what is being said should be present when a voluntary statement is being taken by the police, and who signs the statement as a witness when it is complete, is not a rule of law, the failure to comply with which will render a confession inadmissible as evidence, but a principle for guidance, the object of which is to help authenticate the voluntary nature of the statement. In the present case the fact that the statement was in the accused's own hand and the quality of the handwriting, com­bined with the acceptable evidence of a police officer, taken together could have been regarded by the judge as sufficient to establish the voluntary nature of the statement.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The principle here referred to was advocated by the West African Court of Appeal in R. v. Abisa Grunshie (1955) 1 W.A.L.R. 36. It would perhaps be straining the language of the judgment in the present case too far if it were to be regarded as holding that in the case of a holograph the presence of an independent lay witness can safely be dispensed with, for a statement made under a promise or an inducement, as well as under a threat, may be treated as involuntary, and thus inadmissible.]

(ii) Questions as to the admissibility of evidence are matters for the judge alone. The direction to the assessors to disregard the confession if there was a reasonable possibility that it was not voluntary was therefore improper.

APPEAL from a conviction for murder by Murphy J. sitting with assessors at Kumasi Assize on July 31, 1958.

Crabbe, Senior Crown counsel, for the Crown.

Quashie-Idun for the appellant.

The following judgment of the court was delivered by GRANVILLE SHARP J.A.:

The appellant was convicted of and sentenced for murder by Murphy J. sitting with assessors on July 31, 1958. The charge was that on February 22, 1958, at Jacobu in Ashanti the appellant murdered Yaa Nyemekye. The deceased was a woman to whom he had been married and by whom he had had children. She twice separated from him; on the first occasion after confessing to him that she had committed adultery with ten men during her latest preg­nancy, and again after a period of reconciliation when she announced that she no longer intended to stay with him in marriage. The appel­lant visited a herbalist who, at the trial, told the court that he had been asked by the appellant to supply to him certain medicine by means of which he might drive his wife, the deceased, into madness. This witness further stated that the appellant added that if this medi­cine was not supplied to him then he would know what to do. All this evidence as to the existence of motive was denied by the appel­lant. He said that he asked for medicine for his sick child and that the herbalist was concocting a story against him because he had dis­covered an adulterous relationship between the herbalist and the deceased.

The substantive defence raised by the appellant at the trial was that he was not at Jacobu on February 23, which was a Sunday. He said that he had been there on the previous day, but had gone to Adowa where he met one Kwabina Moshie in whose house he stayed on the Sunday and over the following night.

Two witnesses were called who deposed to having seen the appel­lant in Jacobu on the Sunday. He was wearing cloth wrapped right round him and, according to the second prosecution witness, he was going in the direction of a public latrine at about 7.30 p.m. According to the fifth prosecution witness, at 6 p.m. he was near Jacobu and going towards that place; he was wrapped in a cover cloth with his arms underneath, it. The sixth prosecution witness left her house and went to the latrine. She was in company with about nine other women returning from the latrine at about 7.30 p.m. The deceased was with them and they were walking in file, the deceased being some little way behind the witness. A gunshot was fired from the left:

The witness fled, saw the second witness and told him what she had heard.

Later she went with the police and at a place about six yards from where she had been walking when she heard the gunshot she saw the body of the deceased with wounds in the left arm and left breast. No one saw who it was who fired the shot.

After he had been arrested the accused made a statement after being cautioned. He wrote this statement with his own hand. It was a circumstantial confession of having shot his wife after brooding upon her imperfections and upon the wrongs that she had done him. He explained that he had thrown the gun into the River Odu. He had run away when the police had handcuffed him, because his mind was in such a state of unrest. In a second statement the appellant said he did not wish to add to the one previously written by him, but added: "I had no intention to do that, but it was an accident."

The appellant said at the trial that he was forced to make this statement by threats and promises given to him by the police officers in whose custody he was. He further objected that the statement was not witnessed or signed by any lay person and that no such person was present when he was writing it ..

Ghe learned judge heard evidence upon the issue thus raised and admitted the statement and the question whether he was not in error in so doing was the only one upon which the appeal before us was argued] The learned judge believed the evidence of the police wit­ness, Robina Edward Eyison, and rejected the story of the appellant. We are of opinion that he was in this perfectly justified. The state­ment which he saw at the trial was written in a form and with a style of caligraphy that was so careful, distinct and uniform that it is quite impossible to believe that it was written under any kind of stress induced by force or any other means. Furthermore, the fact that it was written by the appellant himself in our view renders unobjection­able the fact that no lay witness was present. The police officer had in fact tried to obtain such a witness, but had failed in his endeavour. In our opinion the objection that the statement was not voluntary was very properly rejected by the learned judge.

Apart, however, from this confession by the appellant there was ample evidence that it was he who shot the deceased and this was added to by the defendant himself who was palpably lying as to his whereabouts on the evening when the crime took place

In only one respect did the learned judge err. His judgment con­tains the following passage:

" I allowed the statement to be put in. I directed the asses­sors that they should disregard it if they thought it a reasonable possibility that it was not made voluntarily."

Such a direction would, if the statement was properly admitted, clearly enure to the benefit of an accused person rather than to his hurt, but it is none the less a misdirection inasmuch as the question of the admissibility of the statement as a voluntary statement is one solely for the judge. Once rightly or wrongly he has decided that question it only remains for the jury to evaluate the weight that should be given to the contents of the statement for reasons wholly extraneous to its admissibility. As has been stated no ill effects have derived from the error which we indicate, but we have thought it right that attention should be called to it because once the statement is admitted it is before the jury and if it is wrongly admitted no direc­tion such as was given in this case can avail to correct the error, it being impossible to divine what weight might in any given case be attached to the statement by the jury, who are in possession of the statement and are bound to consider it together with all the other facts presented in evidence.

Upon every consideration presented to us by counsel on behalf of appellant the appeal failed and was accordingly dismissed.

S. G. D.
