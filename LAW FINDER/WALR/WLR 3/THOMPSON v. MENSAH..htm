The plaintiff sought possession of and a declaration of title to an area of land within the Kokomlemle area of Accra. The land was within that part of the Kokomlemle area that had been found, in previous litigation

Golightly and Another v. Ashrifi and Others (1955) 14 W.A.C.A. 676­otherwise known as the Kokomlemle Oonsolidated Oases) to be held by the Kotey family under the Ga and Gbese Stools and the Korle We. The plaintiff, who had never been in possession, relied on three grants made to him in 1944 and 1945 by persons who derived their title from the Korle Webii. The defendant, who was in possession and who had erected build­ings on the land, claimed at first to hold by a grant from the Atukpai family. When. it became apparent that the land concerned was Kotey family land instead of Atukpai family land he, the defendant, obtained the concurrence of the Kotey family to his occupancy of the land and thereafter he relied upon this.

Golightl-y and Another v. Ashrifi and Others held, inter alia, that for an outright alienation (i.e., of an absolute estate) of Kokomlemle land the consent of the Ga and Gbese Stools and of the Korle We is required, together with the assent of the family in possession: for the alienation of a determinable estate the consents need to be those of the Gbese Stool and the Korle We, together with the assent of the family in possession. The issue raised by the plaintiff, who was the appellant on the appeal, was as to whether he had obtained a good title under a grant derived from the Korle We alone. Thus, he was in the difficulty that, regardless of whether or not the defendant could show a good title, he, the plaintiff, coming forward as he did with a claim to ownership of the land, rather than as a dispossessed possessor, was from the outset in no position to do so. It would appear that, appreciating this after opening his case, the plaintiff obtained an adjournment and used the time thus gained to approach the Gbese Stool. On the resumption the plaintiff, without amending his claim, called further evidence and put in two deeds, each of which bore a date (October 31, 1956) subsequent to the commencement of the hearing, and which purported to be a grant by the Gbese Mantse to each of his immediate grantors.

On the appeal the plaintiff argued that the grants by the Gbese Mantse _­confirmed the original grants to his grantors by the Korle We; that ..the Kotey family in concurring in the grant to the defendant without his (the defendant's) having obtained the consent of the controlling stools,

were acting in denial of the title of the controlling stools and thus must be deemed to have forfeited their own rights and interests in the land; that, therefore, the way was thus clear for a valid grant to be made by the con­trolling stools without the assent of the family in possession; and, that

the grant to his grantors by the Korle We, confirmed by the Gbese Stool, operated to confer upon his grantors, and thus upon him, at least a deter­minable estate in the land.

Held, (i) the plaintiff, in his pleadings, relied upon a grant derived, from one grantor; subsequently he attempted to set up a grant derived  from two grantors. As he had not sought leave to amend his claim so as

to plead the material fact of reliance on a grant derived from two grantors,

he should not have been allowed to lead evidence to establish such grant.

(ii) The deeds of October 31, 1956, were, in any case, inadmissible as evidence since due execution of them had not been proved.

(iii) The deeds, on their face, purported to convey land on the dates on which they were executed but, as they did not appear to have been executed with the consent of the Ga State Council and the Accra Munici­pal Council, as required by Ordinance, they were void of effect.

(iv) Even if the deeds were admissible and were not void of effect, they  nevertheless could not have the effect attributed to them by the plaintiff. They did not, on their face, appear to be confirmatory in nature and, even if they had been so expressed, they did not relate back to a period whim their respective grantees had something that could be confirmed; at the date of the deeds the grantees had long since divested themselves of any interests they might have had ~n the land and that were capable of being confirmed.

(v) The estate claimed by the plaintiff was an. absolute estate and thus, whatever the position might have been if he had claimed merely a deter­minable estate, the claim must in any case fail, for the consent of. one of the controlling stools (the Ga Stool) had not been obtained to the alienation.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The learned judge here speaks of the effect of the failure to obtain one of the requisite consents as being that of rendering the alienation void. This appears to conflict with the general principle that the absence of a necessary consent makes a grant voidable at the suit of the party whose consent has not been obtained, rather than void. It may be that the distinction lies in the fact that in the present case the grantee had never been in possession and that only if he was, or had at some time been in possession would absence of consent have made the grant merely voidable. If this is so the distinction would appear to be somewhat illogical for whether a grant is void or voidable ought to depend on the nature of the grant itself, rather than upon the subse­quent activities of the grantee. It would perhaps be more logical to say that a grant by the controlling stools without the assent of the family or person in possession can only be effective if the family or person in possession had pre­viously committed some act (such as an act of forfeiture) that would deprive them of the right to make a grant. In such a case the question of whether or not the consents of the controlling stools had been obtained would not arise, for then, presumably, the grant would be by the stools as grantors, rather than as consenting parties, and the absence of one of the grantors from the grant would render it void.]

(vi) Even if the plaintiff had claimed for a determinable estate, his claim must necessarily fail, for the alienation on which he founded his claim lacked the assent of the family in possession, and was thus void. It was of no assistance to the plaintiff for him to claim that the family in possession had forfeited its interest in the land by purporting to make a grant to the <1.efendant without the consent of the controlling stools, thereby leaving the way open to the controlling stools to make a grant, for where a person holding for a determinable estate makes a grant of the land concerned on terms that the grantee shall render part of the proceeds of the land, or mesne profits, to the grantor (i.e., where, effectively, the relationship of landlord and tenant is created) this is not an alienation constituting a denial of title of the stool concerned and exposing the possessor to forfeiture. On the evidence, there was nothing to show that the family in possession had done more than grant a licence to the defendant; they thus could not be said to have committed an act of forfeiture,

(vii) Even if the act of the family in possession had been of such a nature as to amount to a denial of the title of their stool grantor, for­feiture does not follow as an automatic consequence of such an act. For forfeiture to be effective the ultimate owner (i.e., the stool-grantor) must take positive steps to enforce its right. If a stool, with knowledge of an act of forfeiture committed by one holding of the stool for a determinable estate, stands by and takes no steps to enforce its rights, it may subse­quently be estopped by its acquiescence from asserting these rights. In the present case there was evidence that the controlling stools, having knowledge of the act of the family in possession, never raised objection to this act and must be deemed to have acquiesced therein.

Golightly and Another v. Ashrifi and Others (1955) 14 W.A.C.A. 676, distinguished.

(viii) In any event, the consent of the controlling stool is not essential to a grant, by a stool-subject in possession for a det€rminable estate, of the whole of that estaw to another stool-subject. And any attempt by a controlling stool, when making a grant of a determinable estate to a stool­subject, to import a term into the grant to the effect that alienation of that estate to another stool-subject shall be effective only upon consent by the controlling stool, win be void of effect as being unreasonable and contrary to established custom.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. See on this point, Ohvmen v. Adjei (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 275. The learned judge appears in this case to go further than the proposition outlined above. He says:

" In my opinion the correct statement of the native custom is that a usufructuary title (i.e., a determinable estate) can be transferred without the consent of the real owner provided that the transfer carries with it an obligation upon the transferee to recognise the title of the real owner and all the incidents of the subject's right of occupation, including the performance of customary services to the real owner."

It would appear to be a reasonable inference from this that the consent of the stool is not required in any case of alienation of a determinable estate, i.e., whether it be to another stool-subject or to a stool stranger. The nature of a determinable estate imports an obligation to observe the incidents of that estate. The act of the holder of a determinable estate that exposes the land to risk of forfeiture is a purported conveyance by him of an absolute estate, since this denies the title of the controlling stool, the ultimate owner. See further Baidoo v. Osei and Owusu (1958) 3 W.A.L.R. 289.]

(ix) The defendant, on the plaintiff's own admission, was in possession of the land and thus, regardless of his own title, was entitled to be protected in that possession against all save the true owner.

Cases referred to:

(1) Golightly and Another v. Ashrifi and Others (1955) 14 W.A.C.A. 676.

(2) Philipps v. Philipps (1878) 4 Q.B.D. 127; 48 L.J.Q.B. 135; 39 L.T. 556.

(3) Adumua-Bossman v. Bannerman, West African Court of Appeal, Feb­ruary 27, 1957, unreported.

ApPEAL from a decision of the Land Court, Eastern Judicial Divi­sion, on November 1, 1956, in favour of .the defendant in an action for a declaration of title to land.

Asafu-Adjaye for the plaintiff. Apaloo for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. In this suit issued on October 20, 1955, the plaintiff claimed a declaration of title to a piece of land situate on Ring Road, Accra; recovery of possession of the said land, mesne profits, and an injunction.

The land is fully described in the writ of summons. The statement of claim, filed on November 11, 1955, contains four paragraphs: in paragraph 2 the plaintiff pleaded that his title to the land was based upon three deeds of conveyance, dated April 26, 1944, May 3, 1944, and March 29, 1945, executed in his favour, as to the first, by. one Halm Owoo, and as to the second and third, by one J. Kofi Parry. On February 15, 1956, the plaintiff filed an amendment to his state­ment of claim wherein he pleaded that his vendors, the said Halm Owoo and J. Kofi Parry, derived their title from the I{orle Webii. In paragraph 3 he averred that the defendant had erected buildings on the said land in spite of being warned of the pHiintiff's title to it.

By his statement of defence filed on November 16, 1955, as amended on November 30, 1955, the defendant pleaded that he entered upon the land under a grant by the Atukpai family, and that he was in negotiations with the Rorle Webii .to perfect his ownership. In the summons for directions, filed on November 22, 1955, the plaintiff asked for an order that the only issue to be tried was: "That the vendor [I think he means each of his vendors] derives his title from the Rorle \Vebii, the proper owners of the land in question."

Upon an order made by the Land Court, on November 30, 1955, in consequence of an amendment to the statement of defence, the plain­tiff, on January 31, 1956, filed a fresh summons for directions setting down the following as the only issue for trial, namely: "That at what time defendant bought his land from the said Korle Webii since the plaintiff and the defendant derive their title from the same vendors." In short the issue raised on the pleadings is whether the plaintiff acquired title to the land under the deeds pleaded.

Hearing of the case commenced on October 11, 1956. Counsel for the plaintiff opened the case in the following words:

" We have documents showing our title to the land; also show documents of our vendor; covered by Golightly v. Ashrifi (1); hold defendant's vendor no right; also pleads land granted to him for farms."

The evidence given by the plaintiff at the trial was very short. It is as follows, leaving out the formal parts:

" I bought land plots on Ring Road. I produce my conveyances -April 26, 1944, May 3, 1944, and March 29, 1945-Conveyances by Halm Owoo and John Parry. My vendors gave me their deeds. Grants by Korle Webii. I know defendant. I warned him to keep off land. He did not."

Cross-examined:

" Korle We the owners? Yes.

You rely on their grant alone? Yes.

Case about land" Kokomlemle Cases "? Yes.

Your land is in that area involved in that case? Yes.

Some of land belong to Kotey family?

Not near me. Did Ga Manche concur in grant? I don't know.

Ask him to? No.

Gbese Manche? He said it was alright. Didn't sign? No."

That was all the evidence the plaintiff himself gave. The case had to be adjourned that day to October 18, 1956, at the plaintiff's request, on the grounds that his witness, the Korle Priest, was not in court. On October 18, 1956, the case was again adjourned at ~he instance of the plaintiff, as he said he was not ready to proceed. It was adjourned to November 1, 1956.

When the hearing was resumed on November 1, the plaintiff called three witnesses, namely, his vendors Kofi Parry and Halm Owoo, and Numo Ayiety Cobblah, the Korle Priest. Each of the vendors, Kofi Parry and Halm Owoo, tendered a deed dated October 31, 1956, purporting to have been made by one Nii Jonathan Ayitey-Adjin, Acting Gbese Mantse.

The grants evidenced by these documents were never pleaded; in \ fact they could not have been pleaded before October 31, 1956, because they were not then in existence. And when they came into existence the statement of claim was never amended in order to plead them. Moreover, the plaintiff in his evidence had said that he relied solely on the grant by Korle Webii for his title. Under Order 19, r. 4, of Supreme Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 1954, a party must plead all material facts upon which he relies to sustain his case, and ought not

to be allowed at the trial to lead evidence in proof of matters not pleaded: see the case of Philipps v. Philipps (2).

Again there was no proof of the execution of either of those two documents. The said deeds purport to have been made by Nii Jonathan Ayitey-Adjin, but at the foot of each there appears " (sgd) Ayitey­Adjin," which seems to indicate that they are not originals.

The fact that these documents were admitted without objection is, (1: in my opinion, immaterial. Each of these two documents purports to ff convey stool land in the municipality of Accra within the Ga State for valuable consideration. Therefore each of them requires the consent, first of the Ga State Council, as provided by the State Gouncils (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 16 (1), and secondly of the Accra Municipal Council as provided by the Municipal Councils Ordinance,

s. 73 (1). On the face of each of the said documents it is clear

that they do not comply with the said statutory requirements. Each of the documents is therefore null and void and of no effect.

Upon the assumption that those documents are valid, Mr. Asafu­Adjaye for the appellant submitted that they confirm the title of the plaintiff's vendors, Kofi Parry and Halm Owoo. I do not think this argument is maintainable. Those two men, Kofi Parry and Halm Owoo, had, by the deeds of conveyance pleaded, parted with the whole of their right, title and interest, if any, in the land to the plaintiff as long ago as 1944 and 1945. What title had they on October 31, 1956, which can be confirmed? N one whatsoever.

Learned counsel also submitted that the confirmations given relate to 1944 and 1945 to give effect to the conveyances made by ~he Acting Korle Priest to Halm Owoo and Kofi Parry. In my opinion, judging from the language of the recitals, those documents were not intended to have retrospective effect. But should I be wrong in that opinion I would say that the effect of the documents would have to be determined in accordance with principles of native custom applicable to the Kokomlemle lands as laid down and the facts found in the judgment in the case of Golightly and Another v. Ashrifi and Others (1) (popularly known as the Kokomlemle Consolidated Cases) a case upon which the appellant much relied.

The material decisions in that case affecting this case are:

(1) That outright alienation of the Kokomlemle lands cannot be effected except by the prior consent of the Ga and Gbese Stools and Korle We;

(2) That the Kotey family, by ancient grant, are entitled to a defined portion of the Kokomlemle lands; and,

(3) That the three controlling powers, the Ga and Gbese Stools and the Korle We, cannot alienate any portion of the land without obtaining the consent and concurrence of individuals or families, being subjects of the Gbese Stool, who are in occupation, or of strangers who have properly been granted some interest, be it farming or occupation interest in the land.

The land in dispute in this case is proved to be within the area adjudged to belong to the Kotey family. Applying these principles and facts, I have arrived at the following conclusions: (a) \Vith or without the confir~ation of the Gbese Stool the alienations of the land by the Korle Priest are not in conformity with the first principle and are therefore void in that the deeds show alienations of the absolute ownership without the prior consent and concurrence of the Ga Stool, and such consent has never been obtained. (b) Even if the alienations of the plaintiff's grantors were with the prior consent of all three controlling authorities, the Ga and Gbese Stools and Korle We, they were without the consent and concurrence of the Kotey family, who are adjudged to be in possession thereof by an ancient grant, and they are therefore of no effect.

Thus in either case those documents vest no .title in the grantees named therein, i.e., Kofi Parry and Halm Owoo, and consequently the plaintiff acquired no title under those deeds of conveyance executed in his favour by the said Kofi Parry and the said Halm Owoo.

Counsel further referred the court to the finding in the said Kokom­lemle Consolidated Cases that the land adjudged to belong to the Kotey family cannot be alienated by transfer of right, meaning usufructuary right, without the prior consent of the Gbese Mantse, or alienated by transfer of ownership without the consent of both the Ga Mantse and Gbese Mantse, and submitted that since the Kotey family had alienated the land in dispute to the defendant, they had forfeited their right to the land and the plaintiff therefore acquired a good title, at least possessory title, through the grant made to his vendors by ~he Korle Priest alone.

The first answer to that submission is contained in a passage appearing in a judgment which was tendered in this case delivered by Jackson J., the same judge who tried the Ko/wmlemle Consolidated Cases (1), in an action instituted by one of the families to whom, also, according to his judgment in the said Kokomlemle Consolidated Cases, an ancient grant had been made for farming purposes, similar to the grant to the Kotey family. The passage reads as follows:

"These interests were discussed by me at length (i.e., in the Ko/wmlemle Cases) and having found that the land in question had originally been acquired by the family for farming purposes, I found that they could not be dispossessed of those rights other than by their free consent so long as their conduct towards the Gbese Stool conformed with the good standards referred by native customary law. In this action the Gbese Stool is not party and makes no complaint. The land had thus acquired the character of family land and which the Head of the Family with the con­currence of its members is entitled to occupy as family land and which right included all the incidents of living whether by resi­dence on the land by members of the family or by leases of ~he land to strangers, i.e., so long as they do not alienate the land from the Stool of which they are subjects. "

Native custom does what is reasonable. Where a man, entitled to farm and occupy land, but unable for some reason or the other to farm it or build on it himself, permits a stranger to farm or build on it on terms that he, the owner, also should enjoy part of the proceeds or mesne profits, i.e., where a relationship similar to that of landlord and tenant is created, the grant of that interest in the land is not regarded by native custom as an alienation of the possession or usufructuary title in the land to the detriment of the stool. In this case there is no evidence of an alienation of the land in any shape or form by the Kotey family to render them liable to forfeit their ancient grant. All the head of the Kotey family said is: "We agreed he should stay on that piece of land."

Secondly, forfeiture according to native custom is not an automatic consequence which must of necessity attend a breach of a condition of grant. Native custom, quite apart from the principles of equity, which are applicable to this country, abhors greed and ill-gotten gain. Therefore, where, with full knowledge of a breach of condition of a grant made in accordance with native custom, a grantor sits by and allows a third party to occupy the land openly in the belief that he has acquired good title, and to improve it, native custom will not look favourably upon a claim to forfeiture; it will refuse it.

This principle of estoppel in native customary law as a bar to a claim of ownership is often illustrated in certain proverbial or figurative expressions in the various vernaculars, often in the form of a question. I shall refer to one such expression, a question which in Ga folk lore the chief and his elders put to Mr. Spider, who, after he had sat upon a tree on his land and watched Mr. Squirrel make farms on the land, quickly jumped down as soon as the crops were ready for harvest­ing and claimed ownership of the farms. The chief and the elders asked him: " 'rsono asoo ni anaa nmo? "-meaning literally: "Can a person just sit on a tree on his land and then become the owner of farms made by others on that land?" In other words: "you would be making a big mistake to think that in these circumstances you can become the owner of the farm."

As the learned trial judge pointed out, the Korle Priest, through whom the plaintiff claims title to this land, was not prepared to contest the title of the I\otey family. I find that the real owners never objected to any alienation by the Kotey family of their usufructuary title to the land. In my opinion the finding of the learned trial judge that there has been acquiescence on the part of the ultimate owners in the use of the land for building purposes is well founded.

The principles to be applied to forfeiture in native custom are the same as those governing a conveyance of stool or family land by the occupant of the stool or the head of the family without the necessary consent. Such conveyances are only voidable, not void; they may only be set aside when steps are taken timeously to avoid them. In the same way forfeiture will not necessarily follow a breach of a condition of the grant, it may only be enforced when steps are taken without delay.

The submission of learned counsel based upon the passage quoted from the judgment of Jackson J., approved of by the West African Court of Appeal in the Kokomlemle Consolidated Cases, "that by native custom the owner of the usufructuary title cannot transfer that title without the previous consent and concurrence of the absolute owner" requires qualification. What the native custom guards against is alienation to the prejudice of the absolute owner, that is to say, alienation which determines the recognition of the title of .the absolute owner and of the customary services due to him.

Every subject of a stool is entitled to occupy a portion of the stool land without actual permission. Such occupation or enjoyment of the usufruct imports a recognition of the title of the stool as owner. Whether the subject obtained the usufruct in stool land by original occupation, or by gift, sale, or other form of transfer from another subject or grantee of the stool, his title will be good even though his original entry or the transfer to him was without previous consent and concurrence of the stool. It would be ridiculous to say that the sub­ject can acquire a good title when he enters upon the land without express permission of the stool but cannot obtain a good title from another who has also entered under similar conditions. How much more when the subject obtains the usufruct from another subject who entered with express prior consent of the stool.

 

It may be argued that when a subject obtains the express consent of the stool to occupy stool land, the stool can attach conditions to such occupation, and one of such conditions may be a prohibition against alienation of the usufructuary title without the previous consent and concurrence of the stool. In my opinion such a condition will be void and unforceable since it will be violation of the subject's inherent right to occupy stool land without any burden except the recognition of the title of the stool which carries with it certain customary services.

The occupation without express permission is deemed by native custom to be with implied permission. Therefore the occupation with express permission cannot carry with it a greater burden than that which native custom imposes upon occupation with implied permission only.

The subject's occupation of stool land with prior expressed permis­sion of the stool does not create a contractual relationship between the stool and the subject analogous to .. abusa" or .. abunu " tenancy. It is nothing more than a positive conferment upon him of his inherent customary rights by native custom in the stool land.

That express permission cannot therefore limit or curtail the rights or incidents which by native custom are attached to the subject's occupation of the land.

Coussey J., as he then was, delivering the judgment, unreported, of the West African Court of Appeal in Civil Appeal No. 117/49 on January 15, 1952, stated the position as follows:

.. In English law there is a clear division between property and possession, but a very usual form of native title is that of a usufructuary right which is a qualification of or burden on the final title of the owner of the land. " The plaintiff had made farms and had an interest in the land which was transferable so long as he recognised the title of the owner."

In my opinion the correct statement of the native custom is that a usufructuary title can be transferred without the consent of the real owner provided the transfer carries with it an obligation upon the transferee to recognise the title of the real owner and all the incidents of the subject's right of occupation, including the performance of customary services to the real owner.

A judgment of van Lare J., as he then was, was cited to the trial judge in this case and a certified copy of it was tendered in evidence. That judgment was given in Adumua-Bossman v. Bannerman (3), and the subject-matter was a portion of the Kotey family land. In that judgment the learned judge held that the plaintiff, who was a grantee of the Kotey family, was entitled to a declaration of his title .to the usufruct in the land.

There is no doubt that the trial judge in this case was influenced by the said judgment. That judgment was subsequently set aside by the West African Court of Appeal.

Learned counsel submitted that since that judgment has been set aside, the judgment of the Land Court in the present case based upon

it should also be set aside. The ratio decidendi in the judgment of the West African Court of Appeal in that case was the well-known I principle that possession by a defendant will prevail against the whole world except the true owner. The same principle must be applied to this case. There is no evidence that 'the plaintiff has ever been in  possession. In paragraph 3 of his statement of claim the plaintiff averred that the defendant is in possession. Thus no matter how defective the title of the defendant is, his possession is good against all but the true owner.

The plaintiff by his writ set up a claim of ownership which should entitle him to immediate possession as against the defendant. He has not been able to establish that title, it appears he laboured hard till the last moment in an attempt to acquire one, but failed miserably. On the evidence no court of law could have given judgment for him.

In the circumstances I would dismiss the appeal.

GRANVILLE SHARP J.A. I agree.

SMITH J. I also agree.

Appeal dismissed.
