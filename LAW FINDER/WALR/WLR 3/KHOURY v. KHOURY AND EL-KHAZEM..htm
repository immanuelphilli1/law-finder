                                                                               July 22,1957

                                                   KHOURY v. KHOURY AND EL-KHAZEM.

                                          High Court, Central Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                                       Windsor-Aubrey J

 

 

Divorce-Conflict of laws-Foreign decree of judicial separation-Decree given in country of domicile-Fresh petition in country of residence-Whether courts will recognise foreign decree of judicial separation and refuse jurisdiction.

        The petitioner sought a decree of judicial separation, claiming residence in Ghana as the basis of the court's jurisdiction. She had already obtained a decree of judicial separation in the courts of the Lebanon, the country of domicile of the parties, although an appeal by the respondent against this decree was pending before the Sacra Romana Court of Rome. For the petitioner it was argued that the decree of the Lebanese courts was not a final one and that therefore the courts of Ghana, as the country of residence, had jurisdiction to entertain the petition. An expert in Lebanese matrimonial law gave evidence proving, as a fact, that the decree of the Lebanese courts finally disposed of the issue before those courts.

       Held, English law recognises the validity of a decree of judicial separation pronounced by the courts of the country of domicile of the parties and therefore the courts of Ghana have no jurisdiction to enter­tain a fresh petition for the same relief

EDITORIAL NOTE. English law accepts residence (in addition to domicile) as conferring jurisdiction where petitions for judicial separation are con­cerned. This was the basis of the preliminary ruling given in this case on March 25, 1957, when Matalon v. Matalon [1952] 1 All E.R. 1052 was cited. Residence in this context means residence of both parties, although there is a presumption that an unseparated wife is resident with her husband.]

PETITION for a decree of judicial separation and for maintenance.

The parties were domiciled in the Lebanon and the petition was founded upon the residence of the parties in Ghana.

Sowah for the petitioner.

Akufo-Addo for the respondent.

WINDSOR-AUBREY J. In this petition for judicial separation the respondent's counsel has already submitted that this court has no jurisdiction to hear this petition, but by an order dated March 25, 1957, the court held that it had jurisdiction to entertain the petition. In that order the court recognised that proof of certain matters of law, raised by the respondent in paragraphs 5 to 9 inclusive of his answer, might operate as a successful defence and, subsequently, on June 12, 1957, both counsel agreed the arguments should be heard on the issues of law raised by the respondent, and that the hearing of the case on its factual merits be deferred for the time being. The arguments were heard on July 1, 1957, and respondent's counsel was given permission to call an expert on the matrimonial laws of the Lebanon. He duly called Abdallah Maroun, a priest of the Maronite Roman Catholic Church, and I have no hesitation in accepting him as a veracious and knowledgeable witness and worthy of credit, although the precise effect of his evidence is not altogether easy to determine.

The points raised by counsel for the respondent are that this court should not proceed with the hearing of the petition until the conclusion of nullity proceedings in 'the Lebanon which are now on appeal to the Sacra Romana Court at Rome, and that the court is debarred from hearing the petition because a valid decree of judicial separation has already been pronounced in the Lebanon and accordingly that the petition should be dismissed.

In the first place it is necessary to consider the evidence of Abdallah Maroun. He has produced a copy of the judgment of the Maronite Patriarchal Appeal Tribunal and a translation of the appeal therefrom. 1 must confess that on the hearing of the legal arguments before me I had not appreciated the true significance of these judgments or their relation to the annulment proceedings instituted by the respondent and rejected by the lower courts but which are now on appeal to the Sacra Romana Court. Having carefully studied the judgments it seems clear to me that although the question of the respondent's claim for annul­ment of the marriage was considered in the appeal judgment, the earlier proceedings were instituted by the present petitioner in order to obtain a decree of judicial separation. The first proceedings are headed " Separation and Alimony": in the appellate judgment the court, in the penultimate paragraph of page 1, remarks as follows: "She (the petitioner) replied by an action for separation and maintenance." It is correct to say that the appellate court also considered at great length the respondent's plea that the marriage was a nullity and decided on thait issue against the respondent, but it confirmed the lower court's judgment granting judicial separation, subject to a certain modification which I shall consider in the next paragraph of this judgment.

I now turn again to Abdallah Maroun's evidence. He said in his evidence-in-chief that the legal effect of the first judgment is to bar the parties coming back together, even if they were willing, until the conclusion of the annulment proceedings. In cross-examination he was referred to the appeal judgment, which confirmed the judicial separa­tion granted by the lower court subject to the qualification" until the husband shall consent to cohabit with his wife." Concerning this passage and his evidence-in-chief he stated that the first judgment prevented the parties coming together without the consent of the court, and that the latter judgment modified the first judgment by permitting the parties coming together again if they so desired.

To my mind the effect of Maroun's evidence is that in his view a valid decree of judicial separation exists until the conclusion of the annulment proceedings now on appeal. I am satisfied also that the evidence means that these two judgments might or might not be reversed or varied should the respondent's appeal on the annulment proceedings be allowed.

Now the petitioner's counsel argues that because the final decision on the annulment appeal might result in a reversal or variation of the two judgments, these judgments are interlocutory and do not bar pro­ceedings for judicial separation in the Ghana courts. To this the respondent's counsel replies 'that in English law a decree of judicial separation is always recognised as a final judgment although it creates no finality, not affecting the status of the parties, who remain married and are so prohibited from contracting fresh marriages. Furthermore, the parties may come together again if they wish to, and either party, if grounds arise, may subsequently petition the courts for divorce which, if granted, automatically puts an end to the former judicial separation.

I am attracted by the argument of the respondent's counsel and it seems to me that Cheshire on International Law, pp. 615 and 616, supports the respondent's argument. The test as to whether or not a judgment is final is whether the court pronouncing it has power to vary its judgment, in other words, is it provisional only? U is not seriously suggested that either court can vary their judgments already pro­nounced-certainly not so far as. judicial separation is concerned. Neither the fact that a judgment may be reversed on appeal nor the stronger fact, as exists here, that an appeal is pending, affects the finality in law of the judgments of the pronouncing courts. I am satisfied, therefore, that the Lebanese courts have delivered final judgments.

Now what is 'the effect of a foreign judgment in a matrimonial cause? On this aspect of the matters before me counsel for the respon­dent has referred to Cheshire on International Law (4th ed.), at pp. 367 and 379. From this authority there appears to be no doubt whatsoever that an English court recognises any decree of a foreign country in a matrimonial cause where the parties thereto were domiciled in such foreign country at the time of 'the proceedings. Here there is no dispute that both parties were domiciled in the Lebanon at the material time. Accordingly, this court must recognise that decree of judicial separa­tion and cannot hear a fresh petition in Ghana for the same relief. I have, therefore, no option but to dismiss the petition. It follows, therefore, that my former order for maintenance pendente lite must be and is hereby discharged.

S. G. D.
