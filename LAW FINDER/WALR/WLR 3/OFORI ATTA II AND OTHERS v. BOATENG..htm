                                         OFORI ATTA II AND OTHERS v. BOATENG.

(High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

Ollennu J.

 

Native Law and Custom-Stool property-Representation--Person' suing or sued on behalf of stool-Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, c. 98 of 1951, s. 22.

In this action for the recovery of land the first and second plaintiffs were respectively a paramount and a divisional chief, suing on behalf of their stools. In the trial Native Court these stools were represented by their linguists but, the trial Native Court, strictly applying the pro­visions of the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 22, refused to accept this representation save upon written authority given by the respective chiefs.

The trial Native Court rejected the plaintiffs' claim on the ground that they were estopped per rem judicatem from making it.

Held, (i) by native custom there is a class of persons who, together with the occupant of the stool, are its natural representatives. This class includes the linguist and other office holders and, save in exceptional circumstances, the person representing the stool in litigation will be drawn from it. If' a person, claiming to represent a stool, establishes that he is a holder of an office within the class, this will be sufficient for the purpose of the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 22 (and the corresponding sections of equivalent ordinances), to establish his authority to represent the stool. It is undesirable that a Native Court should insist upon an express authority given by the stool occupant since this might result in persons other than the natural representatives representing the stool.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. The Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 22, confers a limited discretion upon Native Courts in the matter of permitting representation, see, e.g., Yeboah v. Bonko (1956) 2 W.A.L.R. 107. One effect of this decision would appear to be to restrict the application of this discretion by providing, in effect, that it will not be exercised where the person coming forward as representative of a stool is one of the class of " natural representa­tives" of the stool. An alternative approach to the matter, and one that would leave the discretion unfettered, would be to hold that representation where communal interests are involved, as, for example, in issues concerned with stool or family property, is entirely beyond the ambit of section 22. It could be argued, on reading the section as a whole, that it is concerned with providing a means (in courts from which legal practitioners are excluded) of assisting litigants suffering, for example, from a disability such as illiteracy. This would appear to be a matter which is a proper concern for a court but which is quite distinct from the problem created by the necessity for having an authorised person to speak where communal interests are involved. Representation here would seem to be a matter of concern more to those with interests in the property than to the Native Court and the question of authority to represent could thus be settled by the application of the normal rules as to the burden of adducing evidence-see Keelson v. Mensah (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 271 and Chapman v. Ocloo and Kporhanu (1957) 3 W.A.L.R. 84.

(ii) 'fhe prior proceedings regarded by the Native Court as raising an estoppel by res  judicata were in fact res inter alios acta, the defendant in the prior proceedings being sued jn the capacity of stool occupant and in the present proceedings as a private citizen.

APPEAL from a decision of the Akyem Abuakwa Native Court" A •• on November 20, 1956, in favour of the defendant in an action to recover land.

Dua Sakyi for the plaintiffs.

 Lassey for the defendant.

OLLENNU J. The first plaintiff is the Omanhene (Paramount Chief) of Akyem Abuakwa, the second plaintiff is the Ohene (Divisional Chief) of Asafo Akyem. The defendant is an ex-Ohene (Divisional Chief) of the said Asafo Akyem. The subject-matter of the suit is a cocoa farm on a portion of the land of the said Asafo Akyem Stool.

In 1953 the third plaintiff instituted an action, suit No. 97/53. in the Akyem Abuakwa Native Court "A" against the defendant in respect of this farm. At that date the defendant was the occupant of the Akyem Asafo Stool and the action was against him, qua chief, that is to say, against the Stool of Asafo Akyem, represented, according to native custom, by the then occupant, who happened to be the defendant in the present case.

In the present suit the Stool of Asafo Akyem is one of the plaintiffs, and the defendant has been sued in his private or personal capacity. The defendant pleaded that the matter was res judicat,a by reason of the proceedings and judgment in suit No. 97/53. The trial Native Court upheld that plea, and dismissed the suit. From that ruling the plaintiffs have appealed to this court.

Mr. Lassey, for the defendant, when called upon to support the ruling properly submitted that he could not do so. Evidently the Akyem Abuakwa Native Court "A" found themselves unable to appreciate the difference between two personalities vested in one man. They seemed unable to understand that the defendant in suit No. 97/53 was the "Stool of Asafo Akyem" and not the private gentleman Kwame Boateng who then represented the stool; whereas in the present suit the Stool of Asafo Akyem is one of the plaintiffs and the defendant is Kwame Boateng, a private individual who is quite a different person from Badour Kwame Boateng, Ohene of Asafo Akyem.

In the majority of cases I have come across from Native Courts involving res judicata it has appeared that the principles of res judicata have often proved too technical for them, and have been obstacles over which 'they have often stumbled. It seems they are not to blame. Perhaps it may be advisable to relieve the Native Courts of the burden of such technicalities with which they are not quite familiar.

One small but very important point in this case also calls for comment. It is 'the interpretation which the Native Court seemed to place upon the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 22. The Native Court appeared under this section to insist upon authority being given in writing by Nana Ofori Atta, Okyenhene, and Barima Osei Agyeman, Ohene of Asafo Akyem, to their respective linguists, to represent them in the suit. Now a stool institutes or defends a suit in the name of the occupant. By native custom there is a class of persons who are" natural representatives" of a stool, apart from the occupant of the stool himself. Th'e linguist is foremost among such "natural representatives." Therefore, where a stool is a party to a suit, all that a Native Court requires to permit a person, o'ther than the chief, to represent the stool, is satisfaction that that person is a recognised linguist or holder of any of the offices of the stool bringing him within the class of " natural representatives" of the stool.

If a Native Court ignores this important principle of native custom and insists upon a strict interpretation of section 22 of the Ordinance, that is, insists upon au'thority being given personally by the chief suing or sued on behalf of the stool, cases may arise where the chief suing or sued may authorise a person of his choice, within the class of persons named in section 22, but not within the class of " natural representa­tives," to represent him in the suit. The Native Court's acceptance of such representation will be contrary to native custom and the whole proceedings may prove to be irregular, as happened in the case of Ofuniahu v. Tabri Yaw and Sie Kwaku (1), where the matter is fully discussed.

In my opinion the basis of representation laid down in section 22 of the Ordinance, and in the other Native Courts Ordinances, applies only to a party appearing in a suit in his personal capacity and not in his capacity as chief or head of family.

As pointed out above, the Native Court misdirected themselves in ruling that the matter in dispute was res judicata; that ruling cannot stand. The appeal is therefore allowed, the ruling of the Native Court set aside, and the case is remitted to the Native Court constituted by a different panel for trial on the merits.

Appeal allowed. Order for trial de novo.

S. G. D.
