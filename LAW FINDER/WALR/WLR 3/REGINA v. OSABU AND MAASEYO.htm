                                                        November 8, 1957.

                                        REGINA v. OSABU AND MAASEYO

                                                                               Court of Appeal

                                  van Lare Ag.C.J., Granville Sharp J.A., Adumua-Bossman J

 

 

Criminal Law-Evidence-Practice-Submission of "no case "-No evidence to go to jury-Submission wrongly rejected-Nature of "all the evidence in the case" before appellate court-Court of Appeal Ordinance, 1957, s. 10 (1).

The appellants, who had been jointly indicted and tried, were con­victed of murder. The evidence of the prosecution was circumstantial only and, save for cross-incriminatory voluntary statements made by the accused, was insubstantial. Moreover, as far as the second accused was concerned, the prosecution had adopted her voluntary statement which provided answers that substantially destroyed any case there might have

. been against her. Nevertheless a plea of "no case" put forward on behalf of the second accused was rejected and she was convicted, appar­ently, on the strength of the subsequent evidence of her co-accused.

In the case of the first appellant there was no submission of "no case," for the prosecution had, apparently, established a prima facie case. However, the rejection of the submission of "no case" in the case of the second accused resulted in her giving evidence and adopting her voluntary statement, which thereby seriously incriminated the first accused.

Held, (i) a distinction should be drawn between the situation where, on a submission of "no case," there is some evidence which could go to the jury and, the situation where there is no evidence at that stage to go to the jury. In the former case, if there is a subsequent conviction, the appellate court is entitled to look at all the evidence in the case. This, even if the submission was wrongly rejected, will include all the evidence subsequently admitted so that, notwithstanding the wrongful rejection, the appellate court may exercise the power conferred upon it by the Court of Appeal Ordinance, 1957, s. 10 (1), which empowers the dismissal of an appeal which would otherwise be successful if in fact no substantial miscarriage of justice has occurred. In the latter case, however, where there was no evidence that could have gone to the jury at the time of the submission, the wrongful rejection of the submission amounts to a wrong decision on a point of law. This being so the evidence put in subsequently was not part of "all the evidence in the case," and the appellate court was thus effectively precluded from con­sidering it and hence from considering whether it was a fit case for the proviso in section 10 of the Ordinance to be applied.

R. v. Abbott [1955] 2 Q.B. 497; [1955] 2 All E.R. 899 applied.

R. v. Akinpelu Ajani (1936) 3 W.A.C.A. 3, and Eregie v. Inspedor­General of Police (1954) 14 W.A.C.A. 453 distinguished.

(ii) In the case of the first accused, although a prima facie case had been made out, the accused was gravely incriminated by the wrongful admission of the evidence of the second accused and by the directions given to the jury on this evidence and, as it was not possible to speculate on the effect on the jury of this evidence and misdirections, it would be unsafe to allow the conviction to stand.

(iii) In situations such as the present it is desirable to have the prosecution decide, before preferring charges, as to which of the persons suspected to be involved should be prosecuted and then to call those not so charged as witnesses.

Cases referred to:

(1) R. v. Abbott [1955] 2 Q.B. 497; [1955] 3 W.L.R. 369; 39 Cr.App.R. 141; [1955] 2 All E.R. 899.

(2) Eregie v. InspectoT-General of Police (1954) 14 W.A.C.A. 453.

(3) R. v. Akinpelu Ajani and Others (1936) 3 W.A.C.A. 3.

(4) R. v. Power [1919] 1 K.B. 572; 14 Cr.App.R. 17; 35 T.L.R. 283; 26 Cox 399.

(5) R. v. Cohen and Bateman (1909) 73 J.P. 352; 2 Cr.App.R. 197.

(6) Danso and Whenton v. The King (1950) 13 W.A.C.A. 16.

APPEAL from convictions for murder by Windsor-Aubrey J. on August 6, 1957, at Accra Assizes.

Amissah, Crown counsel, for the Crown.

Quarshie Sam for the first appellant.

Koranteng for the second appellant.

ADUMUA-BosSMAN J., who delivered the judgment of the court, dealt first with the evidence, and continued:

We have been at some pains to set out the evidence of the principal prosecution witnesses in some detail as above, not only to facilitate easier reference thereto but also to make it the more easily discernible that, except by reference to the statements of the appellants, the prosecution had not and were unable to put before the court any evidence as to how or by whom the death of the deceased had been caused.

It was in this state of things that learned counsel for the female appellant, after the close of the case for the prosecution, submitted that the evidence adduced was not sufficient to establish a prima facie case against her.

Counsel for the prosecution resisted the application that the case of the female appellant should be withdrawn from the jury at that stage and argued as follows:

" There is circumstantial evidence calling for an answer ... the second accused purchased a large quantity of kerosene-I refer to her first statement. Admits being present when deceased was killed. I heard noise' Gbu.' I also rely on sister's evidence. Her statement shows motive-husband going to leave. She told three inconsistent stories."

Upon a careful examination and consideration of the arguments of learned counsel for the prosecution, we find it difficult to appreciate or understand how the female appellant's presence at the killing in the circumstances described by her in her statement, together with the evidence of her telling lies about the movements of the deceased and of her purchasing a relatively large quantity of kerosene, can be said to lead to the irresistible and only possible conclusion that the said female appellant either killed or aided and abetted the killing of the deceased. Indeed, it is our opinion that the adoption by the prosecution of her statement as part and parcel of their case, substantially destroyed even the suspicion of her having in any way aided and abetted the killing, as the statement explained the circumstances of her presence at the killing, and the reason for her telling lies about the movements of her husband when she knew he had been killed by the male appellant, her nephew, as well as the reason for and the circumstances how she came to purchase the kerosene.

The learned judge, however, upheld the contention of counsel for the prosecution and ruled as follows:

" There is very substantial circumstantial evidence implicating the second accused which calls for an explanation and answer. In my opinion a prima facie case has been made out."

Thereupon her counsel put her in the box and she gave evidence on oath adopting or confirming her statement to the police as her defence, the male appellant having previously likewise on oath adopted his state­ments to the police with certain variations as his defence, and the respective cases of the two appellants concluded on the assumed basis that the statement of one was legally and properly admissible evidence against the other.

In this connection reference might be made to the following significant passages in the summing-up of the learned judge:

" Statements to police having been adopted are now admissible against both accused. On Crown's case both are accomplices and where, as here, both are self exculpatory but inculpatory of the other, the evidence should be viewed with the greatest caution and not safe to convict either, unless there is independent cor­roborative evidence-there is considerable corroborative circum­stantial evidence .. ',' Statements fresh in your minds-Be-read close of prosecution case."

It is against this background of the respective statements of the appellants having been assumed to be, or accepted as, admissible evidence against each other, and their respective cases having been put to the jury on that hypothesis-that their respective appeals have to be considered.

Turning now to deal separately with each appellant's appeal, it will be more convenient to deal with the appeal of the female appellant first.

Learned counsel on her behalf has made it his first ground of appeal that

: " The judge of the court of trial wrongfully refused to withdraw the case from the jury at the close of the case for the prosecution on a submission being made to him that there was no evidence (or not sufficient evidence) to be left to the jury."

He has also expressed reliance on the case R. v. Abbott (1), where it was held, in exactly similar circumstances to the present:

" That the appellant was entitled to have his appeal allowed as the judge had come to a wrong decision in point of law in rejecting the submission of no case and in leaving the case to the jury, when there was no evidence against him at the close of the case for the prosecution."

Learned counsel for the prosecution has, however, contended that there was sufficient evidence establishing a prima facie case against the female appellant at the close of the case for the prosecution, and has referred to the same pieces of evidence to which counsel for the prosecution at the trial had referred and on which he relied, and counsel has argued that from those pieces of evidence the inference could be properly drawn that the female appellant either killed or had aided and abetted the killing of the deceased.

As we have already observed earlier in this judgment, when we examined the arguments of learned counsel for the prosecution at the trial in answer to the submission of " no case," it is our opinion that separately or collectively the evidence relied on by the prosecution at the close of the case was capable of perfectly good, credible and reason­able explanations, and had in fact been reasonably explained, by the female appellant in her statement, and above all was as consistent with complete innocence as with guilt, and did not establish an irresistible presumption of guilt and therefore a prima facie case against her.

After a thorough and most anxious and careful examination and consideration of all the evidence properly available to the prosecution against the female appellant at the stage when the prosecution's case closed, and without the statements made by the male appellant to the police against her by which alone she was at all incriminated (which statements the learned judge had himself directed the jury at the time of their admission not to be evidence against the female appellant), we have come to the conclusion that there was not sufficient evidence establishing a prima facie case against the female appellant at the close of the case for the prosecution and that the learned judge erred in overruling the submission of learned counsel on her behalf and in not withdrawing the case from the jury and directing her acquittal and discharge.

In such circumstances what is the legal position?

Learned counsel for the female appellant, as already indicated, relies on R. v. Abbott (1) already referred to and submits that this court has no option but to allow her appeal:

" As the judge had come to a wrong decision in point of law in rejecting the submission of "no case" and in leaving the case to the jury when there was no evidence against her at the close of the case for the prosecution, and that in those circumstances this court is not obliged to take into account the adverse evidence given against her when the case was wrongly left to the jury."

Able counsel for the respondent, however, argued that this court is not bound by Abbott's case, which is an English authority, but by the local authority of Eregie v. Inspector-General of Police (2).

Eregie's case followed the earlier authority of R. v. Akinpelu Ajani (3), which decided that where upon a submission of " no case" made on behalf of an accused at the close of the case for the prosecu­tion the presiding judge wrongly overrules the submission and calls for a defence, then if the accused himself or a co-accused gives evidence therea.fter which may warrant the conviction of the accused and he is convicted, the appellate court will look at the evidence as a whole, including that made available during the case of the defence and will not interfere.

It should be observed, however, that Akinpelu Ajani's case was itself decided upon and purported to follow the English case of R. v. Power (4).

In the course of his judgment in the Akinpdlu Ajani case, Kingdom C.J. (Nigeria) and President of the Court observed as follows:

"The law in England on this point has been open to some doubt and still appears to be in a curious state."

He then went on to review the decision of the English Court of Criminal Appeal on the point ending with Power's case (4), and he referred to and adopted the portion of the judgment of Darling J. in that case, as fo1l0ws :

" The authorities in this court on this point are not in accord.

A statement made by myself when delivering a judgment of the court in R. v. Joiner was not followed by Lord Alverstone C.J. in R. v. Fraser. In that case Lord Alverstone said: Where an objection is taken by counsel unsuccessfully and he then calls evidence this court is not bound to disregard the effect of that evidence.' In R. v. Pearson, where the conviction was quashed, I said: 'Of course if admissions by the defendant had supplied the evidence that was wanting we should not have interfered with the jury's verdict,' and in R. v. George Phillimore J. said: 'As the case proceeded to the end, the Court of Criminal Appeal are of opinion that they ought to look at the whole of the evidence.' It is true that in R. v. Joiner Darling J. said: 'If there was no evidence (of the offence) then it was the duty of the court to withdraw the case from the jury; we have no right to look and see whether any evidence of larceny was elicited after the close of the case for the prosecution, when counsel for the prisoner submitted that there was no evidence to go to the jury.' At present the court is not prepared to follow R. v. Joiner, but prefers the reasoning of the other two cases. This court agrees that Lord Alverstone's statement of the law was accurate, and that the judgment in R. v. Joiner did not put the matter rightly. It is therefore impossible to quash the conviction in this case on the ground that the case for the prosecution disclosed no evidence against the appellant. As a result of the evidence given by the other defendant, there was a case against the appellant."

The learned Chief Justice concluded thus:

" And that is how the law stands today, and it appears to amount to this: that in cases where the prosecution has made out 'no case' against an accused, but in spite of that he is called upon by the court to enter upon his defence instead of being dis­charged ... (c) if at the close of the case for the prosecution he or his counsel made a submission which was wrongly overruled then, if either he or his counsel took any part in the subsequent proceedings, an appeal against a conviction resulting from those proceedings will fail. ... If this state of the law appears to make it difficult for an accused in England, it seems to amount to a positive hardship in this country where so many of the accused are illiterate, where trials of several accused together are frequent and where judges owe a special duty to protect their interests. But it is for the legislature, and not for this court, to effect an altera­tion of the law, and we feel bound to give effect to the law as we found it." Akinpelu Ajani's case (3) was in 1936; and when in February, 1954, Eregie's case (2) came before the West African Court of Appeal it was the same state of the law that Verity C.J. (Nigeria) referred to in the following words, at p. 452:

" Lt is clear that by the old-established rule it was envisaged that a defendant might wrongly be called upon for a defence, but it is, we think, equally clear that at no time was it contemplated that the trial judge would so call upon the defendant, unless it appeared to him, albeit erroneously, that a prima facie case against him had been made out. It is, in our view, this rule neither more or less that has ,been embodied in the provisions of section 286 of the Criminal Procedure Ordinance."

But after both judgments, based as indicated above on Power's case (4), Abbott's case (1), relied on for the female appellant, came before the Court of Criminal Appeal in England (Goddard L.C.J., Finnemore and Devlin JJ.), and Power's case, relied on for the prosecution, was discussed and considered.

In the course of the arguments of counsel for the appellant Devlin J. made the following observation concerning Power's case (4), at p. 501:

"The whole point of the argument in Power's case was whether the court was bound to consider the evidence. There might well be a difference between cases where there is a doubtful point for the judge to decide, and cases where he wrongly allows a case against one prisoner to continue and another prisoner gives evidence against him. It would be wrong for the court to take that evidence into account."

Then Finnemore J. observed, at p. 502, during the course of the arguments of counsel for the prosecution, as follows:

" It is clear from the argument of Mr. Eustace Fulton for the Crown in Power's case that some stolen property was found in the prisoner's possession. The court did not deal with the position where there was no evidence against the prisoner."

Thereafter Devlin J. again made the following observation, at p. 502, to counsel for the prosecution:

" If the court is against you on your submission that the judge was right not to stop the case, then the appellant was convicted on an error in law. If it had not been for that error of law, he would have been entitled to leave the dock."

Goddard L.C.J. supported the new point of view of Devlin J. and referred to R. v. Cohen and Bateman (5) before proceeding in due course to give the judgment of the court, in which the following pertinent observations about Power's case are worthy of note.

" Reliance is placed by the prosecution on the decision of this court in R. v. Power ... , It is said that if a judge wrongly overrules a submission of ' no case' and allows the case to go to the jury and accused persons give evidence and one gives evidence against the other, this court is not bound to say that it will quash the conviction because it may take into account the whole of the evidence. But R. v. Power did not decide that if there was no evidence against a man who was indicted together with another person it was right to allow the case to go to the jury against that man, or that if there was no evidence against him it was other than a wrong decision in law ... , The question, therefore, is whether the case R. v. Power obliges us to take a different view and to say that as the judge, although in our opinion wrongly, allowed the case against the appellant to go to the jury we must uphold the conviction because Mrs. \Vales did give evidence. The case to which I called attention during the course of the argument was R. v. Cohen and Bateman. The judgment of Channell J. in that case has often been treated in this court as a classic judgment on the construction of section 4 (1) of the Criminal Appeal Act, 1907. The judge said: 'Taking section 4 with its proviso, the effect is that if there is a wrong decision of any question of law the appellant has the right to have his appeal allowed, unless the case can be brought within the proviso .... ' The question of the proviso does not arise in this case. Here we have a question whether or not there was a right decision in point of law by the judge. In our opinion the judge ought to have said at the end of the case for the prosecution that there was no evidence against the appellant Abbott and therefore he was wrong in law in giving the decision he did.

It is then said R. v. Power has given a different interpretation to s. 4 of the Criminal Appeal Act. There is no question that in Power's case a submission has been made on behalf of an appellant to the Commissioner at the Central Criminal Court which he over­ruled. Both prisoners went into the witness-box; one prisoner gave evidence against the other and certainly supplied a great deal of evidence against the appellant .... But what the court desires to point out with regard to Power's case is this: the court was not pronouncing upon whether the judge ought to have stopped the case at the end of the case for the prosecution; and it is observable that Mr. Eustace Fulton, arguing for the Crown, submitted that there was some evidence given by witnesses for prosecution ... ' Whether the court was dealing with the case on the footing that there was no evidence against Power we do not know. What was said was that if the case did go to the jury, the evidence given by the prisoners respectively was part of the sum of the evidence in the case, and this court, when asked to quash a conviction, might take the whole of the evidence into account. They did not say that the court must do so but that this court might do so. They certainly did not say that if there was no evidence given against one of two or more prisoners the judge would simply leave the case to the jury to see whether, when the case for the defence was opened, one or other of the prisoners would support the case set up by the prosecution."

In the light of this very recent review and explanation as to the ratio decidendi of Power's case (4) on which Akinpelu Ajani's case (3) was decided in 1936 and Eregie's case (2) in 1954, it seems clear that the latter two cases if not virtually overruled are clearly distinguish­able and Abbott's case (1) is now the correct law and the authority binding on this court. It is significant that the terms of section 4 of the Criminal Appeal Act, 1907, are reproduced in section 10 (1) of our Court of Appeal Ordinance, which provides as follows:

" The Court of Appeal on any such appeal against conviction shall allow the appeal if they think that the verdict should be set aside on the ground that it is unreasonable or cannot be supported having regard to the evidence or that the judgment of the court before whom the appellant was convicted should be set aside on the ground of a wrong decision or any question of law or that on any ground there was a miscarriage of justice, and in any other case shall dismiss the appeal:

Provided that the court may, notwithstanding that they are of opinion that the point raised in the appeal might be decided in favour of the appellant, dismiss the appeal if they consider that no substantial miscarriage of justice has actually occurred."

There being" no case" against the female appellant at the close of the case for the prosecution, and learned counsel's submission to that effect having been wrongly overruled, there has been a wrong decision on a question of law as enunciated in Abbott's case (1), and we would therefore sustain learned counsel's first ground of appeal, allow the appeal of the female appellant, set aside her conviction and enter a verdict of acquittal in her favour and order her to be discharged.

There remains to be considered the appeal of the male appellant, based on the ground that the verdict is unreasonable and cannot be supported, having regard to the evidence. In his case there was no suggestion that at the close of the case for the prosecution there was not sufficient evidence establishing a prima facie case against him, i.e., without the statement of the female appellant which at that stage was inadmissible against him: see Danso and Whenton v. The King (6).

Learned counsel on his behalf made no submission of " no case" for him, as was done for the female appellant, and indeed counsel has admitted before us that there was evidence upon which the jury, upon a proper direction, could have properly found him guilty.

But his case, in our view, was also affected by the unfortunate turn which the trial took, namely, the overruling of the submission of " no case" made on behalf of the female appellant and her wrongful retention in the case which led her to go into the witness-box and on oath adopt her statement to the police and make it evidence against him, by which he became seriously incriminated. Again, on the authority of Abbott's case (1), it appears to us that the adverse evidence given by the female appellant against the male appellant when she was wrongfully in the case must be excluded and disregarded -leaving only the rest of the evidence, including his own statements to the police which his counsel has admitted would have been sufficient to support his conviction. Unfortunately, however, the jury were not properly directed to disregard and exclude the evidence of the female appellant against him, but were instead wrongly directed to consider the same as the evidence of an accomplice which required corroboration.

The learned judge also directed that there was sufficient corrobora­tion. We consider this a serious misdirection, and cannot speculate the effect this misdirection would have had on the minds of the jury so as to be able to say that a substantial miscarriage of justice in the circumstances had not actually occurred within the proviso to section 10 (1) of the Court of Appeal Ordinance, 1957.

On this ground of misdirection, which although not taken by learned counsel for the male appellant the court cannot ignore, we do not consider the conviction of the male appellant also can be supported. In the case of Danso and Whc'nton v. Thc King (6), at pp. 18-19, which is in point, Blackhall P. said as follows.

"There is no doubt in our minds that there was plenty of evidence, if the jury accepted the testimony of the witnesses for the prosecution, to justify a verdict of guilty against Danso, and if they had been properly directed such a verdict would not be open to criticism. But in view of the seriousness of misdirection and its possible effect on the minds of the jury, the majority of the court do not feel on the whole quite satisfied that the case is one to which the proviso should be applied ... and the appeal of the first appellant Danso must therefore be allowed and the conviction quashed."

We feel strongly that the difficulty that has arisen might well have been avoided if the prosecution had made up its mind after full inquiry as to which of the two accused persons should be prosecuted; and the other, though prima facie an accomplice, called to give evidence. Had this been done the learned judge's summing-up would not have been open to criticism.

In the result we allow the appeal of the male appellant also and set aside his conviction, enter a verdict of acquittal, and order his discharge accordingly.

C.onvictions quashed.

S. G. D.
