                                                   June 4, 1958

                                                  BESEMUNA v. DONKOR.

                                     High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

.                                                      Ollennu J.

 

 

Native Law and Custom-Violation of custom-Dispute between chiefs­ Offence against dignity of chief-Whether actio1'lable before Native Court -Whether a cause or matter of a constitutional nature-Native Courts (Southern Togoland) Ordinance, s. 14A.

According to the Ohene of Krachi, the Mankralo of Krachi alleged that the Ohene had violated the Great Oath of the State. The Mankralo con­demned him in his absence, slaughtered two sheep and (in accordance with custom) sent two thighs of the two sheep to the Ohene as a person who had been found guilty of violating the Great Oath; but the Mankralo sent the thighs by mere messengers. By the custom of Krachi this procedure was wrong, and degrading to the Ohene. Even if the Ohene had violated the Great Oath, custom required that chiefs and linguists (persons of his own rank) be sent t<> arrest him as an oath prisoner, or to invite him to a meeting of the chiefs and elders of the state for the matter to be investigated. Until he had been propertly tried and found guilty by such a tribunal, sheep could not be slaughtered under an alleged decision that he had been guilty of violating the Great Oath. The Ohene accordingly issued against the Mankralo a writ in the Buem-Krachi Native Court "A," claiming damages for the Mankralo's violation of the procedure ordained by native custom on bringing such a charge.

In reply to the case set up by the Ohene, the Mankralo did not deny that he had condemned the Ohene, and had performed the custom in the latter's absence He alleged, however, that he had sent to invite the Ohene to attend a meeting of the chiefs and elders of Krachi, but that the Ohene had refused to attend, and that it was upon the Ohene's failure to attend the meeting that the custom of slllughtering sheep was performed.

The Native Court finding for the Ohene, the Mankralo appealed to the Magistrate's Court constituted by the Government Agent, Jasikan, where the only point canvassed was that the Native Court had no jurisdiction to entertain the suit because it concerned a matter of a constitutional nature. The Magistrate held that it did not concern such a matter, and dismissed the appeal. The Mankralo took it second appeal to the High Court, where counsel for the appellant re-argued the same ground of appeal, and (further) submitted that the Native Court's finding of fact was against the weight of evidence.

Held, (i) the writ of summons, and the whole proceedings, revealed a dispute between two individuals, chiefs or not, the one having wronged the other within the field of native law and custom. Therefore, although the parties held constitutional positions, and although the dispute could not have arisen otherwise, the dispute itself was not a cause or matter of a constitutional nature and was thus not beyond the jurisdiction of the Native Court by reason of the Native Courts (Southern Togoland) Ordi­nance, s. 14A.

(ii) The Native Court's findings of fact were amply supported by the evidence of witnesses whom they had seen and heard. Further, as the Native Courts are the repositories of custom, it was not for an appellate c<>urt to upset their findings unless the facts found were not supported by the evidence, or the custom applied was contrary to natural justice, equity or good conscience.

ApPEAL from a decision of the Magistrate's Court constituted by the Government Agent, Jasikan, dismissing an appeal by the defen­dant from the decision of Buem-Krachi Native Court " A" on January 8, 1957, giving judgment for the plaintiff in an action for damages for the defendant's violation of custom when charging the plaintiff with having violated the Great Oath.

Koi Larbi for the plaintiff.

Puplampu for the defendant.

 

OLLENNU J. stated the facts and continued: The Native Court did not believe the evidence led by the defendant and his witnesses ~hat the plaintiff was sent for. It did believe the evidence of the plaintiff and his witness that the messengers were sent merely to inform him (the plaintiff) that he had been found guilty of violating the Great Oath; and his evidence that later, when sheep were slaughtered, two thighs were sent to the plaintiff. The Native Court also held that the two messengers sent to the plaintiff to announce his guilt were not persons of the rank that should be used to send a message to a chief in such circumstances.

Upon these facts learned counsel for the defendant argues that the claim, and the whole of the proceedings, show that a dispute relating to the constitutional and political relatio1Jship between the chief and chiefs in the state has been raised. The issue tried, therefore, was a matter of a constitutional nature as defined in the Native Courts (Southern Togoland) Ordinance, and the Magistrate was wrong in ruling that it was not.

I am unable to accept those submissions. The writ of summons, and the whole proceedings, reveal a dispute between two individuals, be they chiefs or not, the one having wronged the other. No question of the constitutional or political relationship between those two chiefs is raised. I therefore agree with the judgment of the Magistrate that the matter is not one of a constitutional nature, and that the Native Court had jurisdiction to entertain the suit.

Learned counsel has endeavoured with great skill to convince the court that in any event the judgment of the Native Court was against the weight of evidence, and therefore wrong. He failed in his endeavours. As already pointed out, the only point to be considered is whether or not the plaintiff was sent for to attend a meeting of the chiefs and elders of Krachi for the investigation of the complaint against him; and, if so, whether the proper persons were sent accord­ing to his rank. The defendant says that he sent to invite the plaintiff to a meeting, and that he sent the proper persons. The plaintiff, on the contrary, says that when the messengers came he asked them whether they had been sent to arrest him as an oath prisoner or to invite him to a meeting, and that the messengers replied that they had not been sent to do either, but merely to inform him of his guilt. The evidence of each party on this point was supported by his wit­nesses. The Native Court, who saw and heard these witnesses, came to a definite conclusion upon sound reasons that the plaintiff's version was the true one, that he was not sent for or invited to attend a meeting for the matter of the oath to be discussed, and that the persons sent were not of the appropriate rank. That being the case, I cannot interfere with the decision of the Native Court (based upon their evaluation of the evidence) that the plaintiff was not in fact sent for, or invited, to attend the meeting.


     Where an oath has been violated the proper procedure according to native custom is for the person alleged to have done so to be taken as an oath prisoner (or to be invited) to a meeting of the chiefs and leaders of the town or state, as the case may be, for the allegations to be gone into and tried. When the matter has been properly dis­cussed at the meeting, if the prisoner has been found guilty of violat­ing the oath he is then made to slaughter sheep, but not otherwise. The Native Court found that this customary procedure was llot followed, and the action of the defendant amounted in native custom to a wrong against the plaintiff. Those findings of fact are amply supported by the evidence. As to custom, the Native Courts are the repositories thereof, and (as the trial court) they are the judges of fact. This court, as an appellate court, is not entitled to upset their judgment unless the native custom applied is contrary to natural justice, equity and good conscience, or the facts found are not sup­ported by the evidence. None of those factors is present in this case. For these reasons I dismiss the appeal with costs.

Appeal dismissed.
