                                                    December 16, 1958

                                             AHADJl v. SWANlKER AND OTHERS.  

                               High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court

                                                                Ollennu J

 

 

Land--Mortgage-Conditional sale-Unsecured loan made against promise to convey property on failure to repay-Subsequent conveyance in terms of outright alienation-Essential nature of mortgage-Distinction between mortgage and conditional sale.

S., since deceased, sought a loan from the plaintiff who, on January 10, 1956, advanced £267 to him, unsecured and without interest, but against a promise by S. that, if he found himself unable to repay the loan within two months, he would sell and convey to the plaintiff certain house property. In March 1956 S., who had failed to repay the loan, implemented his promise by conveying the property to the plaintiff. The conveyance, on it~ face, disclosed an outright alienation, but S. remained in possession and subsequently, and without the plaintiff's consent, let rooms in the house to the second, third and fourth defendants. S. died in November 1957, and the plaintiff, seeking possession of the premises, advised his family of her interest in the property. This was resisted by the famiJy who, acting through the first defendant, who was the duly appointed successor to S., claimed that, viewed as a whole, the transaction was one by which property was conveyed as security for a loan, rather than an outright sale.

Held, (i) if the true nature of the transaction was that of an advance against the security of house property, the principle "once a mortgage always a mortgage" must apply. This principle does not, however, extend to a transaction which is primarily a sale even although there is a collateral or subsequent agreement for reconveyance on repayment of the purchase price.

(ii) One essential distinction between a mortgage and a conditional sale turns upon mutuality of rights. With a mortgage, the mortgagor has a right to redeem and the mortgagee a right to foreclose, but with a conditional sale, although the vendor has a right to repurchase, the purchaser has no right to foreclose or to insist upon repayment within a specified time.

(iii) Matters that assist in determining whether a transaction is an outright sale or a mortgage are the payment of interest and the fact that the purchase price is less than the actual value of the property.

(iv) On the facts of the present case the true transaction was one of outright sale.

'Tapply v. Sheather, 8 Jur.(N.s.) 1163; 7 L.T. 298 followed.

Cases referred to :

(1) Bunning v. Bunning, 1 L.J.Ch. 56.

(2) Beckett v. Tower Assets Co. [1891] 1 Q.B. 638; 60 L.J.Q.B. 493; 64 L.T. 497; 7 T.L.R. 400.

(3) Allenby v. Dalton, 5 L.J.K.B. 312.

(4) Tapply v. Sheather, 8 Jur.(x.s.) 1163; 7 L.T. 298.

ACTION for a declaration of title to, and recovery of possession of, a house. The plaintiff had lent money to the then owner on his promise to convey the house to her if he could not otherwise repay. The plaintiff sued upon the conveyance which was subsequently made.

Bentsi-Enchill for the plaintiff.

Apaloo for the defendants.

OLLENNU J. referred to the pleadings, and continued: Learned counsel for the defendant submitted that the main issue to be deter­mined in this suit is whether the transaction between the plaintiff and the late Swaniker was a mortgage to provide security, or a sale of the house. He submitted that the evidence taken as a whole shows that the deed was intended to be security on the house for the payment of a loan, and not a bona fide sale and purchase of the house.

I agree with counsel in his view as to the issue to be decided, for if the original transaction is to provide security, the principle" once a mortgage always a mortgage" will apply. On the other hand, if it is a bona fide sale and purchase, a collateral or subsequent arrangement to recover the~ property upon refund of the purchase price will not make the transaction any the less a sale (Bunning v. B1lnning (1); Beckett v. Tower Assets Co. (2)). One essential distinction between a mortgage and a conditional sale is that in the former case there is mutuality of rights-the mortgagor has a right to redeem, and the mortgagee has a right to foreclose-whereas in a conditional sale the vendor has a right to repurchase, whilst the purchaser has no right to foreclose or insist upon repayment of his money within a specified time. The question of defeasible or conditional purchase and mortgage is discussed exhaustively in Cootes on the Law of Mortgages (9th ed., Vol 1, pp. 27 and 34). Among the matters which assist a court to determine whether what appears to be an absolute conveyance is in fact a mortgage are payment of interest (Allenby v. Dalton (3)), and the fact that the purchase price is less than the actual value of the property.

I shall now examine the evidence on the record, in order to ascer­tain whether the transaction between the parties at the date of the execution of the "conveyance" was one of sale, or of providing security for the payment of a loan. I must say at once that the evi­dence given by the first defendant and his only witness (his sister) was inconclusive and evasive. It left me with the impression that either they had completely lost touch with their brother (the late Hansen Peter Swaniker) during the later years of his life, and there­fore know next to nothing about his affairs-in particular about the transaction which led to the execution of the deed-or else that they are deliberately hiding the truth from the court. I have no hesitation in rejecting their evidence. But my rejecting their evidence does not relieve the plaintiff of the onus upon her to prove that the transaction was a sale.

Now to the evidence of the plaintiff. The material piece of evi­dence which she gave as to the transaction between her and the late Hansen Peter Swaniker was as follows. She said, in chief:

" During his lifetime the late Hansen Peter Swaniker used to come to me for financial help whenever he was in need, and I used to give it to him. On one occasion he came to me in the company of his son, Christian Swaniker, and told me that he was in trouble, and wanted me to help him out of that trouble. He asked me to give him money to pay a debt for his son. I told him I could not. He then told me that he would sell his house to me in payment of the amount if he failed to repay me the money. This transaction took place on January 10, 1956. I agreed to the proposal, and gave him the amount he wanted­£267. He promised to refund it within two weeks, but I gave him two months, i.e., up to March 16, to pay the money. I asked him to confirm his statement in writing. He did so by deposing to that fact in an affidavit. He gave me a copy of the affidavit and kept the original. He did not sign the copy which he gave me I handed the £267 to the officer at the Housing

     Department ....... , When the time fixed for payment came,

Mr. Swaniker said he could not afford to pay the money, and he would therefore sell and convey the house to me. I agreed to buy it, and Mr. Swaniker therefore sold it to me."

And under cross-examination she said:

" Yes, in January 1956 he was to have mortgaged the house to me as security for the money."

Christian Swaniker, first witness for the plaintiff, is the eldest son of the late Hansen Peter Swaniker. By Ga custom (i.e, the Ga area excluding the town of Accra), where succession is patrilineal, this witness inherits his father's property, and is one of the beneficiaries of the estate, real and personal, of his said father. I cannot imagine him giving evidence against his interest, if that evidence is not true. His evidence is that as far as the house is concerned, the only transac­tion between his late father and the plaintiff was one of sale and purchase.

Mr. N. C. Addo, an officer of the Department of Housing, to whom the plaintiff handed moneys in January 1956 at his office, and who in turn gave a portion of these moneys to the late Hansen Peter Swaniker, deposed as follows under cross-examination:

"Yes, on January 10, 1957, when the people came to me, they told me the house was being sold to the plaintiff. It was the late Mr. Swaniker himself who told me that he was selling the house to the plaintiff. No, he did not tell me that he was mortgaging it to the plaintiff."

I do not attach any importance to the evidence of Abblorh. As counsel for the defendant puts it, that witness was either telling deliberate lies, or had forgotten what really took place.

From the evidence as a whole I am satisfied that:

(1) the late Hansen Peter Swaniker appealed to the plaintiff in January 1956 for a loan of £300;

(2) to induce the plaintiff to make this loan, the late Mr.

Swaniker offered to convey the house in dispute to the plaintiff at a future date, in repayment of the loan, if he should be unable otherwise to meet the obligation at the date fixed for repayment;

(3) at the request of Mr. Swaniker, the plaintiff paid to the Department of Housing an amount of £94 3s. Sd. which Swaniker owed the Department on the house, to enable him to obtain an unencumbered freehold title to the said house;

(4) on January 10, 1956, the plaintiff made to Swaniker a loan of £267, without interest;

(5) the loan was not secured at that date (January 1956) by any charge, legal Or equitable, on the house in dispute. All that the plaintiff had was an ordinary receipt acknow­ledging the debt, and an unsigned copy of an affidavit which Swaniker had sworn to, setting out his offer to sell the house to the plaintiff at a future date in the event of his becoming unable to repay the loan;

(6) the value of the house in March 1956 was £190;

(7) on March 16, 1956, Swaniker, by the deed exhibited, sold and conveyed the house to the plaintiff, in pursuance of the agreement entered into between the two of them in January 1956, in repayment of the loan which the plaintiff

had made to him, and of the amount she had paid on his behalf to the Department of Housing.

In the case of Tapply v. Sheather (4) a sum of money was lent without security, and an agreement was entered into that if the money so lent, together with further advances, was not repaid by a specified day, a lease of a farm held by the borrower should be assigned to the lender without any further consideration. It was held that that agree­ment constituted the relationship of vendor and purchaser, and not that of mortgagor and mortgagee (see also Cootcs on Mortgages, Vol. 1, 9th ed., at p. 27, and the case there cited).

Guided by this authority I am bound, upon the facts which I have found, to hold that the transaction between the late Mr. Swaniker and the plaintiff, which culminated in the exhibited deed, was one between vendor and purchaser, and not one between mortgagor and mortgagee. This plaintiff has therefore proved that she is the owner of the premises, and entitled to immediate possession of the house in dispute.


Judgment for the plaintiff.
