                                                December 23, 1957.

                                   LOROMEKE v. NEKEGHO AND AYO.

                     High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Divisional Court

                                                          Simpson J. 

                                                 

 

Native custom-Custom contrary to natural justice, equity and good con­science - Custom that would deprive mother of her children- Courts Ordinance, c. 4, s. 87 (1).

Evidence-Proof of custom-Custom of region- outside jurisdiction of Court­Whether necessary to prove b-y expert witness or as a fact.

Evidence-Documents-Authority given by family to a member-Necessity for proving due execution.

The plaintiff had obtained orders in the lower courts requiring the second defendant, who was the widow of his late brother, to pay him £12, being a return of her dowry, and to deliver to him her two children by his brother. From these orders the defendants appealed. The first de­fendant was the second defendant's father. The plaintiff was a Nigerian from Urhobo living in Accra. He led evidence that by a binding Urhobo custom the family of a deceased man select a member of the family to marry the deceased's widow: this custom was admitted by the defendants. By the custom the widow is entitled to refuse the levirate marriage but in this event the family is entitled to demand a repayment from her marriage dowry and the custody of the children. The plaintiff claimed that he had been selected by the family to marry the second defendant; that she had refused him; and, that he had been appointed by his family to receive £12 return of dowry and custody of the children. It was argued for the defendants on the appeal that, inter alia, Urhobo customary law was not a customary law prevailing within the jurisdiction of the trial Native Court and its existence should therefore be proved, as with foreign law, by the testimony of an expert.

Held, (i) the particular Urhobo custom was admitted by the defendants but even if it had not been so admitted, there was nevertheless sufficient evidence before the court to establish its existence-i.e., the custom was proved, like other customs, as a fact rather than as a rule of foreign law.

(ii) Due execution of the document of authority put forward by the plaintiff as issuing from his family was not proved by him. The document was therefore inadmissible and should have been rejected by the trial court.

(Hi) Even if the document of authority had been admissible, never­theless the plaintiff must still fail as regards the order for custody of the children, for a custom that requires a woman to deliver her children to a man she declines to marry must be regarded as " repugnant to natural justice, equity and good conscience" within the meaning of the Courts Ordinance, s. 87.

Case referred to:

Ayo v. Emajaka and Another, Eastern Judicial Division, Unreported.

APPEAL from a decision of the Magistrate's Court, Accra, on June 7, 1957, upholding a decision of the Accra Municipal Court" B " on February 15, 1957, in favour of the plaintiff in an action in which he sought a return of dowry and an order for custody of children.

Amoo Lamptey for the plaintiff.

Sowah for the defendants.

SIMPSON J. This is an appeal from a decision of a Magistrate reluctantly upholding a judgment of the Accra Municipal Court" B " in favour of the plaintiff. The plaintiff, a Nigerian now resident in Accra, sought an order enabling him to marry the second defendant, a widow, and to be responsible for the maintenance of her two children. The first defendant is her father. The plaintiff claimed that according to an Urhobo custom which is binding upon the parties, if a husband dies leaving a wife, a suitable member of the family is selected by the family to marry the widow; that he had been so selected; and, that the appellants had refused the marriage. Evidence was given that the widow was entitled to refuse but in that event was bound to refund to the family of the deceased £12 from the money paid to her on her marriage as dowry and the family of the deceased took the custody of the children and accepted the responsibility of maintaining and looking after them. The judgment of the Municipal Court was as follows:

" Court found defendants liable. Plaintiff to claim ~he dowry of £12 from defendants and take the custody of the children of late Jones who are now in the custody of the second defendant, namely Esierabome and Aba."

The Municipal Court derives its jurisdiction in this case from ~he Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 15 (b), under which it is empowered to administer" the provisions of any law binding between parties subject to the jurisdiction of Native Courts." Counsel for the appellants argued that before it can administer Urhobo customary law, which is not a native customary law prevailing within its jurisdiction, it must have evidence of that law by an expert such as a professional lawyer, the holder of an official situation which requires and therefore implies legal knowledge, or some other person who from his profession or business has had peculiar means of becoming acquainted with Urhobo customary law. In this case evidence was given by three persons who fell into none of these categories. The evidence however was not seriously disputed by the defendants and in fact in cross-examination the first defendant confirmed the custom. There was therefore evi­dence from which the court might reasonably conclude that such a custom existed.

It was further argued by Mr. Sowah for the defendants that the custom relied on was held by Dennison J. in the unreported case of Ayo v. Emajaka and Another which was put into evidence in the Municipal Court (Ayo being the second defendant in this case) to be repugnant to natural justice, equity and good conscience and therefore contrary to the provisions of the Courts Ordinance, s. 87. I do not however agree that this decision was binding on the Municipal Court. The learned judge was careful to qualify his view by the words "in this case, at any rate. "

Although the decision referred to was not binding on the Municipal Court nevertheless this court is entitled to consider whether or not the custom relied on is one to which the court will refuse to give effect on the grounds that it is repugnant to natural justice, equity, and good conscience. In general it is doubtful that the custom is so repugnant, nevertheless in the present case where in purported compliance with custom the custody of the widow's children has been given by the lower court to a man whom she has refused ~o marry the application of the custom is undoubtedly repugnant to natural justice, equity, and good conscience.

Mr. Sowah also argued that the document purporting to come from the family of the deceased and ordering that the children be placed under the guardianship of the plaintiff was fraudulent. No evidence was led to support this contention but on the other hand the genuine­ness of this document was not proved. It was put in by the plaintiff but was not admitted by the defendants to be genuine. It should not have been difficult to call one of the signatories or a person with a knowledge of the signatures but this was not done. The document should therefore have been rejected by the Municipal Court. The plaintiff's mise rested on this document. Without it there would have been no evidence before the court that the family of the deceased delegated to the plaintiff the responsibility of maintaining and looking after the children nor would there have been evidence that he had been deputed by them to claim the dowry repayable to the family by the widow.

For the above reasons I allow the appeal and set aside the judg­ments of the Magistrate's Court and the Accra Municipal Court.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
