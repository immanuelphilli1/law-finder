                                                    December 3, 1957

                                        WOYOME AND OTHERS v. AGBOMASU AND OTHERS.

                               High Court, Eastern Judicial Division, Land Court               

                                                          Ollennu J.

 

 

Native Courts-Practice-Res judicata-Whether a decision on a plea of res judicata a final or an interlocutory order-Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Procedure Regulations, 1945, regs. 17 and 18.

Native Courts-Practice-A ppeals-Decision b.y Magistrate's Court on appeal from Native Court-Order given without jurisdiction-Effect of adoption of such order by Native Court without an inquiry on merits of the case.

The defendants in an action concerning land before a Native Court filed a motion asking for the complaint against them to be dismissed on the ground that it was res judicata. 'the plaintiffs filed a motion asking for an interim injunction. The Native Court granted the interim injunc­tion but dismissed the motion for res judicata. The defendant thereupon appealed to the Magistrate's Court, as against an interlocutory order. The Magistrate's Court reversed the finding of the Native Court and ordered the proceedings to stop on the grounds that the claim was res judicata. The Native Court adopted this ruling of the Magistrate's Court and dismissed the claim. On appeal,

Held, (i) a decision on a plea of res judicata is not a decision merely on a matter in a cause but is a decision on the cause itself. It is thus a final rather than an interlocutory order. The fact that the Native Court (Southern Ghana) Procedure Regulations provide that on failure of a plea of res judicata the case is to proceed on its merits does not make such failure anything other than a final order for, if the point is taken on appeal and the appeal court upholds the plea, this is a decision on the merits of the case and finally disposes of it.

[EDITORIAL NOTE. For a contrary view see the decision of Acolatse J. in Kpanyinli v. Western Nzima State CfYUncil and Others (1957) 3 W.A.L.R. 190. In that case the contrary situation arose. A plea of res judicata was upheld by a State Council but reversed by the Governor"General on the advice of the Appeals Commissioner. The question then arose as to whether the State Council was still seised of the complaint made to it or whether it had been finally determined.]

(ii) Because the issue raised by the plea of res judicata went to the root of the cause the Native Court erred in dealing with it as a motion at the same time as the motion for the interim injunction. Their pro" cedure was contrary to the requirements of the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Procedure Regulations, reg. 17.

(iii) By the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 52 (2), only appeals in interlocutory matters lie from a Native Court to a Magis" trate's Court. As the decision first appealed from in this case was a final order the Magistrate's Court had no jurisdiction to entertain it. The proceedings before the magistrate were therefore null and void for want of jurisdiction and, as the Native Court had subsequently merely adopted the magistrate's ruling without hearing the plea on its merits, their decision also was given without jurisdiction.

OLLENNU J. stated the facts outlined above and continued:

Under regulations 17 and 18 of the Native Court (Southern Ghana) Procedure Regulations made under the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 70, where a plea of res judicata is set up, the native court has to hear and decide it as an issue. The said regulations are as follows:

" 17. Where a defendant or accused person wishes to plead that the Native Court has no jurisdiction or that the claim or charge does not disclose any cause of action or offence or that the subject" matter of the claim has already been adjudicated upon, or that (if it is a criminal cause) he has been previously convicted or acquitted of the same offence, the defendant or accused person shall make such plea at any time after he is asked what he has to say in answer to the charge or claim, and his plea shall be written in the Record Book.

" 18. The Native Court shall consider whether a plea made under Regulation 17 is made out and give its decision which shall be written in the Record Book. If the Native Court is satisfied that the plea has been made out, the suit must be dismissed or the accused discharged, as the case may be. If the Native Court is not satisfied that the plea has been made out, it shall order the defendant or the accused (as the case may be) to plead in the ordinary way under Regulation 15, or that the hearing shall continue. "

The motion filed in this case by the second defendant is, in my opinion, a substantive plea of res judicata, which should have been tried in manner provided by the Regulations. It is not an interlocutory application but one which goes to the root of the case, and I consider that the Native Court was wrong in combining it with the plaintiff's interlocutory application for an interim injunction and trying them together.

A decision on a plea of rcs judicata is not a decision merely on a matter in the cause, it is a decision on the cause itself. When it upholds the plea, the cause is finally determined and no further proceedings can be taken except on appeal. The mere fact that the case will proceed on the merits upon a ruling disallowing the plea does not, in my opinion, make the decision on the plea any the less a final order on the merits; because should the appeal court differ from the trial court and uphold the plea as it did in this case, it would be deciding the claim on its merits. Therefore, the court to which an appeal would lie from a deci­sion on a plea of res judicata, whether upholding or refusing the plea, must be a court vested with jurisdiction to entertain appeals from decisions of the particular trial court on the merits in the subject-matter of the suit.

Under the Native Courts (Southern Ghana) Ordinance, s. 52 (2), an appeal from an interlocutory decision given by a Native Court which is the court authorised to sit as a Native Appeal Court lies to the Magis­trate's Court, as a final court of appeal. But under section 47 of that Ordinance an appeal from a decision on the merits given by a Native Appeal Court or a grade" A " Native Court in a land cause or matter lies to the Land Court and not to a Magistrate's Court. The Magis­trate's Court has neither original nor appellate jurisdiction in land causes and matters.

The judgment which the Magistrate's Court purported to give in this case, on the appeal against the ruling of the Native Court on the plea of res judicata, was an adjudication which finally disposed of the land suit on its merits. The Magistrate's Court therefore assumed jurisdiction which does not belong to it. Its judgment is therefore a nullity; and, it will be so-even if the ruling of the Native Court on the plea of res judicata from which the appeal was taken can be said to be an inter­locutory decision, which in my opinion it is not.

That decision of the Magistrate that the matter is res judicata may be correct on the merits, but since it is given without jurisdiction it is null and void and of no effect.

Had the Native Court themselves after .the judgment of the Magis­trate's Court reconsidered the plea independently of that judgment and come to their own decision that it was rcs judicata, I would have been bound to hear the appeal on its merits. But as indicated in their judg­ment, they did not; they merely made an order consequential to ~he judgment of the Magistrate. This is what they said Court Order:

Pursuant to an order made by the Magistrate's Court, Sogan­kope, dated 11th day of June, 1957, in an interlocutory appeal emanated from the issue of claims of parties herein in the above titled case; this Tongu District Native Court .. A" hereby dis­missed the proceedings of the claims in the main summons as indicated in the Magistrate Court's Order above cited."

A judgment given by a court of competent jurisdiction but based upon a decision which is null and void is erroneous; it is not a decision on the matters in issue between the parties. It amounts to a registra­tion of the judgment without jurisdiction in the court of competent jurisdiction. Such registration does not confer jurisdiction where none exists and the registered judgment should not be allowed .to stand.

It is also apparent that the manner in which the Native Court dealt with the plea of res judic.ata is irregular, and consequently the order they made thereon cannot stand and must also be quashed.

The appeal is therefore allowed and the judgment of the Native Court is set aside. The order made by the Native Court on the motion or plea of res judicata is also set aside. The case is remitted to the Native Court for hearing de novo by a different panel. At the fresh trial the second defendant's plea of res judicata can be properly dealt with in manner provided by the regulations.

Appeal allowed.

S. G. D.
