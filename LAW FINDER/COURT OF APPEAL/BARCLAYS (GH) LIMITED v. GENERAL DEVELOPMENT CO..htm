JUDGMENT

OWUSU-ANSAH, J.A.

The defendant/Appellant allegedly defaulted in the payment of facilities granted to it, by the Plaintiff/Respondent, despite repeated demands, and the Defendant/Appellants own numerous promises to repay the facilities.

By 8th March 2002, the Defendants/Appellants total indebtedness to the Plaintiff/Respondent under various facilities stood at ¢5,154,243,371.48

On the 21st March 2002, the Plaintiff/Respondent commenced action in the High Court Sekondi, in suit No. CS 69/2002 for the following reliefs:

1. The recovery of the sum of ¢5,154,243,371.48 being the balance due and owing on Banking facilities granted to the Defendant at its request and which it had failed to pay, and/or refused to pay despite repeated demands.

2. Interest on the said sum of ¢5,154,243,371.48 at the prevailing Bank rate from the 8th March, 2002, up to and including the date of final payment.

OR in the alternative

An order for the judicial sale of

All the assets of the Defendant as described in the Debenture Deed, executed in favour of the Plaintiff dated the 9th day of September, 1999, stamped as document No. LVB 122259/99, and registered at the Registrar of Companies as No. 11,613 under a Certificate of registration of a mortgage charge:

a. All that piece or parcel of land with building or buildings thereon, situate, lying and being at Ngyiresia comprised in the mortgage deed dated the 8th day of May 2001 and bearing land registration No. 235/2002.

b. Costs.

The Defendant/Appellant was served with the Writ of Summons and acompanying Statement of Claim on the same 21st March, 2002.

On the 11th April, the Defendant/Appellant entered a conditional appearance to the suit, but took no further steps thereafter.

On the 6th May, 2002, i.e. 25 days after the entry of Conditional appearance, by the Defendant, the Plaintiff/Respondent filed Summons for Judgment under Order 14 as amended by LI 1129, and the Summons was fixed for hearing on the 27th May, 2002

The Defendant was served with the Summons on the 6th May 2002 at 10.30 a.m. and a number of documents were attached to the Summons as exhibits to be referred to at the hearing including:

•  The debenture Deed dated 18/2/99

•  The Legal mortgage dated 9/5/2001

•  Plaintiffs various demand letters served on the Defendant/Appellant;

•  Statement of Accounts in respect of the Defendant/appellants accounts.

When the summons came on for hearing on the 27th May 2002, the Defendant/appellant was absent even though there was proof of service of the summons on the Defendant.

Moreover, the Defendant/Appellant also failed to file any affidavit in opposition to the summons for summary judgment.

Nevertheless the learned trial judge in the fullness of his wisdom and out of abundance of caution, adjourned the summons to Monday the 24th June 2002, and ordered that hearing notice be served on the Defendant to appear on the 24th June, 2002.

Accordingly the Defendant was served on the 30th May 2002 to Appear on the 24th June 2002

Notwithstanding the service of the said hearing Notice on the Defendant to appear on the 24th June 2002 the Defendant not only failed once again to appear but also filed no process whatever in respect of the suit.

The summons was moved, and summary judgment was entered for the Plaintiff, "To recover the liquidated sum of ¢5,154,243,371.48 plus interest thereon at the current Bank rate from 8th March 2003 to the date of judgment with ¢60 million costs against the Defendant.

Entry of judgment was filed on the 19th July 2002, and served on the Defendant/Appellant on the 19th July 2002 at 10.15 a.m.

On the Defendants failure to satisfy the judgment debt, the Plaintiff went into execution, and attached the mortgaged property. A motion was filed by the Plaintiff/Judgment creditor for determination of the reserve price. This was again served on the Defendant/Appellant on the 25th October 2002.

It was at this stage that the High Court differently constituted granted the Defendant/Judgment debtor leave to appeal out of time but refused the Defendants application for a stay of proceedings.

The Defendant/Appellant now appeals to this Court ostensibly on one ground of appeal, only namely,

"The learned Trial Judge ought to have dismissed the Plaintiff's application as the Plaintiff could not have (upon their failure to produce for inspection and taking copies thereof the documents requested for) relied on same for judgment".

The appellant invites this Court to reverse the judgment on the basis that the trial judge ought have dismissed the Plaintiff/Applicant's claim, in view of his failure to produce certain documents for inspection

From the evidence it is quite clear that the alleged notice to produce was never served on the Plaintiff/Respondent. In any event these documents had been attached as exhibits to the Plaintiffs summons for summary judgment which the defendant never resisted.

Order 14 r. 1 as amended, states:

"where in an action began by a Writ, a defendant has been served with a Statement of Claim and has entered an appearance, the Plaintiff may apply to the Court for judgment against him, on the ground that he has no defence to a claim included in the Writ or to a particular part of such a claim or that he has no defence to such a claim or part except as to the amount of any damages"

Quite clearly that is the case here. In my view the learned judge was right in entering judgment for the Plaintiff, having regard to the overwhelming evidence and the facts before him.

As already indicated all the security documents were attached to Summons for judgment served on the Defendant/Appellant. Why did it fail to react to it in anyway? The contents have not been denied so far.

I find the Defendant's approach and attitude to the suit so causal and so lackadaisical in the face of mountain evidence of liability against the Defendant, in a suit that involved such a huge amount by any standards.

Equity aids the vigilant, not the indolent! In my opinion, a Defendant cannot ignore the service on him of such important processes and him of such important processes, and go to sleep and subsequently expect the courts sympathetically to come to his aid. He does so at his own peril. Nor can a Defendant, or any other party, take for granted that a filed document has been served without checking if the document is intended to be relied upon. The conduct of the Defendant seems to be totally devoid of good faith. Be that as it may, the Defendant/Appellant could have availed himself of the provisions of Order 14 Rule 11 which state.

"A judgment given against a Defendant,, who does not appear at the hearing, of an application under this order, may be set aside or varied by the court on terms it thinks

just."

In the instant case, the Defendant/Appellant did not file any application or any other process praying the High Court to set aside the judgment given in the absence of the Defendant, but merely asked for leave to appeal out of time, realizing as he must have done, rather belatedly that he had slept on his rights.

That is not in the least surprising, considering that the Defendant did not at any stage of the proceedings file any document remotely alluding to the possibility of a good defence to the Plaintiff's action. It seems to me that the Defendant was merely employing delay tactics — to postpone the evil day, as it were!

The defendant did not file, or raise any triable issue before the High Court. Surely, any defence would be better than no defence at all.

However, the question arises: why did the Defendant not raise the issue of the Notice to produce, especially when it was served with the Summons for Summary judgment. The answer appears to be that the Defendant/Appellant must have been perfectly aware of the non-service of the Notice to Produce. The record of proceedings bears eloquent testimony to this fact.

For no one can seriously be accused of non-compliance with an order of which he was not aware or which had not been brought to his notice or attention.

As already indicated there is no suggestion that the Defendant/Appellant had a defence to this action. In Duncan v. Kwaoko Ltd. 1981 3 GLR 476 it was that the defence need only show that there is a triable issue for leave to defend to be granted.

As Justice Taylor once put it "If a Defendant is unable to set up a good Defence or raise an issue which ought to be tried, or where no fairly arguable point is raised by the defence, it is the Court’s duty to give judgment for the Plaintiff; for the Defendant is bound to show some reasonable grounds of defence."

Halsbury’s Laws of England 4th Edition vol. 37 neatly sums up the position "The Procedure of Summary judgment under Order 14 has been described as Beneficent in the sense that the Plaintiff may obtain speedy and summary Judgment without trial, even though the Defendant expresses an intention to Defend the action, but the Plaintiff may obtain judgment if only his claim is properly formulated and comes within the scope of the order, and is duly proved by affidavit evidence to which the Defendant is unable to set up a good defence or raise an issue which ought to be tried where no fairly arguable point is raised by way of a defence it is the Court's duty to give judgment for the Plaintiff, for The Defendant is bound to show some reasonable grounds of defence"

It is also a well-established rule that on failure to apply to set aside a Writ or take any further steps within 14 days after entering conditional Appearance, the Conditional Appearance becomes unconditional and the usual procedure must be followed.

The court of Appeal decision in Renault Vehicles v. Ashanti Engineering 1991 1 GLR 443 confirms this position, as does the case of Dede II v. Ansah and others 1980 GLR.

I have carefully weighed and considered all the submissions by learned counsel for the Defendant/Appellant. I must confess I find my self regretfully unable to associate myself with those submissions.

In my view the judgment of the court below is wholly unassailable. I would dismiss this appeal as being totally without merit. For the avoidance of doubt the judgment of the Court below is hereby affirmed Costs.
