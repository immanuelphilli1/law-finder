JUDGMENT

ACQUAYE, JA:-

This an appeal by the two accused persons against their conviction and sentence by the Trial High Court sitting at Sekondi on 7th December 2005 on three counts of robbery. The facts of the case are that the complainants lived in three houses situate about 100 meters apart at West Anaji in Takoradi. On 27th July 2005 at about 1.00am 5 armed men one of whom wore a face mask attacked the complainants in the three houses one after the other and made away with a total sum of Gh¢10,350,000.00, 31/2 pieces of cloth, 2 gold wrist watches and 2 mobile phones after assaulting some of the complainants. On 17th August 2005 the 1st Appellant was arrested on a tip off. On 24th August 2005 the 2nd Appellant who lived in the same house with 1st Appellant was also arrested. The two were later identified by the complainants at an identification parade as being among the five who attacked them on 27th July 2005. After a summary trial without jury the trial judge convicted the appellants and sentenced each to life imprisonment on each of the three counts of robbery and ordered that they be repatriated to their home country Nigeria when they attain the age of 70 years. It is against the conviction and sentence that the accused persons have appealed. The first ground argued by Counsel for the Appellants is ground 5 of the notice of appeal which states that "The trial judge is tainted with bias and real likelihood of bias by importing discrimination into the judicial task of having and assessing evidence, when by law a) All persons shall be equal before the law b) A person shall not be discriminated against on grounds of race, ethnic origin and/or social or economic status" Counsel argued that the trial judge allowed the country he believed the appellants came from to influence his decision when he held that they were aliens who should be deported to Nigeria, their home country when they are certified to be 70 years of age. By this ground Counsel for the appellant was imputing bias or the real likelihood of bias on the part of the judge in determining the case before him. For a judge to be imputed with bias there should be evidence of pecuniary or proprietary interest in the outcome of the case. As stated in the case of ADZAKU VRS GALENKU (1974)1 GLR 198 to invalidate a decision, the allegation of bias must be supported by evidence. A mere or reasonable suspicion of bias was not enough; the law recognized not only actual bias but that interest, other than interest of a direct pecuniary or proprietary nature, which gave rise to a real likelihood of bias. Counsel did not provide any evidence of bias. On the contrary there exists at page 15 of the record of proceedings the evidence in chief of the 1st appellant Line 32 - my father is from Lagos, my mother is from Gambaga, Northern part of Ghana. I was born in Nigeria. I schooled in Nigeria. I lived for 22 year in Nigeria ------------ I hold a Nigerian passport. The 2nd Appellant also stated at page 20 line 5 that "I have been in Ghana since 2001. I am married, my wife is in Nigeria with my one year old child --------------- I changed in Nigeria, my documents are in Nigeria". There thus seems to be evidence on record to justify the trial judge's comments that the appellants were aliens who must be deported back to Nigeria. In exercising his power in a trial a judge has discretion to pass comments on matters arising from the trial which cannot be questioned unless it is shown that the judge has violated his duty to be fair and candid or that he has been arbitrary and capricious in the conduct of the trial. As this is not the case in the instant case this ground of appeal fails and it is dismissed. The second ground argued in this appeal is ground 1 of the notice of appeal which is "That the trial judge did not give any consideration whatsoever to the alibi pleaded by the 2nd appellant as confirmed by PW4 (the police investigator) in his evidence which occasioned substantial miscarriage of justice to the appellant". Counsel for the appellant quoted PW4's evidence that when he checked on the alibi of 2nd appellant at the Hotel Chavez at New Achimota, the hotel attendant confirmed the 2nd appellant and Albert Koomson checked in on 24th July 2005 and vacated on 28th July 2005. According to Counsel for the 2nd Appellant the judge rejected this evidence on the basis of the appellant's girl friend who could not be specific on the days 2nd appellant spent with her. Counsel argued that whether the 2nd appellant was in a hotel at New Achimota or with his girl friend at Achimota the fact still remained that he was not in Takoradi let alone being at the scene of robbery. I have read the judgment of the trial judge and I am satisfied that the trial judge adequately considered the defence of alibi put up by the 2nd Appellant. The trial judge perused the defence of alibi but at the end rejected it as a ruse. The trial judge at page 48 of the proceedings noted that the 2nd Appellant gave two statements to the police during the investigation. In the first statement given on 24th August 2005 admitted as Exhibit F the 2nd Appellant stated that "On the 27th August 2005 I was in Accra to collect my goods and therefore could not be part of the robbery. If the police want the truth they can contact one Osei Nyanko of Dansoman and find out from him". In the second statement Exhibit G taken 16 days later the 2nd Appellant stated that "I travelled to Accra together with my brother Albert Koomson on 24th July 2005 and slept in a hotel called Chez at New Achimota. During my time there which was from 24th July to 26th July 2005 my girl friend one Eartha used to visit me in the day time. This is because I was using the same bed with my brother Albert Koonnson. I have to state also that it was Albert Koomson's name that we used in the registration card. We left the hotel around 12.00 and went to my girl friend's house at Nii Boye's and slept with her up to 28th July 2005 before I came back to Takoradi". The trial judge noted at page 48 of the record of proceedings that "To my utter surprise Osei Nyarko of Dansoman by whom the 2nd accused was to establish his alibi did not feature at all in the second statement. Now it was a hotel at New Achimota registered not in the name of 2nd Accused but in the name of Albert Koomson" and a girl friend Eartha who testified as witnesses. At page 53 of the record of proceedings the trial judge also noted the uncomplimentary demeanor of the girlfriend Eartha before contradicting the details of her evidence with that of the 2nd accused now 2nd appellant. To quote from the judgment at page 53 of the record. "Eartha said they have been together for a year. 2nd accused put it at 5 months. Eartha said the 2nd accused had slept in her house on two occasions. Later she changed to about five (5) times. 2nd accused said he has slept in her house only once.According to Eartha the last time she spent the night with 2nd accused was on 28th July 2005 {but 2nd accused said he left Accra in the afternoon of 28th July 2005}. Eartha goes on to say on the 26 we ate rice, and on the 28th we ate rice in my house. I did not eat with him on the 27th On the contrary the 2 nd accused said on the 27th he was at a restaurant with her and they ate Banku and Okro. Then in the evening she prepared bread with eggs and sardines. Eartha said 2nd Accused left on the 29th while 2nd accused said he left on the 28th,, I have quoted at length page 53 of the record to show that the trial judge considered the defence of alibi but rejected it. There is on record many other contradictions in the evidence of alibi put up by the 2nd Appellant and his witnesses which makes the defence of alibi highly improbable. For example at page 24 line 10 the prosecutrix asked the 2nd Appellant during cross- examination. Q. what did you do on the 27th A. I was in Accra with my girlfriend. The whole day I was with her driving about in my car. At page 28 line 40 when the prosecutrix asked DW1 Albert Koomson during cross examination. Q. On the 27th what did you do? A. Kennedy and me drove round town. Eartha was at home. When Eartha Lartey who testified as CW1 was asked on page 25 of the record Q. Did you prepare food on the 27th? A. I can't remember Q. What did you do on 27th? A. I can't remember. At page 55 of the record of proceedings the trial judge had this to say. "The robbery took place from the midnight of the 26th through to the early hours of 27th. I find from the evidence that the inconsistency in the evidence of the three key players is mostly displayed from what they say happened from the 26th to the 27th. Why did the 2nd Accused abandon his alibi with Osei Nyarko of Dansoman. Why did the l Accused not mention the trip of his brother in his statement? How could it be that you go on a business trip yet on cross examination you could not name one customer or a place you visited in pursuit of the business. The reason is that the trip is a trick. I find that the alibi was an attempt to cover up and outwit detection. It has failed. I reject the defence of alibi of 2nd accused. But under our law as explained earlier, that is not what would make the 2nd accused culpable. The prosecution must discharge the burden cast on it by law. And to my mind that has been done. Both accused have been identified". It would be seen from the above that the learned trial judge considered the defence of alibi put up by the 2nd Appellant but rejected it and we think he was entitled to reject it on the evidence before him. The trial judge also found from the above that "Both have been identified". The 1st Appellant was identified by all three complainants who also gave evidence to that effect. The 1st Appellant has not challenged his identification. With regards to the 2nd Appellant none of the complainants testified that 2nd appellant was identified. PW4 however testified that the 2nd appellant was identified by PW2 and a photograph taken during the identification parade was tendered as Exhibit C. As a result of this piece of evidence not being challenged or denied by the defence the trial judge overruled the prosecutions application to be allowed to recall PW2 to testify in respect of the identification of 2nd accused. The issue of the identification of 2nd accused was confirmed by the 2nd accused himself when in his evidence in chief he testified at page 23 line 39 as follows. "The next day there was an identification parade. On the day of the identification parade I was pointed out by a lady (PW2) and a tall and fair man was also pointed out together with another man". The trial judge was therefore right in finding that "Both have been identified". The third ground argued by the lawyer for the appellants is that the investigator PW4 admitted under cross-examination that in all 4 people were pointed out as those who committed the robbery on 27th July 2005 but only two were charged. The answer to the lawyers query is to be found at the end of the prosecutors (PW4), cross-examination when he answered that the other two people identified were brought in to form the role and were not suspects. Counsel for the appellants did not argue grounds 2 and 4 of the notice of appeal in his written submission of the appellant's case so he is deemed to have abandoned them. After going through the record of proceedings and the appeal filed we are satisfied that the conviction of the appellants on charges of robbery was lawful and the appeal against conviction is hereby dismissed.The lawyer for the appellant lastly argued the trial judge sentenced the appellants to life imprisonment which is too harsh.Section 149 of the Criminal Offences Act 1960 Act 29 provides that "A person who commits robbery commits a first degree felony". Section 296 (1) of Criminal and other Offences Act 30 of 1960 also provides that "Where a criminal offence is declared by an enactment to be a first degree felony and the punishment for that offence is not specified, a person convicted of that offence is liable to imprisonment for life or any lesser term". It was submitted for the Republic that the prevalence of the offence of robbery, the intrinsic seriousness of the offence, the degree of revulsion felt by law-abiding citizens and the impunity with which the crime was committed called for severe punishments to the appellants to act as deterrent to those who intend to engage in this heinous crime. However true this may be the law sets life imprisonment as the maximum sentence for armed robbery so life imprisonment is obviously on the high side. In the circumstances of this case we will set aside the sentence of life imprisonment imposed by the trial court and substitute therefore a sentence of 30 years I.H.L on each count for each appellant. The sentence are to run concurrently from the date of first sentence. We also order that the accused person be deported to their home country, Nigeria, after they have served the sentence. COUNSEL: SAMUEL OFORI FOR THE APPELLANTS
