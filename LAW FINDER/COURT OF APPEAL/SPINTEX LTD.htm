JUDGMENT MARFUL-SAU JA:

This is an appeal against the judgment of the High Court, Accra dated the 31st October 2008, dismissing the plaintiff action and entering judgment for the defendant on his counterclaim. The plaintiff is thus the appellant herein and the defendant is the respondent. The appellant per the writ issued on 28-1-93 claimed the following:- a. Recovery of an amount of ¢110,175,349.74 b. Interest at the current bank rate from 31/7/92 to date of final payment c. General damages for breach of contract In a terse statement of claim of 7 paragraphs, the appellant simply alleged that it supplied goods to the respondent between August 1990 to July 1992 worth ¢203,656,469.08. However, in July 1992 when the respondent's account was audited his indebtedness stood at ¢110,175,349.74. The appellant further averred that the respondent had failed to settle the said indebtedness notwithstanding repeated demands.The respondent disputed the claim and filed a 28 paragraph statement of defence and counterclaimed for the following: a. The sum of ¢6,772,000 being an amount wrongfully withheld by the plaintiff since November 1992. b. Interest on the said sum at the prevailing bank rate from November 1992 till the date of final payment. By respondent's defence and counterclaim the appellant was compelled to file a 9 paragraph reply and defence to the counterclaim which was later amended on 18-8-95: From the pleadings exchanged by the parties it is obvious that the appellant in his statement of claim suppressed material facts which formed the basis of the dispute. As the pleadings showed the amount claimed by the appellant was not a simple debt arising out of goods supplied and unpaid. Rather the real facts were that the appellant's Chief Accountant one Isaac Forson in the course of respondent's dealings with the appellant company, had instructed the respondent to be paying the cost of his supplies to him, the Chief Accountant, so that respondent's orders would be processed promptly. As a result the respondent was prevailed upon by the Chief Accountant and he paid cash and issued corresponding cheques to the Chief Accountant as payment for his orders. Respondent after tendering the cash and corresponding cheques to the Chief Accountant continued to receive his supplies without any problems, till in July 1992 when his account was audited and it came to light that the Chief Accountant was not crediting the respondent account with the cash. When the cheques issued to the Chief Accountant were presented they were dishonoured, hence the debt of ¢110,175,349.74 against the Respondent. The respondent reported the Chief Accountant to the management of appellant company and he admitted that he failed to credit respondent account with the cash he received from him. The Chief Accountant then gave an undertakig to settle the amount due on the respondent account. After this event, the appellant requested the respondent to continue the business as a result the respondent deposited an amount of ¢6,772,000 to the appellant for further supplies. Appellant later sought to treat the amount of ¢6,772,000 as payments made by the respondent to settle part of the debt the Chief Accountant had undertaken to settle as narrated above. The ¢6,772,000.00 forms the basis of respondent's counterclaim.From the facts narrated above it is clear that appellant did suppress material facts from its statement of claim which portrayed the dispute as a case of the respondent taking delivery of goods and simply refusing to pay for them. Appellant's statement of claim as formulated was contrary to the general rules of pleadings and that practice ought to be deprecated. At the end of the trial appellant's claim was dismissed and respondent's counterclaim was upheld by the trial court. This appeal is thus against the dismissal of the appellant's claim with a prayer that this court sets aside the judgment of the court below and judgment entered for appellant. In the original Notice of Appeal filed on the 22-1-2009, the appellant raised four grounds of appeal namely: i. The judgment is against the weight of evidence II. The cost of GH¢10,0O0.00 is excessive and unjustifiable iii. That the court erred when it failed to hold that the defendant perpetrated fraud on the plaintiffs when he issued dud cheques, for goods collected from the plaintiff and in some cases connived with some officials of plaintiff to suppress the clearance of cheques. iv. The court erred when it failed to hold that the counterclaim of GH¢667.20 was part payment of goods collected during the audit of the defendants accounts with the plaintiff. Pursuant to notice in the Notice of Appeal that additional grounds of appeal would be filed, the appellant sought leave and same was granted it on the 12-4-2010 to file additional grounds. Appellant then filed what he called two additional grounds on the 13-4-2010 namely:- v. Whether or not the defendant's prosecution of Isaac Forson for fraud against him in the sum being claimed by the plaintiff against the defendant disables him from raising the defence of payment to the agent of the company on behalf of the company. vi. Whether or not in law the defendant can claim the benefit of the judgment in a criminal proceeding for fraud against him as complainant and dispute his liability in the civil suit in the circumstance of his case. I shall first address the additional grounds stated above and ask whether these are grounds of appeal properly so called? In other words are these grounds of appeal allowed by the rules of this court? I think as counsel for respondent rightly contended in his statement of case the above additional grounds are very irregular. By those grounds of appeal, appellant sought to present determinable issues as grounds of appeal. Those grounds as formulated do not disclose any determination of issues raised therein by the trial court for which this court had to re-examine. Simply put those issues formulated as grounds of appeal are such that it ought to have been raised at the court below for their determination, so they could be examined by this court. In our jurisdiction even though appeals are of right they are regulated by statute, for that matter parties who seek to prosecute appeals ought to comply with the statute that govern procedures for prosecuting such appeals. This court is regulated by the Court of Appeal Rules 1997, C.l 19 as amended, in its proceedings and parties invoking the jurisdiction of this court must conform to its rules. Rule 8 of C.l 19 provides as follow:- "(5) The grounds of appeal shall set out concisely and under distinct heads the grounds on which the appellant intends to rely at the earing of the appeal without an argument or a narrative and shall be numbered consecutively. (6) A ground which is vague or general in terms or which does not disclose a reasonable ground of appeal is not permitted, except the general ground that the judgment is against the weight of the evidence. (7) A ground of appeal or a part of the appeal which is not permitted under sub rule (6) may be struck out by the court of its own motion or on application by the Respondent" As observed the additional grounds so filed are not grounds but issues raised for determination for the first time. This is not a court of first instance even though an appeal is by way of rehearing. I am of the opinion that grounds of appeal can only arise from issues raised and determined one way or the other by a trial court. A ground of appeal should not take the form of issues for the appellate court to try, as if this court is one of first instance. I find the two additional issues very irregular and I will strike them out. They are accordingly struck out. I will turn to the original grounds of appeal. The first is that the judgment is against the weight of evidence. What this ground means is that the trial court failed to properly consider the evidence adduced at the trial or that the findings made by the trial court are not supported by evidence on record, either that it had excluded matters which were relevant in law for the determination of the action. Upon the examination of the record of appeal, the resolution of the dispute at the court below depended on a fundamental issue whether or not the monies paid by the respondent to appellant's Chief Accountant could be deemed as payments received by the appellant, for which reason the respondent's account was to be credited. In resolving that issue, the trial court had to consider the import and effect of section 142 of the Companies Act 1963, Act 179 which was relied upon by the respondent. The section provides as follows: "142 (1) A person having dealings with a company or someone deriving title under the company is entitled to assume; (b) That a person described in the particulars filed with the Registrar pursuant to sections 27 and 197 as a director, managing director or secretary of the company or represented by the company, acting through its members in general meeting, board of directors, or managing director, as an officer or agent of the company, has been duly appointed and has authority to exercise the powers and perform the functions customarily exercised or performed by a director, managing director or secretary of a company cat-tying on business of the type carried on by the company or customarily exercised or performed by an officer or agent of the type concerned, and the company and those deriving title under it are estopped from denying the truth of that assumption" Then section 142 (2) (a) provides thus: "For purposes of sub-section (1), a person is not entitled to make any of those assumptions if that person had actual knowledge to the contrary or if having regard to the position with, or relationship to the company, that person ought to have known the contrary" From the record of appeal respondent case was that he did not owe the amount claimed by appellant because he was made to pay same to appellant's Chief Accountant, whom he regarded as a senior officer in appellant's company. From respondent's evidence, he claimed that when he was making the payments to the Chief Accountant he knew the Chief Accountant had the authority to receive payments oil of the appellant's company. The appellant on the other hand challenged respondent defence and claimed that it had put ill place a procedure for the supply of goods to customers and they were not required to make payment to the Chief Accountant but rather to the Sales Manager. The appellant submitted that since respondent knew of the established sales procedure but went ahead and made payments to the Chief Accountant, he could not avail himself of section142 (b) of Companies Act. In the circumstance of this case, however, the question one need to answer is, can it be said that the respondent had actual knowledge to the contrary that the Chief Accountant could not receive payments on behalf of the appellant company? I think the answer is No. The reason is that from the record the respondent did not just walk to the Chief Accountant and started making payments to him in his house. Rather, it was the Chief Accountant being the head of the Finance Department who requested respondent to pay the cash and issue the cheques to him, so that respondent's orders would be processed expeditiously. How then could the respondent know that the Chief Accountant had no authority to receive the payment? Yes, the respondent was very much aware of the sale procedures of the appellant's company but that per se did not mean that he knew the Chief Accountant had no authority to receive payments on behalf of the appellant. It is on record that even though the monies paid by respondent, were not credited to his account, he received his supplies as ordered. That is to say respondent received his supplies even though the Chief Accountant was not paying monies received from respondent to appellant's account. The continued supply of goods to the respondent when payments were made to the Chief Accountant naturally made it impossible for the respondent to suspect or doubt the authority of the Chief Accountant. The fact that respondent continued to receive his supplies even though, unknown to him,his payments were not credited to his account, shows that the. sales procedure relied on by the appellant was not so effective to bind a customer. Clearly from the evidence, the Chief Accountant acted as the agent of appellant and holding that senior management position, the respondent would have no doubt at all about his authority to receive the payment on behalf of appellant since the Chief Accountant traditionally and functionally was in charge of the finance of the company. In discussing the effect and scope of section 142 of the Companies Act, Afreh JSC in the case of GODKA GROUP OF COMPANIES VS. P S INTERNATIONAL LTD. (2001-2002) SCGLR 918 had this to say at page 932: "In any case a person contracting with a company is not required to demand the production of a resolution authorizing the board, general meeting, an officer or agent of the company, as the case may be to enter into the contract. It has been established since the case of the Royal British Bank v. Turquard (1843-60) All ER Rep 435 that a person dealing with a company is entitled to assume in the absence of facts putting him on notice or inquiry, that there has been due compliance with all matters of internal management and procedure required by the regulations of the company. This is the Rule in Turquard's case or the Indoor Management Rule" This rule has been codified by our Companies Code 1963 (Act 179) in section 139-143. Under section 142 (2) if the company has held out someone as its agent, it is estopped from denying the appointment, and it could be assumed that a person de jure or de facto officer of the company has the usual powers and duties of that sort of officer" The learned Judge of blessed memory continued on the same page that: "Section 142 (2) is also in accordance with normal agency principles, where a person enters into a contract with an officer or agent of a company, who has apparent or ostensible authority of the company to enter into the contract on its behalf, it will be estopped from denying the authority of the officer or agent" And in Hely-Hutchinson V. Bray Head Ltd. (1968) 1 QB549 at 583, CA Lord Denning MR delivered himself thus: "Ostensible or apparent authority is the authority of an agent as it appears to others." In this case I am of the considered opinion that appellant's Chief Accountant demonstrated ostensibly and apparently to the respondent that he had the authority as the head of finance department of appellant's company to receive the payments from the respondent. Indeed the situation would have been different if the respondent had paid the monies to an officer who had nothing to do with the finance department of the company; for example, making payments to a Human Resource Officer of appellant's company. Obviously, such an officer would have nothing to doing with the financial management of the company and as such a request from such officer naturally ought to put one on notice. From the record of appeal no credible evidence was adduced to prove that respondent knew that the Chief Accountant had no authority to receive payments for goods supplied to customers on behalf of the appellant company. For this reason the first ground of appeal will fail and it is dismissed. I will now address original ground (iii) of the appeal which is that the court erred when it failed to hold that the respondent perpetuated fraud on the appellant when he issued dud cheques for goods collected from the appellant and in some cases connived with some officials of the appellant to suppress the clearance of the cheques. Evidence on record which is undisputed is that appellant's Chief Accountant prevailed on the respondent to pay cash and as well issue cheques to pay for the same transaction. The respondent was told that paying the cash and issuing the cheques will administratively facilitate early supplies to him. The evidence is not that respondent issued dud cheques in payment for the goods supplied to him but that he paid cash and issued cheques for the same supplies. Why would somebody who wanted to defraud a company for goods supplied him, pay cash and also issue cheques to cover the same supplies. The appellant in its reply and defence to counterclaim at paragraph 3 particularized the fraud as follows: 1. Agreeing with the company through Sales Manager and Directors to effect payment in cheques and by-passing them to deal with the said Accountant. 2. Colluding with the said Accountant with the intention of defrauding and collecting goods not paid for. From the record the appellant could not adduce credible evidence to establish that the respondent had been fraudulent. The evidence of appellant's representative Jean Adel Matter as well as PW1, Nii Tackie Yarboi and Yaw Buabeng, PW2 could not establish any fraud against the respondent. There is nothing oil by way of evidence to prove that respondent's dealing with the Chief Accountant was fraudulent as particularized, neither is there any evidence of collision. It would have been different if respondent collected goods and with the tacit consent of the Chief Accountant issued cheques which respondent knew would be dishonoured. The general principle of law is that an allegation of fraud was to be established by evidence called by the person alleging it. The evidence led by the witnesses of the appellant at the trial failed the test. in Strouds Judicial Dictionary (4th ed), Vol 2 at 1095-95, Wills J., in Ex-parte Watson, 21 Q.B.D. 301 is quoted as follows:"'Fraud' in my opinion, is a term that should be reserved for something dishonest and morally wrong, and much mischief is, I think, done, as well as much pain inflicted, by its use where 'illegality and 'illegal' are the really appropriate expressions" I do not think that in the circumstance of this case the allegation of fraud against the respondent was proved. The respondent rather was prevailed upon by the appellant Chief Accountant to pay cash and also issue cheques for goods supplied to him. Indeed from the conduct of the Chief Accountant, who at all material time relevant to this case was an agent of the appellant, it can be concluded that respondent and the appellant were rather victims of fraud perpetuated by the Chief Accountant, who was an agent of appellant. Ground (iii) has no merit and I accordingly dismiss same. The next ground is that the court failed to hold that the counterclaim of GH¢667.20 was part payment of goods collected from the appellant but unpaid for was detected during the audit of the respondent's accounts with the appellant. This is ground (iv). From the record of appeal, this amount is what respondent allegedly paid to the appellant when the Managing Director asked respondent to continue business with appellant but the amount was later seized on the ground that respondent owed appellant. The respondent's claim for this amount was pleaded at paragraph 23 of his statement of defence, to which appellant replied at paragraph 7 of its reply and defence to counterclaim. "23. The Defendant says that the Plaintiff's Managing Director subsequently asked him to continue making purchases from the Plaintiff and he continued to do this until an amount of ¢6,772,000 he had with the Plaintiff was seized by the Plaintiff on the pretext that he owed the Plaintiff. Then appellant's paragraph 9 of the reply and defence to counterclaim reads as follows:- "9. In reacting to paragraphs 23 to 28 of the statement of defence and the counterclaim the Plaintiff deny them and say that upon detection of the initial ¢46 million against the Defendant and upon appeal from him, he was advised by the plaintiff to make payments and receive part supplies pending the outcome of the audit and the outcome of all the credits and debits that has given rise to this action............” From the above averments the trial court found that indeed the appellant seized the amount being claimed by the respondent in his counterclaim. By appellant's pleading at paragraph 9 of the reply and defence to counterclaim, appellant admits that it advised the respondent to be making payments after the audit, which exposed the Chief Accountant. The admission clearly corroborated the evidence of the respondent as averred at paragraph 23 of his defence and counterclaim. The claim by the respondent was therefore more probable of belief than that of appellant, who alleged that the money was paid to settle part of the amount taken by the Chief Accountant. In fact from the record there is evidence that respondent made the payments claimed per the counterclaim, after the Chief Accountant had given an undertaking to refund the monies he received from the respondent. That being the case there was no reason for the respondent to also start paying the debt, as appellant wanted the court to believe. Clearly evidence on record support the finding of the trial court that the amount being claimed in the counterclaim was seized from the respondent after appellant's Managing Director had encouraged respondent to continue the business. This ground also has no merit and fails. The last ground is ground (ii) which is that the cost awarded was excessive and unjustifiable. Award of cost generally is a discretion exercise by the court. However Order 74 of C.l 47 provides a guide for the award of cost in civil proceedings in the High Court. Counsel for the appellant has submitted that the cost of GH¢10,000.00 awarded the respondent is not only excessive but unjustifiable. The only reason for which the trial court awarded cost in that quantum was that the case had been in court for about fifteen years. The record of appeal does not show that both counsel addressed the court before cost was awarded as Order 74 R 2 (2) provides. In awarding the cost of GH¢10,000.00 the trial court took into account the number of years that parties had been in court litigation the dispute. This was perfectly right under Order 74 Rule (4) (c) which provides that the length and complexity of the proceedings may be considered in awarding cost. However this is only one of the stated guidelines that the court had to consider in awarding costs under Order 74. In this case hearing commenced on the 18-11-96 and the case had to pass through the hands of about four Judges before judgment was entered on the 31st of October, 2008, a period of twelve years. The delay cannot be blamed on counsel for appellant alone as counsel for respondent sought to urge on us in his statement of case. Counsel for the respondent argued that appellant counsel abandoned the cross examination of respondent between 1998 till 2005. That fact notwithstanding, in this case respondent had a counterclaim so he could have prosecuted his counterclaim without waiting for the appellant since the counterclaim was a cross action. From the record the delay cannot be blamed on the Appellant alone, hence I think the trial court was wrong in awarding the cost of GH¢10,000.00 against the appellant in the circumstance making it seem that it was appellant that delayed the case in court for about 15 years. The delay in the proceedings was attributable to both parties and for that reason I would review the cost so awarded. I think in the circumstances cost assessed at GH¢5,000.00 for the respondent against the appellant would be fair. Accordingly, ground (ii) of the appeal succeeds and the cost awarded by the lower court is set aside and in its place cost of GH¢5,000.00 is awarded to the respondent. But for the setting aside of the cost awarded by the trial court, the entire appeal fails and same dismissed. COUNSEL: 1. SAMMY ADDO FOR PLAINTIFF/APPELLANT 2. NATHAN P. YARNEY FOR DEFENDANT/RESPONDENT
