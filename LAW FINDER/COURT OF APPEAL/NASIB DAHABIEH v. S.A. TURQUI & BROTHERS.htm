JUDGMENT

ESSILFIE-BONDZIE, J.A.

This is an appeal against the judgment of His Lordship Justice Sarpong, J.A. (Sitting as an additional High Court Judge dated 29th November, 1996.   The appellants (hereinafter referred to as TURQUI) obtained judgment in default of appearance for recovery of their premises. No.61, Kojo Thompson Road, Accra in suit No.104/75 against Nasib Dahabieh (hereinafter simply called Dahabieh) then carrying on business under the name and style of Technical Trading Company.  On 2 February, 1976 Turqui went into execution by a Writ of possession and recovered possession of the premises.

Dahabieh, as the 1st plaintiff and also in his business name, Technical Trading Company as the 2nd plaintiff instituted suit No. 152/76 in the High for:

“(1)  a declaration that the judgment obtained by Turqui (defendant) in Suit No.104/1975 is null and void as is viliated by fraud. 

(2) An order setting aside the judgment in the said suit No.104/75 and all subsequent processes.

(3)Damages"

On the 7th June, 1982, the High Court set aside the said judgment and awarded exemplary damages.  The concluding and relevant part of the judgment which set aside the judgment in suit No.104/1975 stated:

“For the reasons herein before given I do hereby act aside the judgment and the order for possession given in favour of the plaintiffs in the earlier suit No. 104/75 who are the defendants in the present case.

The 2nd plaintiff i.e. Technical Trading Company is hereby struck out as that name is not a legal name, leaving the 1st plaintiff as the only plaintiff (DAHABIEH).

I award as exemplary damages, in favour of the plaintiff against the defendants (TURQUI) the sum of  ¢100,000.00.

There will therefore be judgment for plaintiffs against the defendants in the sum of ¢100,000.00 and ¢10.000 costs including" counsel's cost of ¢5,000.00. The court also ordered that Dahabieh was entitled, if, he so desired, to reposses, the premises"

As the record reveals Dahabieh was not satisfied with his victory in suit No.152/76 and therefore in suit No.1907/88 instituted an action against Turqui in the High Court Accra for general and special damages for the detention or conversion of his stock-in-trade and furniture as a result of wrongful execution of a writ of possession.

It is evident from the record of proceedings that while this action was pending, the Dahabieh brought another action numbered suit No.453/89 entitled NASIB DAHABIEH vrs: S.A. TURQUI & BROS. in the High Court.  The claim was for money due from the Defendants to plaintiff on a judgment of ¢100,000.00 awarded the plaintiff against the defendants in an action by the plaintiff against the defendants in the High Court, Accra in suit No.152/76.

The particulars of the claim given were: (a) 7/6/82 judgment debt ¢100,000.00 (b)There is due to plaintiff the sum of ¢100,000.00 together with interest thereon at the prevailing bank rate on the said sum of ¢100,000 including costs from date of Writ herein until payment or judgment in the action.

Suit No.543/89 was consolidated with suit No.1907/88 by an order of the Court and the trial commenced.  As said the High Court presided over by His Lordship Sapong, J.A. (as he then was) on the 29th November, 1996 gave judgment for the 1st Respondent (DAHABIEH).  He also dismissed TURQUI's Counter-claim against DAHABIEH and 2nd Respondent.

It is against this judgment that the appellants (TURQUI) have appealed on several grounds.   With the exception of three grounds of appeal, I do not find the other grounds relevant to the determination of the appeal.  And do not propose to give consideration to those grounds.

The three grounds which are going to be treated in this appeal were formulated by counsel for the appellants as follows.

(a) The trial judge erred in dismissing the appellant's plea of RES-JUDICATA.

(b) The trial judge erred in dismissing the appellant's plea of estoppel.

(c) The trial judge erred in dismissing the appellants Counter-Claim.

The first and 2nd grounds will be dealt with together. On these learned counsel for Turqui (appellant) submitted in the Written address that since the trial judge failed to pronounce upon Turqui's plea of estoppel the Court of Appeal is invited to rule on it. Counsel submitted that Turqui's plea in suit No.1907/88 is that DAHABIEH claim inter alia damages for the wrongful execution in suit No.152/76 and that the learned trial judge granted the relief.  For that reason DAHABIEH was not entitled to harass Turqui with that grevance, any more in suit No.1907/88.

In paragraphs 4 & 5 of the statement of defence in Suit No.453/89, Turqui pleaded as follows:

“4. The defendants contend that the plaintiff is estopped in so far as the present action seeks to re-open the case referred to in paragraph 4 of the statement of claim and finally adjudicated upon in the High Court and affirmed in the Court of Appeal.

5. Further and in the alternative the defendants say that the present action is an abuse of process in that the plaintiff seeks, to recover reliefs he could not have recovered in the said judgment.”

Again in paragraph "6A of the composite amended statement of defence in Suit  No.1907/88 filed on the 7/6/95, Turqui again pleaded as follows.

“6A.  the defendants say that the plaintiff is estopped per rem judicatem by the judgment in suit No. 104/75."

It is to be observed that the learned trial judge dismissed Turquis contention that DAHABIEH was estopped per rem judicatem from instituting the action herein.  Concerning the issue of estoppel the learned judge made the following remarks in his judgment.

 

"When the plaintiff got judgment which set aside the recovery of possession obtained by the defendants the latter appealed to the Court of Appeal. The defendants got a stay of execution Exhibit 'A' at the High Court pending the appeal to the Court of Appeal.

The stay of execution was obtained in 1982.   It lasted till 1988 when the Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal. Thus from 1982 to 1988 the plaintiff could not get access to his stock in trade and his goods because of the stay of execution pending the appeal. It was therefore not the plaintiff’s fault that he took action in 1988 and 1989.  By operation of legal process the plaintiff could not have done otherwise but to abide by the result of the appeal. It cannot therefore be said that the action of the plaintiff is statute barred as the defendants would want the court to hold.”

The learned judge went on to say that, “Further more from the careful analyses of the facts and law epitomised above the plaintiff cannot be said to be estopped from bringing his action,”

It can be easily discerned from the judgment that although the learned trial judge claimed in his judgment, that he had carefully analysed the facts and the law, a reading of the judgment does not disclose that he decided the question of estoppel.

In considering the principle of estoppel in Foli and Ors. vrs: AGYA-ATTA & ORS. (Consolidated) (1976) GLR. page 194 at page 197. Amissah, J.A. in delivering the judgment of the Court of Appeal stated:—

“It is a well-known rule of law that a person cannot bring an action where the cause of his claim or the issue which he seeks to have determined has as between the parties or their privies already been disposed of by a competent Court.  This is a statutory rule intended to prevent the harassment of people by a multiplicity of Law suits: Accordingly the rule is framed widely enough to cover not only matters which are actually dealt with in previous judgment but those as well which ought to have been brought up then but which were not”. (The emphasis is mine)

In HENDERSON vrs: HENDERSON (1843) 3 Hare 100 Wigram VC at 114-115 made a very important statement which determined the confines of the plea of re judicata: He said: 

“I believe I state the rule of Court correctly, when I say that, where a given matter becomes the subject of litigation in, and of adjudication by a court of competent jurisdiction the court requires the parties to that litigation to bring forward that whole case and will not (except under special circumstances) permit the same parties to open the same subject of litigation in respect of matter which might have been brought forward as part of the subject in contest but which was not brought forward only because they have, from negligence, inadvertence or even accident omitted part of their case.

The plea of res judicata applies except in special cases, not only to points upon which the Court was actually required by the parties to form an opinion or pronounce a judgment but to every point which properly belonged to the subject of litigation and which the parties, exercising reasonable deligence might have brought forward at the time.”

The above cited principle was applied with approval by the Court of Appeal in Foli vrs: Agya-Atta (Supra).  These cases are being cited to highlight the principle that plea of res judicata covers not only issues, actually decided by the Court but also matters that the parties could have raised in the action.

For as Romer, J. said in Shoe Machinery Company vrs: Cutland (1896) 1 CH page 667 at page 672 and applied in Ojo vrs: Abadie (1955) 15 WACA. 54  “If he were held to be so entitled, I do not see, how there could be any finality of the questions in an action.   According to this contention the defendant might try his case piece meal.   He might raise such objections as he thought convenient and when he was defeated, he might then raise other points at his leisure and might, in that way, try the case piecemeal and so far as I can see extend it over a long period as he pleased.   In my opinion the defendant is not entitled to do that, he is bound to put his whole case before the Court: and if he does not do so, then it is his own fault or misfortune.  He cannot be allowed to put part of his case or to put his case in an incomplete manner.”

In suit No. 152/76 Dahabieh claimed (a) a declaration that the judgment obtained by Turqui was null and void and is vitiated by fraud (b) an order setting aside the said judgment (c) damages (The emphasis is mine).

The High Court presided by Anterkyi, J. gave judgment for Dahabieh and awarded him exemplary damages of ¢100,000 against Turqui.   The judgment of Anterkyi is Exhibited and marked as Exhibit 'B'. The question which begs for answer is why did the learned trial judge award exemplary damages against Turqui (the defendants)?  The answer can be discerned from the latter part of the judgment where his Lordship declared thus:

“(TURQUI) ......... by their vindictive and wrongful conduct, did deceive the Court by portraying to it that (Dahabieh) was within the jurisdiction and thereby caused the issuing of a void writ of possession pursuant to the execution of which his items of furniture and equipments of the store, among other things, were: thrown out and exposed to the elements to his opprobruim and to the degradation of his business. This was at a time, when he owed nothing by way of rent.”

His Lordship gave other reasons for awarding the examplary damages. He said in his judgment "By their unlawful conduct of over-reaching the Court to grant the respective Motions they have ruined the business of the 1st plaintiff (Dahabieh) from the time of the execution in February, 1976 to date—a duration of more than 6 years."  His Lordship went on “The circumstances under which he was thus unlawfully ejected from the premises during his absence were grave: They were a contumelious disregard of his rights which calls for award of exemplary damages as the appropriate remedy"

Not satisfied with the judgment of the High Court the appellants (Turqui) appealed to the Court of Appeal.  The Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal and upholding the award made at the Court below stated “having regard to the treatment given to the Respondent (Dahabieh) by the appellants, (Turqui) and the fraudulent tactics they resorted to, to obtain the judgment and for other reasons adverted to by the trial judge, I think the exemplary damages of ¢100.000 awarded and the order as to cost are all very reasonable and I am not disposed, to vary them.”

In suit No. 1907/88 DAHABIEH claimed all over again" general and special damages for the detention and or conversion of the plaintiff's stock-in-trade and furniture as a result of wrongful execution”. DAHABIEH filed a statement in support of his claim.  In paragraph 6 of the statement of claim, he set out the basis of his claim thus:

“6. On 7th of June, the plaintiff was given judgment against the Defendant whereby the Defendant's action for recovery of possession was set aside and the plaintiff herein was awarded ¢100.000.00 exemplary damages.  The learned judge also ruled that the plaintiff was entitled to bring a separate action to recover loss suffered for damages to his property”. As already indicated one of the reasons given by Anterkyi, J. in awarding exemplary damages in suit No.152/76 was explained thus” I take into consideration the renovations he had made to the premises and accretion to them by the flooring them terrazo and the dismantling by the defendants (Turqui) of the equipment of the store he had made at his own expenses.”

It is observable from the clear words of Anterkyi, J. that in Suit No.152/76 he did not grant the plaintiff (DAHABIEH) liberty to institute a fresh action for general and special damages: Be that as it may, Turqui's plea in Suit No.1897/88 was that the plaintiff Dahabieh claimed inter alia damages for the wrongful execution in suit No.152/76 and the trial judge gave judgment for him and awarded exemplary damages of ¢100.000.00 against Turqui. It was obvious that the damages prayed for by Dahabieh was to compensate him for the losses that he allegedly suffered from the wrongful execution. In other words Dahabieh’s stock-in-trade that was affected, by the execution was part of his claim in Suit No.152/76. Now having made a claim for damages for wrongful execution and having obtained exemplary damages, DAHABIEH is not entitled in Suit No.1907/88 to claim all over again general and special damages inter alia for wrongful execution and I so hold. I further hold that the award made in Suit No.152/76 covered all damages suffered in respect of the wrongful execution and Dahabieh was estopped from re-litigating that issue in Suit No.1907/88.  To employ the language of COUSSEY, J.A. in Ojo vrs: Abadie (1955) 15 WACA. 54 at p.55 ……… “when once it is made clear that the self-same question is substantially in issue in two suits, the precise form in which either suit is brought or the fact that the plaintiff in one case, was the defendant in the other is immaterial , the estoppel subsists between the parties.”

In suit No.453/89, DAHABIEH the plaintiff claimed the very sum of ¢100,000.00 awarded in the judgment of Anterkyi, J. in suit No. 152/76 together with interest. In paragraph 4 of the amended statement of defence Turqui pleaded as follows:

“4. The defendants contend that the plaintiff is estopped in so far as the present action seeks to re-open the case referred to in paragraph 4 of the statement of claim and finally adjudicated upon in the High Court and affirmed by the Court of Appeal.”

In paragraph ‘8’ of the Statement of claim the plaintiff Dahabieh himself conceded that Turqui had paid into Court the judgment debt of ¢100,000.00 and cost long before suit No.453/89 was instituted.  In his judgment in Suit No. 1907/88 the trial judge found that the judgment debt had been paid into Court and yet he proceeded to give the following decision.

"The defendants say they had paid into court on 6/4/89 ¢115,000 being the judgment debt and costs.  This is evidenced by Exhibit 'I'.  Therefore in awarding the damages I will take into consideration the amount paid into Court.  I do award the plaintiff ¢12 million as general damages and one million cedis cost. This award is to attract interest of 5% by virtue of LI.1295 from the date of issuance of the Writ to date of judgment.”

It is note worthy that Anterkyi J, in suit No.152/76 did not award interest obviously because Dahabieh was not entitled.  It is settled that an interest was not for the asking and (see Ghana Commercial Bank vrs. Odoom (1975) 2 GLR. 54) that a plaintiff who claimed interest ought to establish the basis of the claim.  It should be observed that the Courts (Award of Interest) Instrument 1984 (L.I.1295) came into force in 1984 and did not have retroactive effect.

As already pointed out Anterkyi did not award interest in Suit No.152/76 and the Court of Appeal did not consider it relevant when it affirmed Anterkyi's decision in 1988.  I do not think Dahabieh could re-open the matter and pursue his claim for interest subsequently in suit No.453/89.  It is my judgment that Dahabieh’s claims for damages and interest in suit No.453/89 are also res judicata. 

In the light of the matters canvassed above the appellant (Turqui) are entitled to the judgment of the Court.  I would therefore set aside the judgment against the appellant.  The appellant also complained that the judgment of the learned trial judge in suit No.1907/88 was against the weight of evidence. As it will be recalled in the above suit Dahabieh's claim for general and special damages was for detention and conversion of his stock in trade etc. In his judgment however the trial judge found that Dahabieh's action lay in CONVERSION and not DETINUE.

The basis of the trial judge's said finding was stated as follows in his judgment.

“The defendant contend that the goods had always been in legal custody for it was the Deputy Sheriff who had the Keys to the place where the goods had been stored. In this case a demand for the goods cannot be made (a) there was a stay of execution (b) the case was on appeal.  Further the Deputy Sheriff became the agent of the Defendants. His acts were therefore the acts of the Defendants. The Defendants I hold from the above have committed trespass to the good etc. of the Plaintiff and so are liable in conversion and not detinue”

In Halsbury's Laws of England (4 ed) vol.45 paragraph 1422, street on Torts (5ed) at p.35 defines the tort of conversion as:

“an intentional dealing with a chatel which is seriously inconsistent with the possession or right to immediate possession of another person.”

In WINFIELD & JOLOWICZ ON TORT (13ed) at p.471, the learned authors put it:

“Taking possession of another's goods will normally be conversion as well as trespass, but there will be no conversion where the interference is merely temporary and is unaccompanied by any intention to exercise any right over the goods. If I snatch your hat with intent to steal it, that is conversion as well as trespass only, ……… So too, if you shift my bicycle from a public stand in order to get at your own and forget to replace mine so that it is stolen by someone, that may be trespass but not conversion” (The emphasis is mine).

In the instant case the goods were in legal custody and all that was required of Dahabieh was to demand that they be released to him.  There was no evidence that he ever so demanded.  There is no evidence on record that Turqui ever dealt with Dahabieh's goods in manner inconsistent with his right of ownership or title.  It is therefore my judgment that the trial judge erred in holding that Turqui was guilty of conversion.

The ground I want to consider finally is that "The trial judge erred in dismissing the appellants counter-claim” In paragraphs 6B, 6D and 6E of the Counter-claim, Turqui, the plaintiffs to the counter-claim averred.

“6B. The plaintiffs to the counter-claim, say that the premises in issue were let to the 1st defendant to the counter-claim who until 20 September 1972 operated thereat under the name and style Technical Trading Company.

6D. The 1st defendant to the counter-claim has now sublet or otherwise parted with possession to the 2nd Defendant to the Counter-claim herein the said premises without the knowledge and consent of the plaintiffs to the counter-claim.

6E. Wherefore the plaintiffs (Turqui) to the Counter-claim prays against the defendants to the counter-claim, herein jointly or severally an order of (a) recovery of possession and (b) damages for trespass”

The defendants to the counter-claims defence is contained in paragraphs 5 and 6 of the REPLY AND DEFENCE TO COUNTER-CLAIM.

“5. The plaintiff denies that he has now sublet or otherwise parted with possession to the said defendants or anybody else.  Further and in the alternative the plaintiff will ask for strict proof of any document to which the plaintiff is a party which requires prior consent as alleged.  It is contended, that in point of law such a restrictive (sic) if it exists at all will be unreasonable in respect of business tenancies.

6. The above-named Tetra Ghana Limited adopts the Defence to the counter-claim and denies that the plaintiff has sublet any premises to him under the Rent Laws of Ghana or under any other law and hence the counter-claim should be disallowed as misconceived”

In suit No.152/76 Anterkyi, J made the following remarks as to the nature of Dahabieh’s tenancy.   He said as follows in his judgment.

“As the rent was payable with reference to a month, namely ¢240 or ¢540 per month, the tenancy was a monthly tenancy.  At the end of the period Dahabieh became a statutory tenant ………. Therefore, it seems to me that, in the present case, at the expiration of Six month's notice under section 18(1) (h) the 1st plaintiff became a statutory tenant, within the above definition and remained so during the course of the earlier case up to date.

As a statutory tenant the plaintiff necessarily became a tenant from month to month, see section 29(1) of Act. 220”. In this case there is unchallenged evidence that the premises House No.61 Jojo Thompson Road Accra was let to Dahabieh in his personal capacity, then trading as Technical Trading Company.  That there is no written tenancy agreement between the parties is also not in dispute. The relationship between the parties must therefore be governed by the Rent Act.

In his judgment in Suit No.1907/88 Sapong, J.A. stated inter alia: "Plaintiff (Dahabieh) was orally let into the premises.  There is no written agreement to cover the renting of the premises.  The Defendants (Turqui) maintain that they did tell the plaintiff (Dahabieh) not to sublet it without their consent.  This is denied.

There is also no evidence of such COVENANT.  The plaintiff (Dahabieh) carried on business in the said premises under the name and style Technical Trading Company Limited."

He continued by saying that "The Defendants (Turqui) are saying the plaintiff.  (Dahabieh) has breached a COVENANT i.e. not to let the premises to anybody without their consent.  The plaintiff (Dahabieh) says he has not breached any covenant.  He has just changed the name of the business. Besides there were no covenants when the premises was rented out to him.

He who avers must prove. The Defendants (Turqui) who averred a breach of a COVENANT could not substantiate it.  Their representatives the only person who spoke for the Defendants (Turqui) says he got his information about this from their records.  No records were tendered, therefore I do not believe the Defendants. I prefer the account of the plaintiff (Dahabieh) to theirs.” He finally held that there was no breach of a COVENANT.

In his written submission learned counsel for Turqui argued that the learned trial judge failed to appreciate the legal effect of the oral tenancy and therefore erred in holding that there was no proof of the implied term and breach thereof.

 Now section 22(1) of the Rent Act.1960 Act 220 provides.

“22 (1) No person, in any case of a monthly or shorter tenancy or any premises, shall sub-let such premises without written consent of his landlord” The application of section 22(1) of the Rent Act.1963 – is subject to premises as defined in section 36 of the Act which includes every agreement for the letting of any premises, whether oral or otherwise...”

The combined effect of section 22(1) of Act 220 and section 36 of the Act is that whether or not the tenancy agreement is oral the tenant cannot sublet the premises without the written consent of the Landlord.  The prohibition here is absolute.

In this case since a covenant was implied by law the Defendant (Turqui) was not required to lead oral evidence on it and I so hold.  The trial judge therefore erred when he held that Turqui did not prove the said covenant.

There is evidence on record that the premises has been sub-let or is now in possession of Tetra (Ghana) Ltd. without Turqui's consent. Turqui's representative led on unrefuted evidence that Tetra (Ghana) Ltd. was put into the premises without Turqui's consent.

Under cross-examination Dahabieh admitted that the premises was now occupied by Tetra (Ghana) Ltd.  He however explained that since he is the majority shareholder of Tetra (Ghana) Ltd he did not need the consent of the Landlord (Turqui) to put Tetra (Ghana) Ltd in possession.  It is to be observed that Technical Trading Company is not the same as Tetra (Ghana) Ltd. The Law is that the Shareholders of a Company are different from the Company (See SALOMON vrs: SALOMON CO. LTD. (1897) AC.22.

It is my judgment that Dahabieh being a tenant from month to month was required by law to seek the previous written consent of the appellants (Turqui) before subletting the premises to the 2nd respondent (Tetra (Ghana) Ltd). Having breached the COVENANT the appellants (Turqui) were entitled to recover possession under section 17(1) (b) of Act 220 and therefore learned trial judge went wrong in dismissing Turqui's counter-claim.

In the result the appeal is allowed. The judgment of trial court in respect of the Dahabieh claims against Turqui is set aside and judgment given in favour of Turqui. In respect of the counter-claim the judgment of the Court below which dismissed it is hereby set aside and judgment is given in favour of Turqui on his Counter-claim. An Order for recovery of possession of the premises is made in favour of the Appellants. (Turqui).

A. ESSILFIE-BONDZIE
