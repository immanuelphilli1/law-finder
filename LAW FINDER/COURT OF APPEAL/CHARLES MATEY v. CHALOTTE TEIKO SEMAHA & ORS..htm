ARYEETEY, J.A.

A brief background to this appeal is as follows: The late Comfort Mateko was the sister of the late Yodjo who died in 1953 leaving behind his four children namely the plaintiff/appellant and his three sisters. Comfort Mateko, whose only child was the first defendant, took care of her deceased sister’s children and played the role of their foster mother. It is the case of the plaintiff that before the death of Yodjo, his mother, the late Comfort Mateko and her late mother had jointly purchased a piece of land on which they built three rooms. The late Mateko customarily adopted the plaintiff and his siblings as her own children. Her adopted children helped her in her trading and she was able to increase the rooms in the house to seven with the plaintiff helping physically with the construction work. Thereafter the late Mateko lived in the house with the plaintiff and his sisters until 1987 when she suffered a stroke, which stopped her from engaging in any gainful employment. It is the case of the plaintiff that when the late Comfort Mateko lay on sick bed the first defendant connived with her son, the late Winfred Ofoe Semaha, who was the original second defendant and sold the house in dispute to the third defendant without the knowledge and consent of the head and all members of their family.

The family got to know of the fraudulent transaction in November, 1992 and that prompted the head of the family and two principal members of the family to approach the late Mateko on her sick bed and questioned her about the sale of the house. She expressed surprise and denied any knowledge about sale of the house. When the first defendant was asked to give explanation for her conduct she could not do so. When Comfort Mateko died the family had the information that the house in dispute had indeed been sold to the third defendant. Meanwhile the plaintiff was appointed successor to the late Comfort Mateko at a general meeting of the family and authorised to write to the third defendant about his unlawful occupation of the house in dispute. The third defendant refused to accept the letter on the basis that he had nothing to do with the plaintiff's head of family. The plaintiff on his own behalf and on behalf of his family first took action against the third defendant for Declaration of Title and Recovery of possession of House No. B.20, Asesewa as depicted in the proceedings of the Somanya District Magistrate Grade 1 Court recorded at page 119 of the record of appeal. The Statement of Claim, which the plaintiff filed in that case reads:

"1. Plaintiff is the eldest son of the late Comfort Mateko Matey the original owner of House Number B 20 at Asesewa and lives at Manyakpongunor.

2. The defendant is a Northerner and presently living at Asesewa.

3. Comfort Mateko now deceased was a mother of many descendants living in various places including House No. B 20, Asesewa. That she died in January, 1993 at Manyakpongunor.

4. That defendant is never a member of the Matey Oddah family of which Comfort Mateko formed part but has now trespassed into the house and started renovation on the house.

5. That when the family noticed the defendants trespass into the house, the plaintiff was empowered to write a warning to the defendant and invite him to appear before the family so as to know how the defendant got into the house.

6. That the letter was accordingly written and served on the defendant by the plaintiff, the defendant refused accepting the letter saying that he has no letter writing with the family.

7. That by defendants behaviour I believe the same dubious deal might have happened or is happening somewhere that if steps are not taken to check the situation it will cause a very great harm to the family and mostly the plaintiff "

The defendant who happens to be the third defendant/respondent in this appeal filed a defence denying the claim of the plaintiff. An interesting development in the case was an application for joinder as co-defendant by Emmanuel Narh Matey who described himself as head of the plaintiff's family. His affidavit in support of the application for joinder is as follows:

“I, Emmanuel Narh Matey of Manya-Kpongunor make oath and say as follows:—

1. That I am the deponent herein.

2. That I have got to know of this Suit (Counsel will refer to all the processes filed)

3. That I am the head of the Matey Oddah family of Manya-Kpongunor on whose behalf the plaintiff claims he brings this action.

4. That the plaintiff is not the head of family and he has not my authority to bring this action.

5. That I know one Comfort Mateko who was my sister sold the house, House No. B 20, Asesewa, subject matter of this suit to the defendant.

6. That the said house was the self acquired property of Comfort Mateko. That the action is brought in bad faith,

7. That my title as the head of family is at stake in this suit”.

The record of proceedings of the Somanya District Court Grade 1 dated 28th April, 1994 shows at page 120 of the record of appeal that after the application had been moved on behalf of the applicant the plaintiff's response was to inform the court that he had brought to court one Otseame Donkor, an elder in his family who wished to attempt an out of court settlement in the case. Applicant's counsel objected to what he perceived as interference in the action "because all along the plaintiff has been disturbing the peace of mind of the defendant in the enjoyment of the property.” Subsequently the court ruled that "As the plaintiff has expressed the intention of himself and elders of settling this action outside court, then the action is otiose and hereby struck out.” The court awarded ¢30,000.00 costs in favour of the defendant. On 2nd April, 1996 after the death of the head of family, Emmanuel Narh Matey the plaintiff/appellant as “successor of the late Comfort Mateko suing for himself and on behalf of Yodjo descendants” filed a writ of summons in Odumase-Krobo circuit court claiming for the following reliefs:

“(a) Declaration of title to House No. B 20 at Asesewa, which 1st and 2nd defendants unlawfully sold to the 3rd defendant without the prior consent and knowledge of the head of family.

(b) An order of the court to declare the sale of the house No. B 20, Asesewa which 1st and 2nd defendants sold unlawfully to 3rd defendant, null and void.

(c) An order of the court to compel the 3rd defendant to return all documents pertaining to House No. B 20, Asesewa to the plaintiff.

(d) Perpetual Injunction restraining the defendants, their agents, workmen or servants from interfering with the House No. B 20, Asesewa.

(e) General Damages.”

Meanwhile the second defendant died and upon application his sister, Ruth Ogbo Semaha was substituted as the second defendant which is not reflected in the title of the case. Also the first defendant could not testify in court on account of ill health.

The defendants’ version of the case is that the late Comfort Mateko did not buy the plot of land jointly with the plaintiff’s mother, Yodjo. Also, they deny Comfort Mateko ever adopting the plaintiff and his three sisters as her children even though she brought them up and cared for them. She built the rooms in the house, which she never shared among her daughter, the plaintiff and his three sisters. It was the late Comfort Mateko herself who sold the house in dispute to the third defendant who immediately went into possession and carried out extensive renovation works. The third defendant counterclaimed for Declaration of Title and “Perpetual Injunction against the plaintiff and his agents, assigns, workman etc. from interfering with the said house No. B 20 Asesewa in whatsoever manner.”

The trial circuit court dismissed the plaintiff’s claim in its entirety and gave the third defendant judgment in respect of his counterclaim. The plaintiff appealed and filed three grounds of appeal namely:

i.  The judgment is against the weight of evidence.

ii. The learned trail judge was biased and wrong in preferring the evidence of Ruth Ogbo Semaha and Rev. W.K. Semaha, not members of the plaintiff’s family to that of the head of family, George Mate.

iii. The learned judge was wrong in admitting exhibit 3, which was not pleaded and relying heavily on it.

His additional grounds of appeal are so follows:

(a) The learned judge was wrong and biased in accepting exhibit 4, described as conveyance, which was not registered and thumb printed without a jurat.

(b) The learned judge showed bias when he declared in his judgment as follows: “How much can a kenkey seller make in a town like Asesewa to enable her feed herself and leave some money to undertake a building project”.

(c) The learned trial judge was wrong in law then he held that H/No. B 20, Asesewa which was sold to the 3rd defendant was valid, in the face of the overwhelming evidence on record that the rooms in the house were shared to Plaintiff and his three sisters and the 1st defendant for which customary 'aseda' was provided.

(d) The learned trial judge erred in law when he relied on a report which was incorporated in the judgment when the same did not form part of the evidence.

(e) The learned trial judge was wrong when he relied on the situation report, the authenticity of which was doubtful.

I would follow the same order pursued by counsel for the appellant in his written statement of the appellant's case. I take ground (ii) of the original grounds of appeal first, namely: “The learned trial judge was biased and wrong in preferring the evidence of Ruth Ogbo Semaha and Rev. W.K. Semaha, not members of the plaintiff’s family to that of the head of family, George Mate.” In his written submissions counsel for the appellant sounds as if the case was pursued along family ownership of the property in dispute. However, a cursory look at the description of the plaintiff in the writ of summons reveals that he portrays himself as “successor of the late Comfort Mateko suing for himself and on behalf of Yodjo descendants”. That means the interest of his family is not as stake. So far as he was concerned those who have interest in the disputed property are himself, his siblings and their descendants. He did not include the only daughter of the late Comfort Mateko and her descendants. In any case what the witnesses mentioned in the ground (ii) of the grounds of appeal related to ownership of the land by the late Comfort Mateko and the sale by her. In his judgment the learned circuit judge took time to examine the testimonies of all the witnesses before him in relation to the issues for trial. He gave ample reasons why he preferred the evidence of the defendants' witnesses to that of the plaintiff's witnesses. At pages 105 and 106 of the record of appeal we see the carefulness with which the learned circuit judge examined the evidence of the plaintiff's witnesses before he came out with his conclusions. He states in his judgment as follows:

"Mr. George Mate, P.W.3 who described himself as head of family said in his evidence that both Comfort Mateko and Yodjo jointly bought and put up a two room house on it. He said before they put up the house they offered a sheep and a bottle of Schnapps to the Matey Oddah family that they were putting the house under the care of the family. The family accepted the offer and slaughtered the sheep. After the two rooms have been put up the family divided the rooms and gave one to Mateko and another to Yodjo. He stated that Yodjo died not long afterwards. According to P.W.3 the house in dispute is family property.

I think that I would like to state here and now that there is no evidence that the property in dispute is family property. I do not believe the evidence of P.W.3 that as soon as Mateko and Yodjo put up the house they gave it to the family and that from that day the structure became a family house. P.W.3 did not state why Mateko and Yodjo found it necessary to give the house to the family. If they did that then it is most unusual. I do not however believe that Mateko and Yodjo, women with children would do that. I do not believe that the two rooms or three rooms, which were allegedly put up by Mateko and Yodjo were divided by the family and each given one room to occupy. I believe that the evidence of P.W.3 is a fabrication and I reject it as unworthy of consideration. Again there is conflicting evidence as to the number of rooms, which were initially put up. While P.W.2 stated in his evidence that three rooms were initially put up P.W.3. stated that only two rooms were constructed…

The evidence is however indisputable that the land was bought by Comfort Mateko alone, Exhibit B, which is a receipt, indicates that the late Mateko paid an amount of 9 pounds sterling as the value of the land and it was paid to Tei Bigoe on 17/11/53. The name Yodjo was not indicated on the receipt as joint purchaser. When the plaintiff’s attention was drawn to this during cross-examination he only said that that cannot be true and if a receipt like that exists then Madam Mateko’s name was used in the receipt because she was senior to Yodjo. Plaintiff also said that Yodjo’s name was excluded from the receipt because it was issued after her death. I am afraid the above explanation cannot be accepted having regard to the evidence on record. According to the plaintiff Yodjo died in 1958. According to exhibit 3 the receipt was issued on 17/11/53. if Yodjo died in 1953 then this receipt was issued five years before she died. The plaintiff’s evidence that the receipt covering the land was issued after the death of Yodjo cannot be true. The plaintiffs evidence that the land was acquired jointly by Madam Mateko and Yodjo cannot therefore also be true.”

I am of the view therefore that in evaluating the evidence adduced at the trial the learned circuit judge did not look at the personalities involved and their relationship with the family of the plaintiff. As expected what engaged his attention was the truthfulness of the witnesses or otherwise in the light of circumstances of the family of the late Comfort Mateko as well as the undisputed documentary evidence respecting the sale of the land. The conclusions of the learned circuit judge were not actuated by bias and right in every respect and they should not therefore be disturbed.

We now move on to ground (iii) of the grounds of appeal namely: “The learned judge was wrong in admitting exhibit 3, which was not pleaded and relying heavily on it.” In his submissions apart from pointing out that exhibit 3 was not pleaded learned counsel for the appellant did not demonstrate how that affected the conclusions of the court below. I would do well to reproduce the relevant part of the written submissions of the appellant’s counsel appearing at page 4 of his written submissions as follows:

“Exhibit 3 purported to be the document purporting to transferring the interest of the one Teye Biyoe to one Mateko. Exhibit 3 appears on page 124 of the record of proceedings. Exhibit 3 was issued to Madam Mateko of Dorm, Nuaso. The person Mateko who is the center of the dispute was Comfort Mateko of Manya Kpongunor. There is no evidence that Comfort Mateko ever lived at Dorm Nuaso. It is submitted that if the trial judge had examined exhibit 3 critically he would not have arrived at the conclusion that the name on the document referred to Madam Comfort Mateko.”

With due deference to learned counsel for the appellant his above submission in respect of the content of exhibit 3 defies logic. In the first place the document was supposed to have come from the late Comfort Mateko herself. Even if we go by the plaintiff’s version that the first defendant, taking advantage of her mother’s illness and her custody of the box containing the belongings of her late mother discovered documents on the house, which she in connivance with the second defendant sold to the third defendant/respondent, we are still faced with the fact that exhibit 3 came from the late Comfort Mateko herself. Therefore the description of the late Comfort Mateko on exhibit 3 does not in any affect the document’s relevance to the third defendant’s counterclaim, the basis of which is that he purchased the house from the late Mateko and obtained exhibit 3 from her. On the description of Mateko as “from Dorm, Nuaso” there is no evidence that the late Mateko never lived in that area. Therefore it would not be reasonable to come to the conclusion that the suggestion in exhibit 3 that she lived in a place other than Manya Kpongunor meant that she was not the person described in the document even though she had the document in her possession.

The next ground of appeal tackled by counsel for the appellant is Additional Ground (a), i.e. “The learned judge was wrong and biased in accepting exhibit 4, described as conveyance, which was not registered and thumb printed without a jurat.” The defendants led evidence as to why the late Mateko decided to sell the house, the decision to sell the house and the steps that were taken by the late Comfort Mateko for the sale to be effected. The substituted second defendant, Ruth Semaha testified as D.W.2. She was a grand daughter of the late Comfort Mateko and a daughter of the first defendant. According to her evidence her grand mother was paralysed because of stroke and she took care of her. Her grand mother faced financial difficulties and when a tenant in her Asesewa house, Alhaji Issah visited her at Manyakpongunor she offered to sell the house to him. Since Alhaji Issah was not in the position to buy the house the late Mateko asked him to look for a buyer. Two weeks later Alhaji came with the third defendant and other men who were all Hausa people. Alhaji Issah introduced the third defendant as the man who was interested in the purchase of the house. The agreed price of the house was ¢500,000.00. The late Mateko sent the late Ofoe, the original second defendant, to Agomanya to call in a letter writer, which he did. The Letter writer was asked to prepare the document evidencing the sale of the land by the late Mateko to the third defendant. When the letter writer brought the prepared document, he read the contents to the late Mateko who collected it from the letter writer and gave it to the late Ofoe to read. After that the late Mateko thumb printed it and so did the third defendant. Four other persons who accompanied the third defendant also thumb printed the document. The first defendant thumb printed it while the late Ofoe signed it. Finally a copy of a photograph of the late Mateko was given to the letter writer who pasted it on the document. The document did not have a jurat on the face of it. The evidence of Ruth Semaha found support with the testimonies of Alhaji Issah and the third defendant who were present at the sale. In the case of Kotokoli v. Sarbali reported in [1981] G.L.R. 497 it is recorded in the head notes as follows:

“The general rule of evidence was that where an illiterate executed a document, any other party to the document who relied upon it should prove that it was read over and if necessary interpreted to the illiterate. That was a rule based mainly on common sense. Evidence should be preserved of the fact that illiterate natives who were parties to conveyances had the contents of such conveyances clearly interpreted to them in their own native language and that they fully appeared to comprehend the nature and effect of the transactions evidenced by the document. However where the document does not bear a jurat on the face of it but the party who executed it did not object either to it or the transaction which it sought to evidence, the general rule should not be allowed to defeat the document on the mere technical ground of lack of a jurat. In the instant case, the receipt which counsel sought to avoid was issued by one illiterate to another and it bore no jurat but it could be inferred from the evidence that the appellant understood and fully appreciated its meaning and effects and he was therefore bound by its contents.

Waya v. Byrouthy (1958) 3 W.A.L.R. 413; Dadzie and Boateng v. Kokofu [1961] G.L.R. 90 and Youhana v. Abboud [1973] 1 G.L.R. 258 cited.”

In the instant appeal there is every indication on the face of the record that the late Mateko fully appreciated the meaning and effect of the document, which evidenced the sale transaction. She gave all documents respecting her ownership of the house to the third defendant/respondent after she had received payment of the price of the house. The respondent was allowed to go in to possession with Alhaji Issah, the former tenant of the late Mateko becoming his tenant. The late Mateko did not challenge his ownership and possession of the house until her death in 1993. It would therefore not be proper at this stage to allow the absence of jurat on the document to defeat the ends of justice.

Ground (b) of the Additional Grounds of Appeal reads: The learned judge showed bias when he declared in his judgment as follows: “How much can a kenkey seller make in a town like Asesewa to enable her feed herself and leave some money to undertake a building project”. I think we would be in a better position to appreciate the import of the above quotation from the judgment of the circuit court if we are made to see the context in which the statement was made. I would therefore reproduce a relevant portion of the judgment appearing at page 107 of the record of appeal as follows:

“The plaintiff tried to suggest to the court that his sister worked with Comfort Mateko and there by in various ways contributed to the continuation of the house. He said that Mercy Matey, Dandjie assisted and sold kenkey and Adjo assisted Mateko to sell cloth and that it was from these trading activities that Mateko was able to extend the house to seven rooms.

I do not believe that the two sisters of the plaintiff made any contribution toward the continuation of the house. How much can a kenkey seller make in a town like Asesewa to enable her feed herself and leave some money to undertake a building project. I do not believe that they are capable of making any financial contribution towards the continuation of the house. If however they helped in any way then it was because they were staying with Comfort Mateko and were being fed by her and so they might have carried sand or water while the construction was taking place. They do not thereby [become] joint owners of the house with Comfort Mateko”

Looking at the context within which the statement complained of was made by the learned circuit judge I do not think that that evidence bias on his part.

I would like to take the last two grounds of appeal argued by counsel for the appellant together, namely ground (c) of the Additional Grounds of Appeal and ground (i) of the original Grounds of Appeal. Ground (c) of the Additional Grounds of Appeal reads: “The learned trial judge was wrong in law when he held that H/No. B 20, Asesewa which was sold to the 3rd defendant was valid, in the face of the overwhelming evidence on record that the rooms in the house were shared to plaintiff and his three sisters and the 1st defendant for which customary ‘aseda’ was provided.” Ground (i) of the original grounds of appeal also reads: “The judgment is against the weight of evidence.” I must say at this stage that Ground (i) of the original grounds of appeal has been looked at in some respect as we considered other grounds of appeal in this judgment. All the same we can still look at some other aspects of that ground. The conclusion of the learned circuit judge is that the house in dispute is not family property but the self acquired property of the late Comfort Mateko. In his bid to establish that the house in dispute was family property and not the self acquired property of the late Mateko the plaintiff led evidence to show that before the death of Mateko she had allotted rooms in the house to the sisters of the plaintiff and two uncompleted rooms to the plaintiff as gift in respect of which they provided the customary “aseda”. The gift of the rooms to the plaintiff and his siblings was supposed to have been preceded by the help and support the plaintiff and his sisters gave to the late Mateko in building four more rooms in addition to the existing ones. The circuit court did not accept the evidence in that regard as reliable. It must not be forgotten that the focus of that line of evidence was aimed at establishing that the house in dispute is family property. As demonstrated in the plaintiff’s Statement Claim in the case against the third defendant/respondent before the District Court, Grade 1, Somanya, the plaintiff made admission of the fact that the late Mateko was the original owner of the house in dispute. The plaintiff alleged in that Statement of Claim that he was authorised by the family to proceed with the case. As demonstrated by the proceedings of the Somanya District Court quoted above, when one Emmanuel Narh Matey applied to be joined as co-defendant in that case and his counsel actually moved the Motion for Joinder the plaintiff interrupted the proceeding on the pretext that the elders of the family would settle the case. The case was struck out and the third defendant, the defendant in that case was awarded costs. Since there is no evidence in the record of appeal that the plaintiff did anything to facilitate settlement in that case I am inclined to come to the conclusion that his intervention in the application was a ploy to avert the determination of the applicant, Emmanuel Narh Matey to bring the truth to light. After all if the plaintiff did not accept the challenge that the applicant for joinder was the head of family and that the plaintiff had no backing of the family to take action against the defendant in that case, who had purchased the self acquired property of the late Mateko prior to her death, he could at least have filed an affidavit in opposition. In my view he calculatingly misled the district court to strike out the case when he had no intention to take steps to facilitate settlement in the case. No doubt he took action before the Circuit Court sometime after Emmanuel Narh Matey had died. I am of the view that the circuit court came to the right conclusion that the house in dispute was self-acquired property of the late Comfort Mateko, which she sold to the third defendant/appellant. For the reasons given in this judgment the appeal is dismissed as being devoid merit.

B.T. ARYEETEY
