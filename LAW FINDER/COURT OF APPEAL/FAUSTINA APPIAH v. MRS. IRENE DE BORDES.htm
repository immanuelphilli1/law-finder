JUDGMENT

ARYEETEY, J.A.:

A brief background to this appeal is as follows: The late Mr. Harold Smith Boatin, the testator herein was married to Mrs. Rebecca Boatin in 1939 and had children by her, one of whom is the defendant/respondent. Due to the illness of his wife the couple were separated. The late Harold Boatin who was also known as Papa Boatin cohabited with the plaintiff/appellant in House number 32, Kwadaso and had two children by her. The appellant maintains that the relationship between her and the late Harold Boatin was that of husband and wife under customary marriage. Before his death on 8th March 1988, the late Papa Boatin approached a lawyer by name Mr. J. A. Darko to prepare a will for him. After the testator had approved the draft of the said will the lawyer caused it to be typed by his secretary, Anna Ewusi-Amoako (PW4). He then asked his secretary to take the completed will to the testator for execution to be carried out. Upon direction from the secretary the testator invited two witnesses who arrived at his residence to play the role of attesting witnesses. The will, exhibit A was duly executed. After the death of the testator one of the executors named in the will applied for probate of the said will only to be confronted with a caveat from the respondent. The caveat was based on the following grounds: (a) That the signature on the said will was not that of the testator, (b) That the late testator lacked the mental capacity to have given instructions for the making of his Will neither did he give instructions to the lawyer for the making of the purported Will.

Consequently the plaintiff as beneficiary under the will was called upon to prove the will in solemn form. On 21st October, 1988 she filed a writ of summons before the Kumasi High Court claiming the following reliefs: “(a) A Declaration that the LAST WILL AND TESTAMENT of the late HAROLD SMITH Boatin made on the 8th day of February, 1988 is valid. (b) An order that the plaintiff is as against the defendant, entitled to a grant of probate of the said WILL of the said deceased person who died on the 8th of March, 1988. (c) Any other and further orders as are in the circumstances of this case, just in terms of Order 63 Rule 6. The issues that were spelt out for the determination before the trial court as listed in the summons for the directions were as follows: (1) Whether or not the last Will and Testament of the late Harold Smith Boatin dated the 8th of February, 1988, is valid or was his act or deed. (2) Whether or not the late testator had the testamentary capacity to make or execute the said will. (3) Whether or not the plaintiff is entitled to the reliefs claimed by her.

In her judgment the learned trial judge looked at the evidence adduced on both sides respecting the mental capacity of the testator and concluded as follows:

“In determining whether a testator had the mental capacity to make a will, the law required that there should be a sound disposing mind both at the time when the instructions for the will were given and when the will was executed. See the case of IN RE COLE (Decd): CUDGOE VRS. COLE, [1977] 2 G.L.R.315. According to PW5, the deceased gave him instructions on 6/2/88, a day after DW1 had seen him and said nothing about his condition. On that same 5/2/88, the deceased wrote exhibit CW1, a letter to the Regional Manager, State Housing Corporation, telling him of the loss of his lease in respect of H/No. E32, Kwadaso. In that letter he asked for a duplicate copy of the lease to be made available to him at his earliest convenience. He notified him of a change in his former address.

I have looked at the letter and I am unable to accept the assertion that the deceased person’s mental capacity was in any way impaired at the time… In the case of BANKS vrs. GOODFELLOW (1870) L.R.5 Q.B.549 at 564 referred to in In Re Cole, Cockburn, C.J. in his illuminating judgment laid down a test for determining mental capacity to make a will. He said and I quote:

‘It is essential to the exercise of such a power that a testator shall understand the nature of the act and the effects, shall understand the nature of the property of which he is disposing, shall be able to understand and appreciate the claims to which he ought to give effect, and with a view to the latter object, that no disorder of the mind shall poison affections, perverse his sense of right, or prevent the exercise of his natural faculties.’ ”

Even though the learned trial judge was of the view that the testator was of sound mind at the time that he was supposed to have executed the will, she came to the conclusion that the signature on the will exhibit A did not come from him. She was of the view that the signature on the will differed materially from the known signatures appearing on various documents.

In evaluating the evidence of the expert witness the learned judge reminded herself of the decision in the Court of Appeal case of Sasu v. White Cross Insurance Co. Ltd. [1960] G.L.R. 4, to the effect that expert evidence is to be received with care. At page 83 of the record of appeal the learned judge expressed her views in the following words:

“Counsel submitted that in the light of the evidence led by the plaintiff in proof of due execution of the will, the burden shifted onto the defendant to prove the contrary. In this case does it appear on the face of the WILL that it was executed by the deceased? Looking at the signature on the face of the WILL, there is something which is conspicuous and which does not appear in any of the known signatures of the deceased. There is a line beneath the signature on the WILL.  This line does not appear on any of the documents with the admitted signatures of the deceased. In a letter written by the deceased to the Regional Manager of State Housing Corporation dated 5/2/88, three days before the will was allegedly executed, this line does not appear. CW1, the handwriting expert to whom the signature on exhibit A and other known signatures of the deceased were sent for comparison, told the court in his report, exhibit CW6 that in his opinion the deceased did not sign exhibit A. He had arrived at that conclusion because of the dissimilarities, which he detected as indicated in exhibit CW5.

Admittedly, such evidence of an expert is to be received with reserve and does not absolve or preclude a judge from forming his own opinion on the evidence as a whole. The handwriting expert was a court witness. He was not called by any of the parties. He gave me no cause to believe that he came to court to fight a cause or with a biased mind… I have considered his opinion in the light of the totality of the evidence and I am inclined to accept it as representing the truth of the matter. I therefore find as a fact that the signature on exhibit A is not that of the deceased.”

The appellant filed three grounds of appeal as follows: (1) That the judgment is against the weight of evidence adduced. (2) That the learned trial judge erred in law when she preferred the expert opinion of the court witnesses to the evidence of PW1, PW3 and PW4 (the attesting eyewitnesses) which was corroborated in the relevant material issue of the signature of the testator, by the evidence of DW3, the testator’s brother of the full blood. (3) That the learned trial judge therefore erred in law when she pronounced against the validity of the said will. I intend to take all the three grounds together since they are closely interrelated. It is the submission of the appellant’s counsel in the main that since the evidence of the witnesses, including the attesting witnesses to the execution, who were present at the time of the execution of the will, was not effectively challenged the learned trial judge should have at least given some explanation as to why she did not take that into account in arriving at her conclusion that what appears on exhibit A is not the signature of the testator.

Having come to the conclusion that the testator had the mental capacity to make the will the remaining all important issue to determine was whether or not the last Will and Testament of the late Harold Smith Boatin dated the 8th of February, 1988, was valid or was his act or deed. The plaintiff called as her witnesses the two attesting witnesses, Kwadwo Owusu Banahene (PW1) and Daniel Quainoo (PW3) as well as Anna Ewusi-Amoako (PW4) who typed the will upon the instructions of the lawyer whom the testator engaged to make his will. What came out clearly in their evidence is that all three of them were present at the same time when the late Papa Boatin signed the will followed by the two attesting witnesses who signed. The first to sign after the testator was PW1 followed by PW3. When the three witnesses were cross-examined they stood their ground.

At page 83 of his book, Law of Wills in Ghana, Mr. Justice Azu Crabbe, explains the effect of attestation as follows:

“Where the document propounded as a will appears to be duly signed and witnessed in accordance with the requirements of section 2 of the Wills Act, 1971 (Act 360) unless the contrary is alleged the maxim omnia presumumtur rite esse acta applies, unless the contrary is alleged, and thus shifting the burden of proof on the person propounding it”.

It is obvious that the requirements of proper form and due execution are no mere technicalities. They form part of the substantive law and they are “the first line of defence against fraud upon the dead”: see In the Estate of Fuld (No.3); Hartley v. Fuld [1968] P.675 at p. 719. I quite agree with counsel for the appellant that the learned trial judge should have at least commented on the testimonies of the attesting witnesses PW1 and PW3 as well as the evidence of PW4. Even though the testimonies of the three witnesses tended to show beyond doubt that the will, exhibit A was duly executed, they were completely ignored by the trial judge. When the plaintiff successfully adduced evidence to show that exhibit A was duly signed and executed the burden certainly shifted on the defendant. 

Alhaji Bukari Yakubu, CW1, testified as court witness. His evidence related to the genuineness or otherwise of the testator’s signature. The effect of his report is that the late Papa Boatin did not sign exhibit A. The conclusion reached by the expert witness was preferred to that of the evidence coming from the three witnesses who were supposed to be present when the will was executed. The complaint of the appellant’s counsel is that the trial court completely disregarded the evidence of eyewitnesses to the execution of the will and relied on alleged discrepancies in the known signatures of the testator and the one appearing on exhibit A.

I have taken sometime to examine the enlarged versions of the four known signatures appearing at page 105 of the record of appeal and I notice a short line appearing underneath the letter ‘S’ contained in the disputed signature. That line does not appear in any of the known signatures as observed by the learned trial judge. I have also had a good look at the four known signatures of Papa Boatin at page 105 of the record of appeal. The ‘o’ and ‘a’ in Boatin appearing in exhibits 1 and 2 stand separately. On the other hand the ‘o’ and ‘a’ in Boatin appearing in exhibits AB and BN are joined together by lines at their tops. In case of exhibit AB the line is longer and stretches from the initial ‘B’ and passes over the top of ‘o’ and continues to ‘a’. That demonstrates that the signatures in the ‘known’ and undisputed column are not identical. In my opinion, therefore, it is dangerous to base any conclusion as to the authenticity of the signature of the testator appearing in exhibit A on supposed discrepancies or otherwise, which were not adequately explained in the short testimony of the expert witness, rather than the credible evidence of witnesses who were supposed to be present at the time the will was executed and two of whom duly attested to the signature of the testator. 

The three witnesses testified that they actually saw the late Boatin append his signature to exhibit A followed by signatures of the two attesting witnesses. Their testimony does not encounter any effective challenge from the defendant. I am therefore inclined to agree with counsel for the appellant that the learned trial judge erred in ignoring their evidence and relying solely on the alleged discrepancies appearing in the signatures. I will therefore allow the appeal and set aside the judgment of the trial court. I give judgment in favour of the appellant in respect of reliefs (a) and (b) of her claim.

B.T. ARYEETEY

JUSTICE OF APPEAL

WOOD, JA:

 I agree with the very well reasoned judgment of my brother and would only add these few words. The principal relief sought by the appellant, was for a declaration that the disputed will of 8th February 1998 was valid. At the court below, the respondents raised several objections to the validity of the alleged will, one of them being that those whose names appear on the purported will as attesting witnesses did not subscribe it at the request of, and in the presence of the deceased nor did the deceased sign in their presence. The gentlemen themselves gave evidence to the contrary and indeed there is no evidence on the record to discredit them. The trial judge herself took the position, and rightly so in my view, that their prima facie evidence was unassailable, hence her conclusion that:

“ The plaintiff who is seeking to rely on the will led evidence to establish its due execution”. 

The respondents evidence that they did not subscribe to the will at his request was intended to be inferred from their main contention that the signature is a complete forgery I shall therefore determine that issue shortly. If the answer to that question turns out to be in the positive, then of course their claim will naturally fall flat on its face.

The next complaint is that the deceased was so sick as to be unable to give instructions for a will and execute same. On this issue, the trial judge made no finding one way or the other, although she did review the evidence on both sides of this legal divide. We are therefore at this rehearing under a duty to determine that question on the basis of the available evidence. The law is that, where the sanity of a testator is impeached, the burden of proving the necessary testamentary capacity, lies on the propounder of the will. It must be proved clearly that the testators mind was sufficiently clear when he made his will or when he gave instructions for its preparation, vide In re: Sackity Decd 1982-82 GLR 12. On this issue, again, the appellant relied not only on her evidence, but that of the Pw1, Pw2, Pw3, Pw4 and Pw5, who prepared the will. The learned trial judge appears to have placed very little value on the evidence in particular of the Pw5, for the reason that all he could tell the court was that the deceased was in good condition at the time he gave the instruction. Of course she was expecting him to speak pointedly about his mental condition. I however think that her judgment on the Pw5 was rather harsh and not in the least supported by the record. My reasons are that he did more than speak about his physical health or condition. He unmistakably testified about his state of mind both at the time he gave instructions and when he read through the draft and approved of it. I reproduce a portion of his testimony. “I prepared the will on the instructions of the late Boatin and at the time he was of sound disposing mind. He could remember events which took place in 1987 and told me about them when I took the draft to him for him to read through.” 

Earlier on in his evidence, he had given a detailed description of the process they i.e. the testator and himself, went through in the preparation of the draft will. According to him, he even walked him to a distance to pick a taxi back home. The next day saw him at the testator’s house with the draft which he read through and approved of. Two days before these events the testator had written the clearly unimpeachable Exhibit Cw1. The respondent had alleged in her pleadings that, a serious illness on the 27th December has affected him so mentally and physically as to deprive him of his testamentary capacity. I however find no evidence in proof of these material allegations. On the contrary, when questioned about her condition as around the 30th of December, her answers negate any suggestion that he was mentally and physically unwell. I reproduce her answer. 

Q.     When your father left your house on 30/12/87, he was reasonably well 

A.      He was slightly better.

Q.      At the time your father left your house he had not suffered a stroke

A.      No he had not.”

Why must we reject these piece of cogent evidence in preference to the Dw1’s testimony which the court found to be baseless, in view particularly of the Exh. Cw1 and the fact that he did not see him either on the 8th of February, and did not also indicate from his report that he was unwell when he was with him on the 5th?

Additionally, the sum total of the respondents main objections to the claim as are contained in the paragraphs 6, 8, 9, 10, 11 and 12 of the amended statement of defence filed on the 26th November, 1992 is that the will is a forgery. I would like to produce the relevant portions of the paragraphs 8, 10 and 11.

8  “The defendant says that through the concubinage the deceased impressed upon the plaintiff that the Patasi property plot no 32, Kwadaso Estate, Kumasi would be a gift to his children by his wife Mrs. Rebecca Boatin who together with one of the children had paid so much of the price thereof.

10  The defendant says further that the purported will

a.   Failed to benefit persons such as one of the deceased’s daughters, Barbara, who by reason of her condition was entitled to be an object of her bounty whilst it benefited the plaintiff who could not be an object of the deceased’s bounty.

b.   Spelt at least one word in a manner in which the deceased would not have spelt his name.

c.    Purported to dispose of property which known to the deceased did not belong to him

11.   “The defendant contends that by reason of the alleged matters above he did not know and approve of the contents of the purported will, did not have testamentary capacity at the material time, did not give instructions for the preparation of the purported will, the contents of which were known to the plaintiff even before the said will was read.

12. The defendant says the purported will does not bear the true signature of the deceased; and it was not signed by him.

True the respondents did not expressly allege nor charge the appellant with forgery, and understandably therefore no particulars are pleaded thereof in support of that. But quite plainly the substance of the matters articulated very clearly in the defence, particularly paragraph 12, is to accuse the appellant with criminal conduct of the gravest kind in the preparation of and or the making of the said will. She therefore stands out either as the real perpetrator or as an aidder and abettor.  

Under our evidence Decree 1975, NRCD 323, one cannot in a civil or for that matter a criminal action, allege the commission of a crime directly in issue by his opponent and succeed by offering in proof of the matter alleged, the lower burden of proof that obtains in civil cases i.e. proof on the balance of probabilities. The degree of proof required in such cases is proof beyond reasonable doubt, the collorary being that, if the crime alleged is not directly in issue one could safely get away with the lighter burden of proof. The self explanatory rule stipulates:

13. “(1) In any civil or criminal action the burden of persuasion as to the commission by a party of a crime which is directly in issue requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt.”

With the main complaint of the appellant being that the judgment is against the weight of evidence, the two critical questions that arise for our consideration are, whether the respondent discharged the burden that fell on him by virtue of the above provision. The critical question then would be this? Would any reasonable tribunal exercising criminal jurisdiction, if called upon to, find the appellant guilty of the crime alleged?

Pure suspicions no matter how numerous cannot in law amount to guilt. The respondent accusers evidence in proof of the allegations was made up of only her own evidence that she knows her fathers signature so well that, that which appears on the will is not his and the opinion evidence of the expert. As opposed to that, we also have for the accused appellant, who had to raise a reasonable doubt only in her defence, we have the evidence of the lawyer who prepared the said will, his clerk, the attesting witnesses as well as the deceased testators own brother. None of the persons who took part in the preparation and execution of the will were discredited under cross examination, thus making it difficult to understand the basis of the trial judges finding that the presence of the straight line under the disputed document is overwhelming proof of the forgery. The weight of evidence adduced at the trial points more to the fact that he signed it than the contrary. Nonetheless, even if we took the position the trial judge did, namely that the burden shifted onto the defendant to prove that he signed the will, the understanding being that the burden on the respondent was the lighter burden that exits in criminal cases, the fact still remains that the appellants evidence was weightier than the respondents and one cannot in all good conscience prefer their evidence to the appellant.

Finally, by the par 7 of the defence, the respondent alleges that the relationship between the deceased testator and the appellant was such that he could not possibly have given all his properties to her children use to the exclusion of his other ten children by four other women. Though respondent catalogued in great detail the conduct of the appellant, (it included continuous neglect, failure to take care of him and provide food and etc) describing their marriage as an unhappy one, she led not a shred of evidence in support of them some of which were in any case was denied by the appellant. The respondent never elicited any such information about the marriage from a person who is bound to know if such was their marriage. No marriage is a perfect one. All marriages have faults. But I am unable to hold on the facts of this case that there are circumstances which are bound to excite the suspicion of this court. In which case the appellant has no duty to erase any for none is demonstrated here.

G. T. WOOD (MRS.)

JUSTICE OF APPEAL

S. T. FARKYE, JA:

I agree.
