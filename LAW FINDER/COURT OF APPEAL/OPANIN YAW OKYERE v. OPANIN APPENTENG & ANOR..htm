JUDGMENT

ANSAH, JA

The plaintiff claimed the following against the defendants:

(a) “a declaration that all items being farms and a house bequeathed, devised and/or gifted by the late Akua Attaa under the will of Akua Attaa dated 11/8/83 are Family property.

(b) Recovery of possession of the said property, i.e.

(i)  Mmenkrom Farm,

(ii)  Brepro Farm,

(iii)  Seniagya Kwaemu Farm,

(iv)  Kwame Atta Farm, and

(v)  House Number A.D. 26 Seniagya Ashanti.

(c) An Injunction restraining the defendants all by themselves and each by himself or herself from in any way interfering with the property."The second defendant, while denying each and every allegation of fact or law in the plaintiff’s statement of claim counterclaimed for

(i) A declaration that the properties devised to the 2nd defendant were self acquired properties of the immediate family members made up of Madam Amma Ayewa and her three children namely, Opanin Ntim, Kwaku Brobbey and Akua Ataa (the testatrix),

(ii) …Order for interim injunction restraining the plaintiff…from in any way interfering with the devises made to the 2nd defendant in the said will.

(iii) An order of the court compelling the sole executor to take control over the properties devised in the will till the final determination of this suit."

 (iv)  General damage.

I have decided to comment briefly on a point of pleadings first.

"In law, where a claim in an action involving land was not for possessing, an interlocutory injunction could not be endorsed on the writ of summons. Therefore in the instant case, the endorsement for interim injunction on the writ should be struck out because an interim injunction was, in the circumstance, a substitute for damages for the period between the issuing of the writ and the trial. The plaintiff was at liberty to a motion for interim injunction after the issue of the writ."

See Ediyie v Honny [9757] 2 GLR 142.

The above applies in a counterclaim where there was no claim for possession of land as in this case. Claims (ii) and (iii) in the counterclaim were for control of the properties till the final determination of the dispute. It smacks of and sounded in the nature of an interim injunction, which by practise is to be applied for in a motion after the issuing of the writ of summons or taking filing a counterclaim. It was wrong for them to be put in the counterclaim. The court below should have struck them out and we hereby do so.

Many issues were set down in the summons for directions. The defendant added more to them.

At the end of the trial, the learned trial judge subjected the evidence to scrutiny and held that upon the evidence the action of the plaintiff failed and was not entitled to any of the reliefs he sought. He therefore dismissed the claims, upheld the counterclaim and entered judgment for the defendant accordingly.

This appeal is against this judgment and has been brought on the original omnibus ground that:

"The judgment is against the weight of evidence."

The additional grounds filed pursuant to the leave of court were that:

1. "The judgment is null and void having been delivered almost nine moths (sic) after written addresses had been filed upon the Order of the Court, since the period of nine months contravened the mandatory period of six weeks prescribed by the provisions of Order 7 Rule 2 (1) of the High Court (Civil Procedure) (Amendment) Rules, 1977, LI. 1107.

2. The learned trial judge erred in law when he failed/neglected and or refused to enter judgment against the 1st defendant who failed to enter appearance, file a defence, attend court of (sic) adduce evidence at the trial despite being served with the writ or summons, although Counsel for plaintiff urged the learned trial judge to do so in the written address submitted to the Court.

3. The learned trial judge erred in law when he failed to consider the legal effect of Section 1 (1) of the Administration of Estates Act, 1961, (Act 63), which provides that:

'1 (1). The movable and immovable property of a deceased person shall devolve on his personal representatives with effect from his death."'

It is necessary that the respective cases of the parties be set out in brief at this stage before considering the appeal on its merits.

The plaintiff said that he and the defendants were members of the Anima Kokoo family, headed by the plaintiff. Kwadwo Mensah, Brobbey, Ntim and Amoako were all related to Akua Ataa, the testatrix herein. These persons added more properties to what their forbearers acquired in their life times when they succeeded them. The properties remained in the family till they devolved unto Akua Ataa when she became the successor (to these persons). This woman purported to make certain dispositions in her will which met some resistance by the plaintiff representing the family. The reason for the resistance is just like what usually comes to the courts, namely that some of the properties mentioned in the will did not belong to the testatrix/testator but were family properties. That in brief was the casus belli of this suit.

In seeking to discharge the onus that lay on it, the plaintiff family called the PW1 Nana Kwabena Peprah, it' lawful attorney, Yaw Peprah and the PW2 Kwaku Nti. The plaintiff’s case was significant in one respect, namely that, all the persons named in their evidence though belonged to the Ekuona clan, did not cross inherit each other; that was to say in the words of plaintiffs attorney, they did not go from one root to inherit the other. In fact the evidence had it that the Ekuona clan migrated from Fomena, Asunafo Abomosu and Kwahu to settle at Seniagya in four groups each with its own head.

The second defendant counterclaimed for the same properties an the footing that the testatrix herself acquired them and so had the testamentary capacity to dispose of them in her will. In support of her counter claim, she traced the origins of Akua Ataa; that Amma Ayewa, her mother, originated from Effiduase Asokore but once stayed at the 'ahemfie' in Kumasi. From there, the then Asokorehene, Ayewa's mothers, brother took her and Ntim, her child, first, to Asokore and eventually to Seniagya. Then a group of members of the Ekuona clan including the mother of the second defendant visited Ayewa from Abomosu, Fomena and Kwahu to settle at Seniagya. None of the members of these migrant groups stayed with Ayewa.

The second defendant traced her root of title to the properties in dispute and asserted that Opaning Pusuo gifted the Alata and Breporo farms to his wife Amma Ayewa and the Mmenkrom farm to Akua Ataa. When this farm became fallow, she gave it to the second defendant who went into occupation thereof and resisted acts of trespass to it by one Opaning Fokuo. She maintained this peaceful possession till when the plaintiff disturbed it by forcibly entering the farm and also harvested oil palm and cocoa trees from it.

Concerning the Kwayeam farm, the second defendant said that Akua Ataa's husband acquired it and carved a portion for her. Amma Ayewa acquired the farm at Mmonkrom and cultivated it with her children, Brobbe and Akua Ataa, who later took over as the survivor of the three persons; she gave portions of the farm to the second defendant and others.

On house number AD 29, Seneagya, the evidence was that Amma Ayewa built it with assistance from Brobbe and Akua Ataa. The second defendant led evidence by herself and her witnesses who corroborated her on all material particulars.

At the end of the trial, the learned Judge considered meticulously, the factual issues in the dispute and for a resolution thereof, directed himself on matters like 'the credibility and weight to attach to the witness evidence, corroboration of evidence on a particular point, contradictions and the exercise of acts of ownership... the nature or character of the property, and the line of inheritance ... the difference between an executor and a customary successor.' No one can say that in so doing he misdirected himself, considered irrelevant matters or excluded relevant ones. In short the learned trial judge rather showed a firm grasp and understanding of the matters in dispute and how to resolve them.

Before making findings of facts, he observed that the plaintiff called no member of the Ekuona Clan to confirm his version of the facts before the court. Secondly, he noted a departure between the plaintiff's attorney and the PW1 on the identity of the family to which the parties belonged, for whereas the plaintiff said that Akua Ataa was from the Anima Kokoo family, the PW1 doubted that the family originated from the Ekuona family. He concluded that two separate branches of the family existed after Gyesaw, starting from Anima Kokoo and Buaduwaa respectively. He inferred from this that: "In such a situation, then each of Anima Kokoo and Buaduwaa would begin her own family and the descendants in each line would be deemed to keep their inheritance separate from the other branch, and one might inherit the other only where one branch has not anymore immediate survivors or goes extinct." For this reason, he held that members of the Anima Kokoo group might only inherit Buaduwaa's group if the last member thereof, that is, Akua Ataa died intestate." No one has cast any doubt on the correctness of this proposition in law.

From there, the learned trial judge said that the shortcomings in the case of the plaintiff were absent in that of the second defendant. He held that: "On the whole the account given by the 2nd defendant and her witnesses appear more probable and therefore more acceptable", for in his opinion, the defendant did not only show cogently the source of the respective items of the disputed properties, she showed by evidence that Akua Ataa handled them by apportioning, demarcating or gifting them away without informing or seeking any prior consent from anybody. She dealt with them as her own properties.

Again when an attempt was made to lay the mortal remains of one Boakye from the plaintiff's family in the house in dispute, Akua Ataa succeeded in resisting that and the defendants sat by indolently. By these the learned judge was referring to the exercise of acts of unchallenged ownership towards the properties.

On the issue of the succession in the family, the trial judge observed that the plaintiff gave two accounts that diametrically opposed each other. He traced the line of succession thorough Kofi Onwo to Kwasi Antwi, Kwadwo Duah, Kwaku Addae and finally, Gyesaw. The judge found that some of the properties were in the hands of the family and said that then the action was otiose and a mere academic exercise that profited no one. I do not think he was wrong. In her will, the testatrix gave the Alata farm to her family and she could not have owned them after her death.

The trial judge considered the conflicts in the evidence of the plaintiff and the PW1 and found as a fact that Akua Ataa was not of the Anima Kokoo family. Also, though, Yaw Okyere and the plaintiff's attorney were of the same Ekuona family, they were not from the same family with Akua Ataa. Finally, the trial judge held that the plaintiffs action failed and that he was not entitled to any of the reliefs claimed. He resolved all the additional issues by the second defendant in her favour, upheld the counterclaim and entered judgment for her. This appeal was against the judgment and on the grounds indicated above.

This was an action for a declaration of title to land and to succeed the plaintiff and the counterclaimant bore the onus of proof on the mode of its or her acquisition conclusively. This may be either by traditional evidence or by overt and repeated acts of ownership exercised in respect of the land in dispute; see Odoi v Hammond [1971] 1 GLR 375, CA, Bedu v Agbi [1972] 2 GLR 239, CA. In Kuma v Kuma [1934] 2 WACA 178 at 179, Yates Ag. C.J. said that the plaintiff must show clear title. In discharging the onus that lay on him, the plaintiff is to rely on the strength of his own case but not on the weakness of the defendant's case: see Kodilinye v Odu [1935] 2 WACA 336, explained in Rickets & Another v Addo & others [1975] 2 GLR 158, CA and Oppong Kofi & Another v Fofie [1964]  GLR 174.

Counsel argued the original and additional grounds together. Two matters of procedure and practice were urged upon us first. One was that the judgment was given outside the time limited by the rules of Court for that purpose. The law on the matter not having been complied with, the judgment was invalidated therefore.

The law governing time for the delivery of judgments in the High Court was the High Court (Civil Procedure) (Amendment) Rule, 1977, LI 1107. It pegged the time at six weeks after the conclusion of 'speeches'. The language of the rule was mandatory. However, a careful reading of the law shows that failure to comply does not attract as draconian a sanction as rendering the judgment invalid automatically, for the law provided in its wisdom a saving clause, in the event of non-compliance.

For the sake of clarity I have decided to quote the salient parts of the law in full hereunder:

"7 2A. (1) At the close of a case before it, the Court shall fix a date, which shall be not later than six weeks after the close of that case, for the delivery of judgment therein.

(2) It shall be the duty of the Court to deliver judgment as soon as possible after the close of each case before it, and in any event not later than six weeks after the close of any such case.

(3) For the purposes of this rule, a case shall be deemed to be closed when the evidence has been given to the Court and the speeches subsequent thereto have been concluded.

(5) Where for any reason judgment has not been delivered within the period of six weeks referred to in this rule, the Court shall forthwith inform the Chief Justice in writing of that fact and shall state the reasons for the delay in so delivering judgment and the date upon which it is proposed to deliver judgment.

(6) Where judgment has not been delivered within the period of six weeks referred to in this rule, any part to the proceedings may in writing notify the Chief Justice of that fact and request that a date be fixed for the delivery of judgment.

(7) Upon receiving a notification from the Court or a party under paragraph (5) or (6) the Chief Justice may fix a date for the delivery of judgment by the Court and notify the Court accordingly, and it shall be the duty of such Court to ensure that judgment is delivered upon the date so fixed by the Chief Justice".

The procedure is for first, the court to notify the Chief Justice of the delay and the cause and the expected date for the delivery of the judgment. Any party to the proceedings may also write to the Chief Justice and request a date to be fixed for the judgment to be delivered. After the Chief Justice has been so notified, he may fix a date for the judgment to be delivered. The Court has a duty to comply with the directives of the Chief Justice. I incline to the view that it is the non-compliance with the Chief Justice's directives that attracts that harsh consequence of invalidating the judgment.

Nothing showed that this procedure was followed; the plaintiff did not write to inform the Chief Justice of the failure to deliver the judgment within time nor request for a date for the judgment to be delivered and for him to exercise the power he has to issue the necessary directives. Where a law cast a duty on a party and imposed a precondition to exact a penalty in the event of a failure to do an act, (within a prescribed time), before the party could take advantage of that law he should show that he had first discharged the obligation so imposed on him. It has not been shown that the appellant fulfilled the conditions precedent to invalidating the judgment. That additional ground of appeal does not avail the appellant.

Another additional ground was that the trial judge should have entered interlocutory judgment in default of appearance and defence by the first defendant. I think that point was misconceived. Where there was a default of pleadings, or appearance, the trial judge did not enter judgment suo motu. Such judgments were always given upon application so that the plaintiff should not ask for the judge to enter one for him in his written submissions to this court or the court below.

The plain fact in this case was that the plaintiff filed a motion for judgment in default of defence on 9/3/93; the second defendant opposed the application but the plaintiff never moved the court for a ruling on it. Thus the application was never heard and was presumably abandoned. On 21/4/93, the 2nd defendant filed a motion for interim injunction and an order to account and the suit progressed to trial and eventually, judgment. It was wrong for the plaintiff to appeal to this court to enter the judgment at this stage of the proceedings, for he should have been more vigilant and seen to it that his motion was heard and default judgment entered for him. His indolence contributed in no default judgment being entered for him.

The pith of the second additional ground of appeal was that the second defendant had no locus standi to counterclaim, as the executor had vested no assent in her. The vesting of an assent acknowledged the right of a legatee or a devisee to property under a will. It related back to the date of the death of the testator. There was no doubt that the testatrix gave and bequeathed landed properties to Akua Adoma in her will that on the pleadings has been admitted to probate. The Administration of Estates Act, 1963, Act 63 governs the administration of estates in Ghana and provided in section 1 (1) that:—

"The movable and immovable property of a deceased person shall devolve on his personal representatives with effect from his death.

By the interpretation in section 108 of Act 63, 'personal representative' means the executor, original or by representation, or administrator for the time being of a deceased person'. In her will, the testatrix appointed Opanin Kwaku Pusuo and Kwabena Apenteng as her Executors/Trustees of her will. The properties in dispute devised to the second defendant in the will, became vested in her on the death of the testatrix; see Elliot v King [1966] GLR 654 which decided among others that:

"The will was regarded as a conveyance of the property... and took effect from the death of the testator or testatrix to vest the property in the devisee." The properties having been devised to the second defendant in the will, they vested in her by the operation of law and the fact of the death of the testatrix, hence there was no need for executing any further formal vesting assent by the executor so as to clothe her with the capacity to sue or be sued in respect thereof. Therefore, the ground of appeal that she did not have any locus standi crumbles to the ground and is accordingly dismissed. The question is if the second defendant lacked the locus standi then she was not properly sued as a defendant and the plaintiff's action should fail against her. If she could be sued as a defendant then she could also sue in respect of the same properties. Equality is equity in the eyes of the law. In short then, that additional ground of appeal fails.

The pivotal issues in the case were whether or not the testatrix had the capacity to dispose of the properties in her will. That in turn depended on whether or not she owned them, which was a question of fact. As stated already, the trial judge appreciated the issues in the matter before him, painstakingly dissected the evidence and weighed it carefully and after directing himself properly; he made findings on the essential facts and resolved them. With regard to the omnibus ground of appeal that the judgment was against the weight of the evidence, this attacks generally the findings made by the trial judge on the facts. The generally accepted principle of law is that:

".....findings of fact made by a trial court should not be disturbed unless they are perverse or not supported by the evidence on record. In Bruce v. Attorney General [1967] GLR 170, it was held inter alia, that an Appellate Court should not disturb findings of fact made by a trial judge but was equally true that an Appellate Court was not precluded from so." See Doku v. Doku Part I [1992-93] GBR 367 at 370 CA and also Azagba v. Negov [1964] GLR 450: Praka v. Ketewa [1964] GLR 423; Nyame v. Tarzan Transport & Another [1973] 1 GLR 8 at 11 CA. I would like to conclude this aspect of the appeal by referring to what a former Court of Appeal said in Glala & Another v. Dzaba & Another unreported, Civil Motion No. 86/62, dated 26 June 1967. that" This court will interfere with the decision of a trial court which has had the advantage of seeing and hearing the witnesses only if it comes to the affirmative conclusion that a finding is wrong; the presumption being that the decision of the trial court on the facts is right (see Twimahene Adjeibi Kojo II v. Opanin Bonsie & another (14 WACA) 242."

In this case, as I have striven to say, the trial judge weighed the evidence before him and accepted that of the second defendant and her witnesses but rejected that of the plaintiff and his witnesses. The probabilities weighing heavily in favour of the second defendant according to him, he entered judgement for her on her counterclaim. We have not spared ourselves the task of reading the record over and have come to agree that the evidence supported all the findings made and they preponderated more in favour of the counterclaim than the claim. We must be very slow and loth to interfere with with his findings of facts and rather hold him better entitled to a verdict of this court than the plaintiff on his claims both in the court below and here too.

In the result we affirm the judgment of the court below, and dismiss all the grounds of appeal. The only addition we make is that the farm at Alata should go to the family according to the wish to the wish the testatrix expressed in her will.
