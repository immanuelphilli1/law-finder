JUDGMENT

OWUSU J.A.:

The plaintiffs/appellants hereinafter referred to as Appellants, issued a writ against the Defendants/Respondents referred to simply as Respondents jointly and severally for the following reliefs:

(i) "An order that plot No. 17 Block 111, Ampabame, Kumasi was acquired by Kwame Appiah (deceased) and developed by Akwasi Obeng (deceased) and is thus family property.

(ii) "An order that the transaction which took place between Akwasi Obeng (deceased) and defendants herein which led to the possession of the house by Defendants was a customary law pledge and not a sale and for a further order nullifying any purported sale of the said house and land."

(iii) An order for recovery of possession of the said house from Defendants upon tendering the pledge sum of N ¢600.00 (six Hundred Cedis).

The Respondents resisted the Appellant's Claim contending that the plot No.17, Block 111, Ampabame, Kumasi with the building thereon was the property of Kwasi Obeng. That they bought the said building outright from Kwasi Obeng after satisfying themselves that Obeng was the undisputed owner of the plot with the building thereon.

They therefore counter-claimed for "title to House number Block 111, plot 17 Ampabame, Kumasi through sale to them by late Kwasi Obeng. At the end of the trial, the Appellant's Claim was dismissed and even though the court did not decree legal title in the 1st Respondent it declared him equitable owner and entered Judgment in favour of the Respondents on their counter-claim. Dissatisfied with the Judgment, the Appellants have come to this court, complaining that:

"The Judgment is against the weight of the evidence on record."

Four more additional grounds were filed alleging errors of law on the part of the trial Judge.

Going through the grounds of Appeal the main issue which calls for determination is whether the transaction between Kwasi Obeng and the 1st Respondent which led him into possession and subsequent occupation was a pledge as contended by the Appellants or an outright sale as claimed by the Respondents.

On the evidence before the court, the issue of ownership of the house was resolved by the trial court which made a positive finding that the house is the self-acquired property of Kwasi Obeng and not his family property.

In this appeal, that finding is not being seriously challenged. Indeed, in counsel for the Appellant's written statement of case, he submitted that whether the property belonged to Kwasi Obeng or Opanin Kwasi Appiah or both, is neither here nor there. According to counsel

"what is material is that both of them are dead and by law, the property (which ever of the two it belonged to) has devolved upon their family represented by Plaintiffs"

This does not represent the true state of the law with regard to Kwasi Obeng who died in 1993. If the property was Obeng's self-acquired property then upon his death intestate 1993, it would devolve upon his wife and children in accordance with P.N.D.C. Law 111 which came into force in June 1985 but not on his family as counsel stated.

To establish the family character of the property, the plaintiffs who claim title to the property, must support their claim with cogent and convincing evidence. The well settled rule of law is that in an action for declaration of title to land, a plaintiff could only succeed on the strength of his own case. This was re-echoed in the case of DUAGBOR VRS AKYEA DJAMSON [1984-86] 1 GLR 697 by Abban J.A. of blessed memory (as he then was). The learned Justice of Appeal had this to say—

"The plaintiff having sought a declaration of title to the land in dispute could only succeed on the strength of his case and not on the weakness of the co-defendant's case. It is true that the co-defendant also counter-claimed for declaration of title but that is beside the point. For the co-defendant's counter claim could not in any way lighten the weight of the burden on the plaintiff to establish his title to the land by preponderance of admissible evidence."

The other well known principle of law is that a claimant could take advantage of any weaknesses in his opponents case only where he had succeeded in establishing a prima facie case himself. See the case of ODAMTEY VRS CLOCUH and Anor. [1989-90] 1 GLR p. 14.

In this appeal, we need not look far to discover that what the Appellants are doing is to use the weakness in the Respondents counter claim, to prop their weak claim. Their claim is one against the estate of a deceased person and as such must be scrutinised with the utmost suspicion as Kwasi Obeng is now dead.

In the case of MOSES AND OTHERS VRS. ANANE [1989-90] 2 GLR, 694 at 697 the court per Francois J.A. held that:

"A claim against a deceased's estate must be viewed with suspicion. Proof must be strict and utterly convincing, as one of the protagonists is dead and cannot assert himself."

Brett MR stated the law in the case of GARNET, IN RE GANDY VRS MACAULEY [1885] 31 ch. D p.1 as follows:

"The law is that when an attempt is made to charge a deceased person in a matter, in which if he were alive he might have answered that charge the evidence ought to be looked at with great care; the evidence ought to be thoroughly sifted and the mind of the Judge who hears it ought to be, first of all, in a state of suspicion ..........."

What is the nature of the evidence led in the court below in proof of (i) the family character of the property, the subject matter of the dispute, (ii) that the transaction between Kwasi Obeng and the 1st Respondents was a pledge but not a sale? The 2nd plaintiff who gave evidence in proof of the family character of the property told the court it was his grand uncle Kwame Appiah who acquired the plot from Twafohene of Ampabame.

For a long time, the plot was undeveloped so the town development committee wrote to Appiah that they wanted to recover the plot. Appiah and his family went to the committee and pleaded with them so the land was given back to him. He tendered Exhibit "A" the letter from the committee to Appiah, the contents of which I reproduce hereunder—

"I am writing this to inform you that, according to your letter written to me on the 3rd DAY OF JUNE, 1961, you stated that you would be able to erect a building on your plot within (1) year. This was agreed between you and the committee members, hoping that on the 3rd of June, 1962, something will be done by you.

Since that time has passed, and no sign or no attempt has been made by you about the erection of the building, I am writing to tell you that the plot has been taken from you, and is now the property of the Town Committee and the Committee is ready to give the plot to anybody who is ready to erect the building.”

From Exhibit "A" the committee wrote to inform him that the plot had been taken away for failure on his part to develop it but not to warn him that the committee was going to take it away.

The evidence of the 2nd plaintiff is that his great uncle Appiah started development on the land but it was Kwasi Obeng who continued with the development to its completion. He however told the court they (presumably members of the family) contributed money to purchase cement. He did not state how much was contributed.

This piece of evidence contradicts the plaintiffs own pleadings. In paragraph 4 of the statement of claim, the plaintiffs averred that, Kwame Appiah, many years after acquiring plot No. 17, Block 111 did not develop it. That upon a threat from the committee, Opanin Appiah caused his nephew Akwasi Obeng to develop it by putting up a three bedroom out house with kitchen and toilet. According to the statement of claim, it was Obeng who financed the whole construction while the plaintiffs kept the cement, fetched water and supervised the labourers. From the statement of claim, the property acquired family character because the plot was acquired by one member of family and developed by another.

If indeed the plot was acquired by Appiah and developed by Obeng it would have acquired family character from the beginning and Obeng would have acquired only a life interest in the property and could not transfer any more interest than what he himself had.

From the evidence however, the property acquired family character because other members apart from Obeng contributed money towards its development.

The position of the law is that the assistance in cash or labour which members of the family give to another member to develop his self-acquired property must be substantial to give the property a mark of family character.

In answer to this claim, the Respondents case is that indeed plot No 17 Block 111, in the Kumasi District was taken away from Kwame Appiah in terms of Exhibit 'A". That the said plot, from Exhibit "l " was allocated to Kwasi Obeng who single-handedly developed it and therefore was his self-acquired property.

The evidence of D.W. 1 Samuel Oliver Nyamesem Adusei is that in the middle of the 1960s, he was secretary to the Town Development committee which was responsible for allocation of lands. He told the court that when the committee came into being, a plot had already been allocated to Opanin Kwame Appiah. For a long time this plot was not developed. Opanin Appiah was invited by the committee to find out from him whether he was ready to develop the plot. He told the committee that he did not have the means to do so. The committee therefore took the plot from him because its desire was to have the town developed. At the same time, his grand nephew Kwasi Obeng, approached the committee for a plot and because the committee was not merely interested in denying Opanin Appiah the use of the plot, it was allocated to his grand nephew Obeng.

Under cross-examination this witness denied that the plot was restored to Opanin Appiah. He insisted that it was allocated to his grand nephew Obeng. D.W. 1's evidence completely debunks the plaintiffs contention that the plot was restored to Appiah.

If Obeng acquired the plot as evidenced in Exhibit "l " and single-handedly developed it, as the plaintiffs evidence failed to establish the family character of the property, then it is his self acquired property as the trial court rightly found and he could in his life time deal with it without any reference to the family.

The parties are ad idem that during his lifetime there was a transaction between him and the 1st Respondent affecting this property. They sharply differ as to the nature of the transaction.

The plaintiffs contend that Kwasi Obeng pledged this property to the Respondents and as such pledged property, it can be redeemed at any time. The big question that quickly comes to my mind, is by whom? With the coming into force of the intestate succession law P.N.D.C. Law 111, can the family maintain an action for its redemption even if the property was pledged? Kwasi Obeng died in 1993 and from the evidence of P.W. 2, his son, he left behind children upon whom the properly would have devolved on his death intestate under the law.

The 1st Respondent however contends that the transaction between him and Obeng in connection with the property was an outright sale.            

The plaintiffs therefore had to lead sufficient and admissible evidence to establish that indeed the property was pledged. The second plaintiff's evidence is that he only heard that the building had been pledged and could not even remember when. It was when he heard of the pledge that the family appealed to Boshenhene Nana Adom Dankwa to intervene for them to refund the money to redeem the property as same did not belong to Obeng.

It was when they approached the Respondents that they (Respondents) refused to give the property back to them because they claimed they bought it. At this time, according to him, Opanin Appiah was dead.

Under cross-examination, the following questions were put to him which I find interesting and note worthy with the answers given by the witness—

Q: “You regarded this house as your family house with contributions from your sister and Kwaku Duah and you allowed Kwasi Obeng to pledge it?

A: He was in control of the house and at the time he was pledging it, the family did not know. That was why we later sent appeal to redeem it?

Q: When you got to know that Akwasi Obeng had pledged the house, Akwasi Obeng was alive?

A: "Yes he was alive and he was quarrelling with the family as a result of his misdeeds".

Q: So Kwasi Obeng in his life time, never accepted that he had pledged the house to the defendant?

A: "He did not accept that."

Cross-examined further however, he said Obeng said he had pledged it.

Obeng did not make any attempt in his lifetime from 1967-1993 when he died to redeem the property.                                                        

Even in 1973, when a similar action was instituted, Kwasi Obeng was not a party to it even though he was alive. This action was never prosecuted and after so many adjournments, it was struck out.

The evidence of the 1st Respondent is that when he bought the house from Obeng, it was uncompleted. He completed it before he went into occupation. This piece of evidence is confirmed by P.W. 3 who said he was engaged by Kwasi Obeng to develop the property to roofing level "Pledge in customary law is the delivery of possession and custody of property, real or personal, by a person to his creditor to hold and use until a debt due is paid, an article borrowed is returned or replaced, or an obligation is discharged."

See principles of customary land law in Ghana by the eminent Jurist N.A. Ollenu (2nd edition) p. 101.

"Its purpose essentially is not so much to hold the pledged property as security, as to have its use as interest on the amount borrowed  ............." (emphasis is mine)

If the plaintiff's assertion that the property was pledged is true, of what use could the 1st Respondent put an uncompleted building? Would he have parted with the ¢600 at the time, just to take custody of a property, from which he could not derive any benefit? The answer readily to my mind, is no.

I am therefore affirming the trial Judge's finding that the transaction between Kwasi Obeng and the 1st Respondent was not a pledge.

Was it then a sale as the 1st Respondent contended? The Respondents counter-claimed for a declaration of title which they claimed was acquired through sale. They also, notwithstanding the fact that the Appellants failed to establish the pledge, had an equal task to discharge the burden of persuasion by leading sufficient evidence to establish a requisite degree of belief concerning the sale in the mind of the court.

What evidence then was led by the 1st Respondent in proof of the fact that he bought the uncompleted property which he later completed from Kwasi Obeng. The 1st Respondent gave evidence and called one witness, the secretary to the Town Development Committee.

His evidence is that he heard that Kwasi Obeng who was the owner of the house was selling it. He saw the allocation paper and site plan on the property and they were in the name of Kwasi Obeng.

Obeng prepared a document which he and Obeng together with the 2nd Respondent his brother, took to the Chief of Ampabame for execution. The document was an agreement which was witnessed by the 1st Plaintiff and his mother.

According to him, the house consisted of 3 bedrooms, Kitchen, toilet and bathrooms. It was roofed, with the kitchen, toilet and bath having concrete floor. There were windows and doorframes.

He added a storeroom and fixed doors, windows, locks and keys and plastered and ceiled the rooms. He also constructed a gutter behind the house, painted it and connected water and electricity. That these facilities bear his name and he pays property rate in his name.

He told the court it took him about 1 year to put the house in habitable state and in 1968 he moved into it with his family and was in occupation since then.

He tendered photocopies of the allocation paper and the site plan with Kwasi Obeng's name at the trial. The agreement was not produced with the explanation that he attached it to his defence in the earlier suit in 1973.

He led no further evidence oral or documentary in proof of the purchase. The evidence of his only witness is to the effect that plot No. 17, Block 111 was allocated to Kwasi Obeng by the Committee. He did not call the 2nd Respondent, his brother with whom he went to the chief of Ampabame for the execution of the agreement between him and Obeng, if indeed there was any such agreement.

In the case of MAJOLAGBE VRS. LARBI & OTHERS [1959] GLR 192, Ollenu J. (as he then was) defined proof in law as follows:—

"Proof in law is the establishment of facts by proper legal means where a party makes an averment capable of proof in some positive way, eg by producing documents, description of things reference to other facts, instances, or circumstances and his averment is denied he does not prove it by merely going into the witness ... and repeating that averment on oath or having it repeated on oath by his witness. He proves it by producing other evidence of facts and circumstances, from which the court can be satisfied that what he avers is true."

Admittedly, from the evidence on record there was no such proof of the sale by the 1st Respondent and I endorse the trial Judge's finding that the evidence in support of the sale was not sufficient enough for a court to decree title in his favour.

The trial Judge therefore erred when he entered Judgment in favour of the Respondents on their counter-claim. I will therefore set aside the Judgment entered in favour of the Respondent. He is however entitled to the protection of the law as someone in possession until somebody with a better title comes.

On the evidence, there is no merit in the Appellant’s appeal and same is hereby dismissed.

MISS R.C. OWUSU

JUSTICE OF APPEAL

ANIM, J.A.:

I had the privilege and the opportunity of seeing in advance the judgment which my learned sister Miss Owusu J.A. has just read. I observed that she dealt exhaustively with the question as to whether the transaction which the litigating parties herein originally entered into was a customary pledge or sale. I am in entire agreement with the reasoning of my learned sister and the conclusions she has come to. I however, propose to make a contribution solely on the issue of onus of proof and to touch briefly on the evidence adduced by the Plaintiff-Appellants at the close of their case.

In the notice of Appeal filed on 20th October, 2001, counsel for the Plaintiff/Appellants stated that the judgment is against the weight of the evidence.

In arguing this ground, learned counsel submitted that the onus was on the Respondents who asserted that the transaction was a sale to establish the sale. Counsel submitted further that the Respondents have failed to produce any

(a) written document evidencing a sale.

(b) registered instrument of sale

(c) witnesses to the sale transaction.

The Respondents, on the other hand maintained that the Appellants who asserted that the transaction was a pledge bore the burden of establishing that pledge. As the matter stood, each party based his case on the issue which, if proved, would exclude the consideration of the other party's case.

Part II of the provisions of the Evidence Decree which deal with the burden of proof covers on the one hand the burden of producing evidence under sections 11, 12, and 13. Taking into consideration section 10, the expression "burden of persuasion,” should be interpreted to mean the quality, quantum, amount, degree or extent of evidence a party is obliged to adduce in order to satisfy the requirement of proving a fact. Thus the burden of persuasion is different from the burden of producing evidence.

Under section 11, 12 and 13, and particularly section 179 (1) the burden of producing evidence means the duty or obligation resting on a party to lead evidence. They relate to which of the parties should be the first to lead evidence before the other's evidence is led.

My view is that it is the Plaintiffs who will lose first, who has the duty or obligation to lead evidence in order to forestall a ruling being given against him: see section 11 (1) N.R.C.D. 323 which provides as follows:

"For the purpose of this Decree, the burden of producing evidence means the obligation of a party to introduce sufficient evidence to avoid a ruling against him on the issue."

The position in the instant case according to the Defendant-Respondents is that the land was given to Kwasi Obeng by the Town Development Committee and he started to develop it in l968. He then proceeded to sell the house he had single-handedly erected thereon in its incomplete state to the 1st Defendant who completed and improved it and took possession of the house. The Plaintiff-Appellant's claim that the transaction which took place between Kwasi Obeng (Deceased) and the Defendants which led to the possession of the house by the Defendants was a customary pledge and therefore their claim for recovery of possession was justified. Pledge is a positive averment which must be proved and its terms clearly established. If the Plaintiffs who pleaded pledge gave no evidence the averments of pledge would remain unproved. A ruling that no pledge has been proved or established would go against the Plaintiff and that would mean further that the basis of the claim for recovery of possession would have failed.

The Evidence Decree, however, makes provision for the duty or obligation to adduce evidence to shift from one party to the other. This is the rational behind the provisions of section 14 of the Evidence Decree 1975 (NRCD 323) which reads:—

“Except as otherwise provided by law, and unless and until it is shifted a party has the burden of persuasion as to each fact, the existence or non-existence of which is essential to the claim or defence he is asserting."

In the instant case where the Plaintiffs relied on a pledge whilst the Defendants stuck to a sale, it is evident from the provisions of the Evidence Decree 1975 (NRCD 323) that the duty or obligation on the Plaintiffs was to have produced sufficient evidence so that the trial court would conclude that the existence of a pledge was more probable than its non-existence.

It is in the light of these provisions in the Evidence Decree (NRCD 323) that I am unable to agree with learned counsel for the Plaintiffs that the burden of producing evidence rests on the Defendants. In terms of NRCD 323, therefore, to duty or obligation is on the party against whom a ruling on the issues before court would be given if he failed to lead sufficient evidence to establish them.

The following cross-examination of 2nd Plaintiff after his evidence in chief is instructive:—

Q: So who was regarded as the owner after the completion of the house?

A: Kwasi Obeng for he was my mothers eldest child.

Q: When was the house pledged?

A: I cannot remember the year but I heard that the building had been pledged hence the appeal to redeem the pledge.

Q: You regarded this house as your family house with contributions from your sisters and Kwaku Duah and you allowed Kwasi Obeng to pledge it?

A. He was in control of the house and at the time he was pledging it the family did not know. That was why we later sent appeal to redeem it.

Q: When you got to know that Akwasi Obeng had pledged the house, Kwasi Obeng was alive?

A: Yes, he was alive and he was quarrelling with the family as a result of his misdeeds.

Q: So Kwasi Obeng in his lifetime never accepted that he had pledged the house to defendants?

A: He did not accept that.

Q: He told you he had sold the house?

A: He said pledged it.

It was plain that at the end of the Plaintiff’s own evidence, the quality of the evidence which they had so far adduced was nowhere near the standard of proof normally required of a Plaintiff who assumes the burden of proving the existence of their claim. On the contrary, the 2nd Plaintiff’s answers to questions rather supported the Defendant-Respondents claim that the transaction which took place between Kwasi Obeng and the 1st Defendant was not a pledge. I agree with my learned sister that the Plaintiffs failed to establish the existence of a pledge as well as all the other conclusions she reached.

S. Y. ANIM

LARTEY, J.A.:

I agree.
