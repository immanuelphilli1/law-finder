JUDGMENT

HONYENUGA J.A.

The appellant, Kingsford Amoah alias Kwame Abaka was jointly tried with one Yaw Duodu in the High Court, Cape Coast. Both accused persons faced a common count of conspiracy to commit robbery contrary to section 23(1) and section 149 of the Criminal Act, Act 29 as amended by Act 646 of 2003 and the substantive offence of robbery contrary to section 149 of Act 29 the Criminal Act, 1960, Act 29. Both accused persons were convicted and sentenced to forty years on each count to run ;. concurrently. The second accused, Kingsford Amoah alias Kwame Abaka has appealed against his conviction and sentence. The facts were that the accused persons who are farmers hatched a plan to snatch a taxi cab and so on the 29th June 2007, the two, armed with two knives contacted one Kwame Takyi, a driver of a Tico taxi cab and hired his services. They directed the driver, now deceased, to convey them from Mankessim to Kromanmu to purchase Akpeteshie. On reaching Kromanmu and after stopping for a while to conduct other businesses including making calls from a space-to-space operator, they further directed the driver to drive towards Mando. Just about a kilometer from Kromanmu and towards Mando, they stopped the driver and lured him into the bush ostensibly to fetch the Akpeteshie from a cottage. On reaching a spot, they inflicted fatal knife wounds on the driver and dragged him into the bush. They then took the keys of the taxi cab and the sum of ¢120,000.00 (GH¢12,00) from him and sped off with the vehicle to Accra leaving the driver in the bush. It was in the course of the transaction for the sale of the taxi that the two accused persons were arrested after notice of the missing taxi and the driver was relayed around The accused persons could not give any tangible reason for possessing the taxi cab which was reported missing and so they were arrested. At the Police Station, the accused allegedly admitted the offence. In the meantime, the driver of the taxi cab was found in the bush the next day dead with stabbed wounds. The accused persons were accordingly charged with the offences of conspiracy and robbery before the High Court. The appellant being aggrieved appealed against his conviction and sentence. The grounds of appeal are:- (I)That the judgment is against the weight of evidence. (ii)Other grounds will be filed on the receipt of the record of proceedings. No further grounds of appeal were filed. Counsel for the accused relied on the sole ground that the judgment is against the weight of evidence. Before I consider this ground, I must state that Counsel for the appellant was most unhelpful in what he called a statement of case. Counsel only repeated the charges and the facts and stated that the plea of Alibi by the accused was ignored by the trial court. It is most disappointing.Lawyers must perform their professional duties more seriously Their clients. Since an appeal is a re-hearing, an appellate court is obliged to review the evidence as a whole and make its own findings of fact, if so justified. See Republic vs Nana Osei Kwadjo II [2008] 1GMJ 42 SC Holding 5. I would review the evidence as a whole and make my own findings of fact to enable me come to a conclusion whether the conviction and sentence was right. I would therefore consider the law on the offence of conspiracy in relation to the evidence on record and later turn my attention to the substantive charge of robbery. Section 23(1) of the Criminal Offences Act, 1960, (Act 29) provides:- "23(1) where two or more persons agree to act together with a common purpose for or in committing or abetting a criminal offence, whether with or without a previous concert or deliberation, each of them commits a conspiracy to commit or abet the criminal offence". For the prosecution to succeed in a conspiracy charge, they must prove:- That the accused persons agreed to act together with a common purpose for or in committing or abetting a crime.The prosecution would need to prove one ingredient or another depending on the charge laid. Before I consider the evidence adduced at the trial, I must state that as in all cases the prosecution ought to prove its case beyond all reasonable doubts as required by sections 11(2) and 13(1) of the Evidence Act, NRCD 323. Section 13(2) of NRCD 323 only required the accused to raise reasonable doubts as to his guilt. In proof of its case, the prosecution called six witnesses. The accused persons also testified and called one witness. Counsel for the appellant did not make any submission with regard to the charge of conspiracy. It is only the learned Principal State Attorney who submitted that the accused persons acted together and that constituted the conspiracy to commit the robbery. In this appeal, it is incumbent upon us to determine by the evidence on record whether the evidence established conspiracy between the appellant and the first convict and whether the appellant's conviction was right and therefore should not be disturbed. PW2 Vida Essuman testified that on the 29th day of June 2007 at about 5pm, she saw the 2 accused persons and the deceased driver of the Tico taxi got down from the car. She also saw the two accused standing by the taxi with the deceased after they got down at Mando Junction. At that juncture, she called the first convict but he told her he was doing some rounds but he refused to come. PW2 saw the deceased driver who made a call at her end and after the call, the deceased asked PW2 the distance to Mando. She asked him why he asked that question, the victim replied that the two accused persons hired his taxi to pick alcohol from the place. PW2 told the victim that the distance to Mando was not far. PW2 testified further that the victim went back to the car and then the appellant came to sit at a beer bar near her and smoked something which looked like cigarette. When the appellant finished drinking, he bought pure water from PW2 and thereafter the three persons drove off. PW2 testified that the next day, she heard that somebody had been killed in the bush and when she went to the scene she identified the victim as the driver who made a call at her place the previous day. She also identified the picture of the victim. PW3 corroborated the evidence of PW2 to the effect that on the day of the incident, she stopped the said taxi but the first convict told her they were going to pick some alcoholic drinks and so she could not be picked. PW3 also saw the 1st convict and the appellant together with the deceased driver in the car and she identified the picture of the victim. In the same vein PW4 a student also saw and joined the taxi in which the appellant was and he alighted at Kromanmu. PW5, a Police Inspector also further corroborated the evidence of PW2, PW3 and PW4 to the effect that upon investigations he found the body of the victim, took photographs of it and sent it to the Central Hospital, Cape Coast. PW5 later arrested the appellants upon information when they sought to sell the taxi in Accra. PW7 the Police. investigator also further corroborated the evidence of the prosecution on the conspiracy charge. The appellants sought to rely on the defence of alibi and called DW1 who rather confirmed the case of the prosecution. DW1 said second appellant stayed with her until 5pm when the first convict came and they left in a taxi to Accra to bring down a truck. I would consider the defence of alibi put up by the appellants in detail when considering the substantive charge of robbery. The alibi could only be described as an afterthought and not reasonably probable. I therefore find that appellant and the convict acted together to commit the robbery. I therefore agree with the learned trial judge that the prosecution had been able to prove the conspiracy charge against the appellants. Now to the substantive charge of robbery, Section 149 of the Criminal Act, 1960, Act 29 as amended by the Criminal Code (Amendment) Act, 2003 (Act 646) provides:- "(1) Whoever commits robbery is guilty of an offence and shall be liable, upon conviction on trial summarily or on indictment, to imprisonment for a term of not less than ten years, and where the offence is committed by the use of an offensive weapon or offensive missile, the offender shall upon conviction be liable to imprisonment for a term of not less than fifteen years. (2) For the purpose of subsection (1), the Attorney-General shall in all cases determine whether the offence shall be tried summarily or on indictment. (3) In this section "offensive weapon" means any article made or adapted for use to cause injury to the person or damage to property or intended by the person who has the weapon to use it to cause injury or damage, and "offensive missile" includes a stone, brick or any article or thing likely to cause harm, damage or injury if thrown". The definition of robbery is provided in section 150 of the of the Criminal Offences Act, 1960, (Act 29) "150. A person who steals a thing is guilty of robbery if, in and for the purpose of stealing the thing, uses any force or causes any harm to any person, or he uses any threat of criminal assault or harm to any person, with intent thereby to prevent or overcome the resistance of that or of any person to the stealing of the thing". This definition of robbery in section 150 of Act 29 was considered in Behome v The Republic [1979] GLR 112 where the court held that: 'Under section 150 of Act 29, A would only be guilty of robbery if in stealing a thing he used any force or- caused any harm or used any threat of criminal assault to B, to prevent or overcome the resistance of B or C to the stealing of the thing. The fear might be either of personal violence to the person robbed or to a member of his family in the restrictive sense of a man, his wife and children. And the thing stolen must be from the person or the one threatened in his presence, if the property was under his immediate and personal care and protection". In sum, the court held that one is guilty of robbery if in stealing a thing he used any force or cause any harm or used any threat of criminal assault with intent thereby to prevent or overcome the resistance of his victims, to the stealing of the thing. In R v Dawson [1977] 64 CAR 170, the court held that when a person is charged with robbery, the sole question is whether he used force on any person in order to steal. To secure a conviction of a person charged with robbery, the prosecution must prove beyond reasonable doubts that:- 1. The accused has stolen a thing. 2. For the purpose of stealing, the accused person used force or caused harm to any person. 3. The force or the threat of criminal assault or harm used must be with the intent to prevent or overcome the resistance of any person to the stealing of the thing. 4. The identity of the accused with regard to the commission of the robbery. What were the facts upon which the trial judge found as established by the evidence from which the appellants were said to have committed the offence of robbery? Robbery is basically stealing and the intention of the accused is primarily to dishonestly appropriate as under section 120(1) of Act 29 something from another person. The accused must not be the owner of the thing appropriated as required under section 122(2) OF Act 29. The accused must not have a claim to the property stolen. There must also be absence of consent from the owner as to its appropriation by the accused person. This means that all the elements of stealing must be present. Further, the accused must have used some force or harm or threat of criminal assault with intent to overcome the resistance of that person to the stealing of the thing. PW1 established that he is the owner of the Tico taxi cab number GW 7537 X driven by his driver and victim. His driver and the taxi got missing but he had information that the appellants attempted to sell his car in Accra, and they were arrested and he got his car ' back, The appellant did not lay claim to the taxi cab. In cross-examination, PW2 was portrayed as having contradicted herself for having said in her evidence-in-chief that she saw appellant smoking something like cigarette and in her unsworn statement, Exhibit 1 she stated that he smoked Indian hemp. Further PW2 was said to have denied knowing the appellant's name in her evidence but in Exhibit "I", she mentioned his name as Kwame Abaka. These two irreconcilable statements of PW2 could be described as confused. Where however in such a circumstance, there is other evidence which overwhelmingly override the uncertain nature of that of PW2, then the overwhelming evidence would reduce the legal effect of the conflicting evidence. In his book Criminal Law in Ghana at page 128 to 129, P. K. Twumasi stated as follows - "It is not unusual for witnesses who were present at the commission of a crime to give evidence which is not free from discrepancies. This is only natural because the ability to observe, describe or to memorize an event may differ from person to person. Where there are discrepancies (or inconsistencies or contradictions in the testimonies of the prosecution witnesses, it is the duty of the judge to direct himself or the jury or assessors to the discrepancies and to tell them that such discrepancies are not fatal to the prosecution's case unless they go to the very root of the matter in issue. A discrepancy in the case of the prosecution is fatal only when it cannot be reconciled with the rest of the evidence or when it is material or goes to the very root of the matter in issue'. See MENSAH VS THE STATE [1963] 1 GLR 26 SC. The learned author however, stated that where however the contradictions are irreconcilable, the prosecution cannot be said to have proved that case beyond reasonable doubt. See Kotey vs The State [1963] GLR 41 SC. An examination of the evidence of PW2 in the light of the totality of the evidence adduced by the prosecution witnesses reveals no such irreconcilable contradictions. See Ametewee vs The State [1964] GLR 551 where the Supreme Court in Holding (3) held that the conflicts in the evidence of the prosecution witnesses complained of by the appellant's counsel were not germain to the crucial issue which the jury had to determine. PW3 stated that she stopped the car to join in. According to PW3 the car stopped but the first convict came out of the car and said they were not going to her destination but were going to pick some alcoholic drinks. She stated that she saw the two accused in The vehicle. PW3's evidence was not seriously challenged under cross-examination. PW4, a student then also boarded the said Tico taxi on the day of the incident and he also identified the two accused persons as those who were on the vehicle with the driver whom he identified in a picture marked as ID1. He also identified the deceased driver when he went to the scene. His evidence was not punctured at all and it corroborated the evidence of PW3. PW5 Inspector William Yaw Crabbe received a complaint, went to the scene and found the body of the deceased driver tying in a blood of blood. He also went to Accra to bring down the accused persons after they were arrested by the Police in Accra to Cape Coast together with the stolen Tico car. He also took the body to the Central Hospital, Cape Coast for autopsy. The evidence of PW5 corroborated that of PW6, Chief Inspector Sackey Odonkor and PW1, PW2 and PW3. It is clear that the stealing, the force and harm used, the appellants' identity and harm caused to the deceased together with signs of the weapons used to overcome the deceased to his resistance to the stealing of the car are on record. PW6 testified that the first appellant told him that he and the second appellant hired a taxi from Mankessim to a village called Kromanmu to convey Akpeteshie with the aim of robbing the car. According to PW6, the appellants purchased two knives which they used to kill the victim. They were tendered as Exhibits "A" and "A1" being a kitchen and "okapi" knives respectively, found at the scene. He tendered the cover of a driving license belonging to the deceased as Exhibit C. PW6 further testified that the second appellant insisted that he was involved. He narrated how the first convict described the scene and actually tallied with what he saw at the scene. Palm seedlings uprooted by the appellant at the scene were tendered as Exhibit D. Photographs of the posture of the deceased were tendered as Exhibit E1E1¬E4. The caution statement of the 1St accused which was a confession statement was tendered as Exhibit "F" and the charged statement as Exhibit "G". The appellant's caution statement was tendered as Exhibit "H" and his charged statement as "J". Exhibit "K" was tendered as the Post Moterm report. The 1st accused's charged statement sought to establish that he and the appellant were not involved. I believe that this statement was an afterthought since the 1st accuseds caution statement led to the clue of the robbery. I am satisfied that the prosecution adduced prima facie evidence to warrant the appellant to open his defence. The prosecution succeeded in establishing all the ingredients of robbery, that is the stealing, the force and harm used and the force and harm caused to the deceased driver to overcome his resistance to the robbery. Now, I am enjoined to examine the evidence of the appellant as required under section 11(3) of the Evidence Act NRCD 323 which provides:- "(3) In a criminal action, the burden of producing evidence when it is on the accused as to any fact the converse of which is essential to guilt, requires the accused to produce sufficient evidence so that on all the evidence a reasonable mind could have a reasonable doubt as to guilt". This section 11(3) was for the appellant to lead such evidence to cast doubt or discredit the evidence proffered by the prosecution. Lutterodt vs The Republic [1963] 2 GLR 429 SC laid down the procedure to guide judges where the prosecution had led a prima facie case. The judge is enjoined to examine the case of the defence in three stages:- (a) if the explanation of the defence is acceptable, then the accused must be acquitted; (b) if the explanation of the defence is not acceptable, but is reasonably probable, the accused should be acquitted, (c) if quite apart from the defence's explanation, the court is satisfied on a consideration of the whole evidence, that the accused is guilty, it must convict. What is the defence put up by the appellant. He said he is a driver and knew the 1st convict, He denied having committed robbery with the 1st convict. He also denied the evidence of PW2, PW3 and PW4. He stated that he was helping his sister when the ls convict came with two others and that one of them wanted a driver to go with him to Accra to bring down a big car and wanted him to go for it. He agreed, took his identity card and informed his sister and they all left for Accra. They were moving on with the car which he called the tico taxi cab and it developed a fault. In Accra the car was taken to a workshop and the mechanic took out what was faulty and gave it to the owner to go and purchase a new one. Appellant then told the first convict he was going to look for something to eat and while returning he saw Policemen standing by the first convict. Upon his enquiry, he was told the Police were looking for the owner of the vehicle. At that juncture, the Police took them to the Police Station and expected the owner to follow up. He said the Police detained them and were beaten up. They were later brought to the Cape Coast Police Station and detained. They were taken to the spot of the incident and he denied uprooting palm seedlings. The appellant admitted in his evidence-in-chief that it was the Tico taxi that he and the first convict and another took to Accra. He also admitted in cross- examination that:- Q.The Tico taxi you took to Accra, is that the taxi by which you were standing with A1 for which your picture was taken? A. Yes, my Lord". The appellant midway filed a Notice of alibi which particulars stated that on the day of the incident he was at home; the whole day with Abena Kwakyewa of House No. 13, Ekrornase. The trial judge ruled that he should call the witness to establish the alibi DW1 Abena Kwakyewa who both the first convict and the appellant relied on. DW1 testified that the appellant is her brother and they stayed together at Ekromase the whole day until 5pm. At about a little after 5pm, the first convict came with two others in a taxi cab and after consultations with them, the appellant informed her he was going to Accra to pick a car for commercial activities. They left and she did not hear from them until she heard that they were arrested.It is clear that the appellants defence hinged on Alibi. Section 131 of Act 30 dealt with Alibi. The trial judge was right when he allowed the appellant to file his notice of alibi with particulars and called a witness to that effect at a time the prosecution had called two witnesses. See R v Val-Vannis (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 199. The defence of alibi does not rule out the possibility of the trial Court rejecting alibi and accepting the evidence of the prosecution witnesses implicating the appellant. See Forkuo vs The Republic [1997-98] 1 GLR 1 CA. In the alibi filed it was stated that the appellant was with DW1 for the whole day. However, in DW1's evidence, she stated that he was with her until 5pm when he left. Incidentally, it was the same time that PW2, PW3 and PW4 identified the appellant as the very person who was with the first convict at the time they were at Krornanmu. I agree with the learned trial judge that the alibi was not proved and that DW1 was only belatedly brought into the picture as damage repair. The alibi is therefore an afterthought and I reject it.Moreover, the accused was only required to only raise a reasonable doubt as to his guilt See section 11(3) of NRCD 323 (Supra). The appellant was therefore required to have explained the inconsistencies in his previous statement with regard to his testimony in court if he was to be believed. In Yaro v The Republic [1979] GLR 10 the court held that a previous statement made by a witness to the Police which was in distinct conflict in his evidence on oath was always admissible to discredit or contradict him and it should be presumed that the evidence on oath was false unless he gives a satisfactory explanation of the prior inconsistent statement. in the instant appeal, the appellant in Exhibit 'H' his caution statement stated that the first convict passed by his house when going to farm around 7am on the day of the incident and told him that his brother had come from Accra to Komanmu and that he would go to Mankessim before visiting his brother. He again stated in his unsworn statement that at about 10am, the first convict came to him to borrow ¢10,000.00 (GH¢1.00) to travel with but he told him that he was going to travel to Mankessim to buy some materials for selling. Both of them joined the same vehicle but he told the driver he would go to his sister before paying him. They got to Mankessim at 11 am and the first convict left for Kromanmu and returned at 3.30pm and at 4.20pm, they left together with a brother of the first convict to Accra. His evidence in court is completely at variance. The appellant testified that he was helping his sister when the first convict came and told him that one of those he came with has a brother in Accra and was looking for a driver so he took his identity card, informed his sister and left in a Tico taxi cab. Furthermore, DW1 told the court that the appellant was with her the whole day until 5pm before they left for Accra. The appellant's evidence is thus discredited and could not be believed.I agree with the learned trial judge that the appellant's presence at the place of arrest in Accra, while attempting to sell the taxi cab with the first convict was not explained enough. The defence put up by the appellant cannot be acceptable and therefore not reasonably probable. I therefore find that the appellant and the convict committed the robbery as charged. I hold that the prosecution proved their case beyond reasonable doubt and therefore the conviction and sentence should not be disturbed. The appeal is hereby refused and the judgment of the trial court is affirmed. COUNSEL: MR. GUSTAV ADDINGTON FOR THE APPELLANT MR. GEORGE OFORI (PRINCIPAL STATE ATTORNEY) FOR THE RESPONDENT
