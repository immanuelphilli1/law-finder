DEFENDANT / APPELLANT

 

K. A. ACQUAYE, J.A:

The Appellants were charged on two counts of conspiracy to murder contrary to Sections 23 and 46 and murder contrary to Section 46 of the Criminal Offences Act 29 of 1960. Murder is an indictable offence which Section 204 of The criminal and other offences (procedure) Act 30 of 1960 mandates that it be Tried by a jury.Before the Fast Track High Court (where the accused have been Arraigned) Could empanel a jury for their trial the first two accused persons applied to the trial court for an order directed at the Registrar to provide to the 1st and 2nd accused persons and/or their Counsel the names and particulars of the potential jurors in the case. The application was premised on the sworn affidavit in support that the Applicants had a legal right to object, reject and challenge any juror empanelled by the court to sit on the case, and for this right to be properly exercised every facility and opportunity must be given the Applicants to take the appropriate objections and challenges to ensure their right to a fair trial is not compromised. Counsel contended that they will be unable to exercise that right to properly object and challenge on any ground whatsoever unless they are given the benefit of knowing in advance the names and particulars of potential jurors in the case to enable them properly inform themselves as to the proper objections and challenges to take in respect of them. Counsel for the Applicants argued that since this case has aroused considerable public interest and discussions in the media and the prosecution had sought an order that the jury be sequestrated it would be appropriate for the court to make the orders sought to ensure a diligent and thorough selection of fair minded jury uninfluenced by the media. The application was resisted by the prosecution. The prosecution contended that the law makes provision for the accused persons to object to any of the jurors either peremptorily or for cause and does not accord the accused to have prior knowledge of the names and particulars of jurors before being empanelled. The prosecution argued that the right to challenge any of the jurors is accessible at the time the jurors are being empanelled and that granting the application would risk the security of the jurors. In his ruling the trial judge agreed that for an accused to be able to lodge any challenge for cause he must know the juror and his background. He however found that the law does not say so and that giving out the full list poses potential risk of intimidation, influence and or threat to the jurors especially to those who would not be empanelled. He therefore dismissed the application. It is against the dismissal of the application that the applicants have lodged this appeal arguing three grounds of appeal, namely i)The ruling of the learned judge undermined the right of Appellants to a fair trial. ii)The ruling of the trial judge will result in a miscarriage of justice to Appellants. iii)The learned judge erred in law when he held that the benefit of convenience favoured the refusal of the application. Arguing the first ground of appeal Counsel for the Appellants submitted that it is a fundamental right of an accused person to be tried by an impartial jury selected from a representative cross-section of the community in which the crime was committed. It is to guarantee this right that the Act accords the accused the right to challenge the selection of the jury peremptory or for cause. With regard to peremptory challenges which allows an accused to challenge three selected jurors without assigning any reason Counsel submitted that the accused can only make up his mind if he has good reason to believe that the juror challenged would not be impartial. Counsel argued that Without knowing the particulars of any juror selected to join the panel of jurors the accused cannot in all sincerity exercise his right to peremptory challenge the selection of any particular juror and recommended that the rejection of a juror without giving reason should be banned. Counsel quoted Section 252 of Act 30/60 which lists the grounds for which challenges for cause may be allowed and submitted that they provide sufficient reasons why sufficient information about potential jurors should be given to accused persons on trial on indictment.In opposing the appeal Counsel for the Republic admitted the right of an accused to challenge jurors to be empanelled on a trial on indictment but restricted the right to only the time the jurors are to be empanelled for the trial of a particular case. Counsel for the Republic also submitted that that right cannot be stretched to mean a right by an accused person to have a list and particulars of the array of all potential jurors selected for the criminal session of the year in question. I have looked at the reasons canvassed by Counsel for the Appellants in support of this ground for the need of accused persons to have sufficient information on potential jurors before their selection to enable an accused person form an informed opinion to challenge the selection of any juror whether peremptorily or for cause. Attractive as the argument may seem it does not conform to the law for trial by jury as set out in our Criminal and Other Offences (Procedure) Act 30/1960. Jury trial in this country is governed by statute and anything or idea not contained in or to be implied from the statute is inapplicable. Sections 205 to 252 of the Criminal and Other Offences (Procedure) Act 30/1960 lists the qualification of jurors and how they are selected. During the session the list is displayed on the notice board of the District Court for three weeks after which the District Court is enjoined by Section 211 to "hold a public sitting for considering and disposing of the notices then received, and shall revise and settle the lists by the addition to or cancellation of names and by correcting the errors ..........". When the list is prepared it is sent to the Registrar of the High Court where the trials are to take place. Section 246 of Act 30/1960 provides that 1. At the sitting of the court to try criminal cases triable by jury the names of the jurors summoned shall be written on separate pieces of card or paper of equal size and put into a box. 2. Where a jury is required the registrar or other officer of the court shall, in open court, draw from the box by lot until the required number of jurors appear. 3. After just cause of challenge is allowed those who remain as fair and indifferent shall constitute the jury for the trial. Section 249 of the Act also provides that "when the jurors are ready to be sworn, the registrar or any other officer of the court shall address the accused person as follows: "The jurors who are to try you are now about to be sworn; if you object to any of them, you must do so as they come to the book to be sworn, and before they are sworn, and you shall be heard". Section 250 of the Act then provides that "There shall not be a challenge to the array, but an accused, personally or by counsel, shall be allowed to challenge three of the jurors by way of peremptory challenge without assigning cause". It is after this that Section 251 allows challenges for cause for a lot of grounds stated therein. It can be seen from the above that jurors are carefully chosen and screened before selection. Even during selection an accused has the right to challenge or object to three of the jurors chosen without giving any reason. For the challenges for cause it is only when the accused or his counsel knows any of the proposed jurors who is not fit to act as juror that they can challenge for cause. The Act does not allow an accused person the right to investigate all the over 30 persons listed as potential jurors for faults or incapabilities as this application seeks to do. As the trial judge rightly asked in his ruling "if a juror/s not going to be empanelled for the case, why should the name and particulars be exposed" The reasons assigned for this ground of appeal is not satisfactory and it is dismissed. The second ground of appeal is that "The ruling of the trial court will result in a miscarriage of justice to the Appellants". Counsel contended that there is a danger that the Appellants may be tried by persons who have pre-existing biases against them which they would have discovered if they had prior opportunity to know the particulars of the jurors as requested. I agree with Counsel for the Respondent that this ground of appeal is speculative and that if in the course of the trial a juror is found to be biased the Appellants have the right to point it out for it to be dealt with according to law. This ground of appeal also fails and it is dismissed. The third ground of appeal is also dismissed as the trial judge was right in dismissing the application and there are provisions in the Act for dealing with recalcitrant jurors.In the whole the appeal fails and it is dismissed.
