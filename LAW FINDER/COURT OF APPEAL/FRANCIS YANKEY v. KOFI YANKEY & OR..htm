JUDGMENT

LARTEY J.A:

This appeal is from the decision of the Circuit Court, Takoradi delivered on the 27th day of November 2001.

By his writ of summons the first plaintiff claimed against the defendant the following reliefs:

1. "Recovery of House Number 93/1 Kojokrom, via Sekondi.

2. Arrears of rent collected from the tenants from January 1998 to date of judgment.

3. Perpetual injunction restraining the defendant from the tenants resident in House Number 93/1, Kojokrom.”

Accompanying the writ was the first plaintiff's statement of claim, which was amended at the end of the trial. Incidentally, all the parties in this action are brothers and sister. In his amended statement of claim the first plaintiff avers that the plot in dispute was gifted to the second plaintiff by the late Nana Panyin Ye Na, their stepfather, by reason of good service rendered by the second plaintiff to the deceased, who requested the first plaintiff to construct a house thereon for the second plaintiff. In the amended statement of claim it was alleged that the defendant who was on pension came to stay in the house from Samreboi, but has arrogated to himself ownership of the house and has been collecting rent without the knowledge of the first plaintiff. The first plaintiff also averred that sometime 1968 the defendant was indebted to one Egya Kum in consequence of which the house was attached, and it was the first plaintiff who successfully claimed it through an interpleader summons.

Similarly, the second plaintiff who earlier on in the proceedings had successfully applied to be made a party as second plaintiff filed her amended statement of claim in which she described herself as a sister to both the first plaintiff and the defendant. She confirmed substantially what the first plaintiff averred. She further alleged that the first plaintiff gave 'Aseda' of one bottle schnapps to the late Nana Panyin Ye Na II.

The defendant denied all the allegation of facts contained in the statement of claim. He contended that he acquired the plot in dispute from the said Nana Panyin Ye Na in 1950 for a consideration and later he put up two rooms where he was residing until R.T. Briscoe, his employers, gave him a bungalow. It was also his contention that he solely put up the house from his own resources and that he permitted the first plaintiff to occupy same as a licensee. It was his case that he has been exercising right of ownership on the house since 1950 without let or hindrance, and therefore he is lawfully entitled to any rent accruing there from. He denied the house ever being attached by the said Egya Kum, and that there has not been any occasion when the plaintiff through interpleader summons had claimed it. The defendant finally counter claimed for a declaration of title to the disputed house and perpetual injunction restricting the first plaintiff, his servants, agents or assigns from having anything to do with the house.

At the trial, the plaintiffs called two witnesses, one of who was PW1, Kwaku Nyamekye, who described himself as the linguist to the late chief of Kojokrom, Nana Panyin Ye Na. He claimed to be present when a grant of the disputed plot was made in favour of the first plaintiff by the said chief for the purpose of building a house thereon for the second plaintiff who had rendered meritorious service to the chief. The witness also testified as to the 'Aseda’ of the bottle of drinks produced by the first plaintiff and accepted by the chief who poured libation with it. He further alleged being one of those who inspected the plot.

After reviewing the evidence produced by the parties, the trial judge resolved the issue of capacity, which for the first time was raised by counsel for the defendant in his address at the end of the trial. The judge also dealt with the issue as to which of the parties in the case built the disputed house. Upon evaluating the whole of the evidence, he consequently dismissed the defendant's counterclaim and entered judgment for the plaintiffs for the recovery of possession of the house.

It is against this judgment that the defendant launched this appeal to this Court on the following grounds:

“(a) The judgment is against the weight of evidence

(b) The judge erred in law when he held that the 1st plaintiff had capacity to institute the action against the defendant.

(c) The judge erred in holding that the defendant's inability and or refusal to call a witness was fatal to his case.

(d)  The judge failed to consider whether or not there was a valid gift to the 2nd plaintiff.

(e) The judge erred in law in entering judgment for the 2nd plaintiff who was technically speaking not a party to the suit.

(f) The judge failed in evaluating the evidence of PW1 who exhibited that he could not be a witness of truth.

(g) Additional grounds of appeal would be filed upon receipt of the record of Appeal.”

The argument which learned counsel for the defendant argued in support of grounds (e) was that the second plaintiff not having been properly joined as a party to the suit, the trial judge erred in law when he entered judgment for her. Furthermore, that in her viva voce evidence the second plaintiff gave her name as Ekua Awortwe even though there is nothing on record to indicate that Ekua Awortwe was the same person as Maame Awortwe who was joined to the suit as second plaintiff. In support of his argument, counsel purported to have relied on order 16 rule (8) of LI 1129, and submitted that by the second plaintiff's failure to comply with the rule, her action must fail.

It must be said in the first place that Order 16 rule (8) of LI 1129 has no relevance whatsoever to the case before us. That rule relates to a judgment obtained between a defendant and a third party, and it simply stipulates that where in an action a defendant serves notice on a third party, the court may during or after the trial, order the judgment to be entered for the defendant against the third party or for the third party against the defendant. It also says in sub-rule (2) that where judgment is given against the defendant and judgment is given for the defendant against the third party, execution shall not issue against the third party without the leave of the court until the judgment against the defendant has been satisfied. The rule as a whole has nothing to do with the matter under consideration.

However in fairness to the defendant, it must be pointed out that the relevant rule, which should have provided some guide to counsel in his attack on the issue of the joinder of the second plaintiff is Order 15 rule 6 of LI 1129, which deals with misjoinder and nonjoinder of parties in an action. In considering this rule we are unable to see how it can be of aid to the contention that the second plaintiff was improperly or unnecessarily made a party, for in sub-rule (1) it has been clearly stated as follows:—

"No proceedings shall be defeated by reason of the mis-joinder or non-joinder of any party; and the court may in any proceedings determine the issues or questions in dispute so far as they affect the rights and interests of the persons who are parties to the proceedings."

It is in the light of the foregoing that Lord Denning's dictum in Gurtner v. Circuit & Anor [1968] 2 Q.B. 587 at p.595 becomes apposite. He states:

“It seems to me that when two parties are in dispute in an action at law, and the determination of that dispute will directly affect a third party in his legal rights or in his pocket..... then the court in its discretion may allow him to be added as a party on such terms as it thinks fit. By so doing, the court achieves the object of the rule. It enables all matters in dispute to be effectually and completely determined and adjudicated upon between all those directly concerned in the outcome."

No one can deny that the second plaintiff was not an ordinary intervener; she applied to be a party in the action for the purpose of protecting her legal rights. And here the test as proposed by Devlin J in Amon vs. Raphael Tuck & Sons Limited (1956) 1 Q.B. 357 becomes relevant: Would the order for which the plaintiff was asking directly affect the intervener not in his commercial interests, but in the enjoyment of his legal right? Similarly, in the local case of Aegis Shipping Co. Ltd (1973) 1 GLR 438 pp 443-444, the scope of the rule was explained to embrace the absolute discretion of the court to determine whether having regard to the nature of the pleadings and the issues raised, the intervener is a person who ought to have been joined, or he is a person whose presence would enable the court, effectually and completely, to decide the issues raised between the parties in the cause or matter.

In the present case we are satisfied that the second plaintiff was properly joined as a party in the action. She disclosed her interest in the plot of land on which the house was built. We are of the view that the court properly exercised its discretion in granting the second plaintiff's application for joinder. This ground of appeal should fail.

In canvassing his argument on the issue of the first plaintiff's alleged lack of capacity, counsel for the defendant relied heavily on the case of Sarkodee I vs. Boateng II (1982-83) G.L.R 715 in an attempt to challenge the capacity of the plaintiff, belatedly in counsel's address to the court. The case, among others, dealt with the issue of capacity to sue, and the principle enunciated therein on the point was that where a plaintiff whose capacity is challenged or put in issue must establish it by cogent evidence. See also Chapman vs. Ocloo & Another (1957) WALR 84 and Sokpui II & others v Tay Agbozo III & others (1951) 13 WACA, 241.

In the instant case, there was no challenge whatsoever by the defendant either in the pleadings or at the trial. In other words, the capacity of the plaintiffs was never raised as an issue. Admittedly, the issue of capacity may be raised at any time, but it must be done timeously so as to offer the party whose capacity is put in issue the opportunity to establish it by cogent evidence. The question that arises is how does the defendant's counsel expect the plaintiff to provide the evidence to establish capacity when counsel raised it for the first time in his address? Clearly, it was failure of the defendant to raise the issue of capacity before or during the trial that disabled the first plaintiff to prove his capacity to sue.

It was also argued on behalf of the defendant that the first plaintiff failed to tender in evidence documents from the Shama Ahanta East Metropolitan Assembly, in particular, exhibits 1-1F, indicating the plaintiff's interest in the land. And that being the case there was no basis for the judge to hold that the first plaintiff had any legal interest in the land. We do not think that the documents per se confer any right of ownership on the defendant. The trial judge made a careful evaluation of the exhibits and found that they were issues between 1999 and 2001. And as the trial judge rightly observed, if the defendant had been in possession of the house since 1982 why could he not tender similar documents in respect of the previous years?

No argument appears to have been made for the rest of the grounds in this appeal other than the capacity of the two plaintiffs. We must say that we find nothing wrong with the decision of the trial judge. We would therefore affirm the judgment and dismiss the appeal with costs.

(SGD)

F. M. LARTEY

JUSTICE OF APPEAL

JUDGMENT OF ANSAH, JA.

I have had the benefit of reading beforehand the judgment just read by the President and I concur with his reasoning and also agree that the appeal be dismissed. I only wish to add these few words of my own that even though the implementary steps consequent upon the order joining the second plaintiff to the action were not taken, contrary to the rules, the proceedings were saved by the rule mentioned in the judgment I am concurring with, to wit, Order 15 rule 6 of LI 1129. I agree that the appeal be dismissed.

(SGD)

ANSAH, JA.

JUSTICE OF APPEAL

I also agree.
