ACCRA, GHANA

SUIT NO. H1/72/2011

A.D. 7TH JULY 2011.

 

GERTRUDE SHARON PERLINGER

Versus

THE COCA COLA BOTTLING COMPANY

 
 

YAW APPAU, J.A.

This is an appeal from a judgment of the Commercial Division of the High Court, Accra. The defendants in the court below, viz; Coca Cola Bottling Company Limited are the appellants herein. Gertrude Sharon Perlinger who is the respondent herein was the plaintiff in the trial court. I would in this judgment maintain their descriptions plaintiff and respondent as obtained in the trial court.

The facts in this case are not in controversy. What happened was that the plaintiff arranged with the defendant for the supply to her of forty-three (43) crates of defendants products made up of coca-cola, fanta, sprite, etc for sale in the plaintiff’s restaurant and bar. This was when plaintiff was about to open her new restaurant which she had established called Golden Oyster Restaurant and Bar. This was in October 2006 as the plaintiff pleaded and the total cost or price of the forty-three (43) crates was four million, four hundred and ninety-eight thousand cedis (c4, 498, 000.00), now four hundred and forty-nine Ghana cedis, eighty pesewas (GHc449.80). Paragraph 5 of plaintiff’s statement of claim filed on 20/11/08 and which appears at page 3 of the record reads:

 ‘’5. Somewhere in October 2006, plaintiff paid defendant c4, 498, 000.00 for 43 crates of soft drinks which were supplied to her by defendant.’’

The defendant supplied the forty-three (43) crates of soft drinks as requested. However, according to plaintiff, when she started the sale of the drinks to her customers who patronised the restaurant and bar, some of her customers drew her attention to the unwholesomeness of some of the drinks. They were said to contain some particles. Plaintiff informed the Food and Drugs Board about the development. Food and Drugs Board (FDB) took samples of the drinks for testing that proved positive. At that time, plaintiff had sold out about twenty (20) out of the forty-three (43) crates already leaving twenty-three (23).

Plaintiff informed the defendant about the existence of particles in some of the drinks. The defendant sent its officials to retrieve the remaining twenty-three (23) crates, for replacement with new ones. It, however, took the defendant about seven (7) months after the incident was reported to it to do the replacement. This was after series of correspondence had gone on between plaintiff’s lawyers and the defendant and its lawyers.

In the first letter dated 9th March 2007, plaintiff through her lawyers bemoaned the inability of the defendant to recover and replace the remaining drinks despite its knowledge of the unwholesomeness of some of the drinks. Plaintiff therefore demanded the immediate replacement of the remaining contaminated drinks or their value. Plaintiff again admonished the defendant to also address the losses she had suffered due to loss of patronage arising from the sale of the unwholesome products of the defendant.

The defendant complied by recovering the said contaminated drinks seven (7) months later and replaced them. It also added a few additional crates as a form of appeasement. Though the actual number of crates added to the replaced 23 crates was not indicated in the record, the plaintiff did not deny that fact. Plaintiff later wrote back to the defendant through her lawyer to demand compensation in the form of loss of profit on the 23 crates of minerals for the period of seven (7) months which it took defendant to replace plus compensation for the loss of her business capital due to the loss of patronage of her restaurant arising from the sale of the contaminated drinks. The defendant offered to pay plaintiff twenty-two million cedis (c22 million), now GHc2, 200.00 for any losses allegedly suffered by the plaintiff but plaintiff rejected the offer and decided to seek redress in court, thus the suit in the court below filed on 20/11/2008 and now on appeal before us.

In the suit, plaintiff sought for the following reliefs:

(a)  Two Million Dollars ($2, 000, 000.00) or its cedi equivalent for General Damages for supplying contaminated drinks to plaintiff’s restaurant.

(b) SPECIAL DAMAGES

(i)                Loss of Ghc500.00 daily business profit from October 2006 till date of final payment.

(ii)             35% interest per annum on the interest owed and or paid to NIB and all other interest accrued on all other loans from other financial institutions expended on the restaurant and lodge.

(iii)           GHc2, 000.00 per month from October 2006 to date of judgment being salaries of workers.

The trial court, after hearing evidence from plaintiff and her only witness who was an officer from the Food and Drugs Board and then the defendant, dismissed plaintiff’s relief (b) of her claim which was for special damages made up of: (i) loss of GHc500.00 daily business profit; (ii) 35% interest per annum on loans contracted from the Bank and (iii) GHc2, 000.00 per month from October 2006 being salaries of her workers. This, according to the trial court, was as a result of plaintiff’s failure to lead evidence to establish those claims on the balance of the probabilities.

The plaintiff has not appealed against this decision of the court below and I have no qualms with this decision of the trial court as from the record, there is no doubt that plaintiff could not lead any evidence to establish the claim for special damages. The trial court, however, found the defendant liable for negligence on its part for supplying plaintiff with unwholesome drinks and granted plaintiff’s relief (a) by awarding her one hundred and eighty million cedis (c180 million), now (GHc18, 000.00) as nominal damages for negligence and it is this decision that forms the crux of this appeal.

Though the trial court admitted that the facts in this case differ completely from the cases it cited in support of its judgment starting from the celebrated case of Donogue v Stevenson [1932] AC 562 - HL, the trial court nevertheless based its judgment on negligence arising from the Donogue v Stevenson principle. It appears the trial court found itself in a quandary with the mere mention of strange particles in bottles of drink. What immediately came into the mind of the trial court and controlled it throughout the trial which culminated in its judgment now on impeachment with the mention of strange particles in bottles of drink was the case of Donogue v Stevenson. The trial court did not avert its mind to the fact that the foundation of the instant case did not place it under the categories of cases based on the Donogue v Stevenson principle on negligence.

The notice of appeal was filed on 28/09/2009. It contained five (5) grounds in all numbered (a - e) with particulars given where the ground alleged error of law. The grounds read:

a.     The trial judge gave a decision in negligence, which is inconsistent with the case in breach of contract before him.

b.     The trial judge erred in law in granting damages for negligence, when the elements of negligence had not been established. (Particulars of errors given).

c.      The trial judge erred in law by failing to consider evidence of plaintiff’s negligence in granting damages.

d.     The trial judge erred in law in assessing damages in the sum of GHc18, 000.00. (Particulars of errors given).

e.     The decision is against the weight of evidence.

The defendant argued or made submissions on grounds (a - d) separately and seemed to have abandoned the last ground (e).

In my view, the defendant could have argued the first four grounds of appeal under two heads; namely grounds (a) and (c) since both grounds (b) and (d) flow from grounds (a) and (c) respectively. Under grounds (a) and (b), the main beef of the appellant was that while respondent’s action was grounded on contract, the trial court gave her judgment on negligence out of which the award of Ghc18, 000.00 as damages was made.

According to defendant the trial court erred in basing its judgment on negligence when the elements of negligence, which are; i, the existence of duty of care, ii, breach of the said duty and iii, injury arising from the breach, were all absent in the instant case. Defendant’s contention was that by the nature of the writ, pleadings, evidence led and even the final address filed by the plaintiff’s lawyer for the trial court’s consideration at the close of the case in the court below, plaintiff’s action was grounded on breach of contract under the Sale of Goods Act, 1962 (Act 137). The trial court therefore had no business substituting the case of the plaintiff by basing its decision on negligence when the elements of negligence were not proven. Some of the cases defendant cited in support of his submissions on these grounds were DAM v ADDO [1962] GLR 200, SC; GIHOC v HANNA ASSI (No 2) [2007-2008] SCGLR 17; BISI v TABIRI [1984-86] 2GLR 282, etc.

With regard to the award of damages to the tune of GHc18, 000.00 in favour of the plaintiff, the defendant was of the view that the trial court erred terribly when it merely awarded damages of GHc18, 000.00 without any basis for the assessment, particularly where the defendant replaced all the twenty-three crates of minerals that were alleged to be contaminated and even added extra crates. The defendant referred to the Supreme Court case of STANDARD CHARTERED BANK v NELSON [1989-99] SCGLR 812 as well as the unreported case of BOGOSO GOLD LTD v ESTATE OF JOSEPH NTRAKWA dated 29th July 2010 in support of this submission.

The defendant therefore prayed this Court to do justice by reversing the decision of the trial court which was based on negligence that was non-existent as well as the high damages that was awarded without any justification.

In her response to the arguments of the defendant, the plaintiff held a different view and said nowhere in the record did she state that her action was based on contract. This was what she exactly said in the opening paragraphs of her submissions in answer to that of the defendant in respect of ground (a):

“From this ground the appellant would want us to believe that the plaintiff took out an action against the defendant in contract and the trial judge suo motu found for the plaintiff in negligence. The appellant contends that the action was based on a claim for breach of contract. I have searched diligently through the record and I have not come across this claim for breach of contract by the plaintiff.”

Having stated as quoted above, plaintiff went ahead to reproduce her claim as endorsed on the writ of summons and maintained that though she did not specifically plead that her claim was one on negligence, the facts, pleadings and evidence clearly show that the claim was founded on negligence but not contract since the respondent was seeking damages for the negligence of the defendant company in supplying contaminated drinks to her. Respondent quoted the dictum of Atugubah, JSC in Hanna Assi (No 2) v GIHOC Refrigeration & Household Products Ltd (No 2) [2007-2008] SCGLR 16, on the modern attitude of the courts on pleadings and contended that since one of the issues for trial was; “Whether or not the plaintiff was negligent”, the trial court did not err in basing its judgment on negligence.

The above submission by the plaintiff that it has not been stated anywhere in the record that her claim was for breach of contract reminds me of the dictum of the Privy Council in the case of VANDERPUYE v BOTCHWAY [1957] 2 WALR 16 at p. 21 that; “...in order to determine into which category a particular suit falls, the court must apply the test of what is the real issue between the parties and not look only at the wording of the plaint.”

Incidentally, plaintiff admitted the fact that she did not also specifically state that her claim was based on negligence as she purported to indicate in her submissions that she was seeking damages for the negligence of the defendant company in supplying her with contaminated drinks.

This principle of determining the real issues in controversy between parties in a civil suit through substance instead of form as laid down by the Privy Council in the above cited case has been religiously followed by our courts. Cecilia Koranteng-Addow, J (of blessed memory) in trying to find out the issue at stake between the parties in the case of AKATI v NARTEY [1980] GLR 218 relied on this dictum of the Privy Council when she stated under holding (1) of her judgment at page 219 as follows: - “the jurisdiction of a court to hear any particular matter was ordinarily determined by the plaintiff’s claim and not the defendant’s defence. Hence a defence should not ordinarily oust the jurisdiction of the court. However, in cases where it was not clear from the plaint of the plaintiff the category into which the particular case fell, it was the issue between the parties which determined the type of case it was”.

Also, in BENYI v AMO [1959] GLR 92, Adumoa Bossman, J (as he then was) who was called upon to decide whether the matter before him was a ‘succession’ or ‘land’ matter in order to determine whether or not he had jurisdiction to try the case, relied on this very dictum laid down by the Privy Council in the Vanderpuye v Botchway case.

The truth is that the instant suit arose as a result of a sale transaction between plaintiff and the defendant company. The plaintiff requested for the supply of minerals or ‘soft’drinks’ as they are usually called, to be sold by her in her restaurant and in the course of the transaction, the defendant unknowingly  supplied her with partly wholesome and partly unwholesome products. As a result of the unwholesomeness of some of the products, which came to the knowledge of some of her patrons, plaintiff said; (1) she lost patronage of her facility and (2) it took defendant not less than seven (7) months to replace the unwholesome drinks for which she made a loss with regard to her profits.

Clearly, this is not a case of negligence since it were not the consumers of the unwholesome drinks who sued for injuries sustained as a result of the negligence of the defendant in supplying unwholesome drinks. It was the plaintiff (i.e. the buyer but not the consumer) who sued for the supply of goods to her which were not fit for the purpose for which they were bought. The defendant did not therefore owe plaintiff any duty of care under the transaction which it breached; neither did the plaintiff suffer any injury or bodily harm for the supply of the unfit goods.

The whole transaction falls under the Sale of Goods Act, 1962 [Act 137] as the defendant clearly spelt out in its submissions. It was a contract of sale. Section 1 (1) of the Act provides: “A contract of sale of goods is a contract by which the seller agrees to transfer the property in the goods to the buyer for a consideration called the price, consisting wholly or partly of money.”

Section 3 of the Act explains how a contract of sale is made. It provides that; “Subject to this Act and subject to any other enactment, a contract of sale of goods (a) may be made in writing or by word of mouth, or partly in writing and partly by word of mouth, or (b) may be implied from the conduct of the parties.”

In the instant action, the plaintiff requested for the supply of forty-three (43) crates of minerals from the defendant company at a cost of GHc449.80, by word of mouth, which the defendant duly supplied upon payment. The contract therefore terminated or ended after the supply of the required quantity and the payment thereof. Section 13 (1) (b) of the Act however requires that the goods supplied by the seller to the buyer must be fit for the purpose for which they were purchased. This section reads: -

“Subject to this Act and to any other enactment, there is no implied warranty or condition as to the quality or fitness for a particular purpose of goods supplied under a contract of sale except;

(b) that where the goods are of a description which are supplied by the seller in the course of the seller’s business and the buyer expressly or by implication makes known the purpose for which the goods are required, there is an implied condition that the goods are reasonably fit for that purpose.”

Section 13 (6) also says section 13 (1) (b) applies to goods delivered in pursuance of the contract and extends to the boxes, tins, bottles or any other containers in which the goods are contained.

The goods in question in this case were soft drinks contained in bottles and the only condition attached as per section 13 (1) (b) of the Act was for the defendant to supply plaintiff with the right quality or specification of goods requested for; i.e. wholesome drinks that plaintiff could sell to the public. So if it turned out that some of the drinks defendant supplied were unwholesome and therefore of not the right quality that plaintiff could sell, the defendant would have breached the sale condition or warranty to supply the right quality of goods requested for as provided under section 13 (1) (b) of the Act. This breach of condition or warranty is clearly one of a breach of contract to supply the right quality of goods and if as a result, the plaintiff made any losses as a result of the breach, the remedy opened to her was damages for breach of contract but not damages for negligence.

Section 55 of the Act (i.e. Act 137) which is headed: Damages for breach of condition or warranty, reads: -

“Where the seller is guilty of a breach of the seller’s fundamental obligation or of a condition or warranty of the contract, the buyer

(a)  may maintain an action against the seller for damages for the breach complained of, or

(b) may set up a claim to the damages in diminution or extinction of the price.”

The plaintiff decided to invoke sub-section (a) of section 55 of the Act as quoted above by suing the defendant for damages for supplying drinks not fit for the purpose for which they were bought. That claim, as a matter of fact and law, was a claim for breach of contract as the defendant asserted in its written submissions, though not specifically stated, but not negligence as the trial court wrongly found.

It must be noted, however that the assessment of damages for breach of a condition of sale or warranty is not an arbitrary exercise. Section 56 of the Act provides the mode through which such damages must be assessed and it reads: - “The measure of damages in an action under section 55 is the loss which could reasonably have been foreseen by the seller at the time when the contract was made as likely to result from the breach of contract.”

The fact is that when the seller (in this case the defendant), got wind about the unwholesomeness of the remaining twenty-three (23) crates which it later replaced, it should have known that any failure on its part to replace the drinks immediately would occasion loss of profits to the buyer, i.e. the plaintiff whose main purpose for purchasing them was to sell same to the public for profit. Having failed to replace the goods immediately until after a period of seven (7) months, it is no gainsaying that the plaintiff did make some loss out of the twenty-three (23) crates for the period in question. The question however is; how much profit would the plaintiff have made from the twenty-three (23) crates of minerals within the seven-month period that it took the defendant to replace them if they had been replaced immediately the unwholesomeness came to its knowledge?

The plaintiff did not provide any evidence to assist the court below to assess that. Instead, the trial court arbitrarily awarded damages of 180 million cedis (GHc18, 000.00), which it termed nominal and which it grounded wrongly on negligence. Granted the plaintiff is entitled to damages for breach of contract for supplying unfit goods, which I think she is entitled to, could the 180 million cedis the trial court awarded in her favour as nominal damages be the sum defendant reasonably have foreseen as the loss of profits plaintiff might suffer for its failure to replace the 23 crates of minerals immediately, when the actual price or value of all the 23 crates was not above the sum of c2, 292, 000.00 (now GHc239.20), not even mentioning the fact that in replacing the 23 crates, the defendant added additional crates to reduce the loss of profits? I do not think so.

Though the authorities are legion that the assessment of damages is peculiarly within the province of the trial court and that the appellate Court is not supposed to interfere unnecessarily, the authorities go further to state that this Court can interfere if the finding is out of proportion to the facts. In the case of AKUFFO v ISSAKA [1966] GLR 476, the Supreme Court held that the appellate court would only set aside an award if it is shown that the trial court/judge left some relevant matters out of account or that the trial court took some irrelevant matters into consideration in assessing damages. The highest court of the land echoed the same point in the cases of BRESSAH v ASANTE & Anor [1965] 1 GLR 117; KARAM v ASHKAR [1963] 1 GLR 38; STANDARD CHARTERED BANK (GH) LTD v NELSON [1998-99] SCGLR 810 @ holding (2) and JUXTON-SMITH v KLM DUTCH AIRLINES [2005-2006] SCGLR, 438.

In the Bressah case supra, the Supreme Court held that; ‘’An appellate court would only interfere with the quantum of damages on the ground that the trial judge acted upon some wrong principle of law or that the amount awarded was so extremely high or so very small as to make it an erroneous estimate’’.

Though the trial court was right when it concluded that the plaintiff was entitled to only nominal damages for her failure to lead evidence to establish any actual loss suffered, it acted upon a wrong principle of law when it based the award on the negligence of the defendant when the main issue before the court did not arise from negligence. Again, the amount awarded as nominal damages was extremely high when the price or value of the drinks the plaintiff was expecting to make profits from within the delayed period of seven (7) months was just c2, 392, 000.00 or just (GHc239.20).

I think the 22 million cedis (GHc2, 200.00), which the defendant offered to pay the plaintiff as compensation for the delay in replacing the 23 crates of drinks in one of its correspondence with the plaintiff’s lawyer in addition to the extra crates added as appeasement was reasonable and the plaintiff should have accepted that in good faith to avoid the litigation that has spanned over a period of about two and a half years, which I find quite unnecessary. I would therefore allow the appeal and vary the damages awarded from GHc18, 000.00 to GHc5, 000.00 not for negligence but for a breach of a condition or warranty under the contract of sale and for the failure on the part of the defendant to effect the replacement timeously. I also reverse the costs of GHc2, 000.00 awarded by the trial court against the defendant.

Parties to bear their own costs of litigation.
