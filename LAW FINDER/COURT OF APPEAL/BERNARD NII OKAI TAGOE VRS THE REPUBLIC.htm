VRS

  

 

THE REPUBLIC

  

DEFENDANT / APPELLANT

A. ASARE KORANG, J.A.:

 The appellant was found guilty of the murder of one Nii Ardey Sackey by an Accra High Court presided over by Mr. Justice B.T. Aryeetey sitting with a jury on 2nd February 1990 and his sole ground of appeal to this court from the conviction and sentence of death imposed on him is that the verdict is unreasonable having regard to the evidence. It must be noted this ground of appeal was filed on 13th February 1990, a mere eleven days after the appellant had been convicted and sentenced. Subsequently the appellant filed two additional grounds of appeal namely: (1) The jury was not properly directed with regard to the disparity between the statement made by PWI to the police and her evidence in court. (2) The medical evidence is not conclusive of the cause of death of the deceased." Apparently these additional grounds were filed well out of time and without the leave of court as they were dated the 18th day of February 2002, twelve clear years after the conviction and sentence of the appellant: Those grounds, therefore, donotmerit any consideration in this appeal. The facts given in evidence by the prosecution were based mainly on the eye witness testimony of the fourth prosecution witness, Elizabeth Mawushie Commey, who strangely at the trial appeared reluctant to testify in support of the case for the prosecution claiming that she had forgotten about the incident that resulted in the death of the deceased.According to the fourth prosecution witness (to be referred to herein as PW4), she was a fishmonger who was working at the seashore with the appellant but because the appellant was stealing from her, she stopped working with him and thereafter engaged the deceased to work for her. She further testified that on the day of the incident, the deceased gathered some boxes in order to pack them. While the deceased was performing that task, the appellant came there and tried to fight with the deceased.PW4 separated them and advised them not to fight. As the deceased was going away, the appellant followed him. Later PW4 was informed that the deceased and the appellant were fighting on top of a hill. She went there and asked the appellant why he was still fighting with the deceased following her advice to them not to fight. The appellant was said to have replied that if he did not kill the deceased, then the deceased would kill him. Describing the scene where the fight took place, PW4 said there were canoes there and the deceased fell into one of the canoes. Then PW4 saw the appellant hitting the head of the deceased on the canoe. As the appellant resisted any attempt to separate him and the deceased, PW4 left the scene. Two or three days after the fight the deceased died. The medical doctor called by the prosecution stated that the cause of death was due to bleeding in or into the brain attributable to a trauma such as something hitting the head in an accident or a knock on the head by a heavy object; or through disease like hypertension where a blood vessel bursts in the brain such that there is bleeding into the brain tissue. The appellant, who testified on oath, stated that on 7th February 1986, he was in a canoe at the seashore waiting for the afternoon catch of fish to be brought in by the fishermen when the deceased came there. The appellant narrated what on previous occasions was happening between him and the deceased when a. catch of fish came in. He said with the arrival of the catch,the deceased would place his basket between the appellant's basket and if some fish fell from a canoe, it would land in the deceased's basket. Sometimes also the deceased would remove the appellant's basket and put his basket in place of that of the appellant. Later the customers of both the deceased and the appellant decided that any time some fish went into the deceased's basket, he and the appellant should share the proceeds. With this arrangement in place, the appellant and the deceased shared the proceeds of the sale of any fish each of them obtained. The appellant said on the day of the incident, he was with his customer, one Odde in the afternoon, when a 'canoe came in. As the appellant rushed towards the canoe, the deceased came there and the customer "warned" the appellant that it was through the deceased that the appellant got to know the customer; therefore anytime fish was shared, it should be given to the deceased to go and sell and share the proceeds with the appellant. When the appellant heard this new arrangement, he was apparently unhappy with it and hurled an insulting epithet at the deceased, to wit: "Onye" (meaning, your mother) . The appellant then asked the deceased whether he knew how he (appellant) had been getting his goods or fish and how his (appellant's) basket got to the seashore. The deceased retorted that he would meet the appellant at the hilltop and followed up by throwing his basket at the appellant who also attempted to throw back his basket at the deceased. PW4, Mawushie, however came between them and uttered a word "P00" which was a word only used by the deceased and PW4.Thereafter, the appellant was asked by his customer to carry some boxes of fish into a room. PW4 also asked the deceased to carry her boxes into the same room. The appellant saw a friend of the deceased called Asitola in the room. It seemed that Asitola had got wind of the fracas between the appellant and the deceased and he told the deceased to fight the appellant because the appellant was not strong. Appellant was changing his dress when he heard a voice behind him saying "hit him with it". The deceased hit the appellant with a metal spoon used for frying and tore his dress. The appellant removed his dress and gave it to the deceased to sew for him. The deceased refused to accept the dress. The appellant then drew nearer to the deceased and the deceased tried to push him down. The deceased held the legs of the appellant in an attempt to push him down and the appellant also got the head of the deceased under his armpit.As they were struggling,they both fell into a broken canoe with their legs up in the air.People watching them fight came and separated them.The appellant and the deceased had sand in their mouths and after the appellant had washed out the sand in his mouth with sea water, the deceased told him to go and prepare and come back. The appellant obliged and as he was leaving for his house accompanied by a friend called Allotey and the deceased, he heard a voice which turned out to be that of PW4 saying, "bite him".The deceased held the dress of the appellant; pushed him and attempted to give him a blow. The appellant managed to hold the hand of the deceased as the deceased brought his head to the chest of the appellant and bit him. The appellant went to the police station to report that he had been bitten by the deceased. He was treated at the hospital for the bite wound and then taken into police custody. Three days later, he heard that the deceased had died but he found the news of his death unbelievable because he had seen the deceased on the morning of his death.On all the evidence including in all probability the evidence of the doctor (PW3) that it was not normal for a person of the age of the deceased (18 yeas) to be hypertensive with the possibility of having a burst blood vessel, and the eyewitness account of PW4 (who inexplicably claimed she was suffering from a sudden bout of amnesia) with regard to the events of 7th February 1986, the appellant was found guilty of Murder by the jury.I must, however, observe that given the large number of people said to be present as the appellant and the deceased were fighting in the canoe, it was strange that none of those present, except PW4, was invited to testify at the trial of the appellant.Against his conviction and sentence the appellant has appealed to this court on the sole ground that:"the verdict is unreasonable and cannot be supported having regard to the evidence." It was submitted on behalf of the appellant that it was the deceased who first attacked him and there was no evidence that the appellant intended to kill the deceased nor did the prosecution lead evidence to show that the appellant intended to kill the deceased. Counsel for the appellant contended that the lack of evidence to prove the ingredient of intention to kill was fatal to the conviction and sentence of the appellant for murder and that from the evidence adduced the offence revealed was Manslaughter only and not Murder in consequence of which the appellant should have been discharged on the murder charge.Counsel for the respondent submitted that there was a fight between the deceased and the appellant which was unlawful by virtue of Section 38 of the Criminal Code 1960 (Act 29) which cannot be justified under the plea of self-defence and therefore the reliance of the appellant on that plea should be ignored as an afterthought.A careful reading of the submissions of appellant's counsel would show that the appellant is not relying so much on self-defence as justification for fighting and consequently killing the deceased as invoking the issue of whether intent to kill as an ingredient of the crime of murder was established on the evidence presented against him by the prosecution. There was no doubt, in the circumstances of this case that the appellant and the deceased engaged in a fight which was unlawful within the meaning of Sections 38 and 39 of Act 29. And even if the issue of self-defence was not addressed by counsel for the appellant in his submissions, that issue was adequately considered by the learned trial judge in his summing up when he stated as follows: "Looking at the evidence, if you come to the conclusion that the accused acted in self- defence when he fought the deceased, the harm will not be unlawful. If you come to the conclusion that the harm was not unlawful, it would mean that one of the five ingredients has not been satisfied and the Accused will be entitled to be acquitted". The learned trial judge also directed the jury on the ingredient of intention. This is what he said on the issue: "Finally, members of the jury you are to look at the evidence and come to a conclusion as to whether the unlawful harm was caused intentionally. As the saying goes, "Even the devil knowest not the mind of man". Unless a person makes his intention manifest by his expressed declaration, his intention is in general inferred from his acts (Section 11(1) and Section 11(2) of the Criminal Code read). In other words, if you seize the head of your friend and hit it against the floor believing that that might cause his death, although you may not intend to cause his death, if he dies, the law presumes that you intended to cause his death because nobody is in your head and nobody will know what you are up to. Nobody may know that you did not intend to kill that person." In SENE & ANOR V. THE REPUBLIC (1977) 1GLR 434 at .436 - 437 C.A., Amissah J.A. held as follows: "The one important factor which distinguishes a case of Murder from manslaughter is the intention. The element of intent in a homicide resulting from a fight was determihed.by considering whether from the circumstances it could be said that the person who killed had the intention to cause death as distinct from a mere intention to fight. Such an intention might be inferred from the instrument or weapon used in the killing or the manner in which the harm which resulted was inflicted .....” Now I find that the learned trial judge, rightly in my view, adverted the minds of the jury to the question of provocation such as the deceased throwing a basket at the appellant and hitting him with a metal frying spoon and tearing his shirt and the trial judge asked the jury to consider whether those acts of the deceased amounted to extreme provocation to the point of the appellant losing his power of self-control. The learned trial judge therefore clearly directed the jury on all the possible defences available to the appellant.While the learned trial judge's direction on the law relating to intent to cause unlawful harm resulting in death was unimpeachable, he left a gaping hole in marrying the intention of the appellant to the facts narrated by the appellant in his cautioned statement and his sworn evidence. While the learned trial judge conceded that the only person to give a firsthand account of what sparked off the fight was the appellant even as the prosecutions own and only eyewitness (PW4) of the fight testified that she did not' see the beginning of it, never at any time did the appellant - not in his cautioned statement or his evidence - state that he hit the head of the deceased on a canoe.And yet the learned trial judge appeared to have largely embraced and accepted the version of the prosecution that the appellant hit the head of the deceased against a canoe and so intended to kill the deceased when he stressed in his summing up: "As I was saying, if you seize the head of your friend and hit it against the floor you ought to appreciate that by so doing, you intend to kill that person." It was indeed a fact for the jury to find as noted by the learned trial judge. But he ought necessarily to have juxtaposed the story of the appellant, who never directly or in any manner acknowledged or admitted that he hit the head of the deceased against a canoe, and the version of the prosecution to enable the jury arrive at a fitting and reasonable finding as to whether the appellant intended to kill the deceased or not. The learned trial judge failed to do that and I think that was unsatisfactory and amounted to a misdirection. The evidence of the appellant that he and the deceased fell into a canoe found corroboration in the testimony of Pw4. The appellant stated that he and the deceased fell head first into the canoe as they were fighting with their legs in the air. It may very well be that the deceased hit his head against the canoe thus injuring himself when he first fell headlong into it and that it was this which contributed to cause his death. The case of the prosecution was further undermined by the fact that it was not the same doctor who conducted a pathological examination of the deceased who tendered the post-mortem report on the deceased. The report was presented by the prosecution through a different doctor who was not a Pathologist and who never examined the body of the deceased. And the report was not subjected to any serious cross-examination or at all.In his inadequate treatment of the element of intent in relation to the testimony of the appellant, the learned trial judge fell short in his otherwise diligent and Admirable direction to the jury and his omission was prejudicial to the appellant It is probable that the jury would have returned a verdict of manslaughter if properly directed on the issue of intent having regard to the indiscernible so- called eye-witness account of PW4 about the fight between the deceased and the appellant.In his written submissions, counsel for the respondent seeks to convince this court that the appellant hit the head of the deceased several times against the canoe into which they fell. But that was not the evidence at the trial. The only eyewitness called by the prosecution, PW4 saw the head of the deceased being hit against the canoe only once and then she left. She was therefore not present to see what happened thereafter. In the circumstances the conviction of the appellant for Murder is quashed and in its stead is substituted a conviction for manslaughter.The appellant has been languishing in prison for the past 20 years on death row and we are of the view that is a sufficient and adequate sentence to have been imposed on a conviction for Manslaughter. Accordingly, we hereby order that having been imprisoned for 20 years on his conviction for Murder the appellant be discharged forthwith from prison. COUNSEL: MR. JOSEPH AMUI FOR APPELLANT MR. GEORGE OFORI (SENIOR STATE ATTORNEY) FOR RESPONDENT
