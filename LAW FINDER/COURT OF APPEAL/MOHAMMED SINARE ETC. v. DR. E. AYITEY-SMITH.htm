JUDGMENT

ANSAH, J.A.

On 23rd March 2001, the Circuit Court, Accra, entered judgment in this case in favor of the defendant and awarded ¢2 million for damages upon his statement of defense and counterclaim. The suit centered on a claim of ownership of a piece of land in Accra.

 

The facts that resulted in the judgment were that at the summons for directions, the court appointed a Government Surveyor to survey the area in dispute and submit a report on it. The parties were to bear the cost involved. Whereas the defendant paid his portion, the plaintiff did not and the court proceeded to strike out the plaintiff's writ on account of this failure to pay. The defendant then went on to prosecute his counterclaim and gave evidence in support of his case.

In the course of the proceedings, the plaintiff died and the defendant applied for an order for his (deceased') wife to be substituted for him in a motion filed on 24/6/99. It is not clear from the record when the application was heard or granted. On 4th august 1999 however, a hearing notice was prepared for service on Salma Sinare, the wife of the deceased plaintiff, informing her that the hearing of the matter had been fixed for 19th August 1999. A certificate of service of this hearing notice stated that it was served on Salma by serving a copy thereof on the co-defendant personally at Nima. Who was that co-defendant, as there was nothing on the record to show any such party in the proceedings? I think Salma ought to be served personally with the hearing notice, especially when she did not apply for the order of substitution. Under the circumstances, the service on a non-existing co-defendant instead of her as certified, was wrong, null and void. No wonder Salma never appeared in court till 18/10/2000 when she did. Nothing shows from the record what happened on that day but there was another notice meant for her that the hearing had been adjourned to 14/11/2000. This time the certificate of service had it that Salma was served by serving a copy of the notice through the sister Jummai personally at Nima, Accra. Why and under what authority Jummai was served was not explained or made clear to the court.

On the 14th November 2000, Salma was absent from court and there was a further adjournment to 11/12/2000 for hearing. On that day when she absented herself from court once again, the matter was adjourned sine die.

Then on 16th February 2001, it appeared the plaintiff moved a motion for substituted service of the hearing notice. Paragraph 3 of an affidavit in support stated that, "Attempts made to effect Hearing Notices on the plaintiff have been futile, and the plaintiff has refused to attend court". Counsel for the defendant applicant prayed the court for an order to serve the plaintiff by 'posting the process on the Notice Board and the last known place of abode of the plaintiff for 14 days'. The court granted the application and ordered the plaintiff to be served by substitution in the manner proposed by the defendant applicant for 14 days. The matter was adjourned for hearing on 23/3/2001. There is no indication on the record of compliance with the court order. I think there should have been proof that they were, so as to satisfy the court that indeed the plaintiff was served. It is only when there was that proof that it could be said the plaintiff had been served albeit by substitution which was as good as personal service.

On that day, i.e 32/3/2001, the plaintiff did not appear in court and the defendant proceeded to give his evidence in chief at the end of which the court entered the judgment aforesaid in his favour on his counterclaim. It is against this judgment that this appeal has been brought.

The original ground was that

"(a) The learned trial judge erred in not ensuring that there is proof of service of the court processes on the plaintiff before proceeding to take defendant's evidence and gave judgment for him." The following additional grounds were subsequently filed, viz,

"(b) The learned trial judge erred when he granted the order for substituted service based on irrelevant consideration.

"(c) The judgment of the trial judge was a nullity because it was given without jurisdiction.

"(d) The learned trial judge erred when he allowed the defendant to lead evidence again in spite of having led evidence previously in proof of his case without stating on the record whether he had adopted the previous proceedings or had ordered the trial of the case de novo."

The ground of appeal touching on service of a hearing notice and service by substitution is crucial and I desire to look at it now.

Order 9 of the High Court Rules, LN140A, governs the service of writ of summons and other documents. Rule 19 (1) thereof provides that:

"Service of a notice, summons, order or other document, shall wherever it is practicable, be effected personally on the person to be served and service thereof shall be completely effected by the delivery of a duplicate or attested copy of any such notice, summons, order or document without the exhibition of any original:

Provided that where service as aforesaid cannot be effected the provisions of rule 2 of this order as to substituted service shall mutates mutandi apply."

Thus under our rules of court, notices and summons must be served personally and the mode is by delivering a duplicate or an attested copy thereof. Order 19 (2) adds that service on the solicitor or recognized agent of the person to be served shall be deemed to be effective service on such person. In law that where a personal service was not requisite under the rules, service of a process on the solicitor on record was service on the party until judgment, and after judgment, until execution. However, when a rule prescribed personal service, it is not permitted to effect it through a solicitor, or for that matter any other person except where the rules of court permits that to be done, see holding (4) of Donkor v Kusi [1977]2 GLR 243.

Another rule on service of documents is that

"where it appears to the court that for any reason personal service cannot be conveniently effected, the court may order that service be effected" by substitution by any of the modes prescribed in Order 10 rule 1 (a) to (e), or Order 67 rule 5, where prompt personal service cannot be effected. So that by these rules service of a document may be served personally or by substitution where to do so proves it cannot be affected practically, conveniently or promptly. Where an order was made for a party to be served by substitution for he did not come to court, the ground for the order was plainly misconceived, for, as seen that is not the basis for the application. Rather the basis is that it was impracticable to serve the party personally.

These rules have been fashioned out so elaborately for a specific purpose, namely:

"......... to give notice to the party on whom it was made, so that he might be aware of, and able to resist, that which was sought against him, and where that had been done, so that the Court might feel perfectly confident that service had reached him, everything had been done that could be required": Hope v Hope (1854) 4 De G.M & G 328 (42-44 ER); 342; Kistler Tettmar [1905] 1 KB 39 at 45, CA.

In Ofori v Lartey [1978] GLR 490, it was said that proceedings can only commence (or continue) against a party when he has been served with a notice. The party must be served the proper way that is, in strict accordance with the rules of court. Thus Benin J (as he then was) state the principle succinctly in Jerigoji v Issah [1989-90] 2 GLR 501 at page 502 that,"    . ......... Hearing of a case on its merits being the cardinal principle underlying our judicial system and the entire concept of justice as known, a party directly affected must be given every opportunity to be heard on notice. Non-service of a hearing notice was not a mere irregularity, which could be waived by the losing party. It rendered the entire proceedings a nullity.

Hence Order 70 rule 1 of LN 140 A could not be applied to cure it."

This point was fortified and made abundantly clear in Karletse Panin v Nuro [1979] GLR 194 at 213, CA, that: "The fact that notice of the adjourned date was not served on the defendants personally in compliance with the statutory directions in our view nullifies completely whatever occurred subsequently." Failure to serve the defendant plaintiff in this case rendered any subsequent proceedings null and void, for that is a step not warranted by the rules of court. It was so void that the party affected thereby is entitled ex debito justitiae to set it aside.

The High Court (Civil Procedure) (Amendment) (No.2) Rules, 1977, LI 1129, provided in rule 7 thereof for change of parties 'by reason of death' and further ‘the substitution of a deceased party'. There are elaborate steps to take consequent upon the substitution once of which is to serve the order on him under rule 7 (6) or noted in the cause book under rule 8 (4). It did not appear from the record that these implementary steps were taken or complied with after the substitution. More importantly, there is no evidence that Salma Sinare was served with the order of substitution either personally or by substitution. She was not made aware of the hearing; neither was she made a proper party in the action. That was fatal to the action that yielded the judgment under appeal.

The main thrust of this appeal was that the appellant was not made aware of the hearing before the court. Specific mention was made of the fact that the hearing notice meant to be served on the appellant was served on her sister. That was against the rules on service of notices, for, it should have been served personally on the plaintiff. I am of the opinion that where, such as in this case, the appellant only came into the case by substitution on account of the death of the original plaintiff and there was a long adjournment in the interval between the substitution and further hearing, it was needful that hearing notice should be effected on the party who absented herself from court, in this case, the appellant. It was needful that she should be served personally.

At the trial, the defendant gave evidence through his lawful attorney, after the plaintiff had given his evidence. By then no order had been made by the court for the parties to pay any sums of money for the survey. Then on 23/3/2001, the defendant gave evidence again on his counterclaim. It was said it meant he did so twice in the same cause. That was an abuse of the processes of court. Where a party has given evidence via his lawful attorney, he does not mount the witness box to give another round of evidence again.  Before that can be done there must be an indication that the court had expunged the earlier evidence for one reason or the other, which must be made apparent on the face of the record. There was nothing like that in this case and the complaint was that the court erred in allowing this to happen. When the plaintiff’s case was thrown out for the non-payment of survey fees, the evidence led by the defendant’s lawful attorney went with it automatically. There was no need for a formal order to that effect by the court.

No hearing notice having been served (personally or otherwise) on Salma, the proceedings were vitiated thereby. We allow the appeal and set aside the judgment. In view of the irregularities in the trial, complained of rightly in our view, we order that the counterclaim be hear de novo by a Circuit Court differently constituted, and with the utmost dispatch.

We allow the appeal.
