JUDGMENT

QUAYE, J.A:- The appellant herein was convicted on his own plea of guilty to three counts of offence that arose under Section 54(1) of the Armed Forces Act 1962 (Act 105) on the 16th March 2009. The charges preferred against him were: (1) Count One: An act to the prejudice of good order and discipline which was based on allegation that he, on 13th July 2008 married a female other Rank, 194440 ABI Dosu Theresa at the Word Miracle Church International, Abelemkpe. (2) In Count Two the charge was "neglect to the prejudice of good order and discipline", and it was founded on the allegation that the appellant neglected to ensure that 194440 AB 1 Dosu Theresa obtained the requisite prior approval for her release from the Ghana Armed Forces as required by CSIP Vo13, Section 1, Article 1, 179 before getting married to him, a commissioned officer. (3) The offence in the third count was similar to that of Count One with particulars that he aided the lady aforesaid to marry him without the said lady obtaining prior approval for her release from the Ghana Armed Forces contrary to CSIP Vol. 3. Section 1 Article 1.179 a(2)(d). The General Court Martial held its first sitting on 2nd March 2009 with Capt (GN) S.A. Aboagye (GH/1876) as president of the panel; His Lordship Mr. Justice Anthony Oppong as Judge Advocate and Mr. Pongo Eric as counsel for the appellant. The court was held at the Naval Trade Training School Cinema Hall Sekondi. The events on said first sitting day are worth noting. They were not very different however from normal procedure in criminal trials. On that day, the charge sheet was amended. The charges as amended, were read. The accused person (now appellant) entered his plea of not guilty to all the three counts; after this the Judge Advocate inquired whether the defence would want an adjournment in order to have sufficient opportunity for preparing the defence. This inquiry attracted a response from Mr. Pongo of counsel for the appellant, that he had no problem taking the evidence of witnesses whose names appeared on the record. With this assurance from the defence, the Prosecutor was called upon and he submitted a written address which he also summarized viva voce. This address was tendered into evidence in whole and without any objection from learned counsel for the appellant. The introductory remarks of the prosecutor wherein he sought to place before the court what the prosecution intended to prove were very instructive. He stated: "...The accused person who is a commissioned officer married a female rating otherwise referred to as other Rank (or a soldier) known as 194440 AB 1 Dosu Theresa of 37 Military Hospital on 13th July 2008. On 1st September 2008, the said AB1 Dosu applied to resign voluntarily from the Armed Forces. Obviously, the marriage between the accused person and the rating was contracted before the application for her release from the service. The application and its approval should have preceded the marriage in accordance with command and staff Instructions Procedures (CSIP) of the Armed Forces. Mere submission of the application to resign is not enough to bestow upon a female other Rank (OR) the capacity to marry an officer. Approval for her release must necessarily be granted before she can be clothed with-the capacity to marry the accused person. The reasons for the provision that a female soldier should resign from the service before marrying an officer cannot be far-fetched especially in an institution whose very existence depends largely on good order and discipline. "...The investigation revealed that the accused person did marry the female other Rank on 13th July 2008 in a blatant violation of military regulations governing marriage in the Armed Forces...". With this introduction over, the prosecutor proceeded to identify and explain the material aspects of what they intended to prove in respect of each count. In count one inter alia, the prosecution intended to prove that there was such marriage; that the marriage was contracted at a time when the parties to the marriage were both in the employment of the Ghana Armed Forces; and that the marriage so contracted was a social misconduct which is prejudicial to good order and discipline in the Armed Forces. In count two, the prosecution was to prove that the appellant negligently failed to ensure that AB1 Dosu Theresa obtained the requisite prior approval of her release from the Armed Forces, and further, that this failure on the part of the appellant was a conduct prejudicial to good order and discipline under military regulations. In count three the prosecution intended to prove that by his conduct, the appellant assisted the lady other Rank to marry him, the said assistance was offered in clear violation of military regulations, and that that Conduct offended the provision in section 54(1) of Act 105. In conclusion the prosecutor said that the Ghana Armed Forces just like any Other security institution thrives on good order and discipline. The appellant's conduct amounts to indiscipline and a disregard for orderliness. Two witnesses were they called to testify for the prosecution. Both Pw1 and Pw2 by their evidence established that the appellant and the lady named were joined in Holy Matrimony at the Word Miracle Church at Dzorwulu, Accra on 13th July 2008, Pw1 however denied that in the course of their training any mention was made of how one could marry in the Armed Forces. Pw2 was the Naval Officer who took the summary of Evidence in this case. The summary was tendered into evidence as Exhibit F. Pw2 confirmed under cross examination that the issue of marriage was a subject matter in the , curriculum of training in the Forces, more especially in the Fundamental . Officer Training, adding further, that the marriage between the appellant and the other Rank female constituted an act of indiscipline. I will now quickly turn to Exhibit F which also formed part of the case for the prosecution. The witnesses from whom evidence was taken confirmed : the marriage as a fact. One of the officers Capt (GH) K.P. Faidoo (GH/1970) .gave a vivid statement; part of which read "since this bordered on discipline, and smacked of the type of officer-rating relationship which the CNS has been ,warning about all the time, and since such matters were in my domain as the D.N.A, I inquired further.... I therefore asked for the number of the commanding officer of the officer and Called him. He informed me that the officer had been given casual leave to come to Accra in connection with some attack on his wife. This immediately struck me as odd since I was unaware that the officer had put in a request to marry.... He could not give any tangible reason why he had gone ahead and married a rating in spite of all the warnings from both the CNS and myself to all officers at various fora..." The last reference I will make from Exhibit F is a short excerpt from the statement of the appellant himself. Hear him "I know I made a regrettable mistake for not waiting for the final approval of the release of my wife". The evidence of Pw1 and Pw2 together with the exhibits tendered on that day, ended the proceedings on the first sitting and also the case for the prosecution. On the second day new developments were announced to the court from the defence which abruptly terminated the trial. The learned counsel for the appellant, Mr. Pongo, informed the court that, "having carefully considered the charge and my instructions, I have advised that the accused must change his plea". In consequence, the necessary formalities, like reading the charges afresh were undertaken, whereupon the appellant changed his earlier pleas of not guilty to those of guilty to all three counts. In accordance with procedural requirements the Judge Advocate sought confirmation from the appellant if he indeed appreciated the situation, and the appellant duly replied in the affirmative leading to his conviction on all the three counts. Before sentence was pronounced, the prosecutor again made a statement to call attention to the seriousness of the offence. He stated that: "This marriage was contracted against the background that command has on several occasions exhorted against marriage between officers and ratings as these kinds of marriage are prejudicial to good order and discipline to the Armed Forces. It is by virtue of this that the CSIP (Command Staff and Instructions and Procedures) was promulgated to impose obligation on ratings or soldiers who intend marrying officers to resign before they do so.The reasons behind the CSIP cannot be overemphasized especially when one considered that the very survival of such an institution depends on discipline and good order. The Ghana Armed Forces deems the marriage between the accused and AB 1 Dosu as such act that is prejudicial to good order and discipline. It is against this background that the accused was charged under section 54(1) of the Armed Forces Act, 1962 (Act 105)..." At the end of the prosecutor's address learned counsel for the appellant, Mr. Pongo said, " My client has been charged under section 54(1) of the Armed Forces Act. The sentence attached to the offence is either dismissal or a lesser punishment. Having pleaded guilty, we plead for mitigation of sentence...". It is needless to say that the court passed a sentence of dismissal. from. the Armed Forces in terms of section 78(i)(e) of Act 105. The above, admittedly long preamble, forms the basis of this appeal on the grounds that: (a) The punishment imposed on the appellant was. too harsh considering the offence committed. (b) The conviction and sentence of the accused was wrong in law since there was no obligation on the accused to perform under any regulation of the Armed Forces. (c) The law under which the appellant was dismissed is discriminatory and therefore Contrary to the fundamental human rights of the couple as enshrined in the 1992 constitution of Ghana. Learned counsel for the appellant argued the 1st and 2nd grounds of appeal together. He contended that the punishment provided under the Armed Forces Regulations include but not limited to reprimand, forfeiture of pay, loss of seniority and reduction in rank. He argued that the punishment imposed by the trial court being dismissal with disgrace for just marrying a female other rank amounts to a travesty of justice since the marriage per se did not occasion any act of indiscipline. He submitted that the appellant was of good record, besides, there is no law in Ghana which prohibits any official of the Armed Forces from marrying a female other Rank. In a supplementary address, learned counsel made reference to the evidence of Pw2 and submitted that the statement that officers were instructed not to marry from other rank was not proved in evidence and therefore cannot be relied upon. In respect of his third ground of appeal, learned counsel expressed the view that the law which bars marriages between commissioned officers and other ranks is a colonial legacy, discriminatory, in conflict with the constitution of Ghana 1992 and should be expunged. On their part the respondents have raised a preliminary legal issue contending that in so far as the appellant set down only one ground of appeal in his application for leave to appeal without indicating that he was likely to file additional grounds later, the appellant was estopped in law from amending his application for leave to appeal and filing new grounds of appeal, albeit with leave of this court. The format that I intend to follow is to address this preliminary. Legal issue first, after which I will, sua sponte, consider the propriety in law of this appeal before I finally tackle the grounds of appeal. I have read both the Armed Forces Act 105 being the substantive legislation as well as the Armed Forces (Court Martial Appeal Court) Regulations 1969 (LI 622) and I have come to the conclusion that neither the parent Act nor the subsidiary Regulations give any inkling as to whether or when a notice or application for leave to appeal, can or could, be amended. In fact there is no mention at all as to whether that right exists and can be exercised. Reading section 90 of Act 105 however satisfies me somewhat, that an appellant can base his appeal on. (a)The legality of any or all findings ; and (b)The legality of the whole or any part of the sentence. The above provision is not, to my mind, exclusive or intended to foreclose the filing of any additional ground once an application had been filed and granted. other rank was not proved in evidence and therefore cannot be relied upon. In fact there is no mention at all as to whether that right exists and can be exercised. Reading section 90 of Act 105 however satisfies me somewhat, that an appellant can base his appeal on. (a) The legality of any or all findings; and (b) The legality of the whole or any part of the sentence. The above provision. is not, to my mind, exclusive or intended to foreclose the filing of any additional ground once an application had been filed and granted. Regulation 5(1) of LI 622 talks about leave to appeal " being an application in the prescribed form and specifying the grounds on which leave to appeal is sought..." If I am not mistaken, this is the only provision in LI 622 where an allusion has been made to "ground of appeal". We may borrow a leaf from the Court of Appeal Rules 1997 (Cl. 19) even though that Instrument is specifically for application in the Court of Appeal. Rule 8(2) thereof states: "8(2)The notice of appeal shall be filed in the Registry of the court below and shall (a) set out the grounds of appeal. "8(7) The appellant shall not, without the leave of the court, urge or be heard in support of any ground of objection not mentioned in the notice of appeal, but the court may allow the appellant to amend the grounds of appeal upon such terms as the Court may think just. "8(8) Notwithstanding subrules (4) to (7) of this rule, the Court in deciding the appeal shall not be confined to the grounds set out by the appellant but the court shall not rest its decision on any ground not set out by the appellant unless the respondent has had sufficient opportunity of contesting the case on that ground". With the greatest respect to learned counsel for the respondent, I would say that even in the Court of Appeal Rules CI. 19. I do not find any reference made to the phrase " additional grounds to be filed later". I believe that phrase appears or occurs more in the form of practice than any specific rule. The important thing is that since the courts exist for administering justice, all parties may, within the rules, be admitted to place before the court all relevant or material issues that would help the court do justice. One essential matter that the law would frown upon is where there is an element of surprise, and this would occur where the respondent was not at all made aware of the matter being urged by the appellant. Where the respondent had sufficient notice or opportunity of contesting the case on that ground therefore; the ground added would not be struck-out. In this case the appellant applied for leave to file additional issues, which application the respondent did not oppose. The preliminary ground is therefore dismissed. In this case, the appellant in the trial court changed his plea from not guilty to guilty after the trial had proceeded to a certain stage. In the ordinary sense, the counts would not entertain an appeal against conviction on a plea of. guilty. Indeed section 34 (2) of The Courts Act 1993 (Act 459) specifically states that (2) Without prejudice to the generality of subSection (1) of this section an appeal against conviction in a criminal case may be dismissed summarily: under that subsection where the appellant has pleaded guilty and has been convicted on his own plea" In the case of ALPHA ZABRAMA VRS THE REPUBLIC (1976) 1GLR 291 at 302, 303, Taylor J (as he then was) found a leeway on technical grounds and after entertaining the appeal, laid down ingredients for the guidance of the courts. The seven situations he enumerated were: 1. If it can be shown that .an appellant did not appreciate or understand the charge or procedure and thus pleaded guilty by mistake. 2. If it can be shown that the appellant has pleaded guilty to a non-existent crime. 3. If the plea, of guilty is so ambiguous that the appellant cannot be said to have unequivocally pleaded guilty. 4. If the appellant pleaded guilty but gave an explanation which practically amounted to a defence or negatived the plea of guilty. 5. If the plea of guilty is such as in fact to be no plea at all. 6. If on the admitted facts upon which the prosecution is founded, no offence is disclosed upon which the appellant could legally be convicted on the charge preferred. 7. if there has been a miscarriage of justice by an apparent wrong acceptance of a plea of guilty. In this case, it is important to note that the appellant did not enter a plea of guilty, at the onset. The trial had actually proceeded to the stage: where the prosecution had closed its case after leading viva voce evidence and also tendering documentary evidence. It was at the stage where: the appellant who had all along appeared by counsel, was called upon to open his defence that he decided to change his plea from that of not guilty to guilty. After that he confirmed to the court that he fully well knew the import of the step he was taking. It is important to draw a distinction between the situation where an accused person, on being called upon to plead to charges against him for the first time, would enter a plea of guilty, and the other scenario, like it happened in this case, where the appellant first pleaded not guilty, and waited until after the prosecution had closed their case, and he the appellant had fully weighed his chances, so to speak, before deciding to change his original plea of not guilty to that of guilty. In electing this second option under the circumstances that prevailed, the appellant who had the full benefit of the experience or legal expertise of his counsel cannot be heard to question the conviction based on his own plea subsequently. This is more so having regard to the prosecutor's statement; exhibit F; and learned counsel's statement in.plea in mitigation of sentence. In any case, learned counsel has alleged that the appellant pleaded guilty to a non-existent crime and that no offence was disclosed. I will now turn attention to the grounds of appeal. In his answer to ground one of the appeal learned counsel for the respondent tried to justify the imposition of the sentence on what he called the prevalent practice of intermarriages between officers and other ranks. This allegation is not in anyway supported by evidence and I will discount it. In similar vein learned counsel for appellant over-exaggerated the sentence by calling it a dismissed with disgrace. The evidence on record however shows a lesser sentence of dismissal simpliciter, and not dismissal with disgrace. I am satisfied that the existing law in the Ghana Armed Forces while not specifically prohibiting marriages between officers and other ranks, succinctly sets out procedure for such marriage and specifically forbids marriages between officers and female other rank unless they both, or at least the female first submitted an application for voluntary retirement and that had been granted. Until such an application for voluntary retirement had been granted, and the female other rank had ceased being a member of the Armed Forces, any marriage contracted between her and any officer would fall foul of the law. Section 54 (3) (6) makes "any regulations, orders or instructions published for the general information and guidance of the Armed Force to which he is attached or seconded;.. an act, conduct disorder or neglect to the prejudice of good order and discipline". In his evidence PW2 said the issue of marriage was a subject matter of their curriculum in training adding that that formed part of the Fundamental Officer Training. Even though Capt (GN) K.P. Faidio (GH/1970) did not testify in the trial, to be cross-examined, his statement in Exhibit F, the summary of evidence, formed part of the case of the prosecution and learned counsel for the appellant could have exercised his right of cross-examination to discredit that as part of the prosecution's case, had he not elected to change the appellant's plea to guilty. The plea of guilty, once recorded, no further proof of the case against the accused was necessary. It is rather surprising that after learned counsel had advised the appellant to change his plea, that counsel would in this appeal submit that the evidence of PW2 that officers were instructed not to marry from other rank was not proved in evidence. On the record, after Pw2 had given his evidence in chief, he was subjected to cross examination by counsel for the appellant. Counsel however, failed rather woefully to discredit Pw2's evidence. Where counsel failed to properly fight his defence through, he cannot be heard on appeal to allege that the issue was not proved. I will uphold the submission by counsel for the respondent that the conviction and sentence were not wrong. There was overwhelming evidence to show that the appellant's conduct was in everyway prejudicial to good order and discipline and therefore compromised the security of the Armed, Force and that section 54 of Act 105 imposes an obligation on the appellant while serving as a naval officer to conduct himself in a manner not prejudicial to good order and discipline and proceed to dismiss this ground of appeal also. Is the law in the true sense discriminatory. Every organization which is interested and concerned in its own welfare, development and progress, would operate on laws to best project its growth and prosperity for the enhancement of its goals. The respondent is not an exception to this, Here I see them saying that they are a security organization: whose success is built on high disciplinary code. One of the best ways to maintain their image is to make laws to the effect that any commissioned: officer who decides to enter into matrimonial relation with a female other rank is not forbidden to do so, provided that the woman first retires or resigns from the service. I do not see the law or regulation as discriminary or anathema to the enjoyment of fundamental rights. One who enters an organization as a member, is presumed to know the rules and regulations existing therein and to have explicitly or implicitly agreed to be bound by them. I do not detect or associate any discriminative aspect of the law, much more to say it frowns on fundamental liberties. COUNSEL: ERIC PONGO FOR THE APPELLANT CAPT. BINDITI CHITO WITH HIM CAPT.
