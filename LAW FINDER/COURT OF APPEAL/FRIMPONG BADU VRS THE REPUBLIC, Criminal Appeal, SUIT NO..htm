JUDGMENT

APALOO JA;

This is an appeal from the High Court, Accra where the appellant was on 25th September 2008 convicted on two counts of offences related to narcotics. The counts were (1) Attempted exportation of Narcotic Drug without Licence issued by the Ministry of Health and (2) Possession of Narcotic Drug without lawful Authority. Both counts were contrary to Sections 1(1) and 2(1) respectively of the Narcotic Drugs (Control, Enforcement and Sanctions) Law 1990 (PNDCL 236). The Court below convicted the appellant and sentenced him to a term of 12 years IHL on each count to run concurrently. this notice of appeal the appellant raised two main grounds of appeal viz that; "1. The Court erred in not considering the defence of the appellant and 2. The Court erred by doing selective consideration of some facts ignoring others." Under sub items 2(a - d) appellant gave five factual situations he claimed the Court failed to consider. These factual situations were matters that emanated from the evidence led in the trial. Briefly, the Appellant arrived at the Kotoka International Airport on 10th July, 2006 at about 7:30 pm to board a KLM flight to Amsterdam. During the pre- departure formalities at the Airport, the appellant was picked at random by an official of the Narcotics Control Board (NACOB) on suspicion of possessing narcotic drug and was taken to 37 Military Hospital for an X-ray examination. The examination however proved negative. Appellant thereafter was taken back to the airport and in the offices of NACOB, a search was conducted on the appellant which included his hand luggage. When a green hand bag which the appellant was holding was searched, four (4) parcels of some whitish substance were found in two false compartments in the bag. There were two parcels of the substance in each compartment. A field test of the substance proved positive for cocaine. For confirmation the suspected cocaine was sent to the Ghana Standards Board for testing. The test proved positive for cocaine with a net weight of 1656.8215 grams. it is important to note that before the green hand bag was eventually sealed the appellant requested that items in the nature of boxer pants, singlets and socks in the bag be released to him for personal use. On these facts the appellant was tried by the High Court which convicted him. Counsel for the appellant argued in his submissions filed in this Court that, the lower Court erred seriously in not giving adequate consideration to the evidence relating to the explanation of the accused. Counsel referred to section 13 of the Evidence Act 1975 which explains the position of the law and the standard of proof required for conviction in criminal trials. According to Counsel the term "reasonable doubt" as explained by Lord Denning in the famous case of Miller vs. Minister of Pensions (1947) 2 All ER 372 was not followed because according to Lord Denning. "Proof beyond reasonable doubt does not mean proof beyond a shadow of doubt. The Law would fail to protect the community if it admitted fanciful positions to deflect the course of justice" Counsel argued further that the trial Judge was not fair to the appellant as it appeared to Counsel that the Judge had already made up his mind against the appellant when the trial judge made a finding in the following words that; In his defence, the accused person had given detailed account about the circumstances leading to the arrival of Kenny at the Airport. There has been afew anxious telephone calls between the accused person and Kenny. The anxiety was indeed overwhelming, not the type that would ordinarily be associated with or expected of a really disinterested person as the picture sought to be painted by the accused person would suggest." Counsel was emphatic that the evidence of the accused never made mention of anxious telephone calls to warrant the conclusion arrived at by the trial Judge. In sum according to Counsel, the conclusion reached by the trial Judge when he assessed the evidence of the appellant was not fair and that was prejudicial to the trial of the appellant. Before we proceed to assess the evidence led at the trial, it will be instructive to comment on the trial judge's summation of what he believed occurred at the airport. A judge is under duty to make comments concerning the conduct of an accused person during a trial. Such comments may directly emanate from the demeanor of a witness when testifying. Indeed the appellant did not directly use the word "anxiety" to describe his mood at the airport when he was expecting Kenny. It is obvious that the trial judge found from the surrounding circumstances that the accused was indeed anxious to meet Kenny at that time. The appellant had given evidence that when he was waiting for Kenny at the airport to bring the bag to him, he and Kenny engaged in telephone conversation when Kenny delayed in coming to the airport with the green hand bag. These are the exact words of the appellant in his evidence in chief: "When I was at the airport, Kenny called me and I told him that I was in the queue checking-in my luggage. He told me to wait because he wanted to be with me a little while. I waited for about30 minutes. He did not turn up and I got back to him on the phone and told him there was no time on my side. He kept on insisting that I should still hold on and he still did not turn up. Within that period, the Airline Counter was about to close for check-in so I had got no other option than to check-in my luggage which I did. About twenty to twenty-five minutes later after I had checked-in my luggage, I had a call from Kenny again. This time around he informed me that he was at the front of the Departure Hall,I told him I had already checked-in my luggage but he told me that it was not something so big, so I went out from the Departure Hall to meet Kenny out there. When I met him I found out that it was indeed a small sports bag. At that point I said that was not going to be a problem because I was only having this small PUMA bag, so I could still carry what Kenny brought as my hand luggage. Marvin being a good friend to me, I took that baggage from Kenny to be delivered to Marvin. We bid good-bye and I went back to the Departure Hall." Having read the evidence of the appellant concerning the events that occurred before he was given the hand bag, we tend to agree with the trial judge that indeed failure on the part of Kenny to appear at the airport created anxiety which was "overwhelming". The statement did not reflect a prejudiced mind on the part of the trial judge but a conclusion lie reached based on the evidence before him as narrated by the appellant himself. We do not find that conclusion reached by the trial judge offensive or made to cause injustice to the appellant. In assessing the whole trial, we are left in no doubt that the trial judge correctly advised himself as to the ingredients related to the two counts before him. In respect of count 1, that is, Attempted Exportation of Narcotic Drug without Licence issued by the Minister of Health, evidence was led in our view to establish that what the appellant had in his possession was a narcotic drug; that the appellant attempted to export the narcotic drug on 10th July 2006, through Kotoka International Airport; and that the appellant had no licence issued by the Minister of Health for that purpose.The evidence of the various witnesses, including that of the Principal Scientific Officer who conducted the test on the substance which proved positive for cocaine was sufficiently overwhelming to establish the facts contained in the charge sheet for count 1. In respect of count 2, there was evidence from the record to establish that, the substance found on the appellant on 10th July, 2006 at the Kotoka International Airport was a narcotic drug. It was indeed established as well that the appellant had possession of the said narcotic drug and that he had no lawful authority to possess the drug. In establishing that the appellant was in possession of the drug, the evidence of the prosecution indicated that ,the narcotic drug was found in a green hand bag the appellant was carrying at the Kotoka International Airport iii the course of his journey to Amsterdam. Indeed, there was this piece of overwhelming evidence that should not be overlooked. The evidence of PW4, the Police Investigator was that, when he was in the process of sealing the green bag for examination by the Ghana Standards Board the appellant pleaded with him to be allowed to take some items contained in the green hand bag for his personal use. There was evidence that the appellant took out some "singlets, boxer shorts and socks". Indeed there was evidence from the appellant himself that before the green hand bag was given to him by Kenny, he had checked-in his luggage. If indeed his luggage had been checked-in, why did his singlets, boxer shorts and socks not go with the luggage but were deposited in a bag belonging to another person? A necessary inference which is not far fetched may lead us to the conclusion that the green bag and its contents belonged to the appellant and not to any other person. Having claimed portions of the contents of the green bag to be his, the appellant cannot deny having knowledge of the presence of cocaine in the hand bag. Indeed the circumstances of this case clearly establish also that the appellant was in constructive and legal possession of the cocaine. The evidence abundantly indicates that appellant was conscious and had knowledge of the existence of the cocaine in the green hand bag which at all material times contained his personal clothing as well. Our law is clear that the gravamen of the offence under section 2(1) of PNDCL 236 was possession and not ownership of the drugs... See the dictum of Acquah JA (as he then was) in Osafo Vs The Republic [1993-94] 2 GLR 236.There is evidence that the appellant was not in lawful possession of the cocaine found on him and that he had no authority to possess the substance. Having established these basic facts and ingredients in the charges laid, the appellant as accused was under duty to offer explanation in answer to the charges.The appellant in his defence testified that the green hand bag and its contents including the cocaine did not belong to him. He said the bag was given to him at the airport after he had already checked in his luggage. He named the person who gave the bag to him as one Kenny for onward transmission to one Marvin Asamoah in Holland. The details of how the hand bag came into his hands have already been well articulated in this judgment. To further prove that the green hand bag and its contents did not belong to him he gave the phone number of Kenny to PW1 and PW3 for tracing in order to arrest Kenny who was then at the Tetteh Quarshie Interchange. Of particular interest in this case is the question of illegal possession of narcotic drug. Counsel for the appellant forcefully played on a number of rhetorical questions the trial judge posed in the judgment to assist him determine what amounted to possession. Some of the questions were; when was the cocaine put in the bag? Did Marvin put it in the bag? Did Kenny put the parcel in the bag? Can it be said that accused did not know that cocaine was contained in the bag? To all these questions, Counsel had a simple answer, that is the accused was incapable of answering the questions because the facts were not within his knowledge.In our view those rhetorical questions may appear irrelevant, but the facts disclose that the appellant had in his possession when he was arrested, cocaine cleverly concealed in false compartments of a bag. The Supreme Court summed up the law concerning illegal possession of a narcotic drug in these words; "To prove the charge of illegal possession of a narcotic drug under section 2 of the Narcotic Drug (Control, Enforcement and Sanctions) Law ..... It must be shown that the appellant not only had physical possession but also knowledge of the nature and quality of what was possessed, namely a narcotic drug since physical possession without that requisite knowledge would amount to no offence" See Bonsu Vs The Republic [1989 - 99] SCGLR 115 The trial judge painstakingly considered this principle of law in his judgment and after referring to various authorities on the point, it is our view that he came to the right decision when he stated that the accused person herein cannot claim total ignorance of the quality and nature of the substance he was carrying. We do not find any reason to fault the trial judge on this finding of fact. From the record we are convinced and are left in no doubt that the trial judge considered the defence raised by the accused in every material detail and came to the irrefutable conclusion that the defence was a sham. The trial judge concluded that he found no merit whatsoever in the defence raised by the accused. We have closely and diligently studied the record and do not see any reasons to fault the assessment of the evidence by the trial judge. Counsel's view that the trial judge failed to consider the defence of the accused cannot be tenable in the face of the record. We are of the further view that in the Court below the prosecution proved its case beyond reasonable doubt and we find accordingly that the conviction and sentencing of the appellant was appropriate. For these reasons we dismiss the appeal and affirm the decision of the lower Court. COUNSEL; • NKRABEA EFFAH DARTEH FOR APPELLANT • MATHEW AMPONSAH CHIEF STATE ATTORNEY FOR RESPONDENT
