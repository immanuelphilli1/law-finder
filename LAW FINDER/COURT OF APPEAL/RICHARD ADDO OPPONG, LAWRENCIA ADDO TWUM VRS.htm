KANYOKE, J.A.

This appeal is from the judgment of the High Court, Sekondi dated the 20th day of February 2009 in which judgment was entered in favour of the plaintiffs/ respondents, now the Respondents against the 1st defendant company and the 2nd defendant church, now the Appellant church in respect of a building at Takoradi known as "SCOA Building" in the Western Region of Ghana. The appellant church feeling aggrieved by the judgment is praying this court to set aside the judgment and in its place enter judgment in its favour on eight grounds of appeal. However in his written submissions filed on 8th June 2009 learned counsel for the appellant argued only four of the grounds of appeal. It is deemed therefore that the other grounds of appeal have been abandoned and consequently nothing will be said about them in this judgment. The grounds of appeal argued are as follows: "(a) The learned trial judge misdirected himself in law by granting to the plaintiffs reliefs which were not endorsed on the plaintiffs writ of summons. (b) The learned trial judge erred in law by holding that the 2nd defendant/ appellant and the lst defendant/ respondent had perpetrated fraud on the plaintiffs/ respondents for lack of evidence to that effect. (c) The holding by the trial court that the late Addo Twum paid half of the purchase price of the SCOA building and thereby made him the owner of the disputed premises is not supported by the evidence on record. (d) The learned trial judge did not give adequate consideration to the case of the 2nd defendant/ appellants." The defendant company neither filed an appeal against the judgment nor did it file any statement of case in response to the appeal herein. It is therefore not a party to this appeal. Briefly, the facts of this case are that there is a building at Takoradi called and popularly known as "SCOA Building". The SCOA Building which consists of an office and business premises (warehouse) originally belonged to a Limited Liability Company called "SCOA Ghana Ltd.". By a Lease dated 20th day of February 1961 between the Government of Ghana and SCOA Ghana Ltd. (herein the Company), Plot No. 50B in the Harbour Business area of Takoradi on which the SCOA Building is situated or erected, was demised or leased to the Company for a term of fifty years (50 years) certain. On the 30th day of December 1994, SCOA Ghana Ltd. by a Deed of Assignment, assigned the unexpired or residue term of its Leasehold interest in the entire SCOA Building to the Resurrection Power & Living Bread Ministries International (the appellant herein) for a consideration of forty million old cedis (¢40,000,000.00). The Deed of Assignment was signed by the SCOA Ghana Ltd.’s attorney, one Mr. Xavier Boucherit on behalf of the company and Rev. Bernard Atta-Boateng, the then Regional Pastor of the appellant church on behalf of the church. One Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum (deceased) also signed the Deed of Assignment as a witness on the side of Rev. Bernard Atta-Boateng. The late Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum was a founding member and one of the elders of the appellant church. This Deed of Assignment was tendered in evidence during the trial as Exhibit E. Exhibit E on its face has been stamped and registered. S.G.S. (Ghana) Ltd. - the defendant company herein was occupying the offices section of the SCOA Building as tenants. It is alleged that the late Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum contributed and paid half (i.e. ¢20,000,000.00) of the purchase price of the SCOA Building and that because of that the appellant gave the offices portion i.e. the warehouse of the building to him. It is also the case of respondents who are the administrators of the estate of Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum that during his lifetime Mr. Halifax Addo Twum was collecting the rents from the defendant company as owner of that section of the SCOA building and continued to collect those rents until his demise and that even after his demise the respondents were collecting those rents until somewhere later when the appellant wrote to them to stop collecting these rents, claiming that the whole of the SCOA Building was the property of the appellant church. The appellant through its solicitor also wrote to the defendant on 31-10-1995 informing it that the whole of the SCOA Building was the property of the church and therefore they (i.e. the defendant) should cease any further payment of rent to the respondents. The evidence suggests the defendant did not comply with the church's request so the appellant church per its Trustees issued a writ of summons in Suit No. E2/59/2006 against the defendant company at the High Court, Sekondi claiming a recovery of possession of the portion or part of the SCOA Building occupied by the defendant and an order of perpetual injunction restraining the defendant, its agents, assigns and workers from having anything to do with the disputed property. The defendant after entering appearance to the writ did not file any statement of defence in the matter. In the course of the proceedings the defendant and the appellant church negotiated a settlement of the matter out of court and subsequently filed terms of settlement. On the 13th day of July 2006, the High Court, Sekondi accepted and adopted the terms of settlement and entered a consent judgment thereon in favour of the appellant church. When the respondents herein got to know of the consent judgment they became aggrieved. Consequently, on the 14th day of November 2006 the respondents as plaintiffs issued a writ of summons in another High Court, Sekondi seeking against the defendant company and the appellant church the following reliefs. "(1) An order to set aside the consent judgment in Suit No.E2/59/2006 dated the 13th day of July 2006 obtained against the 1st defendant and in favour of the 2nd defendant then plaintiffs on ground of fraud. (2) An order to grant the plaintiffs liberty to defend the suit." In their statement of claim the respondents have pleaded the particulars of fraud in paragraph 11 therein as follows. (11) The plaintiffs say that the judgment was fraudulently procured. Particulars of fraud (a) That the 1st defendant failed and or refused to inform the plaintiffs of the action against it by the 2nd defendant. (b) That at the time of the consent judgment the defendants knew or ought to have known that the plaintiffs were and are owners of the property. (c) That the defendants condoned and connived to keep the plaintiffs uninformed of the pending suit. (d) That the 1stdefendant clandestinely refused to file a defence so that the 2nd defendant would have a field day which it indeed had." Each of the defendant and the appellant resisted the action by filing a statement of defence inter alia denying the allegation of fraud levelled against them. The appellant also counterclaimed for the following relief against the respondents: "(1) An amount of eight thousand dollars ($8000.00) being the last rent collected from the 1st defendant without due permission and lawful authority at the current bank rate." l will first of all proceed to consider the complaints of the appellant forming the basis of grounds (a) and (b) of the appeal together. Essentially the complaints and submissions of learned counsel for the appellant on these grounds of appeal are that, (1) the learned trial judge erred in law in granting to the respondents reliefs which were not endorsed on the writ of summons; (2) that the learned trial judge proceeded on a wrong premise when he suo motu amended the endorsement on the writ of summons and the relief in the counterclaim of the appellant at the time he was writing his judgment to include a declaration of title to the disputed property, a relief not asked for particularly by the respondents; (3) that the learned trial judge erred in law when he ruled that the principle enunciated in holding (1) of the case of Lamptey v. Hammond (1986-87) 1 G.L.R. 327 applies to only default judgments, when in fact that principle applies as a general principle of law to all cases, and (4) finally, that the learned trial judge erred in law in finding and holding that the defendant and the appellant perpetrated fraud on the respondents in respect of the consent judgment when there is no evidence to support that finding and conclusion. Learned counsel for the appellant cited cases like Lamptey v. Hammond (supra), Dam v. Addo & Brothers (1992) 2 G.L.R. 200, Okofo Estates Ltd. v. Modern Signs Ltd. (1991-92) S.C.G.L.R. 253 in support of his submissions. The response of learned counsel for the respondents as contained in his written answer filed on 9th February 2009 is that, (1) even in the absence of proof of fraud all that the respondents needed to set aside the consent judgment was to disclose that there is a reasonable defence to Suit No. E2/59/2006 available to the respondents to warrant giving them leave to join and defend that suit and cited the case of Adu v. Ankumah (1972) 1 G.L.R. in support of that submission; (2) that the rule in Dam v. Addo & Brothers (supra) is not inflexible and it does not also represent the present state of the law as indicated by the cases of Butt v. Chapel Hill Properties Ltd. (2003-2004) 1 S.C.G.L.R. 636; J.K. Serbeh & Co. Ltd. v. Mensah (2005-2006) S.C.G.L.R. 341; Delle & Delle v. Owusu Afriyie (2005-2006) S.C.G.L.R. 458 and Order 1 rule 1(2) of the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 2004 (C.1.47) and finally, (3) that in any case the respondents had established fraud against the defendant and the appellant and therefore the learned trial judge was right in so finding and concluding. The law is clear that setting aside a judgment or an order of a court is entirely within the discretion of the trial court or judge and therefore where a judge has exercised his discretion and set aside or has refused to set aside a judgment or order, anybody who seeks to impugn that exercise of discretion, in order to succeed, must be able to show that either the exercise of that discretion was based on wrong principles of law or that it was exercised on wrong or inadequate principles or materials; or that the trial court acted or misapprehended the facts. It is however equally true that an appellate court like this one has the power and the jurisdiction to interfere with the exercise of a discretionary jurisdiction by a trial court if the above factors stated herein above are established. See dictum of Lord Atkin in Evans v. Bartlam (1937) A.C. 473, H.L. at 480, and the local cases of Brempong II v. Amofa (2001-2002) 2 S.C.G.L.R. 177, S.C. and Klimotechnik Engineering Ltd. v. Skanska Jensen International (2005-2006) S.C.G.L.R. 913 at 933, S.C. I will therefore proceed to consider whether the learned trial judge acted properly and procedurally in proprio motu amending the endorsement on the writ of summons and the relief in the counterclaim at the stage he did and whether he rightly granted the consequential reliefs following that amendment. The procedure for amending a writ of summons and pleadings is provided under order 16 of the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 2004 (C.1.47). Under the rules of court (C.1.47) there are two types of amendment of a writ of summons and pleadings, namely, (1) Amendment without the leave of the court and secondly, (2) amendment with the leave of the court. The plaintiff may amend the writ only once without the leave of the court at anytime before the close of pleadings. See order 16 rule 1(1) of C. 1.47. Also, a party may, without the leave of the court, amend his pleadings once at anytime before the pleadings are closed. See order 16 rule 3(1) of C. 1.47. Where the plaintiff or a party has amended his writ of summons or pleadings, before the close of pleadings the amended writ or pleadings must be served on the defendant. The other or second type of amendment is amendment of a writ or pleadings with the leave of the court after the close of pleadings. See Order 16 rule 5(1) and 7(1) of C.1.47. This type of amendment may be applied for or may be ordered by the court or judge suo motu at any stage of the proceedings. Thus order 16 rules 5(1) and 7(1) of C. 1.47 provide as follows: "5(1) Subject to order 4 rules 5 and 6 and to the following provisions of this rule the court may at any stage of the proceedings upon an application by the plaintiff or any other party grant leave to (a) the plaintiff to amend the plaintiff's writ or (b) any party to amend the party's pleadings on such terms as to costs or otherwise as may be just and in such manner as it may direct. 7(1) For the purpose of determining the real question in controversy between the parties or of correcting any defect or error in the proceedings, the court may, at any stage of the proceedings, either of its own motion or on the application of any party order any document in the proceedings to be amended on such terms as to costs or otherwise as may be just and in such manner as it may direct. (2) This rule shall not apply in relation to a judgment or order." It appears to us that the learned trial judge purported to have suo motu amended the writ of summons and the counterclaim under order 16 rule 7(1) of the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 2004 (C.1.47). We think, however that, order 16 rule 7(1) of C.1.47 does not confer power or any jurisdiction on a court or judge to suo motu make the amendment himself. What the rule authorizes is that the court or a judge either on his or its own motion or on the application of any party may order any document such as a writ of summons or pleadings, to be amended on such terms as to costs or otherwise as may be just and in such manner as it may direct. In other words, what order 16 rule 7(1) of 0.1.47 permits is that the court or judge may on his or its own motion or application order such an amendment as it may appear to be just and in such manner as he or it may direct. This purpose of order 16 rule 7(1) of C. 1.47 has been made abundantly clear by rule 8 of C. 1.47 which provides that: "8 Where the court makes an order giving a party leave to amend a writ, pleading or other document, then if that party does not amend the document in accordance with the order before the expiration of the period specied for that purpose in the order, or, if no period Is so specified within fourteen days after the order is made the order shall cease to have effect but without prejudice to the power of the court to extend the period" (my emphasis). Order 16 rules 9(1), (2) and (3) of C.1.47 then provides the method of amendment. Under rule 9(3) of C.1.47: "A writ, pleading or other document which has been amended under this order shall be indorsed with a statement that it has been amended, specifying the date on which it was amended, the name of the judge who made the order authorizing the amendment and the date of the order or, if no such order was made, the number of the rule of this order under which the amendment was made." The evidence on record does not show that the learned trial judge complied with order 16 rule 7(1) of C.1.47. In the instant case, what the learned trial judge should have done if he thought the pleadings raised an issue of title to the disputed property, was to have ordered the parties earlier on in the course of the proceedings or during the trial to amend the writ of summons and or the counterclaim to reflect that in accordance with order 16 rule 7(1) of C.1.47. His power or jurisdiction was to order the amendment and not to make the amendment himself as the trial judge did in the instant case. He had no power and or jurisdiction to do that. The law is that after service, a writ of summons can only be amended either upon application or ordered to be so amended by the court or judge in accordance with the rules, namely order 16 rules 5(1) and 7(1) of C.1.47. However, under order 16 rule 10 of C.1.47 the court has the power or jurisdiction to amend clerical errors in the proceedings or in a judgment or order either on its own motion on notice to the parties or on an application without an appeal. In the instant case, the amendment the learned trial judge proprio motu made at the time he was writing his judgment was not the type of amendment envisaged under either order 16 rule 7(1) or under order 16 rule 10 of C.1.47. We are of the view therefore that, that amendment was made or done without jurisdiction. It was an irregular exercise of jurisdiction. The amendment is therefore null and void and is accordingly set aside. At page 246 of the ROA, the learned trial judge also said this as one of the reasons why he entered judgment in favour of the respondents: "Besides the plaintiff (i.e. in Suit No. E2/59/2006) was not clothed with the capacity to institute that action at the time he did. Reverend Nti admits he is not a trustee of the church. That action was instituted in the name of the Trustees of the church on 27th January 2006. He admits he instituted that action before he obtained the Power of Attorney which was given on 22nd March 2006." The conclusion by the learned trial judge that the plaintiffs in Suit No. E2/59/2006 had no capacity to institute that action is with all due respect to the learned trial judge wrong in law and on the facts. The learned trial judge has himself admitted in his above pronouncement that, that suit was initiated in the name of the Trustees of the appellant church. It was initiated on 27th February 2006 and not on 27th January 2006 as stated by the learned trial judge (see a copy of that writ at page 322 of the ROA). The writ of summons in that suit was filed and signed by the appellants Lawyers "Messrs Tackie Otoo & Co." Reverend Nti's name does not appear anywhere on that writ of summons. The Power of Attorney (Exhibit 3) did not also authorize Reverend Nti to institute that action and he did not institute it. It is clear on the face of Exhibit 3 (see page 299 of the ROA) that the Power of Attorney only authorized Reverend Nti to among other things represent the Trustees of the church in court and prosecute that case for and on behalf of the Trustees and that is exactly what he did in that suit. Exhibit 3 (the Power of Attorney) stated as follows: "Power of Attorney We bishop Akwasi Asare Bediako, Rev. Richard Kwasi Owusu, Rev. Ernest kwadwo Pianin, Mr. Oppong Peprah and Mr. Alex Opoku Fordwour, who are the Trustees of the Resurrection Power and Living Bread Ministries, do hereby donate this Power of Attorney to Rev. Kwame Bugyei Nti of House No. 50, Harbour Area, Takoradi in the Western Region of Ghana to do any or all of the acts and things following: 1 To represent us as the plaintiffs in the case before the High Court Sekondi titled: The Trustees of the Resurrection Power and Living Bread Ministries VS. G. S. Ghana Ltd. 2 To prosecute the case mentioned in paragraph 1 (supra) and also defend any action which might arise in connection thereto. 3 To execute legal documents including affidavits which might be necessary for the exercise of the powers bestowed on the Donee in paragraph 2 supra. 4 And we hereby agree to ratify and confirm anything done by the Donee by virtue of this Deed. Dated at Sekondi this 22nd day of March 2006. Signed" The signatures of the Donors appear below at the right side of Exhibit 3. It is abundantly clear on the face of Exhibit 3 that Reverend Nti was only empowered by the Power of Attorney to represent the Donors in court and prosecute the case for and on behalf of the said donors. There is no evidence on record to show that it was Reverend Nti who instituted the action in that case or that the Donors were not the Trustees of the appellant church at the time that suit was instituted or that the Trustees of the church had no capacity to institute that action. The learned trial judge did not therefore comprehend properly the facts stated in Exhibit 3, also he misdirected himself on the legal effect of the Power of Attorney (Exhibit 3). We will therefore, sitting as appellate judges in this appeal, interfere with that finding and conclusion by rejecting same and accordingly reverse it. We hold that the plaintiffs (now appellants in this case) had locus standi or capacity to institute Suit No. E2/59/2006 and that the trial judge erred in finding and concluding to the contrary. The next issue to consider is whether the respondents being 'strangers' to the consent judgment in Suit No. E2/59/2006 had the locus standi or capacity to seek to have it set aside. The law is that before a person not a party to a judgment or order can apply for an order or seek by a fresh action to set aside a judgment for the reason of fraud or for any other reason must first of all prove or establish his or her capacity or locus standi to do so. See the cases of Asahin Industries Co. Ltd. v. Kofifoh Boat Building Co. Ltd. (1984-86) 1 G.L.R. 453; Gbago v. Owusu (1972) 2 G.L.R. 252, State Insurance Corporation v. Opoku-Darkwa (1974) 1 G.L.R. 439 and Lamptey v. Hammond (supra). In the latter case of Lamptey v. Hammond (supra) this court laid down the procedure a person not a party to a judgment may use to establish his or her locus standi or capacity to have the judgment or order set aside as follows in holding (1) thereof: “….(1) there are two well established modes whereby a person who was a stranger to a judgment or order which adversely or injuriously affected him could have it set aside. He could either obtain the leave of the defendant in the case to use his name and then apply to the court in the defendant's name to have the judgment set aside or if for some reason he could not use the name of the defendant, he could take out a summons in his own name but then the summons should be served on both the plaintiff and the defendant asking for the leave of the court to set aside the judgment and to be allowed to defend the action on such terms of indemnifying the defendant as the judge might consider just." Similar sentiments were expressed in all the above cited judicial decisions. It is also trite law that where a party is seeking to have a judgment or order of a competent court of justice set aside for the reason of or on the ground of fraud the procedure is that he must do so by a writ of summons or by a fresh action. It is equally trite law that a consent judgment may be set aside for reasons of fraud, or mutual mistake or some other ground upon which a court can set aside an agreement. Thus, it was held in holding (1) in the case of In re Arthur (dec'd) Abakah & Another v. Atta-Haggan & Another C.A. (1992) 1 G.L.R. 430 as follows: "(1) a consent order made by a court is binding on a party and it cannot be withdrawn if it is made as in this case as a result of a consent by counsel with authority and full knowledge of the facts. However, it can be set aside if the party discovers that counsel had exceeded his instructions and also on the ground of fraud, mutual mistake or on some other ground upon which a court can set aside an agreement" (my emphasis). In the instant case, the evidence shows that the respondents had adopted the second mode of seeking to set aside the consent judgment in Suit No. E2/59/2006 because they issued the writ of summons in their own names and had same served on both the defendant and the plaintiffs, now appellants in this case and they issued a writ of summons because they are alleging fraud in respect of that consent judgment. We hold therefore that the respondents had adopted the right procedure in their bid to have that consent judgment set aside. We are also satisfied that the respondents had sufficiently established their locus standi or capacity in seeking to have the consent judgment set aside. We do not buy the opinion expressed by the learned trial judge that the principle enunciated in Lamptey v. Hammond (supra) applies to only default judgments. Perhnps the learned trial judge expressed that opinion or was influenccd in making that pronouncement on the basis of the fact that Lamptey v. Hammond (supra) like all those other cases referred to supra were default judgments but in our opinion that is no reason to conclude that the rule applies to only default judgments. Under order 43 rule 11 of the High court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 2004 (C. 1.47) a party to a judgment or order may apply for an order of stay of execution of the judgment or order or "for any other relief" relating to that judgment or order. By the use of the words "other relief" we understand that phrase to include for instance the relief of seeking to set aside that judgment or order. Furthermore, by the use of the words 'judgment or order" without specifying the type of judgment or order, we shall construe those words to include any judgment or order", whether it is judgment after trial or a default judgment. In our view therefore, the principle or rule enunciated by the court of appeal in Lamptey v. Hammond (supra) is applicable as a general principle of law to all judgments or orders whether after trial or in default given or made by a competent court of jurisdiction. Order 43 rule 11 of C.1.47 provides: "Without prejudice to order 45 rule 15, a party against whom a judgment or order has been given or made may apply to the court for a stay of execution of the judgment or order or other relief on the ground of matters which have occurred since the date of the judgment or order, and the court may by order grant the relief, on such terms as it thinks just" (my emphasis). A consent judgment, depending on the facts and circumstances of each case may be interlocutory or in default of a party allowing it to be entered by his default of appearance or a defence or it may be a judgment after trial or by settlement out of court (i.e. by agreement) and conclusive on the matters in controversy between the parties and it is binding on them. Parties or any of them to a consent judgment or order cannot withdraw or resile from such a consent judgment unless they are or he is able to establish that, that consent judgment was arrived at by the lawyer of the party without his (the party's) consent or that the lawyer exceeded his authority and instructions or that the judgment or order was obtained by fraud or mutual mistake or on some other good and sufficient reason or ground. See the case of In re Arthur (dec'd) Abaka & Another v. Atta-Hagan & Another (supra). I will next deal with the issue of fraud. It is not disputed that fraud when proved vitiates every transaction including a judgment of a court of law. See Amuzu v. Oklikah (1998-99) S.C.G.L.R. 141 and Sasu v. Amua Sekyi and Another (2003¬2004) 2 S.C.G.L.R. 742, S.C. An allegation of fraud for all intents and purposes connotes a criminal conduct for which the sanction may include incarceration in prison. Therefore it is not without doubt that it is for this reason that the law requires that in civil cases fraud must be specifically pleaded and the particulars given so that the person or persons against whom it is alleged is given the opportunity to prepare adequately to meet his accuser in court and fight that allegation boot for boot. The law also provides that the person who alleges fraud bears the burden of strictly proving it to the extent of requiring proof of it beyond all reasonable doubt because of its quasi criminal nature and implications. An assertion of fraud must not therefore be based on mere flimsy or baseless accusations. See Okofo Estates Ltd. v. Modern Signs Ltd. (supra) and Zotope v. Hahormemme III (1987-88) 2 G.L.R. 681. Now, a careful examination and evaluation of the particulars of fraud pleaded in paragraph II of the respondents statement of claim reveals, in our opinion that even if those particulars are true they certainly fall short of establishing fraud. The defendant had no legal obligation to have informed the respondents of Suit No. E2/59/2006 against it. The defendant was a tenant in the disputed premises and the evidence suggests that it was prepared to pay the rent to whoever was proved to be the owner of the disputed building. The evidence shows that before that suit was commenced, the defendant was paying the rent to the respondents (see Exhibit C series at page 174-182 of the ROA) until it received the letter (Exhibit 6) from the appellants Lawyer warning and instructing them to cease paying the rents to the respondents. In that letter, the appellants lawyer had made it clear to the defendant that the appellant church was the owner of the whole of the SCOA building. In the teeth of these facts the respondents cannot be right in alleging that the defendant knew at the time of the consent judgment that they (respondents) were the owners of the disputed property. Besides, there is even evidence on record showing that the respondents were in fact aware of the action in Suit No. E2/59/2006. Thus in his own evidence in chief at page 95 lines 6-7 of the ROA the 1st respondent (plaintiff) made this admission and revelation: "In 2006 I went to collect rents and I was told second defendant had taken them (i.e. defendant) to court claiming that the house they (i.e. defendant) occupied belonged to second defendant and not me..." The question is this: When 1st respondent was told this what did he do? Was this not sufficient information to have put the respondents upon further inquiry from the defendant or from the appellant about that action? We find that the respondents were aware of Suit No. E2/59/2006. We are also satisfied that the respondent failed to strictly prove fraud against the defendant and the appellant. They also failed to prove collusion between the defendant and the appellant. According to counsel for the appellant in granting those reliefs to the respondents the learned trial judge acted without jurisdiction. Learned counsel for the appellant cited the Supreme Court case of Dam v. Addo and Brothers (1962) 2 G.L.R. 200 in support of his submission. On the contrary learned counsel for the respondents sought refuge under order 1 rule 1(2) of the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules, 2004 (C.1.47) to justify the learned trial judge's action in granting those reliefs in favour of the respondents, contending that the learned trial judge out of abundance of caution amended the statement of claim and the appellant's counterclaim to encapsulate the consequential orders he subsequently made giving due regard to the pleadings and the issues filed for determination by the appellant. In the view of learned counsel for the respondents the case of Dam v. Addo and Brothers (supra) only prescribed the substitution of a new case by the court so that case is distinguishable from the facts of the instant case and that in the instant case the respondents had established that even without proof of fraud they had a reasonable defence to the action in Suit No. E2/59/2006 and cited the case of Adu v. Ankomah and others already referred to above in this judgment, in support of his submission. As a point of correction the learned trial judge did not amend the pleadings i.e. statement of claim and Reply of the respondent and the statement of defence and counterclaim. What he proprio motu amended is the endorsement on the writ of summons and the relief in the counterclaim. Secondly, the issue of the respondents' or Mr. Halifax Addo-Twurn's ownership of the disputed property was not made an issue for trial in the summons for directions. It is only the appellant's ownership of the disputed property that was made an issue for the trial as issue number VII in the summons for directions. In effect the issue of Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum's or the respondents' ownership of the disputed propriety or any portion of it was not joined between the respondents and the appellants. Thirdly, the usual phrase of "Any other issue(s) raised by the pleadings" is completely absent from the summons for Directions (see same at page 39-40 of the ROA). It is therefore incorrect as contended by learned counsel for the respondents that the learned trial judge amended the statement of claim and the appellant's counterclaim out of abundance of caution in order to encapsulate the consequential orders he subsequently made giving due regard to the pleadings and the issues filed for determination. We have already held that the learned trial judge committed a jurisdictional error in suo motu amending the writ of summons to include a declaration of title to the disputed property and subsequently giving judgment on that relief and also proceeding to make or grant the so called consequential orders all in favour of the respondents. His act amounted to substituting a new case for the respondents. That was a jurisdictional irregularity which renders that declaration of title and those consequential orders null and void. See Dam v. Addo and Brothers (supra). In the recent case of Nana Ama Amponsah v. Franklyn Amoah Nyamah (2009) 6 G.M.L.R. 1 where the High Court granted title to a house to the respondent but which house was not one of the reliefs asked for by the respondent in a petition and cross- petition for divorce and other reliefs and that was upheld by the Court of Appeal, the Supreme Court held allowing the appeal inter alia that: "In as much as the Atwima Afiashebon house was not a relief that any party made in the petition and cross-petition of the respondent and the appellant respectively a ruling on it was irrelevant for the reliefs sought. This is because it is the duty of the court to make pronouncements on the reliefs that a party seeks. Since a judge who makes an order for a relief not sought can be held to have exercised a jurisdictional irregularity the decisions of both courts below in the circumstances as to the ownership of the Atwima Afiashebon house were irregular exercise of jurisdiction... Thus as the Atwima Afiashebon house was never an issue it was not within the courts mandate to make pronouncement as to the ownership of it. Therefore the Court of Appeal's decision declaring Ama Amporisah as the owner of H/No. Plot 38 Block A was done without jurisdiction and same is accordingly set aside..." The case of Dam v. Addo and Brothers (supra) was cited with approval. See also Amoyaw v. Amoyaw (1999-2000) 2 G.L.R. 124, C.A. and Allotey v. Quacoe (1981) 1 G.L.R. 420, S.C. In the instant case, no issue was joined between the respondents and the appellant on the ownership of the disputed house or any portion of it or a declaration of title of same by the respondents or Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum. In other words, the respondents never asked for the relief of a declaration of title to the disputed house or any part of it in their writ or pleadings so the trial judge had no business amending the endorsement of the writ to include that relief. It was not therefore the duty of the learned trial court to make any pronouncement on that issue. The respondents did not also ask for a relief that the defendant should be ordered to attorn tenant to them or that the court should order the appellant to convey the disputed property to them or that the court should order that any rents paid into court should be paid to them. The respondents did not even plead ownership of the whole or the entire SCOA building. It is clear from the statement of claim and their Reply that the respondents were asserting or claiming that Mr. Halifax Addo¬Twum having paid half of the purchase price of the disputed house became owner of the offices section of the disputed property and that after his death, they were entitled to the portion of the building allegedly transferred to the deceased by Exhibit A. But the learned trial judge decreed ownership of the entire SCOA building in favour of the respondents. It is our view that, that declaration as well as the consequential orders made therein tantamounted to the substitution of a new case for the respondents and were made without jurisdiction and are therefore null and void and are accordingly set aside. Grounds (a) and (b) of the appeal accordingly succeed. This brings me finally to a consideration of grounds (c) and (d) of the appeal. Ground (c) is complaining that the learned trial judge's holding that the late Mr. Addo-Twum paid half of the purchase price of the SCOA building and thereby made him the owner of the disputed building is not supported by the evidence on the record whilst ground (d) complains that the learned trial Judge did not consider the appellant's case adequately. Since ground (c) is complaining that that finding and conclusion is against the weight of the evidence on the road, this court is entitled to peruse and examine the totality of the evidence on the record taking into consideration the undisputed or admitted facts in order to arrive at our own decision on the matter. See Akuffo-Addo v. Catheline (supra). It is also settled law that it is the appellant who bears the burden to prove that the judgment is not supported by the evidence on record. However, since it is the respondents who had asserted that late Halifax Addo-Twum paid half (i.e. ¢20,000,000.00) of the total purchase price of forty million old cedis (¢40,000,000.00) of the SCOA building and therefore became owner of part of that building the burden of proof and persuasion rested on them to prove that issue. This burden never shifted until and unless the respondents had adduced sufficient cogent and satisfactory prima facie evidence of that issue. In other words, the respondents had the duty to establish a prima facie case of Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum's said half contribution of the purchase price. See sections 11(1) and (2) and (4) of the Evidence Act, 1975 (N.R.C.D. 323). On the evidence on the record, the respondents based that claim on the following; (1) that Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum paid half of the purchase price of the SCOA building; (2) that by virtue of (1) and Exhibit A the offices and warehouse portions of the SCOA building became the property of late Halifax Addo-Twum and after his death it became the property of the respondents, and (3) that during his lifetime late Halifax Addo-Twum collected rents from the defendant in respect of that portion of the disputed property. I must concede that the respondents evidence on the above facts has been corroborated by Rev. Bernard Atta-Boateng (Pwl). However, in our opinion, a careful evaluation of the totality of the evidence on the record reveals that the respondents did not at the end of the trial establish on a preponderance of the probabilities that late Halifax Addo-Twum contributed or paid half of the purchase price of the SCOA building and became the legal owner of any portion of the SCOA building. We are of the view that Pwl is a fraudulent character who came to court to give evidence in support of the respondents in an attempt to absolve himself from his fraudulent misdeeds in the whole affair in relation to the purchase of the SCOA building. In the first place, apart from the oral assertions or testimonies of the respondents and Pwl that late Halifax Addo-Twum paid half (i.e. ¢20,OOO,OOO.00) of the purchase price of the disputed property, there is no documentary evidence to support or corroborate that claim or those assertions which have been denied by the appellant. On the contrary, there is documentary evidence on record which establishes that it was the appellant church which alone bought the disputed property. That documentary evidence is the Deed of Assignment (Exhibit E). Quite interestingly, even though Pwl in his evidence in court insisted that Halifax Addo-Twum paid half of the forty million old cedis (¢40,000,000.00) being the purchase price of the disputed property he tendered Exhibit E which seriously and fatally contradicts his testimony. Exhibit E completely negatives the respondents and Pwl's testimonies that Halifax Addo-Twum paid half of the purchase price of the SCOA building and because of that he became the owner of the offices and warehouse portion of the SCOA building. No receipt whatsoever was produced and tendered in evidence to support or corroborate that claim of the respondents. Again, the receipts for payment of the purchase price of the disputed property tendered during the trial as Exhibits1A -1E (found at pages 295--296 of the ROA), which are all in the name of the appellant church seriously and fatally contradicts and dents the claims of the respondents and that of Pwl that Halifax Addo-Twum paid half of the purchase price of the disputed property. In fact in his judgment, the learned trial judge conceded that there is no documentary evidence to support or corroborate the respondents and Pw 1 's evidence that Addo-Twum paid half of the purchase price of the disputed property (see page 260 lines 17-19 of the ROA). Therefore, in our view in the light of Exhibits E and1A-1E, the assertions of the respondents and Pwl that Halifax Addo¬Twum paid ¢20,000,000.00 being half of the purchase price of the disputed property should have been rejected by the learned trial judge and accordingly we hereby totally reject these assertions. Our finding and conclusion on a proper examination and evaluation of the totality of the evidence on the record especially having regard to the undisputed facts on the face of Exhibits E and1A-1E is that late Halifax Addo-Twum did not contribute half (i.e. ¢20,000,000.00) of the purchase price of the disputed building or property. We find that it was the appellant church which alone bought and paid for the entire or the whole SCOA building at Takoradi. Exhibit A on which the respondents also relied on to establish their claim is a worthless piece of information and it is not worth the paper on which it is written. Exhibit A which was addressed to the Manger of S.G.S. (Ghana) Ltd. and copied to late Halifax Addo-Twum and was written by Pwl reads in part: "TRANSFER OF S. G. S. offices And Warehouse in Takoradi On behalf of the Board of the above church I will like to inform you that the building of the S.G.S. office and warehouse, Takoradi has been transferred to Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum of Halifax Construction Ltd., P. 0. Box 172, Takoradi. Mr. Halifax is now the owner of the said office and warehouse. Contact him for any information arrangements or you would like to make. Thanks. Yours faithfully, (Signed) Re: Bernard Atta-Boateng Head pastor CC: Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum CC:The Manager Halifax Construction Ltd. S.G.S.(GH) Ltd. P .O. BOX 172 Takoradi.” Takoradi Exhibit A is dated 6th February 1997 whilst the Deed of Assignment - Exhibit E is dated 30th December 1994. By virtue of Exhibit E, the whole of the disputed property became the property of the Resurrection Power and Living Bread Ministries International (the appellant herein). Therefore, it was only the Trustees of the appellant church that is clothed with the legal power and authority to deal with or transfer the disputed property or any part of it to any person or corporate body. The Western Region Board of the appellant church or Pwl therefore had no legal power capacity and authority or right to transfer any portion of the disputed property to late Halifax Addo-Twum. There is completely no documentary evidence to support or confirm the information carried by Exhibit A that the offices and warehouse of the SCOA building had been transferred to Halifax Addo-Twum by the Trustees of the appellant church. But if the point is made that Exhibit A is the document or Deed of Transfer itself, then the respondents cannot rely on it to establish Mr. Addo-Twum's claim of ownership to that section of the disputed property. This is because Exhibit A which touches and concerns land has not been stamped in accordance with the Stamp Act nor has it been registered in accordance with section 24(1) of the Land Title Registry Act, 1962 (Act 122). Exhibit A cannot therefore form the basis of a judicial decision. The learned trial judge therefore erred in law in relying on Exhibit A to declare title of the disputed property or any part of it in favour of the respondents. See Thompson v. Mensah (1957) 3 W.L.R. 240, A.C., Asare v. Brobbey (1971) 2 G.L.R. 331, Hammond v. Odoi (1982-83) 2 G.L.R. 1215 and Nti v. Mantse (2008) 15 (G.M.L.R. 185. There is even no evidence including the facts recited in Exhibit A that the said Exhibit A was executed by Rev. Atta¬Boatend (Pwl) and late Halifax Addo-Twum as a deed of conveyance of the portion of the SCOA building in favour of the deceased. Thus, if Exhibit A has any value at all at best it is a mere letter conveying a piece of information but an information can either be true or false depending on the facts of each case. The facts in the instant case establish that the information conveyed by Exhibit A is false to the extent that there is completely no evidence that the offices and warehouse section of the SCOA building have been transferred to the late Halifax Addo-Twum by the Trustees of the appellant church. The respondents also assert that late Addo-Twum collected rents from the defendant during his lifetime and that the respondents stepped in after Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum's death to collect these rents and therefore that this further shows that late Halifax Addo-Twum became part owner of the SCOA building. The evidence on record establishes that that assertion is partly correct and partly false. For example in Exhibit 6 (page 310 of the ROA), the appellant's solicitors (Messrs Tackie Otoo & Co.) made this admission: "WesternLaw Chambers 3B/20 Maxwell Rd Valley P.O. Box 657 Sekondi, Ghana, W/A 31-10-05 The Managing Director S.G.S. (Ghana) Ltd. Accra Dear Sir, Re: The Takoradi Office of S.G.S. (Ghana) Ltd. Payment of Rent. We write for and on behalf of the Resurrection Power and Living Bread Ministries: the Bona Fide owners of your Takoradi office. As you are aware in the past all rents accruing from your Takoradi office was collected by Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum and after his death by his widow. The couple were collecting the rent in respect of the demised premises by virtue of an arrangement between our clients and the said couple. We are instructed to inform you that the authorization given to the couple to be collecting the rent has been withdrawn and therefore henceforth all rents should be paid to our clients at its Takoradi office... Yours faithfully, (Signed) Solicitor for Resurrection Power And Living Bread Ministries." Consequently, on the basis of the admission by the appellant church contained in Exhibit 6 that in the past all rents in respect of the offices of the S.G.S. (Ghana) Ltd. in Takoradi were collected by Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum, We find that between 1994-1997 i.e. from 1994 until the death of Addo-Twum in 1997, the deceased was collecting the rents in respect of the portion of the SCOA building occupied by the defendant herein. We find however that there is no conclusive and proved evidence on record on how much rent Mr. Addo-Twum collected from 1994 until his death in 1997. There is also no satisfactory evidence to support the respondents' claim that late Halifax Addo-Twum collected those rents because he was the owner of the portion of the premises occupied by the defendant or that it was because he paid half of the purchase price of the entire SCOA building. We therefore reject those claims and or assertions as not supported by the evidence on the record. On the contrary, there is evidence which we accept that late Halifax Addo-Twum collected those rents with the authorization and approval of the appellant church and that he collected those rents as a set-off of an amount of eight million old cedis (¢8,000,000.00) which he gave to the appellant church as financial assistance through Pwl towards the payment of the purchase price of the SCOA building. See Exhibit 6 (supra) and Exhibit B. Exhibit B shows clearly that because Halifax Addo-Twum was receiving rents until his death in 1997 that prompted the respondents to continue to collect those rents and to enter into a new tenancy agreement in respect of the rent with the defendant. It is also clear that it was on the basis of Exhibits A and B that the respondents continued collecting the rents from the defendant after the death of Addo-Twum in 1997. Thus, paragraphs 2 & 3 of Exhibit B state as follows: "We refer to a letter dated 6th February 1997 addressed to you by the Resurrection Power and Living Bread Church informing you that our client had purchased the above property. Our client is prepared to enter into afresh tenancy with you in respect of the above property with effect from 1st March 1997." The letter "dated Oh February 1997" referred to in Exhibit B is Exhibit A. The Exhibit C series i.e. Exhibits C1 - C9 (pages 274¬283 of ROA) further show that between 1997-2004 it was the respondent who collected the rents from the defendant. But it is clear on the evidence on the record that the appellant church never accepted the late Halifax Addo-Twum or the administrators of his estate (respondents) as the owners of the portion of the SCOA building the defendant occupied. This is so because there is documentary evidence from Exhibit 2 (page 298 of ROA) that on 8th February 1995 i.e. even during the lifetime of Halifax Addo-Twum, the appellant church wrote to the defendant informing the defendant they were to pay a monthly rent of ¢350,000.00 and that the defendant should pay two years advance rent of eight million, four hundred thousand old cedis (¢8,400,000.00). There is also further evidence in Exhibit 2A (page 277 of ROA) that in July 1995 the appellant church had or was negotiating with a company called Supermaritime (Ghana) Ltd. to rent the offices and warehouse portion of the SCOA building i.e. the portion occupied by the defendant company to it. The facts in Exhibits 2, 2A and 6 therefore show or establish a reasonable inference that between 1994-1997 it was the appellant church which had effective and actual control and possession of the entire SCOA building or the disputed property and that it was the appellant church which authorized late Halifax Addo-Twum and after his death the respondents herein to collect the rents from the defendant and not that Addo-Twum or the respondents collected those rents as owners of the offices and warehouse section of the disputed property, and we so find and accordingly conclude. Further in a letter dated 11th November, 2006 from the appellants' solicitors to the respondents solicitors - Exhibit F (page 294 of the ROA) the appellant gave reasons why it permitted the late Addo-Twum to collect the rents from the defendant. Paragraphs 3 and 4 of Exhibit F read as follows: "Dear Sir, Re: S. G. S. Premises in SCOA Building Our instructions are that our client is the sole assignee in respect of the whole of the said premises (please see attachment). We are further instructed that when the premises was put out for sale, the said Halifax Addo-Twum gave our client 08 million as financial assistance to make up the purchase price and that to enable Halifax Addo-Twum recoup his 08 million and all accruals thereon our client permitted him in his lifetime and his wife for sometime after the demise to collect rent from the tenants of our client who were occupying parts of the premises." Paragraph 3 of Exhibit F is true because it is supported by Exhibit E - the Deed of Assignment. Paragraph 4 of Exhibit F is also supported by the facts established by Exhibits1A-1E,2& 2A, 6 and Exhibit B. Again, that is also supported by the fact that there is completely no evidence to support the claim that Addo-Twum paid half of the purchase price of the SCOA building. It is further supported by the absence of evidence documentary or oral about how much rent Addo-Twum collected during his lifetime. In our view in the absence of evidence to the contrary, we can safely find and conclude that whatever rent late Addo-Twum collected was meant to set off the eight million cedis (¢8,000.00) Mr. Addo-Twum had assisted the appellant church financially to purchase the SCOA building. Thus, it is not surprising that late Halifax Addo-Twum was putting pressure on Pwl, according to Pwl himself to give him a receipt, obviously in respect of the amount he advanced to Pwl to enable Pwl buy the disputed property when he got to know that Pwl was going on transfer from Takoradi. We think it is reasonable to infer and we do infer that it was under that intense and persistent pressure for a receipt that Pwl wrote Exhibit A. This finding and conclusion finds support in Pwl 's own evidence in which he tried to explain the circumstances leading to his giving Exhibit A to Halifax Addo-Twum. At page 116 of ROA Pwl testified inter alia, as follows: "... When I came back (from general meeting of Senior Pastors at headquarters in Kumasi), I had a meeting with the Takoradi branch and I listed and gave to them the things I did and bought in the name of the church. This Included the SCOA building. It was because of that I wrote Exhibit A to show the portion that belonged to Halifax Addo-TwuTn so that whether I was there or not it would be known that it is his. My last meeting with the elders was in March. At that meeting Halifax Addo-Twum demands I get him a receipt. I told him I could not give him a receipt until a demarcation was done. I gave him Exhibit A to enable him deal with S.G.S." (my emphasis). It is not clear what Pwl wanted to demarcate when Exhibit A stated clearly that it was the offices and the warehouse portion of the SCOA building that belonged to Halifax Addo-Twum. The most reasonable inference we draw from this evidence is that it was because at that time Halifax Addo-Twum had not completely set off or exhausted the eight million cedis (¢8,000,000.00) and because Pwl was due for transfer that he (Mr. Addo-Twum) wanted a receipt for his ¢8,000,000.00 so that he would not have any problem with Pwl's successor as to the collection of the rents from the defendant towards the full liquidation of his ¢8,000,000.00. Besides, Exhibit E (Deed of Assignment) overwhelmingly serves as an insurmountable block to whatever claim of ownership late Halifax Addo-Twum or the respondents herein to have in respect of the disputed building or any portion of it. The law is that facts recited in a written document are conclusively presumed to be true as between parties to it and all persons claiming through them. It is also trite law that where a party, by his own statement, act or omission intentionally and deliberately caused or permitted another person to believe a thing to be true and to act upon such belief, the truth of that thing shall be conclusively presumed against that party or his successors in interest in any proceedings between that party or his successors in interest and such relying person or his successors in interest. This legal position concerning written documents is provided statutorily in sections 25(1) and 26 of the Evidence Act, 1975 (N.R.C.D. 323). Sections 25(1) and (2) and 26 of N.R.C.D. 323 provide. "25(1)Except as otherwise provided by law, including a rule of equity, the facts recited in a written document are conclusively presumed to be true as between the parties to the instrument or their successors in interest. (2) This section does not apply to the recital of consideration. 26 Except as otherwise provided by law including a rule of equity, when a party has by his own statement, act or omission intentionally and deliberately caused or permitted another person to believe a thing to be true and to act upon such belief, the truth of that hung shall be conclusively presumed against that party or his successors in interest in any proceedings between that party or his successors in interest and such relying person or his successors in interest." Late Halifax Addo-Twum supported Rev. Bernard Atta-Boateng (Pwl) to sign and execute the Deed of Assignment (Exhibit E) by signing Exhibit E as a witness. There is evidence on record that Mr. Addo-Twum was literate i.e. he could read and write. The recitals in Exhibit E are that the original Lessees of the SCOA building, SCOA Ghana Ltd. had assigned its unexpired or residue interest in the SCOA building to the Resurrection Power and Living Bread Ministries International for a consideration (or purchase price) of forty million old cedis (¢40,000,000.00). Exhibit E has described SCOA Ghana Ltd. as the vendor and the Resurrection Power and Living Bread Ministries International as the purchaser. No mention whatsoever is made in Exhibit E of Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum as either a co-purchaser or co-assignee of the disputed property or any part of it or that he has an interest of any kind in the SCOA building. Mr. Halifax Addo¬Twum must have read and appreciated the contents of Exhibit E before he signed it as a witness. The law is that a person of sound mind and full age who signs a written document is bound by it whether he read it or understood it or not. There is no evidence that Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum (deceased) was either a minor or of unsound mind at the time he signed Exhibit E as a witness. There is no evidence whatsoever that late Addo-Twum raised any objection to the recitals of Exhibit E at anytime during his lifetime. The respondents did not plead and prove that Exhibit E is tainted with fraud or that for any sufficient reason Exhibit E is not valid. On the contrary, Exhibit E on its face has been stamped and registered. In consequence in the absence of evidence invalidating Exhibit E the late Halifax Addo¬Twum and his successors in interest are bound by Exhibit E. By intentionally and deliberately signing Exhibit E and causing the appellant church to believe that it is the sole purchaser or assignee of the entire SCOA building and acting on that belief by writing Exhibits 6, and F. Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum and his successors in interest to wit the respondents herein are estopped by conduct from challenging the appellant church's title or ownership of the entire SCOA building. The facts recited in Exhibit E are conclusively presumed to be true as between the appellant church and late Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum and his successors in interest the respondents herein. Exhibit E created an estoppel by a written document (statutory estoppel) and binds Mr. Halifax Addo-Twum (deceased) and his successors in interest and the respondents herein and secondly, the legal effect of late Halifax Addo-Twum's act in signing Exhibit E and failing to raise any objection to the recitals therein, is that Halifax Addo-Twum and all persons claiming through him are statutorily estopped from asserting ownership of the SCOA building or any part of it. The respondents herein have failed to establish that this case falls under the exception created under section 25(2) of N.R.C.D. 323. See the recent case of Kusi & Kusi v. Bonsu Civil Appeal No. JA/14/2009 dated 29th April, 2010, S.C. In that case the defendant was a co-signatory to a petition to the Asantehene in respect of a portion of land at Adum, as family property that was originally compulsorily acquired by the Government of Ghana but was subsequently released to the family. The reconveyance Leasehold was executed in the name of the defendant with the knowledge of the family. The defendant took advantage of that and started dealing with the land as if it were his self-acquired property including using it to obtain a loan from one Kofi Aboagye. When the family got wind of this they redeemed the land from Mr. Aboagye but the reconveyance Lease of the property still remained in the name of the defendant. Unperturbed the defendant again took advantage of that and pledged the land to one Grace Nsiah. When Grace Nsiah died and her customary successor applied to the High Court for Letters of Administration in respect of the estate of Grace Nsiah and listed the disputed property in the inventory, the plaintiff, now head of the defendant's family caveated the application and then took out a writ of summons claiming title to the land as family property. The High Court dismissed the plaintiff's action. On appeal to the Court of Appeal the Court of Appeal reversed the High Court's decision for the reason that the reconveyancc Lease was not registered in accordance with section 24(1) of Act 122. On further appeal to the Supreme Court, the Supreme Court held, inter alia, that: (1) Exhibit B (the petition to Asantehene that was co-signed by the defendant created an estoppel by written document and was also binding on the defendant, (2) that the legal effect of that declaration against his interest was that the defendant and all persons claiming through him were estopped from asserting otherwise and, (3) that the defendant failed to establish that his case fell under the exceptions created under section 25(2) of N.R.C.D. 323. In the instant case, late Halifax Addo-Twum signed Exhibit E against his interest. He and all persons claiming through him are all estopped from asserting ownership of any portion of the SCOA building. In effect at the close of the trial aside other pieces of evidence on the record entitling the appellant church for judgment in its favour, Exhibit E (the Deed of Assignment) stood out as a solid and impenetrable rock against the claims of late Halifax Addo-Twum and all persons including the respondents herein claiming through him. We therefore agree with learned counsel for the appellant that the learned trial judge did not give adequate consideration to the case of the appellant. On the facts and evidence we conclude that the respondents had no good or reasonable defence to the action in Suit No. E2/59/2006 to warrant giving them leave to join that suit. The appeal on grounds (c) and (d) also succeed. In conclusion the appeal succeeds and is allowed on grounds (a), (b), (c) and (d). The judgment of the learned trial judge together with all orders made therein is hereby set aside. In its place judgment is entered in favour of the appellant on its counterclaim. Interest on the $8,000.00 rent collected by the respondents after they had been warned from receiving same by the appellant is granted to take effect from the date of the trial court's judgment i.e. 20th July 2009 at the prevailing bank rate. • MR.
