JUDGMENT

WOOD, J.A.:

On the 14th of March, 1997, the Plaintiff/Appellant sued the defendant/respondent for the sum of £8995 said to represent the balance outstanding on money lent to her and interest thereon.

An application for Summary judgment submitted after the respondent had duly filed a defence and counter-claim was dismissed by the learned trial judge on the grounds that the respondents defence raises triable issues. Not being satisfied with this decision, the appellant appealed to us in this court on two grounds, the first being the original grounds viz:

“The trial judge erred in law in dismissing the plaintiff’s application for summary judgment on the grounds that the defendant/respondent’s response raises triable issues when there was before the court, the defendant/respondent’s uncontroverted undertaking admitting the sum owed to the plaintiff appellant, which error of law has occasioned the plaintiff/appellant a substantial miscarriage of justice”.

Before dealing with the substantive grounds of appeal I would like to address one important preliminary issue raised by the respondent counsel. Counsel contends, and rightly so, in my view that under Rule 20 (1) of the court of appeal Rules, 1997 CI.19 the appellant had twenty one days of being notified that the record was ready, within which to file a written submission of his case. The further argument is that since in the instant case, he filed his case not within the stipulated period but several weeks after the said notification, this appeal ought not to be entertained, as the same by operation of law, is deemed under the rule 20(1) of CI.19 to have been struck out. On my part, the rather difficult question is whether if this objection must be upheld, the appeal ought dismissed in limine and the appellant asked to take the necessary steps to have the said appeal restored and disposed of on the merits. I would not choose this option.

Although by the rule 20(1) of CI.19, where an appellant breaches the rule 20(1) the appeal is without more considered or deemed to have been struck out, the same rule imposes a mandatory duty, albeit an administrative duty on the registrar to inform the parties accordingly of the state of the pending appeal. The importance of the registrars compliance with this rule cannot be overemphasised. I believe it is intended to instruct the parties of the state of the appeal, to enable each party pursue his or her rights as exists thereunder. So for example the errant “appellant” may quickly run to the court and pray for an extension of time within which to comply with the rules in question.

Again, although respondent counsel does not so expressly state, the point raised is clearly in the nature of a preliminary objection. That being so counsel is obliged under the rules of court, as provided for under Rule 16 of CI.19 to give three clear days notice before the hearing of the objection. Additionally, he is also required to set out the grounds of objection in the notice specified in form 8 in part 1 of the schedule. The rule is clearly so self explanatory. I would reproduce it. It stipulates (i) “a respondent who intends to rely upon a preliminary objection to the hearing of the appeal shall give the appellant three clear days notice before the hearing of the preliminary objection setting out the grounds of objection, and shall file the notice specified in form 8 in part 1 of the schedule together with five copies of the appeal with the Registrar within the same time.”

Now, a respondent who fails to comply with the rule 16(1) of CI.19 may not be with any one of these three consequences. In other words the court has three options with the third giving the court a fairly wide choice of other orders to make. I would first reproduce the rule. It states:—

“2 If the respondent fails to comply with this rule, the court may refuse to entertain the objection, or may adjourn the hearing at the cost of the respondent or may make such other order as it thinks fit”.

(1) Failure to comply with the above rule may lead the court to refuse to entertain the objection or

(2) adjourn the hearing at the cost of the respondent, and I think the purpose here is to enable the respondent comply with the rule or

(3) the court is at a discretion to make any such order(s) as it thinks the circumstances justify.

Given the peculiar facts of this case, namely

(1) The parties were never notified as it is required under the rules that the appeal has been struck out,

(2) The respondents themselves failed to comply with the rules governing the taking of preliminary objections.

I would in order not to jeopardise the right of the parties and further delay the hearing of this appeal on the merits refuse to entertain the objection. We will proceed to determine on the appeal the merits.

By the first ground of appeal, the respondent counsel criticizes the learned trial judges for concluding that the pleadings disclose triable issues. He submitted that on a close examination of the pleadings, “when shorn of all the verbiage and red herrings” reveals no triable issues. He contends further that the learned trial judge fell into error because he took into consideration matters that were not pertinent to the crucial issue in this case, namely whether or not the respondent owed the sum claimed.

I find this criticism against the learned trial judge most unfortunate. In my view he deserves commendation, not condemnation for the conclusions that he reached.

It is plain from the appellant counsel’s own submissions, in which he identified one important matter in controversy, that indeed the pleadings disclose triable issues and the learned trial judge was perfectly right in so concluding. Indeed, the very authority relied on by the appellant counsel reinforces the point that the respondent is entitled to defend this suit, for she has ably demonstrated that she has a bonafide defence to this action.

On the appellant’s own showing, the case rested on the paragraph 10 and 11 of the statement of claim, both of which are reproduced hereunder:

“10. Immediately upon plaintiff’s return from his trip abroad, he demanded payment of the money from the defendant who then wrote an undertaking dated 27th September, 1996 setting out the dates she intended to make instalment payments to liquidate the debt of £19,595. As compensation for the considerable inconvenience she had caused plaintiff, defendant voluntarily undertook to pay plaintiff a further sum of £2000 making her total indebtedness to plaintiff £21,595.

11. As was her wont defendant did not abide by the undertaking of 27th September, 1996 and to still sustain the confidence of plaintiff made a further written undertaking dated 11th October, 1996 promising to make payment of the first instalment of £10,000 by 18th October, 1996 and liquidate the entire debt by 31st October, 1996”.

The respondents reaction to these allegations are contained in the paragraphs 15 and 16 of her statement of defence namely.

“15. Defendant admits paragraph 10 of the statement of claim but says that this was at the request of the plaintiff to whose knowledge the contents of the undertaking especially as relates to the amount of her indebtedness did not send the true situation between them.

16. Defendant admits she wrote the second undertaking referred to in paragraph 11 of the statement of claim but says that as with the undertaking of 27th September, 1996, this undertaking was also at the request and dictation of the plaintiff” (emphasis mine).

The essence of these two paragraphs is that these two undertakings, being at the request and dictation of the appellant do not qualify as voluntary acknowledgements of the debt. The further explanation contained in the paragraph 19 of the affidavit in opposition which sets out the alleged purpose for the making of these undertaking was intended to buttress the claim that these undertakings were not voluntary. The paragraph 19 reads:

“That the plaintiff applicant dictated the terms of Exhibit S.S.4 and when I queried him he said he needed a statement in these terms to enable his suppliers ship his X’mas goods to him. He assured me that he would not use the statement for any other purpose"”.

It therefore comes as a surprise that appellant counsel who himself concedes that these pleadings raise the rather crucial issue of the validity of these undertakings (which were annexed to the application for summary judgment as Exhibits S.S.4 and S.S.5:) would argue that no triable issues have been raised and further that the appellant ought to have been allowed to sign final judgment.

Nevertheless, apart from this principal issue identified by the dissatisfied appellant himself, both the trial judge and the respondent counsel in his submission, justifiably (as it is borne out by the pleadings) identified two other critical factual issues. These are:—

(1) The issue of whether or not the respondent has paid an amount of £3,000 as per the international money order No. 5502-0000-436-860 dated 5/7/96 as contended by her and consequently therefore (2) what if any, is the quantum of the respondent’s indebtedness, for she contends she has fully satisfied the entire debt. These are all factual issues which in accordance with the rules of procedure must be tried by viva voce evidence and the parties and or their respective witnesses, if any, subjected to cross examination rather than as it seems to be the suggestion of appellant counsel, through affidavit evidence which, it must be understood, was presented solely for the purpose of determining whether or not this is a case fit for summary judgment

In my opinion, the approach advocated by appellant counsel, namely that these issues be determined on the strength of the affidavits and so he does this by presenting arguments why their version ought to be preferred, stems from a misapprehensive of what constitutes triable issues and also a trial judge’s duty in applications of this kind. I think that in such applications, ie applications to sign final judgment, what is required of a trial judge is that he/she examine the pleadings and determine whether these does exist a bonafide or a good defence, that is a defence known in law. In my view any such bonafide or good defence or a defence known in law when raised would constitute a triable issue or an issue fit to be tried. It could be an issue of fact or law. But a judge is not empowered to test the success or otherwise, of the defence intended to be relied on, otherwise stated, he is not to try the merits of the respective claims using the affidavit evidence on hand. Indeed, the affidavit evidence presented in an application for summary judgment is not intended to be used for the resolution of triable issues that may emanate from the pleadings. The court is not supposed to adopt a short cut procedure by examining the rival claims in the light of the affidavit and annexures and so determine which claim on the balance of probabilities succeeds. Such a procedure is palpably wrong and ought not to be encouraged, for it undermines the very foundation of justice.

Local and English authorities—both case law and text writers confirm these views that I have expressed. I would refer to a few of those referred to by counsel on both sides.

We find the following general principles stated in one of the older editions of the English Supreme Court Annual Practice known also as the White Book page 191 under the heading “leave to defend”.

“Leave to defend is not to be given unless it is clear that there is no real substantial question to be tried (Codd vrs: Delap 1905, 92 LTHL) that there is no dispute as to facts or law which raises a reasonable doubt that the plaintiff is entitled to judgment Jones vrs: Stone 1984 AC 122...........

As a general principle where a defendant shows that he has a fair case for defence or reasonable grounds for setting up a defence or even a fair probability that he has bonafide defence he ought to have leave to defend (emphasis mine)”.

Furthermore at page 192 the learned authors writing under the heading “Question of Fact”; cite the following as instances in which leave to defend has been granted.

“Uncertainty as to the amount actually due Lynde vrs: Waithman 1985 2QB 180, Pearson vrs: Fane 5 TLR 323).

Again, Giles Francis Harwood in his well known book Ogders Principles of pleading and Practice in civil actions in the High Court of Justice the 19th Edition at page 63 buttresses the points I have already made. He writes:

“The defendant is not bound to show a good defence on the merits. He must however satisfy that there is an issue or question which ought to be tried or that there ought for some reason to be trial of the claim (r 3). “If therefore the defendant shows such a state of facts as leads to the inference that at the trial of the action he may be able to establish a defence to the plaintiff’s claim he ought not to be debarred of all power to defeat the demand made upon him.... and leave to defend may on such terms as may be thought just (Per Brett LJ in Ray vrs: Baker 1897 4 Ex D at page 283)

More importantly, in Wilson vrs: Smith 1980 GLR 152 Anin JA (of blessed memory) as he then was, states the principles which govern applications of this nature. True the application was brought under the old order 14 r 1 of LN 140 whose wording is different from the new. But the substance remains unchanged and the same legal principles govern their respective applications. His stated position was that (page 161)

“While it is true that the rationale behind the Summary Procedure under order 14 of LN 140 is to provide the plaintiff with a speedy mode of recovery of judgments ... provided of course that is no arguable defence to the action nevertheless the order was not intended as an engine for the suppression of the defendant”.

In his view defence filed “ought to be scrutinised for any triable issues”. It follows that once the defence is found to contain any such issues, the application ought to be turned down and the defendant asked to defend the action.

Finally, let me say that in granting leave, in this case unconditionally, the learned trial judge exercised a discretion. As an appellant court we are not to sit on appeal from his discretion to ours. We can only interfere with his decision if he failed to act judicially, in this case, if he did not act on the facts before him.

It is regrettable that appellant counsel describes the respondents defence as mere “verbiage” that is to say too many useless words. Not only is the remarks unsavoury but it is incorrect. On the contrary the learned trial judge’s finding that the defence raises triable issue is supported by the record and it ought not to be disturbed. In the result the appeal on this ground fails.

The Additional Ground 1 reads:—

“The trial judge erred in not taking into consideration the inconsistencies in the affidavit of the defendant/respondent which made her defence a sham, which error has occasioned for the plaintiff appellant a substantial miscarriage of justice”.

It is again plain from the arguments proffered by the appellant counsel that he expected the trial judge to determine the rival claims on the strengths of the affidavits before him. Indeed, at this rehearing he continued in the same vein, raising queries, putting up strong arguments reasons and by pointing out inconsistencies and conflicts which in his view ought to make the respondent’s story improbable and their version on the balance, the more probable. Little wonder in concluding his submission he states:—

“Since this application is to be determined on affidavit evidence the trial judge should have preferred the evidence of the appellant which was concise, consistent and credible as against that of the respondent which was shifty, inconsistent, after thought and continued”.

It is interesting to note that both sides in their submissions presented arguments showing that some explanation is needed from the deponents of the respective affidavits. But does this stand taken by both sides not rather justify the conclusion that this is a case which ought to go to trial? The point which I have already made and which, at the risk of sounding repetitive I do not fear to repeat, is that the application for summary judgment is not intended to be the forum for determining triable issues or rival claims. How can the deponents offer explanations as they are entitled in law to do, and explain inconsistencies if they do not mount the witness box and be examined on oath?

In my view, the learned trial judge, having satisfied himself that there are triable issues was right in not proceeding to determine the issues by reference to seeming inconsistencies in the affidavits. He was right in asking the parties to submit themselves to trial, where through the process of examination the queries raised on both sides of the divide may be effectively addressed. Indeed, it is after this process that the trial court would be in a position to determine who is consistent and who is not, who prevaricates or shifty and who remains resolute and firm and who is or is not concise and consequently who is credible and who is not.

I find no merit in this ground of appeal also and the appeal based on it fails.

JUSTICE OF THE APPEAL COURT.

BROBBEY, J.A.:

I agree

JUSTICE OF APPEAL COURT.

ARYEETEY, J.A.

I also agree
