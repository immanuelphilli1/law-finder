YAW APPAU, J. A: I have read beforehand the judgment of my learned and respected colleague Acquaye J.A. and I am ad idem with him that the appeal as it stands has no merit and must be dismissed. As a corollary to what my brother has rightly alluded to in his judgment, the power granted an accused person in a jury trial to challenge peremptorily or for cause any of the jurors at the time they are being empanelled is a statutory creation and neither a common law nor and equitable right. It is provided under sections 249, 250 and 251 of Act 30/60. It is trite law that where a statute creates a new right which previously did not exist at common law and the statute provides a forum or machinery for protecting that right, a person wishing to claim appropriate relief for an infringement of that right must resort to the forum or the machinery set up by the statute. See S.A. de Smith's Judicial Review of Administrative Action (3rd ed) at page 316 and then the majority decision of the Supreme Court in the case of IN RE PARLIAMETARY ELECTION FOR WULENSI CONTITUENCY; ZAKARIA V NYIMAKAN, per DR. SETH TWUM, JSC (2003¬-2004) Vol. 1 SCGLR page 1 @ page 10. There is nothing in the statute (i.e Act 30/60) that says that before the empanelling of the jury is done in open Court for trial to begin in respect of any indictable offence, the accused person or his counsel must be supplied with the names and particulars of all the potential jurors listed for the session for the accused to conduct a background check on the said persons before they could be empanelled. Nothing could therefore be imported into the statute that created the right or power that is already not provided for in the statute. The essence of the power granted such an accused person to challenge the inclusion of a particular juror or jurors is to ensure that someone already known to the accused as having or harbouring any of the inhibitions provided under section 251 of Act 30/60 is not made to sit, in judgment against the accused to avoid the likelihood of bias on the part of such person.The law cannot be extended to cover the unimaginable as the appellants were praying for in the Court below; i.e to dig into the background of people they did not know to find out whether these people would be fair-minded or not before allowing some of them to be selected as judges of fact to determine their fate. The question is; how could the appellants carry out this laborious exercise of determining the pre¬existing biases of people unknown to them, armed with only the names and particulars of such people? Is it not tantamount to saying that accused persons or litigants be made to dig into the background of judges to find out whether such judges would be fair-minded or not before allowing them to sit in judgment against them? That would be over-stretching the statutory power granted accused persons under sections 249 - 251 of Act 30 beyond its elastic limits. I do not think that is what the law envisages so no matter the ingeniosity that abounds in the submissions of appellants' counsel on that point, the submissions cannot be sustained. Aside of this, I have nothing further to add. COUNSEL MR. THADDEUS SORY FOR 1ST & 2ND APPELLANTS PRE MR. EDWARD DUODO FOR THE REPUBLIC PRESENT.
