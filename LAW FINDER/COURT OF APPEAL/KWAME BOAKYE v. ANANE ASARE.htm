JUDGMENT

WOOD, J. A.:  

I agree that the appeal be allowed.  The trial judge’s finding that while the appeal was pending in this Court, the parties submitted themselves and the matter to arbitration and compromised the decision of the trial High Court is unimpeachable. It is supported by evidence on the record. That being so, the Court ought also to have held in the appellants favour that the failure to make the following disclosure to the Court amounts to or constitutes fraud on the appellants.

(a)  The fact of the compromise

(b)  The fact that the parties have agreed to discontinue the pending appeal and not to persue it.

Not surprisingly, for the record supports such a view, the trial judge found that by proceeding to prosecute the appeal in the absence of the appellant and without making a full and frank disclosure of their respective positions the respondent had practised a deceit on his opponent.

Apaloo JSC's speech in Barclays Bank DCO vrs: HEWARD MILLS 1964 GLR 332 would clearly support the view that the respondent’s fraudulent misrepresentations (for clearly that in effect is what his non disclosures amount to) are sufficient grounds upon which the judgment so obtained could be set aside on the grounds of fraud.

His Lordship stated: "we are not aware of no rule of law which lays down that if a man is misled by this naked form of deceit from resisting an action against himself, he cannot subsequently ask that judgment obtained against him be set aside, if he subsequently discovered the truth. We think a judgment can properly be said to be obtained by fraud even if no evidence was led and where, as in this case the respondent abstained from offering resistence because of fraud". (Emphasis Mine)

The principle of law clearly discernible from the speech of his Lordship is that it is not only cases in which fraud is practised directly on the court that one could succeed in setting aside the decision so obtained, as for example obtained a judgment by fraud practised directly on court.

In the circumstances I think the learned trial judge erred in dismissing the appellant case.  I would set aside the dismissal and in its place grant the appellant’s prayer.

G. T. WOOD (MRS.)

JUSTICE OF APPEAL COURT.

AFREH, J. A.:

This is an appeal against the judgment of D.K. Okyere J, sitting in the High Court, Sunyani, on 18/10/93.

By his Writ issued on 4/1/89 (as amended) the plaintiff/appellant (appellant) claimed against the defendant/Respondent (respondent) for:

(a) A declaration that whilst there was an appeal by the Defendant to the court of appeal (CA 140/89) the defendant submitted to an arbitration to settle the Land in dispute between the parties and the defendant is bound by the decision of the said arbitration.

(b) The defendant acted in fraud of the Plaintiff when the defendant pursued the said appeal without informing the plaintiff after the settlement where the defendant told the gathering that he had abandoned the said appeal.

(c)  An order setting aside the judgment of the court of appeal as the said judgment was attained by fraud.

The facts of the case are not in dispute. The predecessor of the appellant, one Alasa Awua alias Yaw Awuah, sued the respondent in the District Court Grade I sometime in 1977. That court gave judgment in favour of Awuah. The respondent appealed to the High Court, Sunyani, and won. Awua appealed to the court of appeal. But while the appeal was pending he died and the present appellant was substituted in his place.

There was another development before the appeal was heard. As a result of a C.D.R. intervention the dispute was settled and the land in dispute was demarcated between the parties.  As the court below found, the respondent also agreed not to pursue the appeal. But contrary to the settlement, and without informing the appellant, the respondent pursued the appeal and got judgment in his favour. The judgment of the court of appeal is reported as Awuah vrs: Adututu and another in [1987-88] 2 G.L.R. 191 (Coram Abban JSC, Osei Hwere and Lamptey JJA.)  The report states at page 194 that there was no appearance for or on behalf of the respondents".

After obtaining judgment in the court of appeal the respondent harvested and carried away the appellant's cocoa and food crops. In November, 1988 the appellant applied to the court of appeal to set aside its judgment on the ground that it was obtained by fraud. The court dismissed the application on 19/12/88. Because of its importance to this case I think it should be quoted in full:

"BY COURT: We are of the opinion that if the applicants' allegations are true then the respondent acted fraudulently in coming to this court to pursue their appeal and also for failing to bring to the notice of the court the said compromise of the judgment in the applicants' favour.  The respondent is however denying the allegation and we do not feel it will be appropriate for us to go unto the matters asserted and denied. We feel that the applicants can have their proper cause of action if so intended. So far as this court is concerned the appeal was listed before us and properly heard.  We accordingly dismiss the application with ¢5,000.00 costs to the respondent.”

Taking the cue that he could have his "proper cause of action if so intended", the appellant instituted the action from which this appeal has been brought.

After hearing evidence the learned trial judge made the following findings, among others.

“I find the defendant an untruthful witness. I find that those who went to the land demarcated the boundary in the presence of the plaintiff and the defendant and the three grantees ....

I am satisfied that there was a settlement between the parties and that each party paid ¢1,000.00 to signify his acceptance of the boundary between them. I find further that the parties agreed not to pursue the appeal which was then pending in the court of appeal. They thus compromised the judgment of the High Court .... The parties had, prior to the determination of the appeal by the court of appeal, come to a compromise. Both of  them were therefore bound by the award of settlement. They agreed not to pursue the appeal in the court of appeal.  Without any rejection of the settlement and notice to the applicant, the defendant went to the court of appeal and had the appeal granted by his counsel. The court of appeal was not informed of the compromise. It reversed the judgment of the High Court. Clearly, I am satisfied that the defendant deceived the plaintiff."

In spite of these categorical findings the judge gave judgment in favour of the respondents. I think he felt bound by judgment of Van Lare J. (as he then was) in Gbadago vrs: Tsili (1955) 2 WALR II where the learned  judge held that the essential ingredient of a claim for the setting aside of a judgment on the grounds of fraud is that the judgment concerned must have been obtained as a result of a fraud practised upon the court (usually by way of fraudulent evidence).  The learned trial judge did not think the two particulars of fraud pleaded by the appellant contributed to the appeal being allowed by the court of appeal. The particulars were:

(a) The defendant pursuing the appeal despite his compromise of the judgment.

(b) The defendant’s failure to bring to the notice of the court of appeal the compromise."

In my view the learned trial judge erred in holding that the judgment was not obtained by fraud.  If he had considered the ruling of the court of appeal which was put in evidence he would have realised that the appellant based the particulars of fraud on the ruling of the court of appeal. The court made it clear that if the allegations were true then the respondent acted fraudulently, (1) in coming to this court to pursue his appeal and (ii) for failing to bring to the notice of the court the said compromise of the judgment in the appellant's favour.

In my view if the court of appeal had proof of the respondent’s fraud it would not have dismissed appellant's application the way it did.

By his judgment the learned trial judge in effect granted the first and second reliefs sought by the appellant by his writ. Having done so he should have set aside the judgment of the court of appeal. A binding compromise is a contract.  By going to the court of appeal to pursue the appeal he broke that contract. He went back on his promise. If the court of appeal had known this fact it would not have allowed him to do that because that would have tantamounted to condoning a breach of contract, an illegality, and no court should or would do that and allow its processes to be used to achieve that end.

It is in the public interest for the courts to encourage reasonable settlements. That is why section 72 of the courts Act, 1993 (Act 459) imposes a duty on any court with civil jurisdiction and its officers to promote reconciliation, encourage and facilitate the settlement of disputes in an amicable manner between and among persons over whom the court has jurisdiction." Therefore a court which knows that a party is pursuing a litigation in breach of a reasonable settlement should not allow him to do so. And the court of appeal would not have allowed the appellant to pursue his appeal if it had known that he was doing so in breach of a reasonable settlement. We in the instance appeal, know that that was what he did.

The arbitration constituted an estoppel. The respondent was estopped from asserting any claim to the land he had voluntarily given up at the arbitration. It was therefore improper for him to conceal this from the court of appeal.

In my view all these amounted to a fraud on the court of appeal.  Since the court of appeal does not normally hear evidence it cannot be deceived by evidence adduced during or at a trial at first instance. But it can be deceived as in this case, by certain processes being concealed from it.  As the Supreme Court of the first Republic said per Apaloo J.S.C.  (as he then was) in Barclays Bank D.C.O. vrs: Heward-Mills [1964] G.L.R. 332, at page 339 "a judgment can properly be said to be obtained by fraud even if no evidence was led." In that case the respondent thereon abstained from offering resistance because of fraud.  The court applied the decision of the English court of appeal in the case of Thorme vrs: Smith [1947] K.B. 307. In that case too the plaintiff a tenant had consented to an order for possession because the defendant and the Landlord of the plaintiff had falsely represented to the plaintiff and the court that he needed the premises for this own occupation. But as soon as he got the order he gave it to an agent to sell. In an action for compensation the English court of appeal rejected the landlord's contention that the order for possession had not been obtained by his misrepresentation.

In the instance case there is no doubt in my mind that the respondent got the court of appeal to hear his appeal, in the absence of the appellant, by deceit. This was a fraud practised on the appellant and the court. In similar circumstances the court set aside a judgment obtained by the Barclays Bank D.C.O. vrs: Heward-Mills (supra).

In that case the bank had obtained a default judgment by concealing the real nature or cause of the debit in respect of which the action had been brought. The Supreme Court affirmed the decision of the High Court setting aside the judgment. The court held that there is no rule of law which lays down that if a man is misled by a naked form of deceit and because of this does not defend an action against himself he cannot subsequently, on discovering the truth, ask that the judgment be set aside. The deceit in this is in my opinion even worse and I cannot see any reason why the judgment of the court of appeal cannot be set aside.

It is immaterial that court decided the case on its merits; or the respondent would have won if the appellant had participated in the appeal. The fact is that the respondent had no right to prosecute the appeal. He knew that. He deceived the court to hear him. He should not be allowed to enjoy the fruits of a victory he obtained by fraud and abuse of the process of the court.

The High Court itself recognised this and dismissed the respondent's counterclaim based on the judgment of the court of appeal.

In my opinion the appeal should be allowed and the judgment of the court of appeal should be set aside on the ground of fraud.

D.K. AFREH.

JUSTICE OF THE APPEAL COURT.

BROBBEY, J. A.:

This case turns on its own peculiar facts.

From the record, there was clear case of fraud on the basis of which the judgment was obtained.

I also agree that the appeal should be allowed.

S. A. BROBBEY.
