JUDGMENT

ARYEETEY, J.A.

This is an appeal against the judgment of Kumasi Circuit Court dated 4th August, 1997 which went against the Defendant Appellant. The Plaintiff/Respondent's claim in the Court below was for

(a) A declaration that the said Abdul Kareem Kwabena Kareem is the owner lessee of all that piece or parcel of land more particularly described as Kumasi-Boadi, Plot 6, Block “A”.

(b) An order for recovery of possession.

(c) Damages for trespass.

(d) An order for perpetual injunction restraining the Defendant herein, his agents, assigns, workers, successors-in-title and all other persons claiming any interest through or under him.

The Defendant/Appellant also counter-claimed as follows:

(a) A declaration that the Defendant is entitled to Plot 6, Block “A”, Kumasi-Boadi.

(b) Order of Injunction to restrain the Plaintiff by himself, servants or agents or otherwise from in any way interfering with the Defendant's occupation and use of Plot No. 6, Block “A”, Kumasi- Boadi or continuing any acts of trespass.

(c) Damages for trespass.

The facts which were not disputed before the trial Court were as follows: On 24th September, 1984 Nana Huahi Tutuwaa II succeeded her grandmother as Obaapanin of Boadi near Kumasi. Her predecessor Obaapanin Akosua Addae had abdicated on account of her old age. After her enstoolment, Nana Huahi Tutuwaa II (PW1) was shown the stool’s properties. It came to her notice that one of the stool’s properties, Plot No.6, Block A. Boadi had a building structure on it. Upon enquiry, it was revealed that the structure on the land had been put up by the Defendant/Appellant. PW1 therefore caused her solicitors to write to the Defendant inviting him to see PW1. The relevant portion of the letter dated 16th June, 1986 reads as follows:

“Our client informs us that you are in occupation of her land Plot No. 6, Block A, Boadi and you have developed the land without her consent.

By this letter, we wish to indicate to you that our client has instructed us to write that you are given a week from the date on receipt of this letter either to see our client or otherwise in absence of that, we shall issue writ of summons against you at High Court for declaration of title against you”.

That letter is marked Exhibit 2. The Defendant/Appellant explained that he was put in possession of the land after he had approached elders of the stool for land and after accredited representatives of the Queenmother Obaapanin Akosua Addae and Odikro Opanin Kwadwo Dabo of Boadi had shown him the land and collected ¢6,000 from him as customary drinks. He obtained a receipt, Exhibit One from the elders which reads:

“I, C. K. Amoako, head of family, have received an amount of ¢6,000 as Customary drinks on a piece of land at Boadi allocated to Mr. Patrick Asumadu, of State Fishing Corporation on this day 15th day of February, 1981 on behalf of Queenmother Obaapanin Akosua Addae and Odikro Opanin Kwadwo Dabo of Boadi. Allocation paper would be issued after new site plan has been approved by the Town Planning Department, Kumasi”.

Exhibit One was signed by C. K. Amoako, who described himself as head of family and Malam Issah Donkor who became the Akwamuhene of Boadi and known as Nana Issah Tabi (DW2).

Eventually, the Defendant/Appellant and PW1 met at the Chambers of the Solicitors of PW1 where PW1 asked the Defendant to pay her ¢140,000 if he were to retain the land. Even though that money was not paid to PW1, the threat in Exhibit 2 to issue a writ against the Defendant/Appellant was not pursued by PW1. However, when he realised that his occupation of the land was being threatened, the Defendant/appellant made a complaint to the Asantehene’s liaison officer who could not resolve the matter.

The totality of the grounds of appeal are as follows:

1. That the judgment of the Circuit Court, Kumasi was against the weight of the evidence.

2. The learned trial Circuit Judge erred in holding that the receipt of ¢6,000 dated 15/2/81 given to the Defendant being the purchase consideration for Plot 6, Block A, Kumasi Boadi was not from the authorised representatives and delegates of Huahi Stool of Boadi or from the occupant of that stool, the customary grant to the appellant was null and void when there was evidence that the signatories of the receipt and along with others, were authorised to grant stool lands to prospective grantees for valuable consideration and thereby pass title to them.

3. The Court erred in coming to the conclusion that the Defendant who acquired his title to Plot 6, Block A, Kumasi Boadi after payment of customary grant consideration of ¢6,000 in 1981 could be divested of title subsequently because he failed or refused to pay a subsequent demand of ¢140,000 from the new stool authority Huahi stool of Boadi for the same plot when the Defendant was in possession and had developed the land.

4. To hold that the appellants counter-claim should fail is not supported by the evidence.

5. The learned trial Circuit Judge failed or refused to consider the case of the appellant diligently and adequately, thereby jumping to conclusions not supported by the evidence.

By far, the most important ground of appeal is additional ground (1) which I have listed as ground (2) after putting the original grounds and additional ground together. In my humble opinion on that ground hinges substantially all the other grounds. According to that ground:

“The learned trial Circuit Judge erred in holding that the receipt of ¢6,000.00 dated 15/2/81 given to the Defendant being the purchase consideration for Plot 6, Block A, Kumasi Boadi was not from the authorised representatives and delegates of Huahi stool of Boadi or from occupant of that stool, the customary grant to the appellant was null and void when there was evidence that the signatories of the receipt and along with the owners were authorised to grant stool lands to prospective grantees for valuable consideration and thereby pass title to them”.

The all important question to ask is whether at the time of the lease to the Plaintiff as evidenced by the lease agreement, Exhibit C there had been an effective customary grant of the land to the appellant. I quite agree with the conclusion reached by the learned trial Circuit Judge that the identity of the land was not in dispute. It was the same Plot 6, Block A, Boadi which was occupied by the appellant that was leased by PW1 to the respondent. The learned Circuit Judge adverted his attention to the issue as to whether there was effective customary grant to the appellant prior to the lease to the respondent. He made the following observation in his judgment at page 114 of the record of proceedings.

“The Plaintiff’s allocation is documented. He had his allocation in 1992 from the Huahi stool and its occupant and elders who are accredited custodians of the land…The Defendant on the other hand pleaded Plot No. 6, Block A, Kumasi Boadi was the subject matter of a customary grant from Boadi stool to the Defendant for a consideration of ¢6,000 on the 15/2/96.

The customary grant was evidenced in writing … I have reminded myself of the fact that merely having documents on the land does not entitle a person to judgment for a declaration of title to land. As was stated by Justice Adade in the case of Hammond v. Odoi [1982-83] GLR p. 1215, esp. pg. 1303-4 where after citing the case of Aryee v. Adofoley (1951) 3 WACA 161, 162 he concluded “where prior oral customary grant can be established, no amount of subsequent conveyance registered or not can defeat the customary title”.

In this case, DW2 testified in support of the Defendant/appellant's case that it was the accredited representatives of the stool who granted the land to appellant. Even though C. K. Amoako was not called as a witness by the Defendant there is evidence that when the Defendant made a report to the liaison officer of the Kumasi Traditional Council, C. K. Amoako was called as a witness. From the evidence of DW2 and the appellant, Amoako’s evidence and that of DW2, were carefully excluded from the report of enquiries made by PW2. Indeed, the head of family, C. K Amoako and DW2 had the occasion to explain their positions on the matter when they met with the liaison officer of Kumasi Traditional Council, Gabriel Bretuo Kyei-Agyen (PW2). From the look of things, the report of the meeting Exhibit G as shown by the cross-examination of PW2 was not a true reflection of what took place at the proceeding at which both DW2 and C. K. Amoako were present. For example C. K. Amoako was not listed in Exhibit G as being present. Even though the appellant and DW2 testified that there were two meetings with PW2, the first one at which C. K. Amoako was present and at which it was established that the grant to the appellant was proper, PW2 was positive that there was only one meeting at which Amoako was present on 4th August, 1992. As it turned out Exhibit G which is dated 4th August, 1992 does not list C. K. Amoako as being present. The cross-examination of PW2 at page 56 of the record clarifies the position.

Q. From what manuscript did you produce Exhibit G.

A. From the notes taken by my assistant Tuffour Samuel.

Q. Is he alive or dead.

A. He is alive.

Q. You will agree with me that Exhibit G is silent as to what C. K. Amoako said at that meeting.

A. That is correct because he did not say anything.

Q. So is that of Mallam Donkor.

A. He did not say anything either.

Q. You are so experienced, is it normal that you sit in on arbitration and parties come before you and they do not say anything and you make your findings.

A. They did not say anything because they knew they had done something wrong. And I also did not bother to find out from C. K. Amoako and Issah Donkor whether they had given the receipt out.

From the answers given by PW2 in cross-examination quoted above, he insists that C. K. Amoako was present at the meeting of 4th August, 1992 as recorded by the report, Exhibit G. When his attention was drawn to the fact that Exhibit G is silent on what C. K. Amoako said at that meeting, his response was that that was because C. K. Amoako did not say anything at the meeting. He added that he “did not bother to find out from C. K. Amoako and Issah Donkor whether they had given the receipt out”. However, the report of the meeting of 4th August, 1992, Exhibit G, which PW2 insists was the only meeting, he had with the Defendant/Appellant and PW1 does not list C. K. Amoako as being present. In fact, PW2 at Page 50 line 29 of the proceedings testified in his examination-in-chief that C. K. Amoako was not present at the meeting at his office in response to his invitation letter, Exhibit F.

The evidence of the appellant and DW2 was that at an earlier meeting with PW2, C. K. Amoako and DW2 owned up as accredited agents of the stool who collected the ¢6,000 from the appellant. PW1 was therefore, advised to have the matter settled since her own head of family had admitted collecting ¢6,000 from the appellant for the land. After that PW2 there and then, asked PW1 how much drinks she would accept from the appellant for the matter to be settled. She asked for a week. The head of family, C. K. Amoako was not present at the second meeting which Exhibit G represents and which comes to a conclusion different from the earlier conclusion at the meeting at which C. K. Amoako was present. As seen from the cross-examination of PW2, he alleged that C. K. Amoako was present at the meeting of 4th August, 1992 even though Exhibit G does not list him as being present. As pointed out earlier, PW2 had stated in his examination-in-chief that C. K. Amoako was not present at that meeting. In any case, if PW2 recollects that C. K. Amoako was at a meeting relating to the land in dispute which is obviously not the meeting on 4th August, 1992. That would lend support to the testimony of the appellant that there was an earlier meeting with C. K. Amoako and Mallam Donkor, DW2 who also testified in support of the appellants case. According to the evidence on record coming from the Defendant/Appellant and DW2, that meeting enabled the appellant to establish his case that he had a valid customary grant of the land from accredited representatives of the stool. However, according to the liaison officer, PW2 there was no such meeting even though he admits at least that there was a meeting at which C. K. Amoako was present. Still on the issue as to whether there was effective customary grant of the land in dispute to the appellant before the enstoolment of PW2, the evidence on record is that the appellant was in effective occupation of the land and I do not think that the nature of the structures of the land as depicted by the photographs which were tendered in evidence would escape the notice of the important members of the stool family who would have a say in disposal of the land to outsiders. As it turned out, the presence of a stranger was not challenged until PW1 was enstooled to replace her grandmother who had abdicated. In the case of Ankrah v. Ofori and others, [1974] 1 GLR 185, it was held that the first and most essential requirement of an alienation of land by customary law was publicity. In that case, it was held that the act of taking possession constituted sufficient publicity to the Plaintiff who owned a house nearby and to the whole world that the land had been sold to the Defendants. In the instant case, there was sufficient publication when the appellant built structures on the land and operated his poultry business there. If there was anything improper about the grant of land to the Defendant in 1981, objection would have been raised from the appropriate quarters since by his activities on the land there was sufficient notice of the presence of the appellant on the land which constituted publication of the customary grant of the land in dispute to the appellant. As it turned out, no objection was raised by any of the members of the stool family until a new queenmother, PW1 was enstooled. In the case of Ankrah v. Ofori (supra), the Court of Appeal held that a conveyance of land made in accordance with customary law was effective as from the date it was made, a deed subsequently executed by the grantor for the grantee could add to but could not take from, the effect of the grant, and where there was an omission to execute the deed of conveyance, that omission could not affect the grantees title. For the reasons given in this judgment, the appellant succeeds in respect of additional ground one. He also succeeds in the original ground of appeal as well as, additional ground two, three and four. The appeal is allowed. I would therefore, set aside the judgment of the Court below and give the appellant judgment in respect of his counter-claim. He is awarded ¢100,000 nominal damages for trespass.

B. T. ARYEETEY

JUSTICE OF THE APPEAL COURT

BROBBEY, J.A.

I am of the view that the appeal succeeds and should be allowed.

The facts have been detailed out in the judgment of my brother Aryeetey J.A and so I do not need to re-state them. There is also no dispute that at the time the land was leased to the respondent, the appellant had been in occupation since 1981. I do not need to dilate on this either. In sum, my reasons for allowing the appeal are the following.

(1) The basic question in the appeal was whether the appellant’s grant was legal and valid. I hold the view that the grant was valid because there was no occupant of the stool at the time of the grant and PW1 as well as the parties and the other witnesses agreed that the Odikro and Queen parties and the other witnesses agreed that the Odikro and Queenmother were those authorised to grant those lands. The evidence of the appellant was that when he desired to obtain land he was made to appear before the Odikro, by name Opanin Dabo, the queenmother, Akosua Addai, C.K. Amoako Head of family, Mallam Issah who later became Akwamuhene and other elders. His request was considered. He was asked to pay ¢6000. He paid it there and then. Later C.K. Amoako issued a receipt for the payment.

This scenario leads me to conclude that the money was paid for the land and it was meant for those who named the price. It was also meant for those who were leasing the land, who in actual fact were the Odikro, Obaapanin and the elders present when the money was received.

(2) Secondly, the grant was a customary one and I consider it as such. Being a customary grant, it would have been valid even if there were no receipt, provided there was some evidence that money was demanded and money was paid. The significance of the receipt, exhibit one lies in the fact that it evidences the fact of the payment of ¢6000 for the land following the naming of a price by the landlord. Even if the receipt did not expressly mention that the money was given to the Odikro or Obaapanin or the elders, it stated categorically that it was received on behalf of the landlords. I can place no other interpretation to what happened than that the money was meant for the land and was received for the land. Finally they, eventual recipients, were to be the very people who named the price to be paid, namely the Odikro, Obaapanin and the elders.

(3) To my mind, the appellant’s testimony that he obtained the land from C.K. Amoako and Mallam Issah was correct but that has to be understood to mean that it was those two who actually demarcated the land to him.

In his evidence, he said C.K. Amoako and Issah were mandated to show him the land. Since they did that following the demand by the landlord for and payment of ¢6000, they showed the land to him on behalf of those landlords.

4. It can safely be stated that judicial notice may be taken of the fact that at customary law, the Odikro and the queenmother do not handle cash in public. If moneys had to be received, it is received on their behalf.

In the instant case it was done by no less person than C.K. Amoako whom none of the parties disputed to be the head of the family.

5. The hard facts are that if the new queenmother claimed that she could grant land, there was no reason why the person whom she succeeded could not grant land.

6. By the time she became the queenmother, the disputed land had been granted to the appellant by her predecessor. Therefore, there was no other land available to her to grant. This is the principle well settled by the Supreme Court in REPUBLIC VS. LANDS COMMISSION: EX PARTE VANDERPUYE ORGLE STATEDS 1999.

7. What makes her case even worse is that she agreed to extract more money from the person she saw working on the land by authority of her predecessor. If she had been given the ¢140,000 she would have left the appellant alone. That clearly demonstrated that she was more interested in making money of the land. But she acted at the wrong time because by the time she became queenmother, there was no land for her to grant.

For these and the reasons stated in the judgment of my brother Aryeetey, J.A. I would allow the appeal, set aside the judgment of the trial court and reverse its outcome.

S.A. BROBBEY

JUSTICE OF APPEAL

WOOD (MRS.), J.A.

Unfortunately, I do not agree with my learned brothers that the appeal be allowed and the judgment of the lower court reversed. I think the learned trial judges findings and conclusion were borne out by the record. The inferences are justified and I do not think we ought to disturb them. At the trial, both parties sought for a declaration of title to land, perpetual injunction and damages for trespass, with the plaintiff respondent additionally putting in a claim for recovery of possession. To my mind, the state of the pleadings clearly placed on the respondent first, the burden of establishing title to the land in question. The respondents claim had been that he obtained a valid lease of the disputed land Plot No. 6 Block A Kumasi Boadi from the owners the Huahi Stool through the person authorised to grant lands, Obaapanin Huali Tutuwaa II. The response of the defendant/appellant who the undisputed facts show was in possession of the said land prior to the grant, to these allegations is two folds. First, he denied the respondents claim that he had obtained a valid lease of the said land, and second, and even more importantly he maintains that even if there were any such grant, it was of  no legal effect since as far back as February, 1981, he had obtained a valid customary grant of the said land from the owners acting through their accredited agents C.K. Amoako and one Mallam Issah. These gentlemen he had pleaded, had acted for and on behalf of the Queen mother Obaapanin Akosua Addai, and Odikro Kwadwo Addai, the persons authorised to dispose of Huahi stool lands of Dabo. Before proceeding to counter-claim, he also although in my view the facts as pleaded by him do not disclose an action in fraud or deceit, claimed that the lease was procured through fraud and was therefore a complete nullity. Inspite of these reservations I have expressed, he ought at least to be commended for acting in accordance with the rules. In compliance with the rules governing pleadings, he particularised the fraud. These particulars are useful for they give further and better particulars of how he acquired the land. It is trite law that he is bound by the pleadings and that is what he has therefore to prove. I reproduce them

“(a) the plaintiff knew or is presumed to know that the land the subject matter of dispute had been customarily granted to the defendant on or before 25th of August, 1992 (SIC)

(b) .…..........................

(c) ............ the land the subject matter of dispute was the subject of an earlier grant to the defendant by the said C.K. Amoako as Head of Family and one Mallam Issah Donkor both acting for and on behalf of queenmother Obaapanin Akosua Addai and Odikro Opanin Kwadwo Dada of Boadi and the purported Lessor knew in 1986 or before executing the lease that the land the subject matter of dispute had customarily being granted to the defendant.”

In his reply and defense to counter-claim, the respondent had denied each of these material allegations and asserted emphatically that there has been no prior lawful or valid grant of the said land to the appellant. The following paragraphs are indeed pertinent to the respondent defense to reply and defense to counter-claim and raise the sole and if not the most critical issue for consideration in this appeal.

“3 Paragraph 2 (supra) is repeated and the plaintiff further states that Nana Huahi stool of Boadi who were the owners and or entitled to the land the subject matter in dispute before same was granted and or allocated to the plaintiff had not by themselves granted the subject matter in dispute to the defendant nor had they at any time authorised any other person(s) to allocate or grant same either customarily or otherwise to the defendant or any other person (s)

Paragraph 3 above is also repeated and the plaintiff states that any alleged grant, be it customary or otherwise to the defendant is improper, invalid, null and void and of no legal effect.

In further answer to paragraph 3 of the statement of defense, the plaintiff repeats paragraph 2, 3 and 4 supra and denies the validity of the alleged customary grant.”

If any authority should be required for the proposition that the burden lay on the respondent

(a) to prove title to the dispute land

(b) further that he succeeds on the strength of his own cases and not on the weaknesses respondent, case I would firstly refer to Duagbor vs: Akyea Djamson. 1984 - 1986 1 GLR 697, a case in which the parties sought for virtually the same reliefs as the parties in this case. The plaintiff brought the action for declaration of title and damages for trespass.

The co-defendant maintaining among other things that he was the chief and owner of the land and further that defendant the land with his consent, counter-claimed Abban JA (as he then was) spelt out the plaintiff's duty under the circumstances as follows:—

 

“The plaintiff having sought a declaration of title to the land in dispute, could only succeed upon the strength of his case and not on the weakness of the co-defendants case. It is true that the co-defendant also counter-claimed for a declaration of title but that is beside the point. For the co-defendants counter-claim could not in any way lighten the weight of the burden on the plaintiff to establish his title to the land by preponderance of admissible evidence. The defense having denied the plaintiffs title and then counter- claimed for title, a burden was placed on the plaintiff to prove his title satisfactorily or else he must fail: see Nkyi vrs: Kumah 1959 GLR 281 CA and Nelson vrs: Amoah 1940 6 WACA 134.

Should respondent have failed to prove his title, his claim would have been dismissed leaving the plaintiff in possession. Again, another authority for proposition is Asare and others vrs: Appau II 1984-1986 1 GLR 599. The position of the law was detailed as follows:—

“The common run of land suits in our Courts has as the plaintiff a person who claims title to land. Such a defendant need not and usually does not seek any relief in the proceedings being content with things as they are Kodilinye vrs: Odu (supra) says that in that event the plaintiff must rely on the weakness of his opponents i.e. lack of title in the defendant. So that if the plaintiff fails to prove that he is entitled to have a declaration made of his title to the land, the action ought to be dismissed, leaving the defendant in possession of the land”. Per Amua-Sekyi JA (as he then was) page 63. By way of emphasis, may I repeat that the mere facts that appellant had counter-claim would not change the position of the law. Undoubtedly the respondent succeeded in establishing a prima facie title to the land in question. He demonstrated, as he had pleaded that

(a) he validly acquired a leasehold interest in the said land from Obaapanin Huahi Tutuwaa II of Boadi, the person authorised to alienate Huali stool lands.

(b) He went into immediate possession and mailed cement blocks on the said land.

The plain truth is that none of these important matters deposed to by respondent were challenged by the appellant in any manner. It comes as no surprise. Although in his defense, he had denied the existence of any such lease and so as it were put him to strict proof, his main challenge to his opponents claim is that he had, prior to this grant acquired a valid freehold customary interest in the subject matter. As regard the respondents grantor the PW1 Nana Huahi Tutuwaa II she was emphatic both in her evidence in chief and cross examination that neither Amoako nor Mallam Issah Donkor, the appellant’s alleged grantors have been given authority to grant lands belonging to the stool. Having established a prima facie case to title, the evidential burden shifted on to the appellant to prove a better or superior title.

In real terms what was required of him was simply to rebut the prima facie led by the respondent by establishing on a preponderance of probabilities that he had prior to the respondents acquisition acquired a valid customary grant of said land from the lawful owners, who incidentally are the appellant’s grantors. Under the circumstances of his case, he was to prove that the said C.K. Amoako and Mallam Issah Donkor had the authority of the Obaapanin and Odikro at the time of alienate the said land. The necessity of proving these persons authority with particularly arises not only because of (a) the state pleadings. (b) the persistent denials by the incumbent Obaapanin PW1 that her predecessor authorised the persons named but (c) because also of the evidence of PW2, the liaison officer of the Kumasi Traditional Council who deals mainly in land matters of the traditional area. He is the person to whom the appellant ran for refuge when he felt PW1 was wrongfully dispossessing him of his lawfully acquired property. His unchallenged testimony on the question of who ordinarily or prima facie has authority to allocate Boadi stool lands is as follows:

“I know that the occupant of Huahi stool is the person authorised to allocate Boadi lands. In 1981, there was no occupant of Huahi stool and so the Odikro and his Obaapanin were the persons authorised to allocate lands of the Huahi stool. The Obaapanin of the Odikro was called Akosua Addai. The Odikro was also called Opanin Kwadwo Dado of Boadi. In 1981, they had not allocated any plots to the defendants.”

It is plain that where a party seeks to justify an action which on the face of it is unlawful by alleging he had authority so to act it is certainly incumbent upon him to prove his authority clearly.

Indeed the law which enjoins him to prove on the balance of probabilities these matters i.e. that he had the authority of the Obaapanin and Odikro to grant appellant the land are S. 11 (1) and S. 14 of the Evidence Decree 1975 NRCD 323. They provide

“(1) For the purposes of this Decree, the burden of producing evidence means the obligation of a party to introduce sufficient evidence to avoid a ruling against him on that issue.

(2) In a criminal action………

(3) In a criminal action………

(4) In other circumstances the burden of producing evidence required a party to produce sufficient evidence so that all the evidence, a reasonable mind could conclude that the existence of a fact is more probable than its non-existence. S 14 Except as otherwise provided by law unless and it is shifted a party has the burden of persuasion to each fact the existence or non-existence of which is essential to the claim or defense he is asserting, the importance of discharging this burden cannot be over emphasised. Where he succeeded in so doing and thereby clearly succeeds in proving a prior oral customary grant, no amount of subsequent conveyances, registered or not, can defeat the customary title: (see Hammond vs. Odoi 1982-1983 GLR 1215 per Adade JSC at 1304.

Having read the record, I find the appellant counsel criticism of the learned trial judge, as may be gathered from the various grounds of appeal wholly unjustified. In other words, the learned trial judges findings both in law and fact would clearly find support from the record. Indeed it is difficult to fathom the inclusion of the additional ground 3 which claims the learned trial judge erred in holding that “the appellant’s counter-claim should fail for non description of the allotted plot” when she made no such finding. I do not think the appellant succeeded in establishing the critical matters raised by his own defense and consequently he was unable to prove that he had as far back as 1981 acquired a prior valid customary grant of the said land.

Quite apart from the reasons advanced by the trial judge for dismissing his claim I find the evidence led on these matters inconsistent and so wholly unreliable. In the first place although by the paragraph 5 (c) of his defense and counter-claim he had alleged “that the land the subject matter of dispute was the subject on earlier grant…by C.K. Amoako……and Mallam Issah both acting for and on behalf of the Obaapanin and Odikro his evidence in chief on the question of who made the grant is different. He disposed.

“On the agreed date J went with Mr. Asante to the Palace of Boadi where we met C.K. Amoako. The Chief of the town called Opanin Kwadwo Dado, the Obaapanin, Akosua Addai, Mallam Issah Donkor and Opanin Amoah and elders were also present. I told the chief and his Obaapanin that I wanted a plot of land to work on. They said they will give me the land. C.K. Amoako, Mallam Issah and another were mandated to show me a plot of land. I gave a bottle of schnapps. They brought out a plan and showed the layout of the kind they were going to give me and they showed me the land physically I expressed interest in the land. I was asked to pay ¢6000 by the chief and Obaapanin. There and then I paid the ¢6000 and was issued with a receipt.”

It does come out from his evidence, which to my mind is at variance with his own pleadings, that the persons who granted him the land were the two who had power to do so the Obaapanin and Odikro and not Amoako and Issah as he had all the trial contended. His own account on the role played by the two gentlemen including a third Amo, namely that they were merely mandated to go and show him the land would rather confirm the position of the respondents that they had no authority to grant Huahi-Boadi lands and more importantly, they had no authority to alienate land to the appellant. Again their inconsistency weakens or destroys their case (see Dam v. Addo 1962 GLR.)

Indeed the evidence of this DW2, the person he called to prove that the grantors acted on authority and so had the capacity, and power to alienate the land had a double if not a triple negative effect on his case. His evidence if it is to be believed disclosed that it was the elders—Abusuapanin C.K. Amoako Akwasi Amo, Krontihene Mr. Ampofor Opanin Dabo and Obaapanin Akosua Addai who sold the appellant the land a contention which my view is different from the facts pleaded.

This also naturally raised the question, if the grant was made by the elders, indeed if as he contends payment was made to the Obaapanin who from his account did the actual allocation, why do we have a receipt in the name of C.K. AMOAKO? who is alleged to be acting on behalf of the grantors. In my view then the authority of his alleged grantors is being challenged he has to prove their authority strictly. The best way to have proved same was to have called the person who granted him the authority i.e. Obaapanin Akosua Addae and or the Odikro. He was quite understandably unable to call any of them to testify. The Obaapanin is said to be incoherent. This evidence came from the PWl since this evidence cause from his opponent, on the authority of the principle discernible in cases like

Osei Yaw vs. Domfeh 1965 GLR 418

Asante vs. Bogyabi & anor., 1966 GLR 232 244 he cannot be blamed for not producing her not the Odikro who is dead.

But for same reason which is not evident on the record he never produced C.K. Amoako the principal actor in the drama to the source of their authority, if any. That was total liaison to testify. We have the unchallenged evidence of the PW2 the officer in charge of Kumasi Traditional Council to the effect that at the time of the Exhibit 1 the Boadi laws had not come under statutory planning scheme.

It was intended to cast doubts on the genuiness of the transaction for he also pointed out that the receipt did not affect an allocation site and bore no plot number. It was therefore essential to have called Amoako to testify on the crucial matter of how he came by or obtained his authority to grant land to the appellant.

Be that as it may the evidence of the only witness he called ought to have satisfied the court on these matters. But he gave no evidence on when where and how they were given authority to grant land. On the contrary the evidence he gave showed that he himself made no such grant as had been pleaded by the respondent. If his evidence were anything to go by the only role they play in the process was a caretakership role, looking after the stool lands and pointing out to prospective grantees land granted them by the lawful authorities. I do not think the appellant succeeded in establishing the burden that was placed on him. Question like why it took the present Obaapanin that long before challenging the authority of the appellant may be described as weaknesses. These can be of benefit to the appellant only if the respondent had succeeded in establishing his title. It is then that he can successfully these matters to strengthen his case and make the respondent contention that they had no authority hollow and sham. In other words he cannot use these weaknesses as proof of the important matters in contention. I do not think he is therefore entitled to judgment.

G.T. WOOD (MRS.)
