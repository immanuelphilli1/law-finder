JUDGMENT

WOOD J.A.:

Unfortunately, I find myself unable to agree with my two learned brothers and I am constrained therefore to give my opinion in this case. This appeal which started off as a simple action between a farmer living at Manso, Mpatum Ashanti and a mining Company, Bonte Gold Mines Limited, raises interesting legal questions. I would however, before addressing those issues, give a brief resume of the facts giving rise to this appeal.

The plaintiff respondent sued the defendant appellant company in the Circuit Court Kumasi for (1) a declaration of title to land and (2) damage for trespass. The action was premised on the fact that the appellant company claiming to have completely destroyed the whole of the 4000 teak trees cultivated on it, and have refused or failed to compensate the respondent for the financial loss suffered. In sum then, their case was that the appellants had no legal right to be on the land.

At the hearing, the trial court found as a fact that the appellants, having acquired a valid mining right and licence in the land, was lawfully on the land and consequently dismissed the claims. She however awarded respondent the sum of ¢2.6m as compensation. The appellant company being dissatisfied with the decision has appealed to us on three grounds namely:

“(a) Since there was evidence on the record that the defendant had a legal right to be on the land by the grant of a lease by the Government of Ghana to mine gold in the disputed area, and since by the provisions of PNDCL 153 section 50 the defendant can destroy any crop trees on the land for the purposes of carrying on their mining activities, the learned trial judge erred in law when she held that the defendant had trespassed on to plaintiff land.

(b) Since the plaintiff did not lead any evidence as to the value of the teak tree and since by the provision of PNDCL 153 section 71 it is only the Land Valuation Board that can assess and approve compensation to be paid to farmers whose crops are to be destroyed for mining purposes the learned trial judge erred in law and occasioned a grave miscarriage of justice when she awarded an amount of 2.5million cedis against the defendants as compensation to the plaintiff.

(c) The judgment is against the weight of evidence”.

It is hardly surprising that the appellants abandoned the ground (a) Her conclusion as far as original claims were concerned were dutifully explained as follows:

“from the evidence on record, it is not in dispute that the defendant company has the right to be on the land Exh.1 and the fact that plaintiff accepted compensation for his food crop bear testimony to this fact. That being so, plaintiff’s claim for trespass cannot be sustained and same is dismissed. It is also not in dispute that plaintiff owns that piece of land prior to Exh1”.

The arguments advanced in support of the ground (2) is straight forward. It was forcefully submitted that in matters of compensation for crops and other things destroyed on any land pursuant to a mineral right granted by the Government of Ghana, the only body that shall have authority to determine the quantum of compensation is the Land Valuation Board. That being so, the argument proceeded, the court was palpably wrong in holding that:—

“the non valuation of the teak trees by the Land Valuation Board is not the sine qua non of the payment of the compensation”.

The further submission then was that the court erred in awarding the sum of ¢2.6m as compensation even when he found as a fact that the respondents teak trees have been destroyed, since the same amounts to a usurpation of the powers and functions of the Board. Respondent counsel’s answer is simply that the interpretation the appellant counsel has placed on the s.71 of the minerals and mining law PNDCL 153 is clearly erroneous and the jurisdiction of the courts were never intended to be ousted. His contention is that by a clear construction of the s. 71 of the PNDCL 153, the board assumes the jurisdiction to determine the quantum of compensation only where the parties fail or are unable to reach an agreement as to the amount of compensation. Even so, it does so in consultation with the Secretary.

Counsel’s argument therefore is that in this instant case, in the situation in which the respondent found himself, the proper forum for seeking redress is the court. Counsel’s argument therefore is that in this instant case, in the situation in which the respondent found himself, the proper forum for seeking redress is the court. The s. 71 of the Mineral and mining law PNDCL 153 provides that:

“(1) The owner or occupier of any land subject to a mining right may apply to the holder of the right for compensation for any disturbance of the rights of such owner and for any damage done to the surface of lands, buildings, works or improvements or to livestock crops or trees in the area of such mineral operations”

(2) An application for compensation under subsection (1) of this section shall be copied to the Secretary and the Land Valuation Board.

(3) The amount of compensation payable under subsection (1) of this section shall, subject to the approval of the Land Valuation Board, be determined by agreement between the parties concerned and if the parties are unable to reach an agreement as to the amount of compensation, the matter shall be referred to the Secretary who shall in consultation with the Land Valuation Board determine the compensation payable”

The pertinent question is do these provisions oust the jurisdiction of our courts in determining the quantum of compensation to be paid to the occupier or owner of any land who suffers loss as a direct result of the mining activities of a holder of a mineral right in the land? Put in other words, does the statute vest exclusive jurisdiction for the determination of the amount of compensation (if any) to be paid to a person so affected in the board? I would answer this question in the negative. Ouster clauses are not unknown in our jurisdiction. We have had, and continue to have some of them on our statute books. But the well established position of the law is that where the intention of the legislature is to oust the jurisdiction of the court from determining any particular question of matter, it must do so in express, clear and unambiguous language in giving effect to them. Even so such clauses are construed narrowly. The following provide sufficient authority for the proposition.

Re Vexatious Act 1896 per Somtton LJ

“The right of the subject to have access to the courts may be taken away or restricted by statute, but the language of any such statute will be jealously watched by the courts and will not be extended beyond its least onerous meaning unless clear words are used to justify such expansion. Also in Godsack vrs. Shore 1950 1 AER 276 AT 277 Master of Rods Evershed Mr. had this to say”.

“The jurisdiction of the Kings Court must not be taken to be excluded unless there is clear language in the statute which is alleged to have this effect”.

There is also the more Modern Precedent Anismic Foreign Compensation Commission 969 2 AC a decision of the House of Lords, a decision which has subsequently been followed in a member of our own local courts see for example.

(1) Bakuma vrs. Ekor 1972 1 GRL 133 CA

(2)  Republic vrs. Military Tribunal Ex Parte Ofosu – Amaah 1973 GLR 227, on

(3) Appeal 2 GLR 5.

Republic vrs. Special Tribunal Ex-Parte Forson 1980 – 2 GLR 529. But we find no express words in the Minerals Law PNDCL 153, either expressly and completely taking away the jurisdiction of the courts to determine questions like the quantum of compensation due to a claimant under the law, such as these pure ouster clauses found in laws like as s.34 (3) (4) and (5) of the 1992 Transitional Provisions for example. Neither do we find words vesting the determination of these matters exclusively in the Land Valuation Board as has been urged upon us and as be may found in the Stool Lands Boundaries Settlement Decree 973, PNRCD 172, The Chieftaincy Act, 1971 Act 370. In my view, the role of the Board under the s.71 of PNDCL 153 is clearly circumscribed and it has no such exclusive jurisdiction to determine the quantum of compensation as has been contended. Under the s. 71 of PNDCL 153 the Board has power to do only two things. First, to approve or disapprove the quantum of compensation which a land occupier/owner and the mineral rights holder may have agreed on. Second, where the two are unable to reach an agreement, we note it is not the board alone, but the board acting in consultation with the secretary who determines the compensation payable. Beyond these two limited functions, which has to do with the quantum or amount of compensation payable under any given circumstances, I find nothing in this section nor indeed in the whole of the minerals and mining law stipulating that the courts would have no power to determine questions or matters related to compensation or claims under the law. Specifically I would not think and courts would not have power to determine in the first place issues like whether in any given situation compensation is at all payable. But, may it not be argued that claims for compensation under the law must follow the procedure laid down in the enactment and does this procedure not impliedly oust the jurisdiction of the court in that it vests the determination of the compensation payable in the Board and the Secretary? I do not think so, for the simple reason that there are no clear words to that effect but even if the implication here is that it does and that the proper body for determining the compensation payable is the Board acting in  consultation with the Secretary, I am of the view that there may be instances like in the this instant case, where the ouster clause may be inapplicable either because the conditions for its application are not or cannot be met or because there may be no basis for its application. In such cases, my humble opinion is that the courts intervention in determining what the quantum if any is to be paid as compensation would be justified. It would be presumptuous on my part to state all these instances where such an interference may be lawful and justified but the circumstances this respondents was met with would exclude an application of the statutory provisions. In other words, we find that in this instant case, the condition precedent for invoking the rule procedure was non-existent case. What then are those circumstances which preclude an application of this rule? First, the law envisaged that in claims for compensation the Board alone, and then, failing that the Secretary acting in consultation with the Board step in to determine the quantum. Again, it seems to me that since the Board has by the s.71 been vested with such awesome powers as (1) approving or disapproving the compensation parties have themselves agreed on, (2) in consultation with the Secretary fixing the quantum, the Board as a true arbiter ought not to compromise its position. She must demonstrate the willingness and ability to carry out the functions as spelt out under the law—fairly, justly and in accordance with the basic rules of natural Justice. The functions I am here referring to are approval and disapproval of the quantum of compensation, and in the case of disagreements proceeding to Act in consultation with the Secretary to fix the quantum of compensation. In this instant case when the respondent raised with the respondents the question of compensation, appellants invited the Board to Value the crops and to advise them on the compensation to be paid. The evidence of the DW1 the valuation officer plaintiff shows that he advised against the payment of compensation in respect of the teak trees. His advice was followed thus leading to a disagreement. It follows that the first step in the procedure laid out in PNDCL 153 was not followed. The compensation paid was not negotiated by the parties but was arbitrarily fixed by appellants acting on the advice of the Land Valuation Board. The same, ie the quantum was not approved or disapproved by the Board. And, when clearly there was a disagreement the Board naturally in view of the position it had taken, having lost its independence, having compromised its position by acting as independent advisors to the appellants on the quantum of compensation it should pay to the respondent, was in no position to perform and was utterly incapable of perusing out the mediatory role assigned to it under the statute ie acting in consultation with the Secretary to fix the compensation payable. To have done so would have amounted to inviting them to sit as a judge in their own cause —  a breach of the rules of natural justice. It follows it is utterly impossible if not impracticable and unjust to insist on compliance with the statutory procedures. In the circumstances, respondent who felt aggrieved by the unilateral decision of Bonte was right in seeking redress in the courts and the court was right in assuming jurisdiction. If Bonte would at all material times would want to invite the courts to enforce the s.71 of the Act 153, and also that the Board be made to perform its statutory functions of assessing the quantum of compensation payable in any given case, then it must review its can operations and dealings with the Board as regards this rather sensitive issue (of assessment of compensation) that I have touched on. After all, there are other agencies and bodies Bonte can seek advise from before entering into negotiation with claimants.

Lastly the appellants argue that by the non production of receipts the respondents were unable to prove that they had teak trees to the quantity and value complained of, and consequently the court was wrong in giving judgement in their favour. The court examined this issue and nevertheless relying on Benin J’s (as he then was) dicta in Yanusah vrs. Habena 1991 GLR 559; concluded that he had proved his claim. I think she was right. The Majolagbe vrs. Larbi 1959 GRL 211, case did not lay down any mode for corroborating evidence. In other words if the court found the evidence of a blood brother corroborative, there is little an appellate court can do. True, that the endorsement was for declaration and damages and based on facts pleaded, but the evidence which was not objected to shows that he is entitled to some relief at law. I am like the trial Judge minded to award him compensation. I affirm the award of the court below and dismiss the appeal.

G .T. WOOD (MRS.)

JUSTICE OF APPEAL

BENIN J.A. 

I would agree that the appeal by the defendant/appellant be allowed. I’d only wish to emphasise the point that the court’s jurisdiction to entertain any case is subject to any performance or satisfaction of any prerequisite acts that are imposed by any statute or law. It should never be assumed that because the 1992 constitution or for that matter any other existing law allows uninhibited access to the court to any aggrieved person, an aggrieved person can always go to court even in total disregard for well laid down prerequisite procedures set out by law. For instance under section s.17 of the Rent Act, 1963 (Act 220), a landlord who requires his premises for certain stated purposes has to give the tenant some six months notice of his intention to recover the premises. Such a landlord cannot go to court for the court to give the required notice after it has found that the landlord actually requires the premises for the stated purpose. The entire proceedings will be a nullity for non-compliance with the prerequisite notice.

The plaintiff claims that the defendant has destroyed some trees on his land. It is not in dispute that the very land on which the trees stood is the subject of a mining concession granted the defendant. And it was in the course of operating the concession that the damage is said to have occurred. And according to the plaintiff the defendant has refused to pay any compensation to him for the damage. For that reason the plaintiff sued the defendant at the circuit court in Kumasi. The defendant objected to the writ on ground that the power to assess compensation is by law vested in the Land Valuation Board. The court rejected it and went ahead to assess the compensation.

The Minerals and Mining law, 1986  (PNDCL 153) has set out the procedure to follow in such matters. Section 71 is applicable and it reads:

“(1) The owner or occupier of any land subject to a mineral right may apply to the holder of the right for compensation for any disturbance of the rights of such owner and for any damage done to the surface of the land, buildings, works or improvements or to livestock, crops or trees in the area of such mineral operatives.

(2) An application or compensation under subsection (1) of this section shall be copied to the Secretary and the Land Valuation Board.

(3) The amount of compensation payable under subsection (1) of this section shall, subject to the approval of the Land Valuation Board, be determined by agreement between the parties concerned and if the parties are unable to reach an agreement as to the amount of compensation, the matter shall be referred to the Secretary who shall in consultation with the Land Valuation Board determine compensation payable”.

The case here falls squarely within the four corners of this legislation. The defendant is a mining company which holds mineral right over the land in question. The plaintiff claims to own some teak trees on the land which defendant is alleged to have destroyed. So the legislation says the plaintiff must first apply to the defendant for his compensation. It appears from section 71 (1) that the application should be in writing hence the requirement in section 71 (2) that it shall be copied to the Minister previously called the Secretary (as in PNDCL 153) responsible for mines as well as the Land Valuation Board (LVB).

This is the first step. But it appears from the record that the plaintiff never complied with these provisions for he never pleaded the application, nor did he tender the application, let alone to talk about the fact that he notified the Minister of Mines and the Land Valuation Board as required by the statute. That was enough to defeat his claim.

But the defendant averred they took the trouble of getting the Land Valuation Board to assess the value of the trees destroyed and for this they paid the plaintiff. The defendant insists it is the Land Valuation Board which alone is empowered by law to do such assessment. This is an equally wrong argument for PNDCL 153 is explicitly clear that the Minister and the Land Valuation Board come in only where the parties themselves have failed to reach an agreement as to the amount of compensation payable. And even then by section 71 (3), the Land Valuation Board alone is not empowered to fix the compensation, it can only do so in consultation with the Minister of Mines. Thus in my view PNDCL 153 was breached by both parties, indeed they did not apply it at all to their relationship, as they were bound to do. The case was thus not ripe for court the time the writ was issued and so the court should not have ignored PNDCL 153 to assume jurisdiction which it did not have. See this court’s decision in Republic vrs. District Magistrate Accra; Ex-parte Adio (1972)2 GLR 125. The entire proceedings were a nullity and are accordingly vacated as though no action at all was instituted by the plaintiff.

I would remark that in all such matters the defendant ought to raise the legal issue and set it down for argument at the earliest opportunity and have it argued as a preliminary point, he should not wait for the case to go through a full trial for the obvious reasons (1) that time and expense have to be saved in every litigation wherever possible; (2) that a decision thereon will decide the entire case or a substantial part of it. For the position of the law is that if the legal point has been pleaded, it should be set down for argument immediately since as pointed out above it will dispose of the entire case or a substantial part of it as was stated in the case of Anisminic Limited vrs. The Foreign Compensation Commission (1964) 108 Sol  J.O. 541 CA, per Denning, Mr. This  has been the accepted law all through in order to reduce time and cost of litigation. Earlier decisions on this point are to be found in Grosvenor vrs. White (1889), 61 L.T. 663, and Coksedge vrs. Metropolitan Coal Consumers Association Limited (1891) 65 L.T. 432 C.A. at p 434 per Lindley L.J. And even there are some authorities which say that such fundamental preliminary points of law may be raised at the trial although not pleaded. On this see the case of Independent.

Automatic Sales, limited vrs. Knowles and Foster (1962) 3 All E.R. 27. See also Corl – Zeiss – Shifting vrs. Herbert Smith & Co. (1969) 1 CH. 93 C.A.; (1968) 2 All ER 1002.

Lest its proceedings and decision should be declared void, as has happened in this case, the lower court must draw attention to such prerequisite requirement of the law even where the parties and/or their counsel fail to do so and invite arguments thereon for by so doing the court would be helping the parties both in their pocket and with their time and save the court itself unnecessary trouble; this would not be descending into the area of conflict.

The claim for trespass will fail automatically because the defendants committed no trespass, since they were there by right having acquired the concession in accordance with law. Thus even on the basis of the relief sought the plaintiff could not be awarded the damages.

A. A. BENIN

JUSTICE OF APPEAL.

TWUMASI J. A. 

The  appellants are a limited liability company engaged in mining business. Sometime in October 1994, they acquired a mining concession in the Bonte Fufuom area in the Ashanti Region. A piece of land of which the respondent claimed right of ownership fell within the concession area. In the course of their mining operations the appellants caused extensive destruction to food crops and economic trees cultivated by the respondent on his land. The case was clearly governed by the Minerals and Mining Law, 1986 (PNDCL 153). Under the Law all minerals in the country were declared to be the property of the Republic of Ghana by virtue of section 1 of the law and under section 2 thereof the government was empowered to acquire compulsorily any part of the lands of Ghana found to be rich in mineral deposits. Under section 14 of the law, the rights of the owner of any land found to be blessed with mineral deposits are at par with any other citizen of Ghana in the sense that he cannot prospect for or mine any mineral without a valid licence issued by appropriate Government authorities. The Law recognised and reserved certain rights to owners or occupiers of any lands released for mining. Section 70 (3) of the Law provided that:

“70 (3) the lawful occupier of any land within an area subject to a mineral right shall retain the right to graze livestock upon or to cultivate the surface of such land in so far as such grazing or cultivating does not interfere with the mining operations in the area.”

The respondent’s case was that he cultivated food crops on the land and also some economic trees called teak trees. He was therefore entitled to some compensation for the destruction of the food crops and the teak trees. A reading of paragraph 5 of the statement of defence and 3 of the plaintiff’s reply thereto makes it quite clear that the parties were in unison in their knowledge of the application of the PNDCL 153 regarding payment of compensation to any person who suffers any destruction of his property as a result of mining operations on his land. It is provided under section 71 (1) to 4 of the PNDCL 153 that

“71 (1) The owner or occupier of any land subject to a mineral right may apply to the holder of the right for compensation for any disturbance of the rights of such owner and for any damage done to the surface of the land, buildings, work or improvements or to livestock crops or trees in the area of such mineral operations.

(2) An application for compensation under subsection (1) of this section shall be copied to the Secretary and the Land Valuation Board.

(3) The amount of compensation payable under subsection (1) of this section shall, subject to the approval of the Land Valuation Board, be determined by agreement between the parties concerned and if the parties are unable to reach an agreement as to the amount of compensation, the matter shall be referred to the Secretary who shall in consultation with the Land Valuation Board determine the compensation payable.

In my opinion the whole action instituted by the plaintiff against the defendants was incompetent and so were the subsequent proceedings including the judgment being appealed against. The reason is too obvious to admit of any question. This is a claim governed by laid down statutory procedure which a claimant ought to follow but which apparently out of ignorance of the law the plaintiff failed to follow. The pity of it all is that the learned trial judge also erroneously thinking that the plaintiff could exercise the normal common law rights over his property fell into serious and abysmal misdirection of the law. In the result I allow the appeal and set aside the judgment and costs on the ground that the proceedings were a nullity. The appeal is allowed.

P. K. TWUMASI
