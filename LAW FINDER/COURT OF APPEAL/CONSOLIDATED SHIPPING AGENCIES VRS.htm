JUDGMENT

ADUAMA OSEI J.A This is an appeal against a judgment of the High Court. Tema, dated the 23rd of February, 2007, in which the High Court held that having agreed to carry the Plaintiff/Respondent's goods from the Port at Tema to the Tema Free Zones enclave for reward, the Defendant/Appellant was under an obligation to carry the goods to its destination "safely, carefully and securely" and that, having failed to do so, the Defendant/Appellant was liable to pay to the Plaintiff/Respondent as special damages the sum of US$24,937.75 or its cedi equivalent, being the repair cost of the Plaintiff/Respondent's goods damaged in the course of carriage. The action that has given rise to this appeal was commenced by a Writ of Summons issued on 30th November, 2005, and indorsed for the following reliefs: "(i) A declaration that the defendant being a common carrier is liable to compensate plaintiff for any loss or damage suffered by plaintiff as a result of defendant's failure to transport the goods entrusted to it by plaintiff to their destination carefully, safely and securely. "(ii) A declaration that having failed to transport the goods entrusted to it by plaintiff to their destination carefully, safely and securely plaintiff is entitled to claim compensation from defendant all losses and damages suffered by plaintiff as a result of defendant's aforesaid failure on a full indemnity basis. (iii) Recovery of the sum of $24,937.75 or its cedi equivalent being the financial loss incurred by plaintiff as a result of the damage caused to the goods entrusted to defendant by plaintiff for purposes of careful, safe and secure transportation to their destination. "(iv) Interest oil aforesaid sum of $24,937.75 from 27/05/05 being the date on which Harlequin international (Gh) Ltd debited plaintiff's account with the aforesaid amount. "(v) Costs on a full indemnity basis." In the Statement of Claim accompanying the Writ of Summons, the Plaintiff/Respondent described the Defendant/Appellant as a common carrier and alleged that on or about the 16th of May, 2005, in the performance of its business as a freight forwarder and acting on behalf of a company called Bredoro Shaw Pipe Coaters Limited, it contracted the Defendant/Appellant to transport various goods, including conveyor rails, belts and other heavy duty accessories from the Port at Tema to the Free Zones enclave also at Tema for reward. The Plaintiff/Respondent contended in the Statement of Claim that, beyond its contractual obligation, the Defendant/Appellant also owed a legal obligation to convey the goods to their destination "carefully, safely and securely". It was the case of the Plaintiff/Respondent, however, that the Defendant/Appellant failed to meet its said obligation, with the result that some of the goods fell off the Defendant/Appellant's vehicle in the course of carriage and got damaged. According to the Plaintiff/Respondent, it incurred a cost of $24,937.75 in order to have the damaged goods repaired. The Plaintiff/Respondent contended that the Defendant/Appellant was liable to compensate it for the loss and damage it had suffered. The Plaintiff/Respondent stated that it had sued claiming the reliefs indorsed on the Writ of Summons because the Defendant/Appellant had denied liability and had refused to pay compensation fur the damage suffered in spite of demands made. The Defendant/Appellant however considered that the Plaintiff/Respondent was not entitled to any of the reliefs claimed in the Suit. In its Statement of Defence filed on 21st December, ,2005, the Defendant/Appellant denied that it was a common carrier, and contended that it was a private carrier and that it agreed to carry the Plaintiff/Respondent's goods on specific terms. According to the Defendant/Appellant, one of the key terms of the contract was that the Plaintiff/Respondent would be responsible for the loading of the flat rack on which the goods were packed onto the Defendant/Appellant's trucks. According to the Defendant/Appellant, it played no role in the loading of the container onto the trucks and that the damage to the goods during their carriage was neither caused by its negligence nor the negligence of any of its employees. The Defendant/Appellant contended in the Statement of Defence that the damage was caused because the goods were either negligently loaded onto the container, or sufficient care was not exercised in their loading. The responsibility for the loading was however the Plaintiff's and the Defendant therefore bore no responsibility for any damage to the goods.In its application for directions filed on 24th January, 2006, the Plaintiff/Respondent raised five issues for determination, and the Defendant/Appellant also filed five additional issues. Both the issues raised by the Plaintiff/Respondent and those filed by the Defendant/Appellant were set down as issues for trial by the trial Court. and the ten issues are set out below: i) Whether or not the defendant owes plaintiff a duty to transport its goods to their destination safely and securely. ii) Whether or not defendant's obligation to carry plaintiff's goods to their destination safely and securely is extinguished or diminished by its alleged non paritcipation in the process of loading the goods. iii) Whether or not the defendant breached its duty to plaintiff when it failed to carry plaintiff's goods to their destination safely and securely. iv) Whether or not the defendant is liable to pay to the plaintiff damages for breach of its duty to plaintiff to carry plaintiff's goods to their destination safely and securely. v) Whether or not the damage to plaintiff's goods resulted from defendant's negligence. vi) Whether the defendant is a common carrier or a private carrier. vii) Whether or not the goods were already packed onto the flat rack container by the plaintiff before the loading of the goods onto the defendant's trucks was done. viii) Whether or not the plaintiff failed to properly pack the goods and secure same on the fiat rack container. ix) Whether the goods skidded off the flat rack container as a result of its negligent packing or the container skidded off the defendant's trucks. x) Whether or not the damage to the goods was caused by the insufficient and negligent packing of same on the flat rack container by the plaintiff. The trial Court considered that the issues numbered (I), (ii), (iii), (vi), (viii), (ix) and (x) above, together raised the important questions of the duty of care owed by the Defendant/Appellant to the Plaintiff/Respondent, and the manner in which the loading or packing of the goods was done. The trial Court therefore dealt with the said issues together. After considering the issues in the light of the evidence, the trial Court came to the conclusion that the Defendant/Appellant was liable for the damage caused to the goods and entered judgment for the Plaintiff/Respondent on all the reliefs claimed, except relief (iv), by which the Plaintiff/Respondent claimed interest on the sum of $24,937.75 which the Plaintiff/Respondent alleged was the cost at which the damaged goods were repaired. Not satisfied with the judgment of the trial Court, the Defendant/Appellant has appealed against the same to this Court on two grounds, which are that: a) The learned judge erred in coming to a conclusion that the DefendantlAppellant is liable to Plaintiff for damage to Plaintiff's goods, having regard to the evidence adduced, and b) The learned judge erred in not considering the fact that the Defendant was a private carrier which was a fundamental issue in this case. Not satisfied with the trial Court's refusal to award it interest on the sum of $24,937.75, being the cost of repairing the damaged goods, the Respondent has also filed a cross-appeal asking this Court to reverse the trial Court's decision refusing to award it interest. The sole ground of the cross-appeal is that 'Shaving regard to all the circumstances of the case the trial judge ought not have refused the claim for interest on the sum of $24,937.75 or its cedi equivalent from 27/05/05 when plaintiff incurred the financial loss as a result of defendant's negligence to the date of judgment." The judgment appealed against is at pages 123 to 132 of the Appeal Record and the Notice of Appeal is at pages 138 and 139 of the Record. The Plaintiff/Respondent's cross-appeal can also be found at pages 140 and 141 of the Record. In this judgment, the Plaintiff/Respondent is referred to as "the Respondent" and the Defendant/Appellant is referred to as "the Appellant". Under ground (a) of this appeal, the Appellant contends that, having regard to the evidence adduced, the trial Court erred in concluding that the Appellant is liable to the Respondent for the damage to the Respondent's goods. Arguing in Support of this ground of appeal, Counsel for the Appellant contends that the trial Court held the Appellant liable because it failed to adequately consider the evidence before it on the issue of negligence. According to Counsel, the evidence led by the Appellant showed clearly that the Appellant was not negligent hut that the goods skidded off the truck as a result of poor packing, which was a specialised function undertaken by Safebond, and was not the responsibility of the Appellant. Counsel argues that while the Respondent failed to lead any evidence to show that the accident was caused by the Appellant's negligence, there was sufficient evidence from the Appellant to show that the Appellant was not negligent. In support of his contention that the Appellant led sufficient evidence to show that it was not negligent, Counsel refers to the testimonies of the Appellant's representative and the driver of the vehicle from which the damaged goods fell. Counsel again contends that the evidence of the Respondent's witness confirms that the Appellant's driver drove the vehicle with due care and attention, and that the accident was not caused by the negligence of the driver. Counsel says further that the evidence of the Respondent's witnesses confirms the position that Safebond was exclusively responsible for loading goods at the Port of Tema, and that the loading in the present case was done exclusively by Safebond. According to Counsel for the Appellant, the evidence is overwhelming that the Appellant's driver drove the vehicle with due care and attention and had taken due care to secure the Respondent's goods and that the conclusion by the trial Court that the Appellant was negligent was misplaced and not supported by the evidence on record. From my reading of the judgment of the trial Court I discern the following as the reasoning behind the trial Court's conclusion that the Appellant was liable for the damage to the Respondent's goods: i) that there was a contract of carriage of goods between the Appellant and the Respondent in which the Appellant was the carrier; ii) that upon acceptance of the goods for carriage, the Appellant became a bailee of the goods; iii) that as bailee, the Appellant was liable to account for the goods entrusted to it; iv) that while the goods were in the possession of the Appellant and were being transported, they skidded off the Appellant's truck and some of them got damaged; v) that in the absence of a special term in the carriage agreement exempting the Appellant from liability, the Appellant must he held liable for the damage; vi) that there was no evidence of any term exempting the Appellant from liability; and, therefore; vii) that the Appellant was liable for the damage. There is no dispute in this case that there was a contract for the carriage of goods between the Appellant and the Respondent in which the Appellant was the carrier, and that in pursuance of the contract, the goods were entrusted to the Appellant. It is also not in dispute that in the course of carriage, the goods skidded off the Appellant's truck, with some of them getting damaged. The Appellant insists that it carried the goods as a private carrier and not as a common carrier. And yet its representative at the trial conceded that in the transaction in issue in this Suit, it assumed an obligation to transport the goods safely and securely, which is normally an obligation of a common carrier. But even if we grant that the Appellant was a private carrier, it is trite learning that a private carrier is a bailee of the goods entrusted to him and he owes a duty to take reasonable care of the goods. Further, the fact of damage or non-delivery is prima facie evidence of negligence on the part of the private carrier. The Appellant contends that it was not negligent and that the goods got damaged because they skidded off the truck and further, that they skidded off the truck because they were not properly loaded onto the truck by the Respondent's agent. Yet, contrary to this contention, it is on record that the Appellant's driver confirmed during cross-examination that before taking off with the goods, he found them to be properly loaded. Now, with the Appellant's position regarding the loading of the goods contradicted by its own driver and the Appellant's representative stating that in the transaction in issue the Appellant assumed an obligation to transport the goods safely and securely, what evidence is there that rebuts the prima facie evidence of negligence on the part of the Appellant? The Appellant's representative testified that there were bumps in that area of the road where the goods skidded off the truck and that the skidding occurred while the driver was negotiating a curve. The representative says that so long as the road was straight, there was not much of a problem manoeuvering the vehicle. It seems, from the representative's evidence, that as far as the Appellant is concerned, what made the difference and caused problems were the curve that the driver was negotiating, the bumps in the particular section of the road where the accident occurred, and the alleged improper loading of the goods onto the truck. But with the contention that the goods were not properly loaded contradicted by the Appellant's driver, who was present during the loading, what else could explain the skidding off of the goods? Could it be that the driver, who had an obligation to transport the goods safely and securely, did not exercise the care and attention that the conditions at that particular section of the road demanded? in the course of negotiating the curve and in the face of the humps in the road, was the driver overwhelmed? To my mind, the driver's testimony that the goods were properly loaded seriously weakened the Appellant's explanation as to why the goods got damaged. It seems to me that there is sufficient evidence on record to justify the finding by the trial Court that the Appellant was liable for the damage. The principle is established that the findings of fact made by a trial Court are presumed to be right. An appellant who contends that the judgment is against the weight of evidence therefore carries the burden of displacing this presumption and he does so by showing from the record that, indeed, the judgment is not supported by the evidence. I have considered the arguments advanced by Counsel for the Appellant under ground (a) in the light of the evidence on record and I am not satisfied that Counsel has been able to displace the presumption that the trial Court was right in its finding. In my view, this appeal cannot succeed under ground (a) and 1 reject the same. Under ground (b), the Appellant says that the trial Court failed to take account of the fact that the Appellant was a private carrier. The Appellant says the issue whether or not it was a private carrier was a fundamental issue in the case and that the trial Court, therefore, committed an error in failing to consider that issue. In arguing this ground, Counsel for the Appellant laments the failure of the trial Court to show appreciation of the liabilities associated with private carriers, as opposed to common carriers. Counsel says this failure led to the trial Court arriving at the wrong conclusion iii the case and occasioned a substantial miscarriage of justice to the Appellant. According to Counsel, the authorities show that the liability of a private carrier extends only to loss or damage caused by his own negligence or the negligence of his servants. He however acknowledges that the burden of disproving that he or his servants were not negligent is on the private carrier. Turning attention to the evidence, Counsel for the Appellant contends that there was unchallenged evidence that the Appellant was a private carrier and, therefore, that it was only for damage caused by the negligence of the Appellant or its servants that the Appellant could be held liable. Counsel contends that, from the evidence on record, the accident was not caused by the negligence of the Appellant. Counsel says while the Appellant had led evidence to show that it was not negligent, the Respondent was unable to demonstrate by evidence that the accident was caused by the Appellant or that the Appellant was a common carrier and was therefore strictly liable for all loss or damage that occurs in the course of transporting goods. Counsel contends that in the present case, neither the Appellant nor any of its servants was negligent and that if the goods had not skidded off the container, the damage complained about in this case would not have occurred. Counsel submits that no negligence on the part of the Appellant was established and therefore that the trial Court erred in holding that the Appellant was liable for the damage to the goods From his submissions in response, Counsel for the Respondent seems to consider that having regard to the facts of the present case, whether the Appellant is considered a private carrier or a common carrier is not relevant. In the view of Counsel for the Respondent, unless there is an express agreement exempting it from liability, the carrier, whether private or common, is liable for damage resulting from its negligence and that the burdern of proving that it was not negligent is on the carrier. Counsel for the Respondent considers that the trial Court's finding that the Appellant is liable is supported by the evidence and that this Court should not interfere with the same. Carriers are persons who transport other persons or goods, either for reward or gratuitously, from one place to another. In accordance with the nature of their trade or business and the circumstances under which they operate, carriers are classified, mainly, as common carriers and private carriers. The rights and liabilities of common carriers are determined by the common law, subject to statutory qualification, or by special contract. In the absence of statutory qualification or special contract, the common carrier is considered willing to carry any goods or persons for payment on the route he covers. The common law imposes on him the duty to accept any goods of the type he carries or any persons so long as he has space, and he also has a duty at common law to charge by a reasonable rate. Again, at common law, the common carrier is strictly liable for all loss or damage to goods in the course of transportation. The private carrier, on the other hand, is not available to the public for employment in the same manner as the common carrier is. Though he may invite the public to employ him, he reserves to himself the right to reject or accept the offers of goods for carriage, and in deciding whether or not to accept offers of goods, the private carrier in not influenced by availability of space. Largely, in any particular case, he is influenced by the attractiveness or otherwise of the offer made to him. Unlike the common carrier, the private carrier is not strictly liable for loss or damage to the goods he carries. The private carrier is a bailee of the goods and his obligation at common law is to take reasonable care and refrain from dealing with the goods in a manner inconsistent with the bailor's rights in the same. The obligations of the private carrier of goods are set out in Haisbury's Laws of England, Fourth Edition, 2004 Reissue, Vol 5(1), par. 507 at page 435 in the following words: "In the absence of any stricter contractual undertaking, a private carrier is liable only where damage, loss or delay results either from negligence or from intentional acts inconsistent with the rights of the consignor or other bailor in the goods, on the part of the carrier or those for whom he is responsible. However, the fact of loss, damage or non-delivery is prima facie evidence of negligence on the part of the carrier or his employees or delegates, and the onus of proving that the misadventure occurred without negligence on his part is on the private carrier as bailee." According to Halsbury (supra) in agreeing to carry goods, many private carriers inisist on conditions that limit their liability for loss, damage or delay, and it depends on the facts of each particular case whether such conditions have been incorporated as terms of a particular contract of carriage. As has been noted above in respect of the common carrier of goods, his rights and liabilities at common law may be subject to special contract, and it does appear that the tendency in these modern times is for carriage of goods to be regulated by contract. Haisbury observes, therefore, that it is rare these days to have carriers of goods who operate purely as common carriers but fail to limit their common law liabilities by special contract. Indeed, Haisbury (supra, para. 502 at page 431) considers that "the status of a common carrier, who is under an obligation to carry unless he can show reasonable excuse, and (if a carrier of goods) is an insurer of the safety of the goods carried, has become something of an anachronism".if' this is considered in relation to the tendency of the private carrier to insist on conditions that limit his liability for loss, damage or delay, then it becomes clear that in determining the rights and obligations of the carrier of goods in these modern times, it is more helpful to look at the terms actually agreed between the parties or the responsibilities actually assumed by them than to compartmentalise carriers as common or private and infer their rights or obligations from the tags given them. Now, under ground (b) of this appeal. the Appellant is complaining that the trial Court failed to consider that it is a private carrier and it is also contending that the issue whether or not it is a private carrier was fundamental in the case. It seems to me, from what has been observed in the immediately preceding paragraph, however, that the fundamental issue in the case is not so much the class into which the Appellant generally places itself as a carrier; the fundamental issue, rather, is the obligations the parties agreed to assume in the particular carriage transaction which is the subject matter of this case. In paragraph 3 of its Statement of Defence, the Appellant contended that it is a private carrier and averred that in the transaction in issue, it agreed with the Respondent on specific terms for the carriage of the goods. The Appellant proceeded in paragraph 4 of the Statement of Defence to give as one of the key terms agreed the assumption by the Respondent of responsibility for the loading of the goods on the flat rack container. Then at the trial, during the cross-examination of its representative, the Appellant also acknowledged its responsibility for the safety and security of the goods during transportation. The question was put to the representative, "so what it means is that during the transportation you were solely responsible for the safety and security of the goods", and his response was, "Yes my lord we were." In my view, in the face of acknowledgement that it was responsible for the safety and security of the goods during transportation, the Appellant cannot hide behind the tag of a private carrier and insist that, in the transaction in issue, its responsibility was limited to taking reasonable care of the goods. If in this transaction, the Appellant undertook to ensure the safety and security of the goods during transportation, then in my view, it is bound to meet that responsibility and if the goods get damaged during transportation, it is obliged to satisfactorily explain the damage. Counsel for the Appellant has contended in his Written Submission that the trial Court failed to show appreciation of the liabilities associated with private carriers. But even if we grant that the Appellant is a private carrier who is liable only where damage, loss or delay results from its negligence, as stated in Halsburp, that liability prevails only in the absence of "any stricter contractual undertaking". In the present case, the Appellant, on its own admission, undertook the stricter obligation of ensuring the safety and security of the goods during transportation. Further, where goods in the hands of the private carrier are damaged, the damage is considered as prima facie evidence of negligence on the part of the carrier or its employee or delegate, and the carrier bears the burden of proving that the damage occurred without its negligence or the negligence of its employees or delegates. And in the present case, how has the Appellant demonstrated that neither itself nor any of its employees was negligent? The explanation given by the Appellant regarding the damage was that it occurred because the goods skidded off the flat rack container; that the goods skidded off the flat rack container because they were not properly loaded; that the loading was a specialised function of an agent of the Respondent with whom it had no contractual relationship; and, there fore that it was not liable for the damage caused to the goods. As noted above, however, the driver of the Appellant, who was present during the loading and who claims to have inspected the goods after loading, confirmed that he found the goods to be properly loaded, if the case of the Appellant is that the goods skidded off because they were not properly loaded and it turns out in the evidence that, after all, the goods were properly loaded, then to what else does the Appellant attribute the damage to the goods? in my view, the testimony of the driver seriously undermined the explanation which the Appellant sought to offer and rendered the explanation unacceptable. I agree with Counsel for the Respondent that the trial Court's finding that the Appellant was liable for the damage is supported by the evidence on record. This Court v ill therefore not disturb the finding of the trial Court and this ground of appeal is also rejected. The rejection of this ground means that this appeal has failed. The outstanding matter for consideration now is the Respondent's cross- appeal, which contends that since the Respondent incurred financial loss as a result of the Appellant's negligence, the trial Court ought not have refused the Respondent's claim for interest on the sum of $24,937.75, which was the cost at which the Respondent repaired the damaged goods. In the view of Counsel for the Respondent, the rationale for awarding interest is to compensate the claimant for interest for the commercial loss of his money. He contends that having found in the present case that the Respondent paid the sum of $24,937.75 on 22nd November, 2005 in respect of the repair of the damaged goods, the trial Court ought to have awarded interest to the Respondent on the said sum from that date to compensate the Respondent for the commercial loss of the use of its money. Counsel notes that the award of interest by the Courts may be discretionary. He contends, however, that a discretion must be exercised fairly and reasonably; In his view, the trial Court did not deal fairly with M. e Respondent in the present case in refusing to award it interest. Counsel for the Appellant does not however consider that the Respondent is entitled to interest Counsel for the Appellant contends that interest can be awarded only on the basis of agreement between the parties or under a statutory provision He says that in the present case there is no agreement between the parties for the award of interest, and neither is there any statutory provision under which, in the present circumstance, the Respondent could claim interest. He therefore urges this Court to dismiss the cross-appeal. Interest may be awarded at common law on the basis of an express agreement for payment of interest, or in circumstances where an agreement to pay interest can be inferred from the course of dealing between the parties or from the nature of the trade or a custom or other relationship existing between the parties. Further, interest may be awarded at common law by way of damages for breach of contract, other than a contract merely to pay money, where the contract, if performed, would, to the knowledge of the parties, have entitled the claimant to receive interest. Interest may also be awarded against a defendant in equity where the defendant ought to have done something which would have entitled the plaintiff to interest at common law, or where by the wrongful conduct of the defendant, the plaintiff has been prevented from doing something that would have entitled him to interest, or where, as underscored by the Supreme Court in the case of Royal Dutch Airlines (KLM) & Another Vs. Farmex Limited [1989-1990] 2 GLR 623, the plaintiff has been deprived of the prospect of making more money from money due him from the defendant. In the Farmex case (supra), the High Court awarded in favour of the Plaintiff the sum of £23,800.00 as special damages for the loss of mangoes which were to be carried, between the defendant airlines, from Accra to London. The High Court also awarded interest on the said sum in favour of the Plaintiff, but on appeal to this Court, this Court by a majority of two to one set aside the order awarding interest on the ground that the Plaintiff had been indemnified in full and that there was no justification for awarding interest on the sum awarded as damages. On further appeal to the Supreme Court. however, the Supreme Court considered that this Court was wrong in setting aside the award of interest. In apparent response to this Court's statement that there was no justification for awarding interest, the Supreme Court affirmed per A dude JSC at page 635 of the Report that "(t)he prospect of making more money with the £23,800 or otherwise benefitting from it, is the justification for the award of interest". Where, therefore, a party has suffered commercial loss of the use of his money as a result of the wrongful conduct of the other party, there may be ground for the award of interest. For the purposes of this cross-appeal, however. I consider it necessary that we note three things about the award of interest. These are that, as conceded by Counsel for the Respondent, the award of interest may lie in the discretion of the Court, that generally, for interest to be awarded, it ought to he claimed, and that the award of interest may, in appropriate cases, be by way of damages. In the present case, the Respondent claimed interest but did not claim general damages. The trial Court however refused to award interest to the Respondent but rather awarded it general damages in the sum of ¢10,000,000.00. Was the award of ¢10,000,000.00 as general damages a discretionary award intended to compensate the Respondent for the Appellant's delay in settling the cost of repairing the damaged goods? In other words, was the trial Court awarding interest to the Respondent by way of general damages? And if it was, could such an award be properly made? I was looking for answers to these questions in the Written Submissions of Counsel, particularly, Counsel for the Respondent. Counsel for the Respondent however proceeded with the cross-appeal as if the general damages awarded bore no relationship whatever to the interest claimed. As has been noted above, the authorities suggest that in appropriate cases, interest may be awarded by way of damages. But will an award by way of general damages be appropriate? In the English case of Wadsworth Vs. Lydall [1981]2 All E.R 401, among the claims made by the Plaintiff was one for special damages in respect of interest which he had to pay as a result of the delay in the payment of a debt owed him by the Defendant. The Queen's Bench Division disallowed the claim for special damages in respect of interest and on appeal by the Plaintiff to the Court of Appeal. the Defendant contended that the Plaintiff was precluded from pursuing the claim for special damages by the decision of the House of Lords in the case of London, chatham and Dover Railway Company Vs. South Easter,, Railway Company [1893] AC 429 that, at common law, in the absence of any agreement or statutory provisions for the payment of interest, a court had no power to award interest by way of damages. The English Court of Appeal however distinguished the case from the London, Chatham case, and held that the London, chatham case was concerned only with a claim for interest by way of general damages. The Court of Appeal proceeded further in the following words: "If a plaintiff pleads and can prove that he has suffered special damage as a result of the defendant's failure to perform his obligation under a contract, and such damage is not too remote on the principle of Hadley Vs. Baxendale ..........., I can see no logical reason why such special damages should be irrecoverable merely because the obligation on which the defendant defaulted was an obligation to pay money and not some other type of obligation". The Wadsworth case seems to me to have established in England that while in an appropriate case interest may be awarded by way of special damages, general damages will not normally be awarded as compensation for commercial loss of the use of money. Now, in the present case, if the general damages of ¢10,000,000.00 was intended to be a substitute for the interest claimed by the Respondent, then it would suggest that the trial Court considered that the Respondent was entitled to be compensated for being deprived of the opportunity of making more money from money due him from the Appellant. And if the Respondent was entitled to be SO compensated, was it a fair exercise of discretion to impose ¢10,000,000.00 on the Respondent without any explanation? I do not find from the record any facts or circumstances that justify the award of ¢10,000,000.00 in place of the claim for interest indorsed on the Respondent's Writ of Summons, and drawing on the situation in England, I consider the award of general damages in the circumstances of the present case as inappropriate. In my view, therefore, the award of general damages made by the trial Court ought to be set aside. Further, guided by the principle in the Farmex case, I form the view that the Respondent is entitled to interest on the sum of $24,937.75 paid by it in respect of the repair of the damaged goods. In conclusion, the appeal fails while the cross-appeal succeeds. The general damages in the sum of ¢10,000,000.00 awarded by the trial Court to the Respondent is hereby set aside and judgment is hereby entered in favour of the Respondent for relief (iv) indorsed on its Writ of Summons. The interest shall be calculated at the commercial dollar rate from 22nd November, 2005 to date of judgment. COUNSEL: MR.
