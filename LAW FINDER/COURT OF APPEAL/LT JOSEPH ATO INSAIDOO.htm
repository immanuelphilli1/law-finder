JUDGMENT

QUAYE, JA:-

The appellant herein was convicted by the General Court Martial held at the (MATS) Accra, on 5th June 2007 on charges of (1) Conduct to the prejudice of good order and discipline for improper possession of notes containing Directing Staff Solutions to 2005 Practical Promotion Examination Level A examination question. Information which had not been issued to examination candidates, contrary to Section 54(1) of the Armed Forces Act 1962 (Act 105) and (2) Conduct to the prejudice of good order and discipline for cheating contrary to Section 54(1) of the Armed Forces Act 1962 (Act 105). The particulars of Count 2 are that the appellant cheated during the 2005 Level Alpha Practical Promotion examination by dishonestly making use of yet to be issued examination question and Directing Staff Solutions before they were officially released. Upon his conviction on both charges, the appellant was sentenced to dismissal from the Ghana Armed Forces. This appeal is therefore in respect of the said conviction and sentence. The appeal is founded on two grounds ie. a. the conviction is against the weight of evidence and b. the trial court failed to find that the appellant used directing staff solutions to write notes in his note pad. According to learned counsel for the appellant his client, together with other officers were candidates who were involved in Practical Promotion Examination for Army Officers Level Alpha 2005. In the course of the examinations, it was detected that some of the candidates, including the appellant, had notes in their note-pads which were said to be similar to the Directing Staff Solutions. The appellant was consequently not made to write the practical examinations on the assumption that he had prior knowledge of yet-to-be-issued Requirement 3. The appellant denied any such prior knowledge and insisted that it was as a result of discussions he had with other candidates as well as the trend of the questions which enabled him to put down the notes in his note-pad. Counsel for the appellant contended that the law enjoined the prosecution to adduce cogent evidence to prove the alleged facts. He referred to the Court of Appeal decision in ASARE vs THE REPUBLIC [1978] GLR 193 with approval in support of his contention. The relevant part of the holding was that: "...as a general rule there is no burden on the accused; that he is presumed innocent until his guilt is established beyond reasonable doubt; that the burden is rather on the prosecution to prove the charge against him beyond reasonable doubt i.e the jury must be satisfied upon the evidence so that they feel sure of the guilt of the accused and that if they are left in doubt whether the accused is guilty or not guilty, then the prosecution have failed to prove the case against him and he is entitled as of right to a verdict of not guilty." To strengthen his argument further, learned counsel for appellant cited section 11(2) of the Evidence Decree [1975] (NRCD 323) which provides that: "In criminal action, the burden of producing evidence, when it is on the prosecution as to any fact which is essential to guilt, requires the prosecution to produce sufficient evidence so hat on all the evidence, a reasonable mind could find the existence of the fact beyond a reasonable doubt". Further references were made by learned counsel seeking to drive home his argument more forcefully. I do not need to go into all of that because they really do not add anything new to what is required of the prosecution that they must prove the guilt of the accused beyond reasonable doubt. After the above references to the standard of proof, learned counsel for the appellant repeated the two charges against the appellant and submitted that the prosecution were not able to prove that there was a Directing Staff Solution which constitute the foundation of the offences for which the appellant was charged adding that this failure amounts to a fatal collapse of the charges preferred against the accused person. For the next stage of counsel's argument he carefully examined the evidence led by the prosecution through Pw1 and submitted that from the evidence of Pw1 it is clear that the charge of conduct to the prejudice of good order and discipline does not even have a leg to stand on because the Pw1 had said there was nothing like Directing Staff Solution but rather a guide to assist the Directing Staff. He further submitted that the notes/point that were found in the note pad of the appellant were not Directing Staff Solutions and did not constitute the solution requirement as alleged. This is because there was no Directing Staff Solution, which was alleged to have leaked. To conclude his submissions counsel for the appellant cited the case of LUTTEROOT vs COMMISSIONER OF POLICE [1963]2GLR 429 SC which decided that where a decision of a trial court turns upon the oath of a prosecution witness against that of a defence witness, it is incumbent on the trial court to examine the evidence of the said witnesses carefully along with other evidence adduced at the trial before preferring one to the other. If the court prefers the evidence of the prosecution then it must give reasons for the preference, but if it is unable to give any reasons for the preference, then that means that there is a reasonable doubt as to which of the versions of the story is true in which case, the benefit of the doubt must be given to the defence. In response to the above arguments and submissions of counsel for the appellant the learned counsel for the respondent argued that it is misleading on the part of the appellants to claim that there was no Directing Staff Solution as there was ample evidence on the record to the contrary. He reiterated that the terms "Directing Staff Solution" and "Marking Guide" are used interchangeably, which, in military terminology, refers to what is known in civil language as Marking Scheme and referred to the part of the evidence of Pw1 which sought to emphasize his ground of disagreement with the appellant. What the Pw1 said was that "In practical promotion examinations and for that matter in tactics there are no fixed or permanent solutions to tactical requirement.. .In this case where it is practical, a Guide is given to the Directing Staff to follow in assessing the candidate. In such a case, we call it a Directing Staff Guide, but in essence, it is a solution sort of... Even the suggested Directing Staff Solution is also a Guide... To conclude I will see the Directing Staff Guide and the suggested Directing Staff Solution as virtually solutions to the Requirement". In the view of learned counsel for the respondent Exhibit E was a Directing Staff Solution or Directing Staff Marking Guide and submitted that in the absence of a Directing Staff Solution there would be no basis for assessing the examination candidates. In this judgment, it is my respectful view that the existence or otherwise of a Directing Staff Solution is a matter of fact, not fiction, and this issue can be resolved having regard to the evidence led at the trial. In his evidence in chief, Pw1 was handed a document which he described as containing the narratives, the requirement, the guide to the Directing Staff Solution and some solutions. That document was admitted into evidence without any objection from the appellant, as Exhibit E. After Exhibit E had been tendered, Pw1 again described it as the Requirement and the Guide to the Directing Staff at page 9 of which the questions were stated. This piece of evidence identifying Exhibit E with, among other things, the Directing Staff Solution was confirmed by Pw2 who said that exhibit contained the Requirements i.e. questions, and solutions and instructions to Practical Promotion Exams. Lt Capt 2005. Apart from the evidence of Pw1 and Pw2, the document in question, Exhibit E speaks volumes about itself. I am satisfied that the contention by the appellant that there was not Directing Staff. Solution and therefore the prosecution had not produced. sufficient or cogent enough evidence to meet the required standard of proof fails and it is therefore not upheld in this judgment. The next issue to determine relates to whether or not there was proof beyond reasonable doubt that the appellant conducted himself in a manner. prejudicial to good order and discipline in the Ghana Armed Forces, in accordance to section 54(1) of Act 105. The evidence of both prosecution witnesses, Pw1 and Pw2 showed that when the members of Syndicate 2 which included the appellant, were called in, to consider Requirement 3, the appellant and four other candidates did not turn up until about thirty minutes after the exams had started. When they eventually arrived, Pw1 decided to search them. The search disclosed that the appellant had prepared answers or solutions in his note pad Exhibit F3 on some of the questions in the Requirement, even though at the time of the search, the said examination question paper had not been issued out to him. The prepared answers or solutions were on Deployment, surveillance, Fire Plan and Counter-attack plan and also a mission for left forward platoon. According to Pw1 the appellant was not required to make any entries into the note pad until after the exam question papers had been issued. It is really quite inexplicable that that after Pw1's incisive testimony, counsel for the appellant hardly challenged any statement that Pw1 had made concerning or implicating the appellant. Further to the evidence of Pw1, Pw2 tendered into evidence as Exhibit M the statement he had obtained from the appellant on the issue. In the said Exhibit M, the appellant stated that: "... .I recorded all the discussions in my field note book before the Requirement Three was issued. After being issued with the Requirement Three Lt-Col. Vib Sanziri looked through my note pad and saw that the notes in them corresponded with the answers to be given to Requirement Three.:.." Exhibit M was made by the appellant of 13th May-2005. On the very next day 14th May 2005 the appellant wrote a second or further statement Exhibit N in which he stated that "prior to leaving for the exam, I had a copy of the questions from Lt Debrah. I however destroyed the copy on Tuesday before leaving for the exercise area. Learned counsel for the appellant in his submission before us cited the case of LUTTERODT vs C.O.P [1963] 2GLR 429 SC where the Supreme Court sought to guide all lower courts on the principles regarding oath of the prosecution witness(es) against that of the defence. My understanding of that principle is that it could be applied where there was absence of contradictions in either the prosecution or the defence. Thus in AIDOO vs THE STATE [1963] 2GLR 84 the same Court held that "(3) where a witness gives two different versic5ns of the same incident, the determination of which of the versions is true is a question of fact for the jury. A direction by the judge to accept one version in preference to the other would be usurping the function of the jury; if the cautioned statement and evidence given by the appellant at the trial had been exactly the same, there would have been substances in the submission that the judge should have directed the jury to accept the appellant's evidence on the incident". In view of the inconsistency of the appellant in this case, I am of the view that the decision in LUTORODT's case would not avail him. Exhibits M and. N contradict the appellant's evidence before the court. Furthermore the appellant made a very feeble, lame and unsuccessful attempt to back track from his admission of his criminal conduct. In his own evidence in defence of the charge the appellant confessed to the discussion he had had with one Lt Debrah leading to the question paper that the appellant had received from the said Lt Debrah which according to him, he destroyed before going to the examination area Led by his counsel, appellant told the court that he destroyed the exam paper that Lt Debrah gave to him becaáse "...I knew I was not supposed to send them to the examinations area, So I destroyed all the papers". This evidence in chief was given on 1st June 2006. On 8th June 2006 however, and rather inexplicably, the defence was able to conjure into life the very examination question papers that Lt Debrah had allegedly given to the appellant. Counsel asked and the appellant replied as follows:- Q. The last time you were here you told the court that you had past papers of 2004 Practical Promotion Exam you received from Lt Debarh do you have the papers here? A. Yes my Lord. I want to tender it as evidence? This latter twist from his previous evidence on oath which is also a departure from his previous statement exhibits M and N take the story of the appellant away from the ambit and scope of the decision in LUTTERODT vs COP and with respect, enters the scope of events better envisaged by the decision in AIDOO vs THE STATE (supra). On the whole I am satisfied that the prosecution 1n this case succeeded in proving the case against the appellant beyond reasonable doubt that he, the appellant conducted himself In a way that was prejudicial to good order and discipline within the Ghana Armed Forces. The last ground I find relevant to consider relates to the charge of cheating. I have had occasion to state elsewhere that neither the Criminal Offences Act of Ghana 1960 (Act 29) nor the Armed Forces Act 1962 (Act 105). recognized cheating as a criminal offence. In the face of the further fact that section 8 specifically states that a person is not liable to punishment by the common law for any act, I am fortified to submit that the charge contained in count two does not apply, and that the conviction thereon was bad for the following further reason. The Ghana Armed Forces as an institution did not produce any legislation or regulation that made cheating an offence punishable as a crime. Beside the above, the evidence led in the trial was very scanty and not direct. Where cases had been tried based on cheating, the actual act constituting the offence was stated in 'evidence. Contrariwise in this case, Pw1 specifically said on oath under cross examination by learned counsel for the appellant that the appellant did not cheat. Hear him Q. Would it be correct to say that it was because of your diligence that the candidates did not make use of the notes in their note books. A. That is correct, if I had allowed them it would have been a cheat. Q. In accordance with your duties you ensured that they did not cheat. A. Yes. Clearly upon the evidence before us, the appellant was not given the chance to consummate any intention or attempt to cheat because he was prevented torn taking the examination. In contract to the evidence before us, we may it random take a look at. .BOEHM vs UNIVERSITY OF PENNSYLVANIA SCHOOL OF VETERINARY MEDICINE 392 Pa Super 502 (1990) 573 A2d 575 where two students were found guilty of violating the Code of Student Rights and Academic Integrity by engaging in "behaviour suspicious of and compatible with cheating". It was stated that what constitutes cheating in a test situation includes from the strict definition that even a brief but focussed glance at a fellow student's exam paper is, of itself, a clear act of cheating, and that overt cheating can only be established if the accused can be shown to have benefited from the practice or was caught with tangible evidence such as crib sheets or consistently identical answer sheets. The. court made a few findings of fact including the fact that a comparison of test scores of the two students accused of cheating was introduced as evidence. They were observed on at least three occasions to be engaged in behaviour indistinguishable from cheating. Maria Stanik was seen looking at Cheryl Boehm's paper during the exams etc. I need not emphasize that the two students involved were actually in the examination room during the examination. All their conduct complained of, took place in the exam room and in the process of the examinations. A second case for illustration is COMMONWEALTH vs CABELL 199 Pa Supreme 513 (1962) where the defendant Cabell inter alia made a mark in a certain test given under a civil service regulation by placing on an examination paper, purporting to be his own the examination number of the other defendant, by name Luck. The second defendant, according to evidence had agreed to pay to Cabell US $50 to take a civil service examination for foreman on Luck's behalf. The two defendants, in the examination room, exchanged examination numbers so that Cabell's paper appeared to be that of Luck and vice versa. Cabell's paper which had Luck's index number achieved a high score and was number one on the eligible list. The necessary ingredients to sustain a charge of cheating are obviously absent in the appeal before us. The main distinguishing factors are that (a) no specific law or regulation creating "cheating" as a crime or offence has been cited. (b) the appellant did not enter the examination room and was therefore not observed in the examination room or during the examinations to be indulging in acts or conduct amounting to cheating (c) the appellant was not charged with attempting to cheat. I am of the view that the conviction of the appellant on count 2 for cheating cannot be sustained and I accordingly vote for quashing that conviction. The conviction in count one that the appellant conducted himself in a way prejudicial to good order and discipline is affirmed.
