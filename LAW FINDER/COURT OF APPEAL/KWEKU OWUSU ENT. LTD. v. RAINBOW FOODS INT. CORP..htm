JUDGMENT

AKAMBA, J.A.

The plaintiff/respondent (hereinafter simply referred to as the respondent) is a body corporate based in the state of Nebraska in the United States of America. The defendant/appellant (hereinafter simply referred to as the appellant) is also a body corporate but based in Ghana with its registered offices in Tema. Between January and February 2000, so states the respondent in his statement of claim, the respondent at the request of the appellant sold and delivered to the latter a quantity of poultry product to the total value of US$123250.91. It was agreed between the parties that payment for the goods would be made sixty days after the arrival and delivery to the appellant. A dispute eventually arose between the parties over the issue of payment for the goods. The respondent claims that the appellant after receiving the goods in April 2000, made only a part payment of US$38,905.02 leaving a balance of US$84,345.89, which he failed to pay.

The appellant refutes the claim, averring that the respondent had shipped nine (9) containers of turkey tails F.O.B. Tema, Ghana, instead of four (4) containers of turkey tails and ‘if possible’ four (4) containers fowl leg quarters’ contracted to be delivered C.I.F. Tema. This mode of shipments, ie F.O.B. instead of C.I.F. gave rise to a heavy cost burden on the appellant for which he was obliged to find additional resources to offset. It is also the claim of the appellant that when the respondent was informed of the additional burden occasioned by the F.O.B. delivery, the respondent intimated that he would pay for the freight and incidental charges, only to honour just a portion of this undertaking. The appellant stated that the partial contribution for the freight paid by the respondent was US$14,000 made to Maersk Shipping Co. Ltd. Against the background of these claims and counterclaims the respondent filed his writ of summons at the Fast Track High Court, Accra claiming the following reliefs:

(i) Recovery of the sum of eighty four thousand, three hundred and forty five point eight nine United State dollars (US$84,345.89) or its cedi equivalent at the prevailing forex bureau rate at the time of payment being the outstanding balance of the purchase price of poultry products sold and delivered to the defendant by the plaintiff at the request of the defendant, which said amount defendant has either neglected and/or refused pay to the plaintiff in spite of repeated demands made payment.

(ii) Interest on the said amount at the prevailing commercial bank rate from the 1st day of May 2000 until date of final judgment.

Attached to the writ was a statement of claim clearly setting out the facts upon which the respondent rested his claims. The appellant entered appearance to the writ on 18th October 2001 and on 31st October 2001 the respondent filed summons for summary judgment to be entered against the appellant. On 8th November 2001, the appellant filed his statement of defence and counterclaim as well as an affidavit in opposition to the respondent’s application for summary judgment. The motion for summary judgment was duly argued whereupon the trial High Court judge on 12th December 2001, granted the application and accordingly entered the judgment prayed by the respondent. This appeal is against the decision thus entered in favour of the respondent and is premised upon the following grounds:

(1) The judgment was against the weight of evidence.

(2) The learned trial judge erred in law;

(a) by entering final judgement against defendant/appellant in the face of triable issues contained in the affidavit evidence and pleadings of the defendant/appellant disclosing the circumstances which required investigation e.g.

(i) As to the essential ingredients of the contract (the subject matter of the dispute and

(ii) as to the amount to be paid.

(b) Because there was a good defence on the merits of the case which showed a plausible dispute as to the nature of the contract entered into and the mode of its performance, the amount due to the plaintiff including the banning and sale (which was held to be injurious to the health of Ghanaians)'

It is obvious from the grounds listed supra that the thrust of the appellant’s complaint is that the judgment entered against him is against the weight of evidence with the remaining grounds being the apparent misdirections upon which the appellant premises his complaint. In other words the appellant is contending that the affidavit evidence both in support and against the motion raised tribal issues that warranted a refusal of the motion and in favour of a full trial on the issues. Given this scenario I would consider the grounds (1) and (2) together. In doing so it is necessary to have recourse to the High Court rules under which the motion was considered and granted together with the evidence albeit affidavit evidence and other attachments placed before the court to ascertain the justification or otherwise of the grant. Order 14 of LN140A as amended by LI 1129 is the relevant rule and it provides as follows:

“1. Where in an action begun by a writ a defendant has been served with a statement of claim and has entered an appearance, the plaintiff may apply to the court for judgment against him on the ground that he has no defence to a claim included in the writ or to a particular part of such a claim or that he has no defence to such a claim or part except as to the amount of any damages claimed.

3 (1) defendant may show cause against the application by affidavit or otherwise to the satisfaction of the court.

(2) Where the defendant shows cause, the court may order him (or, in the case of a body corporate, any director, manager, secretary or similar officer thereof, or any person purporting to act in any such capacity to attend and be examined on oath if it appears to the court that special circumstances make this desirable, or to produce any document,......

5 (1) hearing the application the court may—

(a) Give such judgment for the plaintiff against the defendant on the relevant claim or part of a claim as may be just having regard to the nature of the remedy or relief sought unless the defendant satisfies the court, with respect to that claim or part, that there is an issue or question in dispute which ought to be tried or that there ought for some other reason to be a trial of that claim or part; or “

The summary proceedings provided under Order 14 of LN.140A as amended by LI 1129 are designed to fast tract the trial process by enabling a party to obtain quick judgment where there is plainly no defence to the claim. This is achieved by dispensing with a full trial where there is no arguable or triable issue. The Supreme Court Practice 1997 Vol. 1 Page 147 throws a very refreshing searchlight on the said Order 14 making it clear that the rule does not confer a right upon a plaintiff to proceed under Order 14 in every case in which this procedure may be appropriate but only 'on ground that the defendant has no defence' to a particular claim. The summary process should therefore be used only in proper cases and should not be employed for tactical purposes (see per Scott L.J. in Dott vs. Brown (1936) 1 A.E.R.545). An essential requirement for the grant of summary judgment is a statement of the belief that the defendant has no defence to the claim. In this vein it will be an understatement to suggest that the respondent had in his affidavit verifying the motion and in particular paragraph 14 thereof, accordingly stated his belief that the appellant has no defence to his claim. This is how the respondent put it:

"14. It is my belief that defendant has no defence whatsoever in law to plaintiff's claim for the said outstanding balance in the sum of eightyfour thousand, three hundred and forty five point eight nine United States dollars (US$84,345.89) plus accumulated interest thereon."

Let me state at this juncture that the fact that a defendant has served a defence will not, in a proper case, necessarily prevent the plaintiff from making the requisite affidavit swearing to the belief that there is no defence and proceeding under Order 14. (Mc Lardy vs. Slateum (1890) 24 Q.B.D.504). If any defence, however demonstrably false it may be, has been served, "it is essential that the affidavit in support adverts to this and then goes on to depose that notwithstanding such defence the deponent believes that there is no defence and explains why this is so. Not to do so is inconsistent with the duty of candour to the court in interlocutory matters." —(See The Supreme Court Practice 1997, page 150). This much also was answered in the supplementary affidavit in support of plaintiff’s application filed on 15/11/2001 wherein it was deposed that:

“7. The defendant’s deposition that plaintiff owe him could not have been deposed to in good faith and are entirely false as per annexure ‘DAH1’ and ‘DAH2’.

8. It is my belief that the defendant’s statement of defence and counterclaim and in the affidavit in opposition are a complete sham and do not disclose any defence in law to plaintiff’s claim.”

Meanwhile, the appellant in his statement of defence and counterclaim filed on 8/11/2001 raised the area of enquiry to be the following:

“(i) the essential ingredients of the contract, the subject matter in dispute.

(ii) The mode of shipment/delivery of the products was other than that agreed i.e. C.I.F. instead of the F.O.B. resorted to and leading to unwarranted hiked costs.

(iii) That the parties agreed to six containers to be shipped rather than the nine (9) delivered.

(iv) The amount to be paid.

(v) Whether the plaintiff has an agent/principal relationship to warrant the carrying out of instructions given by the principal.”

The combined effect of rules 3 (1) and 5 (1) of Order 14 as amended (supra), the defendant to show cause, to the satisfaction of the court, that the application for summary judgment ought not be granted because there are triable issues to be determined by the court. This is so notwithstanding the applicant's advertence to the defence and his (applicant’s) steadfast belief that there is no defence to the summons. The appellant must therefore demonstrate and satisfy the court as to the triable issues to be resolved.

In the instant appeal it is one of the appellant's contentions that the mode of shipment or delivery of the products was other than that agreed i.e. C.I.F instead of the F.O.B resorted to and which led to unwarranted hiked costs. If the costs were indeed hiked due to non-adherence to the terms of the contract, it certainly is an issue for the court to determine and apportion responsibility. It is apparent that the mode of shipment of the goods, be it C.I.F. or F.O.B., imports different fiscal implications to the parties. The appellant referred to Exb. KO2 in support of his contention. In Exb. KO2, Mr. Kwaku Owusu, Managing Director of appellant company stated amongst others, the following:

"With reference to the conversation we had last night on the phone concerning the $75,000 loan from Exim Bank, I would like you to purchase 4 containers of the turkey tails (if possible) "Delivered CIF, Tema."

I have realised that if we buy it at FOB, Tema, although we would have more containers, I would face some difficulties paying the freight in Ghana. This is because when Maersk is collecting the freight from me, the dollar would be calculated at the current black market rate but not bank rate. This makes it too expensive because if I wire money to you in dollars, the bank rate for the dollar will be more reasonable. "

It is significant to also know that Exb. KO2’s opening paragraph referred to the parties' conversation on the phone concerning the $75,000 loan from the Exim Bank. The requirement for the delivery of the turkey tails C.I.F. Tema appears to be so significant to the appellants as to warrant its confirmation in writing (supra). There is an apparent ambiguity in the opening paragraph of Exb. KO2 introduced by the expression 'if possible', immediately after the words 'turkey tails'. However upon a closer scrutiny of the document as a whole, it becomes obvious that the so-called ambiguity is only a mirage. An impression one gets upon reading Exbs. KO1, KO2 and paragraph 9 of the affidavit in opposition of 8/11/2001 is that the respondent having succeeded in obtaining the credit facility of US$75,000 for the appellant, the latter was now in a position to place his orders which should then be the basis of their contract, if honoured and subject to 15% charges to the respondent. The respondent however holds a contrary position to that advanced supra and therefore the parties are joined on the issue, which must be unravelled by resort to a full trial. The very position of the plaintiff who arranged the loan facility and is to earn 15% financial charges is subject to divergent categorization by the two parties. Is he an agent of the appellant as contended by appellant or what? Whilst I am inclined to the view that there are triable issues pertaining to the very terms of the contract between the parties, I must hasten to 'indicate that the claim by the appellant that the contract was frustrated "by the banning of the importation and sale of same by the Government of Ghana through the Ministry of Health" could not have been one of the reasons for this conclusion.

I am also mindful of the appellant's admission that if possible they owe only fifty seven thousand dollars ($57,000). Aside of this, the defendant raised a counterclaim seeking damages for breach of contract, recovery of all expenses incurred as a result of the breach as well as interest. It is obvious from the counterclaim that is arises from and is so connected with the subject matter of the original claim, which latter it appears to contradict.

It is therefore not correct that the appellant’s defence and counterclaim is a complete sham and discloses no defence to the plaintiff’s claim as contended by the respondent. It is only fair and proper in such an instance that account ought to be taken of the defendant’s counterclaim. Diplock L.J. succinctly states this position in Adam and Harvey Ltd. Vs. Int. Maritime Supplies Co. Ltd. (1966) 1 Lloyd’s Rep. @ 572 thus:

"I think having listened to the argument before us, the learned Judge must have been persuaded that the counterclaim did not relate to the subject matter of the agreement in which case his order might well have been justified. I think, however, that if one looks at the evidence it is sufficiently plain that the whole of the counterclaim does relate to the same subject matter as the agreement and since one cannot say that the counterclaim is bogus or a sham whatever its ultimate merits may turn out to be, I think the defendant-appellant’s here were entitled to unconditional leave to defend."

In the light of the appellant’s admission of partial liability, I am of the view that judgment ought to have been entered only for the sum of US$57,000 as per Exhibit K 014 and paragraph 21 of the statement of defence leaving the difference to be contested in a full trial to determine the outstanding claims of the parties. In the result, I set aside the judgment entered by the High Court on the 12th December 2001 against the appellant and substitute judgment for US$57,000 for the respondent. Subject to the foregoing I order a full trial to resolve the remaining issues. Appeal allowed to this extent. I am of the opinion that this is a proper case to order that each party should bear his own costs.
