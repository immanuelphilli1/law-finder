JUDGMENT

ASARE KORANG, J.A.:

The appellant alleges that he was induced to make certain agreements, documents and Indentures with the respondents by economic duress after he had entered into negotiation with the respondents to become business associates.

Thereafter the respondents submitted to the Chief Registrar of Lands one of the Indentures for registration of title.

When the appellant got to know of this he instructed his solicitor to write to the Chief Registrar of Lands to caveat the intended registration.

The Respondents then sued the appellant in the High Court claiming the following reliefs.

“(a) The sum of US Dollars 175,648.00 being money the defendant (appellant) owed the plaintiffs (respondents).

(b) Interest on the said sum at 10% per anum from 1st November, 2000 to the date of final payment.

(c) Costs.”

The appellant entered an appearance to the claim and filed a Statement of Defence and counter claim which he later amended.

The counter claim reads:

“(1) A Declaration that the Defendant (appellant) was included to make or enter into the agreement or instruments or indentures referred to or alleged in the counter claim by duress on the part of the Plaintiffs (respondents).

(2)   An order to set aside or revoke all the said agreements, instruments or indentures.

(3)   As order for accounts between the parties herein.

(4)   In the alternative damages for breach of contract on the part of the Plaintiff."

Subsequently the appellant filed a motion of notice for an order of interlocutory Injunction to restrain the respondents, their assigns, the Chief Registrar at the Land Title Registry from processing the respondents/application for a Land Title Certificate in respect of the Deed of Assignment made between the appellant and the respondents. The respondents filed an affidavit in opposition to the application for Interlocutory injunction.

The Appellant also filed a summons for Joinder which sought to join the Chief Registrar of Lands to the suit as a Co-defendant.

The Chief Registrar of Lands also caused an affidavit to be filed on his behalf opposing the application for Joinder.

Hearing of the application for Joinder commenced on July 10, 2001 and continued on July 26, 2001.

On the latter date, there is no indication in the appeal record (see page 74) of an adjournment being applied for or of the court ruling for an adjournment. However, further hearing continued during vacation before the learned High Court Judge then sitting as a vacation Judge who on September 18, 2001 refused the application for Joinder.

Soon after the ruling, counsel for the respondents asked the court to order counsel for the appellant to move the appellant's motion for an order of interlocutory injunction.

Counsel for the appellant declined to move as he intended to appeal against the refusal of the application for Joinder whereupon counsel for the respondents asked that the motion for interlocutory injunction which had remained on the list for nine months be struck out as unmeritorious and a further order made by the court directing the Registrar of Lands to register the deed of assignment of the respondents as a measure of protecting the interests of the Respondents.

The court below ruled on the oral application to strike out the interlocutory Injunction as follows:—

"BY COURT:— Although counsel for Defendant (appellant) lost his right to react as he refused to move his motion. I would state here in reaction to ...... issue raised that as he refused to move, the plaintiff (respondent) was placed in an awkward situation whereby he had to move for the motion in the circumstances to be struck out or in the alternative register the plaintiffs (respondents) grave concerns as to the position of the plaintiff (respondent) if the said assignment is not registered if only to secure the interest of the plaintiff until the final determination of the suit.

I am in agreement with the plaintiffs (respondent's) counsel at this point that the court is duty bound to hold the scales evenly balanced to protect the interest of both parties whilst the matter remains undetermined and also not to render the outcome of the winning part nugatory.

In the circumstances, I am compelled to protect the interest of the plaintiff by ordering the Registrar of Land Title to register the plaintiff's (respondent's) assignment in order not to be overreacted (sic).

In the same vein to protect the interest of the Defendant (appellant) it is further ordered that the plaintiffs (respondents) are not to dispose of the property until the final determination of the suit. That way all interested parties are protected and as plaintiff's (respondent's) counsel stated if at the end of the suit it is determined that the Assignment is null and void. The Registration (sic) is hereby ordered to register the assignment of Plaintiff (respondent) forthwith."

Against this ruling, the appellant has appealed to this Court, the sole ground of appeal being that:

"The learned High Court Judge erred in his ruling when she ordered the Chief Registrar of the Land title Registry to register a Deed of Assignment which is the subject matter of the Counter claim before the Honourable Court without a hearing."

Counsel for the appellant argued that by the order contained in the ruling of the learned High Court Judge she had prejudged the Appellant's counterclaim which sought to challenge the validity of the Deed of Assignment and which was an issue to be determined by the High Court.

Counsel submitted that the court below erred in directing the Chief Registrar of the Land Title Registry to register the Deed of Assignment in favour of the Respondent during the pendency of the application for interlocutory injunction, especially as the validity of the Deed of Assignment was a subject matter of the appellant's counterclaim.

No arguments against the appeal were urged on the court by counsel for the respondents and no statement of case was filed by him. Counsel instead asked the court to decide the matter as it saw fit.

We have carefully and thoroughly read the order made by the learned High Court Judge and we are of the view that her order flew beyond the bounds of the invitation thrown to her to strike out the appellant's application for interim injunction.

She, unabashedly, made orders affecting the rights of the parties until the final determination of the suit when all that she was required to do was to strike, or not strike out the appellant's motion for interim injunction.                                               

Declarations in the learned judge's ruling such as her stated desire "to hold the scales evenly balanced to protect the interest of both parties and not to render the outcome of the winning party nugatory" appear to have beclouded her judgment so that she strayed past what she was required to do as a matter of law and procedure.

She appears to have swayed to the side of the respondents when she was compelled to order the Chief Registrar of Lands to register the respondents Deed of Assignment.

Who compelled her, and with the powers given her by the Rules of Court, was the so called compulsion that irresistible?

The learned judge made no order to strike out the appellant's motion for interim injunction.

She was bound to hear evidence on the validity or otherwise of the respondents Deed of Assignment particularly as the appellant had counter claimed for an order revoking all agreements, instruments and indentures made between him and the respondents.

In the end we agree with counsel for the appellant that there is no order striking out the appellant' application for interim injunction.

We express the opinion that the order made by the learned High Court Judge that the Chief Registrar of Lands register the Respondents Deed of Assignment, is incompetent as made without jurisdiction.

The appeal is therefore allowed and the said order declared null and void.

A. ASARE KORANG
