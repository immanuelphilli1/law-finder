JUDGMENT

BENIN, J.A.:

This is an appeal from the decision of the High Court, Koforidua entered in favour of the plaintiff, who sued for herself and on behalf of her children who she had by the late Seth Kwabla Agbosu of Battor–Aveyime.  The defendant is a retired officer of the Ghana Army.  The dispute is over a piece of land with building thereon situate at Gbejirmanya - Somanya. There is no dispute about the identity of the property in question.  The plaintiff sued for declaration of title, recovery of possession, damages for trespass and perpetual injunction against the defendant. Her grounds for these claims were pleaded in a statement of claim in which she averred that she was joint purchaser of the land with her late husband S. K. Agbosu (hereinafter referred to as the deceased). That it was she and her children, with the consent of the deceased, who put up a six room house on the land. That against their protest (that is plaintiff and her children), the deceased sold the property to the defendant.  The deceased sued the children over this property at the High Court but later discontinued same.  The defendant has since January, 1985 entered the land to exercise acts of ownership.  Hence this action.  For his part, the defendant averred that he bought this property from the deceased after he had caused proper search to be made at the Lands Registry, Koforidua which search disclosed the deceased to be owner thereof free from any encumbrance.  That he paid the purchase price and had documents duly registered in his name.  That it was only after the purchase was completed that the plaintiff and her children protested and they entered the land to harvest food crops there.  He denied knowledge of the litigation between the deceased and his children over this property.  He averred that as a wealthy man, deceased would not buy land with money provided by the plaintiff.  That he had acquired a legal title to the property and plaintiffs were estopped from denying that.

In her reply plaintiff averred that the documents on the property bore the name of the deceased alone, but explained this was done purely out of convenience.  That after the sale the deceased was confronted and he apologised for his acts and offered to pay for the land and building but she rejected the offer and insisted on her right.  That the deceased lost his wealth and was dependent to a large extent on the plaintiff.

The issues agreed upon for hearing were:

(1) Whether or not the defendant purchased the land in dispute with knowledge of plaintiff’s interest in the said property.

(2) Whether or not the plaintiff owned the property jointly with her late husband.

(3)  Whether or not the defendant has acquired any legal title and interest in this land.

Evidence was led by and for both parties and at the end of the day the trial court resolved all the issues in favour of the plaintiff.  The court's reasons will be unfolded as we go on to discuss the various grounds of appeal which are:

(1)  Since evidence on record shows the late Seth Agbosu was a wealthy goldsmith and since at the peak of his wealth he purchased the property in dispute in his own name and since during his lifetime he filed a claim to the effect that he was the sole owner of the property and since the plaintiff could not lead any concrete evidence to rebut that, the learned trial Judge misdirected herself and erred when she held that the late Seth Agbosu bought the property jointly with the plaintiff as a normal practice between husband and wife who are illiterates.

(2) Since the claim of the plaintiff was for a declaration of joint title and ownership and since the plaintiff did not discharge the onus required by law on her the learned trial judge erred in giving judgment in her favour.

(3) Since the plaintiff instituted the action for and on her own behalf and on behalf of her children and since plaintiff’s children could be properly described as parties and gave evidence as witnesses the learned trial Judge erred in relying mostly on the evidence of plaintiff’s children who are interested parties in the matter to establish the claim of the plaintiff.

(4)  In view of the fact that the claim of the plaintiff was for joint ownership and in view of the fact that even if plaintiff has succeeded in establishing her claim she would have been entitled to only a portion of the property, the learned trial Judge misdirected herself and in the process occasioned a grave miscarriage of justice when she declared the sale agreement between the late Seth Agbosu and the defendant as null and void.

(5) Since the defendant bought the property from the late Seth Agbosu who was the owner of the property and since defendant was placed in possession of the property and exercised covert acts of ownership for more than three years before plaintiff instituted the present action the learned trial Judge erred in holding that the defendant's act of possession amounted to trespass in law.

(6) The learned trial Judge misdirected herself in holding that Exhibits 1, 5 and 6 tendered in support of the search were dubious.

Counsel for the defendant/appellant argued grounds 1, 2 and 3 together.  Counsel submitted that since plaintiff claimed declaration of title “the legal burden of proof on her is one of proof beyond reasonable doubt”.  He cited in support a dictum of Francois J.S.C. in the case of Banga vrs. Djanie (1989 – 90) 1 G.L.R. 510 at p. 519.  This submission appears to contradict all the known authorities and even legislation, per sections 11, 12 and 13 of the Evidence Decree, 1975 (N.R.C.D. 323) which say that proof beyond reasonable doubt is required in criminal or quasi–criminal matters only and that in civil trials proof on a preponderance of probabilities or balance of probabilities is sufficient.  Let us take and analyse what the learned Judge said at pp. 519 – 520 of the report.  He said:

"Since a declaration of title is sought, it is essential that the usual burden of proof should be satisfactorily discharged for success. That burden was clearly laid in Kodilinye vrs. Odu (1935) 2 WACA 336.  That burden which is now trite learning, ensures that the plaintiff should win on the strength of his own case, which must not be propped up by weaknesses in the defendant's case. The principle has for several decades been the fulcrum for determining ownership in land matters in our courts. In recent times a dangerous trend has been erupting of equating this burden with the normal burden in a civil case of measuring success by a balance of probabilities.  In my view the requirement of a higher burden of proof in land matters cannot be whittled away by glosses on the principle. This quality of proof has sometimes even been equated with proof in criminal matters, i.e. proof beyond reasonable doubt".

In so far as the learned Judge equated proof in land matters with that in criminal matters, I think, with respect, that he erred especially in the light of the clear provisions of the Evidence Decree (cited above) which would overrule any previous decisions to the effect as stated by the learned Judge. Fortunately the other two justices who sat on that appeal did not go to this extent. Be that as it may, the Supreme Court has finally decided against this reasoning of requiring proof beyond reasonable doubt in land claims, see Adwubeng vrs. Domfeh (1996-97) SC G.L.R. 660, holding 3 and also at p. 670 per Acquah J.S.C. Thus the standard of proof required in land claims is one of a preponderance or balance of probabilities.

Let us return to the submissions.  Counsel for the appellant said the plaintiff and her witnesses abandoned the claim for a declaration of title in favour of that of joint ownership". That I think is wrong for, by the statement of claim the plaintiff averred that she jointly purchased the plot with her children, hence the relief sought being declaration of title.

Counsel continued:

“section 35 of the Evidence Decree, 1975 (N.R.C.D. 323) states that: (The owner of the legal title to property is presumed to be the owner of the full beneficial title). So that by law once the title deeds are in the name of Seth Komla Agbosu, he is presumed to be the full beneficial owner with power to dispose of his self acquired property as and when he thought fit. This presumption can be rebutted by concrete and uncontroverted evidence that the beneficial title is vested in someone else or jointly with another"

This is a perfect proposition of law.  Then counsel went on to review the evidence and concluded that the plaintiff did not establish her case.  Counsel referred to the plaintiff’s evidence - in - chief that she and the deceased jointly purchased the land.  That under cross- examination the plaintiff "traced her claim to joint ownership by reason of her marriage to the late Seth K Agbosu".  Counsel's conclusion was based on the following extract from the cross-examination of the plaintiff:

“Q - You will agree with me that no receipt was issued in your joint name.

A - Yes, but it was by consent that it was issued that way.

Q - I  am putting it to you that the receipt was not issued in your husband's name by consent.

A - My husband married me according to custom so everything that is my husband's is mine.

Q - It is because you say everything that is your husband's is yours that is why you are claiming the land in dispute.

A - Yes, we purchased the property jointly and I am his wedded wife."

With all respect to learned counsel I do not think the plaintiff was claiming ownership of this property of virtue of her marriage to the deceased; even the last answer which appears to suggest that fact was very clear that she acquired the property jointly with the husband. The proper inference to be drawn was that as a married couple they did things in common and having bought this property jointly it was meant for their common good. At any rate against the background to the dispute and considering the totality of her case, the plaintiff never claimed this property by virtue of her marriage.  As decided by the Supreme Court in Khoury vrs. Choitel (1964) GLR 100 it was important that the totality of a person's testimony be considered in deciding on an issue.

Counsel went further to say that the plaintiff changed her story again, this time, to that of a gift. This also counsel picked from the cross-examination of the plaintiff, and it reads:

"Q - Did you say in your evidence that your husband gave you a gift of part of his land for you to build on?

A -  Yes, that was when I wanted to build and he said he was not going to build at Somanya

Q -   It is not true your husband made a gift of part of the land to you.

A -    It is true

Q -   If you were a joint owner of the land you will not require a gift from co-owner

A -   He did not gift the land to me he only blessed it for me.

Q -   Your husband did not bless anything for you.

A -    He did.”

To begin with, the fact of a gift was not pleaded so no evidence on it should have been allowed either in the evidence in chief or under cross-examination being totally inadmissible evidence. And in such a situation the trial court should reject it when giving judgment or if it does not do so the appellate court should, see Tormekpey vrs. Ahiable (1975) 2 G.L.R 432 C.A. But in this case the evidence of a gift or blessing or by whatever name called was proffered to show that before the plaintiff and her children built on the land the deceased was informed about it and it was with his consent and blessing that the building was put up. This is in accord with paragraph 6 of her statement of claim whereby she averred that “plaintiff and her children with the consent of the husband put up a six room building (not roofed) on the said plot …..” (Emphasis mine).  Hence the evidence that the deceased gifted part the land and took a bottle of schnapps from her, a fact confirmed by the mason (PW1).  Let me recall the relevant evidence at this stage.

PW1 Michael Dawutey said the plaintiff approached him and his wife to prevail upon the deceased to build for her and her children at Somanya.  But when he put this request before the deceased he refused saying he would prefer building in his own hometown.  After some persistent efforts by PW1 at persuading the deceased, the latter decided to give her part of the plot they bought.  He continued:

“He released the plot to her and the plaintiff gave him a bottle of schnapps in my presence for the land.”

The plaintiff said she approached PW1 to appeal to the deceased to build for her which he refused to do.  She continued:

“My husband finally agreed I should put up a building on the plot. He collected a bottle of schnapps from me in the presence of PW1 before he showed me where to build”.

It was this act which plaintiff described in various ways as a gift or a blessing.  By whatever account it was not a gift perse.  The scenario was that she had contributed to the acquisition of the land which was then conveyed in the name of the husband who she truly described as the head of family.  As a loyal and respectful wife, she would not do anything on the land without the husband’s knowledge and consent.  More particularly as she needed a building for herself and her own children (to the exclusion of the deceased’s other wives, if any, and children), she needed a firm commitment from the deceased that he had released the land to her and her children to build on.  In fact this act was very symbolic both in fact and in law.  In terms of fact the deceased’s consent (as pleaded) in allowing the plaintiff and the children to build on the land would mean that he had given them unconditional access to, and user of, the land to the exclusion of any of his other children.  In law it has been held in several cases some of which were cited by counsel for the appellant namely Odoteye vrs. Odoteye (1984 – 86) 1 G.L.R. 519: Quartey vrs. Martey (1959) G.L.R. 377: Gyamerah vrs. Buor (1962) 1 GLR 196 that assistance offered by wife and children to their husband and father does not entitle them to a part ownership of the property he acquires.  So that if it is said that, even the money contributed by the plaintiff did not entitle her to a share of the land, then the fact that the deceased took schnapps (a very significant customary drink) before allowing her and her children to build on the land would mean he had recognised the woman’s contribution as, at least, an owner in her own right.  And that is the only conclusion one could reach in view of the understanding reached before the schnapps was demanded and paid.  In my view this evidence was not contradictory to the plaintiff’s position of a joint acquirer of the plot, it was an act by which effectively the deceased acknowledged plaintiff’s title and which effectively released the land to the plaintiff and her children.  The wrong use of the word "gift" should not detract from the substance and significance of what took place in order not to defeat the purpose of taking the drink from the plaintiff.

Next, counsel referred to the evidence of Ben Agbosu (PW4), Paul Agbosu (PW7), J. Narh Kwesi (PW2) all of who are related to the plaintiff by blood, and said their evidence should not be relied upon.  His first reason is that as blood relations they are interested persons. He relied on this dictum by Osei-Hwere J. (as he then was) in the case of Adai vrs. Anane (1973) 1 G.L.R. 144 at pp. 149-150;

"A plaintiff in an action for a declaration of title assumed the heavy burden of proof and he does not discharge that onus by merely calling his relations to support his evidence. To do so would invite the failure of his claim. This is exactly what the plaintiff did. All his witnesses, the first and second hailed from a common grandfather with him".

A similar reasoning by Twumasi J. (as he then was) was also referred to in the case of Nkrumah vrs. Foli and Another (1982 - 83) 2 G.L.R. 1046.  Counsel said

“regrettably the learned trial Judge relied heavily on the evidence of these interested parties to reach the conclusion that she arrived at".

I do not consider these decisions to be expressing the rigid view that a plaintiff cannot

succeed if all his witnesses are his relations.  If that is the reasoning then I will beg to differ.  For by section 58 of the Evidence Decree every person, unless otherwise disqualified; is a competent witness.  And section 60 only demands that a competent witness should testify from personal knowledge. Indeed these provisions were introduced to provide a clear break with the past where several rules were introduced as to who did or did not qualify as a witness.  All that the new rule requires is for the court to decide on what weight and credibility to attach to the testimony of a witness.  Thus so long as a person is competent who is speaking from personal knowledge the court is not entitled to reject, disbelieve or ignore his testimony simply on account of his blood or other close relationship to the party on whose behalf he testified.

And since those High Court decisions cited above are saying that the evidence of the close relations do not corroborate that of the party, it means it should be treated or regarded as one and the same evidence as that given by the party himself.  And even in such a scenario the party does not lose a civil case where corroboration is not required by law.  Besides, the Court of Appeal has held that the court could act on the evidence of a single witness who happens to be a relation of the party calling him.  This was in the case of Atadi vrs. Ladzekpo (1981) G.L.R. 218 at pp 226 – 227 where the court, per Edward Wiredu, J.A. said:

“Whilst there is the need for caution in accepting the corroborative evidence of a relation, especially when his evidence is being offered against a stranger to the family, it is not justifiable to reject same without laying a foundation for such a course especially when on the evidence such a witness is the only person known to have personal knowledge of the events at issue”.

In my view the evidence of a relation of the party is entitled to the same consideration as any other person subject to the court cautioning itself that such a witness may give tainted evidence which goes to weight and credibility. If after this caution the court is still satisfied with his testimony nothing prevents it from relying upon it for whatever purpose, for judicial decisions must be based on intelligence and credit of the witness, not on his relationship with a party.

Counsel continued:

"But assuming the learned trial judge was right in relying on the evidence of these interested parties was their probative value sufficient to discharge the onus required by law on the plaintiff?"

He proceeded with an analysis of the evidence of PW2, PW4 and PW7 and said it was either hearsay or based on what the deceased was said to have told them.

He continued:

"What beats our mind is why plaintiff and her children did not institute this action as far back as 1984 when the sale took place but had to wait until after the death of Seth K. Agbosu. The law is well settled on such evidence from witness purporting to emanate from a dead person."

He proceeded with some decided authorities, and submitted:

“............. it was incumbent on the learned trial Judge to have approached the story of PW1, PW2, PW4, PW5 and PW7 with suspicion having regard to the fact that the other actor in this story .............. is dead and cannot therefore give his own version of what took place."

It must first of all be pointed out that not all the witnesses for the plaintiff were related to her. PW1 was only a mason and a friend of the deceased, and had no relationship whatever with the plaintiff. Dickson (PW3) was a nephew of the deceased. Phanuel Agbaklah (PW5) was a cousin of the deceased. Madam Yengwah (PW6) was the wife of the deceased's landlord.  All these persons gave evidence which in one way or the other supported the case of the plaintiff.  PW3 for instance said the deceased told him he (the deceased), the plaintiff and their children were putting up this building because they were being ejected by their landlord.  He said also the deceased told him his son in Accra, Prosper Agbosu, a quantity surveyor, had brought him money to buy iron rods.  PW5 said the deceased told him he had acquired this land with his wife and children.  That he knew the house was put up by the deceased and the wife with financial contribution by the children, and with the physical assistance of the plaintiff and children.  PW6 said the plaintiff objected to the deceased selling the house and rejected an offer from the deceased to give her money to buy flour for her bakery business which offer she rejected and insisted she wanted her house back since she built it with her children.  In the face of all these pieces of evidence, coming as it were, from relations of the deceased and strangers to the couple which counsel has conveniently chosen to ignore, the trial court was perfectly entitled to rely on them alongside other evidence in coming to a conclusion.

In respect of the submission that it is a case against a dead man, Counsel referred to the case of (Thomas vrs. The Times Book Co. Ltd.) (1966) 2 All ER 241 at p. 244 where Brett M.R. said:

“The law is that when an attempt is made to charge a dead person in a matter, in which if he were alive he might have answered the charge the evidence ought to be looked at with great care; the evidence ought to be thoroughly sifted, and the mind of any judge who hears it ought to be first of all in a state of suspicion; but if in the end the truthfulness of the witnesses is made perfectly clear and apparent and the tribunal which has to act on their evidence believes them, the suggested doctrine becomes absurd.”

That principle is accepted if only because it is just fair that, in a situation where a key actor is dead, the court ought to be circumspect in accepting the evidence made against him. Thus in the case of Morris vrs. Monrovia 1 W.A.C.A. 70 citing the case of Re Whitaker (1882) Ch. D. 737 it was held that

“a person making a claim against the estate of a dead man cannot sustain that claim by her  ............ own deposition."

In this case aside of the plaintiff, seven other persons testified for the plaintiff. So the principle in these cases is satisfied. Moreover the Judge did consider the testimony as a whole in arriving at her decision. It is to be noted that during his lifetime the deceased sued some of his children at the High Court. Pleadings were filed and the children, in their statement of defense, pleaded the fact that their mother contributed £18 out of the purchase price of £55 for this plot out of her own resources. That though the indenture bore the name of only their father he alone did not own the plot. That it was their mother and they the children, who put up this house. It is clear from those pleadings that the case being set up in this action is not strange or new to the deceased estate; if he were alive the deceased would readily agree that was the position of the plaintiff and her children all along. Hence the question of viewing their evidence with suspicion did not seriously arise, except that the court could not accept the evidence without a critical appraisal thereof in the absence of the deceased. Counsel for the appellant referred to the same pleadings and said by the statement of claim, a copy of which was tendered in evidence, the deceased had declared that he was "the absolute and beneficial owner of the property which he sold to the defendant. If that was the position of Seth K. Agbosu in his lifetime then he could not and did not tell anyone that he owned the property jointly with the plaintiff and his children. The learned trial judge failed to give any consideration to the above law, ignored the exhibits referred and accepted the evidence of the witnesses the probative value of which did not satisfy the requirements of the law."

The said High Court suit between the deceased and her children did not go to trial as it was discontinued. So the averments contained therein, apart from giving an indication what the position of either party was never constituted proof of any issue. Apart from admissions made, all averments contained in pleadings are subject to proof.  Thus the fact that the deceased had pleaded owning this property all by himself did not mean he never told anybody he owned it jointly with the plaintiff and her children, it's a perfect non sequitur. Parties plead facts which often times turn up to be totally false.

Counsel referred to the evidence of PW4 that it was the deceased who gave him the originals of the indenture and said this could not be true in view of the report the deceased made to the police contained in the police extract - Exhibit 4. This Exhibit 4 reads:—

"14th November 1984 2.00 p.m..

One Seth Komla Agbosu of H/No. Unknown Salesi - Somanya came to the station and reported theft of original indenture in respect of a piece of land bought by him at Somanya, that he highly suspects his children for the theft as such wanted police assistance. 2 p.m. Extract from the station diary 28/29 was prepared and handed over to the complainant to be given to his lawyers for necessary action."

The police detective who received this complaint at the station said when the complaint was recorded, he told the deceased it was a case of theft so there was the need to arrest his children for questioning, and the deceased,

“said he has his own problem so he does not want the police to investigate the matter. Seth Agbosu asked for an extract of the report and I prepared it for him. So the police did not investigate the matter."

With these facts I find it hard to understand counsel’s submission that PW4 was not speaking the truth.  Was it not the deceased who prevented the truth from coming out by blocking police investigations?  It is clear deceased made the report just to enable him secure an extract for his own purpose which at the time was to enable him sell this property without the knowledge and consent of the plaintiff and her children and to satisfy whoever was concerned that the document was in fact lost so as to pave the way for a fresh one to be issued to the defendant.  Where a fact is sought to be used against a party that party must be given the opportunity to know what the fact is for him to decide whether to defend it or not before any comment, adverse or otherwise, may be passed.  In a situation such as this where the deceased's complaint to the police was not even brought to the attention of the suspects mentioned, it could never be used to negative or rebut any evidence given on oath before a court. I reject counsel’s submission on this accordingly.

Next, counsel submitted that the trial Judge did not properly appraise the evidence because she completely ignored "the damning admissions made by PW3 ……” Counsel’s outburst stemmed from portions of the cross-examination of PW3 which are set out in his address and are actually taken from the record as follows:

“Q - Do you know the land in dispute?

A - Yes

Q - So far as you are aware the land in dispute was bought by the late Agbosu.

A - Yes

Q - After he has bought his land he helped you to buy yours'

A - Yes.

Q - It was the late Agbosu alone who asked Dawutey to build the house.

A - Yes he came to me to tell me he had given the work to Dawutey.

Q - Is it correct that the plaintiff came to you that the husband was selling the land and house?

A - Yes,

Q - Is it because you know the land was bought by the late Agbosu and he asked Dawutey to put up the house that you know the property was for the late Agbosu that you asked the plaintiff and his children to buy the property from him?

A – Yes.

Q - You told the court that Agbosu built the house to lintel level alone.

A - Yes

Q -  While Agbosu was a transport owner he was a prosperous man.

A – Yes.

Q -  When did Agbosu put up the building in dispute?

A - He built it when he was running the transport business.”

Counsel said by this evidence PW3 was telling the court that both the land and building were acquired by the deceased alone. He continued that:

"The learned trial Judge did not give any reasons whatsoever for ignoring the evidence of PW3. The law on evidence of witnesses which support the case of their opponent has been stated by the Supreme Court in the case of Asante vrs. Bogyabi (1966) G.L.R. 232 at p. 240 as follows: Where admissions relevant to matters in issue between parties to a case are made by one side, supporting the other as appears to be so in the instant case on appeal, then it seems to me right to say that, that side in whose favour the admissions are made is entitled to succeed and not the other unless there is good reason apparent on the record for holding the contrary view.”

The same principle is stated in Tsrifo vrs. Duah VII (1959) G.L.R. 63 a High Court decision and Banahene vrs. Adinkra (1976) 1 G.L.R. 346, a Court of Appeal decision approving Tsrifo vrs. Duah. Both decisions were approved by the Supreme Court in Achoro and Another vrs. Akanfela and Another (1996-97) S.C.G.L.R. 209 at p. 214.  Rounding up counsel submitted that;

"from the evidence there was no good reason apparent on the record for the conclusion the trial Judge came to in disregarding the evidence of PW3 which supports the defence."

For his part, Counsel for the plaintiff/respondent submitted that PW3

"made no admissions which ought to have enured to the benefit of the defendant/appellant. PW3 did not say under cross-examination that the deceased ........... alone bought the land and put up the building  ........... That PW3's evidence in no way corroborated that of the defendants for which reason plaintiff ought to have lost the suit."

The trial court referred to the evidence of PW3 in these terms;

"PW3 also said the late Agbosu told him PW7 had given him ¢2,000.00 to purchase iron rods to construct the house."

She also accepted PW3's evidence that the deceased told him he was building the house jointly with his wife and children.

Admittedly, the trial Judge did not specifically address what appears in PW3's evidence as corroborative material to the defendant's case, but as I have just pointed out she took into account PW3's evidence. Let us recall the totality of PW3's testimony. On the building his evidence - in - chief was as follows:

"The late Agbosu told me he and the wife and children were jointly building a house as they were being ejected from where they are staying."

He also said the deceased told him his son had provided money to buy iron rods. From these pieces of evidence it could never be interpreted that PW3 knew as a fact that the deceased alone put up the house.  And this is confirmed by the evidence under cross-examination that the deceased told him it was he who engaged Dawutey to construct the house. Thus in all these instances PW3 was relating what the deceased had told him, not what he himself knew or observed as a fact. And these pieces of evidence do not in any way support the defendant's case. And concerning the purchase of the plot all he said was that the deceased bought land from the same vendor from whom he also purchased a plot later on upon the deceased's introduction. That he did not know the vendor before he (PW3) was introduced to him by deceased to buy his plot. If one considers that deceased bought his land before introducing the vendor to PW3 also to buy land from that same vendor, then the only inference to be drawn from the evidence of PW3 was that he was not present when the deceased bought this land. Since he was not present he was not in a position to know who actually provided the purchase price and for whose benefit the plot was purchased, hence his evidence was totally silent on these very material matters. Counsel for the appellant was also interested in PW3's advice to the plaintiff to buy this property from the deceased which he believed amounted to an admission on the part of PW3 that the property was owned by the deceased. This should be considered against the background that PW3's knowledge was based on what the deceased had told him which must have led him to think that the property was owned by the deceased. An admission by conduct might have been implied of the deceased's ownership of the property if the plaintiff had not asserted her own right to it when PW3 suggested to her that she and her children should go and purchase it from the deceased. But in PW3's own words "the plaintiff answered that they cannot buy a house they have jointly put-up,"

In appraising the evidence of this witness one has to consider the fact that the deceased had told him (in the absence of the plaintiff) that he was putting up the house jointly with the plaintiff and her children one of whom he named as the provider of money for the iron rods. Then you consider the fact that the plaintiff told PW3 (in the absence of the deceased) that the house could not be bought by her and her children because they jointly put it up. The only logical inference from his evidence is that the house was jointly put up by the plaintiff and her children and the deceased if the court chose to accept that the deceased took part in the construction.

As regards PW3’s testimony that the deceased told him he engaged Dawutey, that was no conclusive evidence. It would be evidence in support of the defendant if in fact and in truth it was the deceased who engaged Dawutey to construct the house. There is abundant evidence on the record to show that it was the plaintiff, and not the deceased, who engaged Dawutey. Indeed Dawutey himself confirmed this. A court is not entitled to reject the evidence of an eye witness and a participant in an act who is available for cross-examination in favour of the evidence of a person who is narrating what somebody else had told him about the same event, without forthcoming with good reasons, to be found on the record. The totality of the evidence of PW3 did not bolster the defendant's case at all so the rule of evidence which was invoked is not applicable.

Counsel for the appellant next took issue with the trial Judge's decision as regards the view she took on the help and contributions alleged to have been made by the plaintiff and her children. These were the relevant findings of the trial court:

“................ the plaintiff and her sons contributed to the building from their own resources. The plaintiff from her bakery and her sons from their source of employment as

Quantity Surveyor and Internal Auditor respectively. The situation here is unlike the case of the wife and a son in Adom vrs. Kwarley case where the wife sold the produce from her husband's vegetables farm to raise funds for the building, and where the son who was his father's apprentice mason worked with his father on the building."

The case of Adom and Another vrs. Kwarley is reported in (1962) 1 G.L.R. 112. At page 113 these cases were cited: Okwabi vrs Adonu (Consolidated) (1957) 2 W.A.L.R. 268: Quartey Martey and Another (cited above) Adjabeng vrs. Kwabla (1960) G.L.R. 37. The ratio running through these cases is that assistance given to a father and/or husband by a child and/or wife in the former's trade which results in acquisition of property by the man cannot be relied upon to claim a share therein by the child and/or wife. The last case cited further decides that

"by customary law where a son or a ward assists his father or guardian with money, or that money together with other monies of his own, to acquire property, the son or ward does not become joint-owner with the father or guardian of the property so acquired"

These principles are generally accepted, but it is recognised that the intention of the parties before, during and after the acquisition could be considered in determining whether any interest was meant for the wife or son, see Abebrese vrs. Kaah (1976) 2 G.L.R. 46 p. 53.  It is also accepted that where the contribution was very substantial the wife or son, as the case may be, may succeed in claiming an interest in the property. Also since customary law recognised the right of a wife to own her own property outside that of her husband, see Sarbah's Fanti Customary Laws, (3rd edn) (1968) at p. 391, a view accepted by Danquah in his Akan Laws and Customs at pp.191 - 192, where the wife contributes to the property acquisition through her own business she is entitled to claim interest in the property unless a contrary intention is proven.  I think the cases cited by counsel for the appellant, namely Odoteye vrs. Odoteye (cited above ) Gyamaah vrs Buor (cited above) and Button vrs. Button (1968) 1 W.L.R. 457 at 462 C.A. did not rule out the possibility of a wife's entitlement to a share of her husband's building by her contribution. In the last case cited the court was concerned that the woman made no contribution and that the fact that she carried out certain household chores was not sufficient to give her an interest. In the Odoteye case (supra) the woman's claim failed because, as found by the court, she failed to provide any evidence of her financial contribution to the cost of the house. In the Gyamaah case (Supra), the court, whilst dismissing the woman's claim, nevertheless has this to say, albeit obiter, in the head notes:

"where the wife's assistance takes the form of a substantial financial contribution, she will be entitled as of right to a share in the properties acquired by the husband."

The trial court judge considered the relevant law and facts in arriving at her decision, quite contrary to counsel for the appellant's charge that she did not do so. There was sufficient evidence on the record that the plaintiff contributed to the purchase of the plot from her bakery business.  There is overwhelming evidence that it was the plaintiff and her children, some of whom were in gainful employment, who put up the house on the plot for their occupation when they faced the threat of ejectment from where they were living.  The trial court's conclusion cannot therefore be faulted on grounds 1, 2, and 3 in view of the three reasons advanced by counsel for the appellant and I accordingly dismiss them.

I move next to ground 4. Having found for the plaintiff, the trial court held "that the defendant did not acquire any legal title or interest in the property in dispute as the late Agbosu not being the sole owner could not dispose of it without the knowledge and consent of the plaintiff”. So she declared the purported sole by the late Agbosu Seth to the defendant null and void and set it aside." Counsel for the appellant submitted that

"the worse scenario that could happen to the defendant in this drama assuming the plaintiff was able to prove her joint ownership was to divide the property and give the portion of Seth K. Agbosu to the defendant Seth Agbosu did not  die before the property was sold to invoke the right of survivorship which is not the present law in Ghana — see section 14(3) of the Conveyancing Decree, N.R.C.D. 175. The deceased disposed of his interest in the property in his life time and a purchaser in the person of the defendant acquired a valid interest. There was therefore no basis for the learned trial Judge to set the sale aside and declare title in the property in favour of the plaintiff — a claim which has been abandoned in preference to joint ownership.”

The plaintiff went to court seeking declaration of title to the whole land with building thereon, and not a part of it. The averments made it clear the deceased contributed nothing to the construction of the house. The defendant did not plead that he only bought the interest of the deceased in this property, he claimed he purchased the entire land with the building thereon. There is no counter-claim for a fifty-percent interest in the property, so either the defendant gets the whole or nothing at all. Either party not having put in a claim for only a part, it will be wrong for the court to apportion the property between the parties, for that will be substituting an entirely different case for the parties, which practice the Supreme Court disapproved of in the case of Dam vrs. J.K. Addo and Brothers (1962) 2 G.L.R. 200.  I reject this ground accordingly.

Next, is ground 5. Counsel submitted that the tort of trespass to land consists of a direct, intentional or negligent intrusion or interference with land in possession of another. That is a correct view of the law. He continued "The late Seth K. Agbosu was owner in possession of the said property and sold same to the defendant who after the purchase was placed in possession. How can that in law amount to trespass?"

For his part counsel for the respondent said the appellant was put on notice prior to the purchase of the plaintiff’s interest in this property and yet he went ahead to purchase it thereby deliberately committing a trespass thereto.

The trial court rejected the defendant's claim to be a bona fide purchaser. It found the defendant was warned by plaintiff and her children not to purchase this property. The dismissal of this claim was not one of the grounds of appeal, and even if it had been raised I would have dismissed it for the same reasons given by the trial Judge. Where a person is put on notice that property he seeks to buy does not belong to his vendor and he goes ahead to buy it he is clearly acting negligently or even recklessly and so his entry upon the property is clearly trespassory. And so long as plaintiff was able to prove title to the land and to the building thereon and even the economic trees on the land she was effectively in possession (in the absence of anybody being in physical possession) for the purposes of satisfying possession in the law of trespass. It has been held in Seraphim vrs, Amua-Sekyi (1962) 1 GLR. 328, holding 2, that the presence of mango and other economic trees prior to the purchase by the defendant proved that the land was in the possession of someone. In this case apart from economic trees, there was a building on the land which proved possession in somebody who has been found on the evidence to be the plaintiff and her children.  The defendant is saying he bought the house from the co-owner so he committed no trespass.  I have held already that was not the case he set up so he cannot now seek shelter under joint or co-ownership, something he never accepted.  The unassailable evidence is that the deceased abhorred the idea of building at Somanya and so consented to the wife (a native of Somanya) and her children to build there.  Thus as her husband and father the deceased might have been allowed to occupy this house if he were alive but beyond that he would not, and did not, have the right to sell the house.  The law is that a defendant, if he acted on the authority of his vendor, must prove that the vendor was the real owner; where the vendor was not the real owner, such a defendant commits trespass if he enters the land.  And even if the vendor is a co-owner he is not entitled to sell the property all by himself, if he does the purchaser if he assumed possession of the property commits trespass even if he did not know of the co-owner’s lack of authority to sell.  See the case of Bucknor vrs. Essien (1963) 1 G.L.R. 426 S.C.  Accordingly I dismiss this ground of appeal too.

The final leg of this appeal is ground 6.  The trial court Judge considered the search documents emanating from the Lands Department dubious.  Her reasons were these:

“Exhibit 1, a copy of the search report is not dated.  Exhibit 5 an application for a search was dated 6th December, 1984.  Exhibit 6, the original search report prepared at the Lands Secretariat, Koforidua bears the stamp of Accra Lands Department dated 4th December 1984, two days before an application for search was made.  The entries were dated 7th December, 1984. No explanation was offered for this jumble in date.”

Counsel for the appellant referred to these exhibits and said he did not see the basis for the trial court’s conclusion.  It behoves us to examine these exhibits ourselves. Counsel for the appellant said Exhibit 1 is the official search result form issued from the Koforidua Registry of land.  But it is clear Counsel did not address the reason raised by the trial Judge that this report was undated, which is an undeniable fact.  Thus how could the court determine that it was prepared at the request of the defendants and if so on what date so as to help the court determine whether indeed he did make this search before the purchase.  The date of the search was relevant in considering the question of bona fide purchaser without notice.  The trial Judge was right in not attaching any weight to it for lack of date.

Exhibit 5, it is true, is a letter from defendant’s solicitor Mr. Sam Okudjeto addressed to the Registrar of Lands, Koforidua, calling for a search in respect of a piece of land.  The trial court, from its decision, gave no consideration at all to this letter and indeed rejected it for reasons I have quoted above.  She also rejected Exhibit 6 which appears to be the response to Exhibit 4.  I think the trial Judge’s conclusion on these two exhibits cannot be supported.  The reason being that though Exhibit 5 was addressed to the Registrar of Lands at Koforidua, yet Exhibit 6 which is a pre-designed form states that “the report may be collected from the Chief Registrar of Lands, 28th February Road Accra fourteen clear working days ………. after the delivery to him of this application”

Thus it is nothing “dubious” for the report to be actually signed by the Chief Registrar of Lands in Accra.  I think the trial judge fell into the error by mis-reading the date on the report Exhibit 6 which is 7th December, 1984 to be 4th December, 1984, which made the report appear to ante-date the application.  From the judgment she had the original exhibit before her and the way the “7” is written could not easily be mis-read to be “4” The mistake is regrettable.

For the purposes of this appeal what we have to consider is whether the error led her to decide a key issue against the defendant thereby occasioning a grave miscarriage of justice.  It is undeniable that it is not every error by a Judge which vitiates his decision, except that which influences the decision in some serious way.  From the judgment these exhibits were being considered in relation to defendant’s plea of bona fide purchaser.  The rejection of these exhibits meant the trial court did not find defendant made the search.  Whether he made a search or not, the plaintiff herself admitted that the documents on the property were all in the name of the deceased, see paragraph 3 of the Reply filed on 21st May, 1987.  Thus these search exhibits 1, 5 and 6 introduced nothing new to the case, for the principle of law is that where facts are admitted no evidence need be led on them.  The plaintiff never denied the defendant’s averment that he caused a search to be made at the Lands Registry which confirmed the deceased’s title to the land.  Thus these facts were not in dispute and the trial court just had to consider them which she did when she rejected the defence.  More importantly, the search result did not confer an indefeasible title in defendant’s vendor, let alone in him (defendant).

Besides, apart from these exhibits which were rejected, the court found as a fact that the defendant was warned against the purchase of this property and was manifestly aware of other interest in it before he purchased it.  Thus even though the searches disclosed the deceased as owner, as I said that was not indefeasible.  Thus apart from the fact that these exhibits only sought to confirm facts which had been admitted on the pleadings and thus totally unnecessary to prove at the trial, they did not constitute a defence to the plaintiff’s charge that they personally warned him against the sale and also by the posting of notice on the building.  The defendant cannot be heard to say that he ignored the verbal warning and hearing notice on the building because his searches at the lands told him the property belonged to the deceased.  He was duly fixed with notice.  Thus in my view the error caused by the Judge had no bearing at all on the issues decided in this action.  And moreover the trial court did not decide the case in favour of the plaintiff because she found these exhibits dubious.  I will dismiss this ground of appeal too.

In the result I’ll dismiss the appeal with costs.
