JUDGMENT

ANIM, J.A.

This is an appeal from the judgment of the Circuit Court, Kumasi, dated 22nd  January 2002 presided over by His Honour Ato Assan. Before going into the merits or demerits of this, appeal it is necessary to relate the events, which led to the judgment of 22nd January 2002.

On 4th February 1999, the Plaintiff/Respondent (hereinafter referred to as "the Plaintiff') commenced action at the Circuit Court, Kumasi, against the Defendants/Appellants (hereinafter referred to as "the Defendants"). By his Writ of Summons the Plaintiff asked the Court for:—

(1) Declaration of Plaintiffs maternal family's title to and recovery of possession of

(a) House Plot 11, Block 1, New Amakom Extention Kumasi and

(b) 7½ (seven and half) tons of iron rods stored in a kitchen in the said house.

(2) An Order for an account of the Rents and/or compensation for the use of all the rooms in the two (2) flats in the said House from 21st June 1996 up to the date of surrender of possession of the said House to Plaintiff by the Defendants.

(3) Perpetual injunction restraining the Defendants, other members of their family, agents, servants or workers from interfering with the Plaintiff and his maternal family's possession and enjoyment of the house and the iron rods.

The Plaintiff's case is that he had uterine brothers, Opanin Kwame Poku @ Poku Fofie and Kwaku Poku @ Nana Poku Frefie both deceaseds: he also had one uterine sister by name Akosua Dufie.

Opanin Kwame Poku died on 28th April 1977 and was succeeded by Nana Poku Frefre: Nana Poku Frefre also died on 21st July 1996. It was after his death that (he) Plaintiff succeeded Nana Poku Frefre.

The Plaintiff said during the life time of Opanin Kwame Poku he acquired House No. Plot 11, Block 1, New Amakom Extension, Kumasi, consisting of an uncompleted main building and an out-house; the main building was constructed before the out house. Nana Poku Frefre took possession of the said house after the death of Opanin Kwame Poku and remained in possession and managed the house up to 1987 when he became a chief; he then appointed his nephew Peter Kofi Karikari as his caretaker and gave him Power of Attorney to manage the house from 1987 up to the time Nana Poku Frefie died.

The Defendants' case in brief is that during his lifetime Opanin Kwame Poku evinced an intention to make a gift of the house in dispute to them which intention was allegedly made known to Nana Poku Frefre: they also allege that this intention of Opanin Kwame Poku was put into effect by Nana Poku Frefre who informed his matrilineal family about the alleged intention of Opanin Kwame Poku to make an inter vivos gift of the house to the Defendants: they also claim that Opanin Kwame Poku's matrilineal family carried out the former's intention by the acceptance the sum of ¢100,000 and two bottles schnapps from the Defendants as "Aseda" on the fortieth day celebration of the death of Opanin Kwame Poku.

The learned trial judge after evaluating the evidence, gave Judgment for the Plaintiff and rejected the Defendants' evidence in relation to the inter vivos gift. He took the view that the Defendants' claim of having paid the sum of ¢100,000 as "Aseda" was as fantastic as it was outrageous. According to the Learned trial judge he had taken judicial notice of the fact that at the level of 1977 prices, ¢100,000 could comfortable have purchased at least one mansion in a prime area of the city of Kumasi. And that such a huge sum was incompatible with the customary concept or notion of the donees offering a token in acceptance of a substantial gift such as a house.

Aggrieved by and dissatisfied with the judgment, the Defendants have appealed to this Court. In the Notice of Appeal filed on 28th January 2002, one main ground of Appeal has been set out for trial:— i.e.

"The judgment is against the with of evidence".

Appeal is by way of rehearing and confronted with this ground of appeal, I bound to examine thoroughly the entire evidence. In the said judgment the learned trial judge stated the method whereby property becomes impressed with the incidents of family property among the Akans. According to him the disputed house. No. Plot 11, Block 1 New Amakom Extension, Kumasi, was an uncompleted two storey building. It was acquired by the late husband of the 1st Defendant, Yaa Mansah, and by Akan Customary law, his maternal family would in the absence of any evidence to the contrary inherent that house on the death intestate of Opanin Kwame Poku in 1977.

Bearing in mind that the intestate Succession Law 1985 (PNDC Law 111) came into force on 14th June 1985, I entirely agree with the Learned trial judge's assessment of the state of the Customary Law of Akans as it then was.

This proposition of the customary law, of Akans has its root in Professor Allott's unpublished thesis " The Akan Law of Property 1954" where he enumerates four main instances or methods whereby a family may acquire or re-acquire title to land. He hammers on property acquired by succession and says "The family as a whole acquires title to property through decease intestate of a member of the family, and their consequent succession to his self-acquired property". In his dealing with the same topic Professor Woodman in a Learned article entitled "The acquisition of family Land in Ghana" mentions seven methods whereby a family acquires land. On the issue of succession he stresses—by death of an owner of self-acquired property intestate or a devise by will of a family.

In paragraph 5 of the Statement of claim it was stated that after the death of Nana Opoku Frefre, Plaintiff was appointed his customary successor and H/No. Plot 11, Block 1, New Amakom Extension, Kumasi, was entrusted to him by his family. Since the Defendants also in paragraph 5 of the Statement of Defence filed had denied that the House, the subject-matter in dispute, was entrusted to the Plaintiff by his family, it was incumbent on the Learned trial judge to have resolved the issue as to who can sue or be sued in relation to family property.

Lack of capacity to sue is a valid defence to a civil suit and an action for declaration is not an exception to the rule. If a plaintiff brings an action for a declaration of title to land in his own right he must disclose by his pleadings that he has an interest in the land which the law recognises and which must be protected. He must go further to show that at the time of instituting the action the interest he had in the action had been infringed. In other words, he must be clothed with the present cause of action against the defendants otherwise he fails in limine.

Where, however an individual brings an action in a representative capacity either as head of family, occupant of stool, administrator of an estate etc he must be clothed with his capacity before the institution of the suit and not later during the pendency of the suit. If the representative capacity is challenged he cannot succeed in the action unless he satisfies the court that he possessed that capacity at the time of instituting the action.

By the provisions of Order 15 rule 13 (2) of the High Court (Civil Procedure) Rules 1954 LN 140 A as amended by LI. 1129 S.I. the Head of family or occupant of a Stool (while a Stool is vacant, the regent or caretaker of such Stool) may sue and be sued on behalf of the family or stool. Hence where an individual sues in respect of a family land or stool land or any other land communally owned he is bound to be challenged as to his capacity unless he brings himself within the exception laid down in the leading case of KWAN vrs. NYIENI (1959) GLR 67 at page 72 where Van Lare Ag. C.J. (as he then was) delivering the unanimous decision of the Court of Appeal said the special circumstances which would permit a court to entertain an action from an individual were:

 

(a) Where the family property is in danger of being lost to the family, and it is shown that the Head (either out of personal interest, or otherwise) will not make a move to save or preserve it,

(b) Where owing to a division in the family, the head and some of the principal members will not take any steps or,

       (c)             Where the head and the principal members are deliberately disposing of the family property in their personal interest to the detriment of the family as a whole.

The Learned Ag. C.J. went on to stress that there must be

(i) Proof that the member is authorised by the other members of the family to sue.

(ii)  Proof of necessity and

(iii) Proof that the action has been brought to preserve the family character of property.

In another case where the Plaintiff in his capacity as successor and the Head of family sued on behalf of the family, the Court of Appeal in AMPONSAH vrs. KWA TIA (1976) 2 GLR 189 held:—

"When the Plaintiff in his capacity as the head of family and successor, sued on behalf of the family, there was a presumption that he had the consent of the principal members of the family."

The emphasis is mine. I accept and adopt the ratios in these two cases as the correct statement of the law.

This being an action for declaration of title to land, what is the burden of proof cast on the Plaintiff? It was held in RUFAI vrs. RICKETTS (1934) 2 WACA 95 at page 97 per Webber C.J. thus:—

"This is a claim for a declaration of title to land and the onus of proving ownership is on the Plaintiff. The result depends on the strength of the Plaintiff's case and not on the weakness of the defendant case"

Recently, IN NARTEY vrs. MECHANICAL LLOYD ASSEMBLY PLANT LTD. (1987-88) GLRD 86 SC and again in ODAMETEY vrs. CLOUGH (1989- 90) GLRD I SC the Supreme Court said:—

"The present position was that if the Plaintiff in a civil suit failed to discharge the onus on him and thus completely failed to make a case for the claim for which the sought relief, then he could not rely on the weakness in the defendant's case to ask for relief. If however, he made a case which would entitle him to relief if the defendant offered no evidence, then if the case offered by the defendant when he did give evidence discloses any weakness which tendered to support the Plaintiffs claim, then in such a situation the Plaintiff was entitled to rely on the weakness in the defendant's case to strengthen his case. That was amply supported by sections 11 & 12 particularly section 11 (4) of the Evidence Decree 1975 (NRCD 323)"

The next obvious question is: What was the nature of the evidence led in the lower court in proof of the Plaintiff's Capacity? In substance, this is what he said:—

"I am Kingsley Adu Poku Mensah, I am a Cocoa Farmer at Atonsu, Agogo, Kumasi. The first Defendant was the wife of my late brother Kwame, Poku @ Poku Fofie, Kwame Poku @ Poku Fofie was succeeded by my late eldest brother, Nana Poku Frefre otherwise known as Kwaku Poku. He also died on 21/7/1996. Poku Fofie died on 28/4/1977.

I know H/No. Plot 11, Block 1, New Amakom Extension, Kumasi. The house was built by my late elder brother Kwame Poku alias Poku Fofie. After the death of Poku Fofie my eldest late brother Nana Poku Frefre @ Kwaku Poku took control of the house. He did not live in the house though Nana Poku Frefre solely managed the house in dispute till he became a chief and thereafter gave power of Attorney to our nephew Karikari to manage same. After the death of Nana Poku Frefre I was appointed customary successor. Since my appointment I have been paying the property rate in the name of my late brother Kwame Poku. Shortly after Poku Frefre's fortieth day when I was appointed his successor, I decided to go to the house to see to the management of the property. Unfortunately, each time I went there I was met with hostility from the children, Adu Poku and Adu Gyamfi. The first insulted me and called me names and prevented me from entering the property, I left the scene.

I went there again on another occasion and I was met with the same treatment by the same Adu Gyamfi. On the third occasion I went with a builder and his mate. Adu Gyamfi came up to us and started to insult me and threatened to push me downstairs"

The first witness, PW1, who testified in relation to the Plaintiffs successorship said this:

"I am Peter Kofi Karikari: When Poku Fofie died he was succeeded by Kwaku Poku @ Poku Frefre. It was in April 1977. After the succession the family had a meeting and handed over the house to Poku Frefre. He took over the house and managed it. Whilst Poku Frefre was in possession of the house, he appointed me caretaker of the premises up to 1996. When Poku Frefre died the family appointed Plaintiff as customary successor. After Poku Frefre's death, the house and all other property of Poku Frefre were handed over to the Plaintiff as his customary successor. When the Plaintiff as his customary successor went to the house the children threatened to beat him. Even when I went to the house the children did the same thing to me."

The Plaintiff then called his next witnesses, PW2, whose entire evidence was as follows:—

"My name is Kwasi Nyame. I live at Bibiani. I am a farmer. I know the Plaintiff. He came to my cottage four days ago.

I know Maame Yaa Mansah. I know the 3rd and 5th Defendants. They are my children. I was in Bibiani with 1st Defendant and her husband. When the husband died, we were all farming there. Her husband was called Poku. I married the 1st Defendant after the death of her husband. We were married for over fifteen years. We cohabited in my cottage. We quarrelled and 1st Defendant said she would no longer marry. I performed customary marriage rites."

PW3 George Agyei Asamani testified that he was a Civil Servant and a Principal Rent Officer stationed in Kumasi who had been served with Subpoena to testify and to tender certain documents in relation to rent assessment report of House Number Plot 11, Block 1, Amakom Extension, Kumasi. He said when he was going to do the assessment which was at the instance of the Plaintiff, he invited the Defendants. He said nothing about the Plaintiff succeeding to any property.

The last witness, PW4, who testified for the Plaintiff said this:—

"I am Kwame Antwi. I am head of family. I appointed Kwame Poku's senior brother Poku Frefre as his customary successor when he died. At that time Poku Frefre was not holding any position. Later he became a Chief. He also passed away and when he died the Plaintiff succeeded him. Kwame Poku had property. He had a house at Amakom. He was also farming in the forest. He had farms. I never heard and I don't know that Kwame Poku gifted his house to the Defendants. It is not correct that on the 40th day after Kwame Poku's death two bottles of schnapps and ¢100,000 were presented to the family to ratify any gift."

Then came the turn of the Defendant to testify in support of the alleged inter vivos gift. This is what she offered:—

"I am Yaa Mansah. I know the Plaintiff. He is the brother of my husband. My husband was Poku Fofie; Yaa Minka is my daughter. The rest of the Defendants are my children by Poku Fofie. I know house number Plot 11, Block 1, New Amakom Extension, Kumasi. My children and I are now owners of the house. By the time the Plaintiff arrived from abroad the building had been put up. Incidentally during the lifetime of my husband he told Poku Frefre that he had built the house for me and my children so on the fortieth day celebration of his death Poku Fofie told the family about the wish of my husband. The family took drink and money from me and my brother. Yaw Berko Boateng provided the drink and the money. He provided ¢100,000. The "Aseda" was given to Panin Kwame Antwi, then family Head of my husband's family. Panin Kwame Antwi gave evidence in this Court as PW4."

The Defendants evidence was corroborated in material particular by Yaw Boateng, her brother DW1, DW2 Ama Fosua, the wife of the late Kwaku Poku @ Nana Poku Frefre and DW3 Yaw Adu Gyamfi, a son of the late Kwame Poku.

Clearly, on the evidence, the Defendants had not been able to satisfy the requirements of a valid inter vivos gift. It was held in the leading case of SUMMEY vrs. YOHUNO & ORS. (1962) 1GLR 160 SC that:—

"To claim land as a customary gift inter vivos, it must be established, quite apart from the usual customary formalities, that

(1) The gift was voluntary and gratuitous;

(2) That the gift was made in the lifetime of the donor;

(3) There was a full intention that the land shall remain in the property of the donee without restraining it to the donor; and

(4) That the donee accepted the gift and went into possession of the land, either physically or constructively, during the lifetime of the donor.

The evidence did not disclose that the alleged gift was made in the lifetime of the donor and neither did it disclose that the Defendants accepted the gift and went into possession during the lifetime of the donor. In the light of this I agree entirely with the learned trial judge's rejection of the defendants' evidence that Kwame Poku gifted the disputed house to the defendants.

But it was plain that at the end of the Plaintiff own case, the quality of the evidence which they had so far adduced was nowhere near the standard of proof required of a Plaintiff who assumed the bureau of establishing his capacity to initiate action. The evidence adduced established no more than that he was appointed a successor after the death of Kwaku Poku alias Nana Poku Frefre. It did not go to show that he was a successor as well as a head of family so as to raise any presumption that he had the consent and concurrence of the principal members of the family as in the AMPONSAH vrs. KWATIA case.

By the very nature of the relief sought in the writ of summons, and being an individual, it was incumbent on the Plaintiff to have adduced prima facie evidence which could bring him under any of the exceptions to the rule in KWAN vrs. NYIENI cited earlier. On the contrary the Head of family was called as witness.

It is my view that the issue of the Plaintiff's capacity involved a serious point of law which the trial judge should have considered. It is obvious that if the trial judge had analysed the facts in greater detail than he did, the issue of capacity could not have escaped his attention. Indeed, the question of the Plaintiff's capacity when he took over the litigation as Plaintiff seriously undermined his entire case and the relief's which the trial judge granted him.

The Learned trial judge obviously erred in glossing over the issue of capacity and then preceding to consider the Plaintiff's case on its merits. Where a person's capacity to initiate proceedings is in issue, it is no answer to give that person a hearing on the merits even if he had a cast-iron case: (see the Supreme Court case of SARKODIEH 11 vrs. BOATENG 11 (1982-83) GLR 715 S.C.)

Above all, an action for a declaration of title is a discretionary remedy. In the leading case of IKEBIFE ISENEWEKA vrs. PETER EGSUNA (1964) 1 WLR 219 at pp 224-225 Viscount Radcliff stressed the discretionary character of an action for declaration as follows:—

"The general theme of judicial observations has been to the effect that declarations are not lightly to be granted.

The power should exercised sparingly; with "great care and jealousy", with "extreme caution". These are indeed counsels of moderation, even though, as Lord Dunedin once observed such expressions afford little guidance for particular cases. Nevertheless, anxious warnings of this character appear to their Lordships to be not so much enunciations of legal principle as administrative cautions issued by eminent and prudent judges to their, possibly more reckless successors. After all it is doubtful if there is more of principle involved than the undoubted truth that the power to grant a declaration should be exercised with a proper sense of responsibility and a full realization that judicial pronouncements ought not be issued unless there are circumstances that call for their making."

With these anxious warnings thundering into my eardrums, it is only wise that I should revisit the cross-examination of the Plaintiff by Counsel for the Defendants. The following encounter took place:—

Q:  Have you ever lived in England?

A:  Yes.

Q:  Which period?

A:  I lived in England from 1952 up to 27-12-55. Come back to the Gold Coast for about a month, went back and came back to Ghana on 18-1-60 to consult with my late father. In short I lived there from 1952 to 1991 but there were some punctuations.

Q:   When you left England in 1991 where did you go?

A:   I came to Ghana.

Q:  Have you been living in Ghana since 1991 to date.

A:  Yes.

Q:  You described yourself as a cocoa farmer, where exactly do you have your farm?

A: My cocoa farm is at a village known as Bethlehem near Bibiani in the Western Region and previously owned by my late brother Kwame Poku @ Poku Fofie.

Q:   So these farms are not your self-acquired property?

A:   No, they are inherited.

Q:  You personally do not have any cocoa farm any where?

A:  Certainly not".

The Learned Counsel for the Defendants continued further cross-examination thus:—

Q: You have instituted action against the widow and children of your late brother Nana Poku Frefre at the High Court, Kumasi?

A:  Yes.

Q: And in that action too you are claiming the property of Nana Poku Frefre as the property of your family?

A:  Yes.

The emphasis is mine.

I must say that I find the answers to the questions posed by Counsel for the Defendants extremely revealing indeed. There is evidence in the record which shows that apart from house number plot 11, Block 1, New Amakom Extention, Kumasi, the subject matter in dispute, that the deceased Kwame Poku alias Poku Fofie also had large tracks of cocoa farms in Bibiani, Western Region. It was these cocoa farms that the Plaintiff had inherited after the death of Kwame Poku @ Poku Fofie.

If the Plaintiff has been bold to admit that the cocoa farms at Bibiani, Western Region, which became family property by operation of customary law are his "cocoa farms" do I need to look for to infer that the present action is not being brought to preserve the family character of the property. Certainly not.

In BLUNT vrs. BLUNT (1943) AC 517 HL Viscount Simon, L.C. delivering the judgment of the House of Lords said:—

"An appeal against the exercise of the Courts discretion can only succeed on the ground that the discretion was exercised on wrong or inadequate materials in the sense that it can be shown that the court acted under a misapprehension of fact, in that it either gave weight to irrelevant or unproved matters or omitted to take relevant matters into account".

See also KHOURY vrs. KHOURY (1962) 1 GLR 98; TRABOULSI COMPANY vrs. PATERSON ZOCHONIS & COMPANY LIMITED (1973) 1 BOFOUR (1971) 2 GLR 199.

I am inclined to disturb the discretion exercised by the Learned trial judge. The evidence adduced demonstrates to this court that the Learned trial judge in exercising his discretion acted under a misapprehension of fact, in that he either gave weight to irrelevant or unproved matters or omitted to take into account relevant matters.

As I said the trial judge erred in glossing over the issue of capacity and then proceeding to consider the Plaintiffs case, on its merits. The Law is that where a person's capacity to initiate proceedings is in issue, it is no answer to give that person a hearing on the merits even if he had a cast-iron case. (see SARKODIEH case supra).

For these reasons I will allow the appeal, set aside the judgment of the Circuit Court, Kumasi, dated 22nd January 2002, including any order as to costs; any costs paid are to  be refunded

S. Y. ANIM

(JUSTICE OF APPEAL)
